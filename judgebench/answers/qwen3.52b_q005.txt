This question touches on one of the most profound and unsettling challenges of Christian ethics: how to move beyond human instincts of safety and survival into something that feels almost self-destructive. Matthew 5:44–45 presents a paradoxical command. It demands you act in direct opposition to logic, self-interest, and likely political power.

Why do people often reject or take this instruction lightly? It is not usually because the text isn't profound. Rather, it is deeply rooted in human psychology and social dynamics. Here’s a breakdown of why enemy-love feels difficult, categorized by the lenses requested—sentimental, strategic, and moral strength.

### 1. The Psychological (Sentimental) Barrier
The most immediate objection to love for enemies isn't moral; it's emotional. Most people view relationships through a lens of reciprocity: "If you help me, I will help you." This logic breaks down when applied to conflict.
*   **It feels like "bending":** When you say "I love you," you are saying your bond is stronger than the conflict between us. In a world that prioritizes power and judgment, this can feel sentimental because it requires you to deny your own identity or self-defense.
*   **The Emotional Toll:** It is not easy for the one being abused to say "I love my attacker's enemy" without feeling ashamed. The command feels like betrayal of self-protection, even if God says otherwise.
*   **Human Tendency for Self-Salvation:** Most humans think they are "good enough" if they don't get hurt. To truly love an enemy is to say my safety doesn't outweigh our connection. This requires a level of emotional maturity that many find exhausting or unnatural, leading a lot of people to minimize the command rather than face the challenge of it.

### 2. The Strategic Misunderstanding
Some view this as "strategic softness." However, looking at modern strategic thinking (or historical political maneuvering), we often see enemy-love seen as "weakness because we are too focused on winning."
*   **The Trap of Victory:** If the command is framed as "I must love the aggressor to win," it becomes a tool for manipulation. This creates psychological resistance in those who believe they have power over others by virtue of their superiority.
*   **Risk Reversal:** In many conflict zones, loyalty to one's side implies taking risks that don't make sense socially. Loving an enemy means "betraying the group" or "betraying the enemy's interests." This feels strategically unsafe in a way that makes it hard to take seriously on a personal level: *"I have no reason to trust my own conscience if I'm acting against my side."*
*   **Why it's not purely strategic:** It is also because the goal isn't power. The text states this action *is* difficult, not because we are weak, but because the outcome (being children of God) is not a transaction for safety.

### 3. The Deeper Moral Strength
Is it sentimental? No. Is it purely moral strength? It is more than strength; it is a **redefinition of identity** that requires moral courage to live out. Here is why this is the core of the challenge:

*   **Identity Beyond Self-Protection:** If you are your own enemy, you cannot love another person who harms you without destroying yourself as well. Loving an enemy forces you to recognize that your identity is not defined by your safety or value. You can be valuable while being in conflict with others.
*   **Redefining the "Enemy":** Most of us see a moral boundary between "good" people and "bad" people. Jesus redefines this: love your enemies means your worth isn't contingent on them. It's not about how they treat you; it is about how *you* are treated.
*   **A Test of Moral Strength:** Realizing it requires the enemy to be part of a unified moral community, and it creates a "test" in the human heart where one must prove that one's love is stronger than their own safety or anger. This demand for strength comes from the command itself: *"That ye may be children."*

### Conclusion: Why Is It Difficult?
Enemy-love feels hard primarily because it asks us to accept **the opposite of our natural social logic.** We are built on a desire for security and reciprocity. Loving a person who "hates" you contradicts that. However, taking this seriously is not sentimental; it is about recognizing the true nature of power.

When people treat enemy-love as a sentimentality, they often miss its core: **It is an act of courage to choose a higher standard than your own self-preservation.**

To "take it seriously," one must ask:
1.  Is my love for my enemies born from fear or hope? (Fear makes it sentimental; hope makes it meaningful.)
2.  Am I willing to be the child of God even when I am in conflict with you? (This is the true moral test.)
3.  Do I view this action as a moral victory or just a transaction?

Ultimately, enemy-love is not difficult because it's a weak emotion. It is difficult because it requires **cognitive discipline** to shift your focus from "who wins" to "who God makes." This is the deeper form of moral strength that demands a different kind of human nature, one where love isn't just a feeling, but an act of spiritual authority.

The command is clear: *"That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven."* This requires more than patience; it requires a moral maturity that accepts conflict as part of your path to identity, rather than something you need to fix or avoid. If one is truly capable of loving their enemy, they have already mastered this form of moral strength.
