**Short answer:**  
The passage treats speech as **all three** – a symptom of what is already stored in the heart, a **choice** that the person makes in the moment, and a **revelation** that makes the hidden “treasure” of the heart visible to others.  

Below is a step‑by‑step exegesis that shows how the text folds these three dimensions together.

---

## 1. The literary‑theological framework

| Element | Greek (Matt 12:34‑35) | Literal sense | Typical biblical metaphor |
|---------|----------------------|---------------|---------------------------|
| **vipers** | *ὄφεις* (snakes) | a poisonous, deceptive creature | “generation of vipers” = morally corrupt, deceitful people (cf. Matt 3:7) |
| **heart** | *καρδία* | the inner seat of will, intention, and moral character | “treasure” (θησαυρός) = stored value, hidden wealth (cf. Matt 6:21) |
| **abundance** | *πλῆρα* (fullness, overflow) | a surplus that cannot be contained | “out of the abundance of the heart” = whatever overflows from the inner store |
| **mouth** | *στόμα* | the organ of speech, the public interface | “the mouth speaks” = the visible output of the inner life (cf. Prov 10:20) |

The parallelism in the two verses (good vs. evil) mirrors the Hebrew wisdom tradition (e.g., Proverbs 10:20, 15:28) where the **heart** is the hidden source and the **mouth** the visible expression.

---

## 2. Words as **symptoms** (diagnostic signs)

1. **Observational logic** – “for out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh.”  
   - The *mouth* is the *symptom* that a physician (or judge) can see.  
   - The “abundance” is already present; speech merely *displays* it.

2. **Biblical precedent** –  
   - *Jeremiah 17:9‑10*: “The heart is deceitful… but those who trust in the LORD shall be blessed.” The heart’s condition is *diagnosed* by the person’s conduct, especially speech.  
   - *Proverbs 10:20*: “The tongue of the righteous is a well of life, but the mouth of the wicked conceals violence.” Here the tongue is a *symptom* of the inner moral state.

3. **Psychological parallel** – Modern research on “emotional leakage” shows that people often utter what they feel even when they try to conceal it (e.g., micro‑expressions, linguistic markers). The passage anticipates this insight: the *heart* cannot be completely sealed off; its *symptoms* leak out.

---

## 3. Words as **choices** (voluntary acts)

1. **Agency in the act of speaking** – The Greek verb *λαλεῖ* (to speak) is **active**; it is not an automatic reflex but a *deliberate* act.  
   - Even a “good man” *chooses* to bring forth good things; an “evil man” *chooses* to bring forth evil things. The passage does not say the mouth *must* speak whatever is in the heart; it says the *typical* outcome is that the speaker *chooses* language that matches the hidden stockpile.

2. **Moral responsibility** – By calling the audience “generation of vipers,” Jesus holds them accountable for the *choice* of their words. The warning is not that they are powerless; they are *responsible* for the words they utter.

3. **Parallel with the “law of speech”** – In Jewish law (e.g., *Lashon Hara*), the *choice* of words is a moral act in its own right. The passage can be read as an early articulation of that principle: the *choice* of speech reveals the internal moral alignment.

---

## 4. Words as **revelations** (disclosure of hidden treasure)

1. **The “treasure” metaphor** – *θησαυρός* (treasure) connotes a *private* store of value, not publicly visible. By saying “the mouth brings forth” this treasure, the text frames speech as a *revelatory* process: the hidden becomes known.

2. **Revelatory function in the narrative** –  
   - Jesus uses the Pharisees’ own words (e.g., “We speak in the name of the Lord”) to *expose* the discrepancy between their claimed identity and their inner moral stockpile.  
   - The audience is invited to *read* the heart through the mouth, just as a reader decodes a text to discover the author’s intent.

3. **Theological implication** – In the broader New Testament, speech is often a *means of revelation*:  
   - *John 15:7*: “If you remain in me, and my words remain in you…” – words disclose the inner union with Christ.  
   - *1 Peter 4:11*: “If anyone speaks, let it be as if he were speaking the very words of God.” – speech becomes a conduit for divine revelation.

---

## 5. How the three aspects intertwine

| Aspect | How it shows up in Matt 12:34‑35 | Interaction with the other two |
|--------|----------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Symptom** | The mouth *reflects* the heart’s abundance. | The symptom is *observable*; it informs the listener about the hidden treasure, which in turn *guides* the speaker’s future choices. |
| **Choice** | The speaker *decides* what to say; the decision is *filtered* through the heart’s content. | The choice *produces* the symptom; the symptom *reveals* whether the choice aligns with the hidden treasure. |
| **Revelation** | Speech *discloses* the hidden treasure to others. | The revelation *confirms* the symptom’s diagnostic value and *exposes* the moral quality of the choice. |

Thus, the passage does not isolate any single dimension; it presents a **feedback loop**:

1. **Hidden treasure** (heart) → **Choice** (what to say) → **Symptom** (the spoken word) → **Revelation** (others see the treasure) → **Judgment** (the “generation of vipers” is judged).

---

## 6. Practical implications for interpretation

| Context | Emphasis |
|---------|----------|
| **Pastoral counseling** | Treat speech as a *diagnostic tool* (symptom) for inner health; encourage intentional *choices* that align with a “good treasure.” |
| **Ethical teaching** | Highlight that *revelatory* speech can either conceal or expose moral truth; teach believers to guard the “treasure” by cultivating inner virtues. |
| **Biblical hermeneutics** | Recognize the *parallelism* of the Hebrew wisdom tradition; see the passage as a *theological claim* that inner moral reality inevitably surfaces in language. |

---

## 7. Bottom line

- **Words are symptoms** because they *display* the moral state already present in the heart.  
- **Words are choices** because the speaker *actively* selects what to utter, and that selection is constrained by the heart’s content.  
- **Words are revelations** because they *make visible* the hidden “treasure” of the heart to anyone who listens.

Consequently, Matthew 12:34‑35 teaches that **speech is a three‑fold phenomenon**—a diagnostic sign, a voluntary act, and a disclosure of inner moral wealth. The passage thus invites listeners to examine both their **inner store** and their **outward utterances**, recognizing that the two are inseparably linked.
