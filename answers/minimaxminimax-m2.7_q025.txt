Excellent and perceptive question. The verse strikes at the heart of moral psychology, and the distinction you draw is the key to understanding it.

The problem is **not visibility itself**, but the **craving to be seen**—that is, the intention and motivation behind the public act.

Here’s the breakdown:

### 1. The Danger is the Motive ("to be seen of them")
The critical phrase is "to be seen of them." Jesus is describing an act of charity (or righteousness) where the **primary audience is other people**, not God or the beneficiary. The doer performs the act with the explicit goal of **receiving human approval, praise, or admiration**.

*   **Visibility as the Goal:** The good deed is a *means* to an end. The end is a favorable reputation, being thought well of, or building social capital.
*   **God as the Audience is Replaced:** In this mindset, the Ultimate Witness is swapped for an earthly one. The person seeks a reward *here and now* from other people, not from God.

### 2. The Moral Danger: Corruption of the Good Act
This motivation is dangerous because it **nullifies the spiritual and moral worth** of the deed in two ways:

*   **It distorts the moral calculus:** A truly good action is motivated by love, duty, or compassion. When the motive is vanity, the act becomes **self-serving**. It is no longer genuinely "good" in a moral sense; it is a transaction—giving a little material or social currency to buy a larger return of praise.
*   **It forfeits the higher reward:** Jesus says you then have "no reward **of your Father which is in heaven.**" The danger is not just missing out on heavenly points; it’s that the act itself has been *rendered empty* of eternal significance. You've already collected your payment in full—human approval—so there is nothing left owed by the ultimate Judge.

### 3. The Distinction: Visibility vs. Vanity
This is where your question is crucial. **The problem is vanity, not visibility.**

*   **Incidental Visibility:** A good deed done faithfully, perhaps even privately, might naturally become known to others. This is not condemned. The motive was pure; the exposure is secondary.
*   **Vanity-Driven Visibility:** The deed is *designed* to be performed in public. The stage and the audience are part of the plan. This is the condemned hypocrisy.

**Analogy:** A person giving to charity might appear on the news because their contribution is large and newsworthy (incidental visibility). Another person might donate a modest amount but insist on a giant ceremonial check presentation and press coverage (vanity-driven visibility). Jesus warns against the latter's heart condition.

### 4. The Underlying Principle: Seeking Ultimate Validation
The verse gets to a profound spiritual truth: **A person's ultimate loyalty and source of identity is revealed by where they seek their "reward."**

*   If your heart is set on being seen and praised by people, that is the god you serve.
*   If your heart is set on being known and approved by God, then public recognition becomes irrelevant or even a distraction. You act in secret, because that is where true faith lives.

### Conclusion
Matthew 6:1 is a warning against **theatrical righteousness**. The moral danger is **not that others might see your good works**, but that **you might do them for the sole purpose of being seen**. It's a call to audit your heart: *Who is the real audience for my generosity?* When human applause becomes the goal, the goodness of the act itself is compromised, and the higher, eternal reward is forfeited.
