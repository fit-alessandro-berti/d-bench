**Short answer:**  
External compliance is behavior managed from the outside (rules, rewards, fear, habit); a “new heart” is a transformed *capacity* to want what God wants, so obedience flows from the inside rather than being imposed from the outside.  
Hardness is described as a material condition because it is not just a bad choice but a settled, structural state of the inner person—cold, unyielding, impermeable to influence—which therefore requires replacement, not just adjustment.

---

### 1. Compliance vs. a new heart

| Aspect | External compliance | New heart |
|--------|---------------------|-----------|
| **Source of motion** | Outside (command, threat, social pressure, habit) | Inside (a renewed inclination) |
| **What changes** | Behavior (what is done) | Desire (what is loved) |
| **Energy required** | Sustained enforcement or willpower | More natural, spontaneous alignment |
| **Outcome** | Can coexist with resentment, secrecy, pride | Tends toward willing, joyful practice |
| **Vulnerability** | Lapses when enforcement slackens | Still imperfect but resilient because it reshapes motive |

In Ezekiel’s context, Israel had “externally” kept forms of covenant life (circumcision, sacrifices, festivals) while their inner disposition was “stony”—calculating, defensive, self-protective. Compliance can mimic obedience, but a new heart is the gift of *wanting* to keep covenant because the person has become, in basic inclination, the kind of person who does.

---

### 2. Why hardness is a “material” problem

The metaphor is physical—stone, flesh, spirit—because it captures how resistance works:

- **Stone is structurally rigid.** It does not bend when pressed; it either resists or breaks. In a moral sense, a stony heart does not “give” under love, warning, or need. It is set; it has achieved a fixed form.

- **Stone is impermeable.** Water does not soak in; seed does not root. Similarly, a stony heart does not let words, remorse, or grace penetrate to where they can grow.

- **Stone is cold.** It does not generate warmth or respond to warmth. A stony heart is affectively inert toward God and neighbor.

- **Stone is not easily reshaped.** You can polish it, carve it, or cover it, but to change it into living tissue you practically have to replace it.

For Ezekiel, the exile proved that external covenant badges (temple, land, king) could exist alongside a heart that had calcified into idolatry, injustice, and self-reliance. Repair would not come by better instruction or stricter enforcement—something in the *substance* of the person had to be changed.

---

### 3. The contrast in the text

- **Stony heart → heart of flesh:**  
  The contrast is not between “bad heart” and “good heart” as two moral grades, but between two *kinds* of reality: unyielding mineral vs. living tissue. Flesh here signifies vulnerability, responsiveness, warmth, capacity for growth and feeling.

- **Spirit put within:**  
  The solution is not merely moral advice but a re-priming of the engine. The Spirit acts on the level of impulse, desire, and perception so that the statutes are no longer a foreign yoke but fit the new inner constitution.

- **Causality:**  
  God does not just demand obedience; he supplies the *inclination* that makes obedience possible. Compliance says, “Do this.” A new heart means, in effect, “You now *are* the sort of person for whom this is the natural path.”

---

### 4. Theological and practical implication

Hardness is not chiefly an information problem (“you didn’t know better”) but a formation problem (“you have become the kind of person who cannot receive what is good”). Therefore:

- **External measures** (rules, sanctions, programs) can restrain harm and maintain order, but they cannot by themselves soften a stony heart.
- **A new heart** changes the substrate of desire, so that over time the difference between “have to” and “want to” shrinks.
- **The gospel shape** of this promise is that the change is *given*—not earned, not manufactured by self-help. The hardness is removed and replaced; the Spirit is the active, inner cause of walking in the statutes.

In everyday terms: compliance is the difference between a well-behaved prisoner and a free citizen who loves justice. The new heart is the gift of citizenship—new desires, new reflexes, a new inner constitution—so that obedience is no longer a surface performance but the organic life of the person.
