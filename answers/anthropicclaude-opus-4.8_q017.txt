## The Logic of Hatred Toward Light

The passage gives a strange diagnosis. Condemnation isn't presented as a verdict imposed from outside—it's the *preference itself*. "Men loved darkness rather than light." The judgment is the choosing. This is worth sitting with: the condemnation isn't punishment for the choice, it *is* the choice.

So why hate the light?

### Light Doesn't Accuse—It Reveals

Notice the text doesn't say light condemns. It says light *exposes*. The hatred is directed at being seen clearly, not at being punished. This reframes the whole dynamic. What's feared isn't a sentence; it's visibility.

There's a difference between hating a judge and hating a mirror. The judge can be argued with, bargained with, fled from. The mirror simply shows. The peculiar offense of light is that it makes no claim—it just renders things apparent. You can't negotiate with clarity.

### Why Exposure Wounds

Shame is the felt sense of being seen in a state one wishes were hidden. It differs from guilt: guilt says *I did wrong*; shame says *I am exposed, and what's exposed is unbearable to be seen*.

The verse ties this to "deeds." But notice the progression—the one doing evil doesn't merely avoid consequences; he "hates the light" and "neither cometh to the light." There's an active aversion, almost a turning of the whole self away. This suggests something deeper than fear of getting caught:

- To come into light is to lose control of the narrative about oneself.
- In darkness, I can be who I claim to be. In light, I am simply who I am.
- The gap between self-presentation and reality is where shame lives, and light collapses that gap without consent.

### The Counterintuitive Reversal

The third verse turns it: "he that doeth truth cometh to the light, that his deeds may be made manifest."

The honest person *wants* to be seen. This is the strange inversion. The same exposure that terrifies one person attracts the other. Why? Because for the one "doing truth," visibility confirms rather than threatens. There's nothing to manage, no gap to defend.

This implies the issue was never the light. The light is constant. What varies is whether one has something to hide. The deeds determine the relationship to exposure—not the other way around.

### A Subtle Point Worth Noticing

The text says his deeds are "wrought in God"—done *in* God, almost done *through* God. The one who comes to the light isn't claiming his deeds are impressive; he's letting it be shown that they weren't even fully his own doing. There's a relinquishing of ownership that makes exposure tolerable. Pride needs darkness because pride must author itself. Humility can bear light because it has already surrendered the authorship.

### The Human Texture

Strip the theology for a moment and the psychology holds:

- We avoid the friend who knows us too well after we've done something we're ashamed of.
- We curate, edit, perform—and resent whatever threatens to puncture the performance.
- The thing we most fear being known is usually the thing whose concealment most distorts us.

The passage suggests the deepest tragedy isn't that we do wrong, but that we then *prefer the dark*—that we'd rather remain hidden and unhealed than be seen and freed. The avoidance becomes its own prison, self-locking.

The hatred of light, then, is finally a hatred of being known. And the cruel irony is that being fully known is the only condition under which the hidden thing could ever be addressed at all.
