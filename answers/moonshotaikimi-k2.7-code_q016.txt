The elder brother’s deepest problem is **pride in his own obedience, which hardens into a resentment of mercy itself.**

On the surface he is angry and feels the celebration is unfair: “Lo, these many years do I serve thee… yet thou never gavest me a kid… but… thou hast killed for him the fatted calf” (vv. 29–30). But that anger is only the symptom. Underneath it is a self-righteous, contractual view of his relationship with his father. He speaks like a hired servant tallying wages, not like a son. He boasts, “I never transgressed… thy commandment,” and treats his brother as morally worthless. He has stayed in the father’s house, yet he does not share the father’s heart.

That is why he cannot enter the feast: **he does not want grace to win.** The father’s joy is that the lost one “was dead, and is alive again; and was lost, and is found” (v. 32). The elder brother refuses that joy because it is extended to someone who does not deserve it.

So the true problem is not simply anger, envy, or wounded fairness—though all are present. It is **self-righteous pride that cannot bear the father’s free mercy, and therefore cannot love a restored sinner.** In the context of Luke 15, this brother stands for the Pharisees and scribes who objected to Jesus receiving sinners: obedient in externals, yet estranged from the heart of God.
