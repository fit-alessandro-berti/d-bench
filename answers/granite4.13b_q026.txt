The passage from Matthew 12:34–35 draws a clear connection between the content of one's speech and the state of their inner moral character. It suggests that what comes out of our mouths is not merely a reflection but an expression of what resides in our hearts—our "treasure." This idea can be understood through several lenses:

1. **Symptoms:** Speech in this context can be seen as symptomatic of one's underlying moral and emotional state. Just as physical symptoms (like fever) may indicate the presence of illness, words spoken reflect the condition of one’s inner life—whether it is rooted in goodness or evil.

2. **Choices:** The passage implies that there is agency involved in what we say. If someone claims to speak "good things" when they are inherently predisposed to evil (as indicated by their moral character), this suggests a deliberate choice or at least an intentional misrepresentation of one’s true nature. It highlights the importance of personal accountability and integrity in communication.

3. **Revelations:** Perhaps most profoundly, the words we speak reveal—who may not always be aware—what lies within our hearts. The statement "out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speakseth" suggests that speech is a window into one's innermost desires, fears, and values. It underscores the idea that honesty in communication involves being true to oneself, as what we say often reflects who we truly are or are willing to be.

In summary, Matthew 12:34–35 does not simply describe an effect (symptoms) but also emphasizes choices (we can choose how we express ourselves) and reveals truths about the hidden aspects of our character that others may discover through our words. It calls for a reflective consciousness in how we communicate, encouraging us to align our speech with our internal values rather than merely mirroring external pretenses or societal expectations.
