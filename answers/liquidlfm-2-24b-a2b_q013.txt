This passage from **Amos 5:21–24** is one of the most forceful condemnations of hypocritical worship in the Old Testament. To understand why it rejects worship *sharply* rather than asking for "better balance," we must look at the deeper theological and ethical critique being made.

---

### 1. **The Core Issue: Hypocrisy and Empty Ritual**

Amos is addressing a people who outwardly observe religious rituals—burnt offerings, sacrifices, feasts—but whose hearts are far from God. The passage exposes a profound **moral and spiritual fraud**: they perform the *form* of worship while their *actions* contradict its very purpose.

- **Worship without righteousness**: The people bring offerings but live unjustly (as Amos has been saying throughout the book). Their sacrifices are not offered to God but to satisfy social or economic needs, or to gain favor.
- **Ritual without reverence**: The "noise" of their songs and instruments is hollow because there is no genuine devotion behind it.

---

### 2. **Why Not Just Ask for Better Balance?**

A request for "better balance" would imply that the rituals themselves could be improved if only people did them more sincerely or with less excess. But Amos goes further: he says God will *not* accept these offerings at all. This isn't about tweaking the ritual; it's about the fundamental incompatibility between their worship and their lives.

- **The problem is not just insincerity—it’s injustice.** The people’s worship is tainted by their oppression of the poor, their greed, and their disregard for justice.
- **Their sacrifices are an insult** when lived alongside exploitation and idolatry.

---

### 3. **The Contrast: Judgment vs. Ritual**

Amos contrasts two things:
- **Human attempts at worship** (offerings, feasts)
- **Divine expectation** (justice, righteousness)

He says: *“But let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream.”* This is not a suggestion for improvement—it’s a demand for transformation. True worship cannot exist apart from justice and righteousness.

---

### 4. **Moral/Spiritual Fraud Exposed**

The passage exposes a kind of **spiritual fraud** where:
- **Religious performance masks moral corruption.**
- **People believe they can separate their worship from their ethics.**
- **They treat God as a commodity to be appeased by ritual, rather than a God who demands justice.**

In other words, they are practicing **religious formalism**—going through the motions without real commitment or change.

---

### **Summary Table**

| Aspect                  | What’s Critiqued                                                                 |
|-------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Worship                 | Empty, disconnected from justice                                                 |
| Offerings               | Insincere, self-serving                                                          |
| Ritual vs. Righteousness| Worship without righteousness is unacceptable                                     |
| Demand                  | Not just better balance, but radical transformation of life                       |

---

**In essence:**  
Amos rejects worship sharply because the people’s rituals are not just inadequate—they are offensive to God precisely because they coexist with injustice. The passage exposes the danger of separating religious practice from ethical living—a kind of spiritual fraud where outward piety covers inner corruption. True worship requires justice, not just sacrifice.
