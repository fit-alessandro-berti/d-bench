**Step‑by‑step explanation**

1. **The structure of the passage**  
   - *Positive claim*: “I love God.”  
   - *Contrasting behavior*: “…and hateth his brother.”  
   - *Conclusion*: The person who makes that claim is a **liar**; therefore one cannot truly love an unseen God while denying visible love to one’s brother.

2. **The logical link – from the seen to the unseen**  

   | Aspect | Visible (Brother) | Invisible (God) |
   |--------|-------------------|-----------------|
   | **Evidence of love** | Concrete actions, speech, presence, needs that can be observed and responded to. | Intangible reality; we cannot “see” God’s face or directly witness His attributes except through revelation and the moral law. |
   | **Demand of Scripture** | Jesus (Matt 22:36‑40) says that *loving your neighbor* is a direct expression of loving God because the two commandments are inseparable. 1 John follows this pattern, treating brotherly love as the “proof” of divine love. |
   | **Theological reason** | In Christian anthropology humans bear the image of God (imago Dei). By loving another human we display reverence for that shared image; thus hatred toward a neighbor is an implicit affront to the Divine Image itself. |

   Because we can *see* and *test* love for our brother through observable actions, Scripture uses this as a **practical barometer** for invisible love of God. If the gauge (brotherly love) reads “zero,” the deeper meter (love of God) is assumed to be false.

3. **Why hatred or lack of love is singled out**  

   - **Hatred is an extreme, unmistakable opposite**: John does not merely say “fails to show kindness”; he uses *hateth*, a clear moral antithesis. This crystallizes the contrast—any deficiency in brotherly affection already signals a problem, but outright hatred demonstrates total failure.
   - **Moral coherence**: The New Testament repeatedly links love of God with love of neighbor (cf. 1 John 3:14‑18; Matt 25). Claiming divine devotion while acting contrary to that ethic reveals an internal contradiction.

4. **The kind of self‑deception the verse exposes**  

   Self‑deception here is a *cognitive dissonance* where a professant Christian:

   - **Confuses verbal confession with genuine affection**: The person repeats “I love God” (a credal formula) but never translates that profession into concrete care for fellow believers.
   - **Projects an idealized, abstract notion of divine love** onto the self while ignoring its embodied expression in human relationships. In psychological terms this is a *dissociation*—splitting the “religious mind” from everyday moral behavior.
   - **Rationalizes or minimizes sin against neighbor**: The hatred may be justified (e.g., as righteous judgment, personal grievance) but John declares it “liar‑hood.” This shows that any rationalization of hostility toward a fellow image‑bearer is morally untenable if one truly loves the unseen God.
   - **Creates an illusory moral superiority**: By claiming devotion to an invisible deity while neglecting the visible command to love, the person erects a façade of piety that masks spiritual emptiness.

5. **Biblical and theological context reinforcing the point**  

   - **Old‑Testament foundation** – Leviticus 19:18 (“Love your neighbor as yourself”) is presented alongside reverence for God; Deuteronomy 6:5 commands to love “the Lord your God with all your heart…”, implying that wholeheartedness cannot be partial.
   - **Jesus’ teaching** – In the parable of the Good Samaritan (Luke 10) and the Great Commandment (Matt 22), mercy toward a neighbor is synonymous with obedience to God. John’s epistle echoes this: “If anyone says, ‘I love God,’ yet hates his brother, he is a liar” mirrors Jesus’ insistence that *true* worship is inseparable from *practical* compassion.
   - **John’s broader theological goal** – 1 John repeatedly combats *gnostic‑type dualism* (the idea that spiritual matters are separate from earthly ones). By insisting that love of the visible brother proves love of the invisible God, John rejects any notion that “spiritual” devotion can be isolated from ethical conduct.
   - **Practical ecclesial implication** – Early church fathers (e.g., Augustine, Chrysostom) used this verse to warn against factionalism and hatred within the community, emphasizing that communal harmony is evidence of genuine faith.

6. **Summary answer**

> The verse treats love for a visible neighbor as the *observable proof* of an otherwise invisible love for God because human beings are created in God’s image; caring for one another concretely demonstrates reverence for that divine imprint. By juxtaposing “I love God” with hatred toward brother, John exposes **self‑deception**—the false belief that merely professing devotion to an unseen deity suffices when the ethical fruit of such love (brotherly kindness) is absent. The deception consists in equating verbal confession with genuine affection while rationalizing or ignoring concrete hostility toward fellow image‑bearers, thereby creating a split between professed spirituality and lived morality.

---

**Key Takeaway:**  
In 1 John 4:20 the “test” of love for God is not mystical speculation but everyday kindness to one’s neighbor; anyone who claims divine devotion while harboring hatred toward that neighbor deceives themselves, for true love of God inevitably overflows into visible acts of love toward fellow humans.
