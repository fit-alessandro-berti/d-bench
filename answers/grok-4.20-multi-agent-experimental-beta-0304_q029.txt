**The moral posture required is humble, gentle, Spirit-led maturity that is self-aware and redemptive rather than self-righteous or superior.**

In Galatians 6:1, Paul addresses the believing community (“Brethren”) about how to respond when someone is “overtaken in a fault” (caught or surprised by sin, not necessarily a hardened pattern). He specifies that it is “ye which are spiritual” (those mature and walking by the Spirit, in light of Galatians 5:16–25) who should act. Their task is to “restore” (Greek *katartizō*—to mend, repair, set in order, like resetting a broken bone or mending a net) the person “in the spirit of meekness” (*prautēs*—gentleness, humble strength under control, a fruit of the Spirit in Galatians 5:23), while “considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted.”

This is not a posture of:
- Harsh condemnation or public shaming.
- Moral superiority or power-playing.
- Indifferent tolerance that refuses to address sin.
- Self-confident judgment that assumes “I would never fall like that.”

Instead, it is empathetic, careful, hopeful pastoral care rooted in the gospel. The corrector must be spiritually mature enough to help, yet humble enough to recognize that they are not immune. Correction flows from shared frailty and dependence on grace, not from having “arrived.”

### Why restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion are kept together
These three elements are inseparable because each protects the others from distortion, and together they reflect how the gospel shapes relationships in the church:

- **Restoration as the goal** keeps correction redemptive rather than punitive. The aim is healing, reconciliation, and reintegration into the community—not condemnation, revenge, or proving one’s own righteousness. Without this aim, “confrontation” easily becomes destructive or self-serving.

- **Meekness (gentleness)** ensures the method matches the gospel. Harshness or superiority crushes the fallen person and contradicts the fruit of the Spirit. Meekness is not weakness; it is strength (the ability to correct) exercised with humility and kindness. It communicates, “I care about your good and your restoration,” rather than “I am better than you.”

- **Self-suspicion (“considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted”)** supplies the necessary humility and realism. It reminds the “spiritual” person of their own remaining sin, vulnerability, and dependence on grace. Pride is the ever-present danger when we correct others (see Galatians 6:3–4). Without sober self-examination, even gentle-sounding correction can mask superiority, and the corrector may fall into the very sin they are addressing—or worse, into the subtler sin of self-righteousness. It fosters the honest recognition: “There, but for the grace of God, go I.” This echoes Jesus’ teaching in Matthew 7:3–5 about dealing with the log in your own eye first.

When these three are held together, correction becomes an act of love and mutual burden-bearing (Galatians 6:2), not a display of spiritual elitism. It counters the “works of the flesh” (strife, envy, outbursts of anger—Galatians 5:19–21) and displays the fruit of the Spirit. It protects both the fallen person (from despair or further harm) and the restorer (from pride and temptation). Ultimately, it mirrors Christ, who restores us gently while fully knowing our weakness, and who calls us to extend the same grace we have received.

In short, the required moral posture is **gospel humility in action**: mature enough to restore, gentle enough not to destroy, and self-aware enough to do it without hypocrisy. This is how the church lives out the law of Christ.
