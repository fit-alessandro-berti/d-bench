Based on the text of Matthew 6:1 and its surrounding context, the verse suggests that **the problem is not visibility itself, but the craving to be seen (the underlying motive).**

Here is a breakdown of why public goodness becomes morally dangerous according to this verse:

### 1. The Diagnosis: "To be seen of them"
The Greek text is precise about the location of the sin. Jesus says, *"Take heed that ye do not your alms before men, **to be seen of them**..."*

The danger lies in the **purpose clause** (*hina* - "in order that"). The act is not condemned because people are watching; it is condemned because the doer **wants** people to watch.
*   **The Danger:** When the craving to be seen dominates, the act of charity ceases to be an act of love for the neighbor and becomes an act of self-promotion. The neighbor becomes a mere prop in a stage play where the "giver" is the star.
*   **The Result:** This craving corrupts the heart. It transforms a spiritual discipline (almsgiving) into vanity.

### 2. The Consequence: "Otherwise ye have no reward"
Jesus outlines a strict economy of rewards. He implies that every act has a payout, but you only get one "treasure" per act. You must choose your currency:
*   **Earthly Reward:** If you do good to be seen by men, you get exactly what you wanted—their admiration, their applause, their validation. That is your "reward." It is fully paid out in the moment.
*   **Heavenly Reward:** If you do good to please the Father, you forfeit the applause of men, but you store up treasure with God.

The moral danger is that "public goodness" driven by a craving for attention **bankrupts the soul's eternal standing** in exchange for a fleeting moment of human praise.

### 3. The Exception: Visibility is not the problem
The rest of the New Testament suggests that visibility is often necessary and even commanded (e.g., "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works..." - Matthew 5:16).

The distinction lies in the **source of the visibility**:
*   **Dangerous (Matt 6:1):** *You* put yourself on display. You orchestrate the visibility to feed your ego.
*   **Acceptable/Blessed:** The light of the good deed shines so brightly that people see it *incidentally* and are drawn to glorify God, not you.

### Summary
The moral danger in Matthew 6:1 is **“performative righteousness.”**

The craving to be seen shifts the center of the universe from God (who is invisible) to Man (who is visible). Once the heart is addicted to the validation of the crowd, it loses the capacity to hear the quiet affirmation of the Father. As the Reformer John Calvin commented on this verse, the act itself is not evil, but the "vainglory" (empty pride) that motivates it poisons the whole offering.
