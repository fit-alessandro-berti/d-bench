In Matthew 12:34–35, Jesus establishes an unbreakable chain of cause and effect between the inner life of a person and their outward speech. He uses the metaphors of a "treasure" and an "abundance" to describe the heart not as a passive waiting room, but as a active storage vault that has been deliberately filled over time. 

To answer your question directly: **Jesus suggests that words are all three—symptoms, choices, and revelations—operating simultaneously.** However, understanding *how* they function as each requires looking closely at the mechanics of the "stockpile."

Here is how the passage connects speech to these hidden moral stockpiles:

### 1. Words as Symptoms (The Diagnostic View)
A symptom is an outward physical sign of an inward, often hidden, condition. In this passage, Jesus is acting as a divine diagnostician. He has just listened to the Pharisees accuse Him of casting out demons by the power of Satan (v. 24). 

Jesus uses their speech as a symptom to diagnose their terminal spiritual condition: *"How can ye, being evil, speak good things?"* He is saying that their words are the clinical manifestations of a diseased heart. Just as a cough reveals a respiratory infection, malicious, deceptive, or blasphemous words reveal an inner moral corruption. The symptom cannot be cured by merely suppressing the cough; the underlying infection of the heart must be addressed.

### 2. Words as Revelations (The Unveiling View)
The word "revelation" means to pull back the veil on something hidden. Jesus explicitly states that the heart is a hidden "treasure." You cannot see a person's heart; it is locked away. But the mouth is the combination to the lock. 

When Jesus says, *"out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh,"* He is explaining that words are the ultimate revealer of true character. People can curate their appearance, their actions in public, and their reputations, but when they speak—especially under pressure, when they are caught off guard, or when they are passionate—the "abundance" (the surplus, the overflow) spills out. The words pull back the curtain and reveal exactly what kind of "treasure" (good or evil) has been hoarded in the dark.

### 3. Words as Choices (The Volitional View)
This is where the concept of the "stockpile" becomes vital. Does this passage mean we are just helpless automatons spewing whatever happens to be inside us? Not quite, because **the stockpile itself was built by choices.** 

The Greek word for "treasure" (*thēsauros*) implies a conscious gathering and storing up of valuables. A person decides what to watch, what to meditate on, what grudges to nurse, what truths to embrace, and what lies to consume. Over years, these micro-choices accumulate into a moral stockpile. 

When a person speaks, they are indeed making a *choice* in the moment to "bring forth" (*ekballō* — to cast out, draw out) words. However, the passage teaches that our momentary choices to speak are strictly limited by the inventory we have stockpiled. An evil man can temporarily choose to speak "good things" (hypocrisy), but under pressure, the overflow of his true stockpile will betray him. We choose our words, but we only have access to the words we have stockpiled.

### The Synthesis: The Spillover Principle
Jesus is teaching what we might call the "Spillover Principle." If you bump a cup of water, water spills out. If you bump a cup of vinegar, vinegar spills out. The bump (a provocation, a temptation, a conversation) is what causes the spillover, but the *contents* of the cup dictate what spills out. 

Therefore, in Matthew 12:
*   **Symptom:** The spilling vinegar is a symptom that the cup does not contain water.
*   **Revelation:** The spill reveals the true, hidden contents of the cup to everyone watching.
*   **Choice:** Someone, at some point, chose to fill the cup with vinegar instead of water.

Ultimately, this passage is a devastating critique of mere externalism (trying to change behavior without changing the heart). Jesus is telling His listeners that behavioral modification—just trying to say nicer things—is futile. If you want to change the fruit (the words), you must radically change the root (the stockpile of the heart).
