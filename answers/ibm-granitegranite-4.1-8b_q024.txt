### Understanding the Passage

**Ezekiel 36:26–27** describes a profound transformation promised by God to His people. The key elements are:

1. **A New Heart**: This implies an internal, emotional, and volitional change. The old heart, described as "stony," represents a state of spiritual indifference, hardness, and inability to love or obey God genuinely. Receiving a new heart means acquiring a renewed capacity for love, compassion, understanding, and moral discernment—the very essence of who God desires His people to be.

2. **A New Spirit**: Accompanying the new heart is the infusion of God’s Spirit. This “new spirit” is dynamic and active, enabling the believer to not just intellectually understand God's laws but to live them out with genuine joy and purpose.

3. **Walking in God’s Statutes**: The promise includes not merely knowledge (keeping judgments) but active obedience (doing them). This obedience flows from the inner transformation rather than mere external conformity.

### External Compliance vs. Receiving a “New Heart”

- **External Compliance**:
  - **Definition**: Obedience or adherence to God's commands based on external motivation—fear of punishment, desire for reward, societal pressure, or conscious effort to appear pious.
  - **Nature**: It is often superficial, lacking inner conviction. The person may outwardly follow rules but remains unchanged internally, still governed by the same stony-hearted desires.
  - **Motivation**: External compliance is driven by external factors rather than a transformed inner disposition.
  - **Result**: It may lead to temporary moral behavior but does not address the root problem of the human condition, leaving the individual spiritually impoverished and susceptible to relapse.

- **Receiving a “New Heart”**:
  - **Definition**: A transformation that occurs from within, altering one's innermost being—thoughts, feelings, desires, and will—to align with God's nature.
  - **Nature**: It is a profound, holistic change that makes the believer capable of internalizing and embodying God’s will from the heart, not just the mind.
  - **Motivation**: This change is motivated by the divine grace that replaces the old, stony heart with one receptive to God’s love and Spirit.
  - **Result**: The individual experiences genuine joy in obedience, sustained by an inner desire to please God, rather than merely complying to avoid consequences.

### Hardness Described as a Material Condition

- **Metaphorical Materiality**: The term “stony heart” is a metaphor for spiritual rigidity and insensitivity. It likens the human spirit to a hard, inert material that cannot respond to divine love or guidance.
  
- **Need for Replacement**: This metaphor underscores that the problem is not just a lack of knowledge or willpower but a fundamental flaw in the human condition—our innate inability to love and obey God apart from a divine intervention. The old heart, like stone, is inert, cold, and resistant; it cannot be merely “reformed” by external means.

- **Divine Intervention**: The necessity of replacing the stony heart with a “heart of flesh” points to a supernatural act—God’s grace must intervene to soften, animate, and nurture this new heart. Only through His Spirit can the inner being be transformed from cold, unresponsive stone to warm, responsive flesh capable of love, joy, and obedience.

### Conclusion

The distinction between external compliance and receiving a “new heart” lies in **depth** versus **breadth**. External compliance addresses the **observable behavior** without altering the **inner disposition**, whereas a new heart transforms the **core** of a person's being, enabling authentic, joyous obedience rooted in love and gratitude. The hardness of the old heart, described as material, emphasizes that this change is not merely intellectual or superficial but requires a **radical, divine renovation** that renews the very essence of humanity. This transformation aligns the believer’s inner will with God’s will, facilitating a life lived not out of obligation but out of love.
