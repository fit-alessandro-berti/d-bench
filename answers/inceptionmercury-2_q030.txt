**1. The “negatives” are the guard‑rails of a self‑emptying love**

The passage is not a list of things love *does* feel; it is a catalogue of the passions that would *undo* love if they were allowed to take root.  In the original Greek the verbs are all in the a,: μὴ ζηλοῦν, οὐ φῇναι, οὐκ ἀναφροδινόμενος, οὐ λυποῦσθαι, οὐ λογίζεσθαι, etc.  By spelling out what love *does not* do, Paul makes it clear that love is defined more by the absence of certain vices than by a set of positive emotions.

* **Pride (boasting, puffed‑upness)** – love is not a claim to superiority; it is a “self‑forgetting” openness.  The Greek *ἔνδοξος* (proud) is the opposite of the humility that allows one to see the other as a value in its own right, not as a mirror for one’s ego.  
* **Envy** – the Greek *ζηλόω* (to be jealous) is a desire that the other’s good be taken away from us.  Love that is *not* envious is already oriented toward the other’s flourishing, not toward a competitive ranking.  
* **Irritability / quick‑temperedness** – *λυσσάω* (to be easily provoked) would make love volatile and dependent on the other’s behavior.  A love that “does not get angry easily” is anchored in a deep‑steady patience that can absorb offense without breaking.  
* **Suspicion / thinking evil** – *λογίζομαι* (to think wickedly) is the mental habit of assuming the worst.  By refusing to “think evil,” love gives the other the benefit of the doubt and the space to be what they are, not what we fear they might be.

These negatives are not merely “good‑behaviour rules.”  They are the *conditions* that make love possible at all.  If any of the listed vices were present, the relational focus would shift from the other to the self, and the relational bond would become a transaction, a rivalry, or a power play rather than a genuine self‑giving.

---

**2. The vision of strength behind the negations**

The “strength” Paul is proposing is *inner* rather than *external*.  It is the power to:

| Negation | What it eliminates | What strength it reveals |
|----------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| Not boastful / not puffed‑up | Self‑aggrandizement | **Humility** – the capacity to be seen without the need for validation |
| Not envious | Possessive desire | **Generosity** – the ability to rejoice in another’s good |
| Not easily provoked | Reactive anger | **Patience / long‑suffering** – the ability to endure insult or hardship without losing composure |
| Not thinking evil | Cynical suspicion | **Trust** – the willingness to give the other the benefit of the doubt |
| Not seeking one’s own | Self‑centered ambition | **Self‑sacrifice** – the willingness to put the other’s needs before one’s own |

These virtues are *self‑mastery*; they require a firm inner will that can restrain the impulses that would otherwise dominate us.  In the language of virtue ethics (Aristotle, Augustine, Aquinas) this is the “golden mean” between excess and deficiency: love is not a reckless indulgence (excess of feeling) nor a cold indifference (deficiency), but a disciplined, purposeful orientation toward the other.

Because the negations are about *absence* of destructive attitudes, the positive statements that follow—“bears all things, believes all things, hopes all things, endures all things”—are the *fruit* of that disciplined interior.  The strength lies in the capacity to *hold* the other even when circumstances are hostile, to *trust* the other’s goodness when the world is hostile, to *hope* for the other’s future when the present is bleak, and to *endure* hardship without capitulating to bitterness.

---

**3. Why this matters in the larger Pauline context**

Paul writes this hymn to love in the middle of a discourse on spiritual gifts (1 Cor 12‑14).  He is warning the Corinthian church that the most spectacular gifts—tongues, prophecy, knowledge—are empty without the *character* that love supplies.  The “strength” of love is therefore the *foundation* for any other Christian power.  Without the self‑emptying, patient, trusting posture described in the negatives, a gift becomes a tool for personal prestige (pride), for competition (envy), for conflict (irritability), or for suspicion (evil‑thinking).  Love’s strength is the *mastery* that keeps all other gifts in the service of the community rather than the ego.

---

**4. Summing up the vision**

The portrait of love in 1 Cor 13:4‑7 proposes a strength that is:

1. **Self‑negating** – it deliberately turns away from the ego‑driven passions that would corrupt relational goodwill.  
2. **Inner‑controlled** – it is a disciplined habit of the heart, not a fleeting feeling.  
3. **Enduring and generous** – it bears, believes, hopes, and endures, not because it is immune to pain, but because it has already disarmed the very sources of that pain (pride, envy, irritability, suspicion).  

In other words, true strength is *the ability to love without being swayed by the very emotions that seek to make love a selfish, competitive, or fearful enterprise.*  It is a quiet, resilient power that transforms the self‑centered will into a self‑giving, other‑oriented life.
