All of the options you list are present in the elder brother, but they’re symptoms. The deepest problem is a heart of self‑righteousness — an identity and security tied to merit and fairness rather than to the father’s love — which makes him unable to rejoice in undeserved mercy.

Why that fits best
- Anger, envy, wounded‑fairness and pride are all visible in his words: “I serve thee… yet thou never gavest me a kid… thou hast killed for him the fatted calf.” Those are real, but they flow from a deeper stance: he measures relationship by what he has earned, not by the father’s gracious love.
- Because of that stance he resents grace. Mercy looks like injustice to him, not joy. He cannot welcome the returnee because the return threatens his merit‑based self‑worth and his sense of what is “fair.”
- The father’s reply (Son, thou art ever with me…) exposes the root: the elder brother is present outwardly but not at home in the relationship. He has missed the point of the father’s loving generosity — he’s been treating the father as an employer or banker rather than as a father.

Practical implication
- The cure is not primarily better behavior but a reorientation of identity: accepting that he, too, lives by the father’s undeserved love and learning to rejoice in mercy when it restores others.
