The tension in Luke 6:37 lies in the distinction between **moral discernment** (the ability to perceive right from wrong) and **judicial condemnation** (the assumption of a position of authority to pronounce a final verdict on a person’s soul or worth).

To suggest that this verse means moral judgments *never* matter would be to render the rest of the New Testament contradictory. Jesus frequently identified sin, called for repentance, and warned against false teachers. Therefore, the command is not against the *intellect* (discernment), but against the *spirit* (the appetite to condemn).

Here is an exploration of the differences.

### 1. Discernment vs. Judgment
**Discernment** is a tool for navigation and protection. It is the process of evaluating a behavior or a situation against a standard of truth. For example, if someone is lying or stealing, discernment allows us to identify that action as "wrong." This is necessary for safety, justice, and spiritual growth.

**Judicial Judgment** (in the context of Luke 6), however, occurs when we move from evaluating the *act* to sentencing the *person*. Judgment is the act of claiming a "judge's bench" that belongs solely to God. While discernment says, *"This action is harmful,"* the appetite to condemn says, *"You are a harmful person, and I have the authority to write you off."*

### 2. The "Appetite" to Condemn
The "appetite to condemn" is often driven by a desire for moral superiority. When we condemn, we are not seeking the restoration of the other person; we are seeking the validation of our own righteousness.

*   **The Goal of Discernment is Restoration:** When we discern sin in a brother or sister, the biblical goal is always redemption (e.g., Galatians 6:1, "restore him in a spirit of gentleness").
*   **The Goal of Condemnation is Separation:** Condemnation seeks to push the other person away, to exile them, or to feel a sense of triumph over their failure.

### 3. The Mirror of the Plank and the Speck
Immediately following the verse in question, Jesus speaks of the man with the "beam in his own eye" trying to remove the "mote" from his brother's eye (Luke 6:41-42). 

Jesus does not say, "Ignore the mote in your brother's eye." He says, **"First take the beam out of your own eye, and then you will see clearly to take the mote out of your brother's."**

The point is not that moral judgments don't matter—it is that **the judge must be purified before the judgment is valid.** The "appetite to condemn" usually blinds us to our own failings. True discernment only happens *after* we have humbled ourselves and recognized our own need for forgiveness.

### 4. Summary of the Difference

| Discernment | Condemnation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Focus:** The action and its effects. | **Focus:** The identity and worth of the person. |
| **Motive:** Love, truth, and restoration. | **Motive:** Pride, superiority, or anger. |
| **Posture:** Humility ("I am a sinner too"). | **Posture:** Arrogance ("I am better than this"). |
| **Outcome:** Helping the other grow. | **Outcome:** Casting the other away. |

In short, Luke 6:37 is not a call to moral blindness, but a call to **moral humility.** It asks us to relinquish the role of the Judge and instead adopt the role of the Fellow-Sinner, ensuring that our only "verdict" on others is one of grace, because that is the only verdict we hope to receive ourselves.
