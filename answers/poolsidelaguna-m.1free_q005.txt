
This question strikes at the heart of one of Christianity's most demanding and countercultural teachings. Enemy-love sits uncomfortably between our deepest intuitions about justice and our highest ideals about compassion, making it simultaneously inspiring and almost impossible to live out.

## Why It Feels Impossible to Take Seriously

**The Psychology of Reciprocity**: We're wired for proportionality—we expect good for good, harm for harm. When someone curses us or persecutes us, the instinctive response is protection, retaliation, or at least withdrawal. Jesus asks us to short-circuit this fundamental survival mechanism, which feels not just difficult but potentially dangerous.

**The Problem of Authenticity**: There's something deeply unsettling about blessing someone who's actively causing harm. It can feel performative or inauthentic, like smiling through clenched teeth. We wonder: Does genuine love require genuine feeling? And if we can't manufacture that feeling, are we just pretending?

**The Justice Dilemma**: Loving enemies seems to conflict with protecting the vulnerable and standing against injustice. If we're called to love those who exploit others, do we become complicit in their harm? This isn't just theoretical—it's a daily reality in homes, workplaces, and societies.

## Three Ways to Understand the Teaching

**Sentimental** - This view sees enemy-love as beautiful but impractical poetry, like suggesting we should all hold hands and sing "Kumbaya." It's well-meaning naivety that ignores real power dynamics and human nature. From this angle, it's dangerous because it might keep people in abusive situations or enable further injustice.

**Strategic** - Here, enemy-love becomes a calculated approach to conflict transformation. Don't retaliate because it only escalates cycles of violence; instead, respond in ways that might disarm or transform the situation. This reads Jesus's words as practical wisdom rather than spiritual idealism—nonviolence as the most effective path to lasting peace.

**Moral Strength** - This sees enemy-love as the ultimate expression of human freedom—the ability to act against our basest instincts and highest emotional impulses. It's not weakness disguised as virtue, but the hardest thing a person can do: refusing to let another's evil determine your own character.

## A Deeper Possibility

Perhaps the difficulty reveals something crucial: enemy-love isn't primarily about the enemy at all. It's about what we become in the process of trying to live it. 

When Jesus connects this teaching to God's impartial provision of sun and rain, he's suggesting that enemy-love reflects something fundamental about reality—that existence itself operates on a principle of generosity that doesn't depend on merit. Living this way requires us to embody a different kind of power than the world's power of domination.

The early Christians understood this as *imitatio Dei*—not just copying God's actions, but participating in God's own nature. To love enemies is to share in the divine power that gives life even to those who oppose it. That's not sentimental; it's cosmic.

What makes enemy-love so difficult to take seriously might actually be what makes it so essential. It refuses to let us settle for the manageable, safe versions of love that ask nothing too costly. Whether that cost is sentimental, strategic, or genuinely transformative probably depends on whether we're strong enough to pay it.
