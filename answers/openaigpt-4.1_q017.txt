This is a rich and perennial question—the passage from **John 3:19–21** powerfully connects **light** (as a symbol for truth, goodness, or divine presence) with **exposure** and the human experience of **shame, denial, and resistance**.

Let’s break down why “light” might be hated, and how this relates to truth, exposure, and the desire not to be seen clearly:

---

### 1. **Light as Truth and Exposure**
- **Light** in the Johannine context symbolizes **truth, revelation, and God's presence**; it dispels darkness (ignorance, evil, falsehood).
- To “come to the light” means to allow yourself (your thoughts, motives, actions) to be examined honestly and openly.
- **Exposure** is inherent to light—it reveals what is there, unvarnished and unhidden.

### 2. **The Threat of Exposure**
- For those “whose deeds are evil”, exposure is uncomfortable—they risk their wrongdoings, flaws, or hidden motivations being **seen by others and, crucially, by themselves**.
- The text mentions that those who do evil “hate the light”—not just avoid it, but are actively *hostile* to being revealed.
- No one enjoys being called out, judged, or having their shortcomings brought into the open.

### 3. **Shame and Self-Protection**
- **Shame** arises when we are seen in a way that exposes our failings, especially in the context of community or before a higher moral standard.
- Darkness offers **cover**—a refuge where we don’t have to confront uncomfortable truths about ourselves or be exposed to the judgment of others (or God).
- The text suggests that people avoid the light not because the light is bad, but because it **threatens their fragile self-image** and **provokes shame**.

### 4. **Desire for Hiddenness vs. Desire for Authenticity**
- Many people compartmentalize: they want to *appear* good while *remaining unexamined*. The light, however, demands integration and honesty.
- The one who “does the truth” is willing to have their life examined, even at the cost of discomfort, because they value authenticity and transformation.

### 5. **Moral and Spiritual Dynamics**
- It’s not just about being seen by others, but about *self-honesty*. Light compels one to admit what is true, to bring one’s life into alignment with what is good.
- In the biblical sense, “coming to the light” is an act of trust—that God’s truth is not only exposing but *healing*. However, this requires a surrender of pride and self-deception.

---

## **In Summary**
**People hate the light not because light (or truth) is intrinsically hateful, but because it brings exposure:**
- It illuminates actions, motives, and character.
- It provokes shame, discomfort, and fear of judgment.
- It disrupts illusions and demands honesty.

**Loving darkness is ultimately a way to avoid confrontation with our true selves, and with God’s offer of transformation.** Conversely, “doing the truth” means a willingness to step into the light, accepting both the pain of exposure *and* the possibility of genuine change.

---

#### **A final thought:**
The passage invites us to consider: **Do I avoid truth because I’m afraid to be seen—by others, by myself, by God? What would stepping into the light look like for me?**
