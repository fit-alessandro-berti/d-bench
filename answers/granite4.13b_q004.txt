The trust being asked for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is a **humbling faith** that places complete reliance on God, acknowledging one's own limitations and inadequacies. This form of trust goes beyond simply having confidence in God’s existence or goodness; it involves a deep humility that recognizes personal shortcomings and the futility of relying solely on human understanding.

### Distinguishing Humble Self-Distrust from Mere Anti-Intellectualism:

1. **Humble Self‑Distrust:**
   - **Recognizes Human Limitations:** It acknowledges that human intellect, knowledge, and wisdom are finite and imperfect.
   - **Relies on Divine Guidance:** The individual trusts in God’s omniscience and sovereignty, believing that divine guidance is superior to human understanding.
   - **Embraces Obedience Over Certainty:** This trust is grounded in obedience to God’s will (as expressed through Scripture), rather than a desire for certainty or control over outcomes.
   - **Reduces Anxiety Through Dependency on Faith:** It frees the person from anxieties about future uncertainties because they believe that whatever happens, it aligns with the divine plan. There is an underlying confidence that “the Lord works all things together for good” (Romans 8:28).
   - **Encourages Growth and Learning:** This form of trust encourages continual learning and growth as one seeks wisdom from God’s perspective rather than solely human perspectives.

2. **Mere Anti‑Intellectualism:**
   - **Dismisses Human Knowledge:** It involves an outright rejection or disdain for any intellectual pursuits outside a narrow, often religious framework.
   - **Relies on Dogma Over Reasoning:** This can manifest as believing that all knowledge must align with preconceived religious doctrines without critical inquiry or reasoning beyond those doctrines.
   - **Creates Intellectual Suppression:** It tends to suppress critical thinking and open dialogue about complex issues by labeling any questioning of scriptural interpretation as heretical.
   - **Lacks Humility:** Rather than admitting human limitations, it may lead to a prideful confidence that “belief alone is sufficient” without regard for intellectual or empirical evidence.
   - **Isolates from Dialogue:** It can create an echo chamber where only like‑minded opinions are valued, stifling diversity of thought and the possibility of reevaluation based on new information.

### Key Distinction:

- The trust in Proverbs 3:5–6 is intentional humility—understanding that one’s wisdom and understanding fall short—and it is not motivated by fear or ignorance but by a profound reliance on divine guidance.
- Anti‑intellectualism, on the other hand, stems from an overconfidence in existing beliefs or dogma (often religious) without regard for deeper inquiry or the acknowledgment of unknowns.

In summary, the trust being called for here is a sincere, humble faith that recognizes human limitations while placing full reliance on divine guidance and wisdom. This contrasts sharply with an anti‑intellectual mindset that dismisses intellectual exploration and critical thought in favor of blind adherence to orthodoxy.
