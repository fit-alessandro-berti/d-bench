This passage—James 3:9–10—highlights a striking **inner contradiction** in human behavior:

> “Therewith bless we God, even the Father; and therewith curse we men, which are made after the similitude of God.  
> Out of the same mouth proceedeth blessing and cursing. My brethren, these things ought not so to be.”

**Inner Contradiction Exposed:**
James points out the inconsistency between **praising God** and **cursing (speaking harmfully of) fellow humans**. The contradiction lies in the fact that human beings, though capable of worshiping and blessing God with their words, also use those same words to harm others—despite those others being made in God’s image (“the similitude of God”). In short:  
- *We honor the Creator with our mouth, but dishonor His creation with the same mouth.*

This is a moral inconsistency because if we truly revere God, we would also respect and care for those made to reflect God. Cursing another person is a kind of indirect disrespect to God Himself, whose likeness they bear.

**Why does speech reveal moral disorder so quickly?**
Speech is powerful and immediate. Unlike deeds, which often have a delay between thought and action, words flow directly from the inner self:
- **Speech is spontaneous.** It often bypasses filters, especially during emotion or conflict, revealing what lies beneath the surface.
- **Words are expressive of the heart.** Jesus taught that “out of the abundance of the heart, the mouth speaks” (Matthew 12:34). Our words thus manifest the true state of our character and beliefs, sometimes even more honestly than our actions do.
- **Speech is relational.** It constantly involves others—so impatience, pride, anger, or contempt show up quickly in what we say, especially under stress.
- **It's harder to conceal attitudes in words.** Self-control in speech is uniquely challenging. Bitter feelings, prejudice, hypocrisy, and duplicity leak out through what and how we say things.

Thus, speech serves as an almost real-time indicator of inner moral order—or disorder. When the same mouth that blesses God curses people, it exposes a split in values and allegiances—a disorder that needs to be addressed.

**In summary:**  
James confronts the hypocrisy of using words to bless God while cursing His image-bearers, exposing a deep-seated inconsistency in our hearts. Speech quickly reveals this moral disorder because it is an open door to our inner life—showing, sometimes uncomfortably, whether our reverence for God truly shapes our regard for others.
