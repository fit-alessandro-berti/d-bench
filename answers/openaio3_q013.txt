Amos is not attacking worship in itself; he is attacking worship that has become a lie.  
In the eighth century B.C. the shrines of the northern kingdom (Bethel, Gilgal, Beersheba, etc.) were full of pilgrims, sacrifices and music (Am 5:4–6; 8:3). At the same time the nation was enjoying a boom economy that was built on:

• Judicial corruption – “they turn aside the needy in the gate” (5:12).  
• Economic exploitation – “they sell the righteous for silver and the poor for a pair of sandals” (2:6).  
• Violent seizure of land and property – “you trample on the poor and take from him grain taxes” (5:11).

The people therefore kept two totally separate ledgers:

Ledger A – Religion: endless festivals, choirs, incense, generous offerings.  
Ledger B – Daily life: bribes, dispossession of peasants, rigged scales, sexual immorality, violence.

They assumed that a heavy credit on Ledger A could offset the debits on Ledger B. That is the “fraud” Amos unmasks. They were using the cult as a religious cover, almost as a bribe, while systematically breaking the very covenant their worship was supposed to celebrate.

Why the total rejection instead of a polite call for “better balance”?

1. Worship and ethics are not two separable accounts. In Israel’s covenant theology the sacrifice is the outward sign of an inner reality—loyal love (ḥesed), justice (mišpāṭ) and righteousness (ṣĕdāqāh). If the inner reality is missing, the sign becomes a counterfeit. God therefore says, “your” festivals, “your” songs, not “mine.”

2. The worship itself is financed by and soaked in injustice. The “fat beasts” have been bought with money squeezed from the poor; the wine poured on the altar comes from vineyards obtained by foreclosure. To accept the offering would mean endorsing the oppression that paid for it.

3. Rhetorically, only a radical rejection can break the illusion. A mild protest—“try to mix in a little social concern”—would leave intact the fatal premise that ritual can compensate for evil. Amos overturns that premise: without justice, ritual has zero—and less than zero—value. “I hate … I will not accept … I will not hear.”

4. What God wants is not fewer festivals but a different atmosphere altogether: “Let justice roll on like a river, righteousness like a never-failing stream.” A river is continuous, not periodic like feast days; it flows through every field and street every day. Until that happens, any amount of music or incense is noise and stench to God.

Parallel prophetic texts make exactly the same point (Is 1:11-17; Mic 6:6-8; Hos 6:6; Jer 7:4-11; 1 Sam 15:22). The issue is not ritual versus morality but fraudulent ritual that pretends the morality is optional.

In short, Amos 5:21-24 exposes a spiritual swindle: using outward acts of worship to launder ill-gotten gain and to purchase a sense of divine approval. God rejects the whole performance because, divorced from justice and righteousness, it is worse than empty—it is a lie.
