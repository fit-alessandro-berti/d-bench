In Mark 8:34–35, the “self” being denied is not your personhood, dignity, or created individuality. It is the **self as autonomous center**—the ego that insists on ruling, preserving itself at all costs, defining good and evil on its own terms, and making comfort, status, safety, or control ultimate.

Jesus is not saying, “Become less human,” but rather, “Stop making yourself your own god.”

## What “deny himself” means
The phrase means something like:

- **renounce self-sovereignty**
- **refuse the rule of ego**
- **stop centering life on self-preservation**
- **yield the right to define yourself apart from God**

It is less about hating yourself and more about **disowning the false self**—the self built on pride, fear, possession, image, and control.

## Why “take up his cross” sharpens this
A cross was not a symbol of mild inconvenience. It meant shame, suffering, and death. So Jesus is calling people not merely to “be unselfish,” but to a death of the old self that clings to life on its own terms.

The paradox is:

- if you try to **save** your life—cling to it, protect it, absolutize it—you lose it
- if you **lose** it for Christ and the gospel, you find real life

So the denial is of the **self curved inward**, not the self as loved by God.

## Why it feels liberating to some
It sounds liberating when someone has felt the misery of living trapped in:

- endless self-protection
- anxiety about image and success
- slavery to desire, approval, or fear
- the burden of having to manufacture meaning alone

Jesus’ words can sound like freedom because they promise release from the exhausting project of self-salvation. You no longer have to be your own center, defender, and redeemer.

In that sense, self-denial is not annihilation but **deliverance**.

## Why it sounds offensive to others
It sounds offensive because modern instinct often treats the self as sacred and self-assertion as the highest good. So “deny yourself” can sound like:

- repression
- loss of identity
- submission to external authority
- an attack on autonomy
- even abuse, if misunderstood or misused

It especially offends the ego because Jesus does not offer advice; he issues a claim over the whole person. He asks not for addition but for surrender.

## Important distinction
This text does **not** mean:

- deny that you have value
- accept abuse
- erase your personality
- pretend your pain does not matter

Christianly understood, the self denied is the **false lordship of self**, so that the **true self** may be found in communion with Christ.

A concise way to put it:

**Jesus calls for the death of the self that wants to possess life without God, so that the self created and redeemed by God may truly live.**
