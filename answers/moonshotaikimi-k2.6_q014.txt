The text primarily says that **treasure trains the heart**, though it simultaneously **reveals** it. The verse does not merely diagnose what you already love; it warns and promises that wherever you put your treasure, your heart will inevitably follow.

### 1. The Direction of the Arrow
The grammar is decisive. Jesus does not say, *“Where your heart is, there your treasure will be also.”* He inverts it:

> *“For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also.”*

In the Greek, the verb is future—*ἐκεῖ ἔσται καὶ ἡ καρδία σου* (“there your heart **will be**”). The *gar* (“for”) clause gives the **rationale** for the two commands that precede it:

- Do **not** lay up treasures on earth.
- **Do** lay up treasures in heaven.

Jesus commands a specific investment strategy *because* the location of your treasure determines the future location of your heart. The causality runs from possession to affection, from external commitment to internal orientation. Treasure functions less like a thermometer (simply revealing pre-existing temperature) and more like a magnet (pulling the heart toward itself).

### 2. How Possession, Attention, and Love Become Tied

The passage binds these three together through the logic of **vulnerability and investment**.

**Possession demands guardianship.**
Earthly treasure is fragile: moth consumes fabric, rust corrodes metal, thieves breach walls. Whatever you store up, you must now protect. The treasure does not sit passively; it makes a claim on your time, energy, and anxiety. Your *attention* is necessarily drawn to securing what you have accumulated.

**Attention cultivates affection.**
The heart (καρδία) in biblical usage is not merely the seat of emotion but the center of the will, mind, and identity. What consistently occupies your attention gradually colonizes your inner life. When your economic security is tied to a stock portfolio, a reputation, or a hoard of goods, your thoughts orbit around market fluctuations, social comparisons, and security systems. Over time, that gravitational pull becomes **love**—not necessarily warm sentiment, but ultimate commitment.

**Love consolidates around treasure.**
Thus, possession → attention → love forms a feedback loop. The more you have stored in a particular place, the more of your self is bound to that place. Jesus exploits this psychological reality for spiritual formation: if you deliberately store treasure in heaven (in the economy of God, in mercy, in eternal realities), then your attention and ultimately your whole heart will be drawn there.

### 3. Revelation as Byproduct
None of this means revelation is absent. If you want to know where someone’s heart already is, look at what they are stockpiling. But in Matthew’s argument, revelation is a **secondary effect**. The primary thrust is formative: Jesus tells us to **choose our treasure strategically** so that our hearts will be trained away from earthly anxiety and toward heavenly security.

### Summary
The text says that **treasure trains the heart** by capturing attention and thereby redirecting love. Possession creates vulnerability; vulnerability demands attention; sustained attention transplants the heart. To lay up treasure in heaven is therefore an act of spiritual discipline—not just altruism, but a way of ensuring that the core of your being, your heart, rests in what is imperishable.
