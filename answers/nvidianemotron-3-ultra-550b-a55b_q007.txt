The command “Judge not” (μὴ κρίνετε, *mē krinete*) is one of the most quoted and most misunderstood verses in the New Testament. To understand how it differs from the assertion that **"moral judgments never matter"** (moral relativism or nihilism), we must look at the semantics of the Greek, the immediate literary context, and the theological anthropology of the Gospel.

Here is the short answer: **Discernment is the perception of reality for the sake of restoration; condemnation is the usurpation of divine prerogative for the sake of self-justification.**

---

### 1. The Semantic Range of *Krinō* (κρίνω)
The Greek verb *krinō* carries a spectrum of meaning in the New Testament. Context determines which shade is intended:

| Meaning | Example | Relevance to Luke 6:37 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **To discern/distinguish** (technical/legal) | "You **judge** according to the flesh; I **judge** no one." (John 8:15) | **Not forbidden.** Jesus elsewhere commands "righteous judgment" (John 7:24). |
| **To evaluate/approve** | "Let each one **judge** what I say." (1 Cor 10:15) | **Not forbidden.** Critical thinking is a virtue. |
| **To litigate/condemn** (final verdict) | "Why do you **judge** your brother?" (Rom 14:10) | **Forbidden.** This is the sense of Luke 6:37: *katadikazō* (condemn) is the parallel term. |

**The Difference:** Saying "moral judgments never matter" collapses the first two categories (discernment/evaluation) into the third (condemnation). Jesus forbids the *final verdict on a person’s standing before God*, not the *provisional assessment of an action’s conformity to truth*.

---

### 2. The Immediate Context: The Logic of the Kingdom (Luke 6:35–42)
Jesus does not say "Judge not" in a vacuum. He sandwiches it between two metaphors that define the alternative:

**A. The Golden Rule of Mercy (vv. 35–36)**
> *"Love your enemies... Be merciful, even as your Father is merciful."*
The prohibition on judging is the **negative articulation of mercy**. To condemn is to treat someone *as their worst moment defines them*. Mercy treats someone *as their potential in God defines them*.

**B. The Blind Leading the Blind (vv. 39–42)**
> *"Why do you see the speck in your brother’s eye, but do not notice the log in your own eye?"*
This is the death blow to "moral judgments never matter." **Jesus assumes the speck *is really there*.** It is a real moral defect (a splinter). The sin is not *seeing* it; the sin is *operating on it* while blinded by a log (hypocrisy/pride).

**If moral judgments didn't matter:**
*   There would be no speck.
*   There would be no need to "cast it out" (v. 42).
*   Jesus would not say, *"First take the log out of your own eye, **and then you will see clearly to take out the speck**."*

**Discernment is the prerequisite for surgery.** You cannot remove a tumor (discernment/judgment) if you refuse to admit tumors exist (relativism), nor can you remove it if you are hacking away with a rusty spoon while dying of sepsis yourself (condemnation/hypocrisy).

---

### 3. Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn

#### **Discernment (Διάκρισις / *Diakrisis*)**
*   **Teleology (Goal):** Restoration & Protection. "Let the righteous strike me; it shall be a kindness." (Ps 141:5). It aims at the *good* of the other or the *purity* of the community (1 Cor 5:12).
*   **Epistemology (Knowledge):** Provisional & Humble. "We know in part." (1 Cor 13:9). It acknowledges hidden motives, mitigating circumstances, and the image of God in the sinner.
*   **Posture:** "Side-by-side." The discerning person stands *with* the sinner under the Word of God. "Brother, if anyone is caught in any transgression, **you who are spiritual should restore him in a spirit of gentleness.** Keep watch on yourself, lest you too be tempted." (Gal 6:1).
*   **Standard:** The Word of God, not my preference.

#### **The Appetite to Condemn (Καταδικάζω / *Katadikazō*)**
*   **Teleology (Goal):** Self-Exaltation & Exclusion. It aims to *lower* the other to raise the self. It creates an "Us vs. Them" ontology where "They" are irredeemable.
*   **Epistemology (Knowledge):** Totalizing & Arrogant. It conflates *act* with *identity* ("He is a liar" vs "He lied"). It assumes omniscience regarding the heart (1 Cor 4:5: *"Do not pronounce judgment before the time... the Lord will disclose the purposes of the heart"*).
*   **Posture:** "Above." The condemner sits on the bench. They do not offer a towel and basin (John 13); they offer a gavel.
*   **Standard:** My performance / My tribe's shibboleths.

---

### 4. The "Measure You Use" (v. 38): Ontology, Not Transaction
> *"For with the measure you use it will be measured back to you."*

This is often read as a divine transaction: *God will treat you how you treat others.* But in the logic of the Kingdom, it is **ontological**.
*   **The Condemner** builds a world of **Law without Gospel**. In that world, *they* live under the Law, too. They have no plea for mercy because they have denied the category of mercy exists. They are "measured back" their own legalism.
*   **The Discerner** lives in a world of **Law fulfilled by Grace**. They can afford to call sin "sin" (discernment) precisely because they know the Judge has already taken the condemnation (Rom 8:1). They "measure" grace because they have received it.

### 5. Practical Diagnostic: Which Am I Doing?

| Situation | **Discernment** (Speck Removal) | **Condemnation** (Log Surgery) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **A brother sins** | *"This action harms you and violates Christ. I love you too much to watch you destroy yourself. Let's walk toward repentance together."* | *"You are the kind of person who does this. You are disqualified. I knew you weren't real."* |
| **Doctrinal Error** | *"This teaching contradicts Scripture at points X and Y. It will lead people astray. We must correct it for the flock's safety."* | *"He is a heretic/wolf. Cancel him. No nuance, no dialogue, no path back."* |
| **Cultural Moral Collapse** | *"This culture normalizes evil (greed, lust, violence). We must witness to a better way, protect the vulnerable, and vote/act accordingly."* | *"Those people are monsters. They are beyond hope. Let them burn. Good riddance."* |
| **Self-Reference** | *"I am capable of this same evil. But for the grace of God, there go I."* | *"I would never do that. Thank God I am not like that sinner."* (Luke 18:11) |

---

### 6. The Theological Anchor: The Cross
The difference between discernment and condemnation is the difference between **the Cross** and **the Lynch Mob**.

*   **Condemnation** says: *"You are guilty. You must pay."* (The logic of the Accuser, Rev 12:10).
*   **Discernment** says: *"You are guilty. **He paid.** Now, let me help you walk in the freedom He bought."* (The logic of the Advocate, 1 John 2:1).

To say "moral judgments never matter" is to say the Cross was unnecessary (there was no guilt to discern).
To "judge/condemn" in the Lukan sense is to say the Cross was insufficient (I must add my verdict to God's finished work).

**Jesus does not call us to moral blindness. He calls us to become surgeons—whose hands are steady because they have been washed in mercy, and whose eyes are clear because the log of self-righteousness has been extracted by the Great Physician.**
