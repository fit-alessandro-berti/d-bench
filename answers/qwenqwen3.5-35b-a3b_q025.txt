Based on the text of Matthew 6:1 and its immediate context within the Sermon on the Mount, the moral danger identified in the verse is primarily the **craving to be seen (motivation)**, rather than the **visibility itself (location)**.

Here is an analysis of why the text points to internal intent rather than external action.

### 1. The Textual Focus on Intent
The verse does not say, "Do not do your alms before men." Instead, it says, "Take heed that ye do not... **to be seen of them**."
The prepositional phrase "to be seen" acts as a qualifier for the action. It identifies the *purpose* of the act as the deciding factor. The Greek construction implies doing righteousness *for the sake of* public recognition. If the action is performed publicly but the intent is simply to help those in need without seeking attention, the specific warning of this verse does not apply.

### 2. The "Reward" Economy
The verse establishes a zero-sum economy of reward. Jesus states: *"otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven."*
*   **Human Praise as Final Payment:** In this framework, human recognition is viewed as a "reward" that can be collected immediately.
*   **The Conflict:** If a person performs a good deed specifically to gain human praise, they have already collected their payment. Consequently, there is nothing left for God to give. The act becomes a transaction with men rather than an offering to God.
*   **The Consequence:** The moral corruption lies not in the charity itself, but in the theft of glory that belongs to God. The person has "taken the reward" by demanding applause, thereby disqualifying themselves from heavenly credit.

### 3. Contextual Clues: Hypocrisy vs. Secrecy
To fully understand the verse, one must look at the surrounding verses (Matthew 6:2, 5, 16). In each instance, Jesus criticizes the "hypocrites" (*hypokritēs*, meaning stage actors).
*   Hypocrites perform righteousness (almsgiving, prayer, fasting) on stage.
*   The danger is not that they are seen by people, but that they are acting a role for an audience.
*   In the verses immediately following (6:4), Jesus instructs that the left hand should not know what the right hand is doing. This is not a command for physical deception, but a metaphor for the **purity of internal motivation**. The goal is to ensure the donor does not even feel the self-congratulation that usually accompanies public visibility.

### 4. Visibility is Not Inherently Sinful
Within the broader biblical context, visibility itself is not condemned; rather, it is treated as a minefield for the heart.
*   **Jesus' Own Example:** Jesus performed many miracles and taught publicly, not always in secret. He did not condemn the visibility, but he condemned the *seeking* of visibility.
*   **The Danger of Visibility:** Public goodness is dangerous because it tempts the doer to seek validation. It turns virtue into a tool for self-advancement. If the visibility is necessary for the mission (e.g., public witness to inspire others), it is distinct from visibility sought for self-glorification.

### Conclusion
In Matthew 6:1, the problem is **pride disguised as piety.**

The moral danger is the **craving to be seen**. If one gives publicly but is grateful that no one saw them (or if one gives secretly to protect the dignity of the recipient), there is no violation of this verse. The "reward" of God is forfeited only when the giver treats human applause as the goal of the action. The verse teaches that a good deed done for self-glorification ceases to be a spiritual act and becomes a secular transaction.
