In Ephesians 4:31-32, bitterness and forgiveness are directly juxtaposed to highlight the contrast between a negative, harmful attitude and a positive, Christ-like one. To understand why, let's examine the context and the nature of bitterness as presented in this passage.

### Context and the Nature of Bitterness

The book of Ephesians is a letter written by Paul to the early Christian community in Ephesus, emphasizing the unity and the new identity of believers in Christ. Chapter 4 begins with an exhortation to live a life worthy of their calling, emphasizing unity and maturity in faith (Ephesians 4:1-3). Paul then discusses the diversity within the body of Christ and how different gifts are given to believers for the edification of the church (Ephesians 4:7-16). The passage in question (Ephesians 4:31-32) is part of a larger section that encourages believers to put off their old self and be renewed in the spirit of their minds, adopting a new way of living characterized by truth, righteousness, and holiness (Ephesians 4:17-32).

### Bitterness as Described in Ephesians 4:31

The term "bitterness" (πικρία, pikria) here is not limited to a feeling but encompasses a broader attitude or condition that affects one's behavior and interactions with others. It is listed alongside other negative behaviors or attitudes: wrath, anger, clamour (or loud, angry disputes), evil speaking, and malice. This grouping suggests that bitterness is not merely an internal feeling but is associated with outward expressions of negativity and hostility.

### Why Bitterness and Forgiveness are Juxtaposed

1. **Contrast and Instruction**: By placing bitterness and forgiveness directly against each other, Paul is drawing a stark contrast between two ways of living. The instruction is clear: believers are to let go of the former (bitterness and associated negative behaviors) and embrace the latter (forgiveness and kindness).

2. **Understanding Bitterness**: The text seems to treat bitterness as more than just a feeling; it is a chosen posture or attitude that can manifest in various harmful behaviors. It is something that can be "put away," suggesting that it is within the believer's power to change. Bitterness can become a habit or a default response if not addressed, and it can be contagious in the sense that it can affect how one interacts with others, potentially creating a cycle of negativity.

3. **Theological Significance of Forgiveness**: The reason for forgiving others is deeply theological and Christocentric. Believers are to forgive "even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you" (Ephesians 4:32). The model for forgiveness is God's forgiveness of humanity through Christ. This divine forgiveness is not just a transactional act but a transformation that enables believers to forgive others.

4. **Implications for Community Life**: The juxtaposition emphasizes the importance of maintaining a positive, loving environment within the Christian community. Bitterness can erode unity and hinder the witness of the church, while forgiveness and kindness foster a community that reflects the character of Christ.

In summary, bitterness in Ephesians 4:31 is portrayed as a harmful attitude or condition that can manifest in negative behaviors and is contrary to the kind of life that believers are called to live. By directly contrasting it with forgiveness, Paul underscores the importance of adopting a forgiving and kind posture towards one another, modeled after God's forgiveness through Christ. This is not just about feelings or personal choices but about living out one's faith in a way that promotes unity and reflects the grace of God.
