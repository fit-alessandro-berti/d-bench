In Matthew 6:1, Jesus cautions against performing charitable deeds (alms) with the primary intent of being observed and praised by others. The moral danger identified here does not inherently lie in the visibility of the act itself, but rather in the motivation behind it—specifically, the craving or desire to be seen and acknowledged by others.

The key issue here is the intent of the heart, which Jesus frequently emphasizes throughout the Gospels. If the primary motivation to give to the needy is to gain public recognition or social approval, the giving is tainted with self-interest rather than pure generosity or compassion. This is because the act becomes centered around the self (the giver) instead of the needs of the other (the recipient).

The phrase "to be seen of them" clarifies that the problem is the craving for visibility and recognition, not the visibility itself. There could be situations where acts of charity are public and still have pure motives, such as encouraging others to give or bringing awareness to a particular need. In such cases, the visibility is a byproduct, not the goal.

Thus, the verse warns that when acts of goodness are performed with the intention of showcasing one's piety or benevolence to receive earthly rewards (praise, status, etc.), there is no spiritual reward "of your Father which is in heaven" because the act is compromised by self-serving interests. The true reward from the Father, as implied, comes from acts done in sincerity and humility, focusing on love and compassion toward others without self-glorification.
