This passage from 1 Corinthians 13:4–7 is one of the most profound and influential descriptions of love in Christian tradition. The repeated "love does not…" statements—envy, vaunting, being puffed up, behaving unseemly, seeking its own, being easily provoked, thinking evil, rejoicing in iniquity—form a striking contrast to the positive qualities that follow: bearing, believing, hoping, enduring all things.

**Why so many negations?**

The reason Paul includes so many negations is to define love not by what it adds to a person, but by what it subtracts from them. Love, in this portrait, is not about self-aggrandizement or self-protection. Instead, it is about self-emptying, humility, and the abandonment of attitudes that breed conflict and division. Each negation is a rejection of the very things that foster pride, jealousy, and self-interest—qualities that often lead to broken relationships and fractured communities.

For example:
- **Not envying**—Love is not threatened by others' good fortune.
- **Not vaunting itself**—Love doesn't need to prove or display itself.
- **Not puffed up**—Love is not arrogant or self-important.
- **Not easily provoked**—Love is patient, not quick to anger.
- **Not thinking evil**—Love gives the benefit of the doubt, does not assume the worst about others.
- **Not seeking her own**—Love is not self-serving; it prioritizes others' needs.

These negations are not weaknesses; rather, they are the foundation of authentic strength. By renouncing the desire to control, to be right, or to get one's own way, love reveals a deeper kind of power—one that endures, forgives, and sustains even in the face of hurt or injustice.

**What vision of strength is being proposed?**

The vision of strength here is not the strength of domination or force, but the strength of resilience, humility, and selflessness. It is the strength that can bear all things, believe all things, hope in all circumstances, and endure without breaking. This is the strength of the cross—the paradoxical power found in weakness, in giving rather than taking, in enduring rather than conquering.

In other words, the passage proposes that true strength lies not in what we can do for ourselves, but in what we can do for others, even when it costs us. It is the strength to forgive, to wait, to trust, to carry burdens that are not our own. This kind of strength is not visible in grand gestures but in the quiet, persistent acts of self-denial and service that characterize genuine love.

**Summary:**  
The long list of negations is not a description of what love lacks, but of what love is freed from. By casting off the habits of pride, envy, and self-interest, love reveals a deeper, more enduring strength—one rooted in humility, patience, and sacrificial giving. This is the kind of strength that builds up the community and endures all things, as described in the passage.
