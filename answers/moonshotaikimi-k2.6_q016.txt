The elder brother’s **deepest problem is pride in obedience**—a self-righteous, transactional view of his relationship with his father. But that pride necessarily produces all the other symptoms, including a radical **discomfort with mercy**.

Here is how the text supports this reading:

**1. Pride in obedience is the root.**
The elder brother does not speak as a son who loves his father; he speaks as a hired hand presenting an invoice: *“Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment”* (v. 29). His claim to flawless obedience is almost certainly deluded self-justification, and it reveals that he has been “good” not out of love, but as a strategy to secure goods and status. He relates to the father on a merit basis: *I obey, therefore you owe me.* When the father lavishes grace on the younger brother, the elder brother’s entire system of self-salvation is threatened. If the prodigal can be welcomed home simply because the father is merciful, then the elder brother’s “credit” of obedience is devalued.

**2. This pride creates “wounded fairness” and envy.**
His anger (v. 28) and his complaint—*“thou never gavest me a kid… but as soon as this thy son was come… thou hast killed for him the fatted calf”* (vv. 29–30)—are not merely about a party. They are the eruption of a heart that believes rewards should be distributed according to merit. His sense of fairness is wounded because he thinks the father is running a economy of justice rather than a household of grace.

**3. The fruit of this pride is discomfort with mercy itself.**
The father explains the celebration in the language of resurrection and grace: *“this thy brother was dead, and is alive again; and was lost, and is found”* (v. 32). The elder brother cannot enter into that joy. Mercy feels morally offensive to him because it violates his merit-based worldview. He does not want the father to forgive; he wants the father to *pay him* for his obedience and to *punish* the younger brother for his sin.

**4. The context confirms it.**
Luke 15 opens with Pharisees and scribes grumbling because Jesus receives sinners (vv. 1–2). The elder brother is their narrative counterpart. Like them, he is confident in his own righteousness and contemptuous of grace shown to the undeserving (cf. Luke 18:9–14).

**Conclusion:** Anger, envy, and wounded fairness are the visible fruit. The deepest root is **pride in obedience**—a self-righteousness so complete that it makes the free, rejoicing mercy of the father intolerable. Until he abandons his confidence in his own record, he can never share the father’s joy, nor can he realize that, as the father says, *“all that I have is thine”* (v. 31).
