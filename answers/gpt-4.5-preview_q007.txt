Luke 6:37 is often misunderstood as a command to abandon moral judgment altogether, but there's a crucial distinction between discernment and condemnation. Jesus' words aren't a dismissal of moral clarity; rather, they target the attitude of self-righteous condemnation.

**Discernment** is the thoughtful evaluation of right and wrong, recognizing human frailty with empathy. It's humble, reflective, and seeks constructive outcomes—aiming ultimately at restoration or reconciliation. It involves seeing clearly but compassionately, acknowledging our common humanity and our shared moral vulnerability.

In contrast, **the appetite to condemn** is about judgment that takes pleasure in pointing out flaws or failings in others. It's rooted in self-righteousness, superiority, and often masks our own moral blindness or insecurity. Condemnation cuts off relationships, diminishes others, and places oneself above moral reproach, ignoring personal shortcomings.

Thus, Luke 6:37 challenges not moral discernment itself, but the heart behind it. It encourages forgiving as one hopes to be forgiven, knowing judgment becomes reciprocal. True discernment guides us towards understanding and reconciliation; condemnation, instead, isolates and destroys.
