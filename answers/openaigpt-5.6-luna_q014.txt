It says both, but not in exactly the same way.

The final sentence—“where your treasure is, there will your heart be also”—describes a connection: treasure reveals where the heart is. But the command “lay up for yourselves” also implies that what one deliberately stores and invests in will draw the heart in that direction. Treasure is therefore not merely a diagnostic of the heart; it is also a means by which the heart is formed.

### 1. Treasure reveals the heart

“Treasure” means more than possessions in the abstract. It is what one treats as valuable, secures, accumulates, and is unwilling to lose. A person’s use of money and possessions discloses what he or she regards as ultimate:

- security,
- status,
- pleasure,
- family,
- God’s kingdom,
- the approval of others.

Thus earthly treasure can reveal an earthly orientation, while heavenly treasure reveals a kingdom-oriented one. The issue is not simply whether a person owns things, but what occupies the place of highest value.

### 2. Treasure trains the heart

Jesus does not merely say, “Notice where your treasure already is.” He commands, “Lay up … treasures in heaven.” That is an instruction to make concrete choices—how to give, spend, save, and use possessions—in ways directed toward God’s lasting purposes.

Such actions shape desire. What we repeatedly invest in receives our attention; what receives our attention becomes more significant to us; what becomes significant begins to command our affection and loyalty. In that sense, Jesus treats possessions as spiritually formative. We do not always wait for our affections to become pure before acting; faithful action can help redirect our affections.

This is why the saying works in both directions:

- The heart sends treasure toward what it values.
- Treasure draws the heart toward what it has chosen to value.

### 3. Possession, attention, and love

The sequence may be expressed this way:

**Possession and investment → attention → affection and allegiance.**

What we possess or invest in requires care. We monitor it, protect it, think about it, and plan around it. That attention gradually discloses and reinforces love. Jesus’ point is not that money mechanically controls the heart, but that our handling of material goods is inseparable from our worship and allegiance.

“Heart” in biblical language is not merely emotion. It includes the center of one’s will, thought, desire, and loyalty. So the issue is ultimately: What has the organizing claim on the person?

This is confirmed by the surrounding context. Jesus next speaks of the eye as the lamp of the body and then says, “Ye cannot serve God and mammon” (Matthew 6:22–24). The concern is not simply wealth but divided vision and divided mastership.

### 4. “Heaven” is not spiritualized accumulation

“To lay up treasure in heaven” does not mean earning salvation by depositing good deeds in a celestial account. It means orienting one’s resources toward God’s kingdom and trusting in God’s lasting reward rather than in vulnerable earthly securities. In Matthew’s context, this includes practices such as sincere generosity, righteousness before God, and freedom from anxiety about material provision.

So the passage is both **revealing** and **formative**:

- Your treasure shows where your heart is.
- Your chosen treasure helps determine where your heart will be.

Jesus therefore calls for more than detachment from possessions. He calls for a reordering of value, so that possessions become servants of love for God and neighbor rather than instruments of self-security.
