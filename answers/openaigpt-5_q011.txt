Short answer: Isaiah says goodness is more than not doing wrong. It is an actively learned and practiced way of love and justice.

Why the move from “cease to do evil” to “learn to do well”:
- Repentance has two turns: from and to. “Cease” addresses stopping sins of commission; “learn to do well” addresses sins of omission by calling for positive righteousness.
- “Learn” (Hebrew lamad) implies formation, instruction, and habit. Goodness is not automatic once we stop evil; it must be taught, trained, and practiced.
- The imperatives escalate from general to concrete: wash/cease (personal restraint) → learn/seek (active pursuit) → relieve/judge/plead (specific advocacy). It moves from private morality to public justice.

What “goodness” looks like here:
- Not mere restraint but proactive justice: “seek mishpat (justice), correct oppression, judge the fatherless, plead for the widow.” These are the covenant’s emblematic vulnerable (cf. Deut 10:18), signaling social, legal, and economic action.
- It is skillful and communal: terms like “seek” and legal verbs (“judge,” “plead”) point to sustained effort and institutional integrity, not just kind intentions.

Biblical resonance:
- Micah 6:8; Amos 5:14–15; James 1:27 echo the same pattern: worship without practiced justice is empty.
- Thus, goodness is learned, habitual love expressed in concrete advocacy, not merely the absence of harm.
