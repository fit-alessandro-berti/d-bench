The core warning in Matthew 6:1 lies not in the simple fact of being seen, but in the motivation of the heart. The Greek phrase translated “to be seen of them” (*πρὸς τὸ θεαθῆναι αὐτοῖς*) uses a construction that expresses purpose or intent. Jesus is cautioning against performing acts of righteousness *with the deliberate goal* of attracting human admiration.

If visibility itself were the danger, the Sermon on the Mount would contradict itself, since earlier Jesus tells his followers, “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven” (Matthew 5:16). The critical difference is the direction of the glory. In Matthew 5:16, the visible good works point observers toward God. In Matthew 6:1, the works are twisted into a means of self-exaltation, redirecting glory toward the doer.

The “reward” system Jesus describes confirms this. The reward from the Father is forfeited when the craving for human applause becomes the paycheck—the transaction is already settled in full through the fleeting praise of people. The danger is spiritual hypocrisy: outwardly serving God while inwardly serving one’s own reputation. So the moral peril is the craving to be seen, which corrupts the integrity of the act by displacing God as the audience that ultimately matters.
