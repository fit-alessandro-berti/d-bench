### The Passage (Luke 18:9–14)

The parable tells of two men in the Temple:  
- **The Pharisee** prays by declaring how *not like other people* he is—contrasting himself with "extortioners, unjust, adulterers," and claiming he fasts twice a week and gives tithes.
- **The tax collector (publican)**, though outside the religious community (a tax collector was often viewed as socially unclean), prays a simple, humbling plea: "God be merciful to me a sinner." He doesn’t even look up to heaven, but instead beats his breast in remorse.

Jesus says the **publican is justified before God** because of his attitude.

---

## What exactly condemns the Pharisee?

**It’s not about doctrine or the actual amount of tithes or fasting.** The Pharisee’s problem is his **attitude and posture** in prayer and self-regard.

Let’s look at the key elements:

### 1. **Comparison and Self-Righteousness**
The Pharisee is constantly comparing himself to "other men"—those he considers worse sinners. He sees himself as superior because he follows the outward rules (tithes, fasting) and thinks he’s spiritually above them. This is a form of **pride and self-righteousness**, which is actually a *spiritual failing* according to Jesus.

### 2. **Contempt and Disregard for Others**
The Pharisee despises others ("I thank thee that I am not as other men are..."). He is not genuinely concerned with the state of his heart or the hearts of those he judges. He uses his religious observance as a way to *display virtue* and separate himself, rather than as a means of genuine love or humility before God.

### 3. **Use of Virtue as Self-Display**
He prays for himself to be seen as righteous rather than for God's grace. His actions are motivated by the desire for external approval and to distinguish himself, not by heartfelt repentance or awareness of sin. Jesus says this is like "stretching out the hand to receive what is given, but not what is offered" (another similar saying in Luke 18:11). He’s seeking recognition from God through his own efforts, not through faith in Christ’s mercy.

### 4. **Pride and Empty Display**
The Pharisee is proud of his own righteousness and sees himself as above the needs of others. He misses the heart of God’s kingdom—mercy, forgiveness, and humility.

---

## Why is the tax collector’s posture spiritually truer?

The tax collector’s prayer is **humble and dependent**. He admits his sin openly ("God be merciful to me a sinner"), recognizes his separation from God ("I am a sinner"), and doesn’t try to hide or compare himself to others. He doesn’t care about being seen as righteous—he is simply asking for mercy because he knows he deserves it.

This **humble acknowledgment of sin and need for grace** is at the heart of the Christian message. Jesus says the tax collector is "justified" because he has a *righteous heart* (based on faith in God’s mercy) rather than because of his works or external religious performance.

So the **true spiritual quality** here is **humility, honesty about sin, and dependence on God’s mercy**, not external religious acts or self-exalmination.

## Conclusion

**What condemns the Pharisee?**  
- His *pride*, *self-righteousness*, *contempt for others*, and *use of religious observance as self-display* rather than genuine faith.

**Why is the tax collector “truer” before God?**  
Because he has a *humble, contrite heart* and seeks God’s mercy, not his own approval. Jesus values inner transformation and genuine dependence on God’s grace over outward religious performance.

**In short:** The Pharisee’s problem is his *outward display of virtue* combined with *inner pride and lack of humility*. The tax collector’s simple, honest plea for mercy is spiritually deeper because it springs from a place of need and brokenness, not self-satisfaction.
