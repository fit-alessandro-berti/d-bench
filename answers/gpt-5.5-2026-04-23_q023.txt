Repentance in Isaiah 55:6–7 is described as involving both **“way”** and **“thoughts”** because the biblical picture of sin is not merely external behavior, but a whole orientation of life.

- **“Way”** refers to one’s outward path: conduct, habits, practices, the direction a person’s life is taking.
- **“Thoughts”** refers to the inward life: intentions, plans, desires, imaginations, schemes, and the self-justifying reasoning that gives birth to sinful action.

So the verse is saying: the wicked must not only stop walking in a wrong path; he must also renounce the inward patterns that produce and defend that path.

This matters because outward conduct and inward imagination are connected. A person’s “way” often flows from his “thoughts,” and his thoughts are reinforced by the way he lives. Repentance therefore is not mere behavior management, nor is it only private regret. It is a turning of the whole person: actions, desires, plans, and loyalties.

The passage also contains a great promise:

> “Let him return unto the Lord, and he will have mercy upon him… for he will abundantly pardon.”

This means mercy is genuinely possible. The call to forsake wicked ways and unrighteous thoughts is not given because pardon is scarce, but because God is near and willing to forgive. The sinner is not told to despair over the depth of his corruption; he is invited to return to the Lord precisely because God “will abundantly pardon.”

The next verses make this even stronger:

> “For my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways…”  
> —Isaiah 55:8

God’s “thoughts” and “ways” are higher than ours, especially in mercy. Human beings may imagine that guilt is too great, change is impossible, or pardon must be limited. But God’s way is to receive the one who returns, and God’s thought is abundant mercy.

So repentance involves both **outward conduct** and **inward imagination**, and the promise is that the whole person who returns to the Lord may find not reluctant forgiveness, but abundant pardon.
