### Why Would Light Be Hated?
In the verse, hatred of light isn't about literal brightness (like avoiding sunlight) but a deeper aversion to what light *represents* and *does*. Here's the reasoning:

- **Light as a Disruptor of Comfortable Shadows**: People "love darkness" because it allows them to operate without interference. Evil deeds thrive in obscurity—think of how corruption, deceit, or personal vices often rely on secrecy to persist. Light, by contrast, illuminates everything, making it impossible to hide. The verse suggests this hatred stems from self-preservation: if your actions are "evil" (or morally questionable), exposure could lead to consequences like judgment, loss of status, or personal upheaval. Why embrace something that could dismantle the very foundation of your existence? It's like a thief hating a spotlight—not because the light is inherently bad, but because it ruins the game.

- **Aversion Rooted in Human Nature**: Philosophically, this echoes thinkers like Plato in his *Allegory of the Cave*, where prisoners in a dark cave fear the blinding light of truth outside, preferring their familiar illusions. Psychologically, this can be linked to cognitive dissonance: when our actions clash with our self-image or societal norms, we avoid anything that highlights the inconsistency. The verse frames this as a spiritual reality—humanity's fallen state inclines us toward darkness because it feels safer than the vulnerability of light.

In essence, light is hated not for what it is (pure and revealing) but for what it demands: accountability and change. Those who "do truth" (i.e., live in alignment with goodness) welcome it, as it affirms their integrity.

### The Relation Between Truth, Exposure, Shame, and the Desire Not to Be Seen Clearly
These concepts are tightly interwoven in the verse, forming a chain reaction that explains the pull toward darkness. Let's unpack them and their connections:

1. **Truth as the Core Illuminator**:
   - Truth is the "light" in this metaphor—it's objective, unchanging, and sourced in God ("wrought in God," as the verse says). It reveals reality as it is, without distortion. But truth isn't neutral; it judges by exposing falsehoods. For someone whose "deeds are evil," truth acts like a mirror, forcing confrontation with uncomfortable facts. This ties into broader ideas in philosophy (e.g., Søren Kierkegaard's notion that truth demands personal commitment and can provoke anxiety) or psychology (e.g., how denial mechanisms protect us from painful truths). The hatred arises because truth doesn't just inform—it convicts, pushing people to either reform or retreat further into denial.

2. **Exposure as the Mechanism of Light**:
   - Light doesn't just shine; it *exposes*. The verse says evildoers avoid light "lest [their] deeds should be reproved" (or exposed and rebuked). Exposure strips away the veils of privacy, rationalization, or social facades that allow wrongdoing to continue unchecked. In a relational sense, this could mean being "seen" by others, by society, or by God—think of how scandals in public life (e.g., political corruption or personal betrayals) often lead to downfall precisely because they're brought into the light. The desire to avoid exposure is a defense mechanism; it's why people might lie, hide, or compartmentalize their lives. Without exposure, one can maintain the illusion of control, but light shatters that, making deeds "manifest" (visible and undeniable).

3. **Shame as the Emotional Fallout**:
   - Shame emerges when exposure meets truth—it's the sting of realizing your actions don't align with moral or divine standards. Unlike guilt (which is about the act itself), shame is about the self: "I am bad" rather than "I did something bad." The verse implies that evildoers hate light because it amplifies this shame, making hidden flaws public and personal. Psychologically, shame researcher Brené Brown describes it as a fear of disconnection—being seen as unworthy. This creates a vicious cycle: shame drives people deeper into darkness to avoid more exposure, reinforcing the hatred of light. Biblically, this contrasts with the "light" path, where coming forward leads to redemption rather than condemnation (e.g., the idea of confession bringing healing in Christian theology).

4. **The Desire Not to Be Seen Clearly as a Motivating Force**:
   - This desire is the glue holding it all together—it's the instinctive pull toward darkness to evade the truth-exposure-shame triad. People want to be seen on their own terms, not fully illuminated. In darkness, you can curate your image, justify your actions, or ignore their harm. Clarity, however, demands wholeness: no half-truths, no hidden corners. This relates to existential ideas, like Jean-Paul Sartre's concept of "bad faith," where people deceive themselves to avoid the freedom (and responsibility) of authentic living. The verse positions this as a choice: cling to obscurity out of fear, or step into light for validation. Ironically, the desire for obscurity often stems from a deeper longing for acceptance— but without the risk of rejection that true visibility brings.
