Luke 6:37 is one of the most frequently quoted—and frequently misunderstood—passages in the New Testament. In modern culture, "Judge not" is often interpreted as a mandate for absolute moral relativism: a command to never evaluate anyone’s behavior, to refrain from calling anything "wrong," and to adopt a policy of "anything goes." 

However, a closer look at the text and its broader context reveals that Jesus is not advocating for moral blindness. Rather, he is drawing a sharp distinction between **godly discernment** and the **appetite to condemn**. 

Here is how Luke 6:37 differs from the idea that moral judgments never matter, explored through the tension between discernment and condemnation.

---

### 1. The Paradox of Forgiveness: Why Morality *Must* Matter
The second half of the verse provides the key to understanding the first: **"Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven."**

Logically, forgiveness cannot exist without moral judgment. 
* To forgive someone, you must first acknowledge that a genuine wrong has been committed. 
* If moral judgments never matter—if right and wrong are indifferent—then no one has ever truly sinned against you, and forgiveness is unnecessary. 

Therefore, Jesus is not asking us to pretend that bad actions are good, or that harmful behavior doesn't matter. To practice forgiveness, one must have the moral clarity to recognize evil, label it as wrong, and then choose to release the offender from retribution. Far from dismantling morality, Luke 6:37 requires clear moral framework to function.

### 2. Discernment vs. Condemnation: A Tale of Two Motives

The prohibition against "judging" ($krino$) and "condemning" ($katadikazo$) in Greek refers to passing final, damnatory sentence on a person's worth or ultimate destiny. It is a warning against usurping God's role as the supreme Judge. 

Jesus is contrasting two radically different approaches to moral reality:

| Feature | Discernment (Recognizing Truth) | The Appetite to Condemn (Judgmentalism) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Focus** | Evaluates **actions, fruit, and concepts** (e.g., *Is this truth or a lie? Is this helpful or harmful?*). | Evaluates the **ultimate value and soul of a person** (e.g., *They are irredeemable trash*). |
| **Posture** | Rooted in humility, self-awareness, and grief over sin. | Rooted in pride, self-righteousness, and moral superiority. |
| **Motivation** | Love, protection of the innocent, and the **restoration** of the wrongdoer. | Egotism, retribution, and the thrill of **destruction**. |
| **Authority** | Subject to God’s law (recognizing oneself as a fellow sinner in need of grace). | Acts as the ultimate authority (sitting on God’s throne). |

Scripture frequently commands believers to use **discernment**. Jesus himself said, *"Do not judge by appearances, but judge with right judgment"* (John 7:24) and commanded his followers to evaluate prophets by their "fruit" (Matthew 7:15-20). Discernment is the wise, clear-eyed observation of reality necessary for navigating a fallen world.

### 3. The "Appetite to Condemn"
The prohibition in Luke 6:37 targets the **appetite to condemn**—a dark, psychological, and spiritual impulse deeply rooted in human nature. 

The appetite to condemn is addictive because it provides a cheap imitation of righteousness. When we condemn others, we experience a burst of moral superiority without having to do the hard work of personal repentance. Immediately following this passage in Luke 6, Jesus talks about the person who tries to remove a speck of dust from a neighbor's eye while ignoring the wooden beam in their own. 

The appetite to condemn relies on several toxic habits:
* **Writing people off:** Reducing a human being—made in the image of God—down to their worst mistake or belief.
* **Delighting in exposure:** Feeling a subtle sense of joy or vindication when someone else falls, rather than sorrow.
* **Self-exemption:** Using the sins of others as a fog screen to hide one's own moral failures.

### Summary
Saying "moral judgments never matter" leads to apathy, chaos, and the inability to defend the vulnerable. 

Luke 6:37 does not call for apathy; it calls for **mercy**. 

* **Discernment** allows us to see the world accurately: it sees the sin, recognizes the danger, and calls wrong by its name. 
* **The Appetite to Condemn** sees the sin and uses it to destroy the sinner to elevate the self. 
* **The Teaching of Jesus** commands us to use discernment to guard our hearts, while refusing the arrogance of condemnation—offering instead the same merciful forgiveness that we desperately need from God.
