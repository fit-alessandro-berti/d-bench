At first glance, 1 Corinthians 13:4–7 seems to define love partly by what it is *not*: not envious, not boastful, not arrogant, not rude, not self-seeking, not irritable, not resentful. Why so many negations?

**1. Love is love because the ego is deflated.**  
Paul is writing to a church swollen with pride—pride in spiritual gifts, social status, intelligence, and party factions. Against that backdrop, he shows that love is not a feeling of superiority but a *self-emptying* orientation toward the other. The negations are not mere rules; they are the removal of the things that block love: comparison, rivalry, self-promotion, touchiness, and suspicion. Where pride asks, “What about me?” love asks, “What is good for you?”

**2. The “negatives” actually describe a strong, active character.**  
Each negation implies a positive virtue:

- “Not easily provoked” means *self-control* under injury.
- “Thinketh no evil” means *refusing to keep a ledger of wrongs*.
- “Seeketh not her own” means *generosity* that makes room for others.
- “Vaunteth not itself” means *humility* secure enough not to advertise itself.

So the passage is not saying love is weak or passive; it is saying love is *unselfish*.

**3. The vision of strength proposed is resilient, patient, and hope-filled.**  
After stripping away ego, Paul piles on four active verbs: love **beareth, believeth, hopeth, endureth** all things. True strength here is not domination, retaliation, or emotional volatility. It is *staying power*: the ability to absorb wrongs without cynicism, to keep trusting and hoping, and to remain committed when evidence would justify quitting.

In short, Paul proposes that **the greatest human strength is not self-assertion but self-giving constancy**. Love is strong precisely because it refuses to be dragged down by envy, pride, or resentment, and instead keeps bearing, believing, hoping, and enduring.
