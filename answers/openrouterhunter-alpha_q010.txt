The power praised here is **power in inversion** — a deliberate, willing descent from the highest possible station to the lowest for the sake of others. It is not power as domination or coercion, but power as **self-giving, self-emptying love** (*kenosis*). Christ, though possessing divine authority, does not cling to it but enters into human fragility, servanthood, and even shameful death. This is power redefined — strength expressed through sacrifice, authority through service, victory through surrender.

Why does humility joined to greatness feel more unsettling than open domination?

1. **It disrupts our instinctive script of power.**  
   We are accustomed to greatness asserting itself — protecting its status, leveraging its advantage. When greatness *chooses* vulnerability, it exposes our own cravings for control, security, and self-promotion. It feels disorienting because it inverts the expected relationship between strength and self-interest.

2. **It makes a moral demand without force.**  
   Domination compels through fear; humility invites through example. An example of self-giving love, especially from a position of divine authority, carries a quiet but piercing moral weight. It asks, *“If the highest power acts this way, what does that say about how you should live?”* This can feel more confronting than outright coercion because it appeals to conscience rather than mere compliance.

3. **It reveals a deeper, unfamiliar kind of authority.**  
   The world equates authority with might, position, or control. Here, authority is rooted in relational trust and voluntary sacrifice. That can feel unsettling because it suggests true power might be found in places we’ve been taught to overlook — in service, in letting go, in solidarity with the weak.

4. **It undermines transactional thinking.**  
   Open domination operates on clear terms: obey or be crushed. Christ’s humility operates on grace — unearned, uncoerced, offered even to those who opposed him. This kind of power doesn’t fit our systems of reward and punishment, which can leave us feeling both awed and unnerved.

In short, this passage presents a power that **converts rather than conquers, redeems rather than rules**. Its unsettling nature lies in its goodness — a goodness that exposes our lesser definitions of strength and calls us into a deeper, more costly way of being.
