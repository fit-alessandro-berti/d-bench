In Matthew 6:1 the moral danger isn’t *mere visibility* as such, but the **intent and motive** behind doing good.

The verse frames the problem this way:

- “**Take heed** … *that ye do not your alms before men, **to be seen of them***”
- “otherwise **ye have no reward**…”

So Jesus identifies **“to be seen of them”** as the key issue: public goodness becomes spiritually dangerous when it is **performed as a bid for approval, recognition, or human praise**—when the person’s driving purpose is not God’s honor but the audience’s notice.

If someone does good where others can see, that’s not automatically disqualifying. The disqualifying element is when the act is **religiously “aimed” at being seen**—i.e., when the “reward” sought is effectively human admiration rather than God.

In short: **it’s not visibility itself; it’s the craving to be seen (and thus to gain human recognition) as the motive and target of the good deed.**
