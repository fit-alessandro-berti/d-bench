The passage emphasizes that enemies, those perceived as unjust and those whose opposition to one seems insurmountable and dangerous, should be the objects of love and moral concern. The command to “love your enemies, bless them that curse you, do good to them that hate you, and pray for them which despitefully use you and persecute you” (Matthew 5:44) seems inherently at odds with the idea that enemy-love is either sentimental, strategic, or merely a form of moral strength. However, one may wonder why it is so difficult to take it seriously, as well as what the nature of this requirement is. 

Regarding the difficulty of enemy-love taking seriously, there are various perspectives on why it is problematic. First, it clashes with the fundamental instinct of self-preservation. Human beings are wired to protect themselves from harm, and viewing enemies as threats to be avoided, opposed, or even fought against is a natural primal response. Enemy-love, by contrast, demands the opposite: to act virtuously towards those who seem intent on causing harm, which seems counterintuitive and possibly self-defeating. Second, it goes against the normal scope of moral obligations. While people may naturally feel philanthropy—love of others—and moral obligations towards those within their in-groups (family, friends, those who benefit them), extending such care to enemies, who seem to deserve harm, is ethically challenging. It requires a radical re-evaluation of “others” and the boundaries of moral concern, which many people may find difficult to embrace. Finally, it is difficult to act on the principle of “returning good for evil.” Most ethical systems and common moral intuitions support the idea of reciprocity, where wrongs should be answered in some proportionate way. Enemy-love, on this view, asks one to forgo retaliation or punishment, which seems contrary to natural justice and the desire for fairness.

Yet, enemy-love might not be sentimental or strategic but rather a deeper form of moral strength. It is not sentimental because it is not merely an emotional response but an active practice of moral character. It requires overcoming one’s natural inclinations and choosing to act with compassion and justice, even when it is difficult. This is not a fleeting emotion but a sustained choice to embody God’s love, as reflected in the verse that states “he maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust” (Matthew 5:45). Thus, enemy-love is about aligning one’s actions with the moral ideal of divine compassion, going beyond mere sentiment to reflect a commitment to the good of all. It may also not be strategic, as strategic thinking typically involves calculating the costs and benefits of actions for personal gain. The command in Matthew 5 to love enemies, however, is not about seeking a particular outcome but about becoming a “child of your Father which is in heaven” (Matthew 5:45). It is about internal moral transformation rather than external results. Rather, enemy-love is a requirement that embodies the virtue of righteousness as a practice of love that is not limited by the boundaries of in-groups or the threat of harm. It focuses on the inherent dignity of all persons, including enemies, and calls for a commitment to their flourishing, even when it requires great sacrifice.

In conclusion, enemy-love poses a radical challenge to our natural tendencies of self-preservation, in-group bias, and the desire for reciprocity. Yet, its difficulty does not negate its importance. Rather, it is precisely because of the complexity and counterintuitive nature of this command that it represents a deeper form of moral strength: it calls for a transformation of character, a commitment to love and justice that extends to all, and a willingness to act virtuously even in the face of adversity. The difficulty lies in navigating the tension between confronting harm and overcoming resentment, but the biblical demand to love enemies offers a vision of moral strength that seeks to move beyond mere survival or retaliation to embody the care and compassion of divine love.
