**The inner contradiction is hypocrisy: blessing God the Father while cursing human beings made in His image/similitude—from the same mouth.**

James 3:9–10 exposes the incoherence of treating the Creator with praise and the creature (who bears the Creator’s likeness) with verbal destruction. The logic is direct: if men are “made after the similitude of God,” then cursing them is not merely unkind speech; it is a practical repudiation of the very image one has just professed to honor. The same faculty (the tongue) is used for opposite orientations—worship and contempt—revealing a divided heart that has not been integrated by the wisdom from above (cf. James 3:13–18). “These things ought not so to be” is James’s blunt moral judgment on that fracture of integrity.

Speech unmasks moral disorder so quickly for several interlocking reasons already developed in the chapter and the wider biblical anthropology James assumes:

- The tongue is deeply connected to the inner man. What comes out of the mouth proceeds from the heart (see Jesus’ teaching in Matthew 12:34–37 and 15:18, which James echoes in substance). Words are therefore among the least-mediated, most spontaneous revelations of what the will and affections actually love or hate.
- The tongue is small yet potent and hard to govern (James 3:2–8: bit, rudder, spark/fire, untamable animal). Because it is so difficult to bridle, it quickly exposes the residual “double-mindedness” or lack of self-mastery that still operates even in those who claim to bless God.
- Blessing and cursing are speech-acts with inherent moral charge. They do not merely report internal states; they enact honor or dishonor. When the same mouth flips from doxology to verbal assault on image-bearers, the inconsistency is public, audible, and immediate—far harder to rationalize away than private thoughts or delayed actions.
- This disorder strikes at the relational and creational order itself. Cursing those made in God’s likeness is a practical denial of the unity of creation and redemption that James elsewhere insists upon (faith apart from works is dead; pure religion visits the vulnerable; partiality is sin). The mouth becomes the first and most obvious site where that denial erupts.

In short, the passage diagnoses a heart that has not yet been made single. Speech reveals the disorder so swiftly because the tongue is both an instrument of creation/worship and a naked conduit of whatever loves and hates still compete within the person. The remedy James points toward is not mere verbal restraint but the wisdom that is pure, peaceable, gentle, full of mercy—producing consistency between what the mouth says to God and what it says about those who bear His image.
