This is an excellent question that gets to the heart of Paul’s ethical vision. The proximity of verses 31 and 32 is no accident; they are set in direct opposition to show a clear *before and after* of the Christian life.

Here is a breakdown of why they are placed directly against each other, and what the text reveals about the nature of bitterness.

### 1. Why are they placed directly against each other?

The text presents a stark **either/or**. There is no middle ground. Paul is not saying, "Try to be a little bit kind while you hold onto your bitterness." He is saying: **You cannot have both.** They are mutually exclusive spiritual states.

The logic is simple and powerful:
- **Verse 31:** A list of things to **put away** (bitterness, wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, malice). These are the toxins of a broken relationship.
- **Verse 32:** A list of things to **put on** (kindness, tenderheartedness, forgiveness). These are the remedies of a restored relationship.

The hinge between them is the phrase **“even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you.”**

- **Bitterness is the human response to being wronged.**
- **Forgiveness is the divine response to being wronged.**

By placing them back-to-back, Paul forces the reader to choose: will you respond with the currency of this fallen world (bitterness) or with the currency of the Kingdom of God (forgiveness)? The command to forgive is not a suggestion; it is the only logical response for someone who has already been forgiven an infinite debt by God.

### 2. What is bitterness: a feeling, a habit, a chosen posture, or something contagious?

Based on this text and the broader biblical context, the answer is: **All of the above, but most fundamentally, a chosen posture that becomes a contagious habit.**

Here is how the text defines each layer:

- **A Chosen Posture (The Root):** The verb is **"put away"** (Greek: *arthētō*). This is an active, volitional command. You don't "feel" bitterness away; you **decide** to lay it down. Bitterness begins as a **decision to nurse a wound**. It is the choice to hold onto a record of wrongs, to rehearse the offense, and to refuse to release the debt. This is why it is listed alongside "wrath" and "anger"—it is a settled disposition, not a fleeting emotion.

- **A Habit (The Fruit):** Paul doesn't stop at "bitterness." He shows its inevitable progression. Bitterness leads to **wrath** (explosive outbursts), **anger** (simmering resentment), **clamour** (shouting, arguing), and **evil speaking** (slander, gossip). This is a chain reaction. Bitterness is the root; these actions are the fruit. It becomes a deeply ingrained habit of thought and speech.

- **Something Contagious (The Spread):** The word **"malice"** (Greek: *kakia*) at the end of verse 31 is key. It means "badness" or "ill-will" that seeks to spread. Bitterness is not private. It is a spiritual poison that inevitably infects a community. When one person is bitter, their "clamour" and "evil speaking" recruit others to take sides. It spreads like yeast through a loaf, corrupting the entire fellowship. In the body of Christ, bitterness is more dangerous than a physical disease because it attacks the spiritual immune system of the church.

- **A Feeling? (The Soil):** It is *not* just a feeling. Feelings of hurt or injustice are not sin. Jesus felt betrayed. Paul felt abandoned. The sin is not the initial sting of pain; it is what you **do** with that pain. Bitterness is the **cultivation** of that hurt into a grudge. It is the feeling that has been watered, fertilized, and allowed to take over the garden of your soul.

### The "Contagious" Contrast

Notice the perfect parallel in verse 32:

| **Bitterness (v. 31)** | **Forgiveness (v. 32)** |
| :--- | :--- |
| Chosen to hold a debt | Chosen to release a debt |
| Hardens the heart | **Tenderhearted** (softens the heart) |
| Produces **clamour** & evil speaking | Produces **kindness** & gentle words |
| **Contagious** (spreads malice) | **Contagious** (spreads grace) |

### Conclusion

The text treats bitterness as the **active, chosen refusal to extend the same grace you have received.** It is not a passive victimhood but an active spiritual decision. Because it is a decision, it can be *un-chosen*. The command is simple: **Stop.** Put it away.

The placement is so direct because the choice is so critical. You cannot be bitter and forgiving at the same time, just as you cannot be dead and alive at the same time. You must be one or the other. And the Christian’s identity is defined not by the wrongs they have suffered, but by the forgiveness they have received in Christ.
