The elder brother’s deepest problem is best described as a combination of **Pride in Obedience** and **Wounded Fairness**, but they are both rooted in an inability to understand and accept God's grace.

While his anger (verse 28) and envy (desiring what the younger brother received) are visible symptoms, the theological and spiritual diagnosis is found in his mindset regarding merit versus mercy.

**1. Pride in Obedience (The Root)**
This is not merely "being good." It is a belief that his moral record entitles him to greater privilege than the one who has sinned against him. His argument in verse 29 hinges entirely on his own performance: *"Neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment."* He views his relationship with the Father as a transaction. In his eyes, he paid the price (service) and got nothing; therefore, the son who spent it all should not be given a reward (the calf). This legalistic view of obedience ("I was righteous") is what separates him from the younger brother's experience ("I found restoration").

**2. Wounded Fairness (The Mechanism)**
This feeling drives his reaction. He sees the scenario through a lens of "justice," not "love." To him, the Father’s love for the prodigal son appeared like favoritism or injustice toward their older, faithful servant. He feels that the world is unfair to him precisely because he is *too* good compared to the other brother.

**3. The Discomfort with Mercy (The Consequence)**
This leads into his inability to understand why a family celebration is appropriate. For a son who has repented, joy is fitting because it restores what was lost. For an elder brother who views himself as the "good" one, joy in forgiveness feels like a threat to his standing. He cannot accept that God's mercy (the reason for the feast) outweighs his own sense of merit.

### Summary
The **deepest problem** is **Pride in Obedience** (Self-Righteousness). It is not simply anger or envy, because those would imply he wanted the reward but felt unloved. Instead, he demands that his service be rewarded *more* than the sin of a brother who has come back. The Father responds by redirecting him from a perspective of "what I deserve" ($\text{I}$) to a perspective of "who I am in relation to Him" (Father: *"Son, thou art ever with me..."*).

The older brother fails because he cannot celebrate the Father's act of **Redemption** without measuring his own worthiness against it.
