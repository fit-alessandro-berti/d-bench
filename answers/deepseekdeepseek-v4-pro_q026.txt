The passage from Matthew 12:34–35 presents a direct, organic connection between speech and the hidden moral condition of the heart. Jesus uses the metaphor of a “treasure” (θησαυρός) stored up within, from which a person “brings forth” words. The heart is depicted as a repository—a kind of moral stockpile—that overflows into speech. Just as a tree produces fruit according to its kind, so the mouth naturally produces words that correspond to the inner abundance of the heart. An evil heart cannot generate genuinely good speech, and a good heart will not produce evil speech. The link is not merely occasional or superficial; it is a necessary outflow.

As for whether words are **symptoms, choices, revelations, or all three**, the passage and its immediate context support all three dimensions working together:

**1. Words as Symptoms**  
Jesus asks, “How can ye, being evil, speak good things?” The rhetorical question implies impossibility. Speech becomes a diagnostic sign—an involuntary symptom—of the heart’s true condition. Like a bad tree inevitably yielding bad fruit, the “generation of vipers” reveals its venomous nature through its words. This symptomatic quality means that sustained patterns of speech (blasphemy, slander, deceit) are not accidents; they are indicators of a deeper spiritual disease.

**2. Words as Choices**  
The verb “bringeth forth” (ἐκβάλλει) suggests an active, deliberate production. A person draws from their treasury and chooses what to present. While the treasury itself (the heart) determines the available resources, the act of speaking involves volition. The Pharisees chose to accuse Jesus of casting out demons by Beelzebul—a willful utterance that reflected their inner evil. Thus, speakers are morally responsible for their words precisely because they are choosing to bring forth what is already within.

**3. Words as Revelations**  
The phrase “out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh” indicates that speech makes visible what is otherwise hidden. The heart’s treasure—good or evil—is a concealed stockpile. Words are the public disclosure of that private moral wealth. In the larger context, Jesus is unmasking the Pharisees’ inward corruption: their blasphemous words against the Spirit reveal their true hostility toward God, despite any outward piety. Speech, then, is a revelation of character that cannot be permanently disguised.

**All Three Intertwined**  
The passage does not isolate these categories. Instead, it weaves them into a unified picture: the heart’s moral stockpile (whether “good treasure” or “evil treasure”) manifests through **chosen** words that serve as **symptoms** of inner health or decay, and in doing so **reveal** a person’s true moral and spiritual identity. Speech is therefore both an act of the will and a natural emanation, simultaneously a conscious expression and an involuntary disclosure. This is why Jesus can say that on the day of judgment, “by your words you will be justified, and by your words you will be condemned” (Matthew 12:37)—because words are the overflow that proves the treasure, and that treasure is ultimately what God judges.
