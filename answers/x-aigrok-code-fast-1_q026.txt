### Interpretation of the Passage
Matthew 12:34–35 uses metaphorical language to describe the human heart as a "treasure" or inner storehouse of moral qualities. Jesus contrasts the "good man" whose heart produces "good things" (praiseworthy actions or words) with the "evil man" whose heart yields "evil things." The key idea is that speech ("the mouth speaketh") naturally flows from an overflow ("abundance") of what's internally accumulated, revealing the person's true character. This builds on the preceding context where Jesus accuses his critics of hypocrisy, emphasizing how outward behavior reflects inner moral corruption, much like venomous snakes ("generation of vipers") disgorging poison.

### Connection Between Speech and Hidden Moral Stockpiles
The passage directly links words to the heart's "treasure," portraying speech as an inevitable outflow of accumulated internal states. "Good" speech arises from a heart stocked with virtue, while "evil" speech (like slander or malice) bubbles up from a corrupted reservoir. This suggests that words are not random or superficial but diagnostic of deeper, often concealed moral reserves: what you say exposes the "treasure" you've built up through thoughts, priorities, and actions. In broader New Testament theology (e.g., echoing Proverbs 23:7: "For as he thinketh in his heart, so is he"), the heart isn't just emotional but encompasses the will, conscience, and core identity—making speech a window into unseeable stockpiles of righteousness or wickedness.

### Words as Symptoms, Choices, Revelations, or All Three?
The passage predominantly positions words as **symptoms** and **revelations** of the heart's state, with some implicit allowance for choice that isn't explicitly highlighted.

- **Symptoms**: Speech is depicted as a symptom of underlying inner health, much like physical illness manifesting outwardly. The heart's "abundance" causes words to emerge involuntarily ("bringeth forth"), indicating that evil or good talk isn't controlled but reflexively reflects imbalance in the moral "treasure." This aligns with Jesus' hyperbolic point that hypocrites can't "speak good things" despite efforts, because their corrupted core dictates otherwise.
  
- **Revelations**: Words act as revelations exposing hidden stockpiles. By analyzing speech, one discerns the heart's true nature—good men reveal virtue through their words, evil ones betray wickedness. The proverb-like structure emphasizes diagnostic revelation: speech doesn't build the treasure but unveils it, serving as evidence in moral judgment (e.g., Luke 6:43–45 parallels this with trees and fruit).

- **Choices**: While not primary, choice lurks in the intentionality of speaking. People can choose to suppress or express words, yet the passage implies that unchecked outflow leads to "evil things," suggesting choices in guarding the heart (e.g., via prayer, Proverbs 4:23) prevent deviant speech. However, it doesn't frame speech as a free, conscious selection independent of the heart's stockpile; evil utterances happen because the reserves are "evil," implying habitual choices that create the treasure in the first place.

Overall, the passage suggests words are **all three**, but symptoms and revelations are the core, with choice functioning more as a contributing factor to the heart's formation than to speech itself. This encourages self-examination: if your words reveal "evil treasure," it calls for transformative heart-work to restock with goodness. For deeper study, compare with James 3:1–12, which expands on speech's power and control as a "fire ignited by hell."
