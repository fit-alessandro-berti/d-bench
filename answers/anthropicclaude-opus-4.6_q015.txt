# The Mote and the Beam: Image and Argument

## The Power of the Image

The passage's force comes from **absurd disproportion**. Jesus places two objects side by side:

- A **mote** (κάρφος): a speck of dust, a tiny splinter
- A **beam** (δοκός): a massive timber, a structural log used in roofing

The image is deliberately comic and grotesque. One pictures a person squinting with surgical precision at a fleck in someone else's eye while an entire construction beam protrudes from their own face. The physical impossibility is the point: the critic literally **cannot see clearly** to perform the delicate operation they propose. The visual absurdity makes the moral absurdity instantly felt rather than merely argued. No syllogism could achieve what this single picture accomplishes.

The sharpness also comes from the **dramatic staging**. Jesus does not describe hypocrisy abstractly. He scripts an actual scene — one person approaching another, offering help ("Let me pull out the mote"), all while oblivious to their own grotesque condition. The audience sees the hypocrite in action, which makes self-recognition inescapable.

## How It Preserves Moral Correction

This is the most commonly misread aspect of the passage. Jesus does **not** say:

- "Ignore the mote in your brother's eye."
- "Never judge."
- "Moral correction is forbidden."

He says **"first… and then."** The word *then* (τότε) is crucial. It establishes a **sequence**, not a prohibition:

1. **First** — examine and remove your own greater fault
2. **Then** — you will "see clearly" to help your brother

The passage thus makes three simultaneous moves:

- It **condemns** the hypocrisy of correcting others while ignoring one's own worse failings
- It **affirms** that the mote in the brother's eye is real and genuinely needs removing
- It **demands** that the person who would help must earn moral clarity through honest self-examination first

## The Deeper Logic

The critique is not that moral correction is arrogant in principle but that **unexamined moral correction is blind in practice**. The beam does not merely make someone a hypocrite; it literally obstructs their vision. A person who has not confronted their own faults lacks the perceptiveness, humility, and gentleness required to help someone else with theirs.

The passage thus transforms moral correction from an act of **superiority** into an act of **solidarity**: one flawed person, having honestly faced their own condition, gently helping another. The sequence "first… then" turns judgment into a discipline — something earned through self-knowledge rather than assumed through self-righteousness.
