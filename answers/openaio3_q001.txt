A close look at the Hebrew wording shows that the scene is built around three connected pictures.

1. A beast lying in ambush  
   • “Sin (ḥaṭṭāʾt) is crouching (rōbēṣ) at the door.”  
     The participle rōbēṣ is the verb used for a lion or leopard flattened on the ground just before it springs (Job 38:40; Gen 49:9). The writer therefore visualises evil not as an abstract idea but as a live animal squatting on Cain’s threshold, waiting for the first crack in the door.

2. A would-be master  
   • “Its desire is for you” (ʾēlēḵā tǝšūqātô).  
     The clause is the mirror-image of Gen 3:16 (“your desire shall be for your husband, but he shall rule over you”). Just as the woman longs to attach herself to the man, the “beast” of sin longs to attach itself to Cain—in other words, to dominate him.

3. A rival master—Cain himself  
   • “But you must (or ‘can’) rule over it” (wǝʾattāh timšāl-bô).  
     The verb māšal means to govern, to hold sway. YHWH tells Cain that mastery is actually his assignment: the beast can be kept on a leash if he exercises the authority that is his.

Taken together the text offers this portrait of moral agency:

• Evil is pictured primarily as an external, predatory force—something like a lion at the threshold—not as an inborn defect or a settled habit that has already fused with Cain’s personality.  
• At the same time the beast’s “desire” means it seeks inward access; if the door is opened, it will move inside and take possession.  
• The decisive factor is the human will. Cain is neither forced to open the door nor predestined to be devoured. The rhetorical question “If you do well, will there not be a lifting up (nǝśēʾ)?” implies he is perfectly capable of “doing well.” Responsibility therefore rests squarely on him: he is expected to master the encroaching power instead of yielding to it.

To the specific options in your question:

• Impulse?  – only secondarily; the primary image is external.  
• Beast?     – yes, explicitly (“crouching” animal).  
• Habit?     – not yet; habit would be the result if Cain lets the animal in.  
• Temptation? – yes; the beast represents the tempting, stalking presence of sin.  

Thus Genesis 4:6-7 anchors human moral responsibility in the power to choose: evil may be strong and aggressive, but it is still resistible, and the obligation to resist lies squarely with the person who stands at the door.
