The command in Matthew 5:44–45 to love one’s enemies is arguably the most radical and counterintuitive ethical demand in human history. It is difficult to take seriously because it directly contradicts our evolutionary wiring, our psychological instincts, and our societal norms. 

To understand why this command is so jarring—and to answer whether it is sentimental, strategic, or a marker of moral strength—we must break down the mechanics of "enemy-love."

---

### Why is Enemy-Love So Difficult to Take Seriously?

1. **Evolutionary and Biological Wiring:** Humans are biologically hardwired for tribalism, self-preservation, and reciprocity. The evolutionary rule is *tit-for-tat*: reward allies, punish enemies. "Eye for an eye" makes sense to the survival instinct; "bless those who curse you" feels like a survival threat.
2. **The Demand for Justice:** We possess an innate, deeply ingrained sense of fairness. When we or the innocent are wronged, our moral compass demands restitution or retribution. Loving the perpetrator feels like a betrayal of the victim and a violation of justice.
3. **Cultural Definitions of Power:** Society equates power with the ability to dominate, coerce, or destroy an opponent. Jesus redefines power as the capacity to absorb harm and respond with creative, life-giving grace. To the worldly mind, this looks like weakness.
4. **Confusing Love with Approval:** We struggle to take the command seriously because we mistakenly believe that "loving" an enemy means endorsing their actions, minimizing the harm they caused, or abandoning boundaries. 

---

### Is it Sentimental?
**No. It is the exact opposite of sentimentality.**

Sentimentality relies on fuzzy emotions, naive optimism, and a denial of reality. It assumes that deep down, "everyone is nice" or that evil is just a misunderstanding. 

Jesus’ command is rooted in the Greek word ***agape***, which refers to a deliberate act of the will, a commitment to seek the ultimate good of the other, regardless of how one *feels*. 
* Jesus does not say, "Feel warm affection for your abusers." He recognizes the brutal reality of the world: people will curse you, hate you, and persecute you. 
* He prescribes **actions**, not emotions: *do* good, *bless*, *pray*. 
* True enemy-love requires looking at the full, ugly reality of an enemy's malice and choosing to will their redemption rather than their destruction. It is a gritty, painful discipline, not a Hallmark greeting.

---

### Is it Strategic?
**Yes, but strategy is a byproduct, not the primary motive.**

From a pragmatic standpoint, enemy-love is a brilliant mechanism for breaking cycles of violence. As Martin Luther King Jr. (heavily influenced by this exact text) famously noted: *"Returning hate for hate multiplies hate, adding deeper darkness to a night already devoid of stars... Hate cannot drive out hate; only love can do that."*

* **Disarming the Oppressor:** Retaliation validates the enemy's worldview; it proves to them that you are just like them. Responding to hatred with unmerited grace creates cognitive dissonance. It strips the enemy of their moral justification and forces them to confront the humanity of the victim.
* **Preserving the Self:** Strategically, hatred is a poison you drink expecting the other person to die. Refusing to hate protects the victim's soul from becoming a mirror image of the oppressor.

However, if enemy-love is *purely* strategic (e.g., "I will love you so you will feel guilty and give me what I want"), it devolves into manipulation. In the Sermon on the Mount, the strategy of peace is a result of the love, not the selfish motive behind it.

---

### Is it a Deeper Form of Moral Strength?
**Yes. It is the ultimate expression of moral and spiritual strength.**

It requires very little strength to pull a trigger, throw a punch, or type a vindictive insult. Reacting to harm with harm is easy; it is the default setting of a fallen world. It takes profound, almost superhuman self-mastery to absorb an unjust blow and refuse to pass that trauma onto someone else.

Jesus provides the theological rationale for this strength in verse 45: *"That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven: for he maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good..."*

1. **Imitating Divine "Common Grace":** God does not withhold the basic necessities of life (sun and rain) from people just because they are wicked. God’s love is not contingent on human merit. To love an enemy is to align oneself with the very nature of the Creator. It is an act of participating in the divine life.
2. **Separating the Sinner from the Sin:** Moral strength allows a person to hate the *evil* being perpetrated while simultaneously recognizing the inherent dignity of the *person* perpetrating it, as they too are a bearer of the Divine Image (Imago Dei), however deeply distorted.
3. **True Freedom:** If your actions are dictated by your enemy's actions (they hit, you hit back; they curse, you curse), you are a slave to your enemy. Enemy-love is the ultimate declaration of moral independence. It says: *"Your evil will not dictate my goodness. You do not have the power to force me to become hateful."*

### Summary
Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it demands the death of the ego. It is **not sentimental**, because it confronts evil head-on without requiring warm feelings. It is **strategically profound**, as it is the only proven method for severing the endless chain of human vengeance. But ultimately, it is a **deeper form of moral strength**—the strength to act as a conduit of divine grace in a world that operates on the mechanics of retribution.
