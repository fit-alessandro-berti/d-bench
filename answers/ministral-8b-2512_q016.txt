The elder brother’s deepest problem in this parable is **a distorted sense of identity and relationship with his father**, rooted in **pride in his own righteousness and a refusal to recognize the father’s unconditional love and grace**. His issues are not primarily anger (though that is visible), envy (though present), or wounded fairness (though he feels cheated). Instead, the core issue is **a failure to see himself as fully embraced by the father’s love**—he believes his worth is tied to his performance rather than his identity as a beloved son.

### Breaking Down the Elder Brother’s Problem:
1. **Pride in Obedience (Not Humility in Grace)**
   - He boasts, *“I have always obeyed you”* (v. 29), as if his worth is earned by his service. This reveals a **self-righteousness**—he doesn’t see himself as a recipient of the father’s love but as a "good employee" who deserves recognition.
   - His obedience is not rooted in love but in **performance**, which is why he resents the father’s generosity toward his brother. He doesn’t understand that the father’s love is **not transactional**—it’s given freely, even to the prodigal.

2. **Discomfort with Mercy Itself (Not Just Envy or Fairness)**
   - His anger isn’t just about being "cheated" (though he feels that) or about his brother’s sin (though he condemns it). The deeper issue is that he **cannot accept the father’s mercy as a gift**—he wants love to be earned, not freely given.
   - When the father says, *“All that I have is thine”* (v. 31), the elder brother doesn’t hear it as an invitation to **joyful participation in the father’s love** but as an insult to his own self-sufficiency. He doesn’t see himself as a son who can **rest in the father’s goodness** without proving his worth.

3. **A False Sense of Self-Worth**
   - His identity is tied to his role as the "responsible" son, not as a beloved child. He doesn’t see himself as **equally loved** by the father—only as the one who "deserves" the father’s attention.
   - His refusal to enter the feast isn’t just about anger; it’s a **rejection of the father’s offer of belonging**. He would rather stand outside, judging, than accept that he is **already included in the father’s love**.

### Why Not the Other Options?
- **Anger alone** is a symptom, not the root. His anger stems from feeling **unseen and unvalued** by the father.
- **Envy** is present, but it’s secondary to his deeper **inability to receive love freely**.
- **Wounded fairness** is a surface issue—he feels the father has "favored" his brother, but his real problem is that he **doesn’t believe he is already favored**.
- **Discomfort with mercy** is the most accurate, but even that is a symptom of his **pride in self-sufficiency**. He can’t accept that love is **not earned but given**.

### The Parable’s Point:
The elder brother represents **the religious or self-righteous person who resists grace** because they believe their worth is tied to their performance. Jesus contrasts his **exclusionary pride** with the father’s **inclusive, unconditional love**—a love that seeks the lost (the prodigal) **and** the "righteous" (the elder brother) alike.

**Final Answer:**
The elder brother’s deepest problem is **pride in his own righteousness and an inability to receive the father’s love as a free gift**, rather than seeing himself as fully embraced by it. His discomfort is not just with the prodigal’s sin or the father’s mercy—it’s with **the idea that love is not earned but given**, and that he is already included in the father’s joy.
