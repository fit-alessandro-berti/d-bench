In Matthew 12:34–35, Jesus draws a direct, unbreakable line between the hidden interior of a person (the heart) and their external output (speech). He uses two powerful metaphors—**abundance** and **treasure**—to explain this connection. 

To answer your specific question: **Yes, the passage suggests that words are all three—symptoms, choices, and revelations—operating in a continuous, interdependent cycle.** 

Here is a breakdown of how the passage connects speech to hidden moral stockpiles, and how it encompasses all three categories.

### 1. The Connection: The Heart as a Hidden Treasury
The "heart" in Hebrew thought is not merely the seat of emotions, but the control center of the mind, will, and moral character. Jesus calls this inner life a "treasure" (Greek: *thesauros*). A treasure is something accumulated, stored up, and hidden from plain sight. 

The connection between this hidden treasure and speech is one of **inevitable overflow**. Jesus says speech comes "out of the abundance of the heart." Just as a well can only bring up the water that fills it, and a jar can only pour out the liquid stored inside it, the mouth can only speak from the moral stockpile that fills the heart. If the hidden treasury is stocked with evil, the mouth will inevitably produce evil; if stocked with good, it will produce good. 

### 2. Are Words Symptoms, Choices, or Revelations?

#### A. Words as Symptoms (Diagnostic)
When Jesus asks, "How can ye, being evil, speak good things?" He is treating speech as a **symptom** of an underlying moral condition. In medicine, a symptom doesn't cause a disease; it indicates its presence. Similarly, evil speech does not make the heart evil; rather, it is the visible indicator of an already corrupted heart. The "abundance" of evil inside eventually manifests as a symptom on the lips. Just as a doctor looks at symptoms to diagnose an illness, Jesus uses speech to diagnose the spiritual condition of the Pharisees.

#### B. Words as Revelations (Exposive)
The metaphor of the "treasure" implies that the heart is hidden, but speech is the act of bringing that treasure into the open. Therefore, words are **revelations**. The mouth acts as the spigot to the underground reservoir. What a person says reveals what they have been hoarding in their heart. This is why Jesus calls the Pharisees a "generation of vipers"—their speech revealed the venomous, murderous stockpile they had hidden within. Words unveil the secrets of the soul; they make the invisible visible.

#### C. Words as Choices (Volitional)
While the *nature* of the speech is determined by the treasure, the *act* of speaking is a choice. Jesus says a man "bringeth forth" (Greek: *ekballo*—to cast out, to bring out) from his treasure. This is an active, volitional verb. A person chooses to open their mouth and speak. However, the passage places a strict boundary on human autonomy: **you can only choose from what you have.** An evil man cannot choose to speak good things, because he has no good treasure to draw from. Therefore, words are choices, but they are choices constrained by the moral inventory of the heart. 

### Synthesis: How They Work Together
The passage presents a dynamic, three-step reality of human speech:

1.  **Accumulation (The Hidden Stockpile):** Over time, a person fills their heart with either good or evil through their beliefs, desires, and choices.
2.  **Revelation (The Overflow):** When the heart reaches "abundance," the pressure of that hidden treasure forces it out through speech. The mouth reveals what the heart has been hoarding.
3.  **Symptom & Choice (The Output):** The spoken word acts as a symptom of the heart's condition, while simultaneously being a volitional act of drawing from the available stockpile. 

Ultimately, Jesus' point is that speech cannot be fixed in isolation. Because words are the overflow of the heart's hidden treasury, the only way to change the output (speech) is to change the inventory (the heart). As He says later in the same discourse, out of the heart proceed the things that defile a person.
