Luke 6:37 is not a command to become morally indifferent. It is a warning against assuming the position of a final judge over other people, especially from a posture of self-righteousness.

The context is important. Jesus has just commanded love for enemies, blessing those who curse, generosity, and mercy: “Be ye therefore merciful, as your Father also is merciful” (Luke 6:36). The next verses concern the measure we use for others and the hypocrisy of noticing a speck in another’s eye while ignoring a log in our own (6:38, 41–42). Thus, “judge not” does not mean “never recognize good and evil.” It means, among other things, do not turn moral assessment into merciless condemnation.

### Discernment

Discernment asks questions such as:

- What actually happened?
- Was it harmful or unjust?
- What responsibility does this person have?
- What boundaries or consequences are necessary?
- What response would protect the vulnerable and serve restoration?

Discernment can require moral clarity. A person may need to say, “That action was deceitful,” “This relationship is unsafe,” or “Someone must be held accountable.” Parents, communities, and courts cannot function without making judgments of this kind. Even Jesus teaches people to recognize false prophets by their fruit, and he criticizes hypocrisy plainly.

Such judgments should be:

- based on truth rather than rumor or appearance;
- proportionate rather than vindictive;
- open to correction;
- aware of one’s own capacity for sin;
- aimed, where possible, at repentance, protection, and restoration.

Discernment evaluates an action or situation. It does not presume omniscience about a person’s whole soul or eternal destiny.

### The appetite to condemn

Condemnation, in the sense Jesus warns against, is more than identifying wrongdoing. It is the desire to pronounce a person worthless, beyond mercy, or fit for contempt. It often includes:

- delight in exposing another’s failure;
- applying strict standards to others and lenient ones to oneself;
- reducing someone to their worst act;
- assuming motives without sufficient evidence;
- seeking humiliation rather than justice;
- using another’s sin to establish one’s own superiority.

A person can make a correct moral judgment with a wrong spirit. “What happened was wrong” may be true, while “therefore this person is beneath mercy” is a form of condemnation. Jesus’ concern is not only whether our conclusion is accurate, but what kind of heart is producing it and what our judgment is trying to accomplish.

### Forgiveness and accountability

To forgive is not to declare that the wrong did not matter. Nor does it require abandoning boundaries, reporting abuse, or removing legitimate consequences. Forgiveness means relinquishing personal vengeance and refusing to make hatred the ruling principle. Justice may still be necessary; trust may need to be rebuilt slowly; reconciliation may require repentance and change.

Thus someone can say:

> “This was genuinely wrong. It caused real harm. There must be consequences. And I will not treat this person as beyond the reach of God’s mercy, nor will I nourish hatred toward them.”

That is different from saying:

> “This person’s failure proves that they are irredeemable, and I am glad to see them shamed.”

The first is moral discernment joined to mercy. The second is the appetite to condemn.

“Judge not” therefore does not abolish moral judgment. It places moral judgment under humility, mercy, and self-examination. It reminds us that we are not God, that our knowledge is partial, that we too stand in need of forgiveness, and that the purpose of seeing clearly is not to enjoy another’s downfall but to respond truthfully and mercifully.
