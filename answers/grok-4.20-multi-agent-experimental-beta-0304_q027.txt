**The Pharisee is condemned primarily by pride/self-righteousness ("trusted in themselves that they were righteous") and contempt ("despised others"), as Jesus states explicitly in the setup (v. 9).** These are expressed through negative comparison ("not as other men... or even as this publican"), contempt for sinners, and treating his genuine religious acts (extra fasting, meticulous tithing) as self-display and self-justification rather than grateful response to God. The prayer is essentially a self-congratulatory monologue ("prayed thus with himself") that uses virtue performatively to elevate himself and demean his neighbor. It is not mainly "false doctrine" in the narrow sense—his described practices were not fabricated, and the text does not correct the external acts themselves. The deeper error is the heart: reliance on personal merit and works rather than God's mercy. This is classic self-righteousness/legalism in attitude, even when the outward forms have biblical precedent.

The tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer for the opposite reasons: radical honesty and humility before a holy God. He stands "afar off," refuses even to lift his eyes, beats his breast (a gesture of deep contrition and mourning), and offers a simple, unadorned plea: "God be merciful to me a sinner." There are no comparisons, no résumé of good deeds, no self-exaltation. He acknowledges reality—all have sinned—and casts himself entirely on divine mercy (the Greek *hilasthēti* carries overtones of propitiation/atonement). Jesus declares that *this* man "went down to his house justified rather than the other," meaning he was declared righteous by God. This aligns with the consistent biblical pattern: God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble (Prov 3:34; James 4:6; Ps 51:17; the broader teaching in Romans 3:9–20 and the Prodigal Son parable).

The parable is not a general attack on fasting, tithing, or moral effort. It is a warning against trusting those things *instead of* mercy, and against using them to look down on others. The Pharisee leaves with his self-image intact but unjustified; the tax collector leaves broken but reconciled. Humility and repentance open the door to justification by grace; self-righteous comparison and contempt slam it shut. This is why Jesus concludes with the reversal: "everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, but the one who humbles himself will be exalted."
