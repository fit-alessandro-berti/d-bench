In Matthew 6:1, the danger is not visibility itself — it is the *craving* to be seen.

The Greek makes that clear: *pros to theathenai autois* — literally, "with a view to being gazed at by them." *Theathenai* is where we get our word "theater." Jesus is not condemning alms that happen to be seen, but alms done *as a performance*.

We know visibility itself can't be the problem because two chapters earlier, in the same sermon, he says:

> "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works..." - Matthew 5:16

So the contrast between 5:16 and 6:1 is intentional: Same public good works, two completely different moral orientations.

### What makes public goodness dangerous then?

**1. It changes the audience, and therefore changes the reward.**

The act stops being God-directed and becomes man-directed. In the world of the sermon, there are only two audiences that matter: people who can see the outside, and "your Father which seeth in secret" from 6:4 who sees the inside. If you perform for the first audience, you get what they can give — reputation, status, applause — and that *is* the reward. The verse doesn't say God punishes you; it says you have traded a deeper reward for a shallower one.

**2. It corrupts the act itself.**

When the craving to be seen becomes the motive, the person in need ceases to be a neighbor and becomes a prop. Your alms are no longer really *for* them. They are *about* you. Public goodness without love turns charity into self-advertisement.

That is why this verse sits at the start of a triad — alms, prayer, fasting in Matthew 6. All three are good and commanded in first-century Judaism. All three can be theatricalized. Jesus' point isn't to move them into secrecy forever, but to protect sincerity. Give in such a way that even your motive can't easily hijack it — "let not thy left hand know what thy right hand doeth."

**3. It is self-deceptive.**

The danger is subtle because you still *did* the good deed. It is easy to feel righteous while your heart has actually shifted from mercy to vanity. Moral pride can feed on moral behavior — which is more dangerous than obvious vice because it looks like virtue.

So the verse's warning is this: Public goodness becomes morally dangerous when it stops being goodness for the other and becomes goodness for the mirror. Visibility merely reveals the temptation; the craving to be seen is the corruption.
