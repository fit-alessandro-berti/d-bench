Short answer: all three, with an order of causation.

- Source and symptom: Jesus ties speech to an inner “storehouse” (treasure) and to “overflow” (abundance). What habitually fills the heart will spill out in words, especially in unguarded moments. So speech is a reliable symptom of the heart’s moral stockpile.

- Choice and stewardship: “A good man out of the good treasure… brings forth” (draws out). That verb implies agency: people select from what they’ve stored. We are accountable for our words. Yet our choices are shaped and constrained by what we have been treasuring; hence Jesus’ “How can you…?” highlights moral inability apart from heart-change.

- Revelation and verdict: Words disclose the hidden heart to others and before God. In the immediate context (vv. 36–37), Jesus says our words will factor into judgment—because they reveal what we truly are and love.

How the images work together:
- Treasure: a curated stockpile formed by what we love, meditate on, and practice. You will draw out what you’ve stored.
- Abundance/overflow: what fills the heart will inevitably leak into speech; pressure accelerates the leak.
- Fruit (v. 33 context): speech is the fruit that matches the tree; persistent mismatch signals hypocrisy or self-deception, not merely a slip of the tongue.

Implications:
- Reform the source: lasting change in speech requires renovation of the heart (new “treasure”).
- Exercise stewardship: guard the tongue because you are responsible for what you bring out.
- Take words seriously: they both reveal and will be used as evidence of the heart’s condition.

So the passage presents words as symptoms, choices, and revelations—symptoms of what fills the heart, choices from what we’ve stored, and revelations that will stand in judgment.
