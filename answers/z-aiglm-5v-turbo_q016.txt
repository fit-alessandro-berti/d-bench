While the text explicitly mentions **anger** (v. 28), and the dialogue reveals deep **envy**, **wounded fairness**, and **pride**, most theologians and biblical scholars identify the elder brother’s deepest problem as **a fundamental discomfort with mercy (and the grace that underpins it).**

His issue is not merely emotional; it is theological. He has a "mercenary" view of his relationship with his father—one based on earning and deserving, rather than belonging and loving.

Here is an analysis of why **discomfort with mercy** serves as the root from which the other symptoms grow:

### 1. He Operates on a Transactional Economy (Wounded Fairness)
The elder brother’s immediate defense is mathematical. In verse 29, he lists his resume: *"These many years do I serve thee"* and *"neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment."*

He views his life as a ledger sheet. He believes that good behavior equals earned reward.
*   **The Problem:** You cannot understand **mercy** (getting what you don't deserve) if you are obsessed with **fairness** (getting what you earn). A world run strictly on "fairness" leaves no room for the celebration of a repentant sinner. The elder brother is furious because the accounting system he relies on has been overturned by the father's grace.

### 2. He Identifies as a Slave, Not a Son (Pride/Obedience)
Notice the contrast in language:
*   **The Elder Brother says:** "I **serve** (*douleuo* - to slave for) thee."
*   **The Father replies:** "**Son** (*teknon*), thou art ever with me."

The elder brother takes pride in his obedience, but his obedience has made him bitter rather than joyful. He believes he needs to *earn* his place at the table. Because he tries to secure his position through performance, he feels threatened when someone else receives a position through forgiveness. His pride in his own record prevents him from enjoying the father's wealth freely (which the father reminds him in v. 31 is *already* his).

### 3. He Cannot Rejoice in Grace (Discomfort with Mercy)
This is the crux of the parable. When the younger brother returns, the father kills the fatted calf—not because the boy earned it, but simply because he is home.

The elder brother refuses to join the feast not just because he didn't get a goat, but because **the very premise of the feast offends him.** The feast celebrates *resurrection* ("dead, and is alive again"), but the elder brother only sees *injustice*. To join the party would be to validate the principle that love can be unconditional.

If the father is right to celebrate, then the elder brother’s years of slaving were unnecessary for gaining love. That realization is too painful for his ego to bear.

### Summary
While **anger** is the visible outburst and **envy** drives the comparison, the **deepest** problem is **his inability to accept a grace-based relationship.**

*   **The Younger Brother's Sin:** He wanted the father's **stuff** (inheritance) without the **father**.
*   **The Elder Brother's Sin:** He was willing to work for the father, but he didn't actually know or love the **father's heart.** He used his morality to keep people out, while the father uses mercy to bring people in.

The tragedy of the elder brother is that he stands outside the house, arguing about justice, while inside, the feast of mercy is waiting—for both of them.
