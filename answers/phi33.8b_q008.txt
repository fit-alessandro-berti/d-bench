
Let us begin by examining God's Word according to a simple exegesis with the help of context.

The verse, John 4:20 (NIV) 

> “If a man says, ‘I love God,’ but hates his brother, he is a liar: for he that loveth not his brother whom he hath seen, how can he love God whom he hath not seen?”

In context, Jesus goes on to say:

> “I have said this so that you will say, ‘They have only themselves to blame.’ For the flesh profits nothing, but the spirit nothing.

First, this passage is not about physical love. As the next verse explains, Jesus continues:

> “If you were without sin, you would not commit it.”

In other words, he is not addressing a man who simply fails to show love in a specific situation. Rather, he is addressing a man who habitually, systematically rejects or see-saws between loving and hating, being constantly torn between both worlds.

The world of the flesh and the world of the spirit is constantly in conflict, an expression of the ongoing spiritual struggle of being human.

The Greek term for “flesh” (sarx) literally means “flesh,” but often it is used to represent the desires, desires as a way of life and the world of sin (2 Corinthians 7:10). It is the flesh that discredits the truth in the person'semantic flesh” (Jonah 2:1). Christ and His disciples will never be ashamed of their testimony, because their sin is not visible to human eyes nor hidden in their conscience.

Jesus is speaking to a discrediting voice inside men’s hearts, which makes it seem as though to love God is somehow to reject human relationships.

The phrase "I have said this so that you will say" indicates that what had come before is a matter of ongoing debate, and that Jesus is not providing a new rule.

The context of the discussion is the Pharisees and scribes, who had a tendency to look down on those who did not spend so much time on religious practices as on relating to their neighbor that God might manifest Himself in them. They had failed to see "the kingdom of God" as something that would come about in the here and now, and they had misunderstood what Jesus was doing when He told them to sell their possessions and give to the poor. They were also looking down on the women in the community, who played a significant role in Jesus' ministry — especially in the healing of the sick — and they looked down on widows and orphans, people who lived in the faith market.

Jesus' teaching is a reminder that God is not indifferent to their suffering. But Jesus is not arguing that God is incapable of loving us. Rather, He wants to argue that it is our prior and natural commitment to our neighbors that will prove that God is truly in us.

Jesus is not saying that God cannot love us. What He is saying is that God is love.

Let's look back at His words about being blind to the signs of God's presence:

> "And if you only do these things to win a few friends, they will make you enemies. But if you do it to please God, you will be sons of God." (Luke 6:31-32)

In other words, to be the people of God, He calls believers to be His hands and feet in this world.

Let's examine further into these teachings of Jesus concerning love for our neighbor:

> "Blessed are they whose [inner] treasures are not in what the world delights in. If we spend on material needs, we too will find our treasure in heaven. Blessed are those who do good and show kindness." (Luke 16:13)

Jesus speaks of blessing those who refuse to be distracted by worldly desires and instead focus on serving others.

What, then, can we make out of John 4:20 in light of the teachings of Jesus?

It is possible to misuse the passage from a legalistic perspective (as the Pharisees and scribes tended to do) and to view it as a condemnation of one's acts of charity. This is not the correct understanding. Rather, we may understand in a spiritual sense that this is not what our heart has done, and that our actions or inactions are a lie.

The passage is exposing the self-deception of one who is saying the words but not allowing the works of love to follow.

It is this disconnect between saying and doing that Jesus condemns — not the hateful words, but the disconnect in the context of a deep and personal struggle within the heart.

As we see in Matthew 5:43-48 Jesus rebukes the Pharisees and scribes not for judging and condemning others but for failing to follow their own example:

> “You hypocrites! Act accordingly! 4  Just as you hear the saying of the Teacher of the law, You have heard that I told you to love your neighbor as yourself. 5 3  But you are doing many things wrong, looking at the external appearance and hearing only the external appearance. You are pronouncing judgment on your neighbors, so you are doing the very things you despise. 4  Do not lay beside me a broken reed under Myself. 6 3  Do not make my life a burden. 7  When you fast, do not be like the hypocrites of Elijah, who pray for permission to fast in order to appear pious to men. 8  You hypocrites! For indeed, you love the praise that comes from men but dislike the praise that comes from God. 9  Beware of the evildoers; they will wage war against you; do not be overcome by them. 10 4  Your left hand does not know what your right hand is doing. Do not do evil; what you do, do it to the glory of God. And do not be on guard every time a devil, for evil is coming.

This is a rebuke of hypocrisy and not an excuse for condemning acts of kindness and charity.

Jesus is not condemning love for physical needs but using love for spiritual need as the means of demonstrating true love for God. That is, we cannot love God if we do not also love our neighbor in a deeply personal, transformative way.

And in John 4:15 God says:

> “My prayer is for love, brother, so that I may fulfill what my brother has spoken.” 5 1  Your brother has spoken the truth while you have been listening, but your heart is far from the truth.”

Jesus is telling us the opposite of the Pharisees, who said that one must perform ritualistic religious acts to please God. Jesus is saying doing God's will is manifest in love for one's neighbor, not empty religious acts.

We find an answer in John 4:7:

> “There is salvation, true and full, in what Jesus was saying; it comes from hearing his words and doing them.

We find this love manifested in John 4:35:

> “Behold, the hour is coming, and now is, when the true worshippers will worship the Father in spirit and in truth: and such as see the Father in His Son, they themselves also have seen Him; and this will I tell you by a parable:

As we progress through the Fourth Gospel, we also find love's fulfillment in John 6:44:

> “No one can do greater things than this: to eat and be satisfied, because he comes to the Father from the Father.”

Thus, Jesus is declaring that the way to God's fullness is to see His love for us in the love He has shown us in the brokenness of this world. Jesus will feed us spiritually in the holy trinity: Father, Son and Spirit — and this can only happen when we love our brother or sister in need.

Finally, let's see John 5:43, which Jesus says is why He came:

> “I have come for your sake, my joy and your salvation."

As His disciples, our ultimate aim is this salvation, the joy of knowing Him and doing His will. However, this salvation is not apart from love for our earthly neighbors, our neighbors in need.

Let's look at what we can learn from this passage:

We are told love of our neighbor is our first, foremost duty, and we find ourselves blind to the signs of God’s Spirit. The Pharisees and scribes are blind to God’s Spirit in His word and in Jesus as they judge others — and thus, we can too find ourselves blind to God’s Spirit. Yet God’s Spirit (His Holy breath) is in us all, as God does not abandon us until we repent and ask for His forgiveness.

Christ has set before us this simple yet complex task of seeing our neighbors while we seek the salvation of God — His fullness and satisfaction. We will find truth revealed in love, not in dogmatic obedience. We will find this salvation by fulfilling our obligation to be His hands and feet in this world.

A simple but complex truth about Jesus is that God’s love can be seen in the very world of sin and evil. We will find God with us even when the world seems opposed to Him.

As His disciples, we will see God when we see Christ in others, especially in our neighbors in need. This is the fulfillment of God’s promise: "Blessed are those who do good."

Jesus tells us of the "coming" of the hour — meaning these are the times when the blind will see. It is in the face of death that we find God, and we must turn to Him in prayer and faith, not in judgment.

Finally, Jesus is telling us that if we are concerned more with earning God’s favor in this life than in pursuing justice on earth as well, we are in a blind and empty faith.

The true disciple, therefore, is one who first seeks God in the needs of our neighbor. We will see Christ doing our work of love, for God is not only with us in our praying but also in our loving.

Amen.

### Answers to Your Questions: What Is the Meaning of John Ch. 2 Jesus' Death and Resurrection?

Jesus' death and resurrection is the central tenet of Christianity that explains His atonement for our sins. When God declared Jesus dead on the cross, He completed a work that no one could accomplish on their own. Christ paid our debt so that nobody else needed to.

Christians look back with hope as He stood from the dead. As with the prophets in the Old Testament, in Jesus, God will raise up a house with Him as "Redeemer" (2 Chronicles).

In the 10 commandments, we have the first (love God) and the second (love humanity) — the greatest commandment. To fulfill these, love our neighbors in need.

We will find God's love as we seek His salvation — not apart from justice but through it. God’s salvation is in his word and in His truth. God's full life is in the life of Jesus Himself.

May God give us the courage to walk firmly in Him and proclaim His message, and may you find comfort in the hope of heaven ahead and God's invitation for His people to do His will in this life. Amen.
