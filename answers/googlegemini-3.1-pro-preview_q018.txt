The juxtaposition of Ephesians 4:31 and 4:32 is one of the clearest examples in the New Testament of the Apostle Paul’s "put off / put on" theology. He deliberately places bitterness and forgiveness in direct opposition because they represent two entirely incompatible ways of being human, and more specifically, two incompatible ways of responding to injury.

Here is a breakdown of why they are opposed to one another, followed by an examination of how the text treats the nature of "bitterness."

### Part 1: Why Bitterness and Forgiveness are Directly Opposed

**1. The Economy of Debt**
Bitterness and forgiveness operate on entirely opposite economic systems of the heart. Bitterness is the meticulous keeping of accounts. It is born when an offense occurs and a debt is owed, and the bitter heart decides to hold onto the ledger, demanding payment (through revenge, watching the offender suffer, or extracting an apology). Forgiveness (from the Greek *charizomenoi*, sharing the same root as grace, *charis*) is the deliberate cancellation of the debt. You cannot simultaneously harbor a demand for payment (bitterness) and cancel the debt (forgiveness).

**2. The Progression of the Heart**
Look closely at the list in verse 31. It has a sequential, escalating logic: 
*   **Bitterness** (the internal rot) leads to...
*   **Wrath and Anger** (the emotional boiling point) which leads to...
*   **Clamour and Evil speaking** (the external shouting and slander) all wrapped in...
*   **Malice** (the desire to inflict harm).

Verse 32 reverses this trajectory. Kindness and tenderheartedness are the *internal* postures that ultimately culminate in the *action* of forgiveness. Bitterness is the toxic root that produces malice; tenderheartedness is the healthy root that produces forgiveness.

**3. The Theological Mirror**
Paul anchors verse 32 with the ultimate theological logic: "...even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you." Bitterness is essentially a denial of the Gospel. It is the posture of Jesus’ parable of the Unmerciful Servant (Matthew 18), where a man forgiven a multi-million dollar debt by a king goes out and chokes a man who owes him a few dollars. To be bitter is to demand a standard of justice for others that God mercifully suspended for you. Thus, forgiveness eliminates bitterness because the cross renders bitterness entirely hypocritical.

### Part 2: The Nature of Bitterness in the Text

Paul is not treating bitterness merely as a fleeting emotion. The Greek word used here is *pikria*, which denotes a sharp, piercing, poisonous, or deeply resentful spirit. By looking at the commands surrounding it, we can see exactly how the text treats it:

**It is a Chosen Posture (More than a feeling)**
While anger or hurt might begin as an involuntary emotional reaction to being wronged, *bitterness* is the sustained tolerance of that hurt. Paul uses the passive imperative verb: "Let [it] be put away..." (Greek: *arthētō*). This implies an act of the will. You cannot be commanded to fundamentally change a momentary, reflexive emotion, but you *can* be commanded to stop nurturing it. The text treats bitterness as a heavy garment or a piece of trash that the believer must actively choose to lift up and carry out of the house. 

**It is a Fermenting Habit**
In verse 26 of this same chapter, Paul writes, "Be ye angry, and sin not: let not the sun go down upon your wrath." Paul acknowledges that anger is a normal human feeling. However, bitterness is what happens when anger is kept overnight. It is anger that has been allowed to ferment and rot. It becomes a habit of the mind—a rehearsing of the injury over and over until it shapes the very character of the person.

**It is Highly Contagious**
Bitterness rarely stays in isolation. The text shows that it inherently bleeds out into community. Bitterness turns into "clamour" (loud quarreling, making a public scene) and "evil speaking" (slander, whispering to others to ruin the offender's reputation). We see this concept echoed in Hebrews 12:15, which warns of a "root of bitterness springing up" that causes trouble and by which *"many be defiled."* Bitterness is a social contagion; it seeks allies and requires the community to take sides, thereby tearing apart the unity of the church that Paul is striving to build in Ephesians 4.

### Summary
In Ephesians 4:31-32, Paul places bitterness and forgiveness face-to-face because they are the ultimate manifestations of the "Old Self" and the "New Self." 

Bitterness is treated as a **chosen, habituated posture of the hostile heart** that eventually infects community through speech and behavior. Forgiveness is not just an emotional soothing, but a radical, costly choice to absorb the debt of an offense, fueled entirely by the realization that God, through Christ, has already done the exact same thing for us.
