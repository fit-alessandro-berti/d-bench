In Isaiah 1:16–17 the movement is deliberate: it goes from stopping destructive behavior to actively acquiring constructive habits.

1. **“Cease to do evil” = negative change (restraint/repentance).**  
   The command “put away… cease to do evil” targets what is already corrupting the relationship with God and harming others. This is a necessary first step: you can’t “clean up” by keeping the same harmful pattern while just feeling sorry.

2. **“Learn to do well” = positive formation (active practice).**  
   Isaiah then shifts from removal (“cease,” “put away,” “wash”) to formation: *learn*. That implies goodness is not only the absence of wrongdoing; it is **trained, chosen, and practiced**. The parallel terms in the verse make this concrete: seeking judgment, relieving the oppressed, judging the fatherless, pleading for the widow—i.e., justice enacted in specific ways.

3. **What it suggests about goodness:**  
   The text suggests goodness is **more than restraint**. It is **a practiced form of love and justice**, expressed outwardly. In Isaiah’s context, the “religious” problem is not merely moral failure in private; it’s that worship divorced from justice is empty. So God calls for repentance that reorients life toward concrete mercy and fairness.

In short: Isaiah portrays repentance as a two-part process—**stop the harmful pattern** and **learn a new, just way of living**.
