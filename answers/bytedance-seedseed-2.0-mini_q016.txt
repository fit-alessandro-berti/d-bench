Let’s break down each option and the elder brother’s words/actions to identify his deepest root issue:

### 1.  Anger: Eliminated
Anger is the *symptom* of his frustration, not the underlying problem. It is the outward emotion he displays, not the core conflict driving him.
### 2.  Envy: Eliminated
His complaint is not about wanting the fatted calf or the party for himself. He already has access to all his father’s wealth (the father explicitly tells him "all that I have is thine"). His resentment stems from the father prioritizing the prodigal’s return, not a desire for the celebration itself.
### 3.  Wounded fairness: Eliminated
Feeling wronged is the *sensation* he experiences, but not the root cause. He only feels "unfairly treated" because he believes his obedience entitles him to special honor—this wounded fairness flows from his pride, rather than being the deep problem itself.
### 4.  Pride in obedience: Correct
The elder brother leads his rebuke by highlighting his lifelong, perfect service: *"these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment"*. He views his obedience as a moral claim on his father’s favor, and resents that the father extends unconditional mercy to the prodigal, who squandered his inheritance and sinned openly. This is his core failing: he trusts in his own righteous works rather than accepting that his father’s love is freely given, not earned.
### 5.  Discomfort with mercy itself: Eliminated
He does not reject mercy as a concept—instead, he argues the prodigal does not *deserve* mercy. His objection is to mercy being shown to someone he sees as unworthy, not to mercy as a practice. This aligns with his pride: he cannot accept that grace applies to those who have not earned it through obedience.

---
The elder brother’s deepest problem is pride in obedience.
