### What Condemns the Pharisee

The parable condemns the Pharisee primarily for his **pride** and self-righteousness, which manifests in several interconnected ways reflected in the options you listed. It's not about false doctrine—the Pharisee's words don't contradict biblical teaching (e.g., fasting and tithing are valid practices in Jewish law). Instead, the issue is his heart attitude:

- **Pride as Self-Display**: He uses his virtues (fasting twice a week and tithing everything) to portray himself as superior and deserving of God's favor. This turns prayer into a boastful performance, effectively worshipping his own righteousness rather than seeking God humbly. As Jesus notes in Luke 18:9, this attitude arises from those who "trusted in themselves that they were righteous" (KJV). Biblical parallels include Proverbs 16:18 ("Pride goes before destruction") and the broader theme in Matthew 23, where Jesus critiques Pharisees for showing off their piety.

- **Comparison and Contempt**: He compares himself favorably to "other men" (extortioners, unjust, adulterers) and specifically despises the tax collector, calling him out publicly in prayer. This judgmental spirit reflects contempt for others, prioritizing self-glorification over compassion or recognition of shared human sinfulness. Jesus emphasizes this in verse 14, where the humble sinner is favored over the proud comparer.

In essence, the Pharisee's posture reveals a lack of genuine repentance and reliance on God's mercy, making his "righteousness" superficial and hypocritical. Jesus concludes that the tax collector, not the Pharisee, "went down to his house justified" (v. 14), highlighting that true justification comes from humility before God, not self-congratulation.

### Why the Tax Collector's Posture Is Spiritually Truer

The tax collector's humble acknowledgment of sin and plea for mercy aligns with biblical themes of repentance and grace, which Jesus presents as the spiritually superior path. Key reasons:

- **Humility and Self-Awareness**: He stands "afar off" (physically distant, symbolizing spiritual unworthiness), won't even lift his eyes, and beats his breast in contrition—gestures of deep remorse. His prayer is simple yet profound: "God be merciful to me a sinner" (v. 13). This recognizes his unrighteousness without excuses or comparisons, embodying the call in Psalms 51:17 ("The sacrifices of God are a broken spirit") and Matthew 5:3 (blessed are the poor in spirit).

- **Focus on God's Mercy vs. Self-Justification**: Unlike the Pharisee, who lists achievements to justify himself, the tax collector seeks grace externally from God. This posture models true faith in God's forgiveness, as seen in Jesus' repeated emphasis on mercy in the Gospels (e.g., Luke 15, the parable of the prodigal son). Jesus validates this by declaring the tax collector "justified" (declared righteous through God's imputation, not human merit)—a nod to Romans 4:5, where God justifies the ungodly who believe.

- **Implicit Reward and Lesson**: Jesus uses this to teach that outward piety is hollow without inner humility. The tax collector's sincerity leads to spiritual vindication, underscoring that God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble (James 4:6, 1 Peter 5:5). Historically, tax collectors were despised as traitors in Jewish society (collaborating with Roman occupiers), making his repentance all the more striking.

This parable underscores a core Christian ethic: salvation depends on recognizing one's need for divine mercy, not comparative moral superiority. If you're exploring this further, compare it with similar teachings in Luke 15 or Matthew 23 for deeper context.
