**The craving to be seen, not visibility itself.**

In Matthew 6:1, Jesus warns against performing "alms" (acts of charity or righteousness) "before men, to be seen of them" (Greek: *hina phanōsin tois anthrōpois*, literally "in order that they may be seen by men"). The moral danger lies in the **motive**—seeking human admiration or applause—which forfeits heavenly reward. Visibility alone isn't condemned; the verse targets the internal "craving" for public validation, turning good deeds into self-glorification.

### Key Evidence from Context:
- **Hypocrites' motive exposed (vv. 2, 5, 16)**: Jesus illustrates with examples—sounding trumpets for alms, public praying, or disfigured fasting "to be seen of men." They "have their reward" already (earthly praise), leaving none from God.
- **Contrast with pure motive**: The instruction is to give/pray/fast "in secret," so God (who sees in secret) rewards openly (v. 4, 6, 18). Public acts aren't banned outright.
- **Broader Sermon on the Mount**: Jesus affirms public good if God-honoring: "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father" (Matt. 5:16). The issue is whose glory is sought—God's or man's.

This echoes Old Testament warnings like Isaiah 29:13 ("honour me with... lips, but have removed their heart far from me"). Public goodness risks corruption when it feeds pride; true virtue prioritizes God's gaze over the crowd's.
