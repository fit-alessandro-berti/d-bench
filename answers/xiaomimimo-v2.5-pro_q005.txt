Your question gets to the heart of why this teaching is so radical and remains challenging. Let’s break it down.

**Why is enemy-love difficult to take seriously?**  
Because it goes directly against our natural instincts of self-preservation, justice-as-repayment, and tribal loyalty. It feels impractical, emotionally risky, and even foolish in a world where power and protection often operate through adversarial dynamics. We tend to distrust what demands us to be vulnerable.

---

**Is it sentimental, strategic, or a deeper form of moral strength?**

1. **It’s not merely sentimental.**  
   Sentimentalism tends to be based on fleeting emotion or idealized feeling that doesn’t engage reality. Jesus’ command is concrete: *love, bless, do good, pray*. It’s not a vague feeling but an actionable posture toward others—even those who harm you.

2. **It has strategic wisdom, but isn’t ultimately strategic.**  
   Some argue enemy-love can disarm hostility, build bridges, or break cycles of vengeance—and historically it sometimes does. But Jesus doesn’t frame it as a tactic for gaining influence. The reasoning given is **participatory and imitative**: “that ye may be the children of your Father… for he maketh his sun to rise on the evil and the good.” The goal is to reflect God’s impartial generosity, not to manipulate outcomes. It’s strategic in a cosmic sense—it aligns you with the nature of ultimate reality—but not as a calculated move for personal benefit.

3. **It is a profound form of moral and spiritual strength.**  
   This is where the depth lies. Enemy-love requires:
   - **Detachment from retaliation**: freeing yourself from being controlled by another’s hatred.
   - **Empathy beyond affection**: seeing the other’s humanity even when they deny yours.
   - **Trust in a larger justice**: surrendering the outcome to God rather than taking it into your own hands.
   It’s a stance of active grace that refuses to let evil dictate your response. It’s not weakness or naïveté; it’s the discipline of choosing your moral identity based on divine character rather than enemy behavior.

---

In essence, this command is **an invitation to practice a form of moral creativity**. It asks you to break the script of aggression and retribution—not by ignoring wrong, but by responding in a way that refuses to let hatred have the last word. It’s difficult because it requires a transformation of the heart and a trust in a goodness stronger than vengeance.

It’s neither sentimental nor merely tactical—it’s a call to embody a strength that looks like weakness to the world, but is the very power that reconciles and heals.
