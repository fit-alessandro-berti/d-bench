In Isaiah 55:6–7, the call to repentance involves both forsaking the "way" and the "thoughts," highlighting a holistic transformation that God desires from those who turn to Him:

### 1. **Forsaking the "Way" (Outward Conduct)**
- The "way" refers to a person’s actions, lifestyle, and observable behavior.
- Repentance requires a tangible change in the patterns of living—turning away from sinful deeds and habits.
- This implies that outward conduct is crucial; it is what others see and what reflects the state's condition of the heart in practice.

### 2. **Forsaking the "Thoughts" (Inward Imagination)**
- The "thoughts" or "imagination" points to internal attitudes, desires, motivations, and intentions.
- It acknowledges that sin is not just external but rooted deeply in how a person thinks and feels.
- True repentance involves renewing the mind—changing internal perspectives, desires, and plans, not just behaviors.

### 3. **Implication for Mercy and Forgiveness**
- By calling people to repent both inwardly and outwardly, the passage emphasizes that God’s mercy is extended to those who genuinely turn from sin, in entirety—heart and actions.
- It implies that God sees the whole person and desires sincere repentance that leads to authentic transformation.
- The promise “he will have mercy upon him” and “he will abundantly pardon” shows that mercy is available not merely for superficial change but for deep, sincere turning.

### Summary:
Repentance involving both "way" and "thoughts" teaches that God calls for complete renewal—both in what is visible (behavior) and what is hidden (mind and heart). This comprehensive turning to God opens the door for abundant mercy and pardon, affirming that no aspect of a person is beyond His gracious forgiveness if genuinely submitted.
