Bitterness and forgiveness are placed against each other because they represent two opposite ways of carrying another person’s wrong.

In the Greek, **“bitterness”** (*pikria*) suggests more than a passing painful feeling. It is the sourness that develops when anger is retained and allowed to interpret the offender, the injury, and future interactions. The surrounding words show its progression:

- **bitterness** — settled resentment  
- **wrath** — heated emotional outburst  
- **anger** — continuing hostility  
- **clamour** — aggressive, contentious speech  
- **evil speaking** — slander or verbal abuse  
- **malice** — the desire or readiness to harm  

So Paul is not simply commanding people never to *feel* hurt or anger. Earlier he says, “Be ye angry, and sin not” and “let not the sun go down upon your wrath” (Eph. 4:26). The issue is what anger becomes when it is kept, rehearsed, expressed destructively, and turned into a settled posture.

Forgiveness is the direct counter-movement. The word translated **“forgiving”** (*charizomenoi*) is related to *grace* and can mean graciously giving or freely pardoning. Instead of making the wrong the permanent basis of the relationship, the believer responds from the grace already received: “as God in Christ forgave you.”

Thus bitterness here can involve several dimensions:

- **A feeling:** it may begin in genuine pain, disappointment, or anger.
- **A habit:** remembered injuries can be repeatedly rehearsed.
- **A chosen posture:** one can hold onto the debt and relate to the offender through it.
- **A communal contagion:** bitterness does not remain private; it spreads through shouting, accusation, slander, faction, and malice.

That communal aspect matters in Ephesians. Paul is describing how the “new humanity” in Christ is to live together. Bitterness corrodes the body by turning wounds into cycles of retaliatory speech and conduct. Forgiveness interrupts that cycle.

The contrast, then, is not simply **bad emotion versus pleasant emotion**. It is **retained grievance versus grace-shaped release**. Tenderhearted forgiveness does not call evil good, erase accountability, or require instant emotional relief. It means refusing to nurture the injury into malice and refusing to make vengeance or permanent indebtedness the governing principle of one’s response.
