To understand why pride is linked to blindness and collapse, and what the contrast with "the lowly" suggests about the moral psychology of arrogance, it's essential to examine the depths of the meanings conveyed in Proverbs 16:18–19.

1. **Pride and its consequences**: The first part of Proverbs 16:18, "Pride goeth before destruction, and a haughty spirit before a fall," directly links pride with negative outcomes like destruction and falling. Pride is often associated with an overestimation of one's capabilities or importance. This overestimation can lead to blindness, as the proud person is likely to ignore advice, warnings, or even facts that conflict with their self-perception. This blindness is not just an oversight but can lead individuals into poor decision-making, which ultimately results in their downfall or "fall."

2. **The imagery of blindness**:
   - Blindness in this context refers to a lack of awareness or a refusal to acknowledge reality. Pride can make a person blind to their own faults and limitations, blind to the circumstances and needs of others, and even blind to impending dangers.
   - This metaphorical blindness can result in a collapse when the unacknowledged realities eventually come to the forefront, often in a way that the proud individual is unprepared to manage or mitigate.

3. **Contrast with the lowly**:
   - The next verse, Proverbs 16:19, provides a stark juxtaposition: "Better it is to be of an humble spirit with the lowly, than to divide the spoil with the proud." This suggests that there is greater value in humility and sharing experiences with those who are humble or lowly.
   - The "lowly" typically refers to individuals who are modest about their own status or abilities and are often more receptive to input, collaborative in approach, and resilient in adversity. They are the opposite of the “proud” indicating that their humble disposition shields them from the sorts of pitfalls that befall the proud.

4. **Moral psychology of arrogance**:
   - The moral psychology of arrogance involves a self-centered approach to life that prioritizes one's own interests, opinions, and desires above those of others.
   - The contrast suggested in the proverb indicates that arrogance (related to pride) leads to a lack of communal and interpersonal harmony. It portrays that arrogance might allow one to temporarily "divide the spoil" suggesting short-term gains but impairs long-term relationships and communal trust.
   - Furthermore, humility is correlated with better moral reasoning, where a person acknowledges their limitations and values others' contributions, creating a more stable and sustainable interaction with others and the world.

Thus, Proverbs 16:18–19 is highlighting the psychological and practical perils of pride—a lack of self-awareness leading to consequential failures, and contrasting it with the virtues of humility which embed deeper social connectedness and stability. It suggests that the stability and security found in humility are far preferable to the risky, solitary heights of pride.
