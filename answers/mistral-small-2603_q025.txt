In **Matthew 6:1**, Jesus warns against performing acts of righteousness (*alms*, or *charitable deeds*) "before men, to be seen of them," lest the reward come only from human praise rather than from God. The danger here is not *visibility* itself but the **motive behind it**—the craving for human approval and the pride that seeks to be seen.

### Key Points:
1. **The Problem is the Heart’s Desire for Praise**
   - Jesus is not condemning *all* public acts of goodness (e.g., giving to the poor in a way that inspires others to do good). Rather, He critiques the **self-seeking motive**—doing good *primarily* to be admired by others.
   - The Pharisees, for example, made a show of their piety (e.g., blowing trumpets when giving alms, Matt. 6:2), seeking human applause rather than God’s approval.

2. **Visibility as a Test of Motive**
   - Public acts of goodness can reveal whether one’s heart is truly humble or secretly craving recognition.
   - If the primary reward is human praise, then the act loses its spiritual value in God’s eyes (cf. Matt. 6:4, where secret giving is rewarded by the Father).

3. **The Reward Structure**
   - Jesus contrasts two rewards:
     - **Human praise** (temporary, fleeting, and self-centered).
     - **God’s reward** (eternal, unseen, and God-centered).
   - The danger is that seeking the former forfeits the latter.

### Conclusion:
The moral danger is not *being seen* but **loving to be seen**—the prideful craving for admiration that corrupts the purity of the deed. True goodness is done for God’s glory, whether in secret or in public (cf. Matt. 5:16, where good works *are* meant to be seen—but for God’s sake, not our own).
