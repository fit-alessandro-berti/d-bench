The sharpness of this image lies in its **absurd exaggeration** and **self-refuting logic**.  

- A "mote" (a tiny speck) versus a "beam" (a massive plank) creates an absurd, almost comical contrast—so exaggerated that it highlights the absurdity of the hypocrite's blind spot.  
- The imagery is also **visually vivid**: one can almost imagine a person stumbling around with a huge wooden beam in their eye, completely unable to see clearly, yet preoccupied with a tiny speck in someone else’s eye.  
- The core of the criticism is **self-awareness**: the hypocrite fails to see their own severe flaw (*“considerest not the beam”*) while fixating on another’s minor fault.  

The passage criticizes hypocrisy **not by forbidding moral correction**, but by exposing its **inverse order**:  
- You cannot help another if you are blindly blind to yourself.  
- The word **“first”** (*“first cast out the beam out of thine own eye”*) is key—it establishes that self-examination must precede effective correction.  
- The issue is not correction itself, but **presumptuous, unexamined correction** that ignores one’s own greater faults.  

So the passage doesn’t forbid moral correction; it demands **humility and self-reform** as the necessary precondition for it. Only after removing your own “beam” can you “see clearly” to help—not in a spirit of superiority, but in genuine care.
