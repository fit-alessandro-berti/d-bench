
This is a thoughtful and nuanced question that touches on the ethics of charity and the motivations behind good deeds, as presented in Matthew 6:1.

Let’s break down the verse and your question:

**The Verse (ESV, for reference):**
> “Take heed that ye do not your alms before men, to be seen of them; otherwise ye have no reward from your Father which is in heaven.”

**Key Points:**
- The warning is against giving alms (charity) *for the sake of human recognition* (“to be seen of them”).
- The concern is not merely that charity should not be done publicly, but that if the motive is pride, vanity, or the desire for people’s approval, the act is spiritually hollow.
- The real reward comes only from God, not from human approval.

## So: What makes public goodness morally dangerous here?

### 1. **Visibility Itself Is Not the Problem—It’s the Motive**
The verse is not warning against doing good in public (some charity is appropriate and even encouraged in the church). The danger is not in being seen, but in the *reason* for being seen. If one gives to others only to gain praise or admiration, the action is disconnected from true compassion and love for God and others.

### 2. **Craving to Be Seen: The Core Issue**
The real moral danger is the *craving*—the motive. When our desire to do good is rooted in self-interest (the desire to be noticed or respected by others), it becomes self-centered rather than selfless. This is what the verse warns against: “Otherwise ye have no reward of your Father...” The “otherwise” refers to acting out of selfish motives.

### 3. **Spiritual Consequences**
From a Christian perspective, charity given from the heart, motivated by love for God and neighbor (not by the desire for human approval), is what earns divine reward. When our goodness is tainted by pride or the need for recognition, it becomes spiritually empty and fails to reflect the love and mercy of God.

## **Summary Answer:**

**The moral danger is not visibility itself, but the craving to be seen—i.e., the selfish motive behind doing public good.** When good deeds are motivated by a desire for human approval, they lack true righteousness and fail to bring the spiritual reward promised by God. The verse urges genuine, unselfish charity, not performative or prideful acts of kindness.

In short: *It’s the motive, not the visibility, that makes public goodness morally dangerous here.*
