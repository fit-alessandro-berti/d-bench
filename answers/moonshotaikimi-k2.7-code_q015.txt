The image is sharp because it is a deliberate **reductio ad absurdum**: Jesus pairs the tiniest imaginable eye-irritant with the largest imaginable obstacle to sight.

- **“Mote”** translates *karphos*, a speck of chaff, sawdust, or straw—something small enough to be almost invisible.
- **“Beam”** translates *dokos*, a heavy wooden rafter or joist—something so large it would make the eye impossible to open.

Picture a would-be oculist trying to remove a speck from someone else’s eye while a roof-beam protrudes from his own. The scene is physically ridiculous, even grotesque. That disproportion is what gives the metaphor its edge: the person who imagines himself perceptive is in fact the most blind.

### How it criticizes hypocrisy

The passage does not condemn noticing moral faults. It condemns **the double standard of noticing faults selectively**.

1. **Selective vision** – The hypocrite has a “beam” in his own eye but is preoccupied with his brother’s “mote.” He applies a magnifying glass to others and a blindfold to himself.
2. **Self-deception** – Calling the person a “hypocrite” (*hypocritēs*, “actor” or “pretender”) means he is pretending to a moral clarity he does not possess. His correction is not an act of love or truth; it is a performance of righteousness.
3. **Lack of proportion** – The beam is not merely bigger; it is the same kind of problem in the same organ. The sin he judges in his brother is a miniature version of the sin dominating his own life.

### Why it does not forbid moral correction

The key is the word **“then”**:

> “First cast out the beam out of thine own eye; **and then** shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote out of thy brother’s eye.”

Jesus does not say, “Leave the mote alone.” He says, “Deal with the beam first, *so that you can see clearly to help your brother*.” Moral correction is therefore **conditioned, not cancelled**:

- **Condition:** rigorous self-examination, repentance, and removal of one’s own comparable fault.
- **Purpose:** clear sight, not moral superiority.
- **Motive:** genuine help for a brother, not self-exaltation through another’s failure.

So the passage rules out **hypocritical, censorious judgment**, not all moral discernment. It allows the kind of correction that is humble, proportionate, and aimed at restoring the other person—once the corrector’s own vision has been cleared.
