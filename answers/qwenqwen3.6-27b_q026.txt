This passage presents speech not as an isolated performance but as a diagnostic outflow from a deeper reservoir of moral character. Jesus uses two complementary metaphors—**overflow** and **treasury**—to show how what we say is causally and diagnostically linked to what we’ve accumulated within. 

To answer your question directly: **the passage supports all three, but with a clear hierarchy. Words function primarily as *revelations* and *symptoms*, while *choice* operates in the background as the formative force that fills the stockpile.

Let’s break down how the text connects speech to hidden moral stockpiles, and how each category fits.

### 1. Speech as Overflow from a "Moral Stockpile"
- **Heart (καρδία)**: In biblical anthropology, the "heart" is not merely the seat of emotion but the integrated center of will, cognition, affections, and moral disposition. It is the control room of human character.
- **Abundance/Overflow (ἐκ τοῦ περισσεύοντος)**: Jesus says the mouth "speaketh" from what *abounds* or *overflows*. This implies speech is not carefully metered; it spills out from what has filled the heart beyond capacity.
- **Treasure (θησαυρός)**: A treasury implies deliberate accumulation over time. What is stored there—values, beliefs, habits, affections, and surrendered-or-resisted impulses. The "good or evil treasure" is thus the cumulative archive of a person’s moral trajectory.

### 2. Words as Symptoms
The passage treats speech **diagnostically**. Jesus’ rhetorical question—*"how can ye, being evil, speak good things?"*—assumes that speech reliably indicates internal state. Just as a physician assesses health by external markers, Jesus treats words as symptoms of spiritual and moral constitution. The inability of an "evil" heart to consistently produce "good" speech underscores that **symptoms follow from underlying conditions**.

### 3. Words as Revelations
More strongly, the passage treats speech as **disclosive. Words don’t just hint; they *unveil*. The heart’s hidden content is laid bare through speech. This aligns with Jesus’ broader teaching: *"For out of the heart come evil thoughts murders, adulteries... All these things make unclean" (Matt 15:18-19). Speech is the medium by which the invisible moral stockpile becomes visible reality.

### 4. Words and Choice
The text does **not** frame individual utterances as moment-to-moment volitional acts. Rather, the "treasure" is the **cumulative product of past choices**—what one has loved, prioritized, resisted, or surrendered to. So words are the *output* of character, and character is the *archive* of choices. Speech here is the downstream fruit, not the primary decision-point. This doesn’t erase responsibility; it relocates it. Jesus holds people accountable for what flows out *because* it reflects what they’ve cultivated within.

### Synthesis: All Three, But Not Equally
- **Primary function in the passage:** *Revelation* and *symptom*. Speech is the involuntary spill that makes the hidden heart visible and diagnostic.
- **Secondary/underlying function:** *Choice*. The stockpile itself is built by choices, but speech is the overflow, not the selection mechanism.
- **Result:** The passage says "all three," but with a clear causal chain: 
  **Choices → Fill the Treasury → Overflow → Speech as Symptom & Revelation.**

### Why This Matters
Jesus contrasts sharply with philosophical or rhetorical traditions that treat speech as pure rational choice. The biblical view is anthropological: will, affect, and cognition fuse into character, which then expresses itself through words. You don’t *choose* your overflow in the moment; you *reap* what you’ve stored. This is why Jesus’ rebuke is so severe: the scribes and Pharisees aren’t just mis-speaking; they are *mis-being*, and their words are the undeniable evidence.

**In short:** The passage teaches that speech is the diagnostic spill of the heart’s moral inventory. Words are *symptoms* of what’s stored, *revelations* of what we truly are, and the *fruit* of choices made long ago. All three are present, but revelation and symptom bear the immediate weight; choice bears the historical weight.
