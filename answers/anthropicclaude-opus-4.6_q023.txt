# "Way" and "Thoughts": The Double Demand of Repentance in Isaiah 55:6–7

## The Structure of the Call

Isaiah's invitation is remarkable for its deliberate parallelism. God does not simply say "let the wicked stop sinning." He specifies two distinct dimensions:

- **The wicked** must forsake **his way** — the outward pattern, the course of life, the visible conduct
- **The unrighteous man** must forsake **his thoughts** — the inward deliberations, schemes, imaginations, and dispositions of the heart

This is not mere poetic repetition. It is a diagnostic statement about what sin actually *is* and, consequently, what repentance must actually *reach*.

## Why Both Are Named

### Outward conduct alone is insufficient
A man might alter his visible behaviour while his heart remains unchanged — calculating, covetous, resentful, self-justifying. Scripture consistently warns against this. The Pharisees washed the outside of the cup. Mere behavioural modification, without the surrender of the inner life, is not repentance but performance.

### Inward intention alone is insufficient
Conversely, a man might feel sincere regret or entertain pious sentiments while continuing to walk in the same destructive "way." Good feelings that produce no changed course of life are what James would later call dead faith. Sorrow that does not redirect the feet is not yet repentance.

### The two together reveal the totality of the human problem
By naming both **way** and **thoughts**, God declares that sin has corrupted the *whole person* — the external life others can see and the internal life only God can see. The "way" is the fruit; the "thoughts" are the root. Both must be forsaken because both belong to the rebellion.

This echoes what Genesis established early: "every imagination of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually" (Genesis 6:5), and that inner corruption produced a world of wicked *ways*. Isaiah is calling for a reversal as thorough as the corruption itself.

## What This Implies About Each Dimension

**About outward conduct:** It is not morally neutral. The "way" of the wicked is not merely unfortunate or culturally conditioned — it is a *direction*, a trajectory away from God. Forsaking it means an actual change of direction, not just regret about the destination.

**About inward imagination:** God takes seriously what a person *thinks*, not only what a person *does*. The unrighteous man's "thoughts" — his plans, his reasoning, his interior monologue, his assumptions about reality — these are themselves objects of moral judgment and must themselves be surrendered. This is sobering, because men often believe their thoughts are private and therefore beyond accountability. Isaiah says otherwise.

**About the relationship between the two:** The pairing implies that outward reform without inward transformation is unstable, and inward transformation without outward change is illusory. True repentance is a single act that necessarily has both dimensions — a turning of the whole self, feet and mind together, back toward God.

## What This Implies About Mercy

Here is the astonishing thing about the passage: after making this total demand — forsake your entire way of life *and* your entire way of thinking — God does not then say, "and perhaps I will consider your case." He says:

> *"He will have mercy upon him... he will **abundantly** pardon."*

The word "abundantly" is crucial. It means God's pardon is not reluctant, not measured out in proportion to the quality of our repentance, not grudging. The demand is total, but the mercy is *more* than total — it overflows.

This sequence teaches something profound about the nature of divine mercy:

1. **Mercy is not offered to those who minimise their sin.** God does not say, "Forsake your worst habits and I will overlook the rest." He names the whole disease — way and thoughts — before He names the cure. Cheap grace, which asks for pardon without forsaking, finds no support here.

2. **Yet the severity of the demand does not diminish the generosity of the pardon.** God knows that what He is asking — the surrender of both external conduct and internal imagination — is everything. And His response to that everything is *abundant* pardon. The proportion is deliberate: total repentance meets more-than-total grace.

3. **The possibility of mercy depends on God's character, not on the perfection of human repentance.** The text does not say "return to the Lord and prove yourself worthy." It says return "and *He* will have mercy." The ground of pardon is in *Him* — in His nature as the God who abundantly pardons — not in the adequacy of our turning.

## The Urgency

We must not overlook the opening: *"while He may be found... while He is near."* This implies that the opportunity for this total repentance meeting abundant mercy is not permanent in the same way at all times. There is a "while" — a window shaped by God's sovereign patience. The comprehensiveness of what is offered (full pardon for full repentance) makes the urgency of *when* it is offered all the more serious.

## Conclusion

Isaiah 55:6–7 teaches that genuine repentance cannot be partial because sin is never partial. It corrupts both the road a man walks and the mind with which he walks it. But precisely because God demands the surrender of *everything*, He offers in return what no human effort could earn — pardon that is not merely sufficient but **abundant**, flowing from a God whose ways and thoughts are, as the very next verses declare, as high above ours as the heavens are above the earth.
