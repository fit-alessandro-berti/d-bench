## The posture: a fellow casualty, not a magistrate

The vocabulary encodes the posture before Paul states any rule. The man is "overtaken" (*prolēmphthē*) — caught, seized, surprised by a fault. The passive verb pictures someone snared in a trap, not a rebel standing his ground: a casualty, not an enemy. And what is to be done with him is "restore" (*katartizō*) — the word used for setting a broken bone back into joint and for mending torn nets (Mark 1:19). Correction here is surgery, not sentencing; mending, not discarding. The aim is the brother's repair and his return to usefulness in the body.

The manner is "a spirit of meekness," and this is not decoration. Meekness (*prautēs*) has just been named as fruit of the Spirit (5:23). So when Paul addresses "ye which are spiritual," he is setting a test: the title is not a settled rank but a claim verified in this act — you show yourself spiritual precisely by the gentleness of your correction. This matters because the works of the flesh in 5:19–21 are largely *relational* sins: enmity, strife, jealousy, fits of anger, rivalries, dissensions. Which means harsh, self-satisfied correction would be a work of the flesh dressed as zeal for holiness — the corrector committing, in the very act of correcting, the class of sin he corrects. Paul has just warned, "if ye bite and devour one another, take heed that ye be not consumed one of another" (5:15). Fault-finding severity is one of the chief ways communities devour themselves; 6:1 is the antidote.

Then the singular turn: "considering *thyself*" (*skopōn seauton* — keep your eye on yourself). The command to restore is plural; the watchfulness is singular. The community acts, but the self-scrutiny cannot be delegated. The gaze fixed on the brother's fault must rotate inward.

## Why the three belong together

They form a closed circuit — each is the condition of the others' integrity:

- **Restoration** names the end (repair); **meekness** the manner (gentleness); **self-suspicion** the inner ground that keeps both honest.
- Remove meekness, and "restoration" becomes rough surgery that re-breaks the bone — severity that crushes rather than sets (cf. 2 Cor 2:7, where excessive punishment threatens to swallow a man in sorrow, and Paul commands the church to reaffirm its love).
- Remove self-suspicion, and meekness curdles into condescension — gentleness dispensed from above, which is pride in a softer tone.
- Remove restoration, and self-suspicion becomes paralysis — "who am I to judge?" as an excuse to abandon the brother. Paul does not say, "considering thyself, leave him alone." Self-knowledge is meant to govern the *manner* of correction, not to cancel it.

Why is self-suspicion the ground of honest meekness? Because of the small word "also": "lest thou *also* be tempted." Temptation is the common condition. The fallen man was tempted and fell; you are made of the same material. There is no class of the untemptable — even "the spiritual" stand under the warning. A man who secretly believes himself intrinsically superior corrects as a superior: impatient, final, contemptuous. A man who knows his own vulnerability corrects as one casualty pulling another from a ditch he nearly fell into himself. Meekness, then, is not a technique the strong adopt toward the weak; it is the natural posture of accurate self-knowledge. You can set a bone gently only if you know how breakable bones are — and you know because the skeleton is your own.

There is a further edge. The temptation in view may not be only the brother's sin. Paul has just said, "let us not be desirous of vainglory, provoking one another, envying one another" (5:26), and he goes on, "if a man think himself to be something, when he is nothing, he deceiveth himself" (6:3). The corrector has temptations peculiar to his position: the quiet pleasure of the contrast, the vainglory of standing over a fallen brother. The brother's fall is, for me, an occasion of possible sin — not his sin but mine. The most spiritually dangerous moment for the strong is precisely the moment of correcting the weak.

## The frame: the law of Christ

The next verse gives the rationale: "Bear ye one another's burdens, and so fulfil the law of Christ" (6:2). The fault is not a case to be prosecuted but a weight to be carried — together — and carrying it this way *is* what fulfills Christ's law, already summarized as love of neighbor (5:14). So the posture of correction becomes the test of the letter's whole argument: freedom from the law issues neither in license (5:13) nor in severity, but in cruciform gentleness — the meekness of the one who is "gentle and lowly in heart." How a community handles a fallen member is where it becomes visible whether that community actually walks by the Spirit — whether its spirituality is fruit or merely foliage.
