
Excellent question. This parable is a masterclass in exposing the heart's posture before God. The Pharisee is condemned not by a single item on your list, but by a **toxic synergy of all five**, with **pride and self-righteousness as the root**.

Here’s a breakdown of what condemns him, followed by why the tax collector’s posture is truer:

### What Condemns the Pharisee? (The Perfect Storm of Self-Deception)

1.  **Pride (The Core Sin):** This is the foundational sin. His prayer is "with himself" (v. 11). He is his own audience. He trusts **in himself** that he is righteous (v. 9). His virtue is a source of self-congratulation, not gratitude to God. He has made himself the center of the universe.

2.  **Comparison (The Mechanism of Pride):** His righteousness is defined and inflated **in contrast to others**. "I am not like other men... or even as this publican." He uses others as a yardstick to measure his own superiority. This is the essence of spiritual pride: feeling righteous because someone else appears worse.

3.  **Contempt (The Fruit of Comparison):** His comparison immediately curdles into **despising others** (v. 9). He doesn't just see the publican as a sinner; he sees him as *beneath* him. His prayer is an act of social and spiritual distancing, not intercession or humility.

4.  **Virtue as Self-Displays:** His good deeds (fasting, tithing) are **not offered to God** but **waved before God and man** as credentials. "I fast... I give tithes..." The repeated "I" is a trumpet blast announcing his own virtue. These are not acts of worship but acts of self-justification.

5.  **False Doctrine (The Theological Framework):** Implicitly, his sin is a **distortion of the doctrine of justification**. He believes his external performance and moral superiority **earn** him a righteous standing before God. He has reduced a relationship with the Holy God to a transaction where he is the creditor (of good deeds) and God is the debtor (obligated to accept him). This is the opposite of the gospel truth that we are justified by God's grace through faith, not by works.

**In short: The Pharisee is condemned by a heart that has replaced a **God-centered religion** with a **self-centered performance**. His prayer is a soliloquy of self-exaltation, not a dialogue of repentance and faith.**

---

### Why the Tax Collector’s Posture is Spiritually Truer

His posture is the antithesis of the Pharisee's, and therefore "truer" because it aligns with reality—the true reality of who God is and who we are.

1.  **His Posture is Physical & Spiritual Humility:** "Standing afar off... would not lift up so much as his eyes to heaven, but smote upon his breast." Every detail screams **awareness of unworthiness**. He keeps his distance (afar off), avoids direct eye contact (symbolic of not demanding his rights), and beats his breast (a gesture of profound grief and contrition). His body confesses what his words will: *"I am dust and ashes."*

2.  **His Prayer is God-Centered, Not Self-Centered:** He says, **"God be merciful to me, a sinner."** (v. 13).
    *   **Address:** He speaks to **God**, not to himself or the bystanders.
    *   **Petition:** He asks for **mercy** (*hilaskomai* – propitiation, atonement), not for a reward for his deeds. He knows he has no claim on God.
    *   **Confession:** He defines himself **accurately**: "a sinner." Not "a sinner *like that guy*," but "a sinner" *period*. He owns his condition without comparison.

3.  **He Accepts the Only Ground for Justification:** The Pharisee came trusting in his own righteousness. The tax collector came **renouncing his own righteousness** and casting himself wholly on God's mercy. **This is the "truer" position because it is the only position that acknowledges the true depth of human sin and the true height of God's grace.**

### The Parable's Shocking Verdict (v. 14)

*"I tell you, this man went down to his house justified rather than the other..."*

*   **The Pharisee:** Went home **unjustified**. His performance was rejected because it was a rejection of God's mercy. He trusted in himself, so he was left to himself.
*   **The Tax Collector:** Went home **justified** (declared righteous). Not because he had become righteous by his sorrow, but because he had **received** the mercy of the Righteous One. His confession was the open door for God's justifying grace.

**Conclusion:** The Pharisee is condemned by a **self-righteous, comparative, contemptuous religion of self-display** that is built on a **false doctrine of human ability**. The tax collector’s posture is "truer" because it is a **humble, contrite, God-dependent religion of mercy** that is built on the **true doctrine of human sin and divine grace**. The one exalted himself and was humbled; the other humbled himself and was exalted (v. 14).
