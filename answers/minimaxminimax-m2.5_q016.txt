The surface symptom is *anger* (“he was angry and would not go in”), but the parable drives us deeper.  

When we look at what fuels that anger we find a cluster of attitudes that are really different faces of the same root problem:

| Possible label | What it describes | Why it’s not the deepest issue |
|----------------|-------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Anger**      | The immediate emotional reaction. | It’s the outward sign, not the underlying disease. |
| **Envy**       | He envies the younger brother’s “celebration.” | Envy is a subset of a larger inability to welcome grace. |
| **Wounded fairness** | He feels the father is being unjust. | This sense of injustice is itself fueled by a self‑righteous expectation of reward. |
| **Pride in obedience** | He points to his years of service (“I serve thee… I never transgressed”). | This is the engine that makes “fairness” feel violated. |
| **Discomfort with mercy itself** | He cannot celebrate the father’s forgiving love for the “lost.” | This is the heart of the matter. |

**The elder brother’s deepest problem is his discomfort with mercy.**  
He has built his identity on *what he does* – “I have served you all these years.” Consequently, he expects God (or the father) to treat him on the basis of merit. When the father lavishes love on the son who squandered his inheritance, the older brother experiences it as an affront to his own worth. His anger, his sense of unfairness, his pride in obedience, even the envy—all flow from a heart that is *unreconciled to grace*.

In the parable the father says, “Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine” (v. 31). The older brother already possesses everything the father has; what he lacks is the *capacity to delight* in the father’s compassion for the lost. He is offended that the father would “kill the fatted calf” for a “son who devoured thy living with harlots.” In other words, he cannot accept that mercy can be given where it is *not earned*.

This is exactly the attitude Jesus was addressing when He told the parable: the religious leaders of His day were angry because they could not rejoice over sinners who repented. They, like the older brother, were “angry” at the “unfair” distribution of God’s grace. Their root problem was not simply anger or envy; it was a *self‑righteous discomfort with mercy*—the belief that God’s love must be rationed according to human performance.

Therefore, the elder brother’s deepest issue is **discomfort with mercy itself**, a pride‑filled, works‑based orientation that prevents him from sharing in the father’s joy over the recovery of the lost. The other feelings (anger, envy, wounded fairness, pride) are merely symptoms of that underlying heart‑problem.
