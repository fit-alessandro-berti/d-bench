The elder brother’s deepest problem is **discomfort with mercy itself** — though it is manifested through anger, envy, wounded fairness, and pride in obedience.

### Why “discomfort with mercy” is the core issue:

While all the listed emotions — anger, envy, wounded fairness, pride — are present and real, they are **symptoms** of a deeper, more foundational problem: **a failure to understand or embrace the father’s grace-filled, uncompensated mercy**.

The elder brother operates under a **contractual theology**:  
> *“I’ve served you faithfully — therefore, I deserve rewards. He sinned wildly — therefore, he deserves punishment.”*

His sense of justice is transactional. Mercy — especially mercy extended to someone who has squandered, offended, and repented — **threatens his entire understanding of worth, merit, and order**. When the father rejoices over the prodigal’s return, it doesn’t just upset the elder brother; it **inverts his世界观 (worldview)**.

He cannot comprehend why the father would celebrate someone who doesn’t *earn* it. His obedience becomes a tool for self-righteousness, not love. His complaint — *“You never gave me a kid…”* — reveals his heart: He wants **rewards**, not relationship. He sees his father not as a loving parent, but as a paymaster.

The father’s response is the key:
> *“Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine.”*

This is not a rebuke — it is an invitation. The father is saying:  
> *“You already have everything. You’ve never been outside my love. Why can’t you rejoice in the restoration of your brother? Mercy doesn’t diminish your share — it expands the joy of the household.”*

But the elder brother is not ready to enter that joy. His discomfort with mercy is so profound that he refuses to go in.

### The Parable’s Deeper Point:

This story is Jesus’ response to the Pharisees and religious leaders who grumbled at His association with sinners (Luke 15:2). The elder brother mirrors them: morally upright, religiously dutiful, but spiritually cold. They didn’t dislike the sinners — they disliked **God’s grace toward sinners**.

**Mercy is not just an action; it is a posture of the heart.**  
The elder brother’s sin is not disobedience — it is **the refusal to rejoice in redemption**. He loves the system of merit more than the Father’s heart of grace.

### Conclusion:

His deepest problem is **discomfort with mercy itself** — because mercy undermines his self-righteousness, exposes his legalism, and demands that he love what he condemns. Until he can embrace mercy, he remains spiritually lost — even while standing just outside the feast.

> *“It was meet that we should make merry…”* — but he won’t. And that is his tragedy.
