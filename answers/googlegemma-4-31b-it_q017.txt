Based on the text of John 3:19–21, the hatred of light is not a hatred of light for its own sake, but rather a reaction to what light *does*. In this context, light serves as a metaphor for divine truth and moral clarity.

The relation between truth, exposure, shame, and the desire for invisibility can be explored through the following dimensions:

### 1. Light as Exposure (The Mechanism of Truth)
Truth, in this passage, is not merely a set of facts, but a revealing force. Light functions as an agent of exposure. When the text says that "light is come into the world," it implies that a standard of holiness and truth has arrived that makes it impossible for things to remain hidden.

The "hatred" described here is a defensive mechanism. If light reveals the reality of a person's life, then the light becomes the enemy of the secret. To hate the light is to attempt to protect the sanctuary of the shadow where one's actions are not scrutinized.

### 2. The Cycle of Shame and Evil
The passage establishes a causal link: **Evil $\rightarrow$ Shame $\rightarrow$ Avoidance.**

*   **The Deed:** The text notes that men loved darkness "because their deeds were evil."
*   **The Shame:** Shame is the emotional response to being seen in a state of failure or wrongdoing. For the person doing evil, the light represents "reproof" (v. 20). To be reproved is to have one's facade stripped away and one's moral failures acknowledged.
*   **The Avoidance:** Because the shame of exposure is more painful than the comfort of the darkness, the individual chooses the darkness. The "hatred" of the light is actually a fear of the judgment that comes with being seen clearly.

### 3. The Desire Not to be Seen
There is a profound psychological tension between the desire to be loved and the fear of being known. The person described in verses 19–20 desires the *benefits* of the world, but not the *transparency* of the light.

To be "seen clearly" is to be stripped of all pretenses. For those whose lives are built on deception or harmful actions, visibility is equated with condemnation. Therefore, the darkness is not just a place of hiding; it is a perceived place of safety. The darkness allows the individual to maintain a curated version of themselves to the world while indulging in "evil deeds" in private.

### 4. The Contrast: The Freedom of Truth
The passage concludes by contrasting the "evildoer" with the one who "doeth truth." This reveals the ultimate difference in their relationship with light:

*   **The Evildoer** sees light as a **Prosecutor** (seeking to reprove).
*   **The Truth-doer** sees light as a **Witness** (seeking to manifest).

For the person walking in truth, exposure is not a threat but a validation. They come to the light because they have nothing to hide; therefore, the light does not bring shame, but rather reveals that their works "are wrought in God."

### Summary
Light is hated because it **destroys the illusion**. It forces a confrontation between the *imagined self* (the mask) and the *actual self* (the truth). The hatred of light is the desperate attempt of the ego to avoid the shame of exposure and the requirement of repentance that follows when the truth is finally revealed.
