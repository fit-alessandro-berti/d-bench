This is a thoughtful and important question that gets to the heart of Christian ethics and human nature. Let’s break it down step by step.

#### 1. **Understanding the Verse**

The verse from Luke 6:37 is often called the "Judge Not" passage. Jesus is telling his listeners:

> “Judge not, and ye shall not be judged; condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned; forgive, and ye shall be forgiven.”

At first glance, this can sound like a blanket prohibition against making any kind of moral judgment. But in context, Jesus is not saying that we should never distinguish between right and wrong, or that we shouldn’t use discernment. Rather, he is addressing the *attitude* and *motivation* behind our judgments.

#### 2. **Discernment vs. Condemnation**

**Discernment** is the ability to see things as they are, to recognize what is good, true, and beautiful, and what is harmful, false, or ugly. It is a neutral or positive act of understanding. For example, discerning that stealing is wrong is not in itself a judgmental act; it’s an honest recognition of moral truth.

**Condemnation**, on the other hand, is the act of declaring someone guilty, of looking down on someone, or of passing sentence. It often carries a sense of superiority, anger, or self-righteousness. When we *condemn*, we are not just identifying a wrong act, but declaring the person doing it to be irredeemable or unworthy.

#### 3. **Why Jesus Warns Against Condemnation**

Jesus’ warning is primarily against the *spirit* in which we make moral assessments. He is cautioning against:

- **Hypocrisy**: Judging others while ignoring our own faults (see Matthew 7:1-5, the "plank and the speck").
- **Self-righteousness**: Using judgment to elevate ourselves at the expense of others.
- **Lack of compassion**: Refusing to see the person behind the action, or refusing to extend grace and forgiveness.

When we judge or condemn in this way, we harden our hearts and shut ourselves off from God’s mercy and from helping others.

But Jesus *does* expect his followers to have moral discernment. For instance, in Matthew 7:15-20, Jesus gives advice on how to recognize true vs. false teachers—he is not saying “don’t discern, just accept everyone blindly.” The difference is in the *heart* and *purpose*.

#### 4. **So What’s the Difference?**

- **Discernment**: Seeing and understanding moral truth, without attaching it to a person’s identity or using it as a weapon.
- **Appetite to Condemn**: The desire to find fault, to punish, or to declare someone unacceptable. This is self-serving and often rooted in pride or fear.

Discernment is about truth; condemnation is about power or self-justification.

#### 5. **A Christian Perspective**

A Christian is called to:

- **Discern good from evil** (Hebrews 5:14).
- **Warn others in love** (Galatians 6:1), but without arrogance or malice.
- **Forgive others** as we have been forgiven (Colossians 3:13).

The difference is in the *heart* and *motive*. If our goal is to help, correct, or protect, guided by love, that is very different from seeking to shame, belittle, or destroy.

#### 6. **Practical Application**

Ask yourself:

- Am I pointing out a behavior because I want to help, or because I want to feel superior?
- Am I willing to look at my own flaws first?
- Am I extending grace and forgiveness, or am I quick to label and reject?

#### **Summary Table**

| Discernment | Appetite to Condemn |
|-------------|---------------------|
| Neutral, truth-seeking | Emotional, judgmental |
| Sees actions, not just people | Sees people as their mistakes |
| Motivated by love and care | Motivated by pride or fear |
| Leads to correction and healing | Leads to division and shame |
| Open to self-examination | Self-righteous, closed off |

---

**In essence:**  
Jesus is not saying “don’t know right from wrong,” but rather “don’t use your knowledge of right and wrong as a club to beat others with.” Discernment is necessary; condemnation is not. The difference is in our heart and how we use that discernment.
