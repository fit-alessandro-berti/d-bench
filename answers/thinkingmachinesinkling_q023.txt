In Isaiah 55:7 the call to repentance is deliberately two-sided: **“his way”** (*derekh*) and **“his thoughts”** (*machshavot*). By pairing them, the prophet shows that returning to God is not a cosmetic change but a total reorientation—covering both the life we live in public and the world we construct in private.

### 1. “Way” — Outward Conduct and Habitual Life
*Derekh* means path, road, or manner of walking. To “forsake his way” is to abandon the **direction and habits** of one’s life: the repeated choices, relationships, economies, and pleasures that define the “wicked” person’s existence. 

- **Implication:** Repentance must be practical and visible. It is not enough to feel sorry; the feet must turn around (Acts 26:20). Sin is embodied in routine, so mercy requires breaking the patterns—what we do with our time, bodies, money, and influence.

### 2. “Thoughts” — Inward Imagination, Plans, and Desire
*Machshavot* refers not only to passing ideas but to **designs, schemes, and mental frameworks**—the inner narrative that justifies sin, imagines autonomy, and plots self-interest. The “unrighteous man” is called to forsake these.

- **Implication:** True repentance reaches the hidden self. One can stop a sinful action while still cherishing it in fantasy, resentment, or rationalization (cf. Matt 15:19; Prov 23:7). The mind’s “thoughts” include our self-image, our grievances, and our secret ambitions. God demands the surrender of the **imagination**—the factory where sin is first engineered.

### 3. Together: A Holistic Exchange
The coupling of “way” and “thoughts” means repentance is **root and fruit, heart and hand**. You cannot genuinely change your path while keeping the mental map that led you there, nor can you renovate your mind without altering your steps.

This is immediately reinforced in Isaiah 55:8–9:  
> “For my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways…”

The unrighteous man is invited not merely to stop bad thinking, but to **exchange his thoughts for God’s**—to adopt a completely different worldview, one marked by God’s mercy and exalted purpose.

### 4. What This Implies About Mercy
The promise that follows—“he will have mercy upon him… he will **abundantly pardon**”—is all the more remarkable because it addresses **both dimensions of corruption**.

- **Mercy is not superficial.** God does not forgive only the visible “way” and ignore the polluted “thoughts.” His pardon penetrates the conscience and cleanses the imagination (Heb 9:14; 1 John 1:9).
- **Mercy is abundant precisely because corruption is deep.** The Hebrew emphasizes multiplication: God “multiplies pardon.” Our sins run deep in habit (*way*) and deeper still in desire (*thoughts*), yet His grace runs deeper still. There is no part of human life—outer or inner—that lies beyond the reach of His returning mercy.
- **Mercy creates transformation, not just exemption.** Because God asks for both the way and the thoughts, the pardon He offers is not a legal fiction but a regenerative gift. He frees us from the old path *and* renews the mind (Rom 12:2), giving us both new feet to walk and a new heart to think.

### Summary
Repentance in Isaiah 55:7 involves both **“way” and “thoughts”** because human rebellion is total—lived out in behavior and nurtured in the mind. The call demands that we lay down our public lives *and* our private imaginations. The promise of mercy means that God receives this whole surrender and responds with a pardon so lavish that it can cleanse every hidden corner of the heart, replacing our corrupt ways and self-centered thoughts with His own.
