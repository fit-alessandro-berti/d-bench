Luke 6:37 is not a ban on all moral evaluation. It is a warning against a certain *spirit* of judgment: the kind that delights in condemning, elevates self, and shows no mercy.

## 1. “Judge not” does not mean “have no moral discernment”
If moral judgments never mattered, then Jesus could not also teach people to recognize good and evil, truth and falsehood, justice and injustice. In the same Gospel tradition, people are expected to:

- recognize hypocrisy,
- identify harmful behavior,
- repent of sin,
- forgive real wrongs,
- bear good fruit.

All of that requires discernment. You cannot forgive evil unless you first know it is evil. You cannot protect the vulnerable unless you can say, “This is wrong.”

So the verse cannot mean: *Never make any moral distinction.*

## 2. The target is the condemning posture
The sequence matters:

- judge not,
- condemn not,
- forgive.

This suggests Jesus is confronting not thoughtful discernment but the human tendency to move quickly from seeing fault to *pronouncing doom*.

There is a difference between:

- **discernment:** “This action is sinful, harmful, false, or unjust.”
- **condemnation:** “Therefore this person is beneath mercy, beyond hope, and fit for my scorn.”

Discernment evaluates conduct. Condemnation claims the seat of final judgment over the person.

## 3. Discernment is humble; condemnation is hungry
A useful distinction is that discernment is often reluctant, while condemnation is eager.

**Discernment:**
- asks what is true,
- is careful with facts,
- acknowledges its own fallibility,
- seeks restoration,
- leaves ultimate judgment to God.

**The appetite to condemn:**
- enjoys exposing fault,
- assumes the worst,
- reduces a person to their failure,
- withholds mercy,
- uses another’s sin to feel righteous.

That appetite is what Jesus attacks. It is spiritually dangerous because it disguises pride as moral seriousness.

## 4. Moral seriousness without mercy becomes hypocrisy
Jesus often condemns hypocrisy more sharply than obvious sin. Why? Because hypocrisy combines moral language with a hard heart.

A person may be correct about right and wrong and still be wrong in spirit. One can tell the truth about sin while speaking without charity, without self-knowledge, and without any desire for redemption.

Luke 6:37 warns that the measure we use on others rebounds on us. If we live by merciless exposure, we place ourselves under the same standard.

## 5. Forgiveness is not moral indifference
“Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven” does not mean pretending no wrong occurred. Forgiveness presupposes wrong. It means releasing the desire to retaliate, to nurse superiority, or to freeze another person forever in their guilt.

So the verse does not flatten morality. It transforms the way morality is carried:

- not as a weapon,
- but as a call to truth joined with mercy.

## 6. A practical distinction
You might ask of any judgment:

- Am I trying to understand, or am I eager to denounce?
- Am I naming wrong in order to help, protect, or restore?
- Or am I satisfying my desire to feel cleaner than someone else?
- Do I remember my own need for mercy?
- Do I leave room for repentance?

If the aim is truth with humility and love, that is discernment.  
If the aim is superiority, finality, and exclusion, that is condemnation.

## 7. In one sentence
Luke 6:37 does **not** teach that moral judgments never matter; it teaches that moral discernment must be exercised without self-righteousness, cruelty, or the pleasure of condemning others.
