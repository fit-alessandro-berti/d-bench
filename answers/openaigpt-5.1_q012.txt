Jeremiah 17:9–10 is not a blanket statement that “everything about humans is worthless,” but a sharp warning about how *unreliable* we are as judges of our own inner life—and how utterly reliable God is.

### 1. What the passage is saying in context

A few key pieces:

- **Context in Jeremiah 17:**  
  Jeremiah is confronting Judah’s stubborn idolatry and false security. On the surface they may claim loyalty to God, but their “heart” (the inner core—thinking, willing, desiring) is turned elsewhere.

- **“Deceitful” and “desperately wicked”**  
  The Hebrew words point to:
  - *Deceitful / crooked / insidious*—bent, not straight; able to mislead.
  - *Desperately sick / incurable*—a condition beyond self-remedy, not a minor flaw.

- **“Who can know it?”**  
  This is rhetorical: humans can’t fully know or trust their own hearts.
  The answer follows immediately: **God can**—  
  > “I the LORD search the heart, I test the reins (inner self)…”

So the passage contrasts:

- Human self-knowledge: limited, distorted, often self-serving.
- God’s knowledge: perfect, penetrating, just, and the basis for his judgment.

### 2. Pessimistic or realistic?

It’s realistic about fallen human nature, especially about **self-deception**, not nihilistic about all human value.

The Bible holds two truths together:

1. **Humans are made in God’s image** (Gen 1), capable of real love, justice, creativity.
2. **The heart is corrupted and self-deceiving** (Jer 17; also Prov 28:26; Rom 3).

Jeremiah 17 leans into the second truth to shatter complacency and false confidence. It says:

- Don’t treat your own heart as a neutral, transparent guide.
- Don’t assume that because something feels sincere or right, it is.

That’s realism, not mere pessimism. It explains why people can:

- Genuinely believe they’re doing good while doing harm.
- Justify almost anything when it serves their desires or fears.

### 3. How to talk about motives if the heart is unreliable

This passage doesn’t mean we must be silent about motives; it means we must be **humble and cautious**.

#### a) Talking about *your own* motives

1. **Use modest, provisional language.**  
   Instead of “My motives are pure,” say:
   - “As far as I can see, my motive is…”
   - “I *think* I’m doing this to serve God/others, but I know my heart is mixed.”

2. **Assume mixed motives are normal.**  
   Often we want:
   - To love God/others  
   *and also*  
   - To look good, be safe, be admired, win.

   Don’t wait for motives to feel perfectly pure before acting; instead, act in obedience while being honest that refinement is needed.

3. **Invite God’s searching rather than trusting your own clarity.**  
   Echo Psalm 139:23–24:
   > “Search me, O God, and know my heart…  
   > see if there is any grievous way in me…”

   Consciously submit your motives to Scripture, prayer, and time. Ask:  
   - Do my actions align with God’s revealed will, or just with my preferences?
   - Does the “fruit” (what this produces) resemble love, joy, peace, etc., or not?

4. **Let others help you see yourself.**  
   Since self-deception is the problem,
   - invite correction,  
   - listen when trusted people question your motives,  
   - treat that as God’s kindness, not a threat.

#### b) Talking about *other people’s* motives

If our own hearts are hard to read, other people’s are even more so.

- Be very slow to *assign* motives:  
  Avoid, “You’re only doing that because…”  
  Often, you don’t know; and Jeremiah 17 warns that **even they may not fully know**.

- Focus on **actions and patterns of fruit** rather than speculative heart-reading:
  - “When you did X, it had Y effect,”
  - not, “You did X because you are proud/jealous/etc.”

- When motives must be discussed (e.g., in close relationships, church discipline), approach it as *exploration*, not accusation:
  - “Help me understand what was going on in your heart when you did/said that.”
  - “Do you think there might be any mixture of fear, pride, or self-protection here?”

#### c) Where to place ultimate confidence

Jeremiah 17:10 grounds everything:

> “I the LORD search the heart… to give every man according to his ways…”

So:

- **We do examine motives**, but we don’t treat our assessments as final.
- **We act in obedience and repentance**, trusting that God, who truly knows our hearts, will:
  - expose what needs exposing,
  - forgive what we confess,
  - and ultimately judge justly, even where human understanding is foggy (cf. 1 Cor 4:3–5; 1 John 3:20).

---

In short: Jeremiah 17 is a sober, realistic warning about how easily we fool ourselves, not a denial that we can ever know or speak about motives. It calls for *humility, self-suspicion, and dependence on God* rather than naive confidence in our own hearts—both when we evaluate ourselves and when we speak about others.
