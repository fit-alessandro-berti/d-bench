The Pharisee in Jesus’ parable (Luke 18:9–14) is condemned not by any single flaw but by a **combination of spiritual vices**, all of which stem from a fundamental misunderstanding of righteousness before God. Here’s a breakdown of the issues and why the tax collector’s posture is spiritually superior:

### **What Condemns the Pharisee?**
1. **Pride (Self-Justification)**
   - The Pharisee’s prayer is a **monologue of self-congratulation**, not a dialogue with God. He begins by thanking God that he is *not* like others, implying that his righteousness is self-achieved. His focus is on his own merits ("I fast twice a week, I tithe everything"), treating them as grounds for superiority rather than as gifts of grace.
   - **Problem:** True righteousness is not a human achievement but a gift received humbly (cf. Phil. 3:9). The Pharisee’s pride blinds him to his need for mercy.

2. **Comparison & Contempt for Others**
   - He defines his holiness **negatively**—by how much better he is than "extortioners, adulterers, or this tax collector." His piety is **exclusionary**, built on despising others (v. 9: "who trusted in themselves... and despised others").
   - **Problem:** God’s justice is not a zero-sum game where one’s holiness depends on another’s wickedness. The Pharisee’s contempt reveals a heart far from God’s love (cf. 1 John 4:20).

3. **False Doctrine (Legalism)**
   - While his actions (fasting, tithing) are outwardly commendable, he treats them as **meritorious works** that earn God’s favor. This reflects a **works-based righteousness**, which Jesus elsewhere critiques (Matt. 23:23–28).
   - **Problem:** He mistakes the *means* of piety (fasting, tithing) for the *end* (a humble, repentant heart). His religion is **performative**, not transformative.

4. **Self-Display (Hypocrisy)**
   - His prayer is **theatrical**—he "stood" prominently (contrasted with the tax collector "standing afar off") and prayed "with himself" (v. 11, Gk. *pros heauton*, implying a self-referential, almost narcissistic prayer). He uses virtue as a **public spectacle** to reinforce his own status.
   - **Problem:** Jesus repeatedly condemns religious acts done for **human praise** (Matt. 6:1–6). The Pharisee’s piety is a **mask** for self-glorification, not worship.

5. **Lack of Repentance**
   - Unlike the tax collector, the Pharisee **never acknowledges sin**. His prayer is devoid of confession, only self-affirmation.
   - **Problem:** "God resists the proud but gives grace to the humble" (James 4:6). Justification requires recognizing one’s need for mercy (cf. Luke 15:7).

### **Why Is the Tax Collector Justified?**
The tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer because it embodies **theology in practice**:
1. **Humility** – He stands "afar off," a physical expression of his unworthiness (cf. Isa. 6:5). He does not even lift his eyes, a sign of deep contrition.
2. **Honest Self-Assessment** – He calls himself "a sinner" (v. 13), acknowledging his need for mercy. Unlike the Pharisee, he does not compare himself to others but measures himself against God’s holiness.
3. **Dependence on Grace** – His prayer is a **plea for mercy** ("God, be merciful to me"), not a boast. This aligns with Jesus’ teaching that the kingdom belongs to the "poor in spirit" (Matt. 5:3).
4. **No Hypocrisy** – His posture and words reveal a **broken heart**, the very thing God desires (Ps. 51:17). There is no pretense, only raw need.

### **Theological Core: Justification by Faith, Not Works**
The parable illustrates **sola gratia** (grace alone) and **sola fide** (faith alone). The Pharisee trusts in his own righteousness (a **false gospel of works**), while the tax collector trusts in God’s mercy (the **true gospel of grace**). As Paul later writes:
> *"For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast."* (Eph. 2:8–9)

### **Conclusion**
The Pharisee is condemned by **pride, comparison, contempt, legalism, and hypocrisy**—all symptoms of a heart that seeks to **earn** God’s favor rather than **receive** it. The tax collector, in contrast, demonstrates the **gospel in miniature**: **acknowledging sin, seeking mercy, and depending entirely on God’s grace**. His justification comes not from his moral resume but from his **humble faith**—the very thing the Pharisee lacks.
