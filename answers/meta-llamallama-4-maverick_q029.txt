## Step 1: Understanding the moral posture required for correction
The verse Galatians 6:1 advises those who are considered spiritual to restore a brother who has been overtaken in a fault. The restoration is to be done "in the spirit of meekness."

## Step 2: Breaking down the components of the required moral posture
The key elements mentioned are "restoration," "spirit of meekness," and "considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted." Restoration implies a corrective action aimed at helping the individual overcome their fault. Meekness is the attitude or spirit in which this restoration is to be carried out. Self-consideration (or self-suspicion) involves recognizing one's own vulnerability to temptation.

## Step 3: Analyzing the role of meekness in restoration
Meekness is not weakness but rather a strength characterized by gentleness and humility. It allows for a non-judgmental and compassionate approach to correcting or restoring someone who has erred. This ensures that the correction is constructive rather than destructive.

## Step 4: Understanding why self-suspicion is crucial
The phrase "considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted" suggests that the person correcting should be mindful of their own susceptibility to similar temptations or faults. This self-awareness fosters humility and prevents a superior or judgmental attitude, which could hinder the effectiveness of the restoration.

## Step 5: Examining why restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion are kept together
These elements are kept together because they are interdependent in achieving the goal of effective restoration. Meekness ensures that the approach is gentle and non-confrontational, while self-suspicion keeps the restorer humble and aware of their own vulnerabilities. Together, they create an environment where restoration can be both effective and respectful, focusing on helping the individual rather than condemning them.

## Step 6: Synthesizing the information to answer the question
The moral posture required for correction involves a combination of gentleness (meekness), a willingness to help (restoration), and humility (self-suspicion). Restoration is done in a spirit of meekness to ensure it's constructive and not hurtful. Self-suspicion is crucial as it keeps the person restoring others aware of their own potential weaknesses, thus maintaining humility.

The final answer is: The moral posture required involves a gentle and humble approach, recognizing one's own susceptibility to similar faults.
