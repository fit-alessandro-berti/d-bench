The elder brother’s deepest problem is not simply an emotion (anger or envy) or even a surface-level concern for fairness. Those are symptoms. His root problem is that he does **not share the father’s heart of grace**; he relates to his father on the basis of *merit and control*, not love.

That shows up in several key ways in the text:

1. **He relates to his father as a servant, not a son.**  
   “Lo, these many years do I *serve* thee” (v. 29). The verb has the sense of slave-labor. He thinks in terms of performance and wages, not relationship and inheritance.

2. **He believes he has earned blessing and been cheated.**  
   “Neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment: and yet thou never gavest me…” (v. 29). He sees obedience as a contract: “I obey; you pay.” Grace to the unworthy feels like injustice to him.

3. **He doesn’t share the father’s love for the younger brother.**  
   He says, “this *thy son*” (v. 30), not “my brother.” The father answers, “this *thy brother* was dead, and is alive again” (v. 32). The elder brother distances himself from the one the father embraces.

4. **He cannot rejoice in mercy.**  
   The whole chapter (Luke 15) is about joy over the lost being found. Heaven rejoices; the father rejoices; the elder brother refuses to go in. His heart is fundamentally out of tune with grace.

So:

- Anger, envy, wounded fairness, and pride in obedience are all real and present.
- But underneath them is a **deep discomfort with mercy itself**, because mercy shatters his merit-based way of relating to the father.
- That reveals the deepest issue: **self-righteousness and estrangement from the father’s heart**. He loves his own record and his perceived rights more than he loves the father or the brother.

In other words, the elder brother’s core problem is that he does not want a kingdom of grace; he wants a kingdom where his goodness puts God in his debt. That’s why he cannot bear it when the father is lavishly kind to someone who hasn’t “earned” it.
