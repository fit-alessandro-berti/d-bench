**Answer:**

Repentance in Isaiah 55:6–7 is deliberately comprehensive because sin is never merely external or merely internal; it is both. The verse pairs two inseparable dimensions:

### 1. “Let the wicked forsake his **way**”  
- “Way” (Heb. *derek*) refers to the **outward conduct**, the habitual course of life, the actual path one walks day by day.  
- It includes concrete actions: the choices, habits, relationships, and behaviors that can be seen by others.  
- Repentance is not real if it leaves the lifestyle untouched. A man may feel sorry while continuing to lie, steal, oppress, or indulge in sexual immorality; Isaiah says that is not repentance.

### 2. “and the unrighteous man his **thoughts**”  
- “Thoughts” (Heb. *machashabah*) refers to the **inward imagination**, the secret intentions, the mental world of desires, fantasies, grudges, pride, and self-justification.  
- The Hebrew word often carries the idea of “plans” or “schemes.” It is not just fleeting thoughts, but the inner architecture of the heart that produces the outward way.  
- Jesus later echoes this when He says that murder and adultery begin in the heart (Matt 5:21–30). Isaiah already knew that true repentance must reach the root.

### Why both are required

The parallelism shows that **partial repentance is no repentance**.  
- If a person cleans up their behavior but nurses bitterness, lust, or covetousness in the mind, the heart is still unrighteous.  
- If a person claims to have “changed their heart” but continues in the same destructive patterns, the claim is self-deception.  

Repentance is therefore a **total reorientation**: the entire person—outer life *and* inner life—turns away from sin and turns toward the Lord.

### The astonishing promise that follows

> “…and let him return unto the Lord, and **he will have mercy upon him**; and to our God, **for he will abundantly pardon**.”

The double emphasis on forsaking both way *and* thoughts is matched by a double assurance of mercy: “he will have mercy” and “he will **abundantly** pardon” (literally “multiply to pardon”).  

This implies three profound things:

1. **God’s mercy is not cheap.** He does not overlook sin; He requires honest, whole-person repentance.  
2. **God’s mercy is not narrow.** Even when both the outward way and the inward thoughts have been thoroughly corrupted, the Lord is still willing to forgive lavishly.  
3. **True repentance is possible and effective.** The very fact that God commands both the external and internal turn, and then promises abundant pardon, means no one is beyond the reach of His grace—if they will genuinely return.

### Summary

“Way” and “thoughts” together expose the full anatomy of sin: what we *do* and what we *are* at the level of desire and imagination. Repentance must therefore be total—both behavioral and heart-deep. Yet the verse ends not in despair but in one of the sweetest gospel promises in the Old Testament: the wicked and the unrighteous who truly turn will discover that God’s mercy is greater than the sum of their sins. He is not reluctant to forgive; He *abundantly* pardons.
