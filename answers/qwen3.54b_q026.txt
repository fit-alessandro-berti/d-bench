Matthew 12:34–35 presents a foundational theological principle regarding human nature, emphasizing a **causal relationship between inner identity and outer expression**. Jesus in this passage argues that there is no separation between the "treasure" (moral character and spiritual substance) stored within a person and the words they produce.

Here is an analysis of how the passage connects speech to these hidden stockpiles, followed by an examination of whether words function as symptoms, choices, or revelations.

### 1. The Connection: Interiority and Exterior Expression
The passage establishes a **mechanism of consistency**. The connection is not merely that our words *reflect* our hearts, but that our speech is the inevitable **outflow** ("abundance") of what is stored within.

*   **Source and Source:** Jesus identifies the heart as "the treasure." In biblical metaphor, treasure implies value accumulated over time. The mouth's function is described by the Greek verb *ekluei*, meaning to bring out or flow from (like water flowing from a stream).
*   **Moral Integrity:** Jesus argues that there is no duality where one has an "evil heart" and "good words." The integrity of the self requires alignment; if the treasure is sinful, the speech will be sinful. If the treasure is good, the speech will be good.
*   **Diagnostic Causality:** The logic of "abundance" implies a capacity that flows naturally. You cannot claim to have a good heart while speaking from an evil treasury; the speech will expose that impossibility.

### 2. Are Words Symptoms?
**Yes**, this passage strongly suggests words are **symptoms**.

*   **Evidence of Condition:** Just as a physical symptom (like a fever) indicates an internal disease, words indicate the spiritual or moral condition of the heart. Jesus says "O generation of vipers." He does not necessarily mean they *are* speaking about vipers; he means their *very existence* and *speech patterns* are symptoms of an viperous nature.
*   **Unconscious Leakage:** The phrase "out of the abundance" implies a natural overflow. It suggests we often speak what is inside us before we consciously decide to say it or even fully realize it ourselves. In this sense, speech functions like a diagnostic tool—it exposes the "disease" (sin) within without necessarily having been generated by an active desire to deceive at that specific moment.

### 3. Are Words Revelations?
**Yes**, specifically as **self-revelation**.

*   **Exposing Truth:** The primary function of speech in this text is to make known the truth concealed in the heart. Even if a person lies (a choice), words reveal the lie or the fear behind it because they come from an "evil treasury."
*   **Identity Exposure:** Jesus argues that you cannot hide your character through speech. A word spoken acts as a revelation of who the speaker truly is to others and, in Matthew's Gospel specifically, to God. This aligns with John 7:34 where Jesus also speaks on the nature of truth, though here it emphasizes the internal condition rather than prophecy.

### 4. Are Words Choices?
**This is more nuanced**, but **Yes**, implicitly as **choices**.

*   **The "Treasure" Formation:** While the verse focuses on the *output* (speech) rather than the immediate act of speaking, the concept of a "treasure" suggests an accumulation built by prior choices. An evil heart implies a history of moral choices (or lack thereof).
*   **Active Production vs. Passive Output:** The passage uses the phrase "bringeth forth." In English translations, this can mean both natural leakage and active bringing. Theologically, Christian ethics view this as a call to choose the *treasure* over time. We can choose what we fill our hearts with (good or evil), which dictates the words that follow.
*   **Limited Agency:** However, compared to other verses on speech (like "for you are all wrong"), Jesus here focuses more on the **inevitability** ("the mouth speaketh") than the specific choice of the utterance itself. He argues we cannot choose to hide our sin from God and others if it is in our heart.

### Conclusion: All Three, But with a Primary Focus on Revelation and Symptom
The passage suggests that words function as **all three**, but they prioritize Revelation and Symptom as their immediate description in this context.

*   **Symptoms:** They provide the evidence of internal character without the speaker necessarily being able to conceal it.
*   **Revelations:** They make visible what is hidden ("out of the abundance," revealing what the heart knows).
*   **Choices:** They are the result of choices made over time (formation of the treasure) and reflect moral responsibility for one's state.

Jesus uses these words to argue that we are accountable not just because we choose to sin, but because we cannot lie about our own nature long enough to avoid recognition. The "generation of vipers" could not speak good things because their moral stockpiles were already compromised through their choices and actions over time.

In summary, speech in this text acts as a **mirror**: it reveals (Revelation) what is inside and diagnoses (Symptom) the soul's condition, while its content itself points back to the accumulated moral choices of the speaker.
