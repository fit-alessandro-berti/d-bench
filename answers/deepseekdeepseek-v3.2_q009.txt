This passage defines victory over evil not as conquest through force or retaliation, but as **transformation through unexpected, proactive goodness.**  

**1. How it defines victory over evil:**  
Victory here is **moral and spiritual**, not tactical or vengeful. It rejects the conventional cycle of "evil for evil." Instead, it reframes winning as:  
- Breaking the chain of hatred by refusing to be morally deformed by retaliation (“Be not overcome of evil”).  
- Actively defeating evil’s influence by responding with goodness that may awaken conscience or shame in the enemy (“overcome evil with good”).  
- Trusting that God’s justice and human conscience are more powerful than returning harm for harm.  

The “coals of fire” image (from Proverbs 25:21–22) is debated: some interpret it as a metaphor for **burning shame or conviction** that could lead an enemy to repentance, not as a hidden revenge tactic. In Paul’s context, he likely means that kindness to an enemy may morally overwhelm them, possibly leading to reconciliation.

**2. Is it weak, manipulative, or genuinely strong?**  
- **Weak?** Superficially, it might seem passive or weak, but in practice, **it takes immense strength** to choose kindness toward someone who harms you, to maintain moral integrity without surrendering to bitterness.  
- **Manipulative?** If the motive is purely to cause guilt or harm (“heap coals” as retaliation), it could be manipulative. But Paul’s command in context (Romans 12:9–21) stresses sincere love, leaving vengeance to God, and seeking peace. The good act is to be genuine, not a disguised attack.  
- **Genuinely strong?** Yes — because it requires self-control, courage, and faith in a moral universe. It seeks to overcome the evil *act* without destroying the *person*, aiming for redemption rather than defeat of the enemy.  

In essence, **true victory is overcoming evil’s power to corrupt one’s own character, while also potentially redeeming the wrongdoer** — a doubly transformative act that reflects the radical ethic of Jesus’ teaching.
