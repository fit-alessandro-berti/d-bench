Short answer: This verse calls for relational, covenantal reliance on God that subordinates, but does not discard, human reason. It rejects self-sufficiency, not thinking; it asks for humble self-distrust, not anti-intellectualism.

What the words mean
- Trust (batach): place your weight on, take refuge in—a personal, loyal dependence.
- Heart (lev): the whole inner person—mind, will, affections.
- Lean not: don’t make your own perspective the final support.
- Acknowledge him (yada): know, recognize, submit to him in every sphere.
- He will direct your paths: he will make your way straight—clarifying and guiding the course of life, especially morally.

Why this is not anti-intellectual
- Proverbs exalts wisdom, knowledge, and understanding (1:7; 2:1–6; 4:5–7). The same book that says “lean not” also commands, “get understanding.”
- The problem is not using understanding but absolutizing one’s own, uncorrected viewpoint (12:15; 14:12).
- God ordinarily guides through means: Scripture, reason renewed by the fear of the Lord, counsel, and prudent planning (15:22; 16:3,9; James 1:5).

Humble self-distrust vs. anti-intellectualism

Humble self-distrust:
- Posture: “My perspective is limited and biased; I need God’s Word, Spirit, and correction.”
- Practice: Pray, search Scripture, gather facts, seek wise counsel, weigh tradeoffs, then act in faith.
- Authority: Lets reason be ministerial (serving God’s revelation), not magisterial (ruling over it).
- Fruit: Teachability, repentance when shown wrong, steady obedience even when outcomes are uncertain.

Anti-intellectualism:
- Posture: Suspicion of thinking and learning as such.
- Practice: Neglects study, expertise, and counsel; baptizes impulse as “leading.”
- Authority: Replaces God’s revealed wisdom with feelings or slogans.
- Fruit: Presumption, poor stewardship, and resistance to correction.

How to “trust and not lean” in practice
- Begin with God: Pray for wisdom; submit desires to his character and commands.
- Attend to revelation: Let clear Scripture set the boundaries of your options.
- Use your mind: Gather data, project consequences, count costs. Thinking is part of trusting.
- Seek plurality of counsel: Invite mature, godly, and qualified voices to challenge your plan.
- Hold plans loosely: “If the Lord wills” (James 4:15). Be ready to revise.
- Act and accept: Obey the light you have; entrust results and unknowns to him.

Rule of thumb: Do not lean on your understanding as your final trust, but do use your understanding as God’s ordinary tool—shaped by Scripture, humbled by prayer, checked by counsel, and yielded to God’s lordship.
