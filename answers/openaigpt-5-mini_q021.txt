The proverb links pride to “destruction” and a “fall” because arrogance systematically produces the cognitive, social and moral conditions that make collapse likely — not merely a sense of guilt after the fact.

1. Pride blinds.  
   - Cognitive: Pride narrows perception. A proud person is less likely to admit ignorance, listen to correction, or notice risk. Psychologically this shows up as overconfidence, selective attention, and motivated reasoning (the modern parallels are the Dunning–Kruger effect and confirmation bias).  
   - Moral: Pride suppresses honest self‑appraisal and conscience; it makes faults invisible or excusable. Spiritually and emotionally, that is called “blindness” in many biblical texts because it prevents repentance and learning.

2. Blindness leads to collapse.  
   - Practical: If you ignore advice and warning signs, you make poor decisions and take reckless risks. If you overreach to preserve pride or prestige, you overextend yourself and fall.  
   - Social: Pride alienates allies and provokes opposition. The proud lose the very support and checks that prevent disaster, and their self‑seeking behavior can invite retaliation or collapse of relationships and institutions.

3. The contrast with “the lowly” explains the moral psychology the proverb prefers.  
   - Humility (being “lowly”) means teachability, receptivity to others, and willingness to take a modest place in the social order. That makes correction and mutual support possible, and it preserves conscience and character.  
   - The proverb’s point — “better to be of an humble spirit with the lowly, than to divide the spoil with the proud” — values steady moral integrity and communal belonging over temporary gain, prestige, or spoils acquired through arrogance. It implies that the proud’s winnings are poisonous: they come at the cost of character and security.

In short: pride fosters self‑deception and social isolation; those together produce mistakes and loss. Humility keeps a person open, corrected, and connected — a safer, more flourishing moral stance than joining in proud ambition for short‑term gain.
