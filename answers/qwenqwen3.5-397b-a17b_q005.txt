The command in Matthew 5:44–45 is widely considered one of the most radical ethical imperatives in human history. To understand why it is difficult to take seriously, and to categorize its nature, we must look at human psychology, the definition of "love" in this context, and the ultimate goal of the instruction.

Here is an analysis of why enemy-love is so challenging and what kind of virtue it actually represents.

### 1. Why is enemy-love so difficult to take seriously?

The difficulty lies in the fact that this command runs counter to three fundamental human drivers:

*   **Evolutionary Biology (Tribalism):** Humans are hardwired for "in-group/out-group" dynamics. Historically, survival depended on identifying threats and neutralizing them. Retribution serves as a deterrent; loving an enemy removes the deterrent and feels like an existential risk.
*   **Intuitive Justice (Reciprocity):** The natural human sense of fairness is reciprocal ("eye for an eye"). When someone harms us, our internal scale demands balance. To love the aggressor feels like letting them off the hook, which can feel like a betrayal of justice or a dismissal of the victim's pain.
*   **Emotional Integrity:** We tend to believe our emotions should match our actions. If someone hurts us, we feel anger. To act lovingly while feeling anger creates cognitive dissonance. It feels hypocritical or emotionally dishonest to "bless" someone you internally resent.

### 2. Is it Sentimental, Strategic, or Moral Strength?

To categorize this command, we must define what Jesus means by "love." In the Greek New Testament, the word used is *agape*, which refers to self-giving will and action, not *phileo* (affection) or *eros* (romantic passion).

#### A. It is not Sentimental
If enemy-love were sentimental, it would require us to *like* our enemies or have warm feelings toward them. That is psychologically impossible on command.
*   **The Reality:** You can pray for a persecutor without enjoying their company. You can seek the good of a hater without trusting them with your safety.
*   **The Verdict:** It is not sentimental because it does not rely on emotion. It is a **volitional act**. It is something you *do*, not something you *feel*.

#### B. It has Strategic Elements
History shows that enemy-love can function as a potent strategy for social change. Figures like Martin Luther King Jr. and Mahatma Gandhi utilized non-violent resistance, which is rooted in this ethic.
*   **The Mechanism:** Refusing to retaliate breaks the cycle of violence. It refuses to let the enemy dictate your behavior. By returning good for evil, you disrupt the expected script of conflict, often disarming the aggressor morally.
*   **The Limit:** However, if viewed *only* as strategy, it becomes manipulation. If I love you only to defeat you or make you look bad, it is not truly love. Strategy is a byproduct, not the root.

#### C. It is a Deeper Form of Moral Strength
This is the primary category. Enemy-love requires a transcendence of the ego.
*   **Self-Control:** It requires the strength to absorb pain without transmitting it. As the writer C.S. Lewis noted, "Everyone says forgiveness is a lovely idea, until they have something to forgive."
*   **Freedom:** When you hate an enemy, you are bound to them; your emotional state is dictated by their actions. When you love them (seek their good), you reclaim agency. You are no longer a reaction to their evil.
*   **The Verdict:** It is the ultimate moral strength because it conquers the internal impulse for vengeance. It is easier to kill an enemy than to pray for them.

### 3. The Theological Anchor: "That ye may be the children of your Father"

The text provides the reason for this difficulty: **Imitation of God.**

The passage argues that God's benevolence is impartial ("sun to rise on the evil and on the good"). Nature does not withhold rain based on morality.
*   **The Challenge:** To take this seriously is to accept a theocentric view of justice. It suggests that vengeance belongs to God, not humans.
*   **The Transformation:** The goal is not necessarily to change the *enemy*, but to change the *disciple*. By acting like the Father, the believer becomes a "child" of the Father. The moral strength comes from aligning one's will with the divine nature.

### Conclusion

Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it asks us to override our survival instincts and our intuitive sense of retributive justice.

It is **not sentimental**, because it demands action despite negative emotions.
It is **partially strategic**, because it breaks cycles of violence, but that is not its primary purpose.
It is **fundamentally a form of moral strength**, requiring the immense power to absorb evil without replicating it.

Ultimately, the text presents it as a **spiritual discipline**. It is not a natural human capacity but a cultivated one, designed to align human behavior with the impartial, creative grace of God. To take it seriously is to admit that human justice is insufficient and that breaking the chain of hatred requires a strength greater than the hatred itself.
