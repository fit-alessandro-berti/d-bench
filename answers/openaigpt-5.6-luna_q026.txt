Matthew 12:34–35 presents the heart as a kind of moral storehouse. What has been accumulated there—desires, judgments, loves, resentments, faith, hypocrisy—eventually becomes available for expression:

- **“Out of the abundance”** means speech is not usually an isolated production. It overflows from what is already dominant within.
- **“Treasure”** means a stored supply or reservoir. The heart does not merely contain passing feelings; it has been shaped by what a person values and cultivates.
- **“Bringeth forth”** suggests deliberate drawing out from that storehouse, as a person produces something from a possession.

Thus the passage treats words as **all three—symptoms, choices, and revelations**, though not in exactly the same sense.

### 1. Words are symptoms

Speech is an outward sign of an inward moral condition. Anger, contempt, greed, fear, gratitude, or love tend to make themselves audible. The mouth exposes what has become plentiful in the heart. Jesus’ point is especially directed at the Pharisees: their blasphemous interpretation of his works is not an accidental verbal mistake but evidence of an evil moral disposition.

“Abundance” also suggests that speech can reveal what is dominant rather than what is merely present. A good person may utter a foolish or sinful word; the text is concerned with the moral treasury from which speech habitually comes.

### 2. Words are choices

The language of “bringing forth” does not make people passive machines. People select words, frame accusations, conceal motives, and choose whether to speak or remain silent. The heart supplies the material, but the person actively brings it out.

This is why the surrounding context stresses accountability. In verses 36–37 Jesus says that people will give account for their words. If words were merely involuntary symptoms, they would not be morally assessable in this way. Speech is expressive, but it is also an act of the will.

### 3. Words are revelations

Speech discloses more than a speaker may intend. It reveals what a person regards as true, valuable, threatening, or contemptible. In this context, the Pharisees’ words reveal their posture toward Jesus and, ultimately, toward God’s work in him.

Words can of course be deceptive or strategically managed. A single sentence is not an infallible diagnosis of someone’s entire character. But sustained patterns of speech tend to uncover the “treasure” of the heart, especially under pressure, conflict, or moral testing.

The passage therefore does not teach merely, “Watch your vocabulary.” Its deeper claim is that **speech reform cannot be separated from heart reform**. One may temporarily restrain the mouth, but the lasting remedy is to change the storehouse from which the mouth draws. In Matthew’s larger presentation, that transformation is connected with repentance, faith, and becoming a good tree that bears good fruit.

So words are:

- **symptoms**, because they overflow from inward character;
- **choices**, because speakers intentionally bring them forth;
- **revelations**, because they disclose the heart’s accumulated moral orientation.

The same words may be externally polished while the treasure remains evil; conversely, genuinely renewed speech arises from a renewed heart.
