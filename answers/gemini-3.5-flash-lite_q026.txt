Matthew 12:34–35 uses the metaphor of a financial or material treasury to connect a person’s external speech directly with their internal moral condition. To answer your question directly: **the passage suggests that words are symptoms, choices, *and* revelations, functioning simultaneously as evidence of moral reality, the output of a prior accumulation, and the active deployment of one's inner character.**

Here is a breakdown of how the passage connects speech to these hidden moral stockpiles:

### 1. Words as "Symptoms" (The Diagnostic View)
In verse 34, Jesus asks: *"how can ye, being evil, speak good things? for out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh."* 
* **The Connection:** Here, speech is treated as a natural, involuntary symptom of an underlying condition. Just as a physical fever is a symptom of an internal infection, corrupt speech is the inevitable outward symptom of a corrupt heart. 
* **The Implication:** You cannot permanently fake the symptom without treating the source. If the "abundance" (the overflow or fullness) of the heart is evil, the mouth will eventually betray that reality. Speech acts as a reliable diagnostic tool for spiritual health.

### 2. Words as "Revelations" (The Evidentiary View)
In verse 35, Jesus speaks of bringing things out of a "treasure" (*thesauros*, meaning a storehouse, repository, or treasury): *"A good man out of the good treasure of the heart bringeth forth good things..."*
* **The Connection:** The heart is pictured as a locked vault or a hidden storehouse where a person accumulates values, beliefs, memories, and moral dispositions over time. Speech is the act of opening the vault and displaying its contents to the public.
* **The Implication:** Words reveal what a person truly values. What comes out in casual conversation, under pressure, or in moments of unguarded emotion serves as an X-ray of the soul, exposing whether the hidden stockpile is filled with bitterness, greed, grace, or wisdom.

### 3. Words as "Choices" (The Agency View)
While the passage emphasizes the heart as a preexisting storehouse, the language of *bringing forth* (*prosphero* / *ekballo*) also implies moral agency and action. 
* **The Connection:** A treasury doesn't empty itself; a person goes into their storehouse, selects an item, and brings it out. Therefore, speaking is depicted as a deliberate output. 
* **The Implication:** Humans are responsible for what they store and what they release. While words flow from the heart, the heart itself is shaped by what a person dwells on, embraces, and allows to take root. Thus, choosing one's words—and by extension, guarding one's heart—is a matter of profound moral responsibility.

### Conclusion
In this passage, Jesus bridges the gap between the internal invisible realm (the heart) and the external visible realm (speech). Words are not trivial, isolated events; they are **revelations** of the inner vault, **symptoms** of an overflowing spiritual condition, and the result of moral **choices** regarding what one hoards and distributes from within.
