## The sharpness of the image

Several devices are working at once, and they interlock:

**1. Exaggeration to the point of slapstick.** A *karphos* is a fleck of chaff or sawdust; a *dokos* is a load-bearing roof timber. Jesus doesn't say "your fault is somewhat larger" — he makes it grotesquely, comically larger. The absurdity is load-bearing: the scene is unforgettable, and the laugh it provokes is a trap, because the joke is on the hearer.

**2. Same substance, different scale.** Both obstructions are wood. The critic's fault is not a different *kind* of thing from the brother's — it's the same material, vastly magnified. The metaphor quietly encodes a psychological observation: the judgmental person typically condemns in others an inflated version of what he himself is.

**3. The eye is the whole point.** The obstruction sits in the organ of sight, and the passage is saturated with vision-language: *beholdest, considerest, see clearly*. Moral correction is an act of perception — and the would-be corrector is precisely the one whose perception is most obstructed. The man most eager to judge is the least equipped to.

**4. The verbs are precise.** In Greek there's a contrast between *blepeis* ("you notice" — casual sight) the speck and *ou katanoeis* ("you do not consider" — deep, reflective perception) the beam. Sharp acuity outward, no reflective attention inward. That is an exact anatomy of how hypocrisy pays attention.

**5. The operation is physically impossible.** Removing a speck from an eye is the most delicate procedure imaginable — it demands closeness, steadiness, the patient's trust. A man with a beam in his own eye cannot even *approach* the other eye; the timber would strike the brother first. So the image encodes not merely the wickedness of hypocritical correction but its practical failure: it cannot help, only injure.

**6. Theatrical irony.** *Hypokritēs* means stage-actor. The scene is staged as a performance — a man plays the role of oculist while disqualifyingly blind. And note the timing: "and, *behold*, a beam is in thine own eye" — the beam is suddenly pointed out mid-sentence, visible to everyone but the speaker. Dramatic irony in miniature.

**7. The interrogative form.** "Why beholdest *thou*...?" Jesus doesn't describe hypocrites in the third person; he interrogates the hearer, and asks about motive (*why?*). The hearer is made to supply the indictment himself.

## Why it doesn't forbid correction

The hinge of the passage is the sequence in verse 5: "**first** cast out the beam... **and then** shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote." The sentence ends with the mote actually being removed. Jesus prescribes an order of operations, not a moratorium.

- **The mote is real.** The passage never suggests the brother has no fault or that the speck doesn't matter. Eye irritants hurt; getting one out is a genuine kindness. Correction is a good — which is exactly why it must be done by someone who can see.

- **Self-examination yields sight, not silence.** "Then shalt thou *see clearly*." The problem with hypocritical judgment is epistemic before it is moral: the beam-bearer missees, misdiagnoses, mishandles. Self-judgment is the *qualification* for helping others, not a substitute for it.

- **It also yields gentleness.** A man who knows what it cost to extract a beam from his own eye will not go at his brother's speck with pliers and contempt. He has been a patient; now he can be a surgeon. Compare Galatians 6:1 — restore the fallen "in a spirit of meekness; considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted."

- **The other person is "thy brother," three times.** The frame is familial and restorative, not punitive. What is condemned is censoriousness — standing *above* someone in judgment — not the care that gets close enough to help.

- **The wider chapter confirms it.** The same discourse that says "judge not" (7:1) requires discerning dogs from swine (7:6) and false prophets by their fruits (7:15–20). Matthew 7 is not against discrimination; it is against asymmetry — applying to others a standard never applied to oneself (cf. Romans 2:1, 21).

So the passage's target is not correction but *sequence*. Hypocrisy is the inversion of the order: performing surgery before being healed. The remedy is not to retire the surgeons but to heal them — so that the correction which finally happens is clear-eyed, gentle, and actually able to reach the eye.
