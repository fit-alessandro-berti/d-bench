The elder brother's deepest problem is a complex interplay of several of the options you've presented, but **discomfort with mercy itself** seems to be the most overarching issue, which then fuels his other negative emotions.

Let's break it down:

*   **Anger:** This is clearly present ("And he was angry, and would not go in"). It's a reaction to what he perceives as an injustice.
*   **Envy:** While he might feel a pang of envy for the prodigal son's undeserved celebration, his primary grievance isn't that *he* didn't get a party. It's that the prodigal son, who squandered everything, received such lavish recognition.
*   **Wounded Fairness:** This is a very strong contender. He feels his years of faithful service have gone unrecognized and unrewarded, while the prodigal son's recklessness has been met with extreme celebration. He believes the scales of justice are tipped against him.
*   **Pride in Obedience:** This is also a significant factor. He proudly points to his years of faithful service ("Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment"). His obedience is the basis of his claim to fairness, and he seems to have a transactional view of his relationship with his father – service should equal reward.
*   **Discomfort with Mercy Itself:** This is the most profound underlying issue. The father's response isn't about the prodigal son's worthiness or his past transgressions. It's about **grace and forgiveness**. The elder brother cannot grasp this concept. He operates from a system of merit and desert. For him, the prodigal son *deserved* punishment, not celebration. The father's unconditional love and willingness to welcome the prodigal back without demanding repentance or apology (at least not in the immediate context of the celebration) is what throws the elder brother off balance. He cannot comprehend love and forgiveness that doesn't depend on the recipient's actions.

**Why "Discomfort with Mercy" is the deepest:**

The anger, wounded fairness, and pride in obedience are all *symptoms* of his inability to accept the father's merciful embrace of the prodigal son. If he truly understood or accepted mercy, his pride in obedience wouldn't turn into resentment, and his sense of fairness wouldn't be so easily wounded by an act of grace. He sees the situation through the lens of legalism and earning, rather than through the lens of unconditional love and forgiveness.

The father's response, "Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine," attempts to address this. He's trying to show the elder son that his constant presence and faithfulness have *already* placed him in a position of belonging and inherent value, not one that needs to be earned through specific celebrations. However, the elder brother is too entrenched in his framework of earned reward and deserved punishment to fully grasp this.

Therefore, the elder brother's deepest problem is his inability to embrace or understand the nature of **mercy** and **grace**, which is the very heart of the father's actions and the Gospel message this parable represents.
