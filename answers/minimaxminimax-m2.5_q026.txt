This passage from Matthew 12:34–35 presents a vivid and layered teaching on the relationship between the heart and speech. Your question touches on multiple dimensions of this connection, and the text itself offers several interconnected insights.

### 1. The Core Connection: Abundance and Outflow
The central phrase is: **"For out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh."** This establishes speech as an **overflow** or **expression** of what is already present inside. The heart is depicted as a storage container—"the good treasure" or "the evil treasure"—and speech is the natural result of that internal stock. Here, words are indeed a **symptom** of an underlying condition, much like a tree is known by its fruit (cf. Matthew 12:33).

### 2. The Imagery of the "Stockpile"
- **"Treasure"** suggests something accumulated over time—habits, intentions, desires, and choices that have been stored within.
- The parallel structures (good heart → good things; evil heart → evil things) indicate a consistent, causal relationship. It's not that one good thought produces one good word, but that a **character** (the "good man" or "evil man") inevitably produces corresponding words.

### 3. The Nature of Words: Symptoms, Choices, and Revelations
Your question asks if words are symptoms, choices, revelations, or all three. The passage supports **all three**, in a layered sense:

- **As Symptom:** Words reveal the moral "disease" or health of the heart. They are diagnostic.
- **As Choice:** The phrase "how can ye... speak good things?" implies that one *cannot* speak what is not genuinely in the heart. This is not mere performance; it challenges the possibility of hypocrisy. The words are a **volitional outflow**—you choose to speak from what you truly are.
- **As Revelation:** The passage underscores that speech is **transparently revealing**. The goal is not merely to control the mouth, but to transform the heart. Jesus is exposing the fallacy of external ritual without internal reality (as He did with the Pharisees, e.g., Matthew 23:25–28).

### 4. The Warning and Exhortation
The address "O generation of vipers" is a severe rebuke, linking these words to a **wicked character** (cf. Genesis 3:1; Matthew 3:7). The passage functions as a warning: **do not deceive yourself** that you can speak rightly while your heart is corrupt. The "abundance" suggests a fullness—these weren't isolated slip-ups but the natural speech of an entrenched evil heart.

### Synthesis
In summary, the passage teaches that:
1. **Speech is moral evidence.** Words are not neutral; they are tied to the moral state of the inner person.
2. **The heart is the source.** Transformation must begin internally (cf. Luke 6:45: "out of the fullness of the heart the mouth speaks").
3. **Words are relational.** They define the person—whether "good" or "evil"—and reveal the true allegiance of the heart.

Thus, your intuition that words are "symptoms, choices, **and** revelations" is well-founded. This passage presents them as the inevitable, revealing, and morally significant outflow of the hidden heart. It is a call for interior integrity, not merely verbal discipline.
