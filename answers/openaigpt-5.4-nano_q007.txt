Luke 6:37 is often summarized as “don’t judge,” but that can’t mean “moral discernment never matters,” because Scripture elsewhere clearly requires moral evaluation and warns about false teaching and wrongdoing. The difference is between **discernment** (rightly distinguishing what is good/evil and what is true/false) and the **appetite to condemn** (using judgment as a weapon, to put oneself above others, or to write them off as beyond mercy).

### 1) “Judge not” is not “never make moral distinctions”
- In Luke 12:57–56 Jesus tells people to “judge for yourselves” about what is right in their situation.
- In John 7:24 he says, “judge with right judgment.”
- Elsewhere believers are told to “test” teachings (e.g., 1 John 4:1) and to recognize “false prophets” (Matthew 7:15).
So the issue in Luke 6 is not the presence of moral discernment; it’s the **spirit and motive** behind it.

### 2) Discernment aims at truth and restoration
**Discernment** tries to see clearly:
- What is actually true?
- What behavior is destructive or sinful?
- What correction is needed?
- How can a person be helped toward repentance and healing?

Discernment can be firm and even corrective without being cruel. Think of it like a doctor diagnosing an illness: the point isn’t to humiliate the patient, but to treat what’s harming them.

### 3) Condemnation is a misuse of moral judgment
**Condemnation** goes beyond evaluating the act and turns into an attitude about the person—often marked by:
- **Self-righteousness** (“I’m the standard; you’re the problem”)
- **Hostility** (enjoying the verdict more than seeking the good)
- **Preemptive finality** (“you’re done; mercy doesn’t apply”)
- **Impatience with repentance** (refusing to let truth lead to reconciliation)
- **Blindness to one’s own faults** (the immediate context in Luke 6:38–42 about seeing the speck vs. the plank)

So “condemn not” targets the *kind* of judgment that claims ultimate authority over another person’s standing before God—something humans should not seize. It’s not merely “don’t call wrong things wrong,” but “don’t posture as judge in a way that crushes mercy.”

### 4) The reciprocal promise shows the goal: mercy-shaped accountability
Luke 6:37 isn’t saying accountability disappears. It says:
- If you refuse a merciless posture, God’s judgment toward you will be merciful.
- If you love forgiveness, you will receive forgiveness.

That implies moral clarity still exists, but **the tone and outcome** should match God’s character: truth that aims at renewal rather than verdict that seeks to destroy.

### 5) A practical distinction (quick test)
Ask: *Am I trying to help someone see and change, or am I trying to settle the score?*
- If the heart is “so that you might repent and be restored,” it resembles discernment.
- If the heart is “so that I can punish, dismiss, or feel superior,” it resembles condemnation.

### Bottom line
Luke 6:37 is different from saying moral judgments never matter because it condemns **the condemning spirit**—the enjoyment of verdict, the refusal of mercy, and the usurping of God’s final authority—while still allowing (and requiring) **discernment** that distinguishes good from evil and calls people toward repentance and healing.
