The trust described in Proverbs 3:5–6 is not a blind leap into the dark, nor is it a rejection of the mind. Rather, it is a **relational orientation of the will**. To understand the specific nature of this trust, we must distinguish between *humble self-distrust* and *anti-intellectualism*.

### 1. The Nature of the Trust: Wholehearted Dependence
The command to trust “with all thine heart” implies a comprehensive reliance. In the biblical context, the "heart" is not merely the seat of emotion, but the center of the intellect, will, and volition. Therefore, this trust is an active decision to anchor one’s security and direction in the character and sovereignty of God rather than in one's own perceived stability.

### 2. Humble Self-Distrust vs. Anti-Intellectualism
The phrase “lean not unto thine own understanding” is frequently misunderstood as a call to stop thinking. However, a critical distinction must be made:

**Anti-Intellectualism** is the belief that reason, evidence, and intellectual rigor are inherently evil or useless. It suggests that faith and reason are enemies, and that one must "turn off their brain" to believe in God. This view treats the mind as a liability to be discarded.

**Humble Self-Distrust**, conversely, is the recognition of the *limitation* and *fallibility* of human reason. It is not a denial of the mind's utility, but a denial of the mind's **sufficiency**. 

*   **The Analogy of the Map:** Imagine a traveler with a map (human understanding) and a guide who actually knows the terrain (God). Anti-intellectualism would be throwing the map away and walking blindly into a ditch. Humble self-distrust is using the map but recognizing that if the map contradicts the Guide, the Guide is to be trusted.
*   **The Issue of Perspective:** The "lean" mentioned in the text refers to the point of ultimate support. To "lean" on one's own understanding is to make one's own logic the final authority. Humble self-distrust acknowledges that human perspective is finite, clouded by bias, and limited by time, whereas God's perspective is infinite.

### 3. The Role of "Acknowledging Him"
The instruction to "in all thy ways acknowledge him" bridges the gap between faith and intellect. To "acknowledge" (*yada* in Hebrew) implies an experiential knowledge. 

This means that the believer is encouraged to bring their intellect, their planning, and their reasoning *to* God. It is not a call to stop planning, but a call to plan *under the lordship of God*. When we acknowledge Him, we submit our logic to His revealed truth.

### Summary
The trust asked for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is **epistemological humility**. It is the conviction that while our minds are gifts to be used, they are not the ultimate source of truth or the final guarantee of success. 

**Anti-intellectualism says:** "My mind is useless."
**Humble self-distrust says:** "My mind is useful, but it is not infallible; therefore, I will anchor my life in the One who is."
