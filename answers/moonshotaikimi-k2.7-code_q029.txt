In Galatians 6:1 Paul does not describe **correction as a courtroom proceeding** but as a **healing intervention**. The moral posture required is a three‑stranded stance: **restorative purpose, gentle manner, and humble self‑awareness**. The three are inseparable because without each other the act of correction easily becomes proud, harsh, or merely punitive.

### 1. Restoration, not punishment
Paul says the goal is to **“restore”** (*katartizō*) the person. The word originally meant to set a broken bone or to mend a net. The spiritual person is therefore a **healer**, not a prosecutor. The aim is not to win an argument, assert superiority, or expel the offender, but to return the brother or sister to spiritual health and fellowship.

### 2. Meekness, not dominance
This restoration must be done **“in the spirit of meekness”** (*prautēs*). Meekness is not weakness; it is strength under control. It is the opposite of an angry, self‑important, or condescending tone. Because the fallen person is still addressed as **“brethren,”** the corrector must treat him as a fellow member of the body, not as a contemptible failure. Meekness makes room for the Holy Spirit to work rather than driving the sinner further into shame or defensiveness.

### 3. Self‑suspicion, not self‑righteousness
Paul immediately adds: **“considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted.”** The corrector must look at the fallen person and see a mirror of his own vulnerability. This is a deliberate check against spiritual pride. The one who is “spiritual” is not someone who imagines himself beyond sin, but someone who knows that the same flesh, the same temptations, and the same circumstances could trip him up too. This keeps correction from becoming judgmental or prideful.

### Why the three must stay together

| Element | Why it needs the others |
|---|---|
| **Restoration** needs **meekness** because a broken bone is not set with a hammer. Harsh correction wounds rather than heals. |
| **Meekness** needs **self‑suspicion** because gentleness without honest humility can become patronizing or fake. |
| **Self‑suspicion** needs **restoration** because if I know I too am capable of falling, I will not cast away the fallen; I will want to mend them, knowing I may one day need the same grace. |

In short, Paul links these three because **true spiritual authority is always cruciform**: it seeks the other person’s recovery, speaks gently, and never forgets its own weakness. This is what it means to “bear one another’s burdens, and so fulfil the law of Christ” (Gal 6:2).
