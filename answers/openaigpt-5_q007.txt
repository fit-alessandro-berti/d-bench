Short answer: Jesus isn’t forbidding moral discernment; he’s forbidding the self-exalting posture that delights to condemn. He calls for clear moral vision that aims to heal, not a censorious spirit that plays God.

Context and words
- The command sits under “Be merciful, as your Father is merciful” (Luke 6:36). The issue is posture, not the existence of right and wrong.
- Luke uses two verbs: judge (krino) and condemn (katadikazo). The first is evaluating; the second is pronouncing sentence. Jesus forbids taking up the bench as if we were the final court.
- The very next images (the speck and the log, Luke 6:41–42) require discernment: you do need to see a speck as a problem—but only after you’ve dealt with your own log, and then you help, not humiliate.
- Elsewhere Jesus says, “Judge with right judgment” (John 7:24) and “By their fruits you will know them” (Matt 7:16), and the church is told to restore the sinner gently (Gal 6:1). So moral judgments matter, but how we make them and what we seek by them matters more.

Discernment versus the appetite to condemn
- Aim
  - Discernment seeks the good of the other, the protection of the vulnerable, and the glory of God.
  - Condemnation seeks to elevate self, vent anger, or control; it relishes a guilty verdict.
- Standard
  - Discernment submits to God’s standard and applies it first to oneself.
  - Condemnation applies shifting or selective standards and ignores one’s own faults.
- Method
  - Discernment is careful with facts, listens, tests motives humbly, distinguishes act from person.
  - Condemnation is hasty, assumes motives, traffics in gossip, and collapses a person into a failure.
- Tone
  - Discernment tells the truth in love—firm, clear, and hopeful.
  - Condemnation is harsh, scornful, performative.
- Outcome sought
  - Discernment aims at repentance, restoration, and repair.
  - Condemnation aims at finality—exclusion, shame, and no path back.
- Authority
  - Discernment respects God’s prerogative as final judge and stays within one’s calling (parent, pastor, magistrate).
  - Condemnation usurps God’s seat and delights in passing sentence.
- Measure
  - Discernment remembers “the measure you use will be measured to you” (Luke 6:38; James 2:13). Mercy received becomes mercy given.
  - Condemnation uses a strict measure for others and a soft one for self.

Why moral judgments still matter
- Love requires it. You cannot love your neighbor without identifying what harms them and what helps them (Phil 1:9–10).
- Communities need it. Protecting the weak and correcting the wrongdoer are acts of love when done justly (Matt 18:15–17; 1 Cor 5).
- Scripture models it. Jesus names sin yet offers a way home: “Neither do I condemn you; go, and sin no more.” Truth and mercy together.

Practices that keep discernment from curdling into condemnation
- Examine yourself first; confess specific sins before confronting another.
- Check your aim: Am I moving toward this person’s good, or toward a satisfying takedown?
- Gather facts and listen; slow down your conclusions.
- Speak privately and proportionately; involve others only as needed.
- Keep a door open for repentance; pair boundaries with hope.
- Pray for the person you correct; if you cannot pray blessing, wait before you speak.
- Remember your own mercy-story; let gratitude soften your judgments.

In short, “Judge not” forbids a heart that pronounces final sentence and delights in it. It does not forbid the sober, humble discernment that tells the truth for the sake of mercy, justice, and restoration.
