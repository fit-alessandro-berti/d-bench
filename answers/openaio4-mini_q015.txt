Several features make Jesus’s “mote and beam” image so arresting:

 1. Hyperbolic contrast  
    • A mote (or speck) is utterly inconsequential; a beam (or log) in one’s own eye is grotesquely large.  
    • That absurd mismatch—trying to pick out a tiny speck while blind from a plank—hits us immediately.  

 2. Vivid metaphor  
    • Nearly everyone has felt the irritation of something in the eye, or knows how impossible it is to remove anything when vision is impaired.  
    • By choosing an eye-mote rather than a moral fault in the heart or mind, the metaphor becomes concrete and gut-level.  

 3. Repetition and rhetorical questions  
    • Jesus asks “Why do you see the mote… but not the beam?” and “How will you say to your brother…?”  
    • The repeated “mote/beam” language and the direct “you” draw listeners into the absurdity of hypocritical judgment.  

 4. A stinging surprise  
    • The punch line—“Thou hypocrite, first cast out the beam…”—turns the target’s supposed moral high ground completely upside down.  

Why this isn’t a ban on moral correction  
  • Jesus does not end with “never judge anyone.” Rather, He insists that judgment must begin inwardly.  
  • Only after you’ve “removed the beam” (i.e. dealt honestly and humbly with your own serious faults) can you “see clearly” to help someone with a minor one.  
  • The point is less “stop all correction” than “purge hypocrisy first.” Correction offered from a self-righteous, unexamined heart is worthless—or even harmful.  

In short, the image cuts so sharply because it uses absurd exaggeration plus direct address to expose the folly of condemning small faults when far greater ones are ignored.  Yet Jesus immediately allows—and even envisions—constructive help for others, provided it flows from genuine self-awareness and humility rather than blind, hypocritical judgment.
