This passage (Romans 12:20–21) presents a radical and transformative definition of victory over evil. Victory, in this context, is not the conquest of an enemy through force or retaliation, but the transformation of a hostile relationship through active, selfless love. The strength of God is shown not in power exerted, but in love demonstrated, which has the potential to bring about deep conviction and change in the wrongdoer's heart.
Is the command weak, manipulative, or genuinely strong?
The command is genuinely strong. It is weak if we mistake passivity or appeasement for love. It is manipulative if the secret motive is to "heap coals" out of a desire for revenge disguised as kindness. However, the core teaching calls for a strength that breaks the cycle of harm. It takes profound courage and inner fortitude to respond to animosity with compassion, seeking the genuine good of the other. This "power under control"—choosing a proactive, positive path instead of a reactive, negative one—requires immense spiritual strength.
