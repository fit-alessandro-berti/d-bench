Repentance is described in two dimensions because sin is both a **way of life** and an **inner orientation**.

- **“Let the wicked forsake his way”** refers to the person’s chosen course or pattern of conduct. “Way” is a biblical image for the direction of one’s life: practices, relationships, habits, and decisions.
- **“Let the unrighteous man [forsake] his thoughts”** reaches beneath behavior to the inner world that produces it. The Hebrew word can mean thoughts, plans, purposes, or schemes. It does not refer merely to an involuntary passing thought, but to the settled intentions, judgments, desires, and mental strategies by which a person justifies or pursues evil.

Thus repentance is not merely:

- outward conformity without a changed heart, nor
- inward remorse that leaves one’s conduct unchanged.

The two belong together. A person’s “thoughts” shape his “way,” and a changed way gives evidence that the inner orientation is being turned. The command “let him return unto the Lord” shows that repentance is more than self-improvement: it is a turning **from** one’s sinful course and mindset **to** God himself.

The passage therefore presents mercy as genuinely available even to “the wicked” and “the unrighteous.” God’s pardon is not limited to those whose record is nearly clean. “He will abundantly pardon” stresses the lavishness and readiness of divine forgiveness; mercy is greater than the sin being confessed. This does not make repentance superficial or remove all earthly consequences, but it does mean that no one is excluded from forgiveness merely because the wrong has been deep or habitual.

The urgency of “while he may be found” and “while he is near” calls for response now. It is an invitation, not a suggestion that God is reluctant to forgive. In the following verses, God’s thoughts and ways are higher than ours; one aspect of that height is precisely the surprising abundance of his mercy.
