**Victory over evil, according to Romans 12:20–21, is defined as refusing to let evil dictate the terms of the relationship and instead responding with active, tangible good.** 

The passage does not say “ignore evil,” “pretend evil isn’t there,” or “be nice so it goes away.” It says: **“Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good.”** Victory is therefore measured by who shapes the final outcome—you or the evil done to you. If you repay evil with evil, evil has successfully reproduced itself in you; it has “overcome” you. If you repay evil with good (specifically practical kindness: feeding the hungry enemy, giving drink to the thirsty one), then good has overcome evil because the cycle has been broken at the level of your own heart and actions.

### The “coals of fire” metaphor
This is the most debated image in the passage. In context it is almost certainly **not** a picture of cruel manipulation (“I’ll be nice so God burns them harder”). Two main background interpretations exist:

1. **Refining / conviction** – In ancient metallurgy and in some Proverbs imagery (Prov 25:21–22, from which Paul is quoting), “heaping burning coals” on someone’s head could refer to the process of melting and purifying metal, or to the acute shame and conviction that comes when one is treated with unexpected kindness. The enemy is forced to confront the moral reality of his own hatred in the face of your goodness. This can produce repentance, which is the highest possible defeat of evil.

2. **Divine judgment** – The kindness itself is left in God’s hands as an act of trust. You are not taking vengeance; you are handing the ultimate outcome to God. Your good deed becomes a witness against the enemy if he refuses to repent. Either way, the “coals” are not something *you* are gleefully applying; they are the natural spiritual consequence of goodness colliding with evil.

Paul is therefore giving a radically realistic strategy: **good is stronger than evil at the level of human character and ultimate reality.** Evil’s only real power is to make you become like it. Refuse that transformation and you have already won the decisive battle.

### Is the command weak, manipulative, or genuinely strong?

- **Not weak.** This is not conflict avoidance or sentimental “niceness.” It requires serious self-mastery, courage, and trust in God’s justice. Returning evil for evil is the easy, instinctive, and ultimately weak path; it lets the offender control your soul. Overcoming evil with good is the hard, counter-instinctive path of a free person.

- **Not manipulative.** The motive is not “I’ll secretly punish them with kindness.” The motive is obedience to God’s character and confidence that His way of dealing with evil (seen supremely at the cross) is more powerful than the world’s way. You feed and give drink *because that is what God did for you while you were still His enemy* (Rom 5:8–10). The “coals” are a side-effect, not the goal.

- **Genuinely strong.** It is strength of the highest order: moral, spiritual, and ultimately eschatological. It declares that evil does not get to set the agenda for history or for your heart. Jesus Himself embodied this on the cross—feeding His enemies by dying for them, heaping the ultimate “coals of fire” that either melt them into repentance or stand as judgment against them. The resurrection proved that this way of self-giving love overcame the greatest evil ever committed.

**Summary:**  
The passage defines victory as **the conquest of your own heart and the breaking of evil’s reproductive power through concrete acts of goodness.** It is not weak passivity, nor clever psychological warfare. It is the strong, costly, God-like path that trusts that good is more fundamental to reality than evil, and that God will vindicate that goodness in His own time and way. In the end, the only way to truly defeat evil is to stop it from turning you into itself.
