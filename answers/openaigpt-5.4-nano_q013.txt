Amos rejects their worship not because worship is inherently bad, but because in their case it is *performative*—a religious façade that coexists with injustice. So the issue isn’t “too much worship” or “out of balance,” but *worship that functions as moral cover*.

### Why so sharp?
Amos intensifies the language (“I hate… I despise… I will not accept… I will not hear…”) because the people are not simply mistaken about how to worship; they are *treating worship as a substitute for obedience*. The point is: you can’t keep the rituals while ignoring the moral demands that those rituals were supposed to embody. Sharp rejection signals that God is not merely offering feedback—he is exposing hypocrisy and warning of judgment.

### What moral/spiritual fraud is being exposed?
It’s a form of **religious self-deception and transactional piety**, often called hypocrisy, but Amos makes it specific:

- **Worship as a transaction:** They assume offerings and festival observance can “buy” divine approval, even while they oppress others or pervert justice.
- **Public piety masking private injustice:** The same society that fills temples with songs and sacrifices is unwilling to let “justice” and “righteousness” govern real life.
- **Ritual without moral reality:** The rituals (burnt offerings, peace offerings, music, assemblies) are treated as valid in themselves, despite the absence of ethical faithfulness.

So Amos is attacking **a counterfeit religion**: religious acts offered *while* practicing unrighteousness. The “fraud” is not that they worship, but that they use worship to deny or neutralize God’s demand for justice—replacing righteousness “as a mighty stream” with noise, spectacle, and ceremony.

In short: Amos says the problem is *not imbalance but corruption*—worship that is intended (consciously or unconsciously) to excuse injustice.
