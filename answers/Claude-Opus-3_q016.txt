The elder brother's deepest problem appears to be a lack of understanding and appreciation for the profound nature of grace and forgiveness. His attitude reveals several underlying issues:

1. Envy and resentment: He is envious of the celebration thrown for his younger brother, feeling that his own loyalty and obedience have gone unrecognized.

2. Pride in his own righteousness: He emphasizes his own obedience and service, implying that he deserves better treatment than his prodigal brother.

3. A sense of entitlement: He believes his good behavior should be rewarded and is upset when it seems his brother's misbehavior is being celebrated instead.

4. Lack of compassion: He shows no joy or relief at his brother's return and is unwilling to join in the celebration, indicating a lack of love and forgiveness.

While the elder brother's feelings of anger, envy, and wounded fairness are evident, the root of his problem seems to be a fundamental discomfort with the very concept of mercy and grace. He appears to view his relationship with his father as transactional - his obedience should be rewarded, and his brother's disobedience should be punished. 

However, the father's love and forgiveness transcend this transactional view. The father shows mercy and grace to the repentant younger son, not because he deserves it, but because of the father's unconditional love. This challenges the elder brother's understanding of right and wrong, and he struggles to accept and extend the same mercy and grace to his brother.

In essence, the elder brother's deepest problem is a failure to understand and embrace the transformative power of unmerited forgiveness and love. He is more focused on fairness, obedience, and his own sense of righteousness than on the joy of reconciliation and the depth of his father's love for both sons, regardless of their actions.
