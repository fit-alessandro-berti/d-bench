The command in Matthew 5:44–45 to love one’s enemies is widely considered the most radical, counterintuitive, and difficult teaching in the ethical canon of Western civilization. It is difficult to take seriously because it directly contradicts our biological survival instincts, our psychological need for justice, and our cultural narratives about power. 

To understand why we struggle with it, and to categorize what kind of love Jesus is actually describing, we must examine the three possibilities you raised: sentimental, strategic, and a deeper moral strength.

---

### 1. Why Enemy-Love is Difficult to Take Seriously

* **Biological and Psychological Friction:** Human beings are evolutionarily wired for tribalism and self-preservation. When attacked, the amygdala triggers a "fight or flight" response. Retaliation or distancing feels like survival; loving the aggressor feels like suicide. Furthermore, trauma creates a deep psychological demand for justice and vindication. To pray for a persecutor feels like a betrayal of one’s own pain.
* **The Conflation of Love with Weakness:** Modern culture often mistakes enemy-love for emotional masochism, boundary-less submission, or "letting people walk all over you." If "love" is misunderstood as tolerating abuse or pretending the harm didn't happen, the teaching rightly appears dangerous and naïve.
* **The Tyranny of Retributive Justice:** Our societies are built on the principle of *lex talionis* (an eye for an eye). We find deep, visceral satisfaction in seeing the wicked punished and the scales of justice balanced. Enemy-love refuses to participate in this economy, which makes it feel deeply unjust to our natural sensibilities.

---

### 2. Is it Sentimental?

**No.** Sentimentality reduces love to a warm, fuzzy emotion or a superficial positive attitude. If enemy-love were sentimental, it would require us to *feel* affection for our abusers, which is psychologically impossible and ethically unhealthy. 

The Greek word used for love here is *agape*, which is not a feeling, but a **voluntary, deliberate commitment to seek the ultimate good of another**, regardless of their merit or your emotional state. Jesus pairs this "love" with three active verbs: *bless, do good, pray*. These are actions, not feelings. 

Furthermore, *agape* love is perfectly compatible with establishing firm boundaries, pursuing justice, and stopping an abuser from causing further harm. You can actively work to stop an enemy from doing evil (doing what is best for them and the world) while simultaneously refusing to harbor vindictive hatred in your own heart.

---

### 3. Is it Strategic?

**Partially, but this is insufficient.** There is undeniably a strategic wisdom to enemy-love. Philosophers and activists like Gandhi and Martin Luther King Jr. demonstrated that nonviolent, unretaliatory love disrupts the oppressor’s script. When you refuse to return evil for evil, you break the cycle of violence, expose the moral bankruptcy of the oppressor, and often win the moral high ground, eventually compelling change. 

However, if enemy-love is *only* strategic, it becomes a manipulative tactic—a means to an end. It becomes a weapon of the weak to defeat the strong. But Jesus does not ground this command in political victory; He grounds it in **ontology and theology**. He says we do this *“That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven.”* 

If we only love enemies to defeat them or change them, we are still treating them as a means to our own ends, rather than as image-bearers of God. 

---

### 4. Is it a Deeper Form of Moral Strength?

**Yes. This is the most accurate framing.** Enemy-love is the ultimate display of spiritual and moral muscle. It requires a profound mastery over the ego and the primal urge for vengeance. 

* **The Power of Absorption:** The theologian Dietrich Bonhoeffer noted that the only way to break the chain of evil is for someone to absorb the blow and refuse to pass it on. Retaliation merely multiplies evil. Enemy-love acts as a spiritual shock absorber. It takes immense strength to absorb hatred, process it, and refuse to reflect it back. 
* **The Imitation of God (*Imitatio Dei*):** Jesus grounds the command in the cosmic reality of God’s indiscriminate grace: *“he maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust.”* God does not withhold the necessities of life (sun and rain) based on human moral performance. By loving our enemies, we are stepping into the divine nature. We are declaring that a person’s worth is not determined by their current hostility, but by the fact that they are created and sustained by God.
* **Liberation of the Self:** Hatred is a heavy burden. It chains the victim to the perpetrator, allowing the enemy to dictate the victim’s internal life. To pray for an enemy is an act of radical self-liberation. It severs the toxic bond of hatred and reclaims one’s own soul from the corrosive effects of bitterness.

### Conclusion

Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it is terrifyingly costly. It is not a sentimental platitude for greeting cards, nor is it merely a clever political strategy. It is a **profound, costly, and transformative moral strength**. 

Jesus presents it not as a suggestion for a peaceful life, but as the ultimate diagnostic test of spiritual maturity. It is so difficult precisely because it is the highest summit of human ethics: the ability to look at an enemy, recognize the common grace of the "sun and rain" that sustains them, and choose to will their good, thereby proving oneself to be a true child of God.
