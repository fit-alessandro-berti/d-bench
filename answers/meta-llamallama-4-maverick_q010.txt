The passage from Philippians 2:5-8 is praising a different kind of power, one that is often referred to as "selfless" or "sacrificial" power. This power is characterized by humility, obedience, and a willingness to surrender one's own status and interests for the benefit of others.

In the context of the passage, this power is embodied in Jesus Christ, who, despite being in the form of God and equal with God, humbled himself and took on the form of a servant, ultimately sacrificing himself on the cross.

Humility joined to greatness can feel more unsettling than open domination for several reasons:

1. **Subtlety**: Humility can be a more subtle and insidious form of power, as it doesn't rely on overt displays of strength or authority. Instead, it can be a quiet, persistent, and deeply influential force that can be harder to resist or reject.
2. **Moral authority**: When greatness is combined with humility, it can create a powerful moral authority that is difficult to challenge. This is because humility implies a willingness to serve and put others first, which can be deeply persuasive and admirable.
3. **Unmasking pride**: Humility can also be unsettling because it challenges our own pride and self-importance. When we encounter someone who is genuinely humble and great, it can force us to confront our own shortcomings and biases, which can be uncomfortable and even transformative.
4. **Unpredictability**: Selfless power can be unpredictable, as it doesn't follow the usual rules of power dynamics. It can be difficult to anticipate how someone who is driven by humility and a willingness to serve will act, which can create a sense of uncertainty or unease.

Overall, the passage from Philippians is praising a kind of power that is rooted in humility, selflessness, and a willingness to serve others. This kind of power can be more unsettling than open domination because it is subtle, morally authoritative, and challenging to our own pride and assumptions.
