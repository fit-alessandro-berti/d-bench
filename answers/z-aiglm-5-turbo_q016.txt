While all the emotions and attitudes listed—anger, envy, wounded fairness, and pride in obedience—are present in the text, the elder brother’s deepest problem is ultimately his **discomfort with mercy itself**, which stems from a fundamentally transactional view of his relationship with his father.

Here is how the text reveals this hierarchy, moving from the surface symptoms to the deepest root:

**1. Anger and Envy (The Surface Symptoms)**
Anger is his immediate reaction ("he was angry"), and envy is his subsequent complaint ("thou never gavest me a kid"). However, these are just the emotional eruptions of a deeper issue. He isn't just angry that he didn't get a party; he is angry that a party is happening for *him*. 

**2. Wounded Fairness and Pride in Obedience (The Mindset)**
The elder brother reveals his core framework when he says, *"these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment."* He views his relationship with his father as a master-servant contract based on merit and performance. His pride in his obedience has become the very currency he believes should buy him favor. When the father gives lavish gifts to someone who has zero merit, the elder brother's sense of "fairness" is deeply wounded because it destroys the logic of his merit-based system.

**3. Discomfort with Mercy Itself (The Deepest Problem)**
This is the root of the parable, and it is why Jesus tells it. The elder brother cannot comprehend grace. To the elder brother, the father's mercy toward the prodigal feels like an injustice against the elder brother's hard work. 

We see this profound discomfort with mercy in three specific details:
*   **He distances himself from the family:** He refers to the prodigal not as "my brother," but as *"this thy son."* By dehumanizing the prodigal, he justifies why mercy should not apply to him.
*   **He reduces the father's grace to a mere transaction:** He ignores the father's previous statement that the fatted calf is to celebrate that his brother *"was dead, and is alive again."* The elder brother only sees the material cost of the meat and the moral cost of forgiving a sinner.
*   **He fails to see his own standing:** The father gently reminds him, *"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine."* The elder brother's discomfort with mercy blinded him to the fact that he was already enjoying the father's constant presence and shared wealth. He was living in grace but experiencing it as drudgery.

**The Context of the Parable**
It is important to remember the audience. In Luke 15, the Pharisees and scribes are muttering, *"This man receiveth sinners, and eateth with them"* (Luke 15:2). The elder brother represents the religious leaders. Their deepest problem was not that they lacked obedience; it was that they trusted in their obedience to earn God's favor, making them deeply hostile to a God who freely forgives the undeserving. 

**Conclusion**
The elder brother's anger, envy, wounded fairness, and pride are all branches of the same tree. But the deepest root is his **discomfort with mercy**. He cannot rejoice in grace because, in a merit-based world, unconditional love feels like a threat to his own achievements. He is just as lost as the younger brother—though he stayed in the father's house, his heart was far from the father's heart.
