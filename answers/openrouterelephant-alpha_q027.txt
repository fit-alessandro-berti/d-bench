Excellent question that gets to the heart of Jesus’s critique in this passage. The condemnation of the Pharisee is **not** about "false doctrine" in the sense of wrong theological propositions. Both men operate within the framework of Mosaic law. The condemnation is far more nuanced and targets the **heart posture and spiritual orientation** revealed through his actions and attitudes.

Here’s a breakdown of what condemns the Pharisee, and why the tax collector’s posture is considered spiritually truer:

### What Condemns the Pharisee?
It is a **toxic combination** of the listed elements, culminating in a fatal spiritual flaw:

1.  **Pride (The Core Sin):** His entire prayer is a declaration of self-righteousness. He defines himself *against* others ("not as other men"). His identity is rooted in comparison, not in relationship with God.
2.  **Comparison and Contempt:** He actively constructs his self-image by looking down on the "other"—the publican. This isn't just internal; it's vocalized in his prayer ("*I* thank *thee* that I am *not* as *other men*..."). This comparison breeds contempt, a sin the New Testament consistently warns against (e.g., James 2:9).
3.  **Use of Virtue as Self-Display:** His fasting and tithing are not hidden from God; they are performed *specifically to be seen and affirmed by God* (and likely by others). His "righteousness" is a performance for an audience, including God. He treats his obedience as a *possession* that earns merit, rather than a response to grace.
4.  **False Doctrine of Self:** His theology of God is distorted. He views God as a cosmic scorekeeper who needs reminding of his comparative virtue. He lacks the humility to see himself as a sinner in need of mercy (Romans 3:23).

**The Condemnation:** Jesus condemns the **whole package**—the pride that fuels the comparison, the contempt it generates, and the performative use of genuine religious practices. His "righteousness" is a counterfeit that hides a heart far from God.

### Why the Tax Collector’s Posture is Spiritually Truer
The tax collector’s posture—**standing afar off, not lifting his eyes, smiting his breast**—is theologically and spiritually profound for several reasons:

1.  **Recognition of Dependence and Sin:** He does not deny his sinfulness. His posture is one of **acknowledged need**. He comes not to showcase his goodness, but to plead for mercy he does not deserve.
2.  **Humility as the Gateway to Exaltation:** His posture is the physical and spiritual opposite of the Pharisee’s. He lowers himself ("farmer off," "afar off"). In the Christian economy, **"Everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, and the one who humbles himself will be exalted"** (Luke 14:11, 18:14). His humility is the path to justification.
3.  **Prayer as Crying Out, Not Reciting:** His prayer is not a theological statement but a raw, desperate cry: "**God, be merciful to me, a sinner!**" This is the prayer of a heart that knows it cannot save itself and looks to God’s character (His mercy) rather than its own performance.
4.  **The Only Justified "Publican":** In Jewish culture, tax collectors were seen as traitors and extortioners. Yet, Jesus elevates this man—proof that God’s justification is based on **heart condition and faith in mercy**, not ethnic or religious pedigree (a theme central to Paul’s theology in Romans).

### In Essence
Jesus uses this parable to redefine true righteousness. It is not found in **external conformity** to religious rules used for self-glorification (Pharisee), but in **internal reliance on God’s mercy** born of honest self-assessment (Tax Collector).

The tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer because it embodies the **first beatitude**: *"Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven"* (Matthew 5:3). He is "poor" because he recognizes his spiritual poverty and comes to God solely for grace. The Pharisee, in his self-sufficiency, has already declared himself spiritually rich—and thus, in need of nothing.
