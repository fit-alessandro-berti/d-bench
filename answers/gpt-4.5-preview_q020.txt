The passage from Hebrews describes bitterness as a **root** rather than merely an act because it emphasizes that bitterness often originates beneath the surface, unseen, quietly taking hold in a person’s heart. This imagery is deliberate, highlighting several critical aspects of hidden moral dynamics:

1. **Hidden Growth**:
   Like a root growing underground, bitterness can silently and invisibly spread within a person’s inner life. It is not always obvious or immediate, making it particularly dangerous, as it can go undetected until it has become deeply entrenched.

2. **Delayed Damage**:
   Roots take time to grow and spread before visibly affecting the plant above ground. Similarly, bitterness may not immediately manifest as open hostility or visible conflict, but its long-term effects—resentment, cynicism, anger—can gradually damage relationships, attitudes, and communities.

3. **Contagious Influence**:
   Roots nourish and support growth, but a root of bitterness nourishes negative thoughts, attitudes, and actions. When bitterness becomes embedded in one person's life, it rarely remains isolated. It spreads subtly, influencing others through conversations, attitudes, criticisms, or gossip, gradually defiling community life by poisoning trust, joy, and openness.

By choosing the image of a root, the author warns readers that bitterness is not just a surface-level act of resentment but a dangerous moral state. It calls for careful self-examination, vigilance, and active effort to pursue peace and holiness, lest this hidden negativity quietly undermine spiritual and relational health.
