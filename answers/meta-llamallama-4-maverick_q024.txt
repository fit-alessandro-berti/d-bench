Let's dive into the passage and unpack its meaning.

In Ezekiel 36:26-27, God promises to give his people a "new heart" and a "new spirit," which involves removing their "stony heart" and replacing it with a "heart of flesh." This passage is often seen as a key text in understanding the nature of spiritual transformation.

**External compliance vs. receiving a "new heart"**

The difference between external compliance and receiving a "new heart" lies in the motivation and capacity to obey God's laws. External compliance refers to outwardly following rules and regulations, often out of obligation, fear, or duty. In contrast, receiving a "new heart" implies a deep-seated transformation that enables one to obey God's statutes and judgments from a genuine desire to please Him.

The Israelites, to whom Ezekiel was prophesying, were prone to external compliance, following the letter of the law without a corresponding inner transformation. They might have observed the rituals and practices, but their hearts remained uncircumcised, unresponsive to God's will. In contrast, a "new heart" enables one to obey God's laws because they are now written on their heart (Jeremiah 31:33), and they have an inner desire to follow Him.

**Hardness described as a material condition**

The image of a "stony heart" being replaced with a "heart of flesh" is a powerful metaphor that highlights the nature of spiritual hardness. The "stony heart" represents a condition of spiritual insensitivity, unresponsiveness, and hardness. It is as if their hearts have become like stone, incapable of being moved or transformed by God's Spirit.

The use of a material condition to describe spiritual hardness emphasizes the idea that spiritual transformation is not just a matter of human effort or willpower, but rather a divine work that requires a fundamental change in the human heart. The image of replacing a "stony heart" with a "heart of flesh" suggests a radical transformation, where a hard, unyielding, and unresponsive heart is replaced with one that is soft, responsive, and capable of being shaped by God's Spirit.

In this sense, the "stony heart" is not just a metaphor for spiritual hardness, but a representation of the human condition that is resistant to God's will. By describing it as a material condition, the passage emphasizes the need for a divine intervention to transform the human heart, making it receptive to God's Spirit and enabling it to respond to His will.

In summary, receiving a "new heart" involves a deep-seated transformation that goes beyond external compliance, and the image of a "stony heart" being replaced with a "heart of flesh" highlights the need for a divine work to transform the human heart, making it responsive to God's will.
