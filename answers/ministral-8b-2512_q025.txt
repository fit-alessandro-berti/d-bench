Matthew 6:1 addresses a **motivational issue** rather than merely the act of visibility itself. The danger lies not in the *fact* of being seen but in the *intention* behind the act—specifically, when goodness is performed **primarily for human approval** rather than for the sake of God’s honor.

### **Key Distinctions in the Verse:**
1. **Visibility ≠ Problem (If the Heart is Right)**
   - Jesus doesn’t condemn all public good deeds. In fact, He later commands believers to "let your light shine before men" (Matt. 5:16), implying that *some* visibility is appropriate—**when done for God’s glory, not human applause**.
   - The issue arises when the *motive* shifts from worship to self-promotion. A parent who secretly helps a neighbor out of love is commendable; one who does it to be praised is hypocritical.

2. **The Danger: Craving Human Approval**
   - The verse warns against **"doing alms before men to be seen"** (*ὅπως φανῶσιν αὐτοῖς*). The Greek suggests **intentional, self-conscious performance** for the sake of **human admiration** (*φανῶσιν*—"to be seen by them").
   - This reflects the **Pharisaical spirit** (Matt. 6:2, 5), where religious acts become **tools for social status** rather than expressions of heartfelt devotion. The Pharisees "tinkled their cymbals" (Matt. 6:2) not to honor God but to be noticed.
   - The problem is **idolatry of self**: When we seek praise, we make ourselves the ultimate judge of our righteousness, displacing God’s authority.

3. **The Reward Lost: Heavenly Approval**
   - The verse contrasts **human applause** (*"reward of men"*) with **God’s reward** (*"reward of your Father in heaven"*).
   - True worship is **vertical** (to God) first, not **horizontal** (to people). When we perform goodness for others, we risk **distorting its purpose**—turning it into a **transaction** ("I do good so they’ll think well of me") rather than an **act of love and obedience** to God.

### **Practical Implications:**
- **Public good can be good** if the heart is pure (e.g., a parent helping a child in public out of love, not for praise).
- **Private good can be dangerous** if performed for self-righteousness (e.g., hiding charity to feel superior).
- The **real sin** is **self-idolatry**—when we seek validation from others rather than from God.

### **Jesus’ Solution (Matt. 6:3–4, 18):**
He doesn’t abolish public acts of goodness but **reorients the heart**:
- **"Let not thy left hand know what thy right hand doeth"** (6:3) → **Hidden generosity** (not for show, but as a secret act of worship).
- **"But thou, when thou prayest, enter into thy closet"** (6:6) → **Private devotion** to avoid hypocrisy.
- **"But thou, when thou doest alms, let not thy left hand know what thy right hand doeth"** (6:3) → **Discreet service** to ensure the motive is pure.

### **Conclusion:**
The danger isn’t **visibility itself** but **the heart’s dependence on human approval**. When we perform goodness **primarily to be seen**, we **replace God’s glory with our own glory**—making the act about **us**, not Him. True righteousness, by contrast, seeks **God’s approval alone**, whether in public or private.

As C.S. Lewis put it:
*"The danger of praise in this world is not that it is false, but that it is true."*
The question isn’t *"Are people watching?"* but *"Whose glory am I seeking?"*
