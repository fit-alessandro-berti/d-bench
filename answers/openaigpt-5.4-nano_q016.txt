The elder brother’s deepest problem is **wounded fairness (self-righteous grievance), expressed as anger and envy toward the brother’s restored status**—rather than any simple “mere anger.”

Key reasons:

- **He frames his relationship to the father in transactional fairness terms.**  
  “Lo, these many years do I serve thee… and yet thou never gavest me a kid…”  
  That’s not primarily about a wrong command; it’s about feeling *unjustly denied* what he believes he deserves for faithful performance.

- **His complaint is selective and resentful, not repentant or thankful.**  
  He doesn’t say, “I’m sorry,” or “Help me celebrate.” He catalogs what he’s done and contrasts it with what the younger brother received.

- **He can’t actually rejoice—because he resents mercy.**  
  He describes the younger brother’s return with contempt: “which hath devoured thy living with harlots…” He refuses to let the father interpret the situation as “dead… alive again; lost… found.”

So while **anger** is present (“he was angry”), and **envy** is part of the bitterness, the deeper root is that he sees the father’s joy as *unearned favoritism*—and therefore he cannot accept **grace**.

In short: **wounded fairness/pride in being “right,” which makes him uncomfortable with mercy and refuses the father’s joy.**
