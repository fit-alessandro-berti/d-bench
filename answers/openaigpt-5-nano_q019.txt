Short answer
- The inner contradiction is hypocrisy: a person who calls God the Father to bless Him ends up cursing people who bear God’s image. The heart that truly loves God would not treat others as worthless; to bless God and curse fellow humans at the same time shows a divided, disordered heart.
- Speech reveals moral disorder quickly because the tongue is the most immediate outward sign of what a person truly loves and trusts. In James’s view, the tongue articulates the heart’s allegiance: it can bless the God who is Father and also slander those made in God’s likeness, exposing the heart’s real commitments.

What the passage is saying (in context)
- God made humans in His image (imago Dei), so every person bears inherent dignity. To curse someone made in God’s image is, in effect, to curse the God who created them.
- The same mouth that blesses God should not pour out contempt on others; the two kinds of speech reveal a conflict within the soul.
- James calls this out as inconsistent and wrong: “these things ought not so to be.”

Why speech reveals moral disorder
- The tongue is a small part, but it “tells” what the heart believes and desires. Jesus’ teaching echoes this: “Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks” (parallels to Matthew 12:34; Luke 6:45).
- Contempt for others betrays a heart that is not aligned with God’s ways. Since the Christian hope is to love God and love neighbor, speech that both blesses God and curses neighbors shows a deep moral mismatch.
- James emphasizes the danger and paradox of the tongue to provoke readers to examine the root: a heart under the sway of sin and divided loyalties, not a well-ordered life.

What to do with this
- Recognize the symptom: if your speech alternates between praise of God and demeaning or harming others, ask God to search your heart.
- Seek transformation: rely on wisdom from above (which is pure, peaceable, gentle, full of mercy and good fruits) to govern the tongue (James 3:13–18).
- Practical steps: pause before speaking, choose words that build up rather than tear down, and aim to bless rather than curse, remembering every person bears God’s image.

Cross-references to consider
- James 3:3–5 (the tongue as a small member that can cause great effects)
- James 1:19 (quick to listen, slow to speak)
- James 3:11–12 (a fountain cannot pour both sweet and bitter water)
- Matthew 12:34 and Luke 6:45 (out of the heart’s abundance the mouth speaks)
- Galatians 5:22–23 (fruit of the Spirit includes self-control for the tongue)

In sum, James 3:9–10 exposes a fundamental misalignment: worship of God that does not translate into love for God’s image-bearing creatures. The tongue serves as a quick, clear diagnostic of the heart’s true master, and that is exactly why speech so readily reveals moral disorder.
