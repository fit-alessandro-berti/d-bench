In **Matthew 6:1**, Jesus is not condemning *public* acts of goodness in themselves, but rather the *motivation* behind them—specifically, the **craving to be seen and praised by others**. The moral danger lies in the **heart’s intention**, not merely the visibility of the act. Here’s a breakdown of the key distinctions:

### 1. **The Problem Is Not Visibility, but Vanity**
   - **Visibility alone is not sinful.** Jesus Himself performed miracles publicly (e.g., healing the leper in **Matthew 8:1–4**), and the apostles later did good works openly (e.g., **Acts 3:1–10**). The early church also practiced generosity in ways that were observable (e.g., **Acts 2:44–45; 4:32–37**).
   - **The issue is the *why* behind the act.** If the primary goal is to **gain human approval, admiration, or status**, then the act is corrupted by pride. Jesus calls this **"hypocrisy"** (Matthew 6:2, 5, 16)—performing righteousness for show rather than for God’s glory.

### 2. **The Danger of Seeking Human Reward**
   - Jesus warns that those who do good **to be seen by men** have already **"received their reward in full"** (Matthew 6:2, 5, 16). This means:
     - **Earthly praise is their only reward.** They forfeit the **eternal reward** that comes from God, who sees in secret (Matthew 6:4, 6, 18).
     - **Their "goodness" is self-serving.** It becomes a form of **idolatry**—worshiping the approval of others rather than God.
   - **Example:** A person might donate large sums publicly to a charity but do so primarily to enhance their reputation (e.g., naming a building after themselves). Their act is not truly altruistic but transactional—trading generosity for social capital.

### 3. **The Contrast: God’s Reward vs. Man’s Praise**
   - Jesus contrasts two kinds of rewards:
     - **Temporary, fleeting praise from people** (which fades).
     - **Eternal, unseen reward from God** (which lasts).
   - The **humble, secret act** (e.g., giving without fanfare, praying in private) is commended because it reflects a heart that **seeks God’s approval alone** (Matthew 6:4, 6, 18).

### 4. **The Slippery Slope of Performative Righteousness**
   - When goodness becomes a **performance**, it can lead to:
     - **Self-deception:** The person may begin to believe their own press, confusing external praise with true virtue.
     - **Exploitation of the vulnerable:** Public displays of generosity can manipulate recipients (e.g., giving to the poor in a way that humiliates them for the giver’s glory).
     - **Spiritual pride:** The Pharisees (whom Jesus often rebuked) were masters of this—fasting, praying, and giving ostentatiously to be seen as "holy" (Matthew 23:5–7).

### 5. **The Right Motivation: Love for God and Neighbor**
   - True righteousness is **other-centered**, not self-centered:
     - **For God:** Doing good out of love for Him (1 John 4:19) and obedience to His commands (John 14:15).
     - **For others:** Serving without expectation of return (Luke 6:35), even if no one notices (Matthew 25:37–40).
   - **Example:** The widow’s mite (Mark 12:41–44) was a small, unnoticed gift, yet Jesus commended it as greater than the large, public donations of the rich because it was given **sacrificially and sincerely**.

### **Practical Application**
- **Examine your heart:** Before doing a good deed, ask: *"Am I doing this for God’s glory, or for my own?"*
- **Avoid virtue-signaling:** Social media can tempt us to perform righteousness for likes and validation. Jesus’ warning applies here too.
- **Embrace hidden obedience:** Some of the most powerful acts of faith are unseen (e.g., private prayer, anonymous giving, quiet service).
- **Receive praise humbly:** If someone notices your good deed, deflect the glory to God (e.g., *"It was nothing; God enabled me"*).

### **Conclusion**
The moral danger in **Matthew 6:1** is not **public goodness itself**, but the **corrupting desire to be seen and praised by others**. The solution is not secrecy for secrecy’s sake, but a **heart that seeks God’s approval above all else**. As **C.S. Lewis** put it:
> *"Humility is not thinking less of yourself, but thinking of yourself less."*
