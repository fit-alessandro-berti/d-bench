This passage from Jeremiah is one of the most piercing psychological and theological statements in the Bible. To answer your questions, we have to look at both the moral diagnosis of the text and the epistemological problem it presents (how we know what we know).

### 1. Pessimistic about human nature, or realistic about self-deception?

It is **primarily realistic about self-deception**, though it necessarily yields a pessimistic view of human nature. 

If the passage were merely pessimistic about human nature, it might simply say, “The heart is desperately wicked.” But the crux of the verse is the phrase, *“deceitful above all things... who can know it?”* 

The text is diagnosing a profound epistemological problem: **we do not have the vantage point to accurately evaluate ourselves.** The very organ we use to weigh our motives is compromised. The heart is not just sick; it is a con artist that specializes in rationalization. It dresses up selfishness as duty, pride as conviction, and cowardice as peacemaking. 

Because of this, the passage’s "pessimism" is actually the logical conclusion of its realism. If we cannot trust our own self-assessment, then we are in a deeply precarious moral position. We are not just bad; we are blind to our own badness. Thus, it is a warning against naive self-confidence.

### 2. How should someone talk about motives if the heart is unreliable?

If the heart is an unreliable witness, then speaking about motives—both our own and others’—requires a radical shift in how we communicate and evaluate behavior. Here is how we should approach it:

**Lead with Epistemic Humility**
When talking about your own motives, you must abandon the claim to purity. You cannot say, “My intentions were completely pure,” because Jeremiah 17:9 says you are the least qualified person to make that assessment. Instead, the honest way to talk about your motives is to say, “I *think* I was trying to help, but I am aware that I often fool myself. There may be ego or fear mixed in there that I can't see.” Acknowledging mixed motives is the only honest way to speak.

**Judge by "The Fruit," Not the Root**
Notice how God responds in verse 10: *“to give every man according to his ways, and according to the fruit of his doings.”* God searches the heart, but the metric of evaluation is the *fruit*. If we cannot trust the heart’s stated intentions, we must look at the actual outcomes of actions. When evaluating yourself or discussing others, focus on what was actually done and the impact it had, rather than getting bogged down in debating invisible intentions. Jesus echoes this in the New Testament: *“By their fruits you shall know them.”*

**Extend Charity to Others**
If your own heart is an unreliable witness to your motives, then you certainly cannot act as the supreme judge of someone else’s hidden motives. We often project the worst possible motives onto our opponents ("They did that out of pure malice") while giving ourselves the benefit of the doubt ("I only did that because I was tired"). If the heart is deceitful, we must speak tentatively about others' motives. We should say, “I was hurt by your actions,” rather than, “You did that to manipulate me,” because we simply cannot see their reins.

**Rely on Outside Perspectives**
Because the heart is opaque to itself, self-discovery requires external light. Proverbs 20:5 says, *“The purposes of a person’s heart are deep waters, but one who has insight draws them out.”* When discussing motives, we need trusted friends, counselors, or mentors who can hold up a mirror and say, “You say you are doing this for reason X, but your actions and your anxiety suggest it’s actually reason Y.” 

**Defer to the Divine Search**
Ultimately, verse 10 provides the solution to the problem of the deceitful heart: *“I the Lord search the heart, I try the reins.”* If you cannot know your own heart, you must hand the audit over to God. This is exactly what the Psalmist does in response to this very dilemma: *“Search me, God, and know my heart; test me and know my anxious thoughts. See if there is any offensive way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting”* (Psalm 139:23-24). 

In conversation, this means living with a posture of surrender. We don't have to anxiously defend our motives. We can say, “God knows my heart, and He is still searching it. I am open to His correction.” This takes the pressure off of us to prove our innocence and allows us to rest in the fact that the only perfectly reliable Witness is also the one who offers grace.
