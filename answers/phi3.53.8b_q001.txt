In Genesis 4:6-7, the text presents a picture of moral agency that intertwines human actions with the concept of sin, suggesting that individuals are responsible for their own moral choices and the consequences thereof. Here are some key elements from the passage:

1. Personal Responsibility: The Lord's question to Cain, "Why art thou wroth? and why is thy countenance fallen?" places direct focus on Cain's emotional state, indicating that one'sinternal turmoil can be an impetus for sin (as he is angry and depressed, represented by his "countenance falling"). The implication is that people have control over their emotional and mental conditions and are morally accountable for the way they respond to their circumstances.

2. Choice and Accountability: When the text says "If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted; if thou doest not well, sin lieth at the door," it implies that Cain's actions or inactions are within his control, and their immediate consequences can influence his moral standing. It suggests that his personal choices directly impact his relationship with sin and righteousness.

3. Sin as Proximity: The notion that 'sin lieth at the door' – that is, sin is present at the threshold, close enough to be almost within Cain's reach – presents a picture where sin is not necessarily an external force but rather something that one can encounter depending on personal choices. It is positioned as an immediate consequence of one's actions, suggesting the potential for sin in day-to-day activities and decision-making.

4. Exercise of Power: The Lord grants Cain the authority to rule over his younger brother Abel ("and unto thee shall be his desire," likely meaning that Cain will have his brother's possession – Abel's flock and its products – to manage. This power, however, comes with a significant moral weight: "if thou doest not well, sin lieth at the door." Managing others' assets implicates Cain in their moral use or misuse, connecting the exercise of authority with the potential for sin.

5. Preemptive Warning: The Lord's words serve as a preemptive counsel or warning to Cain, suggesting a direct relationship between one's immediate actions and their moral outcomes. He is not merely responding to external temptation, but being actively discouraged from succumbing to it, regardless of the circumstances.

The picture of moral agency being drawn here is one where an individual has the capacity to resist or succumb to sin based on their own decisions. Evil, or sin, isn't depicted just as an impulse or a temptation coming from outside; instead, it's positioned as something that emerges when one fails to do well—when someone chooses poorly or allows their negative emotions to dictate their actions. There is an active role for the person in managing their own ethical behavior. In essence, the text places the responsibility on Cain (and by extension, anyone) to make conscious, good choices and manage his or her impulses and motivations in order to act rightly.

Cain is expected not only to control his own behavior but also to recognize the potential for sin in the way he wields power over others (in this case, the right to manage Abel's animals). The expectation is not merely to refrain from doing wrong, but also to demonstrate positive action that leads to acceptance, which is framed as an opposite of encountering or engaging with sin.

In summary, the text describes a scenario where each individual has dominion over their immediate will, actions, and reactions. Moral agency is exercised by actively choosing what is good (according to the standards the Lord is setting) and by being aware of the influence this authority has not only on oneself but also over others. Cain is urged to take his responsibility seriously, as his choices can have immediate, tangible effects on his righteousness or sinfulness, as well as the wellbeing of his brother, underlining an interconnectedness between their moral conduct and divine judgment. This ancient text frames sin as something that arises from an individual's immediate decisions rather than from an external coercive force – the choice is personal, and so is the responsibility.

The concept of 'doing well' remains ambiguous in the text, but it often signifies actions or attitudes that align with divine justice and equity, while doing not well may suggest anything from selfishness and cruelty to negligence and moral blindness. In essence, the text emphasizes a proactive role in moral behavior where individuals must constantly be mindful of the nature of their actions and their moral outcomes. This ancient moral teaching retains relevance for the question of human agency today, as it continues to highlight the importance of conscious choice for right living and the personal consequences of failing to act morally.
