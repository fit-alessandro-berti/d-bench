Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it offends both our natural instincts and our sense of justice. At the level of instinct, we are wired for reciprocity: we repay kindness with kindness and hostility with hostility. At the level of justice, we believe that moral distinctions matter; the evildoer and the righteous person should not receive the same response. To love an enemy feels, at first, like a collapse of moral discrimination—like pretending the enemy is not an enemy, or worse, like rewarding those who have done harm.

But the command in Matthew 5:44–45 is neither sentimental nor merely strategic. It is something far more demanding: a disciplined, theological form of moral strength.

**It is not sentimental.** Jesus does not say, “Feel affection for your enemies,” which would be psychologically absurd and morally tone-deaf. He commands three specific actions: *bless*, *do good*, and *pray for*. These are performative acts of the will, not emotional states. In fact, enemy-love is often *anti*-sentimental; it requires acting for another’s welfare precisely when every feeling urges contempt or rage. Sentimentality would dissolve the command into a vague wish that everyone be nice. Jesus keeps the category of “enemy” intact—he assumes you have enemies—while forbidding you to treat them as their enmity deserves.

**It is not merely strategic.** One can certainly make practical arguments for non-retaliation: it breaks cycles of violence, it disarms the aggressor, it preserves social cohesion. But the text does not ground the command in utility. The logic is not, “Love your enemies because it works,” but rather, “Love your enemies *that ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven*.” The telos is participatory and eschatological: to be revealed as those who share God’s own character. The appeal to the sun and the rain makes this clear. God does not distribute weather according to moral desert; his beneficence is abundant, non-calculating, and prior to human response. To love the enemy is to imitate this divine indifference to desert—not because desert no longer matters, but because the lover’s goodness is rooted in God’s abundance rather than in the worthiness of the beloved.

**It is, instead, a deeper form of moral strength.** Retaliation is easy; it requires only that you be governed by the last thing done to you. Enemy-love requires a radical self-possession. It refuses to let the enemy’s hatred set the terms of your own soul. When Jesus commands prayer and active goodness toward persecutors, he is demanding that his disciples retain moral agency in the face of the very worst provocation. This is not weakness; it is the strength to remain human when confronted with inhumanity, and to remain godly when confronted with malice.

This does not mean collapsing justice into a syrupy universalism. To love an enemy is not to approve of their enmity, nor is it to remain in abusive passivity. One can resist evil, oppose injustice, and even wield authority against the wicked while refusing the final, soul-corroding step of hatred. The command is to love *persons*, not their actions; to will the good of the other without surrendering the distinction between good and evil.

Ultimately, enemy-love is so hard to take seriously because it operates from a different center of gravity than the world recognizes. It does not ask, “What have they earned?” but “Whose character am I being formed into?” It requires a security so deep—rooted in the Father’s own inexhaustible love—that one no longer needs the enemy to be less than human in order to affirm one’s own humanity. That is not naivety. That is a moral strength almost no one thinks possible until they see it.
