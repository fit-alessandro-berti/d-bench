**Isaiah 55:6‑7** is a prophetic call that rings with the same urgency and grace that runs through the whole Bible.  When the prophet tells the “wicked” to “forsake his way” and the “unrighteous man his thoughts,” he is not giving two disconnected commands; he is identifying the two interlocking spheres of human rebellion that must be abandoned if a person truly turns to God.  Let’s unpack the text, the Hebrew words behind it, and what it tells us about outward conduct, inward imagination, and the hope of mercy.

---

### 1. The text in its original language  

| Hebrew (BHS) | Rough translation |
|--------------|-------------------|
| **רָשָׁע**   | “the wicked one” (the one who lives in rebellion) |
| **יִעַזֵב דַּרְכּוֹ** | “let him *forsake* his *way* (or *course*)” |
| **וְאִישׁ הָעַוְלָה מַחְשְּׁבוֹתָיו** | “and the unrighteous man his *thoughts* (plans, imaginations)” |

- **דֶּרֶךְ** (*derekh*): *way*—the observable pattern of life, the habitual behavior or “road” a person walks. In the Old Testament it often denotes a moral‑ethical trajectory (e.g., Prov 4:19, “the way of the wicked is darkness”).
- **מַחֲשָׁבָה** (*machshavah*): *thought*—inner reasoning, the faculty of planning, imagining, or plotting. It covers everything from fleeting ideas to deliberate schemes (e.g., Prov 6:18; “a heart that devises wicked thoughts”).

The parallelism is classic Hebrew poetry: the *way* is the outward, bodily expression of rebellion; the *thoughts* are the inward, mental and emotional engine that fuels it.  When the prophet pairs them, he signals that **sin is a whole‑person phenomenon**, not a mere set of actions.

---

### 2. Why both “way” and “thoughts”?

| Dimension | What it covers | Why it matters for repentance |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------------------|
| **Outward conduct (the “way”)** | Behavior, habits, choices that affect other people and the world (e.g., theft, oppression, sexual immorality). | The visible fruit of the heart’s rebellion. Turning from the “way” is the first step the law and the prophets always demand (e.g., Amos 5:14‑15). |
| **Inward imagination (the “thoughts”)** | Desires, intentions, mental plots, the internal narrative that precedes the deed (e.g., greed, envy, pride). | Jesus affirms this in the New Testament: “**Whoever looks at a woman with lust has already committed adultery with her in his heart**” (Matt 5:28). The mind is the breeding ground of sin. |  

Thus **repentance must be comprehensive**: “Forsaking the way” without also abandoning the thoughts would be like pruning a tree but leaving the roots untouched—the old patterns would soon sprout again. The Hebrew prophetic demand therefore anticipates the New‑Testament “metanoia” (repentance/renewal) that is both a *turning from* (the old way) and a *turning to* (a new heart).

---

### 3. What this implies about mercy and abundant pardon

The promise in the second half of the verse is striking:

> “*...and let him return unto the Lord, and he will have mercy upon him; and to our God, for he will abundantly pardon.*”

- **Mercy** (*ḥesed* in Hebrew) is God’s steadfast, faithful love that reaches down to the undeserving.
- **Abundant pardon** (literally “*multiply to pardon*”) underscores a *generosity that exceeds the measure of the sin*.

If repentance were limited to outward actions, the scope of God’s forgiveness could be measured by the “size” of the sin we see. But because the text ties the *thoughts* to the *way*, the forgiveness that God promises covers **the entire person**—the hidden motives *and* the visible misdeeds. The “abundance” of the pardon is therefore not only a matter of quantity (lots of sins forgiven) but also of *depth*: the innermost schemes, the secret imaginations, the mental “road‑maps” of rebellion—all are swept away.

This ties into the broader biblical theme that God’s mercy is *full* and *transformative*:

1. **Old Testament** – The Psalms repeatedly ask God to cleanse the *thoughts* (e.g., Psalm 51:6, “Behold, thou desirest truth in the inward parts”).  
2. **New Testament** – In the Upper Room, Jesus tells the disciples, “By this shall all men know that ye are my disciples, if ye have love one for another” (John 13:35). That love is an *inner disposition* that becomes outward behavior.  
3. **Theological picture** – The gospel announces *justification* (the legal declaration of righteousness) that is *simultaneous* with *sanctification* (the progressive renewal of the mind and conduct). The abundant pardon of Isaiah 55:7 is the gracious gateway to both.

---

### 4. A synthetic answer to the three parts of the question

1. **Why does repentance involve both “way” and “thoughts”?**  
   Because the Hebrew poetical parallelism captures the *totality* of human rebellion. The “way” is the visible trajectory of our actions; the “thoughts” are the invisible engine of those actions. Genuine turning to God requires that both be abandoned, otherwise the old pattern would survive under a new label.

2. **What does that imply about outward conduct?**  
   Outward conduct matters—it is the first and most observable evidence of a person’s relationship with God. But it is *not sufficient* on its own. The “way” must be forsaken *and* the inner schemes must be replaced with “the mind of Christ” (Phil 2:5).

3. **What does that imply about inward imagination?**  
   The inner imagination is *the root* of sin. It is where plans, desires, and motives are formed before they become deeds. Therefore, a true repentance calls for a *renewal of the imagination*—a transformation of thoughts into a “new creation” (2 Cor 5:17). 

4. **What does that imply about the possibility of mercy?**  
   Because God’s mercy is directed toward the *whole* person, the promise of “abundant pardon” covers both the outward “way” and the inward “thoughts.” This is why the prophets can announce a *new covenant* in which God will “write my law on their hearts” (Jer 31:33), thereby dealing with the internal problem once and for all. The grace offered in Isaiah 55 is not a “discount” for a few sins; it is a *full, exhaustive* forgiveness that embraces every facet of human rebellion.

---

### 5. A closing illustration

Think of a river that flows from a mountain. The *water* (the outward actions) is visible, sometimes destructive, and can be dammed. But the *source* (the underground springs—the thoughts) is hidden. If you only dam the visible water, the underground pressure will eventually break through in a new flood. The prophetic word of Isaiah 55:6‑7 says, “Plug the source *and* divert the river”—turn from the way you walk *and* change the way you think. When both are surrendered, the river can be redirected into the “river of life” (cf. Rev 22:1‑2), and God’s mercy becomes not just a transactional forgiveness but a *transformative* restoration.

---

**Bottom line:** The pairing of “way” and “thoughts” in Isaiah 55:6‑7 teaches that true repentance is holistic— God invites us to abandon both the outward patterns of sin and the inner imaginings that give them birth. In doing so, He promises a mercy that is “abundant” enough to cover the entire scope of human rebellion, and a pardon that not only erases the guilt but also creates a new heart capable of walking in God’s ways.
