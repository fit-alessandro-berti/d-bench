Romans 12:20-21 defines victory over evil as overcoming it with good, rather than retaliating with more evil. This passage suggests that good actions, even towards those who are considered enemies, are powerful and transformative.

The phrase "for in so doing thou shalt heap coals of fire on his head" is key to understanding this concept. This expression is often interpreted as a metaphor derived from an ancient Egyptian practice where carrying a pan of burning coals on one's head was a sign of penitence. Thus, by treating an enemy with kindness (feeding and giving them drink), one might induce a sense of guilt or shame in the enemy, potentially leading them to rethink their hostility. This is not about inflicting physical pain but initiating a transformative reflection and regret, thereby overcoming evil with good.

As for the nature of the command (whether it is weak, manipulative, or strong), several perspectives may arise:

1. **Weakness:** Some might view the idea of helping one's enemy as a sign of weakness, suggesting that it forfeits a stance of strength or defense against wrongdoing.

2. **Manipulative:** Others could interpret the action as manipulative, as it employs kindness with the aim of eliciting guilt or remorse, which in turn could be seen as a technique to gain a psychological advantage.

3. **Genuine Strength:** Alternatively, and more compelling within the context of Christian doctrine, this command can be seen as reflecting genuine strength. It defies natural human inclinations towards revenge by promoting mercy and forgiveness, demonstrating control over one's impulses and emotions. The passage advocates for moral superiority that does not succumb to the cycle of retaliation but rather seeks to break it with acts of generosity and love.

The overall ethos in Romans 12 (especially in the context of previous verses advocating for peace and humility) supports the interpretation of this passage advocating for a profound inner strength and a commitment to higher moral standards. It underscores a powerful and proactive approach to conflict resolution and personal conduct, emphasizing that true victory over evil is achieved through good deeds that potentially convert hostility into remorse and reconciliation. Additionally, by advising believers not to be "overcome by evil," but to "overcome evil with good," the passage strongly marks this approach not as one of passive subjection but as an assertive strategy in spiritual and moral warfare.
