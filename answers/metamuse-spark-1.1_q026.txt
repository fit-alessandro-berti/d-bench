In Matthew 12, Jesus has just healed a man, and the Pharisees say He did it by the power of Beelzebub. Jesus answers that their accusation doesn't reveal anything about Him - it reveals everything about *them*.

That is the logic of 12:34-35:

> for out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh.

The Greek is more vivid than the KJV. *Ek gar tou perisseumatos tes kardias to stoma lalei* - out of the overflow, the surplus, what remains over after the vessel is full, the mouth speaks.

And in v.35: *thesauros* - treasure, treasury, storehouse, stockpile.

> A good man out of the good treasure of the heart bringeth forth good things: and an evil man out of the evil treasure bringeth forth evil things.

Two metaphors working together:

**1. The heart as a reservoir that overflows.** Speech is what spills out when it gets full.
**2. The heart as a treasury that is curated.** Speech is what you go in and choose to bring out.

That second verb matters: *ekballo* - to bring forth, to throw out, to put on display. It's an active verb.

This is why your question gets at the heart of it. Are words symptoms, choices, or revelations? In this passage, it's all three, in a sequence:

### 1. Words as Symptoms - involuntary diagnosis

"O generation of vipers, how can ye, being evil, speak good things?"

Jesus argues you *cannot* consistently produce opposite of what you are. A viper can't produce dove sounds. As in Luke's parallel in Luke 6:43-45, thorns don't produce figs.

In this sense words are symptoms. They leak. Under pressure, in an unguarded moment, when you think you're just talking about someone else, your habitual inner life betrays itself. The Pharisees thought they were giving a theological analysis of Jesus. Jesus says they were actually giving a medical report on themselves.

In Hebrew anthropology, *kardia* - heart - isn't just emotions. It's the center of intellect, will, and affections. The hidden control room.

### 2. Words as Revelation - what was hidden becomes visible

A *thesauros* is hidden by definition. It's stored up inside, in the dark, over time.

Jesus says speech makes the private public. The stockpile was built in secret - what you rehearse, what you resent, what you cherish, what you consume, what you justify - but your speech hauls it into the light.

That is why verse 34 starts with "generation of vipers." Vipers were thought to be venomous by nature, but to hide in the grass. Their strike *reveals* where they were all along.

### 3. Words as Choice - moral responsibility

If words were *only* symptoms or involuntary revelations, there would be no guilt. But Jesus goes on in v.36-37:

> But I say unto you, That every idle word that men shall speak, they shall give account thereof in the day of judgment. For by thy words thou shalt be justified, and by thy words thou shalt be condemned.

An "idle" word - *argon*, literally un-working, careless, useless - is still counted as chosen. The image of bringing forth treasure implies agency. You are a steward of your own storehouse. You collect it, you keep it, you choose when to spend it.

So the moral stockpile is not accidental. It accumulates by a thousand small choices, and then each utterance is another choice to draw from that pile.

In short:

* **Your heart is accumulated over time** - the treasure.
* **Your mouth is inevitable under pressure** - the overflow, the symptom.
* **Your speech is culpable before God** - the deliberate bringing forth, the revelation and the choice.

That is why the passage is not just about taming your tongue, as James 3 later develops, but about transforming the treasury itself. You cannot permanently speak better than you are, because you cannot permanently draw good currency from an empty vault.
