The portrait of love in 1 Corinthians 13:4–7 is framed with so many negations (e.g., "envieth not," "vaunteth not itself," "is not puffed up," "thinketh no evil") because it is contrasting love with the natural human tendencies toward self-centeredness, pride, and defensiveness. These negations serve to strip away the ego-driven behaviors that often masquerade as strength but are, in reality, signs of weakness and insecurity.

The vision of strength proposed here is not one of dominance, self-protection, or assertiveness, but of *resilience rooted in selflessness*. True love, as described, is strong because it does not need to prove itself—it does not boast, it does not seek its own advantage, and it does not resent others' success. Instead, its power lies in its ability to endure, to trust, to hope, and to persevere without becoming bitter or cynical.

This kind of love is strong because it is *free*—free from the need to control, free from the fear of being wronged, and free from the burden of suspicion. It is a strength that comes from a deep security in God’s love, allowing the believer to respond to the world with patience, kindness, and humility rather than with the defensive posturing of pride or envy.

In essence, the passage redefines strength not as the ability to overpower others but as the capacity to remain steadfast in goodness, even in the face of injustice, betrayal, or hardship. It is the strength of a love that "bears all things, believes all things, hopes all things, endures all things"—not because it is naive, but because it is grounded in a higher reality than mere human self-interest.
