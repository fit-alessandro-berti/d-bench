This passage from Amos is a scathing critique by God through the prophet, and its sharpness serves a specific prophetic purpose. It is not merely asking for "better balance" between worship and justice, but rather **exposing a fundamental fraud: the substitution of ritual for righteousness.**

Here’s a breakdown of why the rejection is so absolute and what moral/spiritual fraud is being exposed:

### 1. The Nature of the Fraud: "External Religion" Without Internal Reality
The core issue is that the people of Israel were engaging in **elaborate, externally correct worship** while **simultaneously committing grave injustices**. The feast days, burnt offerings, songs, and viols were all part of the covenantal worship God had commanded. However, Amos reveals that God finds them **an abomination** when coupled with:
*   Oppression of the poor (Amos 2:6-8, 4:1, 5:11-12)
*   Dishonest business practices (8:4-6)
*   Corruption in legal judgments (5:7, 12, 15)
*   Pride and complacency (6:1-8)

The fraud is one of **hypocrisy**: using religious ritual as a **substitute** for, or a **cover** for, ethical failure. They were attempting to "purchase" God's favor with sacrifices while ignoring the weightier matters of the law—justice, mercy, and faithfulness (cf. Matthew 23:23).

### 2. Why the Sharp Rejection Instead of a Call for Balance?
The prophet does not ask for a 50/50 split between worship and justice because **true worship is inseparable from justice**. The passage reveals:
*   **Worship Without Justice is an Oxymoron to God.** To God, their celebrations and songs are "noise" (v. 23) because the context of their society is tainted by oppression. The ritual is rendered spiritually meaningless, even offensive, when the worshipper's life is at odds with God's character.
*   **It Exposes the Idolatry of Ritual.** By rejecting their worship so completely, God shows that the *idolatry* was not in the ritual itself, but in **trusting the ritual to save them** while they lived in injustice. They had made the external forms of religion into an idol—something they trusted in place of a transformed heart and righteous action.
*   **The Call is for Repentance of the *Heart*, Not Reform of the *Schedule*.** The final verse (v. 24) is the key: "But let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream." This is not a call to add social action to their worship. It's a call for **a flood of righteousness** to replace their corrupted systems. The worship must stop being a performance and become an overflow of a society reformed by justice.

### 3. The Spiritual Fraud Exposed
The moral and spiritual fraud Amos exposes is **the illusion of covenantal security without covenantal faithfulness.** The people believed that because they were God's chosen nation, performed His festivals, and offered His sacrifices, they were automatically accepted. Amos shatters this delusion, stating that **the external markers of election are useless without the internal evidence of a people who reflect God's heart for the vulnerable.**

This is a **fraud against God**—offering Him the leftovers of their lives and their society's cruelty, while expecting His blessing. It is also a **fraud against the poor**—using religion to legitimize a system that crushes them.

### Conclusion: The Timeless Warning
Amos 5:21-24 is a stark reminder that **God's priority is not our ceremonies but our character and our collective justice.** The passage rejects "worship" that is divorced from righteousness because such worship is spiritually hollow and morally deceptive. It is not a critique of worship itself, but of worship that serves as a **mask for injustice**. The prophet calls for a radical transformation where righteousness becomes the very foundation of national and personal life, making authentic worship possible again.

The "fraud" is the belief that one can be right with God through religious performance while being wrong in how one treats others. This is a warning that resonates across all ages for any individual or society that uses faith as a cloak for oppression.
