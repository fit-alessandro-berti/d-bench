The passage from James 3:9–10 exposes a deep inner contradiction in the human heart: the same faculty (the tongue/mouth) is used both to bless God the Father and to curse human beings who are made in God’s own image and likeness. 

This is not merely inconsistent behavior; it is a moral and theological incoherence. To bless God while cursing those who bear His similitude is to honor the Creator with the lips while simultaneously despising the reflection of that Creator in other people. The text itself underscores the absurdity: “these things ought not so to be.” The contradiction is internal because it reveals a divided self—one that claims allegiance to God yet fails to recognize the divine image in neighbor.

Speech reveals moral disorder so quickly because the tongue is an immediate, almost involuntary expression of the heart’s true condition. Unlike carefully planned actions, words often spring out before self-censorship can intervene. James has already described the tongue as a restless evil, a fire, a world of iniquity that no one can fully tame (James 3:6–8). When blessing and cursing flow from the same mouth, the disorder is laid bare at once: the heart has not been unified under the fear of God. The mouth simply reports the fragmentation that already exists inside.
