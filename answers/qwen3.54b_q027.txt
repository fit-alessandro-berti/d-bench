Based on the text of Luke 18 (verses 9–14), the Pharisee is condemned primarily for **Self-Righteousness**, which manifests through the mechanism of **using virtue as self-display**. While he exhibits pride, contempt, and comparison, these are symptoms of the root error: trusting in his own merit rather than seeking God's mercy.

Here is a breakdown of why this is the case and how it contrasts with the Publican's posture.

### 1. The Condemnation: Self-Righteousness over Virtue
The text explicitly identifies the crowd Jesus addresses as "certain which trusted in themselves that they were righteous" (v.9). This diagnosis points directly to **Self-Trust**. In theological terms, this is often called *Self-Righteousness*—the attempt to establish one's standing before God through human effort rather than divine grace.

Among the options you provided:
*   **Pride** and **Contempt** are the emotional undercurrents ("trusting in themselves," "despised others").
*   **Comparison** is the rhetorical tool ("I am not as other men are... even as this publican").
*   **Use of virtue as self-display** is the practical manifestation.

However, none of these fully capture the failure on its own. The Pharisee's specific error was attempting to define his righteousness by a list of deeds (fasting, tithing) that he thought he had perfected. By focusing entirely on what he *could* do, he ignored who God requires him to be. He treats virtue as a scoreboard, whereas true righteousness demands an acknowledgment of need.

### 2. Why the Tax Collector’s Posture is "Spiritually Truer"
The tax collector's posture (v.13) is treated as superior not because he was better dressed or more religiously observant (he wasn't), but because his heart condition was accurate to reality before God. Three key factors drive this:

**a. The Contrast of Prayer (Prayer vs. Presentation)**
The Pharisee prays "with himself." He addresses God as a witness to his own performance, essentially listing credits he deserves. It is a monologue about his virtue that happens to be in the temple. The Tax Collector engages in true communion: he "would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven." This indicates he does not assume God owes him any attention or recognition. His gaze is lowered because he understands his own status; his heart is turned toward God because it acknowledges its dependence on Him.

**b. The Admission of Need (Mercy vs. Merit)**
The Pharisee assumes a position of sufficiency ("I thank thee..."). He believes the "God" he refers to in prayer exists as a judge who recognizes good work, but does not require repentance because he does not believe he needs any help from God.
In contrast, the Tax Collector asks: *"God be merciful to me a sinner."* His posture is spiritually true because it matches his theology. To admit being a *sinner* is to admit that he has a debt that cannot be paid by human effort alone. He recognizes the only thing capable of covering sin is God's mercy, not man's merit.

**c. Justification vs. Justifying**
The text concludes: *"this man went down to his house justified rather than the other."*
This hinges on the Greek word *dikaios*, which implies legal standing or innocence before a court. The Pharisee believes he is "righteous" in court through *works*. The Tax Collector knows he is guilty, so he submits to the verdict of God's *mercy*. In scripture, justification is granted to the ones who see they need saving, not those who think they can save themselves. The tax collector’s posture reflects a posture where justice is found only in the mercy that answers his admission, whereas the Pharisee sought judgment based on his own cataloging of goodness.

### Summary
The Pharisee is condemned because he treats virtue as a means of self-justification, effectively replacing **merit** with **praise**. The tax collector's posture is spiritually truer because it aligns external behavior (lower gaze) with internal reality (sinfulness), creating the necessary condition for receiving divine **mercy**.
