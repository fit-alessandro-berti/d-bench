**It is mainly realistic about self-deception and the opacity of motives, while resting on a sober (not despairing) assessment of human nature.**

The passage is not a blanket cry of hopeless pessimism that “people are simply evil and nothing more can be said.” Its rhetorical force and literary context point first to the limits of human knowledge—especially self-knowledge—and to the unreliability of the heart as a transparent witness to its own motives.

### Linguistic and contextual notes
- **Hebrew terms**: “Deceitful” renders *ʿāqōb* (related to the root of Jacob’s name, “heel-grabber/supplanter”), which carries senses of crooked, insidious, or treacherous. “Desperately wicked” in the KJV is interpretive; the word *ʾānûš* more precisely means desperately sick, incurable, or beyond cure (a term used elsewhere for wounds that will not heal). Many modern translations prefer “desperately sick” or “beyond cure.” The Septuagint leans toward depth/inscrutability rather than pure moral wickedness. The net picture is of something deep, crooked, hard to fathom, and not self-healing.
- **Immediate context (Jeremiah 17)**: The verses sit after the sharp contrast between the cursed person who trusts in man (and whose heart turns away from the Lord) and the blessed person who trusts in the Lord (like a tree planted by water). Judah’s sin is described as engraved on the heart. The point is that outward profession or self-assessment of trust can be misleading; only God truly searches the heart and mind and rewards “according to his ways, and according to the fruit of his doings.”
- **Rhetorical structure**: “Who can know it?” is answered immediately: “I the Lord search the heart…” The text does not stop at the diagnosis of the heart’s condition; it moves to divine knowledge and just evaluation by actual conduct and outcomes.

Traditional Christian readings (especially Reformed) often hear this as supporting a doctrine of the fallen heart’s corruption and self-deception (sometimes linked to total depravity), with the New Covenant promise of a new heart as the eventual remedy. Contextual and some Jewish readings emphasize the heart’s changeability, depth, or resistance to being fully understood by human observers more than an absolute moral diagnosis. Both streams converge on the same practical claim: the heart is an unreliable and incomplete witness.

Modern psychology supplies an independent parallel: motivated reasoning, confirmation bias, self-serving explanations, and the well-documented limits of introspection make self-deception ordinary rather than exceptional. The biblical text simply states the problem with unusual bluntness.

### How to talk about motives when the heart is an unreliable witness
Treat claimed motives—especially one’s own—with epistemic humility rather than naïve confidence or cynical dismissal.

- Prefer the verse’s own criterion: observable *ways* and the *fruit of doings*. Patterns of consistent behavior over time are more trustworthy data than any single self-report or sudden flash of “true motive.”
- Use provisional language. “I believe my motive is X,” “From what I can see…,” “It looks as though…,” or “I may be deceiving myself about this” is more honest than “My heart is pure” or “I know exactly why they did that.”
- Cultivate self-suspicion without paralysis. Regularly test rationalizations against outcomes, against the judgments of trusted and honest people, and against external standards (moral, practical, or, for those who hold the text as authoritative, scriptural). Accountability structures help precisely because the heart is not a transparent reporter.
- Distinguish description from final judgment. You can describe apparent motives and the fruits that follow without claiming god-like certainty about the hidden interior. Leave ultimate knowledge of the heart where the text places it.
- In religious practice this often means inviting divine examination rather than resting in one’s own introspection. Outside that framework it still means refusing to treat the self as an infallible authority on its own intentions.

In short, the passage is realistic about the crookedness and limited self-transparency of the human heart, and therefore realistic about how we should speak of motives: provisionally, with attention to fruit, and without pretending that the heart is a clear window. It is sober about human nature, but it is not a counsel of despair; it points beyond the unreliable witness of the heart to a more reliable standard of evaluation.
