What Jesus criticises in the Pharisee is not the content of his creed or the legitimacy of the practices he names—fasting, tithing, moral probity—but the way he turns those good things into a platform for self-trust and for looking down on others. Luke introduces the story with a double charge that already tells us what is wrong:

1. they “trusted in themselves that they were righteous” (self-reliance, self-justification);
2. they “despised others” (contempt born of comparison).

Everything the Pharisee says and does in the short prayer shows precisely those two faults.

•  False doctrine?  – No.  Fasting and tithing are perfectly orthodox.  
•  Pride / self-reliance?  – Yes.  Instead of asking God for anything he recites his own résumé: “I fast … I give … I am not like …”.  Luke even says he “prayed with (or toward) himself” (πρὸς ἑαυτόν): the circle of reference never leaves his own performance.  
•  Comparison and contempt?  – Yes.  He measures his worth by placing other people underneath him—“this tax-collector”.  The comparison is not incidental; it is the very form his prayer takes.  
•  Virtue as self-display?  – Yes.  The deeds may be good, but they have been converted into self-advertising and therefore corrupted (cf. Matt 6:1 ff.).  

So the Pharisee is condemned because his good works, joined to pride and contempt, have become the grounds of a claim on God instead of occasions for gratitude and mercy. “God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble” (Prov 3:34; Jas 4:6).

Why, then, is the tax-collector’s posture “truer”?

1.  He stands “afar off”, won’t lift his eyes, beats his breast—gestures that embody reluctance to presume on God.  
2.  His only sentence is a plea: “God, be merciful to me, the sinner.”  The verb (ἱλάσθητί μοι) is the word for “make atonement for” or “propitiate”; he knows he needs something done for him, not something he can present.  
3.  He names himself “the” sinner, not merely “a” sinner—no deflecting comparison, only self-knowledge in the presence of holiness.  

The external posture does not justify him by itself; rather, the posture expresses an interior truth: humility, repentance, dependence on grace. Because that inner attitude corresponds to the reality of every human being before God, Jesus calls it “justified.” The story therefore illustrates the gospel reversal Luke often highlights: “everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, but he who humbles himself will be exalted” (v. 14).

In short, the Pharisee is condemned for letting genuine virtues harden into self-justifying pride and contempt, while the tax collector is accepted because his prayer abandons all claims and casts itself entirely on God’s mercy—the only spiritually “true” stance a sinner can rightly take.
