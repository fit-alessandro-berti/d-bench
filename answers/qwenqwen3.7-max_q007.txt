To understand Luke 6:37, we must first dismantle one of the most common modern misinterpretations of the Bible: the idea that “judge not” is a mandate for moral relativism. 

If “judge not” meant that moral judgments never matter, the second half of the verse—“forgive, and ye shall be forgiven”—would be rendered nonsensical. **You cannot forgive an action unless you have first made the moral judgment that the action was wrong.** Forgiveness requires a moral framework; it is the decision to release someone from the debt of a recognized wrong.

Therefore, Jesus is not abolishing moral reasoning. Rather, He is addressing the *posture of the heart* and the *limits of human authority*. Here is an exploration of why this verse does not negate morality, and the profound difference between spiritual discernment and the appetite to condemn.

---

### 1. Why "Judge Not" is Not Moral Relativism
In the Greek text of Luke 6:37, Jesus uses two distinct but related words: *krino* (to judge, separate, or evaluate) and *katakrino* (to condemn, or pass a final sentence against). 

When Jesus says "judge not" and "condemn not," He is prohibiting humans from usurping the role of the Final Judge (God). He is warning against:
*   **Playing God:** Assuming we know the entirety of a person’s heart, motives, and ultimate destiny.
*   **Self-Righteousness:** Using moral standards as a weapon to elevate ourselves above others.
*   **Finality:** Writing off a human being as irredeemable or beyond the reach of grace.

The New Testament frequently commands believers to make moral judgments regarding *actions, teachings, and behaviors* (e.g., calling out hypocrisy, protecting the vulnerable, pursuing justice). What is forbidden is the arrogant, punitive judgment of a *person’s ultimate worth and soul*.

### 2. Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn
To navigate the world morally, one must practice **discernment**. However, human nature often masks an **appetite to condemn** as discernment. The two are fundamentally different in their motives, methods, and ultimate goals.

#### **Discernment (Clear-Eyed Evaluation)**
*   **The Motive:** Love, protection, truth, and restoration. Discernment seeks to align with reality.
*   **The Focus:** It evaluates *actions, fruit, and patterns*. It looks at behavior and asks, "Is this healthy? Is this true? Is this harmful?"
*   **The Posture:** Humble and self-aware. Just a few verses later (Luke 6:41-42), Jesus talks about removing the "log" from one's own eye before addressing the "speck" in another's. True discernment always begins with self-examination. 
*   **The Result:** Boundaries, wisdom, and the capacity to help. As Jesus says in the "speck and log" metaphor, the goal of removing the log from your own eye is so *"you can see clearly to take the speck out of your brother’s eye."* Discernment leads to clear-eyed help, not abandonment.

#### **The Appetite to Condemn (The Will to Punish)**
*   **The Motive:** Ego, fear, insecurity, and the desire for power or superiority. Condemnation seeks to elevate the judge by diminishing the accused.
*   **The Focus:** It evaluates the *person’s inherent worth*. It looks at a failure and says, "You are bad," rather than "You did a bad thing." 
*   **The Posture:** Arrogant and hypocritical. The appetite to condemn is often blind to its own faults (the "log") while obsessing over the faults of others. It treats moral standards as a club to beat others with.
*   **The Result:** Alienation, shame, and destruction. Condemnation closes the door on a person. It assigns them to the trash heap of humanity and washes its hands of them.

### 3. The Antidote: "Forgive [Release], and Ye Shall Be Forgiven"
The bridge between discernment and the refusal to condemn is found in the final command of the verse: *forgive*. 

The Greek word used here is *apoluo*, which literally means to **"release," "let go," or "set free."** 

When you combine these three commands, a complete ethical framework emerges:
1.  **Do not judge (usurp God's role):** Recognize that you do not have all the facts, nor do you possess the perfect purity required to stand in final judgment over another human soul.
2.  **Do not condemn (refuse the appetite for destruction):** When you see moral failure (discernment), do not use it as an excuse to write the person off, rejoice in their downfall, or seek their ultimate ruin.
3.  **Release (forgive):** Let go of the debt. Hold boundaries if necessary to protect yourself and others, but do not hold onto bitterness, vengeance, or the desire to see the other person punished. 

### Summary
Saying "moral judgments never matter" is an act of intellectual and moral laziness; it is a refusal to care about truth, justice, or the well-being of others. 

Luke 6:37 demands something much harder. It demands that we care deeply about what is right and wrong (discernment), but that we apply that moral framework with radical mercy, remembering our own profound flaws, and leaving the final gavel in the hands of God. Discernment sees the sin; the appetite to condemn attacks the sinner; but grace sees the sin, loves the sinner, and releases the debt.
