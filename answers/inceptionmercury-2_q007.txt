**Luke 6:37 in context**

> “Judge not, and ye shall not be judged; condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned; forgive, and ye shall be forgiven.”  
> — Luke 6:37 (KJV)

The verse is part of the “Sermon on the Plain,” a collection of ethical teachings that parallels the more familiar Sermon on the Mount (Matt 5‑7). Its three‑fold command—*judge not, condemn not, forgive*—is not a blanket denial that any moral evaluation is useless. Rather, it is a warning against a particular *attitude* and *practice* of judgment that is:

1. **Self‑serving** – using one’s moral authority to elevate oneself and to punish others for personal gratification.
2. **Unloving** – allowing anger, envy, or a desire for retribution to dominate the heart.
3. **Unforgiving** – refusing to extend the mercy that God extends to us.

Understanding the verse therefore requires separating two concepts that are often conflated:

| **Discernment** (good) | **Condemnation** (bad) |
|-----------------------|------------------------|
| *Seeing* reality correctly (e.g., recognizing sin, injustice, danger). | *Judging* as a weapon—quick, harsh, and final. |
| *Guided by love* and the desire to help the other grow. | *Guided by self‑interest* or a need for superiority. |
| *Open‑ended* – invites dialogue, correction, restoration. | *Closed‑ended* – ends the conversation with a verdict. |
| *Grounded in humility* (“I am also a sinner”). | *Grounded in pride* (“I am the judge”). |
| *Paired with forgiveness*; the judgment is provisional, not final. | *Paired with unforgiveness*; the judgment becomes a permanent label. |

---

## 1. “Moral judgments never matter” – what that claim entails

If one were to say *“moral judgments never matter,”* the implication is that any act of evaluating right and wrong is irrelevant, perhaps even harmful. This stance can be motivated by:

* **Relativism** – “all values are equally valid, so judging is pointless.”
* **Nihilism** – “there is no moral truth, so judgment is absurd.”
* **Anti‑authoritarianism** – “any judgment is an act of oppression, so we must cease all moral appraisal.”

While each of these positions has philosophical roots, they all share a **rejection of discernment**: they deny that there is any objective or even intersubjective basis for distinguishing good from evil, right from wrong, or harmful from harmless. Consequently, they tend to:

* Undermine the possibility of moral responsibility.
* Make it difficult to speak against injustice, abuse, or systemic sin.
* Reduce the community to a purely descriptive, value‑free arena.

In short, “moral judgments never matter” is a **denial of the need for moral evaluation** altogether.

## 2. What Luke 6:37 actually says

### 2.1 “Judge not”

*The Greek* *μὴ κρίνητε* (mē krínēte) is an imperative that can be rendered “do not judge” or “do not pass a judgment.” The verb *κρίνω* (krínō) has a broad semantic range:

1. **Legal judgment** – a formal verdict.
2. **Moral assessment** – forming an opinion about right or wrong.
3. **Personal evaluation** – a snap, often harsh, appraisal of another’s character.

Jesus is not abolishing the *capacity* to see right from wrong. He is warning against the *abuse* of that capacity when it becomes a **self‑serving verdict** that harms the other and entrenches the judge’s own pride.

### 2.2 “Condemn not”

*Condemn* (*καταγνῶσιν*, *katagnōsin*) adds a layer of finality and punishment. It is not merely “seeing a fault” but “declaring the person irrevocably bad.” The verse links this to the promise: *“and ye shall not be condemned.”* The logic is reciprocal: a heart that constantly seeks to condemn others will itself be subject to condemnation—by God, by community, or by the self‑destructive consequences of bitterness.

### 2.3 “Forgive”

Forgiveness is the *counter‑action* that heals the wound inflicted by judgment and condemnation. It is not a denial of moral reality; it is an *act of grace* that says: “I recognize the fault, but I do not let it define the person forever.” Forgiveness is the *restorative* end of discernment, not the *destructive* end of condemnation.

## 3. Discernment vs. the appetite to condemn

| **Discernment** | **Appetite to Condemn** |
|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Purpose** – to protect, guide, and restore. | **Purpose** – to dominate, punish, or assert superiority. |
| **Method** – careful listening, humility, prayer, communal accountability. | **Method** – quick labeling, gossip, public shaming. |
| **Outcome** – opens space for repentance and change. | **Outcome** – closes space, breeds resentment, entrenches sin. |
| **Spirit** – love (agape) and hope (elpis). | **Spirit** – fear, anger, or pride. |
| **Biblical model** – Jesus confronting the Pharisees *with* love (Matt 23:13‑36) and offering them a chance to repent. | **Biblical warning** – “the spirit that is in the mouths of the wicked is a spirit of condemnation” (Prov 24:5). |

### 3.1 Theological grounding

* **God’s own judgment** (e.g., the parable of the wheat and the tares, Matt 13:24‑30) is *patient* and *final*—it will be rendered at the eschaton, not in the moment. In the meantime, God *calls* believers to *judge* *righteously* (John 7:24: “Do not judge by appearances, but judge with right judgment”). The “right judgment” is *truthful* and *loving*.

* **The “law of love”** (Romans 13:10) supersedes a legalistic, punitive approach. When love is the guiding principle, the impulse to condemn is neutralized.

* **The “golden rule”** (Matt 7:12) turns the judgment back on the self: “Do unto others as you would have them do unto you.” If you would not want to be condemned, you should not condemn others.

### 3.2 Psychological insight

Modern psychology distinguishes between **judgmental thinking** (categorical, black‑and‑white) and **mindful observation** (non‑judgmental awareness). The former correlates with anxiety, shame, and aggression; the latter with empathy, self‑compassion, and prosocial behavior. The biblical injunction aligns with the latter: *observe the fault, but do not cling to it as a defining label.*

---

## 4. Practical implications

1. **In personal relationships** – When you notice a friend’s harmful behavior, *discern* the pattern (e.g., “You often speak harshly when stressed”), *address* it with love (“I’m worried about how this affects you”), and *offer forgiveness* if they repent. Do not *condemn* (“You’re a terrible person”) because that will shut down dialogue.

2. **In community/ church discipline** – The early church practiced *discipline* (Matt 18:15‑17) that was *restorative*, not *condemnatory*. The aim was to bring the erring member back into fellowship, not to label them permanently as “unworthy.”

3. **In societal issues** – Public policy must *discern* injustice (e.g., systemic racism) and *address* it, but it should avoid a *condemnatory rhetoric* that demonizes entire groups. Instead, it should promote *reconciliation* and *restorative justice*.

4. **In self‑assessment** – The verse also points inward: we must not *condemn* ourselves for past failures. Recognizing our own sin (discernment) is essential, but the *appetite to condemn* is counter‑productive; forgiveness—both divine and self‑granted—restores us.

---

## 5. Summing up the difference

| **“Moral judgments never matter”** | **Luke 6:37** |
|-----------------------------------|----------------|
| Denies the *need* for any moral evaluation. | Affirms the *need* for accurate moral perception (discernment). |
| Leads to moral relativism or nihilism. | Leads to *loving* discernment that protects the vulnerable. |
| Often results in *passivity* toward injustice. | Calls for *active* love that corrects, restores, and forgives. |
| Removes the *possibility* of forgiveness because there is no “fault” to be forgiven. | Makes forgiveness the *necessary* response to the fault that has been discerned. |
| Treats judgment as a *static* concept: “judgment = bad.” | Treats judgment as a *dynamic* tool: “judgment + love = restoration.” |

In short, Luke 6:37 does **not** say that “moral judgments never matter.” It says that *the way we exercise judgment* matters. When judgment becomes a **self‑serving condemnation**, it blocks the flow of mercy and invites the same condemnation upon the judge. When judgment is exercised as **discernment rooted in love**, it opens the door to **forgiveness** and **restoration**, which is the ultimate aim of the Christian ethic. The passage therefore draws a sharp line between *seeing* truth and *using* that truth to crush another, urging us instead to *see* truth and *respond* with grace.
