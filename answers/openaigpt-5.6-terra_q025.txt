The danger is not simply that goodness is *visible*, but that it is done **“to be seen of men.”** The issue is the intended audience and desired reward.

In Matthew 6:1, Jesus warns against practicing righteousness “before men, **to be seen of them**.” The phrase identifies the motive: public action becomes spiritually dangerous when human recognition is its aim—when praise, status, reputation, influence, or the feeling of being regarded as good becomes the payoff.

That fits the immediate context:

- In **Matthew 5:16**, Jesus says, “Let your light so shine before men,” so that people may see good works and glorify the Father.
- In **Matthew 6:1–18**, he warns against almsgiving, prayer, and fasting performed in order to receive public acclaim.

So the difference is not merely whether others see the deed:

| Public goodness in Matthew 5 | Performative goodness in Matthew 6 |
|---|---|
| Others see the deed and glorify God | Others see the deed and glorify the doer |
| God is the ultimate audience | The crowd is the ultimate audience |
| Good works bear witness | Good works become self-advertisement |

Public goodness is morally dangerous because it can easily turn an act that appears to love God or neighbor into an act of self-exaltation. The poor person may still receive help; the public may still be encouraged; yet the giver may have converted mercy into a means of acquiring admiration.

Jesus’ statement, “they have no reward of your Father,” means that if human applause was the reward sought and received, it has already been paid. The person has chosen the passing reward of reputation rather than the Father’s reward.

This does not require that every visible good act be sinful, nor does it mean one must always hide every good work. Some acts necessarily occur in public: serving, teaching, giving institutional leadership, defending someone, or contributing to a communal cause. The question is: **Why am I doing this, and whose approval would satisfy me?**

A useful test is:

- Would I still do it if no one knew?
- Would I be content if someone else received the credit?
- Does this direct attention to God and the good of my neighbor, or toward my moral image?
- Am I distressed mainly because the good was not done, or because I was not noticed?

Visibility can be a temptation, but the central problem in the verse is the **craving to be seen**—the desire to turn righteousness into a performance for human praise.
