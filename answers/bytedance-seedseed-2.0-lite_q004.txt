Interpretations of this passage from Proverbs vary across Christian traditions, but a mainstream framing of the trust it calls for, and the distinction between humble self-distrust and anti-intellectualism, can be outlined as follows:

### The nature of the trust described
The passage calls for whole-person, covenantal trust in God’s faithfulness and infinite wisdom, rooted in the biblical context of Proverbs’ broader emphasis on divine order as more reliable than limited human perspective. This is not blind faith, but a deliberate choice to center God’s revealed character and guidance (as understood within the tradition) rather than prioritizing one’s own partial, biased viewpoint as the ultimate authority for decision-making.

### Distinguishing humble self-distrust from anti-intellectualism
1. **Humble self-distrust (the posture the passage actually endorses)**  
This posture rejects the idolatry of treating one’s own understanding as infallible or sufficient, but it does not reject human reason or intellectual inquiry. It aligns with Proverbs’ repeated calls to pursue wisdom, learn from community, and practice discernment—efforts that require active intellectual engagement. Humble self-distrust acknowledges that human reasoning is shaped by limited experience, personal bias, and spiritual brokenness, so it submits individual reasoning to the broader framework of divine wisdom and communal discernment. It complements, rather than cancels, the use of critical thinking: for example, a person might research options for a major life choice, seek counsel from trusted mentors, and still hold their preliminary conclusions lightly enough to prioritize guidance they discern through their faith tradition.

2. **Anti-intellectualism (a misreading of the passage)**  
Anti-intellectualism rejects or dismisses critical reasoning, formal learning, and intellectual inquiry as unnecessary or even untrustworthy. This is incompatible with the passage’s context and demands: the verse calls to “acknowledge” God, which is an act of intellectual and spiritual recognition that requires engagement with theological truth, not the abandonment of thought. Proverbs as a whole elevates the pursuit of wisdom, which includes learning, reflection, and discernment—so the passage never frames human intellect as inherently bad, only as insufficient when relied on in isolation from divine guidance.

It is important to note that some smaller religious traditions may interpret this passage through a more fundamentalist lens that blurs these lines, but the majority of mainline Protestant, Catholic, and Eastern Orthodox theological frameworks uphold the distinction outlined above.
