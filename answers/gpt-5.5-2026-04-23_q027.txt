The Pharisee is condemned by the whole posture of his prayer, not by one isolated error.

Luke tells us the point before the parable begins:

> “He spake this parable unto certain which **trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others**.”  
> — Luke 18:9

So the central problem is **self-trust joined to contempt**.

### 1. Not condemned for fasting or tithing

The Pharisee’s practices were not evil in themselves.

> “I fast twice in the week, I give tithes of all that I possess.”

Fasting, tithing, moral seriousness, and gratitude to God can all be good. Jesus is not saying, “Virtue is bad” or “religious discipline is hypocrisy.” The problem is that the Pharisee uses these things as evidence of his own superiority and as the basis of his standing before God.

His virtues have become a mirror in which he admires himself.

### 2. His “prayer” is really self-justification

He begins with “God, I thank thee,” but the content of the prayer is not really God-centered. It is an inventory of himself:

> “I am not as other men are…”

He does not confess sin. He does not ask for mercy. He does not intercede for anyone. He does not adore God’s holiness. He simply presents his religious résumé.

That is why the phrase is so striking:

> “The Pharisee stood and prayed thus **with himself**…”

Whether understood as “by himself” or “to himself,” the effect is the same: his prayer turns inward. God becomes the audience for his self-approval.

### 3. Comparison reveals the disease

His righteousness is comparative:

> “I am not as other men are… or even as this publican.”

He does not stand before God’s holiness; he stands beside another sinner and feels tall. But comparison with worse people is spiritually dangerous because it avoids the real question: What am I before God?

The Pharisee’s confidence depends on finding someone beneath him.

### 4. Contempt is the clearest symptom

Luke explicitly says the parable is aimed at those who “despised others.”

The Pharisee’s contempt for the tax collector shows that his “righteousness” has not made him merciful, humble, or loving. It has made him proud. In biblical terms, that means his righteousness is not whole. He may be outwardly disciplined, but his heart is out of alignment with God.

He thanks God that he is not like the tax collector, but God is about to justify the tax collector rather than him.

### 5. Is it false doctrine?

Yes, but not necessarily in the sense that the Pharisee could not state orthodox beliefs. His false doctrine is practical and spiritual: he acts as though righteousness before God can be secured by moral achievement and religious distinction.

He believes, functionally, “My record makes me acceptable.”

That is the doctrine Jesus rejects.

The Pharisee’s error is not merely that he is proud while having correct theology; his pride reveals a distorted view of God, righteousness, sin, mercy, and justification.

### 6. Why the tax collector is spiritually truer

The tax collector says only:

> “God be merciful to me a sinner.”

His posture is one of truth. He does not bargain. He does not compare. He does not say, “At least I am better than…” He offers no résumé and claims no entitlement.

He simply stands before God as he really is: guilty, needy, dependent on mercy.

His bodily posture expresses this inward reality:

> “standing afar off”  
> “would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven”  
> “smote upon his breast”

These actions do not earn his justification. Rather, they manifest repentance, humility, and dependence on divine mercy.

### 7. The tax collector is not justified because humility is a new merit

This is important. Jesus is not replacing one form of self-righteousness with another: “The Pharisee had fasting and tithing, but the tax collector had humility, so humility earned salvation.”

No. The tax collector is justified because he casts himself on God’s mercy. His humility is not the price of justification; it is the truthful posture of one who knows he needs grace.

### 8. The decisive contrast

The Pharisee says, in effect:

> “God, look at what I am.”

The tax collector says:

> “God, have mercy on what I am.”

That is why the tax collector “went down to his house justified rather than the other.” He came to God without defense, without comparison, without contempt, and without self-display.

### Summary

What condemns the Pharisee?

- **Pride**, because he exalts himself.
- **Comparison**, because he measures himself against other sinners instead of before God.
- **Contempt**, because he despises the tax collector.
- **Self-display**, because he turns virtue into self-advertisement.
- **False practical doctrine**, because he trusts in his own righteousness rather than God’s mercy.

Why is the tax collector spiritually truer?

Because he tells the truth about himself before God: he is a sinner in need of mercy. His prayer is poor in words but rich in reality. The Pharisee has religious achievements but no repentance; the tax collector has nothing to offer but his need, and that is precisely the place where grace is received.
