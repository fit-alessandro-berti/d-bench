The five candidates are not equally present in the text, and the difference matters.

**False doctrine is not the verdict.** The Pharisee fasts twice weekly and tithes everything he owns — both exceed Pharisaic requirements. Jesus does not correct his theology propositional by propositional. There is no heresy refuted. If anything, the parable presupposes that the Pharisee is *doctrinally* orthodox and *morally* diligent; that is precisely what makes the story so unsettling.

**Pride is the root, but the parable locates it more specifically.** Verse 9 already names the audience: people who "trusted in themselves that they were righteous" (the verb is *pepoithotes* — a settled, confident self-reliance) and "despised others" (*exouthenountas* — counted them as nothing). Pride, comparison, and contempt are all in the text, but they hang together: the self-trusting heart *necessarily* compares (otherwise it cannot congratulate itself) and *necessarily* despises (otherwise it cannot preserve its self-image).

**The precise mechanism is the use of virtue as self-display.** Three textual details point here:

1. *He prayed "with himself"* (πρὸς ἑαυτόν, v. 11). The preposition can mean "alone," but the traditional reading is sharper: he was not really addressing God. He was addressing an audience — even if the only audience was himself.
2. *The content of his "thanksgiving" is self-praise.* "God, I thank thee…" is the form of gratitude; what follows is autobiography. God functions as a witness to the Pharisee's excellences, not as the giver of mercy.
3. *His virtues are listed, not offered.* Fasting and tithing become inventory items establishing a credit balance, not responses to grace. They are not wrong in themselves; they are *weaponized* in the act of self-justification.

So the deepest diagnosis is not that the Pharisee was proud (he was), nor that he compared (he did), nor that he despised (he did), but that he treated his righteousness as a possession to be displayed before God rather than a relationship to be received from God. Pride is the soil; virtue-as-display is the crop.

**Why the tax collector's posture is spiritually truer** — because it does the four things the Pharisee's prayer refuses to do:

1. *It looks at God, not at self.* "Would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven" (cf. Ezra 9:6; the gesture of conscious unworthiness).
2. *It locates itself honestly.* "Sinned" — a strong word (not "have fallen short," but *hamartōlos*, the category for open, notorious sinners). He does not relativize his standing.
3. *It refuses comparison entirely.* There is no mention of another person. He does not need another sinner beneath him to feel justified.
4. *It appeals to mercy, not merit.* "God, be merciful to me" uses *hilasthēti* — the vocabulary of the mercy seat (*hilastērion*, Rom 3:25). He asks for atonement, not applause. The verb implies a sacrifice; he is throwing himself on the covering of sin rather than on the covering of his own works.

**The synthesis.** The parable is not "humility wins, pride loses," as if the tax collector's contrition earned God's favor by an opposite arithmetic. It is about the *kind of self* that can stand before a holy God. The Pharisee's self is *closed* — it is self-sufficient, self-disclosing, self-congratulating. The tax collector's self is *open* — it acknowledges need, accepts distance, and receives mercy. Justification in v. 14 is therefore not reward for humility but the *form* that justifying grace takes when it meets a self that will not pretend.

This is why Paul reaches for precisely this text when he speaks of God "justifying the ungodly" (Rom 4:5). The tax collector is justified not because his prayer is morally impressive, but because he is the kind of person who can actually receive justification — namely, someone who has stopped trying to produce it himself.
