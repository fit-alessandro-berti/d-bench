Luke 6:37 — *“Judge not, and ye shall not be judged: condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned: forgive, and ye shall be forgiven.”* — is often misunderstood as a blanket prohibition against all forms of moral evaluation. But this is not what Jesus intends. To understand the difference between Jesus’ warning and a denial of moral discernment, we must distinguish between **judgment as condemnation** and **judgment as discernment**.

---

### ❌ What Jesus Forbids: Condemnation and Self-Righteous Judgment

Jesus is not prohibiting moral reasoning, evaluation, or discernment. He is forbidding:

1. **Self-righteous condemnation** — judging others from a position of moral superiority, ignoring one’s own faults (cf. Matthew 7:3–5: “Why do you see the speck in your neighbor’s eye, but do not notice the log in your own eye?”).
2. **Final, absolute judgment of a person’s soul or worth** — which belongs to God alone (James 4:12: “There is only one Lawgiver and Judge…”).
3. **Judgment motivated by anger, revenge, or hypocrisy** — judgment that seeks to elevate oneself by tearing others down.

This kind of judgment is *condemnation*: it shuts the door on mercy, grace, and restoration. It says, “You are beyond help,” and often reflects more about the judge’s heart than the judged person’s actions.

---

### ✅ What Jesus Endorses: Discernment and Moral Clarity

Discernment — the ability to perceive truth, recognize sin, and distinguish right from wrong — is not only permitted in Scripture; it is *required*.

- Jesus himself discriminated between true discipleship and hypocrisy (Matthew 23).
- Paul instructs the church to judge those inside the congregation (1 Corinthians 5:12–13): “What have I to do with judging outsiders? It is those who are inside that you are to judge.”
- The apostle John says, “Beloved, do not believe every spirit, but test the spirits to see whether they are from God” (1 John 4:1).
- The author of Hebrews exhorts: “Solid food is for the mature, for those who have their powers of discernment trained by custom to distinguish good from evil” (Hebrews 5:14).

Discernment is **observational, corrective, and restorative**. It asks:  
> *“What is true? What is harmful? How can this be healed?”*

Condemnation answers:  
> *“You are evil. You deserve punishment. You are not worthy of grace.”*

Discernment says: *“This is sin — but you are still loved, and you can be changed.”*  
Condemnation says: *“This is sin — and so are you.”*

---

### 💡 The Key Difference: Motive and Method

| Aspect | Discernment | Condemnation |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| **Motive** | Love, truth, restoration | Self-righteousness, pride, control |
| **Focus** | The sin (not the sinner) | The person’s identity |
| **Goal** | Healing, growth, repentance | Punishment, exclusion, status |
| **Tone** | Gentle, patient, truthful | Harsh, final, shaming |
| **Biblical Example** | Jesus with the woman caught in adultery (John 8) | Pharisees condemning the same woman |

Jesus says to the woman, *“Neither do I condemn you; go and sin no more.”*  
He *discerns* the sin — "go and sin no more."  
But he does not *condemn* her — “Neither do I condemn you.”

---

### 🔁 The Divine Mirror: “Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven”

The verse ends with *“Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven.”* This is the key.

Jesus ties our capacity to forgive others to God’s forgiveness of us. The same standard we apply to others, God will apply to us (Matthew 7:2). If we constantly condemn, we reveal a heart that does not understand grace. If we discern with humility and extend mercy, we reflect God’s character.

> To forbid all judgment would be to forbid wisdom.  
> To condone all condemnation would be to destroy community and grace.

---

### ✅ Conclusion: Moral Judgments *Do* Matter — But Not as We Often Use Them

Luke 6:37 does **not** say moral judgments never matter.  
It says: **Don’t judge as if you’re God. Don’t condemn as if you’re above sin. Judge righteously, but always with mercy.**

Discernment is the eye of the healer.  
Condemnation is the fist of the executioner.

Jesus calls us to be discerning — not judgmental.  
To speak truth — not to crush souls.  
To forgive — not because sin doesn’t matter, but because grace matters *more*.

Let us then judge with wisdom, pronounce no final sentence, and always — always — leave room for forgiveness.
