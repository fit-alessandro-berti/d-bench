To understand the dynamics of this parable, we must look to Luke’s introductory framing in verse 9: Jesus is speaking to those who "trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others." This verse serves as the interpretive key to the entire passage. 

What condemns the Pharisee is not merely one of the options you listed, but rather the toxic ecosystem created by **all of them working together**, with **pride** as the root and **contempt** as its fruit. 

Here is a breakdown of how these elements function in the Pharisee’s condemnation, followed by an explanation of why the tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer.

### 1. What Condemns the Pharisee?

**The Root: Pride (Trusting in Self)**
The fundamental issue is that the Pharisee's religion orbits around himself, not God. The text literally says he "prayed thus with himself"—God is merely the audience to his own self-congratulation. His pride convinces him that he is the source of his own righteousness, effectively making him his own savior and god. 

**The Branch: Comparison (Relative Righteousness)**
The Pharisee’s righteousness is entirely *relative*. He does not measure himself against the holiness of God; he measures himself against "other men." By using the criminal behavior of others as his baseline, he lowers the standard of righteousness to a level he can easily clear. Comparison allows him to disguise his sin as virtue.

**The Fruit: Contempt (Despising Others)**
Pride and comparison inevitably birth contempt. The Pharisee cannot elevate himself without pushing someone else down. His "gratitude" is weaponized: "I thank thee that I am not... even as this publican." He looks at the tax collector not with compassion, but as a foil to make himself look brighter. This violates the core of the Law, which demands love for neighbor.

**The Mechanism: The Use of Virtue as Self-Display**
The Pharisee lists his spiritual disciplines—fasting twice a week (beyond what the law required) and tithing everything. These are genuine virtues and rigorous disciplines, but he weaponizes them. He uses his obedience as a currency to purchase God's favor and to assert his moral superiority over others. In his hands, virtue becomes a mechanism of exclusion rather than a reflection of God's grace.

**The Underlying Assumption: False Doctrine**
While the Pharisee does not preach a sermon, his prayer reveals a deeply flawed theology (false doctrine). He believes that justification (being declared righteous) can be achieved by human effort and moral scoreboard-keeping. He believes God grades on a curve, and that his good deeds outweigh his bad. He lacks any consciousness of the depth of his own sin or the necessity of divine grace.

**In summary:** The Pharisee is condemned because he brings a resume to a courtroom that requires a confession. He is entirely self-sufficient, leaving no room for God's grace. 

### 2. Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?

The tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer because it corresponds to **objective reality before a holy God**. 

**His Physical Posture Reflects Spiritual Reality**
*   *"Standing afar off"*: He recognizes his alienation from God due to his sin. He does not presume to take the place of the Pharisee near the Holy of Holies.
*   *"Would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven"*: He feels the weight of shame and unworthiness. He knows he has no right to look God in the face.
*   *"Smote upon his breast"*: In ancient near eastern culture, striking the chest was a sign of intense grief and anguish. He is not merely admitting he made mistakes; he is agonizing over his sin. 

**His Words Reflect the True Mechanism of Salvation**
His prayer, "God be merciful to me a sinner," is theologically profound. The Greek word used for "merciful" here is *hilaskomai*, which is frequently translated in the Septuagint (the Greek Old Testament) as "propitiate" or "make atonement for." 

The Pharisee is in the very same temple where the blood of lambs is being spilled on the altar. The tax collector is not just asking God to be generally nice or to overlook his sin; he is asking God to *apply the atoning sacrifice* to him. He knows his only hope is not his own record, but the provision of a substitute. 

**The Verdict: Justification**
Jesus delivers the shocking twist: the tax collector goes home "justified" (declared righteous, acquitted). Why? Because the tax collector aligns himself with the truth of the Gospel: **there is no one righteous, and salvation belongs to those who bring their emptiness to God rather than their achievements.** 

The Pharisee’s posture is spiritually false because it is a delusion. He believes he is well, so he receives no medicine. The tax collector’s posture is spiritually true because he knows he is dying, and in his desperation, he finds the only One who can heal him. As Jesus says elsewhere, "I have not come to call the righteous, but sinners to repentance" (Luke 5:32).
