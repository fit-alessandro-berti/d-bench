In this verse the moral danger is **not visibility itself, but the *craving* to be seen**.

The Greek of Matthew 6:1 contains a purpose clause: Jesus forbids doing righteousness **πρὸς τὸ θεαθῆναι αὐτοῖς**—“*in order to be seen* by them.” The problem is the *telos*, the aim: the act is performed as a spectacle to win human approval rather than as service offered to God.

### Why the craving is dangerous

1. **It changes the audience.** A good deed done “before men” to impress them is really being offered to men, not to God. The crowd becomes the judge and rewarder.
2. **It turns piety into theater.** Jesus calls such people “hypocrites” in the verses that follow (6:2, 5). The outward act may be generous or devout, but the inward motive is self-display.
3. **It trades a lasting reward for a fleeting one.** “They have their reward” (6:2, 5, 16) means they have already been paid in full—in applause, status, and admiration. Nothing remains for the Father to give.
4. **It corrupts the heart.** Seeking to be seen nurtures pride and a divided self: appearing holy while really loving praise.

### Visibility is not automatically evil

Jesus himself says elsewhere, “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven” (Matt. 5:16). So being seen is not the sin. The difference is the *motive*:

- **Matt. 5:16** — good works seen, so that **God** is glorified.  
- **Matt. 6:1** — righteousness done **in order to be seen**, so that **self** is glorified.

### Practical point

Public goodness becomes morally dangerous when the desire for recognition begins to drive, shape, or sweeten the act. The safeguard Jesus gives is to do righteous deeds—giving, praying, fasting—before the Father who “seeth in secret” (6:4, 6, 18). If others happen to see, the question is whether the heart is still seeking God’s “Well done” rather than human applause.
