### Connection Between Speech and Hidden Moral Stockpiles in the Heart

In Matthew 12:34–35, Jesus uses the metaphor of the "heart" as a repository or "treasure" (Greek: *thesauros*, implying a stored collection) of moral and spiritual content—either "good" (righteous, virtuous inclinations) or "evil" (sinful, corrupt tendencies). These are "hidden moral stockpiles" because they represent the inner life of a person: thoughts, intentions, beliefs, and character that are not outwardly visible but accumulate over time like a hidden cache. Speech, in turn, is portrayed as an outflow or expression from this inner abundance ("out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh"). Just as a full container spills its contents when pressed, words naturally emerge from and reflect the heart's dominant moral inventory. Jesus challenges the Pharisees (called a "generation of vipers," implying deceitful and poisonous hearts) by arguing that their hypocritical or accusatory speech (e.g., earlier in the chapter, attributing Jesus' miracles to demonic power) betrays their inner evil, making genuine "good things" impossible without a transformed heart.

This connection emphasizes that speech is not arbitrary or superficial but intrinsically tied to one's core moral state. It aligns with broader biblical themes (e.g., Proverbs 4:23: "Keep thy heart with all diligence; for out of it are the issues of life") where the heart is the wellspring of all human action, including verbal expression.

### Are Words Symptoms, Choices, Revelations, or All Three?

The passage suggests that words function as **all three**—symptoms, choices, and revelations—interconnected in revealing the heart's moral reality:

- **Symptoms**: Words act as diagnostic indicators of the heart's condition, much like symptoms signal an underlying disease. Jesus rhetorically asks how "evil" people can "speak good things," implying that speech is a telltale sign of inner corruption or virtue. If the heart's "treasure" is evil, words will inevitably manifest that flaw, serving as evidence rather than coincidence.

- **Choices**: The language of "bringeth forth" (Greek: *ekballei*, meaning to "cast out" or "produce") portrays speech as an active process, where a person draws from and selects from their inner storehouse. This implies volition—a good or evil person *chooses* to express what aligns with their heart's abundance, making words not just passive leaks but deliberate acts that align with or reinforce one's moral character.

- **Revelations**: At its core, the passage teaches that speech *uncovers* the hidden stockpiles, exposing what was previously concealed. The mouth "speaketh" from the heart's overflow, providing a window into the soul's true state. This revelatory aspect is why Jesus uses it to judge the Pharisees: their words betray their unregenerate hearts, revealing hypocrisy that might otherwise remain hidden.

In summary, words are not isolated events but holistic expressions of the heart—diagnosing its health (symptom), enacting its will (choice), and disclosing its secrets (revelation). This underscores Jesus' call for inner transformation (e.g., through repentance and faith) to produce truly good speech and life. For deeper context, this ties into the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5–7), where Jesus prioritizes heart purity over outward appearances.
