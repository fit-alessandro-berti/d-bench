Based on Matthew 6:1, the moral danger of public goodness lies **not in the visibility itself, but in the craving to be seen**—specifically, the motivation and intention behind the action. 

Here is a breakdown of why this is the case, according to the text and the broader context of the passage:

### 1. The Problem is the *Motive* (The Craving to Be Seen)
The verse warns against doing acts of righteousness "to be seen of them" (in Greek, *theatrenai*—meaning to be watched as a spectator sport, from which we get the word *theater*). 

The act of giving alms (charity/goodness) is not condemned; in fact, Jesus assumes His followers *will* give alms (starting verse 2 with "When thou doest thine alms..."). The condemnation is reserved for the **intent**: performing righteousness as a performance to secure human applause, praise, or status. When the desire for human approval becomes the driving force, the act ceases to be about love, mercy, or duty to God, and becomes an exercise in self-worship.

### 2. Visibility Itself is Neutral
Visibility cannot be the inherent problem because Jesus commands elsewhere that good deeds *should* be seen:
> *"Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."* (Matthew 5:16)

The difference between Matthew 5:16 and Matthew 6:1 is the **direction of the glory**:
*   **In Matthew 5:16**, the good works are visible, but the audience looks past the doer to praise God ("glorify your Father"). 
*   **In Matthew 6:1**, the good works are weaponized to draw attention to *yourself*, capturing the glory that belongs to God. 

### 3. The Spiritual Consequence
Jesus notes that if the goal is human applause, that applause is the only reward you get ("otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven"). The craving to be seen cheapens a transcendent, eternal act into a transactional, earthly vanity. You trade the eternal approval of God for the fleeting, fickle approval of humans.

### Conclusion
Public goodness is morally dangerous only when the heart's appetite is hooked on human recognition. Visibility is merely a spotlight; the danger is whether you use that spotlight to illuminate the needs of others (and God's goodness) or to cast a shadow that makes yourself look heroic.
