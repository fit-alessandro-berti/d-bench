In Genesis 4:6–7, the description of moral agency centers around the concept of individual responsibility in the face of temptation and sin. The Lord's address to Cain reveals a nuanced view of evil that can be interpreted in several ways.

1. **Evil as Impulse or Beast**: The phrase "sin lieth at the door" can be perceived as depicting sin as a lurking creature, ready to pounce on Cain. This imagery implies that evil is an external force that seeks to overpower him, suggesting an instinctual battle between good and evil.

2. **Evil as Temptation**: The Lord's inquiry into Cain's anger and fallen countenance indicates awareness of Cain’s emotional and spiritual state. It suggests that evil is not just an impulse but also a temptation that arises from one's own feelings and choices. The term "desire" associated with sin implies that such temptations can be alluring, stirring internal conflict.

3. **Moral Responsibility**: The God’s statement “If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?” places significant responsibility on Cain. It emphasizes that while sin may be close and tempting, Cain still has the agency to choose righteousness. The directive "thou shalt rule over him" implies that individuals possess the capacity to govern their actions and resist sin. 

Overall, the passage portrays a picture of moral agency where evil is seen as both an external temptation and an internal struggle. It emphasizes that individuals are accountable for their choices, highlighting the importance of vigilance and self-mastery in resisting sin. Thus, while evil can be compelling, the responsibility to confront and overcome it lies firmly with the individual.
