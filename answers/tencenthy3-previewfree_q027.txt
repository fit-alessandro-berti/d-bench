The story of the Pharisee and the Publican (Tax Collector) is one of Jesus’ most incisive critiques of religious psychology. To answer your question, we must look at the precise Greek text and the theological framework of justification.

The condemnation of the Pharisee is not due to a single factor, but a **lethal combination** where his virtues become the very thing that separates him from God. However, the primary driver of his condemnation is **pride that leads to contempt.**

Here is a breakdown of why the Pharisee fails and why the Tax Collector succeeds.

### 1. What Condemns the Pharisee?

While all the elements you listed are present, they function in a hierarchy of sin:

**A. The Root Cause: Pride (Hubris)**
The text says he "trusted in himself that he was righteous" (v. 9). This is the theological crux. In the ancient world, and specifically in Jewish thought, righteousness is a status *imputed* by God. By trusting in *himself*, the Pharisee essentially dethrones God. He relies on his own "scorecard" rather than God’s mercy. His prayer is not a request for help; it is a self-congratulation.

**B. The Method: Comparison and Contempt**
The Pharisee "prayed thus *with himself*" (v. 11, Greek: *pros heauton*). This is a profound detail. He isn't actually speaking to God; he is speaking to himself, using God as an audience.
*   **Comparison:** He defines his goodness not by God’s absolute holiness, but by comparing himself to "other men" and specifically "this publican."
*   **Contempt:** Jesus says the parable is for those who "despised others" (v. 9). The Pharisee looks at the tax collector and sees a prop in his own story of success, rather than a fellow human being bearing the image of God.

**C. The Fatal Flaw: The Use of Virtue as Self-Display**
The Pharisee lists true virtues: fasting twice a week (beyond the requirement) and tithing everything. The sin is not that he did these things, but *why* and *how* he presented them.
*   He uses his virtue as a **shield** against God’s grace. If you are perfect by your own metrics, you don't need mercy.
*   He uses his virtue as a **weapon** to beat down another.

**Verdict:** It is **Pride** that condemns him. Comparison and contempt are the symptoms of that pride. The use of virtue as self-display is the *vehicle* of that pride. If he had been humble, his fasting and tithing would have been pleasing to God. Because he was proud, his virtues became his idols.

### 2. Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?

The Tax Collector (Publican) is the theological hero of the story because he embodies the biblical concept of *Anfechtung* (spiritual terror/contrition) leading to *Sola Gratia* (Grace alone).

**A. The Posture of Distance and Shame**
*   **"Standing afar off":** Unlike the Pharisee who likely stood near the front (the "best seats"), the tax collector stays back. He recognizes the gap between his sinfulness and God's holiness. He does not feel entitled to approach the Holy of Holies.
*   **"Would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven":** In the Bible, lifting hands or eyes is often a sign of claiming a blessing or making a demand. By keeping his eyes down, he acknowledges he has no claim on God. He is not bargaining; he is begging.

**B. The Physical Sign: "Smote upon his breast"**
This is a gesture of grief and self-incrimination. He isn't looking at the Pharisee; he is looking at his own heart. While the Pharisee cataloged his external deeds, the tax collector confronted his internal corruption.

**C. The Theology of the Prayer: "God be merciful to me a sinner"**
The Greek word used here is *hilaskomai* (to propitiate/be merciful). It is a plea for God to turn His wrath away.
*   **Honesty:** He calls himself "a sinner" (*hamartōlos*). He doesn't say "I've made some mistakes" or "I'm not as bad as him." He accepts the label.
*   **Dependency:** He does not list his good deeds (perhaps he had none, or perhaps he realized they were irrelevant before a Holy God). He relies entirely on the character of God—that God is merciful.

### The Paradox of Justification

Jesus concludes: *"This man went down to his house justified rather than the other."*

The word **Justified** (Greek: *dedikaiōmenos*) means "declared righteous." It is a legal term.

1.  **The Pharisee** approached God on the basis of his **performance**. Since he trusted in himself, he remained in himself. God cannot justify the proud because the proud refuse to admit they need justification.
2.  **The Tax Collector** approached God on the basis of **mercy**. He admitted his bankruptcy. Therefore, God filled his empty hands.

**Summary:** The Pharisee is condemned because he **excluded himself from grace** by being self-sufficient. The Tax Collector is saved because he **embraced his need for grace** by being self-despairing. As the Early Church Father Clement of Rome wrote: *"Let the strong not glory in his strength, nor the rich in his riches; but let him that glories, glory in the Lord."*
