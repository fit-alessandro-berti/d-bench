Luke answers the question before the parable even begins: it was spoken to "certain which trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others" (v. 9). Those two clauses are the diagnosis—self-trust as the root, contempt as its fruit. So the either/or framing of your list dissolves: the parable nests all five options into a single system.

**What it is not: false doctrine.** This is the parable's most frightening feature—everything the Pharisee says is true and formally correct. Thanksgiving is the proper opening for a Jewish prayer; a similar formula ("I thank thee... that thou hast not set my portion with those who sit idly in the street corners") survives in the Talmud without censure. His fasting *exceeds* the Law, which commanded one fast a year; he keeps two a week. His tithing exceeds the Law, which taxed produce; he tithes all he possesses. He even credits God: "I thank thee." No heresy, no lie, no detectable hypocrisy. The parable deliberately builds the strongest possible case—if the man were a fraud, we could dismiss him and relax. The condemnation has to lie in the *use* of true things.

**The root is relocated trust.** The ground of his standing before God is his own record. You can see it in the prayer's grammar: it contains no request. He asks God for nothing because he needs nothing; God is demoted from giver to notary, present to certify a self-assessment already complete. "Prayed thus with himself"—the Greek (*pros heauton*) is ambiguous, "stood by himself" or "prayed *to* himself"—and both readings converge on the same closed loop. He comes to God carrying himself and, verse 14 implies, goes home carrying only himself. The transaction is null.

**Comparison is the mechanism; contempt is the exhaust.** His righteousness is differential—it exists only as distance from others, so it requires identifiable inferiors to verify itself. "Even as this publican": he notices a man repenting in the corner and recruits him, mid-prayer, as a prop. The "despising" of v. 9 (literally, counting others as nothing) is therefore not an incidental vice layered onto sound piety; it is structurally necessary. A righteousness built on comparison feeds on other people's failures. And note the comparison's direction: always downward. He does not measure himself against Isaiah, who in this same temple saw the Lord and cried "Woe is me... I dwell in the midst of a people of unclean lips"—including himself among them. The Pharisee's composure and fluency in God's presence is itself evidence he has never actually met the one he's addressing; his god is small enough to be impressed by fasting.

So: self-trust is the root, comparison the method, contempt the fruit, and genuine virtue the building material—which makes your last option ("virtue as self-display") the closest single answer, except that the display's primary audience is the man himself. He believes his own résumé. That is worse than hypocrisy; hypocrites know what they're doing.

**Why the publican's posture is spiritually truer:**

- **It is accurate.** He is what he says: a sinner with no defense. The Pharisee's facts are true but his fundamental self-assessment is false. True prayer is correspondence—occupying your actual position before God. The publican stands where he really is; the Pharisee stands where he imagines himself to be.
- **It is non-comparative.** "God be merciful to me *the* sinner"—the Greek carries the definite article (*tō hamartōlō*), as though he were the only sinner in the world. He exits the status economy entirely: no foil, no audience, no ranking. The precise inversion of "not as other men."
- **It aims at mercy, not merit.** *Hilasthēti*—"be propitiated"—is atonement language. In the temple, with sacrifice proceeding around him, he appeals to what God provides rather than what he has done. The direction of dependence is correct: rescue located in God's character, an empty hand held out.
- **Body and word agree.** Standing afar off; eyes down (the normal prayer posture was eyes lifted toward heaven); hand striking the breast—the fault located within, not in the neighbors. His whole body prays the same petition his mouth does. Meanwhile the Pharisee's eyes rove the room; even in prayer, his attention is surveillance.

Then the verdict: "this man went down to his house *justified*"—*dedikaiōmenos*, a forensic word, a ruling pronounced. The man who brought righteousness left with nothing; the man who brought need left righteous. God's assessment tracks truth of position, not quality of performance.

The parable's last trick is that it traps its readers. The reflex "Lord, I thank thee that I am not like this Pharisee" is nearly irresistible—and it is the Pharisee's prayer verbatim with one substitution. Comparison survives even our humility. The story offers exactly one safe position, and it is neither the Pharisee's side of the contrast nor the reader's. It's the man in the corner, eyes down, asking for the only thing either man actually needed.
