The parable in Luke 18:9-14 condemns the Pharisee primarily due to his pride, self-righteousness, and contempt for others. Let's break down the reasons:

1. **False Doctrine**: The Pharisee's prayer is based on a false doctrine. He believes that his righteousness, as demonstrated by his fasting and tithing, is sufficient to earn God's favor. This is a misunderstanding of the Gospel, which teaches that salvation is a gift of God's grace, not something earned through good deeds.

2. **Pride**: The Pharisee's prayer is marked by pride. He boasts about his moral superiority over others, stating, "I thank thee, that I am not as other men are, extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even as this publican." This self-congratulatory attitude is a clear manifestation of pride. In the Christian faith, pride is considered a deadly sin because it distances one from God and hinders spiritual growth.

3. **Comparison**: The Pharisee's comparison with the publican is another reason for his condemnation. He is not only comparing himself to others but also elevating himself above them. This comparison is a form of contempt, as he looks down upon others, particularly the publican, who is seen as less righteous.

4. **Contempt**: The Pharisee's contempt for the publican is evident in his dismissal of the publican's prayer. He does not even acknowledge the publican's humility, instead focusing solely on his own righteousness. This contempt is a barrier to spiritual growth and is condemned in the Bible, as it shows a lack of empathy and understanding for others.

5. **Use of Virtue as Self-Display**: The Pharisee uses his virtues (fasting, tithing) as a means of self-display. He prays not to God but to impress others and to make himself feel superior. This misuse of virtue is condemned because it turns good deeds into a means of self-aggrandizement rather than acts of genuine devotion and love for God.

On the other hand, the tax collector's posture is treated as spiritually truer because of his humility and recognition of his own sinfulness. The publican acknowledges his unworthiness before God, saying, "God be merciful to me a sinner." This posture of humility and repentance is highly valued in the Christian faith. It reflects a true understanding of one's need for God's grace and mercy, which is the basis of salvation. The tax collector's prayer is a model of true repentance and humility, which are essential for spiritual growth and acceptance in the eyes of God.
