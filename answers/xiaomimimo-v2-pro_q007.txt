This is a profound and important distinction you’re raising. Luke 6:37 is often misunderstood as a prohibition against *all* moral judgment, but a closer reading suggests it’s about the **spirit and purpose** behind our judgments.

Let’s break down the difference:

### 1. **What the verse is NOT saying**
It does **not** say “discern nothing” or “never recognize right from wrong.”  
Jesus elsewhere teaches discernment (e.g., “know them by their fruits” in Matthew 7:16).  
The command is about **how we engage**—not about suspending moral understanding entirely.

---

### 2. **Judgment as *Condemnation* (the appetite to condemn)**
This is judgment with a **disposition of superiority**, finality, and often hidden self-righteousness.  
Characteristics:
- Focuses on **punishing**, not restoring.
- Seeks to **diminish** the other person’s worth.
- Often blind to one’s own faults (Matthew 7:3–5 “speck in your brother’s eye”).
- Carries an air of **final authority** over someone’s character or destiny.

This is the judgment Jesus warns against — not moral evaluation, but a **heart posture** that condemns without mercy, without humility, and without love.

---

### 3. **Discernment as *Moral Perception***
Discernment is necessary for ethical living.  
Characteristics:
- Seeks to **understand** rather than sentence.
- Recognizes actions as harmful or good **without attaching worth or identity** to the person.
- Usually paired with **humility** (“I could be wrong,” “I have my own flaws”).
- Aims at **repair**, not retribution.

You can discern an action is wrong without internally condemning the person’s soul. For example: “What they did was hurtful and dishonest” vs. “They are a worthless, irredeemable person.”

---

### 4. **The difference in practice**
- **Condemning spirit**: “You lied — you’re a liar and I can’t trust you ever again. You deserve whatever bad comes your way.”  
- **Discerning with grace**: “What you said wasn’t true, and that breaks trust. I need honesty for us to have a healthy relationship. I’m willing to talk about what led to this.”

The first sentences the person. The second addresses the action and opens a path toward forgiveness and reconciliation.

---

### 5. **Why forgiveness is tied to this**
“Forgive, and you will be forgiven” completes the thought.  
The refusal to condemn leaves room for **mercy and restoration**.  
Moral discernment *with* forgiveness means:  
1. Naming wrong clearly  
2. Releasing the demand for ultimate punishment  
3. Making space for change

---

### In summary:
Luke 6:37 isn’t saying “never make moral distinctions.”  
It is saying:  
**Do not wield moral truth as a weapon of contempt.  
Discern clearly, but do not play God over someone’s worth.  
Leave room for mercy, because you too will need mercy.**

It’s the difference between a **surgeon’s scalpel** (used with care to heal) and a **hammer** (used to smash).  
Moral truth should be handled with the precision of love, not the appetite for judgment.
