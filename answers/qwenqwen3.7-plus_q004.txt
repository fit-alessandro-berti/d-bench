The trust demanded in Proverbs 3:5–6 is not a passive, blind resignation, but an **active, wholehearted, and relational reliance on God** that reorients how a person uses their mind, makes decisions, and navigates life. 

To understand this, we must carefully distinguish between the biblical concept of "humble self-distrust" and the modern (or historical) pitfall of "anti-intellectualism."

---

### Part 1: The Nature of the Trust Demanded

The passage outlines a specific posture of the heart and mind:

1. **Wholehearted Reliance ("with all thine heart"):** This trust is total and undivided. It rejects compartmentalization, where one might trust God in spiritual matters but rely entirely on secular pragmatism in business, relationships, or politics. 
2. **Relational, Not Just Propositional:** The command is to trust *in the Lord*, not merely in a theological system or a set of rules. It is a reliance on the character, wisdom, and sovereignty of a Person.
3. **Active Acknowledgment ("in all thy ways acknowledge him"):** This is not a passive "let go and let God" mentality. To "acknowledge" (Hebrew: *yada*, meaning to know intimately) implies actively consulting God, seeking His will through prayer, Scripture, and godly counsel in every decision, big or small.
4. **Expectant Surrender ("he shall direct thy paths"):** The trust is coupled with the promise that God will straighten or guide one’s life trajectory. The surrender of control is the very mechanism that allows for divine guidance.

---

### Part 2: Humble Self-Distrust vs. Anti-Intellectualism

The command to "lean not unto thine own understanding" is frequently misunderstood. It does not mean "do not think." Rather, it is a call to **epistemic humility**. Here is how humble self-distrust differs fundamentally from mere anti-intellectualism:

#### 1. The Role of the Intellect
* **Humble Self-Distrust:** Recognizes that human reason is a God-given gift, but it is finite, fallen, and heavily biased by self-interest, emotion, and limited perspective (Jeremiah 17:9). Therefore, the intellect must be *subordinated* to divine revelation, not abolished. 
* **Anti-Intellectualism:** Views reason, education, and critical thought as inherently dangerous or opposed to faith. It often promotes a "blind leap" mentality, treating ignorance or a refusal to study as a badge of spiritual purity.

#### 2. The Metaphor of "Leaning"
* **Humble Self-Distrust:** Understands the difference between *using* your understanding and *leaning* on it. You are meant to use your mind to study, weigh evidence, and make plans. However, you do not "lean" on it—meaning you do not make your own limited perspective the ultimate foundation or support structure for your life. When your understanding conflicts with God’s revealed character, you yield.
* **Anti-Intellectualism:** Abandons the intellect altogether. It replaces rational thought with mere emotionalism, gut feelings, or uncritical conformity to a religious authority figure, ironically making the individual highly susceptible to manipulation.

#### 3. The Context of the Book of Proverbs
* **Humble Self-Distrust:** The book of Proverbs is literally a manual for acquiring *wisdom, knowledge, and discretion*. Solomon, the traditional author, spent his life gathering proverbs and studying nature (1 Kings 4:32-33). He is not telling the reader to stop thinking; he is telling the reader that true thinking *begins* with the fear of the Lord (Proverbs 1:7). You cannot read a book dedicated to gaining "understanding" and conclude the author hates understanding.
* **Anti-Intellectualism:** Would contradict the entire premise of Wisdom Literature. It would suggest that studying, pondering, and seeking instruction are unnecessary because "God will just tell you what to do."

#### 4. The Motivation
* **Humble Self-Distrust:** Is motivated by awe. The believer recognizes that God’s wisdom is infinitely vaster than human calculation. Yielding one’s understanding is an act of profound trust in a superior mind.
* **Anti-Intellectualism:** Is often motivated by intellectual laziness or pride. It is easier to refuse to wrestle with complex theological, moral, or practical questions than it is to diligently seek God’s wisdom within them.

---

### Summary

The trust asked for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is the trust of a navigator who has a functioning compass and map (human understanding) but recognizes that the terrain is filled with hidden cliffs and shifting sands that the map cannot show. 

**Humble self-distrust** says: *"I will use my mind to the best of my ability, but I recognize my blind spots. Therefore, I will actively seek God’s perspective above my own, submitting my conclusions to His wisdom."*

**Anti-intellectualism** says: *"Thinking is a lack of faith. I will close my eyes, ignore the facts, and hope for the best."*

The former leads to the promised "directed paths" of mature, integrated wisdom. The latter leads to confusion, error, and stagnation.
