## Analysis of the Elder Brother's Deepest Problem

The elder brother's response to his father's joyful welcome of the prodigal son reveals several underlying issues. To determine his deepest problem, let's examine each of the possibilities:

### Anger

The elder brother is indeed angry, as evidenced by his refusal to go into the celebration (Luke 15:28). However, anger is likely a surface-level emotion, a symptom of a deeper issue.

### Envy

The elder brother's complaint about not receiving a kid to celebrate with his friends, while his father's son receives a fatted calf, suggests envy (Luke 15:29-30). He seems to resent the attention and generosity shown to his brother.

### Wounded Fairness

The elder brother's sense of fairness is hurt, as he feels he has been overlooked and underappreciated. He points out that he has served his father faithfully for many years, obeying his commandments, and yet has not received similar recognition or rewards (Luke 15:29).

### Pride in Obedience

The elder brother takes pride in his obedience, boasting about his faithful service to his father (Luke 15:29). This pride might be a significant issue, as it could lead him to feel superior to his brother and entitled to more recognition.

### Discomfort with Mercy

The father's response to the elder brother highlights the true nature of the celebration: "This thy brother was dead, and is alive again; and was lost, and is found" (Luke 15:32). The elder brother's reluctance to accept his brother's return and the father's mercy towards him suggests that he may be uncomfortable with the idea of mercy itself.

## Conclusion

The elder brother's deepest problem appears to be **pride in obedience**, which leads to a sense of entitlement and a lack of understanding for his father's mercy. His pride causes him to focus on his own faithful service, rather than rejoicing in his brother's return. This pride also leads to envy and wounded fairness, but it is the root issue.

The father's words, "Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine" (Luke 15:31), aim to address this pride, reminding the elder brother that his relationship with his father is not based on earning rewards, but on being a son. The father's emphasis on the brother's return and the celebration that follows highlights the importance of mercy, forgiveness, and rejoicing in the restoration of a lost brother.
