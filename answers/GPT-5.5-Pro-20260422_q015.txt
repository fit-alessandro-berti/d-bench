The image is sharp because it is **comic, bodily, and morally exact** all at once.

A *mote* is tiny: a speck, splinter, bit of dust. A *beam* is huge: a plank or roof timber. Jesus imagines someone with a whole timber sticking out of his own eye trying to perform delicate eye surgery on someone else. The absurdity exposes the moral absurdity: the hypocrite is not merely imperfect; he is **blind to the very thing that makes him unfit to judge clearly**.

The image works especially well because the **eye** is both the place of the problem and the symbol of perception. The hypocrite’s fault is not only that he has a larger sin. It is that his larger sin has damaged his sight. He cannot “see clearly.” His judgment is distorted by pride, resentment, self-protection, or double standards.

But the passage does **not** forbid moral correction altogether. The key words are:

> “**first** cast out the beam out of thine own eye; **and then** shalt thou see clearly…”

That is not “never correct your brother.” It is “do not correct your brother while refusing correction yourself.”

So the criticism is aimed at **hypocritical correction**, not correction as such. Jesus condemns the person who notices small faults in others while ignoring greater faults in himself. But he still imagines a proper form of help: after removing the beam, one may help remove the mote.

The order matters:

First: self-examination.
Then: repentance or correction of one’s own fault.
Then: clearer vision.
Then: humble help for the other person.

That changes the whole character of moral correction. Without self-judgment, correction becomes accusation, superiority, or performance. After self-judgment, it can become an act of care.

The passage therefore rejects two errors at once. It rejects **self-righteous judgment**, where I condemn another while excusing myself. But it also rejects **moral indifference**, because the mote still matters and may still need to be removed.

Its ethic is not “Who am I to say anything?”
It is: “Am I seeing clearly enough, humbly enough, and honestly enough to help?”
