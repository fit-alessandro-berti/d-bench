John makes the love of the visible neighbor the test of love for the invisible God because biblical “love” is not primarily a feeling but an enacted, other-directed obedience. The neighbor is the concrete, God-given place where that love must appear. If it does not appear there, the claim to love God is exposed as words without the reality.

Key reasons in 1 John’s logic:
- God’s nature and gift: “God is love” and has loved us in the visible, concrete way of the incarnation and the cross (4:9–10). If we share his life, that love will flow outward in the same kind of concrete acts (3:16–18; 4:7–12).
- God’s command links the two loves: “He who loves God must also love his brother” (4:21; cf. John 13:34–35; 14:15). You cannot keep the command of the invisible God while breaking the inseparable command tied to it.
- Family logic: “Everyone who loves the Father loves whoever has been born of him” (5:1). To love God while despising his children is a contradiction; love for the Begetter entails love for the begotten.
- Image and representation: Your brother bears God’s image (Gen 1:27) and Christ is mystically identified with his people (Matt 25:40,45). To hate or neglect them is to affront the One whose image and presence they bear.
- Epistemic/ethical test: The unseen is easy to idealize. The seen exposes what is actually in us. Real love shows itself where there are costs, frictions, needs, and inconveniences (3:17). If it doesn’t appear there, it is not present toward God either.

What self-deception is being exposed?
- Confusing profession with possession: Saying “I love God” while walking in darkness (cf. 1:6; 2:4). Piety of the lips without the practice of love is a lie.
- Substituting sentiment or doctrine for obedience: Mistaking warm religious feelings, correct beliefs, or mystical talk for the concrete obedience of serving, forgiving, and giving.
- Loving an imagined God: Because God is unseen, we can project an image of God who asks nothing hard of us. John insists the true God is known by the love he produces.
- Compartmentalization: Pretending one can be right with God while wronging or withholding good from people, especially fellow believers (3:10–15,17).
- Spiritual elitism: In the letter’s context, secessionists claimed superior “knowledge of God” yet abandoned and slandered the community; John calls that claim false because it lacks brotherly love.

In short, love for God necessarily takes visible form as love for people. Where that form is absent, the invisible “love of God” is self-deception.
