Romans 12:20–21 defines victory over evil not as defeating, humiliating, or retaliating against the evildoer, but as refusing to let their evil reproduce itself in you.

Paul’s argument in the surrounding verses is:

- Do not repay evil for evil.
- Do not take personal vengeance; leave judgment to God.
- Meet an enemy’s genuine need with practical good.
- In this way, evil does not dictate your character or actions.

So the “victory” is moral and spiritual: the enemy may have acted wickedly, but they have not succeeded in making you become wicked in return.

## “Heap coals of fire on his head”

Paul is quoting Proverbs 25:21–22. The phrase has been understood in two main ways:

1. **Shame leading to repentance.**  
   Unexpected kindness toward an enemy can expose the ugliness of their hostility. It may awaken conscience, produce remorse, and lead them to change. In this sense, the “burning coals” are the painful but potentially healing experience of shame.

2. **God’s judgment, not private revenge.**  
   If the person refuses kindness and persists in evil, the matter is left with God. The believer does not have to avenge himself; God sees and judges rightly.

Either way, the command is not, “Be nice so that you can secretly hurt them.” That would contradict verse 21. If kindness is performed as a weapon of concealed revenge, then evil is still controlling the heart. The point is genuine good, while entrusting the outcome—and justice—to God.

## Is it weak?

No. It is stronger than retaliation, though it can look passive from the outside.

Retaliation is often easy: it mirrors the injury received. It lets the offender determine your response. But feeding an enemy requires self-command, courage, and freedom from the need to “get even.” It says: “Your wrongdoing will not decide who I become.”

This does not mean:

- pretending evil is not evil,
- refusing to seek justice,
- remaining in an abusive situation,
- eliminating consequences or proper boundaries,
- trusting an untrustworthy person without discernment.

Romans 12 itself recognizes that wrongdoing is real and that judgment belongs to God. Elsewhere Scripture also affirms public justice and protection of the vulnerable. The command concerns personal vengeance and personal conduct: do not answer evil with evil.

## Is it manipulative?

It can be misused manipulatively if someone gives “kindness” merely to induce guilt or gain control. But that is not Paul’s intended ethic. Christian love seeks the other person’s good, including, if possible, their repentance and reconciliation. It does not use generosity as a disguised form of revenge.

## The central idea

“Be not overcome of evil” means: do not let evil conquer your heart, shape your actions, or turn you into its image.

“Overcome evil with good” means: answer evil with the kind of action that reflects God’s character—truthful, just, merciful, and free from vengeance.

That is not sentimental weakness. It is the strength of someone who can resist both hatred and fear, pursue good without denying justice, and refuse to let an enemy have the final say over their soul.
