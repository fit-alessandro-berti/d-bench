Galatians 6:1 assumes a *healing* kind of correction carried out by people whose own character is being governed by the Spirit (cf. Gal 5:16–25). The moral posture is therefore not “moral superiority,” but **Spirit-formed humility aimed at repair**.

### The required posture
1. **Restorative purpose (not punitive)**
   - “Restore” (Greek *katartizετε*) is used for *mending* (e.g., nets) or *setting* a broken bone—putting something back into proper condition.
   - The correction is meant to recover a person, not to win a case, vent irritation, or mark someone as “the problem.”

2. **Meek method (not harshness)**
   - “Meekness” (*prautēs*) is strength under control: truth spoken without domination, humiliation, or unnecessary severity.
   - It treats the sinner as a brother to be regained, not an enemy to be crushed.

3. **Self-suspicion (not self-confidence)**
   - “Considering thyself” means watching yourself with realism: you are not exempt from the same pressures and deceptions.
   - This blocks the common temptation in correction: pride, contempt, and the “I would never…” mindset (cf. 1 Cor 10:12).

### Why keep restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion together?
They belong together because each guards the others from becoming distorted:

- **Restoration without meekness** becomes corrective violence—technically “fixing” someone while breaking them further.
- **Meekness without restoration** can become sentimental permissiveness—softness that avoids the hard work of actually helping someone change.
- **Restoration + meekness without self-suspicion** easily turns into moral posturing—helping from above rather than alongside, which invites hypocrisy and tempts the corrector (to pride, anger, or even the same sin).

Paul binds them together to enforce a single ethic: **the spiritually mature correct as fellow-weak sinners who want healing, and who know they too can fall.** This is why the passage sounds like Matthew 7: “take the log out of your own eye” before removing the speck—clear sight and a gentle hand are part of the same moral posture.
