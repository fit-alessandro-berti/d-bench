The text in Matthew 6:19-21 does not explicitly indicate that treasure trains the heart, but it reflects a reciprocal relationship where one's possessions and where they focus their treasure often reveals and shapes their heart'an inclinations and values.[2] There is wisdom in James' adage: "The eye cannot say to the hand, 'I have no need for you.'"[3] The act of possessing, attending to, and loving our treasures can become intertwined, as it appears that our hearts (our wills and desires) are inclined to seek after the possessions they prize. However, this passage is not merely about physical wealth but spiritual treasure.

To possess or cherish, in the biblical sense, is also to believe something valuable in faith.[4] Therefore, if I say I am faithful (posess), the Bible indicates I hold something dear. Contrariwise, if I declare I am an unbeliever, I can be considered an unbeliever (doubting, not holding firmly to my faith as something valuable). This notion of faith's importance is essential for understanding the spiritual treasure Jesus refers to, which can be the most prized possession in one's life.[5] To have this form of wealth and to lay it up for oneself involves nurturing and safeguarding it. This wealth is not material wealth but spiritual treasure – the relationship with Christ – which is eternal and uncorruptible. In Matthew 6:16-18 Jesus admonishes us not to worry or be anxious about material wealth, which will last only a short time, but rather to trust in God for daily needs, which He provides.[6]

So, laying up our treasures or valuables on earth symbolizes our attachment to temporary, physical wealth. And in focusing our love and attention on things that will decay, we fail to recognize the enduring, renewable relationship we have with Christ through grace.[7] When Christ says, "Where your treasure is, there will your heart be," it is a divine indication of our heart's inclinations being focused elsewhere instead of solely on Him. When we accumulate physical wealth, our hearts may find contentment in the material rather than valuing eternal, spiritual riches available through a renewed life with Christ.

This passage does not command we should not possess, as such a command would be detrimental to our spiritual health and well-being.[8] As the psalmist's prayer, Matthew 6:19-21 connotes a metaphor that teaches us to seek our treasures in heaven, beyond what money can offer, in God Himself.[9] Though we may possess, we should remember that Christ is our provider, and it's only in putting our faith in Him that we'll find peace and security. Ultimately, our heart is at the heart of this matter – our spiritual treasure – and Jesus guides us to focus on Him as our true treasure to avoid basing our hearts on material wealth and thereby having a heart that is inwardly focused.[10]

We have a profound desire for material, or spiritual wealth, and as we find contentment, we realize what we really desire and cherish.[11] These treasures are priceless and infinite.[12] The only treasure we are commanded to lay up is one of eternal value: Christ Himself. By turning our focus solely on self, including our possessions and wealth, we forget that Christ is our treasure. When we seek after material treasures, we are not valuing Him and the spiritual wealth He offers. We become like material items that corrupt over time; we become enslaved and ultimately corrupted.[13]

To seek after the temporary treasures available on earth is to show a lack of value for these eternal treasures – the unchanging Christ – available in heaven.[14] Therefore, Matthew 6:19-21 instructs us to seek Him as our treasure, to value Him eternally and prioritize a relationship with Christ. When our hearts become heavenly, they are no longer focused primarily on ourselves or our fleeting, material world.[15] It's when we seek something outside of Christ, outside ourselves, then Jesus says that our hearts will be elsewhere and that our "treasures" are corruptible, prone to fear – as all worldly things are – and ultimately unable to satisfy us. Our spiritual treasures are unshakable and immortal.

In seeking Christ, we are promised peace and eternal security, which allows our hearts to be free and whole. Our hearts will know Christ's love as we align our hearts with His desires and eternal values rather than temporary, mundane ones.[16] As our hearts align with those values, we are no longer coveting what others have or seeking temporary pleasures that can ultimately satisfy us.[17] As we seek Christ, we will find His treasure within us. Our hearts, attentive to His desires, will find peace, joy, and eternal life. As our hearts align with the enduring reality of who Christ is, we will desire to store up treasures – the relationship – in heaven, and our hearts will become focused on Him alone. These treasures will not be corrupted as material can be. The treasures we seek in Christ are only corruptible through sin—it is only our sins that have the corrupting potential to pervert our hearts.[18] Our hearts can corrupt if we place our emphasis on self and worldly pursuits.

Jesus warns us against storing temporal treasures on earth because of the transiency of these things. They can become a focus of our hearts, corrupting us and leading to a reliance on sin (love for the wrong things). The treasures Jesus refers to are not our earthly possessions; they are the relationship with Christ.[19] Laying up our temporal treasures will not lead us into a deep, spiritual, intimate relationship with Christ. Instead, those treasures will corrupt our hearts and our desires for Christ, causing our hearts to be elsewhere. By our misplaced focus on worldly possessions, we fail to lay hold of Christ as our treasure.

In contrast, by putting our hearts in Christ, we can find eternal security. The relationship with God brings our hearts heavenward, a heart aligned with heavenly, enduring things. This heart, focused on eternal treasures, aligns itself with Christ's eternal, enduring treasure.

It's when we seek after temporal (earthly) wealth, whether it's possessions or personal advancements, that we fail to consider the eternal, immortal wealth Christ offers through eternal life. Our hearts will become like physical treasures that corrupt over time, because our eternal treasures are with Christ.[20] The focus on Christ as our only treasure brings our hearts to heaven. Our hearts become heavenly because they are aligned with heavenly things. Our eternal treasures are only corruptible through our sins – it is only our sins that have the potential (capability, not actuality) to tarnish our hearts.

Jesus invites us to align our heart with the heavenly reality He offers. When we seek God, our hearts will find His treasure in us. This God-centered heart brings us heaven and a relationship with Christ.[21] This godly desires for earthly things (including wealth, status, and possessions) are insufficient to fulfill us. Although we can attain such things, they will erode our heart's contentment if our hearts seek after them primarily. However, our hearts become heavenly after we seek Christ as our only true treasure.[22] As described in Ecclesiastes 12:13-14, it is an alignment with God that grants our hearts to be heavenly as our desires are heavenly.[23]

We possess and value material wealth on earth. It is when we place our contentment in this wealth that we find our hearts elsewhere by allowing our hearts to seek after temporal pleasures, leading to brokenness and fear. Our hearts will corrupt if we become attached and possessive of unchanging, temporal things.[24] When we find contentment in a God-centered life, our hearts are heavenly because they are aligned with heavenly, unchanging realities.

By contrast, by seeking Christ as our only treasure, our hearts and desires are heavenly as they align themselves with heavenly things. Our hearts find eternal treasures in Christ, securing us from fear.[25] We find peace in God's word and assurance in Christ, which aligns our hearts with heavenly reality. The earthly treasures we accumulate can not satisfy, enslave, or protect us from fear. Even as we desire worldly goods, it is only God from whom true contentment flows and which is the ultimate treasure.[26] If our hearts are heavenly, our hearts are not primarily focused on the material things they hold but on the eternal joy Christ provides.[27]

As we align our hearts with Christ, they also become heavenly.[28] Our hearts, in tune with heavenly things, are heavenly, and they are no longer earthly – not concerned with worldly pursuits and fear. When our hearts are heavenly, they focus on earthly pleasures as a mere enjoyment of temporal things and not as a necessity of fulfillment.

Therefore, when we have our hearts aligned with the heavenly reality Christ offers, we will desire only earthly treasures as enjoyments, not essentials that are found in a relationship with Christ – His love, assurance, and salvation.[29] We find these earthly treasures as enjoyments, not as an essential necessity. When our hearts concentrate on earthly delights, these cannot fulfill us; we have no assurance in Christ, only uncertainty and reliance on self. The eternal, heavenly treasures that Christ offers bring our hearts heavenly through Christ's eternal, unchanging love we find in Him and in Him alone.

Aligning our hearts with Christ brings eternal, uncorruptible joy that we cannot find in temporal things.[30] Christ's love is eternal and everlasting, offering a heavenly heart to us and us alone. Aligning our hearts with Christ brings us to heavenly joy, making them heavenly as we desire nothing beyond Him.[31] When we seek only worldly treasures, our hearts become like the physical things that break down and corrupt – the moths and rust they mentioned.

Our hearts are heavenly when we desire things only for their temporal enjoyment because, as things that break down and corrupt, they cannot give us the contentment that Christ alone can provide. When we find joy only through Christ, our hearts become heavenly because Christ alone can fulfill us.[32] When we desire God for His joy, not as a means to an end, our hearts become heavenward, which brings us heaven (our heavenward alignment with heavenly things and values).
