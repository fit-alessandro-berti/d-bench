This passage identifies something uncomfortable but psychologically precise: exposure is often more feared than wrongdoing itself.

**Light as diagnostic, not punitive**

The text doesn't say light condemns—it says light *reveals*, and that revelation itself functions as judgment. Deeds "should be reproved" (elenchō—exposed, refuted, shown for what they are). Light doesn't do anything to the deeds; it just makes them visible. The condemnation is self-authored: it's the deeds that are evil, and light merely removes the cover.

This matters because it locates the problem in the deeds, not in the seeing. Hatred of light is really hatred of the accurate self-perception light forces.

**Why exposure feels worse than the act**

There's a strange asymmetry in human psychology: people can do things in private with relative equanimity, but the same acts under observation produce disproportionate shame. Why?

- *Privacy allows self-narrative control.* In darkness, one can maintain a curated story about oneself—rationalizations, exceptions, "it's complicated." Light collapses narrative distance between the self and the act. You can't explain your way out of what's simply *seen*.
- *Exposure implies relationship.* Being seen means someone else's judgment now matters. Darkness isn't just concealment—it's isolation, and isolation is safe because it has no audience to answer to.
- *Shame anticipates rejection.* The fear isn't just "being known" abstractly, but being known *and then abandoned or condemned by the knower*. So hatred of light is really distrust—an assumption that exposure leads to rejection rather than mercy.

**The asymmetry between the two responses**

Verse 20-21 draws a clean contrast:
- Evildoers avoid light *lest* deeds be exposed — motivated by self-protection, fear of consequence.
- Truth-doers come *to* the light *that* deeds may be manifest — motivated by a desire for confirmation, even collaboration ("wrought in God").

The second group isn't sinless; they're transparent. Their orientation to being seen is different—not fear of ruin but willingness to have their work validated or corrected. This suggests the deciding factor isn't perfection but posture: whether one is oriented toward concealment or disclosure.

**The theological turn**

If light exposes without necessarily condemning—if the movement toward light could result in one's deeds being confirmed as "wrought in God"—then what's actually hated isn't judgment per se, but *vulnerability*. The refusal of light is a vote against being fully known, which is also, structurally, a refusal of the kind of relationship where being known could lead to love rather than punishment.

So darkness isn't chosen because people prefer evil in the abstract—it's chosen because visibility threatens the fragile self one has built in hiding. The tragedy the text points to is that this very refusal *is* the condemnation: not an external sentence, but the self-perpetuating choice to remain unseen, and therefore unreached.
