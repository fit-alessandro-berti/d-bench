Your question touches something real about why moral effort so often fails to change the deeper person.

**External compliance vs. a new heart**

External compliance is behavior that conforms to a standard while the inner disposition remains unchanged or even opposed. You can keep a rule while resenting it, obey while wishing you didn't have to, or do the right thing for reasons that have nothing to do with valuing the thing itself. The behavior is correct; the person is divided.

A "new heart" in this passage points to something prior to behavior—the affections, the orientation, what you actually want. Notice the sequence in verse 27: the spirit is put within, and *then* "cause you to walk in my statutes." The walking is downstream of the inner change. This is the opposite of the usual moralistic assumption that right behavior eventually produces right desire. Here, the desire is given first, and conduct follows from it.

This matters because compliance without inner change tends to be brittle and exhausting. It runs on willpower against the grain of what you want, which is why it collapses under pressure or breeds hypocrisy.

**Why hardness is described as a material condition**

I think the stone imagery is doing precise work, not just being vivid. A few things it captures:

1. **Unresponsiveness.** Stone doesn't react. It can't be moved by appeal, warmth, or grief. The text is describing a condition where the self cannot be reached—where instruction bounces off. A heart of flesh, by contrast, is sensitive, capable of being affected.

2. **It's a condition, not merely a choice.** This is the heart of your instinct. The passage doesn't say "decide to be softer." It treats hardness as a *state of the material itself*, something that has to be removed and replaced from outside. This is honest about a real human experience: people often cannot feel what they know they should feel, cannot want what they wish they wanted. You can't argue stone into being flesh. The problem is below the level where exhortation operates.

3. **The need for replacement rather than repair.** You don't soften stone; you take it out. The language suggests the old condition isn't a flaw to be polished but something fundamentally unsuited to the purpose—it must be exchanged for a different kind of thing entirely.

**The underlying claim**

What the passage asserts is that the deepest level of a person—the wanting, responsive core—is not something we can reach by our own effort, precisely because the faculty that would have to do the reaching is itself the thing that's hardened. Hence the repeated "I will": the change is described as something received, not achieved.

Whether or not you take the theological frame, the psychological observation is durable: there's a difference between modifying behavior and being changed at the level of what you love, and the second is much harder to engineer in yourself. That difficulty is exactly what the stone-and-flesh image is trying to name.

A fair question to press: if the change is entirely given, what is the role of human effort at all? The text holds both—divine action *producing* genuine human walking and keeping. It doesn't resolve into pure passivity. That tension is worth sitting with rather than flattening.
