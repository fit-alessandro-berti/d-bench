Because the passage is describing **love by what it refuses to be** as much as by what it is.

### Why so many negations?
Paul’s list clears away the common counterfeits of love:

- **Pride**: love does not put itself at the center.
- **Envy**: love is not threatened by another’s good.
- **Irritability**: love is not ruled by wounded ego.
- **Suspicion**: love does not assume the worst.
- **Self-seeking**: love is not a strategy for advantage.

These are all forms of **self-protection and self-assertion**. Paul is showing that real love is not merely affection or sentiment; it is a way of relating that is free from the need to dominate, compare, defend, or grasp.

### What vision of strength is being proposed?
The strength here is not the strength of control, reputation, or emotional hardness. It is the strength of a self so secure that it can:

- **absorb injury without retaliation**,
- **rejoice in another’s good without resentment**,
- **remain patient under pressure**,
- **hope when proof is lacking**,
- **endure without becoming cynical**.

This is a **cruciform strength**—strength shaped by the cross. It looks weak by worldly standards because it does not fight for status, but it is actually the deepest kind of power: the power to give oneself away without losing oneself.

So the passage proposes that true love is not fragile passivity. It is **moral and spiritual stamina**: a steadfast, generous, resilient commitment to the good of the other.
