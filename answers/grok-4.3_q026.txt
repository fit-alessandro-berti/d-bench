This passage presents speech as an overflow from the heart’s moral reserves, using the imagery of a “treasure” (Greek *thesauros*)—a storehouse or repository. Jesus argues that what a person has accumulated inwardly (thoughts, desires, character, and values) inevitably surfaces in what they say. The heart is not a neutral container; it is either good or evil, and speech functions as its natural product.

### Connection to “Hidden Moral Stockpiles”

The metaphor is agricultural and economic at once. Just as a person draws from a physical treasury what they have previously stored, the mouth draws from the heart what has been previously deposited there. The word “abundance” (*perisseuma*) suggests overflow rather than careful selection. Speech is therefore not manufactured on the spot in isolation; it is the surplus of an inner accumulation. This is why Jesus says it is impossible for an evil person to “speak good things” in any sustained or authentic way—the reservoir simply does not contain the resources.

### Symptoms, Revelations, or Choices?

The text most strongly presents speech as both a **revelation** and a **symptom**, while treating it less directly as a free-standing choice.

- **Revelations**: This is the dominant emphasis. Words disclose what would otherwise remain hidden. The Pharisees could perform religious speech, but their accusations against Jesus (calling His work demonic) exposed the actual condition of their hearts. Jesus’ point is diagnostic: listen long enough and the mouth will show you the treasury’s contents. The heart cannot be fully concealed because speech is its overflow.

- **Symptoms**: Closely related, the passage treats speech as evidence of an underlying moral state, much like a fever indicates infection rather than causing it. An evil heart does not merely *permit* evil speech; it *produces* it. The impossibility of an evil person consistently speaking good things functions like a law of moral cause and effect.

- **Choices**: The text does not deny that speaking involves volition. People do choose their words. However, Jesus is arguing that the range of choices available is constrained by what the heart has stored. An evil treasury does not make good speech impossible in every single instance (a wicked person can occasionally say something true or kind), but it makes *good speech as the natural, consistent outflow* impossible. The choice is real, but it is exercised within the limits of the heart’s accumulated character. Later New Testament writers (e.g., James 3) develop this further by noting that the tongue is hard to tame precisely because it so readily expresses the heart’s disorder.

### Summary of the Logic

The passage teaches that speech is downstream from character. It functions as a reliable (though not infallible) indicator of what has been stored in the heart over time. Attempts to produce good words from an evil heart are ultimately unsustainable because the source remains corrupt. This is why Jesus moves immediately from this statement to the warning about the “evil treasure” bringing forth evil things—He is not offering a technique for better speech but a diagnosis of why superficially good speech from corrupt people eventually fails.
