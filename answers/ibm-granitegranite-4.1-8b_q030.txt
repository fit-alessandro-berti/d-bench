The passage from 1 Corinthians 13:4–7 presents a remarkably detailed and comprehensive portrait of love (charity in Greek, *agape*), emphasizing qualities that are often contrary to human instincts or cultural norms. The inclusion of so many negations—against pride, envy, irritability, suspicion, and self-centeredness—serves to outline a vision of strength that is profoundly counterintuitive in many worldly contexts. Here’s why these negations are crucial and what kind of strength they propose:

### 1. **Contrast with Worldly Virtues**
In a world that frequently valorizes assertiveness, self-promotion, and the pursuit of one’s own interests, the list of negations creates a stark contrast. Love, as described, does **not** seek its own glory (envy, vaunting, being puffed up), but rather **suffers long** and is **kind**—qualities that often appear weak or passive by worldly standards. This juxtaposition underscores that true strength lies not in dominating or asserting one's superiority but in self-effacing generosity.

### 2. **Emotional and Spiritual Resilience**
The traits listed—being slow to anger (“is not easily provoked”), not harboring suspicion (“thinketh no evil”), not rejoicing in wrongdoing but in truth, and bearing, believing, hoping, and enduring all things—highlight emotional and spiritual resilience. This resilience is not about emotional numbness or suppressing feelings but about **profound patience**, **deep trust**, **optimistic hope**, and **unwavering endurance**. Such qualities enable love to navigate the most challenging circumstances without compromising its essence.

### 3. **Interdependence and Unity**
By negating self-seeking behavior (“seeketh not her own”) and emphasizing the desire for truth over personal advantage, the passage promotes a vision of love that is **interdependent** and **unifying**. Love, in this sense, is not a solitary or egoistic endeavor but a force that **forges connections**, fosters community, and aligns individuals with a higher truth or purpose.

### 4. **Inner Strength Over External Power**
The proposed strength is **inner**, rooted in virtues that may appear vulnerable or passive to the untrained eye. It is a strength that **does not rely on external validation or dominance** but is **self-sustaining** and **authentically powerful** because it is grounded in **reverence for others** and **adherence to universal principles of goodness**. This inner strength is demonstrated in the ability to **bear all things**, which implies the capacity to absorb and endure hardships without being corrupted or diminished.

### 5. **A Vision of Love as the Greatest Commandment**
In Christian theology, this passage often serves to exemplify the greatest commandment—to love God and love one’s neighbor as oneself. By highlighting these negations, the text suggests that love is the ultimate expression of faithfulness to God, surpassing even the strict observance of laws or rituals. The strength of love, therefore, is its **transformative power**: it can change hearts, reconcile divisions, and inspire profound acts of service and sacrifice.

### Conclusion
The negations in Corinthians 13:4–7 are not mere qualifications of love but are **essential components** that define its **authentic strength**. This strength is **contrary to the worldly notions of power**—it is gentle, patient, selfless, and steadfast. It proposes a vision where true power lies not in what one can take or control but in what one can give, endure, and embody without compromise. In this way, love becomes a living testament to the highest and most resilient form of strength. 

In summary, the portrait includes these negations to illustrate that love’s greatest power is its ability to **overcome selfishness, jealousy, and resentment**, thereby creating a foundation for genuine strength, unity, and transformative grace.
