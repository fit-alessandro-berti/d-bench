In Matthew 6:1, the moral danger is **not visibility itself**, but **the desire to turn goodness into a performance**.

Jesus does not say, “Never let anyone see you do good.” In fact, earlier he says:

> “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven.” — Matthew 5:16

So the issue cannot be mere public visibility. The issue is the phrase:

> “to be seen of them”

That is, public goodness becomes dangerous when **being seen becomes the point**.

The danger is that a good act can be outwardly generous but inwardly self-serving. Almsgiving, prayer, fasting, charity, service, or moral courage can all be subtly redirected from love of God and neighbor toward the construction of a public image. The needy person becomes a stage. The good deed becomes a signal. The audience becomes the “god” whose approval one seeks.

That is why Jesus says:

> “otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven.”

The person has already chosen their reward: admiration, reputation, applause, moral status. In that case, the act may still help someone outwardly, but spiritually it has been hollowed out. It is no longer simple mercy before God; it is goodness bent back toward the self.

So the verse distinguishes between two kinds of public goodness:

**Goodness that is visible but God-directed:**
Others may see it, but the aim is love, obedience, mercy, and ultimately God’s glory.

**Goodness that is performed for visibility:**
Others are meant to see it so that the doer may be admired, praised, envied, or considered righteous.

The problem, then, is not being seen. The problem is **craving to be seen as good**.

A simple test might be: *Would I still do this if no one knew? Would I still rejoice if God alone saw it?* Matthew 6:1 warns that the soul is in danger when the answer becomes no.
