This verse from Matthew 6:1 highlights a crucial distinction between the motivation behind our actions and the actions themselves. The moral danger of public goodness, as described here, lies not in the mere fact of being seen, but in the **craving to be seen and the intention behind it.**

Let's break it down:

*   **"Take heed that ye do not your alms before men, to be seen of them..."** This is the core of the warning. The phrase "to be seen of them" directly points to the *purpose* behind performing the act of almsgiving in public. The danger arises when the primary or sole motivation is to gain human recognition, admiration, or approval.

*   **"...otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven."** This consequence clarifies the issue. If the intention is to be seen and praised by others, then the "reward" is already received in the form of human applause. In this scenario, there is no "further reward" to be gained from God. God's rewards, as implied here, are for actions done with pure motives.

**Therefore, the problem is not visibility itself, but the craving to be seen.**

Here's why:

*   **Visibility can be neutral or even beneficial:** It's not inherently wrong to do good deeds where others can see them. In fact, public acts of kindness and charity can inspire others to do the same, creating a ripple effect of goodness in society. For example, if someone openly donates to a cause, it might encourage others to contribute as well. This is not the danger Jesus is warning against.

*   **The Craving to be Seen is the Root of the Danger:** The danger lies in the internal disposition. When a person performs good deeds with the primary aim of receiving praise, commendation, or a reputation for being good from their fellow humans, they are essentially seeking their reward in the fleeting approval of others. This is seen as a form of self-serving hypocrisy, where the outward act is a performance designed to boost one's own ego or social standing.

**In essence, Jesus is advocating for sincerity and purity of heart.** The focus of good deeds should be on obedience to God and genuine compassion, not on impressing others. When our good deeds are performed with the intention of pleasing God, even if they are witnessed by others, the motive is pure, and the reward is found in God's eyes.

Think of it this way:

*   **Good deeds done publicly for God's glory and out of genuine love (even if seen):** This is acceptable and can have positive outward effects.
*   **Good deeds done publicly *solely* to be seen and applauded by people:** This is the dangerous act, as the reward is already collected from human opinion, leaving no room for divine favor.

So, the verse is a powerful call to examine our motivations and to ensure our good works are not a performance for the crowd, but a sincere expression of faith and love for God.
