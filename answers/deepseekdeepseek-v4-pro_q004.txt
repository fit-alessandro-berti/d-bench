The trust called for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is not a leap in the dark or a suppression of the intellect. It is a whole-hearted, relational reliance on God that deliberately subordinates one’s own finite and sin-tainted judgment to His perfect wisdom and fatherly care. The verse describes an active disposition of the heart, mind, and will: “trust” (*bāṭaḥ*, to feel secure, to place confident reliance), “lean not” (*’al–tishā‘ēn*, do not prop yourself up) on your own understanding, and “acknowledge him” (*dā‘ēhû*, know Him intimately, be conscious of His presence) in everything. The result is that God himself will make the path straight, providing His unerring guidance.

This kind of trust is a comprehensive surrender that treats God not merely as a theological truth but as a personal, present, and trustworthy guide in all of life. It touches every decision, every plan, every thought—acknowledging that human insight, if isolated from God, is an unreliable foundation. It is not the abandonment of thinking but the reorientation of thinking under the lordship of the One who is Wisdom itself.

### Humble self-distrust vs. anti-intellectualism

The distinction between the two is radical, though they are sometimes superficially confused.

**Humble self-distrust** (what the passage commands) says: “My understanding is finite, fallible, and easily distorted by pride, fear, and appetite. I dare not make it the final arbiter of truth, meaning, or direction. I need God’s revealed wisdom to illuminate and correct my thinking.”  
- It does *not* despise the mind—it consecrates the mind to God.  
- It recognizes that “the fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge” (Prov 1:7), meaning all true understanding starts with reverent submission to God.  
- It actively engages the intellect in dependence on God: wisdom cries out in the streets (Prov 1:20–23); the mind is to be renewed (Rom 12:2); believers are to love God with all their mind (Matt 22:37).  
- It treats reason as a servant, not the master; a tool, not the ultimate standard. It uses logic, observation, and learning while always submitting those to Scripture and the Spirit’s guidance in prayer.  
- An example: when facing a difficult choice, this person researches, counsels with the wise, and thinks hard—but then lays all that before the Lord in prayer, asking for his leading and being willing to have those plans overruled.

**Anti-intellectualism** says: “Understanding is worthless or dangerous. I will not think, question, or study; I will simply feel or blindly obey an inner impulse.”  
- It disparages the mind as an enemy of faith, treating ignorance as a virtue.  
- It often substitutes emotion, tradition, or mystical impression for genuine trust.  
- It ignores the biblical pattern: the same David who said “Thy word is a lamp unto my feet” meditated on God’s law day and night (Ps 119:97–105). The prophets reasoned with their hearers (Isa 1:18). Paul reasoned in the synagogues and quoted pagan poets (Acts 17). The book of Proverbs itself is a collection of carefully crafted, intellectually rigorous sayings designed to teach wisdom.  
- Anti-intellectualism fails to love God with the whole mind, reducing faith to sentimentalism or superstition.  
- Ironically, it often becomes another form of self-trust—trusting in one’s unchecked gut feelings or in isolated “spiritual” experiences instead of in God’s objective word and wise, Spirit-led reflection.

In short, Proverbs 3:5–6 promotes a robust, humble dependence that uses every faculty under God’s authority. The trust asked for is one that says, “Lord, I will think with all my might, but I know my might is not enough; I subject my conclusions to you. I know you, and I need you to straighten what my crooked reasoning would make crooked.” That is the opposite of anti-intellectualism—it is the very foundation of a truly Christian intellectual life.
