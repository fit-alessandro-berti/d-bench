Matthew 6:19–21 is a foundational text on spiritual formation, human desire, and stewardship. To answer your questions, we can look closely at both the grammar of verse 21 and the broader movement of Jesus’s teaching.

---

### 1. Does the text say treasure *reveals* the heart, or that treasure *trains* the heart?

While it functions as both, **the primary force of the text is that treasure *trains* (or directs) the heart.** 

Here is why:

* **The Direction of Cause and Effect:** Notice the specific order Jesus uses in verse 21: *"For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also."* 
  * If the text were purely **diagnostic** (revealing), it would say: *"Where your heart is, there your treasure will go."* That would mean your internal affections determine your external spending/storing.
  * Instead, Jesus states the reverse: **The heart *follows* the treasure.** The location of your treasure acts like a gravitational pull or a magnet. Where you place your "treasure" (your resources, time, energy, wealth), your heart is drawn behind it.
* **The Context of Imperatives:** Verse 19 and 20 begin with commands (imperatives): *"Lay not up..."* and *"But lay up..."* Jesus is commanding a physical, practical action. By consciously choosing *where* to store your resources, you are choosing *where* your heart will eventually land. 
* **The Secondary Diagnostic Value:** Secondarily, the passage *does* reveal the heart. If you want to know what you currently love, you can look at where your resources are concentrated. However, Jesus isn't just giving a personality test; he is giving a method for spiritual transformation. He is teaching that **action shapes affection.** 

In short: **Treasure is a rudder, not just a mirror.** It directs where the heart goes, even as it reflects where the heart has been.

---

### 2. How do possession, attention, and love become tied together here?

Jesus links these three human realities into a dynamic, inescapable loop:

#### A. Possession (What you hold)
Jesus divides "treasure" (possession) into two categories based on their durability:
* **Earthly Possession:** Characterized by vulnerability, decay, and loss (*"moth and rust," "thieves"*). 
* **Heavenly Possession:** Characterized by absolute security and imperishability.

The nature of what you possess determines the health of your spiritual life. If your primary possessions are earthly, they are inherently unstable.

#### B. Attention (Where your mind is consumed)
Possession dictates attention. 
* If your treasure is on earth, **your attention is bound to anxiety, preservation, and fear.** You must constantly guard against the "moths" of inflation/time, the "rust" of decay, and the "thieves" of loss. Earthly possessions demand hyper-vigilance.
* If your treasure is in heaven, **your attention is freed from the fear of loss.** Because heavenly treasure cannot be stolen or decayed, your mind rests in security. 

#### C. Love (Where your heart resides)
In Biblical thought, the "heart" is the center of love, devotion, desire, and the will. 
* What you **possess** commands your **attention**, and whatever commands your attention eventually captures your **love**. 
* If you possess earthly wealth, your attention is consumed by maintaining it, and ultimately your heart’s deepest trust, devotion, and love become anchored to the material world. You become spiritually aligned with things that are actively decaying.
* Conversely, when you invest your possessions in heavenly realities (e.g., generosity, serving God, loving others), your attention shifts toward God's kingdom, and your love (heart) becomes firmly anchored in eternity.

### Summary
In Matthew 6:19–21, Jesus shows that human beings cannot detach their spiritual devotions from their material decisions. **Possession captures Attention, Attention cultivates Love, and Love defines the Heart.** 

By commanding us to reallocate our *treasure*, Jesus is offering a way to rescue our *hearts*.
