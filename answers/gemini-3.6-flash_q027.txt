In Luke 18:9–14, Jesus constructs a stark contrast to illustrate the nature of justification before God. To answer both parts of your question, we must look at how the text links internal posture to outward expression.

---

### 1. What Exactly Condemns the Pharisee?

The Pharisee is not condemned by a single isolated flaw, but rather by **self-righteous pride**, of which comparison, contempt, virtue-as-display, and a functional false doctrine are the necessary expressions. 

Luke’s framing in verse 9 explicitly identifies the core offense: he *"trusted in [himself] that [he was] righteous, and despised others."*

* **The Root: Pride and Self-Trust (Self-Righteousness)**
  The text notes that the Pharisee "prayed thus **with himself**." His prayer was not a dialogue with God, but an exercise in self-congratulation. He feels no need for divine mercy, forgiveness, or grace because he believes his standing before God is earned through his own compliance with the law.

* **The Fruit: Comparison and Contempt**
  Pride cannot exist in a vacuum; it requires comparison to validate itself. The Pharisee measures his righteousness not against the absolute holiness of God, but against the moral failings of others (*"extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even as this publican"*). By lowering the standard of righteousness to "being better than the tax collector," he breeds contempt for others to maintain his own illusion of superiority.

* **The Instrument: Virtue as Self-Display**
  The Pharisee turns genuine spiritual disciplines—fasting and tithing—into currency for merit and tools for self-aggrandizement (*"I fast twice in the week, I give tithes of all that I possess"*). His virtues are condemned not because fasting or tithing are wrong, but because he uses them to obligate God and elevate himself over his neighbor.

* **The Underlying False Doctrine**
  While orthodox in his formal theology, the Pharisee harbors a fatal *functional* heresy: the belief that man can make himself righteous before God through personal merit. He treats God as a debtor who owes him reward, entirely missing the truth that all human righteousness is deeply flawed before a holy God.

**In summary:** The Pharisee is condemned because **his pride blinds him to his own spiritual bankruptcy.** He asks God for nothing, receives nothing, and leaves unjustified because he believes he has no need for grace.

---

### 2. Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture Treated as Spiritually Truer?

The tax collector’s posture is "spiritually truer" because **it accurately reflects reality**—the true relationship between a holy God and a sinful human being.

* **Objective Realism (Truth about God and Self)**
  The tax collector does not engage in self-delusion or comparison. He does not look at the Pharisee and try to justify his extortion. He stands alone before God, recognizing that compared to divine holiness, he is simply "a sinner." 

* **Physical Posture as Internal Truth**
  Every action of the tax collector conveys authentic repentance and reverence:
  * *“Standing afar off”* acknowledges his unworthiness to approach a holy God.
  * *“Would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven”* reflects genuine shame and godly sorrow.
  * *“Smote upon his breast”* is a physical expression of internal grief and self-condemnation for his sins.

* **A Cry for Grace, Not Merit**
  His prayer is brief and destitute of self-justification: *"God be merciful to me a sinner."* 
  * The Greek word translated as "be merciful" (*hilaskomai*) relates to atonement and propitiation—a plea for God to cover his sins through divine mercy. 
  * He brings no works, no tithes, and no moral resume. He approaches God entirely as a petitioner relying solely on unmerited grace.

---

### Conclusion

Jesus concludes with a fundamental law of the Kingdom of God: **"For every one that exalteth himself shall be abased; and he that humbleth himself shall be exalted"** (v. 14). 

The Pharisee is condemned because he brought his self-righteousness to God and received justice; the tax collector is justified because he brought his sin to God and received mercy. The tax collector's posture is truer because salvation is fundamentally an act of divine grace received through humble faith, not a wage earned by human merit.
