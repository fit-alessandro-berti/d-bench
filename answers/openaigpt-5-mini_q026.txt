Short answer: all three — the verse primarily teaches that words reveal what is in the heart (symptom/revelation), but it also affirms human responsibility and choice about what we say.

Why that conclusion fits the text and biblical context
- “Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh” (also Luke 6:45) pictures speech as an overflow from the heart. That makes words symptomatic: they show what is stored, treasured, or dominant inside a person.
- Jesus is addressing Pharisees who are verbally hostile and hypocritical; his point is evidentiary: their words are proof that their hearts are “evil.” In that sense words are revelatory — they expose inner moral reality, often whether the speaker intends to.
- At the same time the Bible repeatedly treats speech as morally accountable behavior (e.g., Proverbs 18:21; James 3). People choose what to say, can bridle the tongue, cultivate different affections, and be transformed so their speech changes (Ezekiel 36:26; 2 Cor 5:17). So words are also choices and products of moral formation.

A little nuance
- Immediate, unreflective speech often functions as a symptom — it lets inner dispositions leak out. Repeated patterns of speech are especially reliable evidence of moral orientation.
- But speech can be deceptive — people can lie or perform piety while concealing intention (hypocrisy). That means a single utterance isn’t an infallible index of the heart, though consistent speech is revealing.
- The passage therefore carries both diagnostic and ethical force: it diagnoses where sin resides, and it calls for inner renewal so that what “abounds” in the heart will be good treasure and produce good words.

Practical implication
- Listen to your own speech as an indicator of what you value and love.
- Take responsibility: cultivate the “good treasure” of the heart (through repentance, prayer, Scripture, community), because transformed hearts will produce transformed speech.
