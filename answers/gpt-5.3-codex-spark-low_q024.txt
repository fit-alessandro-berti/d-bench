• Great question. In that passage, external compliance is behavior done from the outside (keeping rules, religious
  practice, social respectability). A person can do the right actions for the right reasons publicly and still remain
  inwardly unchanged—driven by fear, pressure, habit, or self-righteousness.

  What Ezekiel calls a “new heart” is an inward transformation: motives, desires, and identity are renewed. The “spirit”
  language signals not just better effort but a re-creation of will and affections—an ability to love what is right, not
  merely perform it.

  That’s why hardness is described in material terms (stone vs. flesh). It’s vivid Hebrew imagery for a spiritual state:

  - “Stony heart” = insensitivity, resistance, calloused self-centeredness, and inability to respond to God.
  - “Heart of flesh” = responsiveness, tenderness, life, and capacity for obedience.

  So it’s not literal surgery; it’s metaphorical anthropological language. The point is that sin’s problem is not only
  behavior but the condition of the inner person. External law keeps you from some wrongs; a new heart makes you want to
  do what is right from within.
