Short answer: “Deny himself” means renouncing the ego‑centered, self‑preserving way of life — the will, interests, reputation and attachments that stand between a person and wholehearted allegiance to Jesus and the gospel. Read in that light the command is not an injunction to hate or obliterate the self, but to refuse its lordship so that a truer, freer life in Christ can begin.

What “self” is meant
- The immediate Greek and synoptic context: Jesus calls people to disown their own claims and take up their cross. The verb (deny/disown) targets the self that seeks to save, exalt, protect, or define itself apart from God — the self ruled by fear, pride, possessions, ambitions, honor, or convenience.  
- In New Testament terms this is the “old self” or the egoistic life (compare Paul’s talk of the “old man” crucified with Christ). It’s primarily moral/spiritual — patterns of selfishness and self‑dependence — rather than annihilation of personhood.

Why some find it liberating
- It promises release from the tyranny of the ego: you’re no longer driven by anxious self‑preservation, by proving yourself, or by the need for approval.  
- It opens the possibility of a deeper identity (“I am Christ’s”) and community loyalty that outlives transient gains. The paradox Jesus states — “whoever loses his life for my sake will save it” — points to a fuller, fearless life (and hope of resurrection) that comes when one is no longer imprisoned by self‑obsession.  
- For people exhausted by consumerism, perfectionism, or social pressure, giving up control and finding purpose in service can feel like liberation.

Why others find it offensive
- It demands surrender of autonomy, rights, comforts, ambitions and prestige. In societies that prize individual freedom, self‑fulfillment, and personal rights, such surrender reads as an attack on everything people are taught to cherish.  
- The imagery of “taking up the cross” evokes shame, suffering, even death; few want a religion that seems to call for humiliation or self‑destruction.  
- The command can be abused: communities or leaders can interpret “deny yourself” as permission to suppress dissent, justify harmful asceticism, or erase legitimate personal needs — which makes the call sound dangerous or abusive rather than liberating.

A balanced reading
- The New Testament frames self‑denial as substitutional: dying to the self in order to live truly in Christ, not as negation of personal dignity. It’s meant to reorient loyalties, not to produce self‑hatred.  
- How this plays out differs by tradition and context: some emphasize inner detachment and contemplative freedom, others emphasize costly discipleship and social resistance to unjust powers.

Practical way to hear it
- Ask what governs your choices: comfort, fear, status, or love of God and neighbor? Denying the former often frees you to live the latter.
