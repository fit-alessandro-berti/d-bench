The moral danger of public goodness in Matthew 6:1 lies not in visibility itself, but in the **motivation behind the action — specifically, the craving to be seen and praised by others**.

Let’s unpack the verse and the underlying principle:

> “Take heed that ye do not your alms before men, to be seen of them: otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven.”  
> — Matthew 6:1 (KJV)

### 1. **Visibility is not inherently wrong**
Jesus does not condemn doing good deeds in public per se. In fact, He encourages believers to let their light shine before men (Matthew 5:16). The issue isn’t the *act* being seen, but the **intention** behind it.

### 2. **The real danger: the craving for human approval**
The phrase “to be seen of them” (Greek: *hina theōntai hup’ autōn*) points to a deliberate desire to be observed and admired. This is the heart of the problem: **a heart seeking the reward of men rather than the reward of God**.

When someone gives alms to be seen, their moral action becomes a performance, not an act of love or righteousness. The focus shifts from serving God and neighbor to pleasing the crowd.

This craving for recognition — the desire for praise, status, or reputation — corrupts the purity of the heart and undermines the true purpose of righteous deeds.

### 3. **The cost of misplaced reward**
Jesus says, “otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven.” This is the key consequence: **if your reward is sought in human approval, you forfeit the eternal, divine reward.** The “reward” isn’t just a future payment; it’s the approval of God and the inner fulfillment that comes from genuine righteousness.

### 4. **The broader context: The Sermon on the Mount**
This verse is part of a larger teaching (Matthew 6:1–18) where Jesus warns against performing religious acts — giving, praying, fasting — for show. Each example follows the same pattern: *Don’t do it to be seen by others; do it for God alone.*

This reveals a deeper principle: **true righteousness is internal and heartfelt.** It’s not about performance, but about the condition of the heart.

---

### Conclusion:
The moral danger in Matthew 6:1 is not visibility itself, but the **craving to be seen and praised**. The problem is not the public nature of good deeds, but the **self-centered motivation** behind them. When the heart seeks human applause, it replaces God’s approval with the fleeting, corrupting reward of man — and thus forfeits the eternal reward of the Father in heaven.

> **In short: The danger is not in being seen, but in seeking to be seen.**
