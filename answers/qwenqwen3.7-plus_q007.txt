Luke 6:37 is frequently misunderstood as a blanket endorsement of moral relativism—the idea that we should never evaluate right from wrong. However, within the broader context of Jesus’ teachings, this verse is not a prohibition against *moral evaluation*, but rather a profound warning against *self-righteous, hypocritical, and final judgment*. 

To understand how this verse differs from the idea that "moral judgments never matter," we must carefully untangle the biblical concepts of **discernment** and the **appetite to condemn**.

---

### 1. Why Moral Judgments Still Matter
The Bible is fundamentally built on moral distinctions. Throughout scripture, believers are repeatedly called to evaluate actions, doctrines, and "fruits" (Matthew 7:16). Therefore, Luke 6:37 does not mean we should turn a blind eye to evil, injustice, or sin. 

What Jesus prohibits is **usurping God’s role as the final arbiter of a person’s heart and eternal destiny**, and doing so from a posture of hypocrisy. 

Just a few verses later in Luke (6:41–42), Jesus illustrates this with the famous metaphor of the speck and the log. He says, *"Why do you look at the speck of sawdust in your brother’s eye and pay no attention to the plank in your own eye?"* Notice that Jesus does not say, "Do not look at the speck." He implies that we *must* identify the speck in order to help our brother remove it. However, he demands that we first deal with the log in our own eye. Thus, moral judgment is not abolished; it is subjected to strict epistemic humility and self-examination.

---

### 2. Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn
The crux of Luke 6:37 lies in distinguishing between healthy moral *discernment* and the toxic *appetite to condemn*. Though both involve recognizing wrongdoing, they are entirely different in motive, process, and outcome.

#### **A. The Goal: Restoration vs. Superiority**
*   **Discernment** seeks truth and restoration. Its goal is to identify a problem so it can be healed, corrected, or protected against. It views the wrongdoer as someone who has made a mistake or is in need of help.
*   **Condemnation** seeks superiority and punishment. Its goal is to elevate the judge by tearing down the judged. It uses another person’s failure as a pedestal for the judge's own ego. 

#### **B. The Direction: Inward-Outward vs. Outward-Only**
*   **Discernment** always begins with self-examination. Before evaluating another, the discerning person asks, *"Am I guilty of this? What are my own blind spots?"*
*   **Condemnation** is exclusively outward-facing. The condemning person uses a magnifying glass on others and a blindfold on themselves. 

#### **C. The Posture: Sorrow vs. Gleefulness**
*   **Discernment** is often accompanied by grief. Recognizing sin or moral failure in a community is a sorrowful thing, even when it is one's own community.
*   **Condemnation** is often accompanied by relish. There is a dark, voyeuristic appetite in canceling, shaming, or permanently writing someone off. 

#### **D. The Scope: Evaluating the Action vs. Defining the Person**
*   **Discernment** evaluates the *action* or the *fruit*. It says, "This behavior is harmful, false, or sinful." It leaves room for the complexity of the human heart, which only God fully knows.
*   **Condemnation** evaluates the *entire person*. It moves from "You did a bad thing" to "You are a bad person, irredeemable and worthless." Condemnation attempts to make a final, eternal verdict that belongs only to God.

---

### 3. The Necessary Role of Forgiveness
The verse concludes: *"forgive, and ye shall be forgiven."* 

Paradoxically, **you cannot forgive what you have not first discerned.** To forgive someone, you must first make the moral judgment that they have wronged you. If moral judgments didn't matter, forgiveness would be meaningless (you can't forgive a neutral event). 

Therefore, Jesus is outlining a mature moral framework:
1. **Discern** the wrong (moral judgment matters).
2. **Examine** yourself first (hypocritical judgment is banned).
3. **Refuse to condemn** the person's eternal worth or write them off (the appetite to condemn is banned).
4. **Extend forgiveness** (the ultimate goal of the interaction).

### Summary
Saying "moral judgments never matter" leads to apathy, enabling, and the collapse of truth. Luke 6:37, however, leads to rigorous moral clarity paired with profound grace. It commands us to have **eyes sharp enough to see the truth**, but **hearts humble enough to know we are also sinners**, and **hands generous enough to offer forgiveness** rather than a final, crushing verdict.
