## The Heart as Storehouse

The passage uses a deliberately economic metaphor: **"treasure"** (Greek *thēsauros*) means a stockpile, a reserve, something accumulated over time and held in store. Jesus is saying that speech is not generated fresh in the moment—it is *drawn out* of an existing inventory. The mouth is less a factory than a faucet; what flows depends on what has been collected upstream.

This frames the whole logic of "out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh." The word *abundance* (*perisseuma*) means overflow, surplus. Speech is what spills over the rim once the inner vessel is full. You cannot consistently pour out what you have not stored.

## Symptoms, Choices, Revelations—All Three

Your three categories aren't competing readings; they describe three moments in a single process.

**Words as symptoms.** Like a physician reading a fever, Jesus treats speech as diagnostic evidence of an unseen condition. The Pharisees' venomous words ("generation of vipers" answers their slander in v. 24) betray a corruption they may not even be advertising deliberately. The symptom reveals the disease whether or not the patient intends disclosure.

**Words as choices.** Yet the verbs are active: a man *brings forth* (*ekballei*—literally "casts out") from his treasure. This is not passive leakage but production. And treasure itself is accumulated through countless prior choices. So speech is chosen twice over: in the moment of utterance, and in the long habituation that stocked the heart in the first place.

**Words as revelations.** This is the synthesis. Because speech is both an honest symptom *and* a willed act drawn from a self-built reserve, it functions as revelation—the disclosure of a *settled character*, not a passing mood. The verses immediately following (12:36–37) confirm this: "every idle word" is accountable precisely because even careless speech, said when our guard is down, exposes the true contents of the vault.

## The Sharp Point

The unsettling implication is that **there is no neutral speech**. Even idle words count as evidence, because the unguarded moment is the most reliable witness to what's actually stored within. We curate our deliberate words, but the overflow tells the truth.

So the passage collapses the gap we like to maintain between "what I really am" and "what I happened to say." For Jesus, the second is the empirical readout of the first. Words are symptoms *because* they are choices, and they reveal *because* they are both.
