**The passage connects speech to hidden moral stockpiles by portraying the heart as an inner treasury (Greek *thesauros*—a storehouse or reservoir of accumulated moral “goods”).** What fills the heart in abundance inevitably overflows through the mouth. Evil hearts (addressed to the “generation of vipers,” i.e., the Pharisees whose words reveal corrupt character) cannot consistently produce good speech; good hearts naturally yield good words. The verb “bringeth forth” pictures speech as drawing from this stored inner reality rather than inventing it on the spot. Jesus makes the same point with the tree-and-fruit metaphor in the preceding verse (Matt 12:33): the quality of the fruit (words) reveals and flows from the quality of the tree (the heart).

**This portrays words as symptoms, revelations, *and* choices—all three at once.**

- **Symptoms**: Speech is the natural diagnostic overflow or “fruit.” Just as symptoms reveal underlying health, words diagnose the moral condition of the heart. Evil speech is not an anomaly; it is the expected symptom of an evil treasury.

- **Revelations**: Words “bring forth” and expose what was previously hidden. They make the invisible moral stockpile public. Jesus’ rhetorical question (“how can ye, being evil, speak good things?”) underscores that speech inevitably discloses the true state of the heart, unmasking hypocrisy.

- **Choices**: “Bringeth forth” and the command to “make the tree good” imply moral responsibility. People choose what they store in the heart over time (through thoughts, desires, habits, and responses to truth) and what they utter. Yet those choices are not free-floating; they are constrained and shaped by the heart’s dominant treasury. Jesus elsewhere stresses accountability for every careless word (Matt 12:36–37), showing that speech is a willful act for which we answer.

In short, the passage teaches that speech is not superficial or easily managed in isolation. It is the audible expression of our deepest moral inventory. Genuine change in speech therefore requires transformation of the heart’s treasure—something only God’s grace and the renewing work of the Spirit can accomplish. This is a call both to self-examination (what do my words reveal about my heart?) and to inner renewal rather than mere external speech control.
