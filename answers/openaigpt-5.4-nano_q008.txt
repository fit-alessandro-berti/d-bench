1 John 4:20 treats love of the “seen” brother as a test for love of the “unseen” God because John is arguing that genuine love toward God must become concrete in how one treats people God places within reach.

- **Love can’t stay purely “spiritual.”** God is invisible; you can’t verify your claims about loving him directly. But your neighbor is visible, present, and knowable—so your love for God must express itself in real behavior toward the brother you can actually see.
- **There is a practical link between belief and conduct.** In John’s framework, loving God isn’t merely an inner emotion or religious talk; it’s love that looks like care, mercy, and goodwill toward others. If that is missing toward the neighbor, the “love” claimed toward God is exposed as counterfeit.
- **It’s not asking whether you can love God while disliking people; it’s asking whether your claim has any substance.** If someone refuses love to the person right in front of them, they have no credible basis for claiming love to the God they cannot see.

### The self-deception being exposed
John is exposing a **self-deception that uses religious language to bypass moral responsibility**—a kind of “inward claim without outward truth.”

Concretely, the liar:
- may **sincerely think** he has spiritual devotion (“I love God”),
- but is **unwilling to let that devotion control his treatment of the neighbor** (“hateth his brother”),
- therefore he is **separating God from concrete ethics**, effectively pretending that love for God can exist while hatred for the visible person remains.

So the deception is: **claiming authentic love for the unseen God while excusing hatred toward the seen brother**, treating the lack of love in the real, testable sphere as irrelevant to the supposed love in the abstract sphere.
