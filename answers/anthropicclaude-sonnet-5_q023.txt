# Isaiah 55:6-7: The Two-fold Structure of Repentance

## Why Both "Way" and "Thoughts"?

The parallel structure is deliberate and comprehensive:

- **"His way"** (דֶּרֶךְ, *derekh*) — the outward path of life, one's habitual actions, the visible pattern of conduct
- **"His thoughts"** (מַחְשְׁבֹתָיו, *machshevotav*) — the inward reasoning, plans, and imagination that give rise to those actions

Hebrew thought did not separate deed from intention as cleanly as we might. The "way" is simply the thought made visible over time; the "thoughts" are the way in seed-form. To call for repentance of one but not the other would be to treat a tree without touching its root.

## What This Implies

**1. Outward conduct alone is insufficient.**
A person could theoretically alter behavior while the underlying disposition remains unchanged — going through motions of righteousness while still *scheming* wickedness inwardly. Isaiah's parallelism forecloses this. Reformation of habit without transformation of mind is not the repentance in view.

**2. Inward change alone is incomplete.**
Conversely, an inward "feeling sorry" that never redirects the actual path of life is equally inadequate. The text does not say "let him regret his thoughts" — it says *forsake* and *return*, active verbs implying a change of direction, not merely sentiment.

**3. The whole person is addressed.**
This anticipates the fuller biblical anthropology later expressed in passages like Ezekiel 36:26 ("a new heart... a new spirit") and Romans 12:2 ("be transformed by the renewing of your mind"). Sin is not merely behavioral malfunction; it is a corruption of the imagination that then expresses itself in action. Repentance must therefore reach both levels — will and intellect, conduct and cognition.

**4. Mercy answers total need, not partial reform.**
The structure of the verse matters: *first* the turning ("let him return"), *then* the mercy ("he will have mercy... he will abundantly pardon"). But note — the turning is not the *cause* of mercy in a transactional sense; it is the *occasion* for its reception. God does not pardon based on the adequacy of human reformation (which is never fully adequate), but the totality of the call — way *and* thoughts — indicates that mercy is received by a whole person turning wholly, not by partial gestures.

**5. "Abundantly pardon" matches the scope of the sin.**
The Hebrew intensifies: literally "multiply to pardon." Since the wickedness in view is comprehensive (both act and thought), the pardon offered is correspondingly comprehensive — not a limited amnesty for behavior while thoughts remain condemned, but a total, generous forgiveness answering the total need.

## Summary

The pairing of "way" and "thoughts" teaches that **sin is not merely an act to be corrected but a disposition to be redirected**. True repentance is not partial — a change of habit without a change of heart, or vice versa — but addresses the whole person. And it is precisely because God's mercy is described as *abundant* that such total repentance, however imperfectly achieved, can be met with pardon proportionate to the depth of the need it addresses.
