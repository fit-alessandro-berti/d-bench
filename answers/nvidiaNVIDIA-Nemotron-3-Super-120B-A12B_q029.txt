

**Short answer:**  
The moral posture Paul calls for in Gal 6:1 is a *humble, self‑aware gentleness* that seeks to **restore** the fallen brother or sister, does so **in meekness** (gentle, non‑condemning spirit), and constantly **examines oneself** lest the corrector fall into the same temptation. Restoration, meekness, and self‑suspicion are inseparable because each guards against a different way the correction can go wrong:  

| Element | What it protects against | Why it belongs together |
|---------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Restoration** (καταρτίζετε) | Turning correction into punishment or exclusion | The goal is to bring the person back into fruitful fellowship, not to satisfy our sense of justice. |
| **Meekness** (πραΰτητι) | Pride, harshness, or a “holier‑than‑thou” attitude | Gentleness keeps the corrector’s motives loving and opens the heart of the one being corrected to receive help. |
| **Self‑suspicion** (σκοπῶν… lest thou also be tempted) | Complacency or self‑righteousness that blinds us to our own vulnerability | By remembering we are equally prone to sin, we avoid the trap of using correction to elevate ourselves. |

---

### 1. The Greek words and their nuance  

| Greek | Literal sense | Pauline nuance |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| **καταρτίζετε** (katartízete) | “to put in order, to mend, to restore” (used of mending nets, setting broken bones) | A *repair‑oriented* act: we are to mend the brother’s spiritual walk, not to discard him. |
| **πραΰτητι** (praýtēti) | “meekness, gentleness, humility” (fruit of the Spirit, Gal 5:22‑23) | The attitude that avoids harsh judgment and coercion; it is the opposite of *self‑assertive* zeal. |
| **σκοπῶν** (skopōn) | “looking, watching, considering” (present participle) | Ongoing vigilance: we must continually *watch ourselves* as we engage in correction. |

The three imperatives are linked by the conjunction **καὶ** (and) in the flow of the verse: “*if a man be overtaken…*, **restore** … **in meekness**, **considering** yourself…”. Grammatically, they form a single, compound instruction: the *manner* of restoration is meek, and the *motive* is self‑examination.

### 2. Why restoration is the goal  

- **Community health:** Paul’s letter to the Galatians is preoccupied with preserving the unity of the body of Christ against the divisive influence of legalism (Gal 5:13‑15). Restoration preserves that unity.  
- **Grace over law:** The Galatian error was trying to earn righteousness by works. Correcting with a punitive spirit would re‑introduce a law‑based mindset; restoration reflects the gospel’s reconciling power.  
- **Future usefulness:** A restored brother can again “bear one another’s burdens” (Gal 6:2). Excommunicating or shunning him wastes a gifted member of the body.

### 3. Why meekness must accompany restoration  

- **Prevents pride:** If we correct with harshness, we implicitly claim superiority. Meekness acknowledges that we, too, are recipients of grace (cf. 1 Cor 10:12).  
- **Facilitates receptivity:** A gentle tone lowers defensive walls, making the person more likely to hear the truth and respond positively (cf. Prov 15:1).  
- **Mirrors Christ’s approach:** Jesus restored the woman caught in adultery (John 8:1‑11) not by condemnation but by compassionate truth‑telling (“Neither do I condemn you… go and sin no more”).  

### 4. Why self‑suspicion (self‑examination) is essential  

- **Guards against self‑righteousness:** The Galatian temptation was to think one’s own observance of the law made one superior. By *considering* ourselves, we remember that we are also “subject to the same temptations” (Gal 6:1).  
- **Keeps the corrector accountable:** If we are unaware of our own propensity to sin, we may unwittingly project our own faults onto others, turning correction into a vent for personal frustration.  
- **Cultivates dependence on the Spirit:** Self‑examination drives us back to prayer and reliance on the Holy Spirit, the true source of both the ability to restore and the meekness to do so rightly (cf. Gal 5:22‑23).  

### 5. The integrated moral posture in practice  

1. **Detect the fault with love, not with a spirit of accusation.**  
2. **Approach the person privately (Matt 18:15) with the intent to heal, not to expose.**  
3. **Speak truth gently, acknowledging your own weakness (“I, too, struggle with …”).**  
4. **Offer concrete help (prayer, accountability, resources) that aims at re‑integration.**  
5. **After the encounter, continue to watch your own heart, repenting of any pride or judgment that may have surfaced.**  

This posture embodies the *fruit of the Spirit* (love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self‑control) and fulfills Paul’s broader ethic: *bear one another’s burdens* (Gal 6:2) while *keeping in step with the Spirit* (Gal 5:25).

---

**Bottom line:**  
Correction in Gal 6:1 is not about exercising authority; it is about exercising *restorative humility*. The three elements—restoration, meekness, and self‑suspicion—are inseparable because together they ensure that the corrector’s motive is love, the method is grace‑filled, and the vigilance against self‑deception remains constant. Only when all three are present does the correction truly reflect the gospel’s power to heal and restore the body of Christ.
