Your question touches on a central tension in Christian ethics: how can Jesus command us not to judge while also calling us to moral clarity and discernment?  
The verse from Luke 6:37 is often misunderstood as an absolute prohibition against all moral evaluation, but a closer look reveals it is more about the *spirit* in which we judge, not the act of discernment itself.

---

### **1. What “judge not” does NOT mean**
It does **not** mean:
- That we cannot recognize right from wrong.
- That we should ignore sin, injustice, or harmful behavior.
- That all moral distinctions are irrelevant.
- That we cannot exercise spiritual or ethical discernment (which is elsewhere commanded in Scripture, e.g., Matthew 7:6, 1 Corinthians 5:12, John 7:24).

If moral judgments never mattered, Jesus himself would not have criticized hypocrisy, injustice, or selfishness — which he frequently did.

---

### **2. Discernment vs. the appetite to condemn**

| **Discernment** | **Appetite to Condemn** |
|------------------|--------------------------|
| Seeks to understand and evaluate actions or teachings in light of truth. | Seeks to pass final verdict on a person’s soul or worth. |
| Motivated by love, restoration, and protection (Galatians 6:1). | Motivated by self-righteousness, pride, or desire to elevate oneself. |
| Recognizes one’s own fallibility and need for mercy (Matthew 7:1–5). | Assumes a position of moral superiority. |
| Leaves ultimate judgment to God (James 4:12). | Takes the role of ultimate judge. |
| Is accompanied by humility and grief over sin. | Is accompanied by satisfaction in condemning others. |

In essence, **discernment** is a tool for navigation in a morally complex world; **condemnation** is a posture of final, harsh sentencing that belongs to God alone.

---

### **3. The deeper logic of Luke 6:37**
Jesus’ teaching here is connected to:
- **Reciprocal spiritual principle**: The way we treat others is the measure by which God will deal with us (the “measure” idea follows in Luke 6:38).
- **Mercy over condemnation**: Jesus highlights forgiveness and mercy as kingdom priorities — not because sin is unimportant, but because God’s way of dealing with us is merciful, and we are called to reflect that (Matthew 18:21–35).
- **Hypocrisy check**: Often our rush to judge others ignores our own faults. Removing the log from our own eye first (Matthew 7:5) is a prerequisite for helping others.

---

### **4. Practical implication**
A Christian can and must make moral distinctions — for example, identifying abuse as wrong, false teaching as dangerous, or injustice as evil.  
However, the *attitude* should be:
- **Humble** (aware of one’s own moral failures).
- **Restorative** (hoping for repentance and healing, not destruction).
- **Merciful** (forgiving as we have been forgiven).

Jesus himself showed this balance: He condemned hypocrisy and injustice fiercely, yet offered forgiveness to repentant sinners and warned against self-righteous judgment.

---

### **Conclusion**
Luke 6:37 is not a call to moral relativism or indifference.  
It’s a call to abandon the **self-righteous, harsh, unforgiving spirit of condemnation** and instead exercise **loving, humble discernment**, always remembering our dependence on God’s mercy.  
The difference lies in the heart’s posture: one seeks to lift up or protect in truth and love; the other seeks to tear down in pride.
