The elder brother's deepest problem seems to be rooted in **wounded fairness** and a perception of injustice. In this passage, the Pharisee who had been devotedly serving his father and working hard for his share seemed to feel unfairly treated when the younger brother, who had not only repented of his sins but also returned home and reconciled with his father, was forgiven and celebrated more openly than he was. The elder brother felt he deserved greater recognition for his lifelrances' contribution, as implied by his frustration about not being assigned his fair share, specifically the fatted calf, which was a special delicacy meant to celebrate such events. This suggests that his primary issue is not so much about the merriment itself or being forgiven, but rather how his efforts have been overlooked and equitably honored. While there is no explicit mention of him being unfairly treated or jealousy, his demand for recognition and perceived lack of fairness show elements of wounded fairness and perhaps a sense of entitlement. The elder brother is grappling with his emotions, which have been heightened due to the family's sudden shift in dynamics upon his brother's return. Luke 7:48 sheds more light on their relationship: he was deeply resentful of his brother's release from prison and felt his own hard work went unnoticed and unrewarded. Therefore, the brother's main issue is not anger, envy, pride, or discomfort with mercy, but rather the perception that his sacrifices and efforts were not adequately acknowledged and valued within the family context. In the end, he leaves all to go into the city, indicating he struggles with being alone and unrecognized, despite his service and devotion, which reflects deeper issues of fairness and familial recognition.
