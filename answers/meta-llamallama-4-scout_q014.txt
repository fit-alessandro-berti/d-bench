This text from Matthew 6:19-21 primarily suggests that treasure reveals the heart rather than trains it. The passage indicates that the location and nature of one's treasure serve as an indicator of where one's heart is. The heart, in this context, refers to one's deepest affections, priorities, and commitments.

Let's break it down:

1. **Treasure and Corruption**: Jesus contrasts earthly treasures, which are subject to corruption and theft, with heavenly treasures that are secure and eternal. This contrast highlights the fleeting nature of material wealth versus the enduring nature of spiritual riches.

2. **The Heart's Orientation**: The key verse, "For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also" (Matthew 6:21), implies that the heart follows the treasure. This suggests that what one treasures or values most is a reflection of what is most important or dear to them.

3. **Revealing the Heart**: The passage indicates that treasure serves as a kind of litmus test for the heart's true allegiance or orientation. It doesn't directly suggest that accumulating treasure (whether earthly or heavenly) is what shapes or trains the heart, but rather that the heart is already inclined towards what is being treasured. Thus, treasure is more of an indicator or a reflection of the heart's condition.

4. **Possession, Attention, and Love**: The connection between possession, attention, and love is deeply intertwined in the concept of "treasure." To treasure something is not just to possess it but to value it highly, to give it attention, and to love or cherish it. When Jesus talks about laying up treasures, he's discussing what one prioritizes, focuses on (attention), and has affection for (love). Therefore, the act of treasuring something inherently involves these three elements: one possesses it (or seeks to), gives it attention (by prioritizing it), and loves or values it deeply.

In summary, Matthew 6:19-21 suggests that treasure is a reflection or revelation of the heart's true orientation and affections. It emphasizes that our values and priorities (what we treasure) are indicative of our heart's condition. While it doesn't directly state that treasure trains the heart, the implication is that focusing on heavenly treasures can lead to a heart that is more heavenly oriented. The interrelated concepts of possession, attention, and love are central to understanding what it means to treasure something, and they collectively indicate where one's heart lies.
