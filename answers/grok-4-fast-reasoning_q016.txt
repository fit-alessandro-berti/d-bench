The elder brother's deepest problem is **discomfort with mercy itself**. 

While his outward anger, envy of the celebration, sense of wounded fairness, and pride in his own obedience are all evident in the text (and interconnected symptoms of his resentment), they all flow from a fundamental failure to grasp—or accept—the nature of the father's unconditional, grace-filled love. He views his relationship with the father transactionally, as dutiful service deserving of rewards, rather than as sonship in a family where mercy restores the lost without regard to merit. This mirrors the parable's broader critique of self-righteous resentment toward God's forgiving grace (as seen in the Pharisees' reaction to Jesus welcoming sinners in the chapter's context). The father's gentle reminder—"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine"—exposes this root issue, inviting the elder brother to embrace the joy of mercy rather than reject it.
