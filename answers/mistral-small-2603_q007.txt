This verse from Luke 6:37 is often misunderstood as a blanket prohibition against all moral judgment, but its deeper meaning is more nuanced. The key lies in distinguishing between **discernment** (a necessary moral act) and the **appetite to condemn** (a spiritually destructive impulse). Here’s how they differ:

### 1. **Discernment vs. Condemnation**
   - **Discernment** is the ability to recognize right from wrong, good from evil, or truth from falsehood. It is a *necessary* part of moral living—Jesus Himself engaged in discernment (e.g., calling out hypocrisy in Matthew 23). Discernment seeks to guide, correct, or protect, not to destroy.
   - **Condemnation**, on the other hand, is the act of *passing final judgment* with a punitive or self-righteous spirit. It assumes the role of God (Romans 14:4) and often stems from pride, hypocrisy, or a desire to elevate oneself by tearing others down.

### 2. **The Context of Luke 6:37**
   Jesus is addressing the **Pharisaical tendency** to judge others harshly while ignoring one’s own flaws (Matthew 7:1–5). The command "Judge not" is not a call to moral relativism but a warning against **hypocritical, unloving judgment**. The parallel structure ("condemn not... forgive, and ye shall be forgiven") suggests that the issue is not discernment itself but the *attitude* behind it.

   - If you **discern rightly** (e.g., calling out sin for the sake of repentance), you are not violating this principle.
   - If you **condemn others** out of malice, self-righteousness, or a lack of humility, you invite the same harshness upon yourself.

### 3. **The Fruit of the Spirit vs. the Works of the Flesh**
   Galatians 5:22–23 contrasts the **fruit of the Spirit** (love, patience, kindness) with the **works of the flesh** (hatred, discord, jealousy). Condemnation often flows from the latter, while discernment (when done in love) aligns with the former.

   - **Example of Discernment:** A parent correcting a child’s behavior is an act of love, not condemnation.
   - **Example of Condemnation:** A gossip spreading rumors to ruin someone’s reputation is acting from malice.

### 4. **The Balance: Truth and Grace**
   Jesus never abandoned truth (John 14:6), but He always paired it with grace (John 8:1–11, where He forgives the adulteress). The command "Judge not" is not a call to silence truth but to **avoid the spirit of the Pharisees**—those who used judgment as a weapon rather than a tool for restoration.

### **Conclusion**
Luke 6:37 does not forbid moral judgment entirely but condemns the **spirit of condemnation**—the unloving, self-righteous, or vengeful attitude that seeks to destroy rather than restore. Discernment is necessary, but it must be done with humility, love, and a recognition of our own need for grace. As Jesus says in Matthew 7:1–2:
> *"Judge not, that ye be not judged. For with what judgment ye judge, ye shall be judged: and with what measure ye mete, it shall be measured to you again."*
