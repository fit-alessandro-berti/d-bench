**Realistic about self-deception and the fallen human condition, not mainly pessimistic.**

Jeremiah 17:9–10 is a stark diagnosis of the human heart (*lev* in Hebrew—the seat of mind, will, emotions, and desires). The terms are strong: “deceitful” (*‘aqob*) carries the sense of crooked, treacherous, or slippery; “desperately wicked/sick” (*’anush*) implies incurably ill or gravely wounded. The rhetorical question “who can know it?” underscores that we are not reliable self-diagnosticians. We rationalize, project, and hide motives even from ourselves. This matches the broader biblical picture—Genesis 3 onward, Psalm 51:5, Proverbs 28:26 (“He who trusts in his own heart is a fool”), Jeremiah’s own context of Judah’s self-deceptive idolatry and false trust in human strength (see Jer 17:5–8), Jesus’ teaching on the heart’s evil output (Matt 15:19), and Paul’s realism in Romans 3 and 7. 

It is realistic anthropology, not nihilistic pessimism. The very next verse immediately pivots to hope and accountability: **God** searches the heart and tests the “reins” (the inner control center). He is not fooled. Judgment is not arbitrary but according to “ways” and “the fruit of his doings.” The passage exposes our self-deception so we will stop trusting our own hearts and turn to the Lord who knows us perfectly. This theme recurs elsewhere: the same prophet promises a new covenant with God’s law written on renewed hearts (Jer 31:33; cf. Ezek 36:26). The diagnosis drives us toward grace, not despair. The Bible’s “pessimism” about unaided human nature is always paired with God’s knowledge, justice, and redemptive power.

### How should we talk about motives?

If the heart is an unreliable witness, we must speak about motives with **humility, caution, and external anchors** rather than naïve confidence or speculative psychologizing. Practical guidelines drawn from this passage and the rest of Scripture:

- **Start with self-skepticism.** Acknowledge that your own motives are mixed and partially hidden to you. Phrases like “I *think* my motive is…” or “As far as I can tell…” are more honest than “My heart is pure.” Pray Psalm 139:23–24 regularly: “Search me, O God, and know my heart; try me and know my anxious thoughts; and see if there be any hurtful way in me…”

- **Prioritize fruit and observable ways.** Verse 10 itself tells us how God evaluates people—by actions and patterns, not merely by self-reported intentions. Jesus says we will know people (and test claims) “by their fruits” (Matt 7:16–20). James warns that selfish motives produce disorder (James 4:1–3). Talk more about behavior and results than about invisible inner states.

- **Use Scripture as the mirror.** Let God’s Word examine us rather than our feelings or rationalizations (James 1:23–25; Heb 4:12). It reveals where “good intentions” mask pride, fear, people-pleasing, or self-justification.

- **Seek external input.** Wise counsel, close community, and accountability partners often see our blind spots (Prov 27:6, 17; 1 Cor 4:5). The Lord uses others to test and clarify what we cannot see alone.

- **When speaking about *others’* motives, exercise even greater restraint.** Only God searches hearts infallibly. Presuming we fully know someone else’s inner motives often leads to judgmentalism or false accusation. Better to say, “Based on the fruit I’m seeing, this pattern concerns me…” or “It appears that…” than “You’re doing this because your heart is…” (see 1 Cor 4:5). Focus on clarity about behavior and its effects, then invite the person to examine their own heart before God.

- **Hold intentions and actions together without letting one cancel the other.** “Good motives” do not excuse harmful actions, nor do we dismiss the importance of the heart (the Sermon on the Mount deepens the law to the level of motive). The goal is alignment: heart and life both transformed by grace.

In short, Jeremiah 17:9–10 fosters **gospel realism**: sober about sin and self-deception, hopeful because the Lord knows us completely and offers a new heart to those who trust Him. When discussing motives, speak humbly, test everything by fruit and Scripture, pray for divine searching, and look to the God who judges righteously rather than to our own treacherous hearts. This approach avoids both naive optimism about human goodness and cynical despair.
