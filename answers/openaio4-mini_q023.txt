In this couplet Isaiah is calling for a genuinely whole-person repentance.  By insisting that the wicked “forsake his way” (Hebrew derek, the road you walk—the sum of your outward habits and conduct) and the “unrighteous man” his “thoughts” (Hebrew machashabah, your inner counsels or plans—what you imagine, intend, deliberate) he is insisting that turning to God isn’t merely a change of behavior or religious ritual, but a change of heart and mind as well.

1. “Way” = outward conduct  
   – In the Old Testament idiom your “way” is the path you walk, the shape of your life in deed and habit.  Forsaking your way means abandoning the sinful patterns, the choices and actions that alienate you from God.  

2. “Thoughts” = inward imagination and intention  
   – In Hebrew psychology imagination and intention live in the heart (leb).  Your machashabah are the plans you form, the stories you play out in your mind, the excuses you harbor.  God demands that we not only stop doing wrong but also uproot the sinful thought-patterns that spawn it.

The implication is that true repentance (teshuvah in Hebrew) must be comprehensive: you turn both body and mind back toward God.  Superficial reform of behavior alone can leave the root of rebellion intact, and self-disciplining your thoughts without corresponding deeds leaves God unglorified in your life.

Finally, Isaiah immediately folds this call to genuine repentance into a promise: “let him return unto the Lord, and he will have mercy…for he will abundantly pardon.”  The very fact that God summons us to a full-scale conversion of way and thoughts testifies to how seriously He takes both.  Yet the promise of abundant pardon assures us that no inward barrier or outward record of sin is so great that divine mercy cannot meet it, if only we abandon both our crooked paths and our twisted imaginations and turn wholly to Him.
