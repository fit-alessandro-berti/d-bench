This sentence, spoken by Jesus in John 8:7, is morally destabilizing for several reasons, which can be broken down into the categories you've mentioned and perhaps a few more. Here's a detailed analysis:

1. **Forbids Judgment**: On a surface level, Jesus' statement challenges the right of anyone in the crowd to judge the woman caught in adultery. By saying "He that is without sin among you, let him first cast a stone at her," Jesus effectively points out that no one present is sinless, implying that only someone without sin could justly condemn another for their sin. This forces the crowd to reflect on their own moral purity and thus indirectly forbids judgment based on self-righteousness.

2. **Exposes Hypocrisy**: By turning the tables on her accusers, Jesus exposes the hypocrisy within the crowd. Many of those eager to stone the woman were likely guilty of their own transgressions, despite their zealousness to enforce the law on her. This statement brings to light the double standard at play, where individuals condemn others for sins they themselves commit, highlighting the disconnect between their professed beliefs and their actions.

3. **Interrupts Mob-Righteousness**: The practice of stoning as a form of public execution was often accompanied by a mob mentality, where the participants felt justified and righteous in their actions due to the crowd's consensus. Jesus' intervention breaks this spell of collective moral justification, making each individual accountable for their actions and forcing them to evaluate their participation in the stoning not as part of a righteous mob but as a personal, moral decision.

4. **Something Deeper**: Beyond these immediate effects, Jesus' statement plunges into deeper moral and theological waters. It challenges the foundation of moral authority and the nature of justice and mercy. By essentially disarming the legal and social mechanism of condemnation, Jesus unveils a more profound understanding of justice and compassion. This act emphasizes God's preference for mercy over sacrifice and legalism, pointing towards the inherent value of every human life and the importance of treating others with the same mercy and forgiveness one seeks for oneself.

5. **Redemption over Condemnation**: At its core, the sentence suggests that the path of redemption and personal transformation is preferable to condemnation. By silencing the crowd and sparing the woman, Jesus opens a space for her (and implicitly, for all) to reflect on their actions, seek forgiveness, and change their path. This approach underscores the Christian emphasis on forgiveness, personal repentance, and the belief in the possibility of redemption for all, regardless of past deeds.

In summary, Jesus' statement in John 8:7 is morally destabilizing because it not only challenges the immediate context of judgment, hypocrisy, and mob righteousness but also dives into deeper questions of moral authority, justice, mercy, and redemption. It reshapes the understanding of moral judgments and actions, pushing towards a more compassionate and introspective approach to dealing with sin and transgression.
