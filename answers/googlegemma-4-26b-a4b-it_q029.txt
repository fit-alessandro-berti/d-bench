Based on the text of Galatians 6:1, the moral posture required for correction is one of **humble, restorative stewardship** rather than judicial condemnation. It is a posture that views the fallen individual not as an enemy to be crushed, but as a brother to be healed.

The text links **restoration**, **meekness**, and **self-suspicion** (self-examination) together to create a "safety circuit" that prevents the corrector from falling into the very sins they are trying to remedy.

Here is a breakdown of why these three elements are inseparable:

### 1. Restoration: The Goal (The "What")
The Greek word used for "restore" (*katartizo*) is a medical and nautical term. It was used for setting a fractured bone or mending a torn fishing net. 
*   **The Implication:** The goal of correction is not to punish, shame, or exile, but to make the person "whole" again so they can function within the community. 
*   **The Moral Posture:** If the goal is restoration, the corrector cannot be driven by anger or a desire for superiority. You do not "beat" a broken bone back into place; you set it gently so it can heal.

### 2. Meekness: The Method (The "How")
Meekness is often misunderstood as weakness, but in the biblical sense, it is **"power under control."** 
*   **The Implication:** The person correcting has the moral authority to speak, but they must restrain that authority. Meekness ensures that the correction is directed at the *sin*, not at the *person’s dignity*.
*   **The Moral Posture:** Without meekness, restoration becomes condemnation. If you correct with pride or harshness, you may fix the behavior but destroy the relationship, thereby failing the goal of "restoration."

### 3. Self-Suspicion: The Guardrail (The "Why")
The phrase *"considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted"* serves as the psychological and spiritual anchor for the entire command. 
*   **The Implication:** This is a warning against **spiritual narcissism.** There is a common temptation for those who correct others to feel "safe" or "above" the sin being discussed. Paul warns that the very act of correcting can lead to the sin of pride, or that the corrector may be susceptible to the same temptation under different circumstances.
*   **The Moral Posture:** It requires a posture of **radical solidarity.** Instead of looking *down* at the sinner, the corrector must look *inward* at their own vulnerability.

---

### Why are they kept together?

These three elements form a functional ecosystem of accountability:

1.  **Without Restoration,** meekness is just politeness without purpose, and self-suspicion is just insecurity. You need a goal (healing) to give the correction direction.
2.  **Without Meekness,** restoration becomes an exercise of power (legalism), and self-suspicion is ignored in favor of self-righteousness. You need the "soft touch" to ensure the person can actually be mended.
3.  **Without Self-Suspicion,** restoration becomes a performance of superiority, and meekness becomes a mask for judgmentalism. You need the awareness of your own weakness to ensure your heart remains aligned with the goal of healing rather than the desire to be "right."

**Summary:** To correct someone according to Galatians 6:1 is to approach them as a fellow patient in the same hospital, using the gentle hands of a surgeon (meekness) to mend a wound (restoration), while remaining acutely aware that you are also susceptible to injury (self-suspicion).
