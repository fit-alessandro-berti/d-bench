The verse Luke 6:37, "Judge not, and you will not be judged; condemn not, and you will not be condemned: forgive, and you will be forgiven," is often misunderstood as a blanket prohibition against moral judgment. However, in its full context within Jesus' Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5-7), it addresses the **attitude and hypocrisy** behind judgment rather than the legitimacy of discernment itself. Here’s a nuanced exploration of the distinction:

---

### **1. Judgment vs. Discernment: The Attitude Matters**
- **Judgment (Krino)**: In Greek, *krino* (to judge) can mean making a decision or evaluating a situation. Jesus does not oppose discernment outright but warns against **judgment rooted in pride, hypocrisy, or self-righteousness** (cf. Luke 6:42; Matthew 7:1-5). 
- **Condemnation (Dikazō)**: The term *dikazō* (to condemn) implies a harsh, punitive judgment—actively pronouncing others as wrong or unworthy. This is explicitly rebuked in the verse, as it reflects an unloving and divisive spirit. 

**Key Difference**:  
Discernment involves recognizing truth and right/wrong for the sake of wisdom and ethical action, while the "appetite to condemn" is an internal inclination to criticize others, often to elevate oneself or foster division. Jesus prioritizes mercy and humility over condemning attitudes.

---

### **2. The Reciprocal Principle: Treat Others as You Want to Be Treated**
The verse underscores a **reciprocal dynamic**: 
- If we reject judging/condemning others, we avoid receiving the same from God and others. 
- Forgiveness becomes the response to our own need for mercy (Matthew 6:14-15). 

This does not mean we should ignore wrongdoing but rather **respond to others with grace** (colloquially, "judge with love"). For example, in John 7:24, Jesus calls for "judging rightly," meaning discernment must be:
- **Just** (not partial or biased),
- **Merciful** (rooted in compassion),
- **Self-aware** (free from hypocrisy).

---

### **3. Discernment Without Condemnation**
- **Discernment** is necessary for ethical living (e.g., recognizing sin, making wise choices). The Bible itself models this (e.g., Proverbs 2:6; Romans 12:2). 
- **Condemnation**, however, is a sinful impulse that:
  - Reflects a lack of self-awareness (Matthew 7:3-5),
  - Ignores God’s sovereignty to judge (Romans 14:4),
  - Undermines the call to reconciliation (Ephesians 4:31-32).

Jesus teaches that while we can *discern* moral truths, we must avoid the **attitude of condemnation**—which is incompatible with the kingdom’s ethos of forgiveness and unity.

---

### **4. Moral Judgments *Do Matter*, But With Humility**
Moral discernment remains essential for:
- Guiding personal conduct (Galatians 6:1),
- Addressing systemic injustice (Micah 6:8),
- Protecting the vulnerable (Proverbs 31:8-9).

However, such discernment must avoid the **appetite to condemn**, which stems from:
- **Pride**: Elevating oneself above others (Luke 18:9-14).
- **Self-righteousness**: Ignoring one’s own sin (John 9:48-54).
- **Malice**: Seeking to harm others (James 4:1-2).

Instead, moral judgments should aim to **restore, not destroy** (Galatians 6:1).

---

### **Conclusion**
Luke 6:37 distinguishes between:
1. **Discernment** (necessary, godly wisdom to recognize right/wrong), and  
2. **The appetite to condemn** (a sinful, divisive impulse to judge others harshly).

Moral judgments do matter, but they must be practiced with humility, love, and a commitment to forgiveness. The verse ultimately calls believers to embody the kingdom’s values: compassion over condemnation, wisdom over hypocrisy, and grace over judgment (Luke 6:36).
