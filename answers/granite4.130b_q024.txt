**Step‑by‑step explanation**

---

### 1. What the passage says

> **Ezekiel 36:26–27 (KJV)**  
> “A new heart also will I give you, and a new spirit will I put within you: and I will take away the stony heart out of your flesh, and I will give you an heart of flesh.   
> And I will put my Spirit within you, and cause you to walk in My statutes, and ye shall keep My judgments, and do them.”

The prophecy speaks of God **re‑creating** the inner person (the “heart”) rather than merely commanding outward behavior.

---

### 2. External compliance vs. a “new heart”

| Aspect | **External Compliance** | **Receiving a New Heart** |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Source of motivation** | Law, fear, peer pressure, cultural expectation, personal ambition | Indwelling Holy Spirit; love and gratitude toward God |
| **Nature of obedience** | *What* you do (acts that can be observed) – e.g., keeping the Sabbath, giving tithes, not stealing. It may be done “on the outside” while the inner motives remain unchanged. | *Why* you do it (the heart’s orientation). The obedient acts flow from a transformed desire to please God. |
| **Sustainability** | Often temporary; can collapse when external pressure eases or when temptation is strong. Relies on self‑discipline, rules, or societal enforcement. | Enduring; because the inner disposition has been rewired by divine grace, the believer naturally seeks what pleases God even in private moments. |
| **Relation to “law”** | The law serves as a *guide* that must be obeyed from without → “do this”. | The law becomes an expression of a heart already aligned with God → “I want to do this because I love Him.” (cf. Romans 8:4‑5). |
| **Scriptural illustration** | Israel’s repeated outward ritualism despite idolatry (Jeremiah 7; Hosea 6:6). | New Testament believers described as “living stones” built on the “living cornerstone” Jesus, whose spirit indwells them (1 Peter 2:5; 2 Cor 3:3). |

**Key point:** *External compliance* can look righteous but remains **surface‑level**; it does not guarantee a changed relationship with God. A “new heart” means the inner person—thoughts, desires, affections—is regenerated by God’s Spirit.

---

### 3. Why hardness is described as a material condition

1. **Imagery of stone vs. flesh**  
   - *Stone* (or “stony heart”) denotes something **hard, unyielding, lifeless**, incapable of receiving or transmitting anything beyond its own rigidity. In the Old Testament this metaphor recurs (e.g., Ezekiel 11:19; 18:31).  
   - *Flesh* conveys **softness, flexibility, vitality**—the capacity to feel, respond, and be molded.

2. **Purpose of vivid language**  
   - The prophet uses concrete, tangible images because the audience can picture a heavy rock being removed from their chest and replaced with pliable tissue. This visual drives home that:
     - Hardness is not merely an emotional state; it is like a **material barrier** blocking God’s influence.
     - Divine transformation therefore requires **removal of that barrier** (the “stony heart”) and the implantation of something living (“heart of flesh”).

3. **Theological significance**  
   - The *hardness* symbolizes **spiritual death**: a heart dead to God’s truth, unable to perceive or obey it (Ezekiel 11:19; 36:26).  
   - Only **God can act surgically**, “taking away” the stone and giving flesh—an act beyond human effort (miraculous regeneration).

4. **Parallel in New Testament**  
   - Paul echoes this metaphor: “the natural man does not receive the things of the Spirit… because they are spiritually discerned” (1 Cor 2:14). The “natural” or “unregenerate” heart is functionally a “stone.”  
   - Jesus’ promise in John 7:38‑39 (“out of his heart will flow rivers of living water”) envisions the Spirit replacing stone with living, flowing flesh.

5. **Practical implication**  
   - Recognizing that hardness is *material* helps believers understand why mere behavioral correction (e.g., “stop cursing”) often fails; the underlying *substance* of the heart must be changed by God’s work through the Holy Spirit (cf. 2 Cor 3:18).

---

### 4. Summary

- **External compliance** = outward acts performed because they are commanded or socially expected, without an inner transformation.
- **Receiving a “new heart”** = divine regeneration where the entire inner being—thoughts, desires, motives—is replaced by God’s Spirit so that obedience flows naturally from love and gratitude.
- The **“stony heart”** metaphor stresses that spiritual hardness is like a physical obstacle; it must be **removed** (by God) and **replaced** with a living “heart of flesh,” enabling the spirit to dwell within and guide every step.

Thus, true biblical obedience is not about checking boxes on a law‑list but about being **born anew**—a radical inner renewal that makes walking in God’s statutes a natural expression of who we have become.
