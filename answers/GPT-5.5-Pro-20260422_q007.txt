Luke 6:37 is not saying, “Nothing is right or wrong.” It is saying something sharper: **do not take up the posture of final, condemning superiority toward another person.**

That is very different from saying moral judgments never matter.

In fact, moral judgment **must** matter for Jesus’ teaching to make sense. Forgiveness only means something if real wrong has been done. Mercy only matters if guilt is possible. Repentance only matters if there is something from which to turn. So Jesus is not abolishing moral distinctions; he is confronting a particular kind of moral pleasure: the desire to stand over another person as their prosecutor, judge, and executioner.

## Discernment sees; condemnation enjoys sentencing

**Discernment** is the sober recognition of what is true.

It can say:

> “That action was wrong.”
> “That pattern is harmful.”
> “This person should not be trusted with that responsibility right now.”
> “I need to set a boundary.”
> “Justice requires repair.”

Discernment is not naïve. It does not pretend evil is good or danger is harmless. In Luke 6 itself, Jesus goes on to speak about blind guides, hypocrisy, good and bad fruit, and the need to remove the log from one’s own eye before addressing the speck in another’s. That assumes moral perception. He does not say, “There is no speck.” He says, in effect, “Do not look at another’s speck with a log in your own eye.”

**Condemnation**, by contrast, is not merely seeing wrong. It is the inward movement that says:

> “I now know what this person is.”
> “They are beneath mercy.”
> “Their fault gives me the right to despise them.”
> “I am clean because they are guilty.”

Condemnation turns moral perception into self-exaltation. It does not merely identify sin; it feeds on the sinner’s disgrace.

## The difference is partly in the goal

Discernment aims at truth, healing, protection, repentance, and restoration where possible.

Condemnation aims at finality. It wants the case closed. It wants the person reduced to the worst thing they have done. It wants not merely justice, but the satisfaction of seeing another person lowered.

A parent discerning a child’s lie may correct the child. A friend discerning betrayal may confront it. A community discerning abuse may remove a person from power. None of that is forbidden by “judge not.” In some cases, refusing to discern would be cowardice masquerading as mercy.

But one can do those necessary things without hatred, without relish, and without pretending to occupy God’s throne.

## Jesus forbids the appetite to condemn, not the duty to tell the truth

The appetite to condemn has several marks.

It is **self-exempting**. It sees another person’s sin with perfect clarity and one’s own with remarkable tenderness. That is why Jesus’ image of the log and the speck is so devastating. Condemnation often has excellent eyesight outward and blindness inward.

It is **disproportionate**. It takes a real fault and makes it the whole person. Discernment says, “You lied.” Condemnation says, “You are nothing but a liar.” Discernment names guilt; condemnation erases personhood.

It is **merciless**. It may use moral language, but it secretly hopes the other person does not change, because their guilt has become useful. Their failure gives the condemning person a feeling of righteousness.

It is **final**. It speaks as though no appeal is possible, no repentance meaningful, no grace relevant. In Christian terms, that is dangerous because final judgment belongs to God.

## Forgiveness does not mean moral indifference

“Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven” does not mean, “Call evil good.” Forgiveness requires enough moral clarity to know that a debt, wound, or offense exists.

Forgiveness means refusing to let vengeance, contempt, or the desire for another’s damnation govern the heart. It releases the claim to be the final judge. It may still name the wrong. It may still require restitution. It may still keep distance from an unsafe person. Forgiveness is not the same as automatic trust, instant reconciliation, or the removal of consequences.

So the opposite of condemnation is not permissiveness. The opposite of condemnation is **merciful truthfulness**.

## Moral seriousness without moral vanity

There is a false humility that says, “Who am I to judge?” when what it really means is, “I do not want the burden of moral courage.” Jesus is not endorsing that.

But there is also a false righteousness that says, “I am only standing for truth,” while secretly enjoying another person’s exposure. Jesus is confronting that.

The disciple must learn to say, with trembling honesty:

> “This is wrong, and I too stand in need of mercy.”
> “This must be addressed, but I will not despise the person.”
> “Justice matters, but vengeance must not possess me.”
> “I can discern fruit without pretending to be the Lord of the harvest.”

That is the heart of the distinction.

**Discernment is moral vision under humility. Condemnation is moral vision corrupted by pride.**

Luke 6:37 does not make morality meaningless. It makes morality more demanding, because it judges not only our verdicts about others, but the spirit in which we deliver them.
