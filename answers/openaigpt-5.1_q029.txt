Paul is describing a *very specific* moral posture for correcting someone:  

1. **Restorative, not punitive**  
   - The verb “restore” (Greek *katartízete*) is used for mending nets or setting a broken bone.  
   - The aim is not to expose, shame, win an argument, or “be right,” but to **mend** the person and bring them back to spiritual health and fellowship.  
   - So, correction must be guided by: “How can this person be healed and brought back?” rather than “How can I prove they’re wrong?”

2. **Meekness (gentleness) as the manner**  
   - “In the spirit of meekness” (*pneúmati praütētos*) means with **gentleness, humility, and restraint**, not harshness or superiority.  
   - Meekness is a fruit of the Spirit in the preceding chapter (Gal 5:23), so this is not weak tolerance but Spirit-shaped strength under control.  
   - The corrector recognizes:
     - “This could be me.”
     - “This is a brother/sister, not an enemy.”
     - “My tone and approach must make repentance easier, not harder.”

3. **Self-suspicion / self-watchfulness as protection**  
   - “Considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted” (*skopōn seauton*) means actively watching yourself:
     - your motives (am I correcting out of love or out of ego?),
     - your vulnerabilities (am I prone to a related sin?),
     - your heart (am I starting to feel superior?).  
   - Paul is realistic: the very act of correcting someone can tempt you—  
     - to pride (“I would never do that”),  
     - to the same sin,  
     - or to hardness and lack of mercy.

### Why are restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion held together?

Because each one guards the others:

- **Restoration** gives the *goal*:  
  If the goal is healing, you *must* be gentle. Harshness breaks what you’re trying to mend.
  
- **Meekness** gives the *manner*:  
  Gentleness flows naturally from knowing you are a fellow sinner. It keeps correction from becoming dominance or humiliation.

- **Self-suspicion** gives the *sobriety*:  
  Awareness of your own weakness prevents:
  - triumphalism (“I’m the spiritual one, you’re the failure”),
  - hypocrisy (judging others for what you wink at in yourself),
  - and moral carelessness (“I can’t fall in this area”).

Taken together, Paul is saying:

- Only those **walking by the Spirit** (“ye which are spiritual”) should attempt this.  
- When they do, they must aim at **restoration**, use **gentleness**, and remain **afraid of their own capacity to fall**.

This threefold posture—restorative aim, meek manner, and self-distrusting heart—is what keeps correction from becoming either cruelty on one side or cowardly silence on the other.
