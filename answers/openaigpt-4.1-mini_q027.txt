This parable in Luke 18:9–14 is rich with spiritual and moral teaching, and the condemnation of the Pharisee hinges on several interconnected attitudes, while the tax collector’s posture exemplifies true humility and repentance.

### What condemns the Pharisee?

1. **Pride and Self-Righteousness:**  
   The Pharisee’s primary error is pride—his confidence in his own righteousness. He “trusted in himself that he was righteous” and boasts about his fasting and tithes. His prayer is more a self-congratulation than a seeking of God’s mercy.

2. **Comparison with Others:**  
   The Pharisee not only trusts in his own virtue but *compares* himself to others negatively, especially the tax collector. He exalts himself by putting others down, saying he is not “as other men are.”

3. **Contempt for Others:**  
   The Pharisee despises the tax collector, whom he labels a sinner. This contempt is part of his flawed attitude—he judges others harshly instead of recognizing his own need for grace.

4. **The Use of Virtue as a Mask or Display:**  
   While the Pharisee’s virtues (fasting twice a week, tithing all possessions) might be genuine practices, here they are employed as a way to display righteousness and gain self-validation rather than to humbly seek God.

5. **False Spiritual Posture:**  
   Although not necessarily false doctrine, his attitude reflects a false spiritual stance—reliance on external works and self-approval rather than on internal repentance and God’s mercy.

### Why is the tax collector’s posture treated as spiritually truer?

1. **Humility:**  
   The tax collector stands “afar off” and does not even lift his eyes heavenward, signaling a profound sense of unworthiness before God.

2. **Repentance and Sorrow:**  
   He “smote upon his breast,” a traditional sign of remorse and confession of sin.

3. **Recognition of Personal Sinfulness:**  
   The tax collector acknowledges he is a sinner and appeals directly to God’s mercy: “God be merciful to me a sinner.”

4. **Dependence on God’s Mercy, Not Works:**  
   His prayer reveals dependence not on his deeds—but on God’s grace and forgiveness.

### Summary:

- The Pharisee’s condemnation lies primarily in **pride, self-righteousness, contempt for others, and using virtue for self-display**.
- The tax collector’s prayer and posture reflect **true humility, penitence, and faith in God's mercy**.
  
Jesus concludes, “I tell you, this man went down to his house justified rather than the other,” emphasizing that justification before God comes **not from self-justification or external rituals, but from humble dependence on God’s mercy**.
