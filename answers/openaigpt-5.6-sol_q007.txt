Luke 6:37 does not say that good and evil are indistinguishable or that conduct should never be evaluated. It addresses the **spirit, purpose, and claimed authority** with which we judge.

### Discernment

Discernment asks:

- Is this action truthful, just, loving, or harmful?
- What response would protect people and promote repentance or repair?
- Am I applying the same standard to myself?
- Do I know enough to reach this conclusion?

Jesus himself distinguishes good fruit from bad fruit later in the chapter (Luke 6:43–45), and elsewhere he teaches that a person may need to be rebuked and then forgiven (Luke 17:3–4). Rebuke requires moral discernment. Forgiveness does not mean pretending that no wrong occurred; it means refusing vengeance and releasing the personal debt.

Healthy discernment can therefore name wrongdoing, establish boundaries, protect victims, impose proportionate consequences, and seek restoration.

### The appetite to condemn

Condemnation goes beyond identifying an act as wrong. It treats the offender as someone to be dismissed, despised, or reduced to the offense. It often:

- assumes knowledge of another person’s motives or final standing before God,
- applies standards harshly to others but gently to oneself,
- takes satisfaction in another’s humiliation or punishment,
- closes the door to repentance and restoration,
- confuses accountability with vengeance.

The surrounding context is crucial: “Be ye therefore merciful, as your Father also is merciful” (Luke 6:36), followed by the warning that “with the same measure” one uses, it will be measured back (6:38), and then the image of the plank and the speck (6:41–42). Jesus is confronting **merciless, hypocritical, self-exalting judgment**, not abolishing moral perception.

A useful distinction is:

> **Discernment says, “This is wrong, and it must be addressed.”**  
> **Condemnation says, “Because you did wrong, you are beneath mercy.”**

The test is not merely whether we make a judgment, but whether we make it humbly, truthfully, consistently, and with a desire for protection, repentance, and restoration rather than superiority or revenge.
