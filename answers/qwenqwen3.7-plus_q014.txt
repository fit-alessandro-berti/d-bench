This is a profound question that cuts to the core of human psychology and spiritual formation. To answer it, we must look at both the literal grammar of the text and the broader theological and psychological mechanics Jesus is describing. 

Here is an analysis of how this text addresses both revealing and training, and how possession, attention, and love are inextricably linked.

---

### 1. Does it reveal the heart, or train the heart?

**The short answer is: It primarily *reveals* the heart in its immediate statement, but contextually, it describes a mechanism that *trains* the heart.** It is a self-reinforcing feedback loop.

**How it Reveals (The Diagnostic View):**
Grammatically, the statement *"For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also"* is a diagnostic observation. Jesus is giving us a diagnostic tool to examine ourselves and others. The "heart" (the invisible inner world of desires, devotion, and will) is hidden. However, "treasure" (the visible allocation of our resources, time, and energy) is a tangible, observable reality. By looking at a person's treasure, you can accurately deduce the true allegiance of their heart. In this sense, treasure is the *evidence* of the heart's current state.

**How it Trains (The Formative View):**
While the text does not explicitly state the reverse ("where your heart is, your treasure will follow"), the surrounding context strongly implies it. The verbs "lay not up" and "lay up" are present imperatives in Greek (*mē thēsaurizete* and *thēsaurizete*), which denote continuous, habitual action: "Stop continually hoarding..." and "Keep on storing up..." 

Spiritual formation operates on the principle of habituation: *we become what we repeatedly practice.* If you continually invest your resources in earthly things, you are actively training your heart to crave security, status, or comfort found in the material world. Conversely, if you habitually invest in "heavenly" things (generosity, justice, relationship with God), you are training your heart to find its satisfaction there. 

Therefore, treasure is both the *thermometer* (revealing the current temperature of the heart) and the *thermostat* (setting and shaping the future temperature of the heart).

---

### 2. How Possession, Attention, and Love Become Tied Together

Jesus weaves these three concepts into a tight, inseparable psychological chain. In ancient Hebrew and Greek thought, the "heart" (*kardia*) was not merely the seat of emotions, but the control center of the human person—the union of the will, the intellect, and affection. It is the seat of **love**.

Here is how the chain operates:

#### A. Possession (The Anchor)
Possession is the starting point of the physical reality Jesus addresses. When we acquire something, we naturally want to keep it. However, Jesus points out the inherent vulnerability of earthly possession: it is subject to *moth* (decay), *rust* (corrosion), and *thieves* (malice). Because earthly treasures are inherently insecure, they demand constant maintenance, protection, and defense.

#### B. Attention (The Conduit)
Because our possessions are vulnerable, our **attention** is hijacked by them. You cannot own something fragile without worrying about it. If your treasure is on earth, your mind is constantly drawn to calculating its value, protecting it from thieves, or figuring out how to grow it. Attention is a finite resource; whatever demands our attention consumes our mental and emotional bandwidth. Earthly treasures demand a defensive, anxious attention. Heavenly treasures, being incorruptible, free our attention from anxiety and allow it to focus on higher, eternal realities.

#### C. Love (The Destination)
In human psychology, we inevitably love what we give our sustained attention to. As the theologian David Foster Wallace noted, *"Whatever you worship, you will slowly become."* If your attention is perpetually locked onto earthly possessions, your devotion (your love) will transfer to those possessions. You will begin to derive your identity, security, and joy from them. 

Jesus is stating a law of human nature: **Your heart (your ultimate love and devotion) will gravitate toward the object of your sustained attention, which is dictated by what you possess.** 

---

### Synthesis: The Triangular Loop

The brilliance of this passage is that Jesus is not merely giving a moral command about money; He is exposing the mechanics of idolatry and worship. 

We tend to think the sequence operates like this: 
*I love God -> I pay attention to Him -> I give Him my resources.*

But Jesus warns that the sequence often operates in reverse, driven by habit:
*I possess earthly things -> My attention is consumed by protecting/enjoying them -> My heart (my deepest love and allegiance) becomes anchored to them.*

Therefore, Jesus’s command to "lay up treasures in heaven" is a practical hack for spiritual formation. He is essentially saying: *You cannot simply command your heart to love God more. But you can command your hands to give your resources to God's kingdom. And when you intentionally redirect your resources, your attention will follow, and inevitably, your heart will be trained to love what your hands are doing.* 

We are what we love, we love what we look at, and we look at what we hold. To change the heart, Jesus implies, start by moving the treasure.
