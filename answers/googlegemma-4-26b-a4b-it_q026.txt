This passage from Matthew 12 serves as a profound psychological and theological diagnosis of the human condition. It moves the locus of morality from outward behavior (compliance with laws) to the internal state (the condition of the soul).

To answer your question, the text connects speech to "hidden moral stockpiles" through a metaphor of **capacity and overflow**, and it suggests that words are indeed **symptoms, choices, and revelations** simultaneously.

### 1. The Connection: The Concept of "Abundance" and "Treasure"
The connection lies in the Greek concept of *perisseuma* (abundance/overflow) and the metaphor of *thesauros* (treasure/storehouse).

*   **The Storehouse Metaphor:** Jesus uses the word "treasure" (*thesauros*) to describe the heart. A treasure is not something visible on the surface; it is a collection of items stored away, accumulated over time. This implies that the "moral stockpile" is built through a lifetime of private thoughts, desires, and attitudes.
*   **The Overflow Principle:** The text suggests that the mouth does not function as a controlled valve, but as a spout. You cannot pour from an empty vessel, nor can you pour wine from a jar filled with vinegar. Speech is the "overflow" of what has been accumulated in the secret places of the heart. Therefore, speech is the inevitable leakage of the internal stockpile.

### 2. Are words Symptoms, Choices, or Revelations?

The passage suggests a complex interplay where all three categories are true, functioning in a chronological cycle of moral decay or growth.

#### **Words as Revelations (The Unmasking)**
In the immediate context, Jesus is using speech as a **revelation**. The Pharisees were speaking religious platitudes, but their words were poisoned by malice toward Jesus. 
*   The text argues that speech is the "tell" in a game of poker. One might attempt to mask their intentions through sophisticated language, but under pressure or in moments of passion, the true nature of the "stockpile" is revealed. Speech acts as a window that allows an observer to see the "hidden" treasure.

#### **Words as Symptoms (The Diagnostic)**
On a clinical or observational level, words are **symptoms**. Just as a cough is a symptom of a lung infection, "evil speech" is a symptom of an "evil heart." 
*   If a person consistently speaks with bitterness, deceit, or pride, the words are the outward sign of an underlying internal pathology. You do not fix the cough by silencing the mouth; you fix the cough by treating the lungs. Similarly, Jesus suggests that trying to fix speech through mere willpower is futile if the "treasure" in the heart remains corrupt.

#### **Words as Choices (The Participatory Element)**
While the passage emphasizes the organic link between heart and mouth, it implicitly involves **choice**. 
*   The "accumulation" process (the building of the treasure) is a series of choices. Every thought entertained and every private impulse indulged adds to the "stockpile." 
*   While the *outburst* might feel involuntary (an overflow), the *composition* of the heart is the result of a long-term series of volitional acts. We choose what we "store," and eventually, our speech becomes the inevitable expression of those accumulated choices.

### Summary
Matthew 12:34–35 posits a **causal chain**: 
1.  **Choices** build the "treasure" (the internal stockpile).
2.  The internal state becomes the "abundance" (the capacity).
3.  Speech becomes the **revelation** of that state and the **symptom** of that state's health.

In short: **We do not speak to express what we think; we speak because of who we have become.**
