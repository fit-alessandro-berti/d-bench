This is one of the most misunderstood verses in the New Testament. The verse is often interpreted as a call for moral relativism—as if Jesus was teaching that we cannot distinguish between good and bad, or that all actions are morally equivalent.

However, the context of the Sermon on the Plain (Luke 6) and the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 7) makes it clear: Jesus is not forbidding **truth-seeking**, but rather forbidding **hypocritical authority**.

Here is the exploration of the difference between moral discernment and the appetite to condemn, specifically regarding Luke 6:37.

### 1. The Context of the Pharisees
To understand what Jesus meant, we must look at Luke 6:41–42. The previously written command to "not judge" immediately precedes Jesus' famous illustration of the **beam and the speck**.
> *"First take the beam out of your eye, and then you will see clearly to take the speck out of your brother's eye."*

Not being able to judge means: "Do not get it on, then look for dirt elsewhere."
To the Pharisees, their moral authority was absolute, but their behavior was flawed. Jesus is criticizing their **hypocrisy**, not the moral reality of their offense.

*   **Moral Relativism:** "I cannot say that what you did is wrong. Your actions have no moral weight."
*   **Biblical Instruction:** "Your actions are sinful, but you cannot judge me because you are also sinful."

### 2. Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn
This is the crux of the distinction. There are two ways to navigate a situation involving human failure: **Moral Discernment** and **Punitive Condemnation**.

#### Discernment (Matter of Fact)
Discernment is the ability to perceive reality. It is the internal recognition of objective truth.
*   **Definition:** Recognizing that lying is deception, or that stealing is taking what belongs to another.
*   **Application:** Jesus condemned Pharisees for being like "whited sepulchres" (Matt 23:27). That was a moral judgment of bad. Jesus did not say, "You can't tell who is holy and who is not." He gave them grace for their own offer of grace.
*   **Luke 6:37 doesn't eliminate this. It challenges how you handle it.** You can see a sin, but you must not posture yourself as the punisher.

#### The Appetite to Condemn (Matter of Heart)
This refers to a spiritual reflex to label someone a "sinner," exclude them, or withhold mercy. It is a legalistic/vengeful posture.
*   **Definition:** The desire to claim authority over another human's soul so that you can absolve yourself of guilt.
*   **Application:** Condemnation assumes guilt is fixed by us: "I have the right to pronounce on you." This is where Luke 6:37 intervenes. It challenges the **sin of judgment** (assuming the role of God) rather than the **sin of reality** (the fact that actions are wrong).

### 3. Why the Difference Matters
If we conflate the two, we lose the Christian concept of justice.
If Jesus meant "Do not judge," we cannot warn people about evangelism, church discipline, or protecting the vulnerable.

**The difference lies in:**
*   **Objective vs. Subjective:** There is an objective reality that lies is wrong. (This is discernment). However, your reaction to it must be subjective: You cannot judge the liar's heart or severity at a vengeance. (This is condemnation).
*   **Discipline vs. Justice:** We see justice restored when the perpetrator sees that their actions have consequences. We see discipline in the community. But if we stop asking moral questions, judgment ends up being used as a weapon.
*   **Correction vs. Character Attack:** Moral discernment seeks to correct the *behavior* or *condition*. Condemnation attacks the *character* or *identity*.

### 4. The Reciprocity of Forgiveness
The final line of the verse provides the boundary.
> *"Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven."*

This implies that **abuse does not exist in the moral reality of love.**
*   We cannot forgive if we don't see that an offense occurred.
*   Therefore, moral judgment (seeing the problem) is necessary for forgiveness to have meaning.
*   The verse is a prohibition against the **cycle of reprisal.** If you judge someone harshly, you poison your own heart so you cannot forgive them. If you withhold forgiveness, you cut yourself off from the mercy you need.

### Summary
Luke 6:37 does not mean "Moral truth is relative." It means:
1.  **Do not think you own the law.** You should not assume you are the authority on what is right and wrong for others.
2.  **Do not judge character; encourage virtue.** Mercifully guide the person; do not condemn them with the name "sinner."
3.  **Do not play God.** Condemn is a legal act that God does. Forgiveness is a mercy God facilitates.

**The distinction:**
*   **Moral Judgment is necessary:** To identify sin, we must see it as evil. (Moral Reality).
*   **Moral Condemnation is forbidden:** To try to punish or define another worthy we should define a sinner against. (Moral Reality).

**Conclusion:**
The command is not to be **amoral** but to be **merciful**. To judge versus to condemn is the difference between saying, "This is wrong, and here is how we can fix it, because we are in love with the health of the person," and "This is wrong, and I will decide your fate."

Jesus calls for the former. He forbids the latter.
