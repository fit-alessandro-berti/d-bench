The scandal of the Pharisee’s prayer is not that he got his theology wrong on paper (he invokes God, thanks Him for fasting and tithing, even cites sins from which he is free) but that his whole heart-posture is twisted by self-righteousness.  Four facets of that self-righteousness stand out:

1. Pride and self-justification  
   – He “thanks” God that he is not like other men.  His confidence is in his own virtues rather than in God’s mercy.  
   – Scripture repeatedly warns that “God opposes the proud, but gives grace to the humble” (James 4:6).

2. Comparison and contempt  
   – He measures himself against “extortioners, unjust, adulterers, publicans,” treating them as spiritual inferiors.  
   – Jesus’ parable is a warning against judging others (cf. Luke 6:37; Romans 2:1).

3. Display of virtue  
   – His prayer is more a trophy speech (“I fast twice a week, I tithe all I possess”) than a dialogue of repentance or need.  
   – He uses obedience to the law to parade his righteousness before God and man, turning genuine duties into mere self-promotion.

4. Lack of true contrition and dependence  
   – He never utters “forgive me,” “create in me a clean heart,” or any plea for mercy.  
   – By contrast, the publican (tax collector) stands “afar off, would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven,” and beats his breast in brokenness: “God, be merciful to me a sinner.”

Why is the tax collector’s posture spiritually truer?  Because it embodies the very heart of the Gospel:

• Humble acknowledgement of sin (cf. Psalm 51)  
• Total dependence on God’s mercy rather than on personal performance (cf. Romans 3:20–28; Ephesians 2:8–9)  
• A heart-attitude that trusts grace, not works, for righteousness  

Jesus concludes: “I tell you, this man went down to his house justified rather than the other; for everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, and he who humbles himself will be exalted” (Luke 18:14).  In God’s economy, the only acceptable “merit” is humble faith in His mercy, not a catalogue of our own good deeds.
