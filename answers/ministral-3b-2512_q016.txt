The elder brother’s deepest problem in this parable is a **combination of envy, wounded fairness, and a distorted sense of justice**, but rooted ultimately in **pride and a refusal to embrace mercy**. Here’s a breakdown of the key tensions:

1. **Envy and Wounded Fairness**
   His anger stems from a sense of **unfairness**—he feels cheated by his father’s celebration of his younger brother’s return, even though the younger brother had squandered the inheritance. His complaint ("*I served thee... and yet thou never gavest me a kid*") reveals resentment over what he perceives as neglect or favoritism. He assumes his hard work and loyalty should have been rewarded with the same generosity as his brother’s return.

2. **Distorted Justice**
   He clings to a **rigid moralism**—his obedience was "perfect" by his own standards, but his father’s mercy (killing the fatted calf for the prodigal) seems arbitrary to him. His demand for fairness (*"thou never gavest me a kid"*) reflects a **legalistic view of justice** where rewards must be strictly proportional to effort.

3. **Pride in Obedience (and Its Limits)**
   His pride is not just in his work but in his **self-righteousness**—he assumes his father’s love is earned through his own merit, not received as a gift. His refusal to enter the house ("*I will not go in*") shows a **rejection of the father’s grace**, even when it’s offered.

4. **Discomfort with Mercy Itself**
   The deeper issue is that he **cannot accept mercy as a gift**—it must be earned, and his brother’s return disrupts his sense of order. The father’s words (*"Son, thou art ever with me"*) expose his unspoken fear: that his brother’s return would make him feel **less valued** or **less deserving**. Mercy, to him, is a threat to his self-importance.

**The Core Problem:**
The elder brother’s anger is not just about the younger brother’s return but about **his own inability to receive the father’s love as freely as his brother did**. His pride blinds him to the fact that the father’s joy is not about the younger brother’s repentance but about **restoring the relationship**—and the elder brother’s refusal to participate in that joy reveals his **unwillingness to be part of the family’s reconciliation**.

In short: **He is stuck in a cycle of resentment and self-righteousness**, unable to see that his father’s love is not conditional on his performance but a gift meant for *all* his children—even the one who had been lost.
