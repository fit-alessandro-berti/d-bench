This passage presents a profound and integrated view where words function as **symptoms, choices, and revelations simultaneously**, all rooted in the concept of a hidden moral treasury.

Here’s how the connection works:

### 1. **Words as *Revelations* of the Heart’s Treasury**
The core metaphor is that the heart is a storeroom or treasury (“good treasure” / “evil treasure”). Speech is the **overflow** from that treasury—“out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh.”  
- Just as a storehouse reveals its contents when opened, speech reveals what is stored within.  
- Words are therefore **diagnostic**: they make visible the hidden moral and spiritual condition. Jesus’ sharp address (“generation of vipers”) itself exemplifies this—he identifies their speech as a revelation of their corrupted inner state.

### 2. **Words as *Symptoms* of an Already-Formed Character**
In this framework, speech is not the primary problem but a **symptom** of what is already stockpiled.  
- A person doesn’t become a “viper” by speaking evil; rather, they speak evil *because* the heart’s treasury is already filled with evil.  
- The emphasis is on the causal direction: the inner abundance *produces* the outer expression. Like a tree bearing fruit according to its nature (a theme Jesus expands in Matthew 7:17–18), speech is the natural yield of the heart’s rooted condition.

### 3. **Words as *Choices* That Flow from the Heart**
While speech is an overflow, it is also a **choice**—but a choice deeply constrained by what is stored within.  
- The “good man” brings forth good things *from* his good treasure. The act of speaking is a volitional outflow, but the *quality* of what is brought forth is predetermined by the heart’s contents.  
- This suggests moral responsibility: we are accountable for our words because they truthfully express our inner moral stockpile. Yet it also implies that lasting change in speech requires a transformation of the heart’s treasury itself—a renewal of what we cherish and store up.

### **Synthesis: All Three Intertwined**
The passage does not present these as separate categories but as layers of one reality:
- **Revelation**: Words *uncover* the heart’s true state.
- **Symptom**: Words *result from* the heart’s accumulated moral “stock.”
- **Choice**: Words are the *active expression* of what the heart values and produces.

Thus, Jesus is teaching that speech is never superficial—it is the audible fruit of a hidden root system. To change the fruit, one must change the tree. To speak good, one must first *store* good in the heart.

This aligns with broader biblical wisdom (e.g., Proverbs 4:23, “Keep your heart with all vigilance, for from it flow the springs of life”) and Jesus’ later teaching that “a tree is known by its fruit” (Matthew 12:33). The moral life is integrated: inner treasury and outward expression are inseparable.
