Amos 5:21–24 is a pivotal chapter of the Book of Amos, which prophetically challenges the religious practices and moral integrity of Israel during its period of prosperity before its eventual downfall. While specific translation variances exist regarding exact wording, the text provided in your inquiry clearly points to the core prophetic warnings of the book: the rejection of empty ritualism in favor of justice and authentic righteousness.

### 1. Why Rejection Instead of Balance?
When Amos rejects the "feast days" and sacrifices so sharply that he says he "hates" them, rather than asking the people to give "better balance," it is because he believes **humans cannot truly satisfy God through human effort.**

*   **Inalienable Sovereignty:** The passage suggests that God does not need the perfect rituals of Israel; He needs a surrendered heart and broken covenant. Worship in the Amos prophetic context (750–740 BC) was often performative—people wore religious masks, kept ritual purity, but lived in moral corruption. Because the heart was still wicked, the sacrifices were merely "noise" that masked deeper spiritual blindness.
*   **The Ineffectiveness of Performance:** Amos argues that God is not bound by the rules He might give humanity (such as offering specific amounts of wine or money). If people are corrupt or unjust in their social treatment even while offering food and drink, the offerings cannot cover up the corruption. The "balance" of a human system is insufficient to cover the moral gap caused by the sinfulness of the people.
*   **Rejection of Legalism:** Amos criticizes the legalistic view that religious law can justify human sin. He believes this is a trap: if worship is merely performative, it becomes a "mask" for sin rather than an act of genuine piety. Therefore, He must reject the *legal system* entirely and demand that only true righteousness matter.

### 2. What Moral or Spiritual Fraud Is Being Exposed?
The rejection described in these verses exposes specific **frauds**:

*   **Ritualism (Legalistic Hypocrisy):** The fraud here is prioritizing "empty ceremonies" over the heart. Amos exposes the lie that rituals are enough to earn favor with God. This is a system of "sowing and reaping," where people take what they can get from religion rather than give the truth.
*   **Self-Righteousness:** The passage reveals people who think they are holy while practicing injustice. They offer sacrifices but do not live in peace; they worship while oppressing. Amos calls out this hypocrisy, exposing that the "balance" is false because it does not align with justice.
*   **The Performance Trap:** People pretend to worship ("take away the noise of thy songs"), but their faith is empty. They present a facade of piety where only "noise" exists but no substance. This fraudulent behavior leads to spiritual stagnation rather than true renewal.

### 3. The Prophetic Goal: Justice as a Stream
The final verse of the passage shifts focus from the rejection of ritual to its replacement with truth: **"But let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream."**

This serves as the **ethical counter-point**:
*   **True Restoration:** Instead of just cleaning off the temple, the people must be cleansed morally. The prophet demands that "justice flow"—meaning the system itself is to change how the economy and society function so that sin cannot fester.
*   **The Solution to Fraud:** If God rejects the feast days, it implies the only solution is not more food offering, but a new covenant based on justice and mercy.

### Summary
Amos 5:21–24 critiques religious systems that rely on external ritual without internal reform. It exposes people who worship while still committing murder or injustice as **"empty vessels"**. The prophet argues that the only acceptable response is **Judgment** (justice) and **Justice-flowing water** (truth/righteousness), which are far more effective than "noise" and "sacrifice."
