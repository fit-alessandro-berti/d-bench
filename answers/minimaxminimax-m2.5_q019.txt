The passage in James 3:9–10 exposes a profound **inner contradiction** between the object of worship and the treatment of one’s fellow human beings. Here’s a breakdown of the contradiction and why speech reveals moral disorder so quickly:

### The Inner Contradiction Exposed

1. **The same source, opposite actions**:  
   The tongue is used to *bless* God (the highest act of worship and reverence) and to *curse* human beings, who are created “in the likeness of God” (Genesis 1:27). This reveals a terrifying inconsistency: how can one who claims to reverence God turn around and degrade what God has made in His image?

2. **Worship and contempt from one mouth**:  
   James highlights that out of the *same mouth* come blessing and cursing. This isn’t just a behavioral issue—it points to a disordered heart. True worship should transform how we see and treat others. If blessing God doesn’t overflow into love for people, the “blessing” is hollow or self-serving.

3. **The moral disorder behind it**:  
   The contradiction isn’t merely about *what* we say, but *what* is in the heart. Jesus taught that defiling words come from the heart (Matthew 15:18–20). Cursing others reveals pride, anger, bitterness, or a lack of love—attitudes completely at odds with the humility and love required for genuine worship of God.

### Why Speech Reveals Moral Disorder So Quickly

1. **Speech is a direct overflow of the heart**:  
   James compares the tongue to a small rudder steering a large ship (James 3:4) or a small fire igniting a forest (James 3:5). Speech is a voluntary act that often bypasses careful filtering, especially in moments of emotion. It quickly exposes what’s really inside—whether that’s faith working through love (Galatians 5:6) or corruption.

2. **It’s a visible, tangible evidence**:  
   While inner attitudes can be hidden, words are heard and recorded. They are the fruit that reveals the tree’s condition (Matthew 12:33–37). Speech is a **testable evidence** of spiritual health.

3. **The tongue is “a world of evil” (James 3:6)**:  
   James doesn’t trivializes speech; he calls it a source of chaos, stained with poison, and capable of corrupting the whole person. Because it’s so powerful, it becomes a **ready indicator** of internal moral disorder.

4. **It affects others directly**:  
   Unlike internal thoughts, speech impacts other people immediately. To curse someone made in God’s image is to strike at God’s creation—and thus at God Himself. This is why James says “these things ought not so to be”: it’s an affront to God’s design and a violation of love.

### Theological and Practical Implication

James is not merely addressing rough language; he’s exposing **hypocrisy in the Christian life**. True saving faith transforms the heart, and the transformed heart produces life-giving speech. If blessing God doesn’t lead to blessing others, something is fundamentally wrong. The passage calls for **consistency**—a heart so aligned with God that our speech consistently reflects His love, humility, and grace.

In short, the contradiction is this: **you cannot genuinely honor God while dishonoring His image-bearers**, and your speech is the quickest revealer of whether your heart truly honors either.
