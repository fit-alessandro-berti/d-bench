Paul’s pairing of “bitterness” with “forgiving one another” is deliberate: he is not merely talking about a passing feeling but about an inward disposition that shows up outwardly and undermines Christian community. A few points to keep in mind:

- The words. The Greek pikria (bitterness) normally denotes a sour, acrid bitterness of spirit — a settled resentment or acrimony — not just a fleeting irritation. Paul puts it in a list with wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, and malice, all of which point to inner attitudes that erupt into destructive behavior.

- Put off / put on language. Paul uses the verb apothēsesthe (“put away”/“put off”), the same vocabulary he uses elsewhere for removing garments or sinful habits and taking on a new way of life. That shows he treats bitterness as something you must intentionally discard — a chosen posture or habit you can and must resist.

- Bitterness is both inward and contagious. It is primarily an inner habit of resentment, but Paul’s list shows its social effects (shouting, slander, malice). Hebrews 12:15 uses the image of a “root of bitterness” that can spring up and defile many. In short, bitterness is an inward state with outward, contagious consequences.

- Forgiveness is the positive alternative. Paul doesn’t just forbid bitterness; he commands kindness, compassion, and forgiveness — character traits rooted in God’s own forgiveness “in Christ.” Forgiveness here is an active, practiced stance, modeled on God’s action toward us, that heals relationships and counters the spread of resentment.

So the text treats bitterness as more than a momentary emotion: it’s a pernicious, adoptable posture (and one that spreads) that must be deliberately set aside and replaced by the chosen practice of forgiving, compassionate living grounded in God’s grace.
