Based on the text of Matthew 6:1 and its broader context within the Sermon on the Mount, the moral danger lies not in **visibility itself**, but in the **craving to be seen** (the motive).

Here is an analysis of why the text points to intention rather than location as the moral hazard:

### 1. The Grammatical Modifier: "To Be Seen"
The crucial phrase in the verse is "to be seen of them" (Greek: *theōrhthēnai*). Grammatically, this is an infinitive of purpose. It modifies *why* the alms are being done, not *where*.

Jesus is not prohibiting acts of charity that happen to be witnessed; He is prohibiting acts of charity performed *for the purpose of* being witnessed. If the act is done in public but the intent is to help the poor or glorify God, it does not violate this specific command. The danger arises when the act becomes a performance designed to secure social capital or reputation.

### 2. The Contextual Tension: Matthew 5:16
If visibility were inherently morally dangerous, it would contradict Jesus' teaching earlier in the same sermon. In Matthew 5:16, He commands:
> "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."

Here, Jesus explicitly desires that good works be visible. The distinction between 5:16 and 6:1 is the **recipient of the glory**:
*   **Matthew 5:16:** Visible works $\rightarrow$ People see works $\rightarrow$ People glorify **God**.
*   **Matthew 6:1:** Visible works $\rightarrow$ People see works $\rightarrow$ People glorify **the doer**.

Therefore, visibility is neutral; it is the *teleological goal* (the end purpose) that determines the morality of the act.

### 3. The Economy of Reward
The verse concludes with a transactional warning: "otherwise ye have no reward of your Father." This suggests a spiritual economy where rewards are finite based on intent.

*   **The Reward of Men:** If you give to be seen, you receive the "reward" of human applause, status, and recognition.
*   **The Reward of God:** If you seek the reward of men, you cannot simultaneously claim the reward of God for that same act.

The moral danger is that the "craving to be seen" turns a spiritual act of love into a commercial transaction with society. By cashing in on human praise, the actor exhausts the spiritual credit for the deed. The danger is not that God cannot see public acts (He sees everything), but that the actor has already been paid in full by the audience, leaving no room for divine approval.

### Conclusion
The problem is **the craving to be seen**.

Public goodness becomes morally dangerous in this verse only when it is instrumentalized for self-glorification. The text condemns the **hypocrisy** of using piety as a tool for social advancement, rather than the **location** of the piety itself. The "reward" is withheld not because God is jealous of public acts, but because the actor has already accepted a different currency (human praise) as their payment.
