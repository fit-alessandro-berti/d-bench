Matthew 12:34–35 connects speech to the “heart” by using two overlapping images:

1. **Overflow:** “Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh.”  
   The mouth does not speak in isolation. Words spill out from what fills the inner person—desires, convictions, loyalties, resentments, faith, unbelief.

2. **Treasure/storehouse:** “A good man out of the good treasure…bringeth forth good things.”  
   The heart is pictured as a hidden storehouse. What has been stored there morally and spiritually becomes the material from which speech is produced.

In context, Jesus is rebuking the Pharisees after they attributed His Spirit-empowered work to Beelzebul. Their words were not a small verbal mistake; they revealed a deeper moral hostility. Their speech exposed the condition of their hearts.

So yes, the passage treats words as **symptoms, choices, and revelations**.

### 1. Words are symptoms

Speech indicates the condition of the heart, like fruit indicates the condition of a tree. A diseased tree produces bad fruit; an evil heart produces evil speech. Words are outward signs of inward reality.

Jesus is saying, in effect: *Your accusation shows what is inside you.*

### 2. Words are choices

The phrase “bringeth forth” implies action. A person is not merely a passive pipe through which words flow. One “brings out” from the heart’s treasury. That is why the following verses say people will give account for their words.

So speech reveals the heart, but it is still morally responsible speech.

### 3. Words are revelations

Words uncover what was hidden. The “treasure” is inside; speech brings it into the open. The mouth makes visible the invisible moral contents of the person.

This does not mean every stray word reveals the whole person equally, or that people cannot speak hypocritically for a time. But Jesus’ point is that over time, and especially under moral pressure, speech discloses the ruling condition of the heart.

In short: **words are chosen expressions that reveal the heart’s stored-up moral condition.** They are symptoms of what fills us, actions for which we are accountable, and disclosures of what we truly treasure.
