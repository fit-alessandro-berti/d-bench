The condemnation of the Pharisee in Luke 18:9–14 centers on **false doctrine** and **pride**, which overshadow his other virtues as means to self-display. Here’s why:

### **False Doctrine**
The Pharisee's statement about his practices is explicitly incorrect. Jesus challenges him by pointing out that, despite his actions (fasting, tithes), the Pharisee’s prayer and self-perception are **naïve or incomplete**. The Pharisee views himself as righteous due to his observance of ritualistic rules unduly emphasizing his moral superiority over others, rather than through genuine repentance or faith in God’s grace (e.g., mercy and salvation).

### **Pride**
The text explicitly labels the Pharisee as a *Pharisee, who stood and prayed thus*—a term often used to denote those who pride themselves on their spiritual achievements and seek to appear virtuous through adherence to legalistic or formal religious practices. This pride is demonstrated by two key actions:
1. **Comparing himself unfavorably to others**: The Pharisee claims to be better than the publican not because he is intrinsically worse, but because he perceives the publican to be more evil or unfoxed (even sinners need redemption)—highlighting his own moral superiority as the benchmark.
2. ** Ultra-conservative self-display**: He presents himself as righteous not through repentance or faith, but through his observance of rituals (fasting, tithes). This isolates him from genuine, inward faith, creating a false sense of piety.

### **Why the Tax Collector's Posture is Treatied as Spiritually Truer**
Jesus refuses to hold the same judgmental standard for the Pharisee. Instead, he identifies the tax collector as morally upright despite his physical separation from the market. This contrast underscores the **use of virtue as self-display** and the ineffectiveness of this approach. The tax collector’s posture—a humble, tactile commitment to justice and truthfulness—represents genuine, humble faith, while the Pharisee’s actions reflect false doctrine and pride. The publican’s posture, though outwardly contrite, is deceptively prideful; the tax collector’s is truly spiritually sincere.

### **Extended Analysis**
The text’s critique of the Pharisee’s false doctrine and pride invites readers to reflect on how self-display can lead to hypocrisy. Just as the Pharisee’s rituals appear credible but fail to redeem him, people can adopt virtues for self-aggrandizement rather than genuine transformation. This underscores Jesus’ message that true holiness stems from authentic faith and humility, not from outward ritual or pride. The tax collector’s posture, in contrast, reflects authentic spiritual posture, making it a model for those seeking real justice and mercy.
