# On the Difficulty of Taking Enemy-Love Seriously

Your question cuts to why this teaching gets dismissed or spiritualized away. Let me consider each possibility:

## Why it seems sentimental
- It *sounds* like it demands we suppress legitimate anger or pretend injustice doesn't matter
- Without careful thought, it can appear to ask: "feel warm feelings toward your oppressor"
- This makes it easy to dismiss as naive idealism

But the text says "pray for them which despitefully use you"—*not* "pretend they haven't hurt you." The difficulty might be real, not imaginary sentiment.

## Why it seems strategic
- There's an intuitive appeal: enemies respond better to kindness, de-escalation works, etc.
- But this makes enemy-love *instrumental*—valuable only if it produces results
- The text offers no guarantee it will succeed, which undermines a purely strategic reading

## The deeper possibility
What if the difficulty points to something else: that enemy-love is about **who you become**, not mainly about *them*?

The passage suggests this: "that ye may be the children of your Father." The outcome promised isn't that enemies convert—it's spiritual transformation in *you*. The indiscriminate sun and rain suggest a kind of moral seriousness that doesn't require enemies to deserve it.

**The hardest part might be**: accepting that loving your enemy doesn't require pretending they're good, doesn't guarantee it'll work, *and* doesn't exempt you from resistance or justice—it just refuses to let their cruelty determine your character.

What draws or repels you about that framing?
