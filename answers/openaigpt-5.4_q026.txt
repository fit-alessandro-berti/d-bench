Matthew 12:34–35 presents speech as the **visible overflow of an invisible inner store**. Jesus links the mouth to the heart so tightly that what a person says is not random; it comes from what has been accumulated within.

## The basic image
He uses the idea of **treasure** or stored stock:
- a **good person** has “good treasure” in the heart and brings out good things
- an **evil person** has “evil treasure” and brings out evil things

So the heart is pictured like a **hidden reservoir**, **storehouse**, or **stockpile**, and speech is what gets drawn from it.

## How the passage connects speech and the heart
“Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh” means:
- speech is not merely surface behavior
- words arise from what fills the inner life
- repeated speech reveals settled moral character

Jesus is saying that the mouth is a **window into the heart**. If the heart is full of malice, pride, deceit, or unbelief, those things will eventually show up in speech. If it is full of goodness, truth, mercy, and faith, that too will be heard.

## Are words symptoms, choices, revelations, or all three?
**All three.**

### 1. Words are symptoms
They are symptoms of what is inside. Just as fruit reveals the nature of a tree, words reveal the contents of the heart. Speech exposes underlying attitudes and affections.

### 2. Words are choices
Jesus still treats people as morally responsible for what they say. The broader context includes accountability for words, so speech is not excused as involuntary leakage. What comes from the heart still passes through the will and is therefore blameworthy or praiseworthy.

### 3. Words are revelations
Speech reveals hidden moral reality. The “treasure” is internal and unseen, but words make it known. In that sense, speech functions like evidence: it uncovers what sort of person one is becoming and what one truly values.

## An important nuance
This does **not** mean every isolated statement perfectly defines a person’s entire character. People may misspeak, repent, or speak better or worse than usual. But Jesus’ point is that **habitual speech** is deeply diagnostic. Over time, the mouth tells the truth about the heart.

## The theological force of the passage
Jesus is pushing beyond mere outward morality. He is not satisfied with controlling speech at the surface level; he goes to the source. The issue is not just “say better things,” but “be made good within.” The stockpile must change if the output is to change.

So the passage teaches:
- the **heart stores** moral treasure
- the **mouth draws from that store**
- words are **symptoms** of inner condition
- words are **chosen acts** for which one is responsible
- words are **revelations** of hidden character

In short: **speech is the heart made audible**.
