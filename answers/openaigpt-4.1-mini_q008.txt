1 John 4:20 highlights a fundamental principle in Christian ethics: genuine love for God must be demonstrated through love for others, particularly those we can see and interact with. The verse treats love of the visible neighbor as a test of love of the invisible God because:

1. **Relationship with God is made concrete in relationships with others:** God is unseen, but His presence and character are reflected and revealed through how people treat one another. Loving God in an abstract, invisible sense disconnected from daily life is hollow if it does not affect one's behavior toward others.

2. **Love is practical and relational, not just theoretical:** True love is active and expressed in deeds. If someone claims to love God but hates their brother (someone they see and interact with), it reveals a contradiction. Genuine love for God will overflow into love for others because of the interconnectedness commanded by God's nature and teachings.

3. **Self-deception exposed:** The self-deception here is the claim that one loves God, yet harbors hatred or neglect for others. It is easy to profess love for an unseen deity as a matter of internal belief or ideology, while ignoring or even despising fellow humans. This exposes a form of hypocrisy or internal inconsistency — claiming a spiritual reality without ethical fruit.

In summary, the verse exposes the falsehood in claiming spiritual love without tangible expression. It shows that love for God is not merely a private feeling but something demonstrated through loving relationship with others, and that failure to do so means the person is deceiving themselves about the nature of their love.
