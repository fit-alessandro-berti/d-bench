### What makes the image so sharp?
Jesus’ picture is *deliberately exaggerated and visual*:

- **A “mote” vs. a “beam.”** A mote is tiny—almost trivial—while a beam is massive. The contrast makes the problem obvious in an instant.
- **The absurdity of the task.** The person can “see” their brother’s mote well enough to judge it, yet they have **an obstruction so large that it would normally prevent clear sight altogether**. The image functions like a mock “optical impossibility.”
- **The role-reversal of the would-be helper.** The hypocrite tries to perform surgery—“Let me pull out”—while standing in the middle of their own blindness. That mismatch is inherently shocking.
- **Moral logic stated as physical logic.** Sight becomes a metaphor for moral discernment: you don’t correct what you can’t honestly recognize in yourself.

So the sharpness comes from the way the illustration exposes *misplaced perception* and *performative moral meddling*.

---

### How does the passage criticize hypocrisy without forbidding moral correction?
The key is the sequence and the motive.

- Jesus does **not** say, “Don’t help your brother.”
- He says, **“First”** deal with the beam in *your own* eye—i.e., remove the hidden or tolerated wrongdoing that distorts your judgment.
- Once the beam is gone, the person can “see clearly” and then deal with the mote.

That means moral correction is legitimate **when it comes from clarity and integrity**, not from self-righteousness. The hypocrisy Jesus attacks is specifically:

1. **Self-exoneration:** ignoring one’s own serious faults while focusing on another’s minor ones.
2. **Judgment without self-check:** criticizing in a way that is really about moral superiority or exposure rather than restoration.
3. **Blocked vision:** giving counsel while still living in contradiction.

So the passage permits correction, but it demands **self-auditing first**—and it implies that correction without such transformation is spiritually counterfeit.
