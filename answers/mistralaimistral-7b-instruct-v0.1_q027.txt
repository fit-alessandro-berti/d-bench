 The Pharisee in this parable is condemned primarily for his pride and self-righteousness. He compares himself to others and boasts about his own virtues, such as fasting and giving tithes, in contrast to the sins of others. He looks down on the tax collector and despises him.

The tax collector, on the other hand, recognizes his own sinfulness and asks for mercy. He does not compare himself to others or boast about his own virtues. His posture of humility and repentance is treated as spiritually truer because he understands his need for God's mercy, while the Pharisee trusts in his own righteousness.

The use of virtue as self-display is related to the Pharisee's pride and self-righteousness. He is not truly seeking to please God with his virtuous actions, but rather using them as a way to elevate himself above others and to earn God's favor through his own efforts.

The parable does not directly address false doctrine or contempt, but these attitudes can be related to the Pharisee's self-righteousness and pride. He may hold false beliefs about salvation and how it can be obtained through his own efforts, and he looks down on others who do not share his beliefs or live up to his standards.
