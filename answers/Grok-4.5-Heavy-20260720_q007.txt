**Luke 6:37 does not teach that moral judgments never matter.** It teaches something far more precise and demanding: do not adopt a condemning, merciless, self-exalting posture toward others, because the same measure will be applied to you. The verse sits inside a larger call to mercy (“Be merciful, even as your Father is merciful,” v. 36), reciprocity of measure (v. 38), and the practical illustration of the log and speck (vv. 41–42).

### What the verse is *not* saying
A common modern reading treats “Judge not” as a blanket prohibition on moral evaluation—an endorsement of relativism or the claim that standards of right and wrong simply do not apply. That reading is textually and contextually impossible.

- Jesus and the rest of the New Testament repeatedly call for moral discernment: testing everything (1 Thess. 5:21), recognizing false teachers by their fruit (Matt. 7:15–20, in the parallel sermon), practicing church discipline (1 Cor. 5), and “judging with right judgment” (John 7:24).
- Jesus himself makes sharp moral judgments—against hypocrisy, exploitation, hard-heartedness, and unbelief—throughout the Gospels.
- The command to *forgive* presupposes that real wrongs exist. If moral judgments never mattered, forgiveness would be empty.

If the verse meant “moral judgments never matter,” the surrounding ethic of mercy, the call to remove the speck after dealing with one’s own log, and the entire moral vision of the Sermon on the Plain would collapse into incoherence.

### Discernment versus the appetite to condemn
The distinction is not between judging and not judging. It is between two different *kinds* of judgment, two different heart postures.

**Discernment** is the careful, truth-seeking evaluation of actions, character, ideas, and “fruit.” It is necessary for wisdom, self-protection, justice, and love. It asks: Is this true or false? Helpful or harmful? Aligned with what is good or destructive? Done with humility and self-awareness, discernment can lead to boundaries, correction offered in hope of restoration, or simply clear-eyed realism. It does not require contempt. The Greek *krinō* (used in “judge not”) can mean to distinguish, decide, or evaluate; Scripture elsewhere treats this capacity as a mark of maturity (Heb. 5:14).

**The appetite to condemn** is the eagerness to pass a final, damning verdict on a person—to play God, elevate oneself by lowering the other, and withhold mercy. The stronger verb *katadikazō* (“condemn not”) points more specifically to pronouncing guilty or sentencing. This posture is often hypocritical (the log in one’s own eye), censorious, and driven by pride or the desire to feel superior. It is the opposite of the merciful Father held up as the model in the preceding verse. It treats people as finished cases rather than as those still capable of change and still in need of the same grace one hopes to receive.

The parallel teaching in Matthew 7 makes the sequence explicit: first deal rigorously with your own blindness and faults; *then* you will see clearly enough to help with the speck. The goal of that help is restoration, not destruction. Condemnation short-circuits the process by declaring the other person already written off.

### Why the difference matters
“Moral judgments never matter” is a form of moral abdication. It leaves no ground on which to oppose genuine evil, protect the vulnerable, or pursue the good. The appetite to condemn, by contrast, is a form of moral inflation—using standards as weapons while exempting oneself. Luke 6:37 rejects both. It insists that moral reality is real and that our response to it must be shaped by the same mercy we hope to receive.

In practice this means we can (and must) name actions and patterns as wrong without needing to consign the person to permanent contempt. We can hold people accountable while remaining open to their repentance and our own. The measure we use—harsh or generous, condemning or merciful—will be the measure applied to us. That is not a threat that abolishes judgment; it is a summons to the kind of judgment that reflects the character of the Father who is merciful.
