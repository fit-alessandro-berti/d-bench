The passage is pessimistic about unaided human moral judgment, but its practical point is realism about self-deception—not that every thought or motive is wholly unknowable.

In context, Jeremiah contrasts trusting human strength with trusting the Lord (Jer. 17:5–8), after describing Judah’s sin as deeply engraved (17:1). The “heart” in biblical language is not merely emotion; it includes thought, desire, will, and moral purpose. “Desperately wicked” can also be translated “desperately sick” or “incurable.” The point is that the inner person is both morally distorted and difficult to diagnose.

Verse 10 answers the question, “Who can know it?”: **the Lord can.** God searches the heart and tests the “reins”—literally the kidneys, an ancient image for the deepest inner self. Yet God also judges “according to his ways” and “the fruit of his doings.” Inner motives matter, but they become visible, at least partly, through patterns of conduct and their results.

So the passage does not require saying, “No one can ever know why they do anything.” Rather, it warns against claiming infallible self-knowledge.

A responsible way to speak about motives would therefore be:

- **Use humility:** “As far as I can tell, my reason was…”
- **Allow for mixed motives:** generosity may include genuine love, desire for approval, and self-interest at once.
- **Compare stated motives with patterns and fruit:** repeated behavior may expose motives that self-description conceals.
- **Invite correction:** other people often see inconsistencies we miss.
- **Avoid pretending to know another person’s heart:** assess actions and effects without claiming God’s exhaustive knowledge.
- **Do not use uncertainty to evade responsibility:** “I cannot fully know my heart” does not mean “I am not accountable.”
- **Remain open to divine examination:** the fitting posture is like Psalm 139:23–24: “Search me, O God…”

Thus Jeremiah 17:9–10 is neither simple cynicism nor permission for endless suspicion. It gives a sober anthropology: people can sincerely misunderstand themselves, God knows them fully, and motives should be discussed provisionally, tested by conduct, and held open to correction.
