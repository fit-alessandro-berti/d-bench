Genesis 4:6–7 presents evil as **something external and yet intimately near**, but not as an excuse. The picture is of a person standing at a moral threshold, with a choice that is both real and urgent.

### What is evil here?

The text uses the vivid image:

- **“sin lieth at the door”** — as if sin were a crouching presence, waiting to enter.
- **“unto thee shall be his desire”** — this suggests sin is portrayed almost like a **predatory or intrusive force**, seeking mastery.
- **“thou shalt rule over him”** — humanity is not depicted as helpless before it.

So evil is not mainly described as:
- a mere **impulse**,
- a biological **instinct**,
- or a fixed **habit**.

Rather, it is presented as a **personified power/temptation**, something like a beast crouching nearby, ready to dominate if welcomed. That metaphor implies danger, pressure, and immediacy.

### What responsibility is placed on Cain?

The key point is that Cain is **not treated as morally passive**. God’s words assume:

1. **Cain is accountable for his state**  
   “Why art thou wroth?” and “why is thy countenance fallen?” suggest Cain’s inner condition is his concern and his responsibility.

2. **Cain has a real moral alternative**  
   “If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?” means the right path is still open.

3. **Cain must exercise rule over sin**  
   “thou shalt rule over him” implies dominion is possible and expected. The person is not asked merely to feel differently, but to govern the threatening moral power.

### Moral agency in the passage

The text draws a picture of **strong temptation but preserved freedom**. Sin is near, powerful, and hungry, but the human being is still addressed as a responsible agent who can resist, govern, and choose the good.

In short:

- **Evil** = a crouching, personified threat/temptation
- **Human being** = morally capable ruler, not helpless victim
- **Responsibility** = to master the threatening presence by doing well
