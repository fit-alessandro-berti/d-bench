The parable of the Pharisee and the publican (or tax collector) in Luke 18:9-14 is a rich and complex passage that offers insights into several spiritual attitudes and practices. To understand what condemns the Pharisee and why the tax collector's posture is considered spiritually truer, let's examine each of the elements you've listed: false doctrine, pride, comparison, contempt, and the use of virtue as self-display.

1. **False Doctrine**: The Pharisee's prayer doesn't directly indicate that he holds to false doctrine. His actions and words reflect a certain understanding of righteousness that is based on the law (fasting twice a week and giving tithes of all he possesses), which were practices associated with Pharisaic traditions. The issue isn't necessarily his beliefs about God or salvation but rather his attitude and how he perceives himself in relation to others.

2. **Pride**: The Pharisee's prayer is characterized by a sense of self-righteousness and pride. He thanks God that he is not like other men, listing various negative categories and specifically mentioning the publican. This pride is a significant element that contributes to his condemnation. His focus is on his own virtues rather than on God's grace or mercy.

3. **Comparison**: The Pharisee compares himself favorably to others, particularly the publican. He uses the publican as a benchmark to measure his own righteousness, highlighting his superiority. This comparative attitude is part of what Jesus critiques, as it suggests that the Pharisee's self-worth and righteousness are determined by how he stacks up against others.

4. **Contempt**: The Pharisee's comparison leads to contempt for others. By saying "or even as this publican," he demonstrates disdain for the publican and, by extension, for others he considers unrighteous. This contempt is a manifestation of his pride and further indicates his spiritual condition.

5. **The Use of Virtue as Self-Display**: The Pharisee's prayer highlights his religious practices (fasting and tithing) as evidence of his righteousness. While these actions might be seen as virtuous, the context suggests they are being used to bolster his self-image and to justify his superiority over others. This self-display is part of the problem, as it indicates that his motivation is not to honor God or genuinely follow His commandments but to elevate himself.

Now, considering the tax collector's posture:

- The tax collector stands afar off, indicating humility and a recognition of his unworthiness to approach God directly.
- He does not lift his eyes to heaven, showing a lack of confidence or boldness in his own righteousness.
- He smites his breast, a gesture of sorrow and repentance.
- His prayer, "God be merciful to me a sinner," reflects an acknowledgment of his sinful state and a reliance on God's mercy.

The tax collector's posture is treated as spiritually truer for several reasons:
- **Humility**: He recognizes his status as a sinner.
- **Reliance on Mercy**: He looks to God's mercy rather than his own actions for salvation.
- **Repentance**: His actions and prayer indicate a genuine sorrow for his sins.

In essence, what condemns the Pharisee is a combination of **pride**, **comparison**, **contempt**, and **the use of virtue as self-display**. The root issue is his pride and failure to recognize his need for God's mercy, choosing instead to rely on his own righteousness. The tax collector's humility, acknowledgment of his sin, and reliance on God's mercy make his posture spiritually truer. The parable teaches that justification comes not from self-righteousness or external religious practices but from a humble and repentant heart that seeks God's mercy.
