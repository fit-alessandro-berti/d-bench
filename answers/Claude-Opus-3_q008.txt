This verse exposes a fundamental inconsistency and self-deception in those who claim to love God while harboring hatred toward their fellow human beings. The logic presented is that love for God, who is unseen, is inseparable from love for people, who are visible and tangible manifestations of God's creation. 

A few key points emerge from this passage:

1. Loving one's neighbor is treated as a visible, concrete test of one's claimed love for God. It is easier to profess devotion to an invisible God than to actually embody love in real human relationships. But true love of God must result in changed attitudes and actions toward others.

2. Hating one's "brother" invalidates any profession of love for God. The term "brother" implies a family relationship - we are all children of God. Despising those who are God's children is incompatible with honoring God as Father. 

3. The self-deception exposed is that of divorcing one's vertical relationship with God from horizontal relationships with people. It's possible to feel very devout and spiritual while inwardly harboring bitterness, resentment, and contempt toward others. This verse says that is a form of lying to oneself.

4. Love is portrayed as indivisible - you cannot selectively love God while withholding love from your neighbor. Genuine love for God flows outward and transforms all one's relationships. Claiming a lofty, abstract love while neglecting basic relational virtues is self-deception.

In summary, this verse presents practical, visible love as the authentic expression and validation of the unseen spiritual reality of loving God. It exposes the hypocrisy and inconsistency of an alleged piety that doesn't work itself out in transformed human interactions. Real love for the invisible God must bear visible fruit in gracious, compassionate, forgiving ways of relating to the people around us.
