In Matthew 6:1, Jesus warns against performing good deeds, such as almsgiving, primarily for the purpose of being observed by others. The moral danger lies in the **motivation** behind the act rather than the act of doing good itself. 

### Key Points:

1. **Motivation Over Visibility**: The verse does not condemn the visibility of good deeds per se, but rather the **intention** behind them. When one does good to be seen by others, the reward sought is external validation rather than the intrinsic reward of pleasing God.

2. **Craving to Be Seen**: The problem is the **craving to be seen** or admired by others. This craving transforms a selfless act into a self-centered performance, turning the act of charity into a means of personal glorification.

3. **Absence of Heavenly Reward**: Jesus states that if such actions are done for the sake of being seen, the individual **has no reward from God**. The spiritual benefit and heavenly reward that accompany genuine, selfless acts are forfeited when the act is performed for public approval.

4. **Contrast with God's Expectations**: The verse contrasts human expectations of recognition with God's desire for sincere, private devotion. God rewards those who give "in secret," meaning with pure and unseen motives.

### Conclusion:
The moral danger in public goodness as described in this verse stems from the **craving to be seen** rather than the act of visibility itself. The issue is the underlying self-seeking motive that undermines the authenticity and spiritual merit of the good deed. Genuine charity and righteousness are valued when performed out of a sincere desire to please God and serve others, without the expectation of human accolades. 

Thus, the problem is not merely the visibility of the act but the **intentional pursuit of public recognition**, which nullifies the true spiritual reward.
