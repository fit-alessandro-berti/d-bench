
This is one of the most challenging teachings in the Sermon on the Mount, and its difficulty reveals something profound about the human condition. Let me explore why enemy-love is so hard to take seriously, and what Jesus might be getting at.

## Why It Feels Impossible

Enemy-love strikes at the heart of our most basic survival instincts. When we're hurt, threatened, or wronged, our natural response is defensive—sometimes even retaliatory. To be told to *bless* those who curse us, to *love* those who hate us, feels less like moral instruction and more like spiritual gaslighting.

It can seem sentimental because it asks us to transcend mere emotion—to love not when we *feel* like it, but as a deliberate choice that defies our instincts. This isn't warm fuzzies; it's radical recalibration of how we relate to others.

## But Is It Strategic?

There's definitely strategic wisdom here. Jesus points to God's sun shining on "evil and good" and rain on "just and unjust"—nature itself operates on this principle of generous, indiscriminate goodness. When we mirror this, we become agents of that same generosity in the world.

But I think reducing it to strategy misses the point. If our motivation is simply to get a reward ("look how noble we are") or avoid negative consequences, we've missed the transformation Jesus is talking about. The goal isn't to become skilled at love for our own benefit, but to participate in the way God actually relates to us—graciously, despite our rebellion.

## The Deeper Moral Strength

What makes enemy-love truly demanding is that it requires a kind of moral creativity. It's not just "don't retaliate"—that's relatively easy. It's actively choosing to do good to someone who has no reason to expect it, who may never acknowledge it, who might even continue to harm you.

This isn't weakness; it's a different kind of power. It's the power to refuse to let evil have the final word in your life. It's choosing to live as if God's way is real, even when it costs you.

Jesus says this kind of love is what makes us "children of your Father in heaven." Not our religious observance, not our moral record, but our capacity to reflect God's character—even toward those who don't deserve it.

## Why We Resist It

We resist because it exposes the gap between who we are and who we're called to be. It's easier to love our friends and punish our enemies. But Jesus isn't asking us to be nice; he's asking us to participate in God's own nature.

The difficulty isn't a flaw in the teaching—it's the point. Enemy-love is impossible by human standards, which is exactly why Jesus says it's how we become like God. It's not about achieving a higher moral standard; it's about allowing God to live through us.

So maybe the question isn't whether enemy-love is sentimental, strategic, or strength—but whether we're willing to let God's way of doing things reshape our own. The difficulty itself might be teaching us something about the depth of what's at stake.
