This is a profound and important question, especially in the context of Ezekiel 36:26–27. Let's break it down by looking at the passage and the broader biblical themes at play.

### 1. The Passage in Context

In Ezekiel 36, God is promising restoration to Israel after their exile. But it's not just about returning to the land or resuming rituals. God says He will give them a **"new heart"** and a **"new spirit"**, and He will **remove the "stony heart"** from their flesh. The language is vivid: the stony heart is something hard, unresponsive, and unyielding, while a heart of flesh is soft, responsive, and capable of life.

### 2. External Compliance vs. Internal Transformation

#### **External Compliance**
- This refers to outward obedience: following the rules, performing rituals, or behaving in ways that please God or society, but without a genuine change of heart.
- It's like wearing a mask or putting on a performance. The actions are correct, but the inner disposition hasn't changed.
- In the Bible, this is often criticized. For example, Jesus says, "God looks at the heart" (1 Samuel 16:7), not just at outward appearances.
- External compliance can be motivated by fear, social pressure, or habit—not by love, conviction, or a desire to please God from the core.

#### **Receiving a "New Heart"**
- This is an **internal transformation**. It’s not just about doing the right things, but wanting to do them from the inside out.
- It involves a change in desires, motivations, and character. The "heart" in biblical language is the core of a person—the seat of thought, emotion, and will.
- God promises not just to change behavior, but to change the very nature of the person. The old, hardened, "stony" heart is removed and replaced with a "heart of flesh," which is sensitive, alive, and capable of relationship with God.
- This is often associated with repentance and spiritual renewal. It's about being transformed, not just reformed.

### 3. Why Is Hardness Described as a Material Condition?

The Bible often uses physical metaphors to describe spiritual realities:

- **"Stony heart"** — Stone is hard, cold, unyielding, unfeeling. It suggests someone who is emotionally closed off, resistant to change, unresponsive to God or others.
- **"Heart of flesh"** — Flesh is soft, living, sensitive, capable of feeling and movement. It implies openness, responsiveness, and the capacity for life and relationship.
- This material language helps us grasp the depth of the problem and the depth of the solution. It's not just a minor flaw; it's a fundamental condition of the heart that needs to be replaced, not just repaired.

### 4. Theological Insight

God isn't just interested in correct external behavior. He desires a people whose very being is transformed. The new heart and spirit enable a person to:
- Love God and others genuinely,
- Delight in God's law,
- Live out righteousness naturally,
- Be moved by the Holy Spirit.

This is why Ezekiel 36 is not just about political or ritual restoration, but about a radical spiritual renewal. The promise is holistic: God will change the land, the people, and their very hearts.

---

**In summary:**  
External compliance is about doing the right things for the wrong reasons. Receiving a "new heart" is about God fundamentally transforming who we are from the inside, making it possible to live righteously out of love and conviction. The "stony heart" is described in material terms because the problem is deep and structural—it's a matter of our core nature, not just our actions. God's promise is to change us at the deepest level, so our lives flow from a transformed heart.
