This verse treats love for the visible neighbor as a test of love for the invisible God because authentic love for God must manifest concretely and tangibly in daily relationships with actual people. The logic is that genuine love for God—who is unseen and transcendent—is inherently more difficult to verify or validate; therefore, the true evidence of it is found in how one treats those who are immediately present, tangible, and inescapable.

The self-deception exposed here is the kind of spiritual hypocrisy that claims lofty spiritual devotion while neglecting basic, practical compassion toward others. It reveals the inconsistency and emptiness of professing love for God without demonstrating that love in the real, messy, and difficult arena of human relationships. Essentially, it warns against the subtle pride and self-righteousness of claiming moral or religious superiority while harboring resentment, hatred, or indifference toward one's neighbors. The text is saying that if you cannot love your neighbor—who provides immediate opportunity for compassion and empathy—you deceive yourself by claiming a deeper, invisible devotion to God.
