The trust described in Proverbs 3:5–6 is a wholehearted, humble reliance on God rather than on one’s own limited understanding. This kind of trust involves several nuanced aspects:

### Humble Self-Distrust vs. Mere Anti-Intellectualism

#### Humble Self-Distrust:
- **Acknowledges human limitation:** Recognizes that our own understanding and knowledge are finite and fallible.
- **Opens oneself to divine guidance:** Actively seeks God's wisdom and guidance rather than assuming personal insight is sufficient.
- **Integrates intellect and faith:** Does not reject reason or intellect but submits them under God’s lordship, understanding that true wisdom ultimately comes from God.
- **Is relational and trusting:** It is rooted in a relationship of trust with God, involving faith, prayer, and obedience.
- **Leads to wise decisions:** Because it looks beyond mere personal insight, it positions a person to act in alignment with God's will, which is good and wise.

#### Mere Anti-Intellectualism:
- **Rejects or distrusts intellect outright:** Views human reason and knowledge as worthless or suspicious, potentially ignoring useful evidence or knowledge.
- **Ignores critical thinking:** Often resists learning, questioning, or careful thought.
- **Can lead to irresponsibility:** Without intellectual engagement, decisions may be impulsive, uninformed, or even reckless, mistakenly thinking that faith alone will justify any move.
- **Not necessarily rooted in faith:** May be cultural or emotional rather than spiritual.
- **Often closed off:** It fails to balance faith and intellect and may foster ignorance rather than wisdom.

### Summary:
Proverbs 3:5–6 calls for a **trust that is humble and wise**, not blind or dismissive of the intellect. It encourages believers to recognize their own cognitive limitations and lean fully on God’s wisdom, which surpasses human understanding. This trust does not reject intellect but places intellect under faithful submission to God’s leading, resulting in clearer, wiser paths.
