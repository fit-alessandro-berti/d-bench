1.  What makes the picture so vivid?  
   • Enormous contrast.  Jesus chooses the tiniest thing people knew—a κάρφος, a dry speck of chaff—and opposes it to one of the largest things they could imagine inside a house—a δοκός, the roof-beam that holds the rafters.  Putting the two in the same organ, the delicate human eye, produces a comic but cutting absurdity.  
   • The eye itself.  Because the eye is so sensitive, even a speck there is irritating; the thought of a whole joist in the socket is grotesque.  The hearer “feels” the scene, so the rebuke slips past defences.  
   • Role reversal.  The man with the beam is not only blind, he proposes to do eye-surgery on someone else.  The picture therefore exposes hypocrisy by turning it into slap-stick: the hypocrite is a walking caricature.

2.  What, exactly, is being condemned?  
   • Not moral discernment, but self-righteous judging.  The point is the mismatch between what I tolerate in myself and what I censure in you.  The word ‘hypocrite’ (ὑποκριτής) already warned Jesus’ listeners that the target is play-acting religiosity.  
   • Lack of self-examination.  One can notice another’s splinter only because one’s own beam has never been faced.  The order is therefore crucial: “first remove the beam… and then you will see clearly.”

3.  Why moral correction is still permitted—indeed required  
   • The sentence does not stop with “take the beam out.”  It continues: “…and then you will see clearly to take the mote out of your brother’s eye.”  When the hindrance of hypocrisy is gone, genuine help can begin.  
   • The rest of the chapter assumes continuing discernment (v 6 “do not give dogs what is holy,” v 15 “beware of false prophets”), so Jesus cannot be abolishing moral evaluation.  
   • The Bible elsewhere gives the same sequence: Galatians 6:1 “restore him in a spirit of gentleness, each looking to yourself lest you also be tempted.”

In short, the image is “sharp” because it is wildly exaggerated, painfully imaginable, and comically reversed.  Jesus uses it to forbid censorious, self-blind criticism, not to silence all moral counsel.  We may still help our brother with his splinter—only after we have done the harder work of dealing with the timber in our own eye.
