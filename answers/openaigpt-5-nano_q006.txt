Short answer
- The “self” being denied is the self-centered, ego-driven life—the old life oriented around one’s own wants, control, security, status, and independence. Denying self means renouncing that center of allegiance so that one’s life is reoriented around Jesus and his mission (the gospel).

More detail
- What “deny himself” means:
  - It’s not about hating yourself or erasing personality. It’s about not letting your own desires and plans run your life as if you were the ultimate authority.
  - It involves choosing God’s will over your own preferred path, even when that path costs you comfort, reputation, or control.
- What “take up his cross” implies:
  - A readiness to endure hardship, suffering, or even death for Jesus’s sake and the gospel’s sake. It signals solidarity with Jesus in his own path of obedience, rejection, and, if necessary, martyrdom.
- What “lose his life … save it” means:
  - If you cling to the life you think will keep you secure in this world, you may end up forfeiting the true, eternal life God offers. If you “lose” that self-centered life for Jesus, you discover a richer life in him—often described as life in the Spirit and life eternal.

Why this can feel liberating to some and offensive to others
- Liberating for many:
  - It frees you from being ruled by fear, avarice, status, or controlling others. Your identity is redefined not by what you achieve or possess but by belonging to God and participating in his mission.
  - It gives ultimate purpose: life is less about survival or self-advancement and more about love, service, truth, and community. The paradox is that giving up the old self can yield a deeper, more enduring life.
- Offensive or disturbing to others:
  - It requires surrender of personal autonomy and can feel like a loss of control, comfort, or rights. In a culture that prizes self-determination and security, this sounds like restriction or coercion.
  - For people facing real suffering or injustice, the call to “take up the cross” can seem to justify endurance of pain or to minimize legitimate rights and redress.
  - Not everyone shares the belief that life is defined by union with Jesus and the gospel, so the claim can come across as exclusive or impractical.

Practical angles
- In daily life, self-denial might look like:
  - Choosing to serve others, forgive rather than retaliate, or give time and resources to the poor or church community.
  - Reordering priorities: money, status, and comfort take a back seat to love of God and neighbor.
  - Bearing costs for following Jesus in small ways (integrity at work, honesty in relationships) or large ones (risking social or even physical persecution in hostile contexts).
- Important caveat: denial of self is about reorienting life to God, not about self-hatred or coercive submission. It is grounded in the gospel’s promise that true life is found in Christ and his mission.
