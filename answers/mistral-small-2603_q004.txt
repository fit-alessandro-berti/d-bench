Proverbs 3:5–6 calls for a profound and wholehearted trust in the Lord, but this trust must be carefully distinguished from mere anti-intellectualism or blind obedience. Here’s how we can understand the kind of trust being asked for, and how it differs from these misconceptions:

### **1. The Nature of the Trust: "Trust in the Lord with all thine heart"**
The command is not a rejection of reason or intellect but a call to **prioritize divine wisdom over human wisdom** in moments of decision-making. The phrase *"with all thine heart"* suggests:
- **Total surrender** – Not just intellectual assent but a deep, personal reliance on God.
- **Exclusive trust** – It excludes the idea that human understanding alone is sufficient (cf. Proverbs 3:7, "Be not wise in thine own eyes").
- **Active dependence** – It implies a posture of humility, acknowledging that God’s ways are higher than ours (Isaiah 55:8–9).

This is not a call to abandon reason but to **submit reason to God’s revelation**. The trust is relational—trusting a Person (Yahweh) rather than just principles or self-reliance.

### **2. Distinguishing Humble Self-Distrust from Anti-Intellectualism**
The passage warns against *"leaning on thine own understanding,"* which can be misunderstood. Here’s how to properly interpret it:

#### **A. Humble Self-Distrust (Correct Interpretation)**
- **Acknowledges human limitations** – Human wisdom is finite, flawed, and prone to sinful bias (Jeremiah 17:9, "The heart is deceitful above all things").
- **Seeks divine guidance** – It does not reject intellect but **subordinates it to God’s Word and Spirit** (Proverbs 2:6, "The Lord gives wisdom").
- **Balances faith and reason** – Trusting God does not mean ignoring facts or evidence but **interpreting them in light of Scripture and prayer**.
- **Example:** A scientist who studies God’s creation (nature) but acknowledges that ultimate truth comes from God (Psalm 19:1–2).

#### **B. Anti-Intellectualism (Misinterpretation)**
- **Rejects reason outright** – Mistakes humility for ignorance, assuming that trusting God means ignoring logic or evidence.
- **Leads to gullibility** – May embrace superstition or false teachings under the guise of "faith."
- **Contrasts with biblical wisdom** – Proverbs itself praises wisdom (Proverbs 4:7), and Jesus commends the "wise steward" (Luke 12:42).
- **Example:** Someone who refuses medical treatment because "God will heal me" without considering prudent care (James 2:16, "If a brother or sister is poorly clothed and lacking in daily food, and one of you says to them, 'Go in peace, be warmed and filled,' without giving them the things needed for the body, what good is that?").

### **3. "In All Thy Ways Acknowledge Him" – How This Works in Practice**
The second part of the verse (*"In all thy ways acknowledge him"*) clarifies that this trust is **not passive** but active:
- **Seeking God’s will** – Through prayer, Scripture, and godly counsel (Proverbs 15:22, "Without counsel plans fail").
- **Aligning actions with faith** – Obedience to God’s commands is part of acknowledging Him (James 4:13–15 warns against presumptuous planning without God).
- **Trusting His providence** – Even in uncertainty, we act with wisdom while resting in God’s sovereignty (Matthew 6:33, "Seek first the kingdom of God...").

### **Conclusion: A Trust That Embraces Wisdom**
The trust in Proverbs 3:5–6 is:
✅ **Not anti-intellectual** – It does not reject reason but submits it to God.
✅ **Not passive** – It requires active seeking of God’s will.
✅ **Not irrational** – It is a **wise trust**, recognizing that God’s ways are higher but not illogical.

True biblical trust is **humble, rational, and relational**—it does not pit faith against reason but sees reason as a tool to be used in service of faith. As Augustine put it, *"Faith seeks understanding"* (cf. *Sermon 43*). We trust God **with** our minds, not **without** them.
