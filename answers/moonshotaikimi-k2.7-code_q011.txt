The shift from “**cease to do evil**” to “**learn to do well**” is theologically significant.

In Hebrew poetry, the second line often **intensifies and completes** the first. “Cease from evil” is necessary, but it is only the *removal* of wrong. “Learn to do good” says that righteousness is not simply the absence of sin—it is a **positive skill and habit that must be cultivated**.

Notice that the prophet immediately defines “doing well” with concrete social actions:

- *Seek judgment* (justice)  
- *Relieve the oppressed*  
- *Judge the fatherless*  
- *Plead for the widow*

This shows that goodness, in Isaiah’s view, is **active love and justice**. It is not enough to stop harming others; one must actively work for the vulnerable and the oppressed.

The word “**learn**” is also important. Doing good is not merely a momentary feeling or a private intention. It requires **instruction, practice, and discipline**. Evil habits must be put off; habits of mercy, advocacy, and justice must be put on.

So Isaiah teaches that true goodness is:

- **More than restraint** — it is not enough to do no harm.
- **A practiced form of love** — it must be learned and lived.
- **Justice in action** — especially toward the poor, the orphan, and the widow.

Mere avoidance of evil leaves a person morally empty; genuine righteousness fills that space with active compassion and justice.
