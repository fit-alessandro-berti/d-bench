In the last two verses of Psalm 139 the psalm-writer does something striking.

1.  He does not ask God to confirm his innocence.
2.  He does not ask God simply to console him or to destroy his enemies.
3.  He asks God to look inside him, expose anything that is wrong, and then re-direct him.

Why choose “search me” instead of “save me” or “comfort me”?

1.  Recognition of God’s total knowledge (vv. 1-6).  
   The whole psalm has celebrated God’s omniscience—“You have searched me and you know me.” If God already sees everything, pretending before Him is pointless. The petitioner therefore leans into that reality and invites what is already true: “Since You know, please show me what You see.”

2.  Awareness of possible self-deception.  
   Human beings may be blind to their own motives (Jer 17:9). The request to be searched is a confession that “I might not know myself as well as I think I do.”

3.  Desire for transformation, not mere protection.  
   Defence and comfort deal with circumstances; searching deals with character. The psalmist wants to be the kind of person who actually belongs on God’s side, not merely someone whom God helps. Hence the two verbs:  
   • “search/try” (examine, test, put in the crucible)  
   • “lead” (guide, shape, direct)  
   Exposure is not the goal; change is.

4.  Covenant honesty.  
   In the Ancient Near-Eastern oath of innocence, one swore before the deity and asked to be tested if lying. Psalm 139 adapts that form but deepens it: the psalmist is willing for God to probe not just acts but “thoughts” (lit. “anxious cares”) and “ways” (habitual paths). He stakes the relationship on total candour.

What kind of honesty is being modeled?

• Vulnerable honesty: “Know my heart … my anxious thoughts.” He allows God into the private interior.  
• Humble honesty: “See if there is any wicked way in me.” He concedes that such a way might exist.  
• Teachable honesty: “… and lead me in the everlasting way.” He is ready to abandon whatever God disallows and walk a different path.

In short, the psalmist models the spirituality of open-handed self-examination—letting God name what must change, trusting that the same God who exposes will also guide into “the way everlasting.”
