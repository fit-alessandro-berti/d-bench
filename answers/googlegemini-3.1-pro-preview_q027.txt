To fully understand what condemns the Pharisee and what justifies the tax collector (publican), we have to look at the interpretive key Luke provides in verse 9: *“He spake this parable unto certain which trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others.”*

The condemnation of the Pharisee is not found in a single flaw, but in a toxic, interwoven theology of self that relies on **pride, comparison, contempt, and virtue as self-display.** By contrast, the tax collector’s posture is treated as "spiritually truer" because it perfectly aligns with the reality of the human condition before God. 

Here is a breakdown of exactly what condemns the Pharisee and why the tax collector is justified.

---

### Part 1: What Condemns the Pharisee?

The Pharisee is not condemned for doing evil. In fact, his actions—fasting twice a week and tithing—go above and beyond the requirements of the Law of Moses. His condemnation stems from the inner corruption of his motives and his false theology of grace. 

**1. Pride (Trusting in Himself)**
The root of the Pharisee's error is self-sovereignty. Notice the wording in verse 11: “The Pharisee stood and prayed thus *with himself*.” In a profound sense, he is not praying to God at all; he is admiring his own spiritual reflection. He treats his relationship with God transactionally, viewing his morality as a currency that obligates God to accept him.

**2. Virtue as Self-Display**
The Pharisee treats his righteousness as a resume. He comes to the temple to present his credentials to God. His fasting and tithing, which are meant to be disciplines of self-denial and surrender to God, have been weaponized to build his own ego. It is the ultimate spiritual tragedy: using the tools of worship to worship oneself. 

**3. Comparison and Contempt**
Because the Pharisee's righteousness is self-generated, it is fragile. To feel tall, he needs someone else to be short. He relies on **comparison** (“I am not as other men are”) which inevitably breeds **contempt** (“or even as this publican”). He grades on a moral curve. By comparing himself to extortioners, adulterers, and the tax collector, he shrinks the infinite holiness of God down to a manageable standard that he can effortlessly step over. 

**4. False Doctrine (Implicit)**
While the Pharisee’s *behavioral* doctrine might strictly align with the law, his *soteriology* (the doctrine of salvation) is entirely false. He operates under the doctrine of works-righteousness. He believes he can bridge the gap between human frailty and divine holiness through sheer moral effort. He does not ask for anything from God, because he doesn't believe he needs anything. 

---

### Part 2: Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture "Spiritually Truer"?

The irony of the parable is that the tax collector—a man whose life was likely characterized by actual theft, Roman collaboration, and greed—is the one who goes home "justified" (made right with God). His posture is spiritually truer for several vital reasons:

**1. Radical Honesty**
The publican deals in absolute reality. He has no resume, no moral achievements to cite, and no illusions about his spiritual state. The text says he stood afar off and "would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven." He recognizes the infinite distance between his brokenness and God's holiness. He does not make excuses, and crucially, *he does not compare himself to anyone else.*

**2. The Greek Nuance of "The Sinner"**
In our English translations, the publican says, "God be merciful to me **a** sinner." But in the original Greek, he literally says, "God be merciful to me, **the** sinner" (*tō hamartōlō*). Just as the Pharisee looked around and saw himself as the only righteous man, the tax collector looks internally and sees himself as the foremost sinner. He takes full, unmitigated ownership of his spiritual bankruptcy.

**3. Reliance on Mercy, Not Merit**
The tax collector comes with completely empty hands. Because he cannot appeal to his own track record, his only hope is to appeal to God's character. The word he uses for "merciful" (*hilaskomai*) is closely tied to the concept of *atonement* or *propitiation*—the covering of sin. He is essentially crying out, "Let your grace cover my complete failure." 

### The Conclusion of the Parable

Jesus concludes that the tax collector went home justified rather than the Pharisee. The profound spiritual truth here is that **God cannot fill hands that are already full.** 

The Pharisee was condemned because his hands were full of his own accomplishments; there was no room for God’s grace. The tax collector’s posture is truer because it represents the only way a human being can relate to a holy God: with empty hands, a broken spirit, and an absolute reliance on unearned mercy. The Pharisee built a pedestal and isolated himself; the tax collector threw himself on the floor, and God met him there.
