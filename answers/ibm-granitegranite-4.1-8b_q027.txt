The condemnation of the Pharisee in Luke 18:9–14 stems primarily from **pride and the use of virtue as self-display**, which are inseparable from the underlying issues of false doctrine, comparison, and contempt. Let us unpack these elements:

### 1. **Pride and Self-Display of Virtue**
- **Self-Contrast**: The Pharisee explicitly contrasts himself with “other men” and specifically with the publican, stating, “I am not as other men are, extortioners, unjust, adulterers…” and “I fast twice in the week… I give tithes of all that I possess.” This self-praise is not a humble acknowledgment of God’s grace but a boastful proclamation of personal righteousness.
- **Public Display**: By praying “with himself” and outwardly showcasing his religious practices (fasting, tithing), the Pharisee turns the act of prayer into a public exhibition of his moral superiority. This manipulation of religion for personal affirmation is a form of pride, as he seeks validation from God not for relationship but for status.
- **False Doctrine**: Implicit in this pride is a false doctrine—that one’s external observances alone can secure justification with God. The Pharisee implicitly believes that his meticulous adherence to law renders him inherently righteous in God’s eyes, ignoring the biblical teaching that righteousness is a gift from God, not earned by self-righteous deeds.

### 2. **Comparison and Contempt**
- **Comparative Superiority**: The Pharisee’s prayer is steeped in comparative superiority. He measures himself against others, especially the despised publican, as if his moral standing is a matter of quantitative comparison. This not only exalts himself but simultaneously devalues others, revealing a contemptuous attitude toward those deemed less righteous.
- **Contempt for Others**: By dismissing the publican as a “sinner,” the Pharisee demonstrates a lack of empathy and an inability to recognize his own need for mercy. This contempt blinds him to the spiritual poverty of his own heart despite his external piety.

### 3. **Why the Tax Collector’s Posture Is Spiritually Truer**
- **Humility**: The publican’s posture—standing “afar off,” not lifting his eyes to heaven, but smiting his breast and crying, “God be merciful to me a sinner”—embodies humility. He acknowledges his sinfulness and dependence on divine mercy, aligning with the biblical principle that true righteousness is rooted in a broken and contrite spirit (cf. Psalm 51:17).
- **Repentance and Trust**: His prayer is a declaration of repentance rather than a recitation of virtues. It expresses an honest recognition of need and a trust in God’s mercy rather than in self-accomplishment. This aligns with the gospel message that salvation comes through faith in Christ’s atonement, not through human effort.
- **Spiritual Authenticity**: Unlike the Pharisee’s calculated display, the tax collector’s prayer is spontaneous and genuine. It reflects an internal posture of contrition and humility that is spiritually authentic and thus pleasing to God.

### Conclusion
The Pharisee is condemned because his prayer, while outwardly religious, is underpinned by **pride**, **false doctrine**, **comparative self-righteousness**, and **contempt** for others. In contrast, the tax collector’s posture—which involves humility, sincere repentance, and reliance on divine mercy—is spiritually truer because it reflects an authentic relationship with God grounded in grace and humility rather than in human achievement. Thus, Jesus teaches that those who humble themselves like the publican will be justified, not those who pridefully exalt themselves as the Pharisee does.
