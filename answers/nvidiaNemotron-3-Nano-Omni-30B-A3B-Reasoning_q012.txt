### Short answer  
The passage is **realistic about self‑deception**.  It acknowledges that the human heart is *deceitful*—i.e., it can hide, distort, or rationalize its own motives—so it is not simply a blanket condemnation of humanity but a diagnosis of a problem that only God can see through.

### Why it feels pessimistic, but isn’t

| Aspect | What the text says | How it reads |
|--------|-------------------|--------------|
| **“The heart is deceitful above all things”** | The heart is *the* source of falsehood. | Pessimistic: “people are inherently corrupt.” |
| **“Desperately wicked”** | Emphasizes the depth of the problem. | Pessimistic: “the human condition is hopeless.” |
| **“Who can know it?”** | A rhetorical question implying human inability to see the truth of one’s own heart. | Realistic: *we* cannot reliably read our own motives. |
| **“I the Lord search the heart … give every man according to his ways”** | God alone has the capacity to see the hidden reality and to judge accordingly. | Realistic: the solution lies outside human perception. |

Thus the verse moves from a **pessimistic description** (the heart’s deceit) to a **realistic diagnosis** (human inability to self‑diagnose) and points to a **divine remedy** (God’s searching).

### How to talk about motives when the heart is an unreliable witness

1. **Acknowledge the limitation**  
   - *“Our own feelings and rationalizations are often misleading.”*  
   - *“We tend to justify ourselves in ways that hide the true motives.”*

2. **Invite external insight**  
   - **Trusted counsel** – “Talk with a mature friend or mentor who can see patterns you miss.”  
   - **Scripture or spiritual discipline** – “Regularly examine the biblical teaching on motives (e.g., James 4:1‑2, Psalm 139:23‑24).”  
   - **Self‑reflection tools** – Journaling, prayer, or a “motive audit” (ask: *What am I trying to achieve? What benefit do I get? What am I avoiding?*).

3. **Use observable outcomes**  
   - *“Motives are best inferred from actions and their fruits.”*  
   - *“If someone consistently acts kindly, the underlying motive is likely love or generosity, even if they claim otherwise.”*

4. **Employ humility and grace**  
   - *“Because the heart can deceive, we should avoid jumping to conclusions about others’ intentions.”*  
   - *“Assume the best possible motive until evidence suggests otherwise, while still being honest about your own blind spots.”*

5. **Recognize the role of God (or a higher standard)**  
   - *“Ultimately, only God can search the heart (cf. Jeremiah 17:9‑10). Our responsibility is to be transparent, seek accountability, and allow divine illumination to reveal hidden motives.”*

### Practical conversation starter

> “I’ve been thinking about how easy it is for us to rationalize our own motives. The Bible says the heart is deceitful, so I try to let my actions and the feedback I receive be the main clues, and I also ask a trusted friend to help me see any blind spots.”

This approach respects the biblical realism about self‑deception while offering a constructive way to discuss motives without relying on an unreliable “heart‑witness.”
