The passage in Ephesians 4:31–32 presents a stark, zero-sum contrast: you cannot hold onto the former while walking in the latter. The text places bitterness and forgiveness in direct opposition because, biblically, they are not merely opposing emotions but opposing **spiritual economies**—one is an economy of debt, the other an economy of grace.

To answer your specific questions about the nature of bitterness and why it is positioned as the antithesis of forgiveness, we must look at the underlying Greek terms and the progression of the text.

### 1. The Nature of Bitterness: More Than a Feeling
The text does not treat bitterness merely as a fleeting emotion. The Greek word used here is *pikria* (from which we get the English "picric" acid). It refers to a sharp, pungent taste, like gall or wormwood.

In the biblical context, bitterness is described as a **root** (Hebrews 12:15) rather than a fruit. A feeling is often a reaction to a moment; a root is a deep-seated structural reality. Therefore, the text treats bitterness as:
*   **A Chosen Posture:** It is a settled condition of the soul. It is the decision to nurture a grievance until it hardens into a permanent identity. The text commands that it be "put away," implying that the believer has the agency to evict it.
*   **A "Habit" of the Mind:** Bitterness is the replaying of a tape of injustice. It is the act of keeping a record of wrongs (the opposite of 1 Corinthians 13:5). It is a habitual rumination on how one has been wronged.

### 2. Why They Are Opposites: Debt vs. Grace
The primary reason bitterness and forgiveness are placed against each other is that they represent contradictory ways of relating to "debt."

*   **Bitterness is the insistence on debt.** It says, "You owe me. You took something from me (honor, time, safety, love), and I will not be satisfied until it is paid back." Bitterness holds the offender in a debtor's prison. It creates a chain that binds the victim to the offender indefinitely; you cannot be bitter without constantly mentally engaging with the person who hurt you.
*   **Forgiveness is the cancellation of debt.** The Greek word for "forgiving" here is *charizomenoi*, which shares a root with *charis* (grace). It literally means to "give freely" or to "cancel an obligation."

This is why they cannot coexist. You cannot simultaneously hold a ledger of wrongs (bitterness) and mark the account "Paid in Full" (forgiveness). The text positions them as mutually exclusive postures of the heart.

### 3. Is Bitterness Contagious?
Your question about whether bitterness is "something more contagious" is profound and textually supported. Verse 31 lists bitterness as the head of a toxic watershed:

> "Let all **bitterness**, and wrath, and anger, and clamour, and evil speaking... be put away."

The text suggests a **causal chain**, making bitterness highly contagious and destructive to the community:
1.  **Bitterness (*pikria*):** The internal root. A silent, internal seething over perceived wrongs.
2.  **Wrath and Anger:** As the bitterness is nurtured, it heats up. Internal resentment becomes an active emotional boiling point.
3.  **Clamour (*krauge*):** The internal heat forces an external vent. This is loud, confused shouting or crying out—an explosion of noise.
4.  **Evil Speaking (*blasphemia*):** The noise becomes articulate. This is slander, mockery, or speaking in a way that damages the other's reputation.

This progression reveals that bitterness is never a private sin. It is a spiritual infection that, if left untreated, inevitably breaks the skin of the community in the form of shouting and slander. It contaminates the "body" (the church community), which is why Paul calls for it to be "put away" from *among* them.

### 4. The Theological Pivot: "Even as God"
The ultimate reason they are placed in opposition is found in the final phrase: *"even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you."*

Paul uses the **indicative** (what is true about you) to drive the **imperative** (what you must do).
*   If you are bitter, you are acting as if you are the supreme creditor—the final judge who must be appeased.
*   If you are forgiving, you are acting as a fellow debtor who has been released.

The text implies that holding onto bitterness is a form of theological amnesia. It forgets the magnitude of one's own forgiveness. To remain bitter while claiming to be a Christian is a contradiction; it attempts to hold others to a standard of justice that one has refused to accept for oneself.

**Summary:**
Bitterness is placed against forgiveness because bitterness is a **posture of retention** (holding onto the past and the offense), while forgiveness is a **posture of release**. Bitterness is contagious not just because it spreads to others, but because it spreads within the soul; it moves from being a feeling to becoming a prison that inevitably manifests as social brokenness (shouting and slander). The text commands us to "put it away" because a root of bitterness cannot coexist with the fruit of the Spirit.
