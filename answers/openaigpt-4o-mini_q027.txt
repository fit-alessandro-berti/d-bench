In the parable from Luke 18:9–14, the Pharisee is primarily condemned for his pride, contempt for others, and the use of virtue as self-display. Here’s how each aspect contributes to the overall message:

1. **Pride**: The Pharisee embodies a self-righteous attitude, believing himself to be morally superior to others. His prayer is more a statement of his own virtues than a humble appeal to God. This pride prevents him from recognizing his own shortcomings and need for God's mercy.

2. **Comparison and Contempt**: The Pharisee's prayer explicitly contrasts himself with the publican (tax collector), revealing a sense of contempt for others whom he deems less righteous. By listing sins that he believes he is free from, he positions himself above others rather than focusing on his relationship with God.

3. **Use of Virtue as Self-Display**: The Pharisee mentions his fasting and tithing not as acts of devotion but to showcase his piety. This self-promotion signifies a misunderstanding of the purpose of such acts, which should be about genuine service to God rather than garnering self-approval or recognition.

In contrast, the tax collector (publican) exemplifies humility and a sincere acknowledgment of his sinful state. His posture—standing afar off, refusing to look up, and smiting his breast—symbolizes penitence and recognition of his need for God's mercy. His humility is treated as spiritually truer because it aligns with the biblical principle that God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble (James 4:6). The publican's expression of desperation for mercy contrasts sharply with the Pharisee's self-assuredness, illustrating that true righteousness in the eyes of God comes from a humble heart that seeks repentance rather than self-justification. 

Ultimately, the parable teaches that justification before God is based not on self-righteousness or external virtue but on an attitude of humility and reliance on God's grace.
