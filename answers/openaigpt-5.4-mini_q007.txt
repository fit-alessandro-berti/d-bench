Luke 6:37 is **not** saying that moral judgments never matter. It is warning against a particular kind of judgment: the self-exalting, punitive, condemnatory posture that treats other people as if we were their final judge.

## 1) Moral discernment is necessary
We constantly make moral judgments in ordinary life:

- This action was wrong.
- That choice was wise.
- This person needs help.
- This behavior is dangerous.
- A boundary needs to be set.

Without discernment, we could not love well, protect the vulnerable, or pursue justice. Even the command to “forgive” assumes that wrongdoing is real and should be named.

## 2) What Jesus forbids is the appetite to condemn
To **condemn** is more than recognizing that something is wrong. It is to:

- pronounce a final verdict on a person,
- assume the posture of ultimate moral superiority,
- reduce someone to their failure,
- or delight in their downfall.

This is the attitude Jesus rejects. The issue is not “Can you tell right from wrong?” but “What spirit do you bring to that judgment?”

## 3) Discernment asks, condemnation closes
A helpful contrast:

- **Discernment** says: “This is wrong; how should I respond faithfully?”
- **Condemnation** says: “This person is beneath me; I stand over them.”

Discernment seeks truth and repair. Condemnation seeks blame, scorekeeping, and punishment.

## 4) Jesus’ own life models this difference
Jesus clearly made moral judgments:

- He named hypocrisy.
- He called sin sin.
- He corrected false teaching.
- He warned of judgment.

Yet he also showed mercy to sinners, offered restoration, and resisted mob-like condemnation. He could say, in effect, “This is wrong,” without turning people into disposable moral objects.

## 5) The passage also addresses hypocrisy
In the surrounding context, Jesus goes on to speak about the speck and the log. That suggests one major problem with condemnatory judgment is hypocrisy: we notice others’ faults while ignoring our own.

So the warning is not against moral clarity, but against **selective, self-protective, unmerciful judgment**.

## 6) The standard you use returns to you
“Judge not” is tied to reciprocity: the manner in which you judge shapes the manner in which you will be judged. If you are harsh, merciless, and eager to condemn, you should not be surprised to receive the same standard.

That is very different from saying moral truth is irrelevant. It means that moral truth must be handled with humility, mercy, and self-awareness.

## Bottom line
- **Moral judgments matter.**
- **Discernment** is necessary.
- **Condemnation** is forbidden because it assumes a throne that belongs to God alone.

So Luke 6:37 does not erase moral evaluation; it transforms the way it is done. It calls us to judge with humility, and to correct without the hunger to destroy.
