This parable is deliberately constructed as a study in **contrasting postures before God**. To answer your question precisely, we must distinguish between the *symptoms* of the Pharisee’s failure and its *root cause*, and then examine why the tax collector’s stance aligns with the actual mechanics of biblical justification.

### 🔍 What Condemns the Pharisee?
All four options you listed appear in the text, but they are not equally weighted. They form a causal chain:

1. **Pride (Self-Trust)** → *Root Cause*  
   Luke 18:9 explicitly frames the audience: those who `"trusted in themselves that they were righteous."` The Pharisee’s prayer is self-referential from start to finish. He thanks God, but God functions merely as a witness to his own moral résumé. Pride here is not arrogance in a vacuum; it is **self-sufficient righteousness** that leaves no room for grace.

2. **Comparison & Contempt** → *Expression*  
   His righteousness is defined negatively: `"I am not as other men are... or even as this publican."` Comparison is the engine of his self-assessment, and contempt (v.9 `"despised others"`) is its social fruit. In Jewish thought, righteousness was covenantal and communal; defining oneself by despising others fractures that covenant posture.

3. **Virtue as Self-Display** → *Mechanism*  
   `"I fast twice... I give tithes..."` These are genuine Torah obligations, even commendable if done righteously. But he catalogs them as personal achievements that obligate divine favor. The virtue isn't false; the **orientation** is. He treats holiness as currency rather than response to grace.

4. **False Doctrine?** → *Not the Primary Issue*  
   The text never critiques his theology of the Law, resurrection, or angels. His doctrinal framework may have been largely intact. What’s condemned is **soteriological posture**: the belief that righteousness can be self-generated and self-attested. Jesus later condemns Pharisaic distortion of the Law (e.g., Mark 7), but here the failure is heart-orientation, not propositional error.

**Conclusion on the Pharisee:** He is condemned for **self-righteous pride** that uses comparison, contempt, and performative obedience to construct a righteousness independent of divine mercy. The closing verse seals it: `"for everyone who exalts himself will be humbled."`

---

### 🙏 Why Is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?
The publican’s actions and words are carefully staged to embody the only posture that can receive justification:

1. **Physical Humility Reflects Spiritual Reality**  
   Standing `"afar off,"` refusing to lift his eyes, beating his breast (a Jewish gesture of mourning/repentance, cf. Jer 31:19; Luke 23:48) → These aren't theatrics; they are embodied confession of unworthiness before holiness.

2. **The Prayer Itself: `ἱλάσθητί μοι, τῷ ἁμαρτωλῷ`**  
   `"God, be merciful to me, a sinner."` 
   - `ἱλάσθητί` comes from `ἱλάσκομαι`, used in the LXX for God’s atoning mercy (e.g., Ps 79:9; Dan 9:19) and directly linked to the mercy seat (`ἱλαστήριον`, Rom 3:25; Heb 9:5). He is not asking God to overlook sin; he is appealing to God’s **covenant provision for it**.
   - `τῷ ἁμαρτωλῷ` uses the definite article: `"the sinner."` He doesn't say `"a sinner among many"` but identifies himself as *the* sinner standing before God. No comparison, no qualifiers.

3. **Justification, Not Self-Declaration**  
   Jesus says he `"went down to his house justified"` (δεδικαιωμένον). This is forensic: God declares him righteous. The Pharisee declared himself righteous; God rejected it. The tax collector declared himself guilty; God imputed righteousness. This mirrors the Beatitudes (`"Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom"`, Matt 5:3) and Paul’s later exposition: `"For all have sinned... and are justified by his grace as a gift"` (Rom 3:23–24).

4. **Ontological Honesty**  
   The tax collector’s posture is `"truer"` because it corresponds to reality: before a holy God, no human merit suffices. His stance acknowledges the **asymmetry** of the divine-human relationship. God resists the proud but gives grace to the humble (Jas 4:6; 1 Pet 5:5).

---

### 📖 Theological Synthesis
Jesus is not condemning fasting, tithing, or confidence in God’s promises. He is exposing **the illusion of self-attained righteousness**. The Pharisee’s religion had become a mirror; the tax collector’s was a window. 

In Luke’s Gospel, this parable sits between the persistent widow (18:1–8) and the childlike kingdom (18:15–17), forming a triad on **dependent perseverance, humble reception, and grace-justification**. The tax collector is `"truer"` not because he lacks virtue, but because his posture leaves room for God to act. Justification is never the reward of the self-commend; it is the gift received by the mercy-dependent.

As Augustine later summarized: *"When God crowns our merits, He crowns nothing but His own gifts."* The Pharisee forgot that. The tax collector remembered it.
