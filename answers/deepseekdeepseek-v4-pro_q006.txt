In Mark 8:34–35, Jesus calls His followers to “deny himself, and take up his cross, and follow me.” The “self” He speaks of is not the core identity God created, but the autonomous, self-centered, and self-preserving ego that insists on its own way apart from God.

### The “Self” Being Denied

This “self” can be understood on several interconnected levels:

1. **The self as the ultimate authority (the rebellious will).**  
   In the context of Mark’s Gospel, the cross is an instrument of execution. To take up a cross is to relinquish the right to direct one’s own life. The self being denied is the will that says, “I am the master of my fate; I will define good and evil on my own terms.” It’s the self that resists God’s claim of lordship.

2. **The self as self-preservation above all (the survival instinct elevated to an idol).**  
   The immediate paradox Jesus gives is that whoever wants to “save his life” will lose it. The “self” here is the life (*psychē* in Greek, often meaning soul/life) that we clutch tightly, the impulse to secure safety, comfort, reputation, and control at any cost—even the cost of faithfulness to God. Denying this self means refusing to let fear-driven self-protection dictate one’s actions.

3. **The self as the center of the universe (the prideful ego).**  
   This is the self that seeks its own glory, demands recognition, compares itself to others, and is easily wounded by slights. To deny this self is to dethrone the ego and embrace a posture of humility and hiddenness in Christ.

4. **The old humanity in union with Adam (the fallen nature).**  
   Theologically, this ties to Paul’s concept of the “old self” (Romans 6:6; Ephesians 4:22) that is corrupted by deceitful desires. Denying this self is not a one-time act but a continual turning from the ingrained pull toward sin, selfishness, and independence from God. It is the negation of the false self—the identity we construct apart from God—so that our true self, the one hidden with Christ, can fully emerge.

In essence, Jesus is not calling for the destruction of personality, uniqueness, or healthy personhood. He is calling for the execution of the sinful, self-enthroned ego that stands in the way of a life fully surrendered to God.

### Why This Sounds Liberating to Some

For those who find this calling liberating, it’s because they recognize the exhausting tyranny of the very self Jesus asks them to deny:

- **Freedom from the burden of self-justification.**  
  The autonomous self must constantly prove its worth, manage its image, and justify its existence through achievement, morality, or the approval of others. Denying that self releases a person from an impossible job. The pressure is off; one’s value is now received as a gift from God, not manufactured by the ego.

- **Freedom from the prison of self-absorption.**  
  A life centered on self is a small, anxious, and ultimately lonely life. Taking up the cross and following Jesus radically reorients the heart outward—toward God and neighbor. This expands the soul and fills it with love, purpose, and connection, which is innately liberating.

- **Freedom from the fear of death and loss.**  
  If the self we deny is the one that must “save its life” at all costs, then letting it die means we are no longer controlled by the fear of suffering, obscurity, or even physical death. The worst the world can do is no longer the worst thing that can happen. This is the liberation of the martyrs and of anyone who has ever risked all for love.

- **The discovery of the true self.**  
  Paradoxically, in losing the false, constructed self, one finds the authentic self that God intended. As the grain of wheat falls into the earth and dies, it bears much fruit (John 12:24). The personality doesn’t vanish; it is finally unburdened and vivified by grace. This is not self-annihilation but self-actualization through self-surrender—a deeply appealing prospect.

### Why This Sounds Offensive to Others

To many, this command sounds not like liberation but like a call to psychological harm, enslavement, or the erasure of what makes us human:

- **It sounds like the death of individual dignity and agency.**  
  Modern Western culture places supreme value on self-determination. To “deny yourself” can be heard as a call to gutless submission, to become a doormat without critical thought or personal boundaries. If someone has fought to escape oppressive systems that did indeed demand the crushing of their personhood, Jesus’ words can sound like a divine endorsement of that oppression.

- **It offends the view that self is basically good.**  
  If one believes that the core self is essentially whole, good, and needs only affirmation and expression, then the call to *deny* it is not just strange but morally wrong. It seems like a denial of one’s created goodness, a religious ingratitude for the gifts of reason, talent, and desire. The offense arises because Jesus’ call diagnoses a deep sickness (sin) where others see only a need for acceptance.

- **It can be confused with self-loathing.**  
  Without the context of God’s love and the promise of new life, “deny yourself and take up your cross” can be misconstrued as a command to hate yourself, to seek suffering for its own sake, or to suppress all joy and desire. This sounds like a pathology, not a pathway to life.

- **It seems like a bad bargain.**  
  The logic “lose your life to save it” is counterintuitive. To the natural mind, it sounds like an invitation to be exploited. Why would anyone deliberately let go of the one life they have, on an unseen promise? It appears foolish and weak—an offense to the wisdom that prizes measurable, controllable outcomes. As Paul would later write, the cross is “foolishness to those who are perishing” (1 Corinthians 1:18).

In summary, the self denied is the autocratic ego that seeks to save its life on its own terms. This call is liberating to those who have tasted the exhaustions and chains of that ego and are ready to find their true life hidden in Christ. It is offensive to those who see the autonomous self as the ultimate good, the only source of meaning, or a treasure too precious to risk losing—even for the sake of the gospel.
