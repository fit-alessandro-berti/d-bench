The elder brother's deepest problem is **discomfort with mercy itself**, rooted in his **pride in obedience** and a **lack of understanding of grace**. Here's the breakdown:

### 1. **Discomfort with Mercy**  
   The elder brother's resistance to celebrating his brother's return reveals a fundamental conflict with the father’s extravagant, unmerited love. The father’s response—“It was meet that we should make merry… for this thy brother was dead, and is alive again” (v. 32)—highlights that the father’s joy is not based on the younger son’s worthiness but on his restoration. The elder brother, however, struggles to grasp this grace. His anger and resentment (“thou never gavest me a kid,” v. 29) reflect a transactional view of divine love, where loyalty and merit should dictate rewards. He cannot reconcile the father’s forgiveness with his own sense of justice, exposing a discomfort with mercy that surpasses human logic or fairness.

### 2. **Pride in Obedience**  
   The elder brother takes pride in his faithful service (“these many years do I serve thee…” v. 29), framing his life as a series of dutiful acts. While this pride is not inherently sinful, it becomes problematic when it blinds him to the father’s generosity toward him as well (“all that I have is thine,” v. 31). His inability to rejoice in his brother’s restoration stems from a self-righteous belief that his obedience should earn him recognition, not just gratitude. Pride, in this case, distorts his understanding of belonging to the father’s family, which is rooted in relationship, not merit.

### 3. **Wounded Fairness and Envy**  
   While the elder brother feels wronged by the lack of celebration for his own loyalty (a valid emotional response), his deeper issue is not merely envy or wounded fairness. These are symptoms of his broader struggle to accept a love that operates beyond strict equity. His resentment reveals a failure to internalize the father’s unconditional affection, which extends grace to both sons without regard for their past actions.

### 4. **Anger as a Symptom**  
   His anger (“he was angry,” v. 28) is a manifestation of his internal conflict, not the root problem. Anger arises from his inability to reconcile the father’s mercy with his own sense of entitlement. The father’s patience in confronting him (v. 28) and appealing to shared inheritance (“all that I have is thine”) aims to heal this deeper struggle.

### Conclusion  
The elder brother’s core issue is **discomfort with mercy**, which stems from his pride in his own obedience and a limited understanding of grace. He measures love through the lens of works and fairness, failing to recognize that the father’s family is sustained by lavish, self-giving love—not earned rewards. The parable ultimately challenges listeners (including the elder brother) to embrace a grace that transcends human notions of justice, celebrating restoration over retribution.
