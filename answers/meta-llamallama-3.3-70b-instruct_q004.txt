The kind of trust being asked for in Proverbs 3:5-6 is a deep, wholehearted reliance on God, which involves surrendering one's own understanding and wisdom to His guidance. This trust is not a rejection of intellectual curiosity or critical thinking, but rather a recognition of the limitations of human knowledge and understanding.

Humble self-distrust, as encouraged in this passage, involves acknowledging that one's own perspectives and insights are incomplete, finite, and potentially flawed. It requires a willingness to question one's own assumptions, to consider alternative viewpoints, and to seek guidance from a higher authority. This kind of self-distrust is essential for spiritual growth, as it allows individuals to approach life's challenges with humility, openness, and a dependence on God's wisdom.

On the other hand, mere anti-intellectualism involves a rejection of reason, evidence, and critical thinking, often accompanied by an uncritical acceptance of dogma or authority. This approach can lead to a lack of discernment, a failure to evaluate evidence, and a neglect of the intellectual gifts and abilities that God has given us.

To distinguish between humble self-distrust and anti-intellectualism, consider the following key differences:

1. **Humility vs. ignorance**: Humble self-distrust involves recognizing the limits of one's knowledge, while anti-intellectualism often involves a disregard for knowledge and evidence altogether.
2. **Openness vs. close-mindedness**: Humble self-distrust involves being open to new ideas, perspectives, and guidance, whereas anti-intellectualism often involves a rigid adherence to preconceived notions and a resistance to change.
3. **Dependence on God vs. rejection of reason**: Humble self-distrust involves recognizing one's dependence on God's wisdom and guidance, while anti-intellectualism often involves a rejection of reason and evidence in favor of unexamined beliefs or emotions.
4. **Critical thinking vs. uncritical acceptance**: Humble self-distrust involves critically evaluating information, arguments, and assumptions, whereas anti-intellectualism often involves an uncritical acceptance of information without examination or reflection.

In summary, the trust being asked for in Proverbs 3:5-6 is a humble, wholehearted reliance on God, which involves acknowledging the limitations of human understanding and seeking guidance from a higher authority. This kind of trust is distinct from anti-intellectualism, which involves a rejection of reason, evidence, and critical thinking.
