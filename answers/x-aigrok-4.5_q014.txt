**Treasure trains/directs the heart (heart follows treasure), rather than merely revealing a pre-existing heart-state.**

The text’s logic and grammar run: (1) careful choice of *where* to “lay up” treasure, (2) because location of treasure determines location of heart (“where your treasure is, there will your heart be also”). The future orientation of “will be” and the preceding imperatives (“Lay not up… But lay up”) present the placement of treasure as the formative act that steers affection and attention, not as a mere diagnostic of an already fixed heart. (Of course a hearer who discovers that his or her treasure is already on earth can also *read* the heart from that fact; the primary thrust of the saying, however, is consequential and pedagogical.)

### How possession, attention, and love are bound together
- **Possession** (“treasures… lay up for yourselves”) is concrete investment of what one owns or accumulates—goods, security, future, self.  
- That investment automatically channels **attention**: moth, rust, and thieves become one’s constant worry precisely because the treasure is vulnerable; heavenly treasure redirects the same mental energy toward the imperishable.  
- Attention, once fixed, becomes **love**/heart: the biblical “heart” is the center of desire, valuation, and will. Hence the terse causal claim—“where your treasure is, there will your heart be also”—collapses the three: what you possess draws your watching, and what you watch you come to love and live for.

In short, Jesus treats treasure as a heart-training mechanism: re-locate the store of value, and affection and focus inexorably follow.
