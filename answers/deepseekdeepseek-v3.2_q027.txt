The parable in Luke 18:9–14 primarily **condemns the Pharisee’s spiritual pride and self-righteousness**, which are manifested in his attitude of **comparison with others, contempt for the tax collector, and the use of virtue as self-display**—not necessarily false doctrine in terms of theological error.  

Here is a breakdown:  

1. **Pride and self-righteousness** – The Pharisee trusts in his own moral and religious achievements (“I fast twice a week, I give tithes of all I possess”). His prayer is essentially self-congratulatory, not a humble appeal to God’s mercy.  
2. **Comparison and contempt** – He explicitly contrasts himself with “other men” and singles out the tax collector nearby: “I am not like… this tax collector.” His righteousness is defined by being better than others, not by genuine holiness before God.  
3. **Virtue as self-display** – Though his acts (fasting, tithing) were biblically prescribed, they are used here to elevate himself in his own eyes, turning devotion into performance.  

By contrast, the tax collector **acknowledges his sinfulness and casts himself entirely on God’s mercy**. His posture—standing far off, not lifting his eyes, beating his breast—visually expresses humility, repentance, and a sense of unworthiness.  

The reason his posture is treated as **spiritually truer** is that Jesus repeatedly taught (cf. Luke 14:11; 18:14) that **“everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, and he who humbles himself will be exalted.”** True justification before God comes not from one’s own righteousness but from God’s grace received through repentance and faith.  

Thus, the Pharisee is condemned because his prayer reveals a heart that sees no need for mercy; the tax collector is justified because he recognizes his need and depends on God’s compassion alone.
