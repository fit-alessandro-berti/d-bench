This passage is **more realistic about self-deception than it is merely pessimistic**—though it certainly contains a hard realism about human nature. The key is in the structure: verse 9 describes human blindness, and verse 10 answers it with divine sight.

### 1. Realism, not just gloom
“The heart is deceitful above all things” (Hebrew *ʿāqōḇ*, crooked/twisted) and “desperately sick” or “incurable” (*ʾănûš*) is not saying people are always malicious; it is saying **they are unreliable narrators of their own inner lives**. The rhetorical question “Who can know it?” is answered immediately: not us. We rationalize, we rename vice as virtue, and we mistake passing feelings for deep conviction.

That makes the text psychologically astute. It explains why people can worship idols (in Jeremiah’s context) while still feeling sincere, or why we can defend harmful behavior with perfectly coherent arguments. The pessimism is directed specifically at **self-knowledge and self-assessment**, not necessarily at every human capacity.

Also notice the balance: the passage does not end with human corruption but with God’s justice—“I the LORD search the heart… to give every man according to his ways, and according to the fruit of his doings.” There is pessimism about human transparency, but confidence in divine discernment.

### 2. How to talk about motives
If the heart is an unreliable witness, then human speech about motives should follow these boundaries:

**About yourself: humility and suspicion**
- Do not treat your own motives as self-evident. What feels like “good intentions” can be self-protection, pride, or fear dressed in religious language.
- Examine yourself, but do so with others (community, mentors, Scripture) rather than in isolation. The heart that deceives others deceives its owner first.
- Pray with the psalmist: “Search me, O God, and know my heart” (Psalm 139:23). The proper response to Jeremiah 17:9 is not self-confidence but an invitation for God to do the searching you cannot do alone.

**About others: stick to fruit, not hidden hearts**
- The text itself moves from the hidden heart (“who can know it?”) to observable reality: “according to his ways, and according to the fruit of his doings.”
- In practice, this means judging actions, patterns, and outcomes—not claiming to read souls. You can say, “That action caused harm,” or “That pattern is destructive,” without asserting, “You did that because you hate people / want power / are evil.”
- Be cautious with phrases like “Your real motive is…” Only God tries the “reins” (the innermost seat of affection). Human discourse should stay in the realm of behavior and its consequences, leaving final heart-judgment to God.

**In community and ethics**
- Build structures that depend on actions and accountability rather than claimed intentions. Good institutions ask: “What did you do?” and “What was the result?” not just “What did you mean?”
- When motives must be discussed (e.g., in conflict resolution), speak tentatively: “It seemed like…” or “The effect was…” rather than declaring absolute knowledge of another’s heart.

In short, Jeremiah 17:9–10 teaches that **the heart is a poor witness stand for human trial, but God is the perfect judge.** The practical wisdom is to talk about motives with great humility—rigorously examining your own, cautiously describing others’, and always leaving the final verdict to the One who “searches the heart and tries the reins.”
