Great question. In John 3:19–21, Jesus describes a **moral-psychological dynamic**, not just an intellectual one.

### Why would light be hated?

Because light does two things at once:

1. **Reveals reality** (truth)
2. **Removes cover** (exposure)

If someone’s identity is invested in concealment, then light feels like threat, not comfort.

---

### Truth and exposure

In this passage, “light” is not merely information—it is God’s holy presence in Christ.  
To come into the light means allowing one’s life to be seen as it really is.

That is hard, because exposure brings:

- loss of self-deception,
- loss of control over one’s image,
- confrontation with guilt.

So Jesus says people “loved darkness.” Darkness is not neutral ignorance; it is often **chosen opacity**—a place where one can continue without repentance.

---

### Shame and avoidance

Shame says: “If I am fully seen, I will be rejected.”  
So the instinct is: hide, manage appearances, avoid scrutiny.

That maps exactly onto v.20: “lest his deeds should be reproved.”  
The issue is not that light is evil; it is that light threatens false peace.

In spiritual terms, sin seeks secrecy because secrecy protects autonomy.

---

### “He that doeth truth cometh to the light”

This is striking language: not merely “believes truth,” but **does truth**.  
That means a person oriented toward God becomes willing to be examined.

Why? Because they trust that in God, exposure is not only condemnation—it is also the beginning of healing.  
The same light that judges also cleanses and restores.

So the dividing line is:

- **Darkness:** “I must not be seen.”
- **Light:** “Let me be seen, so what is false can die and what is from God can remain.”

---

In short: light is hated when truth is experienced as threat to the self we are trying to protect. But for the one turning toward God, that same light becomes liberation.
