### Analysis of the Parable
This passage is from the Parable of the Prodigal Son (Luke 15:11–32), a story Jesus told to illustrate God's unconditional love and forgiveness. The elder brother represents those who are outwardly obedient but inwardly resentful, while the father embodies merciful grace. The brother's complaints reveal multiple emotions and attitudes, but interpreting his deepest issue requires looking at the root of his words and actions, as well as how the father responds.

### Evaluating the Options
- **Anger**: This is the most obvious surface-level emotion—the brother "was angry, and would not go in." His refusal to join the feast stems from this, and he vents it through his speech. However, anger is a symptom rather than the core problem; it's triggered by deeper issues.
  
- **Envy**: There's envy evident in his suggestion that the prodigal son has "devoured thy living with harlots," implying bitterness over the brother's lavish reception after squandering wealth. He wishes for similar favoritism ("thou never gavest me a kid"), showing jealousy of the undeserved gift.

- **Wounded fairness**: The brother feels the celebration is unfair—he's served faithfully without reward, while the "wasteful" brother gets a fatted calf. This sense of injustice fuels his complaint, aligning with a transactional view of righteousness (e.g., "I earn, so I deserve").

- **Pride in obedience**: This is a strong contender as the deepest issue. The brother boasts, "these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment," highlighting his self-righteous pride in his own flawless record. He sees himself as superior, which blinds him to grace and prevents him from rejoicing in the father's mercy. Biblical scholars (e.g., in commentaries by C.S. Lewis or modern theologians) often point to this as the root, a form of legalism that values self-earned worth over unearned love.

- **Discomfort with mercy itself**: Closely tied to pride, this captures his unease with the father's boundless compassion. He recoils from celebrating the prodigal's return ("for this thy brother was dead, and is alive again; and was lost, and is found"), viewing it as endorsing immorality rather than embracing forgiveness. The father calls him out gently, affirming that mercy should bring joy, not discomfort.

### The Deepest Problem: Pride in Obedience
The elder brother's deepest problem is **pride in obedience**, as it underpins the other options. His self-righteousness creates envy and a wounded sense of fairness, while his discomfort with mercy stems from believing his works make him more deserving than his brother. Anger is the outburst, but pride is the foundational sin that isolates him from the father's heart of grace. This interpretation is supported by the parable's themes—God welcomes sinners joyfully, challenging the "elder brother" mentality in those who cling to moral superiority. If unchecked, such pride can lead to spiritual stagnation, as it did here, where the obedient son remains outside the celebration.
