Based on the text provided, the Pharisee is not condemned for his *actions* (which were outwardly virtuous), but for the *spiritual orientation* of his heart.

### What condemns the Pharisee?

The condemnation of the Pharisee is not rooted in any one of these factors in isolation, but in a synthesis of **pride, comparison, and contempt.**

**1. Pride and Self-Justification**
The introductory verse provides the key: these people "trusted in themselves that they were righteous." The Pharisee’s sin is the belief that his standing with God is earned through his own merit. He is not praying *to* God as much as he is praying "with himself." His prayer is a report of his achievements rather than a request for grace.

**2. Comparison and Contempt**
The Pharisee defines his righteousness not by God’s standard, but by the failures of others. He uses the "publican" (tax collector) as a foil to elevate himself. By saying, *"I thank thee, that I am not as other men are,"* he transforms a moment of worship into a moment of superiority. His "virtue" is weaponized as a tool of contempt.

**3. Virtue as Self-Display**
The Pharisee lists his fasting and tithing not as acts of devotion to God, but as evidence of his own superiority. When virtue is used to build a pedestal rather than a bridge to God, it ceases to be righteousness and becomes a form of spiritual vanity.

**In short:** It is not "false doctrine" (tithing and fasting are generally commanded), but the **heart's posture**—the belief that one is exempt from the need for mercy because of their performance.

---

### Why is the tax collector’s posture "spiritually truer"?

The tax collector is justified because his outward posture perfectly matches his internal reality. His "truth" consists of three movements:

**1. Honest Self-Assessment (Humility)**
Unlike the Pharisee, who looks at others to feel righteous, the tax collector looks at God and sees his own deficiency. He does not compare himself to the Pharisee; he compares himself to the holiness of God.

**2. Total Dependence (Grace)**
The phrase *"God be merciful to me a sinner"* acknowledges a fundamental spiritual truth: that no human can earn their way into God's favor. By "standing afar off" and refusing to lift his eyes, he demonstrates an understanding that he has no "right" to demand anything from God. He relies entirely on mercy rather than merit.

**3. Contrition (Repentance)**
The act of "smiting upon his breast" is a physical manifestation of grief over his sin. While the Pharisee’s prayer was a celebration of his own ego, the tax collector’s prayer was a surrender of his ego.

**Conclusion:**
The tax collector is "truer" because he approaches God with **naked honesty.** The Pharisee attempted to enter the presence of God wearing a mask of perfection; the tax collector entered as a beggar. In the economy of this parable, the only way to be "justified" is to first admit that you are unjustified.
