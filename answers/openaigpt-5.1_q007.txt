“Judge not” is not a command to stop caring about right and wrong; it is a command to stop *playing God* with other people.

Luke 6:37 draws a line between two things:

1. **Discernment** – seeing clearly what is good, evil, wise, foolish, healthy, destructive.  
2. **The appetite to condemn** – a heart that wants to sit above others, pronounce sentence on them, and enjoy doing it.

They are very different.

---

## 1. The Bible never tells us to abandon moral judgment

If “judge not” meant “never distinguish right from wrong,” much of Scripture would collapse:

- Jesus Himself **makes moral evaluations**:
  - He calls certain actions “evil,” “adulterous,” “hypocritical,” “unbelieving,” etc.
  - John 7:24: “Judge not according to the appearance, but judge righteous judgment.” → Not “don’t judge at all,” but “judge rightly.”

- Paul tells Christians to:
  - “Test everything; hold fast what is good” (1 Thess 5:21).  
  - Correct those who sin (Gal 6:1).  
  - Discern false teaching and harmful behavior (e.g., 1 Cor 5, Titus 1:9–13).

- Jesus in Matthew 7 (parallel to Luke 6) says:
  - “First cast out the beam out of thine own eye; and then shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote out of thy brother’s eye.”  
  → You *do* help your brother with his splinter. You *do* recognize it as a splinter.  
  The issue is not “never notice the splinter,” but “don’t do it with blindness, pride, and hypocrisy.”

So moral discernment is necessary; what is forbidden is a condemning posture.

---

## 2. What “condemning” means here

Luke 6:37 uses two commands:

- “Judge not” (*krínō*) – in this context, not the courtroom function, but **standing over someone to pass verdict as if you were their final judge**.
- “Condemn not” (*katadikázō*) – to **declare someone guilty and disposable**, to sentence.

In other words:
- Not “don’t think,” but “don’t assume the role of final evaluator of someone’s worth.”
- Not “don’t ever say ‘that’s wrong,’” but “don’t delight in pushing people into the category of ‘beyond mercy.’”

To “judge” in the condemned sense is to say, in your heart:  
“You are your sin. You are no longer someone to be patiently borne with, helped, forgiven, or hoped for. You’re a case closed.”

That is what Jesus forbids.

---

## 3. Discernment: seeing clearly in love

Healthy discernment:

- **Takes sin and harm seriously**  
  It doesn’t minimize evil, abuse, injustice, or lies. It says, “This is wrong,” with clarity.

- **Begins with oneself**  
  “First cast out the beam out of thine own eye” (Matt 7:5).  
  Self-suspicion before other-suspicion. You examine your motives, your patterns, your blindness.

- **Aims at restoration, not defeat**  
  Galatians 6:1: “restore such an one in the spirit of meekness.”  
  The goal: healing, truth, reconciliation, not winning a moral argument.

- **Recognizes limited knowledge**  
  Proverbs: “The one who states his case first seems right, until the other comes and examines him” (Prov 18:17).  
  Discernment knows it never has the full story; it leaves ultimate judgment to God.

- **Remembers one’s own forgiveness**  
  Ephesians 4:32: “forgiving one another, even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you.”  
  The conscience that discerns is always standing on mercy, never on superiority.

So discernment *does* evaluate actions: “This is sinful,” “This is foolish,” “This is unjust.”  
But it does so with:
- Humility  
- Awareness of one’s own sin  
- Desire for the other’s good  
- Willingness to bear cost in helping them

---

## 4. The appetite to condemn: when judgment becomes sin

The “judging” Jesus forbids is a *posture of the heart*, not simply an act of reasoning.

Some signs of an appetite to condemn:

1. **Enjoying the superiority**  
   - You feel more alive when someone else is exposed or fails.  
   - Their sin becomes fuel for your identity: “at least I’m not like *that*.”

2. **Reducing people to their worst moment or label**  
   - “He’s a liar.” “She’s trash.” “They’re hopeless.”  
   - No category for repentance, growth, or hidden struggles.

3. **Harshness where you want leniency for yourself**  
   - Your own sins have context: stress, trauma, ignorance.  
   - Others’ sins are just proof of their badness.

4. **Indifference to their good**  
   - You don’t *want* them to change; you want them stuck as examples of what’s wrong.

5. **Speaking as if you are the final court**  
   - Verdicts are total and final: “No good can come from them,” “God is done with them,” etc.

This is why Jesus immediately adds:
> “For with what judgment ye judge, ye shall be judged” (Matt 7:2).  

The heart that insists on living by condemnation will meet a standard of condemnation. If you delight in giving no mercy, you are declaring that you want to live in a world without mercy.

---

## 5. Forgiveness and the end of self-appointed judging

Luke 6:37 ends:  
> “forgive, and ye shall be forgiven.”

That links everything together:

- You don’t **forgive** because sin doesn’t matter.  
  You forgive because sin matters so much Christ died for it—and you needed that death just as much as anyone else.

- You don’t **stop condemning** because all behavior is equal.  
  You stop condemning because only God is fit to be the final judge, and He has chosen to show mercy to you.

So:

- **Moral relativism** says: “Nothing is really right or wrong; don’t judge.”  
- **Christ** says: “Right and wrong matter infinitely—but you are not the ultimate judge. Discern clearly, act truthfully, and never forget you live by mercy.”

---

## 6. In one sentence

“Judge not” does not mean, “Stop making moral judgments”; it means, “Stop enjoying the godlike role of passing final sentence on other people—use discernment to help, restore, and protect, not to feed the appetite to condemn.”
