The passage praises the power of humility, specifically the willingness of Jesus Christ to relinquish his divine status and submit to human limitations, ultimately surrendering to a humiliating death on the cross. This kind of power is counterintuitive, as it involves a voluntary surrender of authority and prestige.

Humility joined to greatness can feel more unsettling than open domination for several reasons:

1. **Contrasts expectations**: When someone with great power or status humbles themselves, it challenges our expectations of how they should behave. We often associate power with dominance, not submission.
2. **Exposes our own pride**: The combination of greatness and humility can highlight our own pride and self-importance, making us feel uncomfortable or even threatened.
3. **Requires vulnerability**: Humility requires vulnerability, which can be perceived as a weakness. When someone powerful shows vulnerability, it can be unsettling because it reveals that even the mighty can be vulnerable.
4. **Defies conventional wisdom**: In a world where power is often associated with aggression, competition, and dominance, humility can seem like an unconventional and even counterproductive approach to achieving greatness.
5. **Inspires self-reflection**: The humility of a great person can inspire self-reflection and introspection, forcing us to confront our own values and priorities. This can be an uncomfortable process, especially if we realize that our own actions and attitudes fall short of the humble example set by someone like Jesus.

Overall, the power of humility praised in Philippians 2:5-8 is a profound and challenging concept that can inspire both admiration and discomfort. It encourages us to rethink our assumptions about power, greatness, and what it means to truly lead and serve others.
