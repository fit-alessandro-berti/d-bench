The danger is not visibility *per se*, but the orientation of the heart that visibility exposes and amplifies. The verse does not forbid public giving (Jesus elsewhere assumes people will give in the synagogue and that good works can be seen—Matt. 5:16). Rather, it forbids doing good *in order* to be seen—“to be seen of them” (*pros to theasthai*). The moral pivot is teleological: the aim governs the act.

Three distinctions clarify why craving the audience is the problem, not merely the fact that others see:

1. **Intentionality vs. externality.**  
   An act done for divine reward and human benefit is not ruled out even if it happens in public; but when the actor’s motivating telos shifts to human applause, the moral quality of the act changes. The goodness becomes instrumental to status, not expressive of love for God and neighbor. The craving collapses the distinction between theater and charity.

2. **Eschatological consequence.**  
   The “reward” (*misthos*) is not a generic benefit but a divine reciprocity tied to covenant faithfulness. Jesus warns that if the human gaze becomes the payoff, the Father has no stake in the transaction. Public visibility does not void the reward; competitive orientation toward public visibility does.

3. **The anthropology of attention.**  
   The craving to be seen is morally dangerous because it makes the actor dependent on the audience for identity and value. This creates a performative self that must maintain its goodness-as-image, which can drift into hypocrisy (the Greek *hypokrisis*—playing a part). Even sincere people can be subverted by the social mirror, letting the audience rewrite the motive mid-act.

Thus, visibility is morally neutral; but when it becomes *ambition*, it converts an act of righteousness into a bid for moral credit in a rival economy. The verse does not demand secrecy as an absolute rule, but checks the heart’s thermostat: whose gaze sets the temperature of your generosity?
