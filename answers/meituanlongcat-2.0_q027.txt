To understand this parable, we must look past the surface-level actions of both men and examine the posture of their hearts. Jesus explicitly states the target of the parable in verse 9: those who "trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others." 

Here is a breakdown of what condemns the Pharisee and why the tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer.

### Part 1: What exactly condemns the Pharisee?

The Pharisee is not condemned by an isolated sin, but by a **syndrome of interconnected sins**. If we look at your list, the answer is not "either/or," but "all of the above"—with one exception. 

**1. False Doctrine (Not the issue)**
The Pharisee is not condemned for false doctrine. Fasting twice a week and tithing on all his possessions were not heretical acts; they were commendable, albeit rigorous, applications of the Law. His theology regarding God’s holiness and the requirements of the Law was technically correct. The problem was not *what* he believed, but *how* he applied it to himself.

**2. Pride (The Root)**
Pride is the foundational sin. Jesus says he "trusted in himself that he was righteous." The original Greek word for trusted (*pepoithotas*) implies a settled, confident reliance. The Pharisee’s confidence was not in God’s mercy, but in his own moral resume. 

**3. Comparison and Contempt (The Fruits)**
Because his righteousness was self-referenced, he had to look down to feel up. He explicitly compares himself to "other men" and specifically to the publican. This comparison breeds contempt. Notice that he does not pray *for* the publican; he prays *against* him, using the publican as a foil to highlight his own supposed superiority. 

**4. The Use of Virtue as Self-Display (The Mechanism)**
This is the most subtle and damning aspect of the Pharisee's sin. He takes genuine spiritual disciplines—fasting and ticing—and weaponizes them. He uses his virtues not as acts of devotion to God, but as currency to purchase God's approval and social superiority. 

**The Ultimate Irony:** Luke notes that the Pharisee "stood and prayed thus *with himself*" (v. 11). Because his prayer was entirely about himself, comparing himself to others, and demanding God notice his resume, God was not his audience. He was essentially talking to himself. 

### Part 2: Why is the tax collector’s posture treated as spiritually truer?

The tax collector’s posture is treated as "truer" because it aligns with the actual reality of the human condition before a holy God. His posture is characterized by three things:

**1. Honest Self-Assessment**
The tax collector stands "afar off" and will not even look to heaven. He beats his breast—a sign of deep mourning and anguish—and calls himself "a sinner." He does not compare himself to the Pharisee; he compares himself to God's standard and finds himself utterly wanting. He does not minimize his sin or make excuses. In the economy of God, truth is the prerequisite for grace. You cannot be healed if you claim you are not sick.

**2. The Absence of Comparison**
The Pharisee’s prayer is entirely horizontal (comparing himself to other people). The tax collector’s prayer is entirely vertical (looking only to God). Because he does not measure himself against others, he has no need for contempt. He is entirely consumed by his own desperate need.

**3. Dependence on Grace, Not Merit**
The tax collector asks for *mercy* (Greek: *hilasthepi*—meaning propitiation, or atoning sacrifice). He is not asking God to notice his good behavior; he is asking God to provide a sacrifice for his bad behavior. He comes to God with empty hands, acknowledging that he has nothing to offer in exchange for his justification. 

### The Verdict

Jesus concludes: *"This man went down to his house justified rather than the other."* 

The tax collector is justified because he embraces the only way any sinner can stand before a holy God: by throwing themselves entirely on God's mercy. The Pharisee, despite his moral resume, leaves unjustified because he rejects grace. If you rely on your own righteousness, you must be perfect; but if you rely on God's mercy, you only need to be honest. 

The tax collector's posture is "truer" because it reflects the reality of the universe: human beings are sinful, God is holy, and the only bridge between the two is mercy. The Pharisee's posture is a delusion, a mirage of self-sufficiency that leaves him standing alone in the temple, praying to himself.
