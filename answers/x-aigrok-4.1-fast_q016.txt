**Discomfort with mercy itself.**

The elder brother's anger (v. 28), envy, wounded sense of fairness, and pride in his obedience ("these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment") are all symptoms of a deeper issue: a fundamental inability to embrace the father's lavish, unearned mercy. 

- He views his relationship with the father transactionally—as a dutiful *servant* earning rewards (e.g., complaining about never getting even "a kid" for a party)—rather than as a beloved *son* who already possesses "all that I have" (v. 31).
- This legalistic mindset resents the "fatted calf" for his "prodigal" brother (v. 30), whom he dehumanizes ("this thy son") and whose repentance he ignores. Mercy to the undeserving undermines his merit-based worldview.
- The father's gentle correction highlights this: the elder brother is "ever with" him but fails to *rejoice* in restoration ("it was meet that we should make merry," v. 32). In Luke 15's context (lost sheep, lost coin, lost son), he represents the self-righteous (like the Pharisees) who cannot celebrate God's grace toward sinners.

Pride fuels his complaints, but the root is rejecting mercy—not just for his brother, but in a way that reveals his own heart lacks the father's generous love. True sonship would mean sharing in that joy.
