In Romans 12:20–21, “victory” over evil is **not** defined as making the enemy lose. It’s defined as **refusing to let evil reproduce itself through you**, and instead **breaking evil’s cycle by active good**.

### 1) What “overcome” means here
The verb (“overcome,” *nikaō*) is the language of conquest. Paul is saying:

- Evil tries to **conquer you** by pulling you into its own pattern (retaliation, bitterness, payback).
- You “win” by **not becoming what harmed you**, and by doing concrete good anyway.

That’s why the surrounding context matters:

- **“Repay no one evil for evil”** (12:17)
- **“Never avenge yourselves… leave it to the wrath of God”** (12:19)

So victory is **moral and spiritual conquest**: evil does not get to shape your character or your actions.

### 2) “Heap coals of fire on his head” — what is that?
Paul quotes Proverbs 25:21–22. The point is not “be nice so you can secretly hurt them.” Rather, kindness in the face of hostility tends to produce one of two outcomes:

1) **Repentance / shame leading to change**  
   The “coals” image commonly functions as a metaphor for **burning pangs of conscience**—the inner heat of remorse when your hostility is met with undeserved generosity.

2) **Or, if they refuse to change, their guilt is clarified**  
   Your good does not create their hardness, but it can **expose** it. Either way, *you* are not acting as the agent of vengeance; you’re leaving judgment with God (12:19).

Importantly, the command is about your action (“feed… give drink”), not about guaranteeing their reaction.

### 3) Is this weak, manipulative, or genuinely strong?
- **Not weak:** It takes more strength to restrain retaliation than to indulge it. This is disciplined, costly love—an active refusal to be controlled by injury and anger.
- **Not meant to be manipulative:** If your motive is “I’ll be nice to make them feel worse,” you’ve reintroduced vengeance through the back door. That contradicts 12:19. The “coals” are an *effect* that may occur, not a tactic you’re told to exploit.
- **Genuinely strong:** It is a strategy of **non-participation in evil** coupled with **positive good**. You win by making evil fail to produce more evil through you, and you also create space for the enemy to be transformed rather than destroyed.

**So the passage defines victory over evil as:** *refusing revenge, doing tangible good to the one who wrongs you, and thereby conquering evil’s power to corrupt you (and possibly converting the enemy), while leaving justice to God.*
