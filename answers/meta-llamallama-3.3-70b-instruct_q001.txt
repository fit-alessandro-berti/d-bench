In Genesis 4:6-7, the picture of moral agency being drawn is one of personal responsibility and choice. The text presents evil as a voluntary action, rather than an impulse, beast, or habit. The Lord's question to Cain, "Why art thou wroth?" implies that Cain's emotions and actions are within his control. The phrase "if thou doest well" suggests that Cain has the ability to choose between good and evil, and that his choice will determine the outcome.

The text places a significant amount of responsibility on the person, Cain, for his actions. The statement "sin lieth at the door" is often interpreted as a warning that sin is crouching, ready to pounce, but it is still up to Cain to choose whether or not to give in to it. The use of the word "lieth" implies a sense of agency and intentionality, rather than a mindless or instinctual response.

The phrase "and unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him" is often seen as a reference to the relationship between Cain and sin. The idea is that sin will desire to control Cain, but Cain has the ability to rule over it, to master it. This reinforces the idea that the text places responsibility on the individual for their choices and actions.

Overall, the picture of moral agency presented in Genesis 4:6-7 is one of voluntary choice, personal responsibility, and the ability to resist or give in to temptation. It suggests that humans have the capacity for self-control and that our choices have consequences. Evil is not presented as an external force that controls us, but rather as a possibility that we can choose to engage with or resist.
