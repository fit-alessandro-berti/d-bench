Luke 6:37, part of the teachings attributed to Jesus, underscnocne the importance of mercy, forgiveness, and non-judgment in interpersonal conduct. It does not suggest that moral judgments never matter; rather, it advocates for restraint and self-reflection when it comes to passing judgment on others. Here's how it differs from the notion that moral judgments never matter and also addresses the difference between discernment and the impulse to condemn:

"Judge not, and ye shall not be judged: condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned: forgive, and ye shall be forgiven."

This verse places a strong emphasis on self-forgiveness, pardon, and abstaining from harsh critique as a means to avoid receiving it in return:

1. Non-judgmental stance as a form of self-protection: The idea here isn't that moral judgments are without value or importance. It's more about recognizing when those judgments might come from a place of personal bias, emotion, or unrestrained emotion rather than fair and objective evaluations. It implies that by refraining from negative judgment, a person can protect themselves from experiencing condemnation they may unwittingly pass on.

2. Condemnation and Forgiveness: The verse doesn't negate the act of judging morally correct decisions (the way one should act, treat others, etc.). Instead, it cautions against condemning others, often a response to misunderstandings, prejudices, or untempered moral indignation. In essence, the verse emphasizes humility, empathy, and understanding over judgment.

Understanding the distinction between discernment and the urge to condemn:

1. Discernment: This is an active form of understanding, often rooted in critical thinking and fair evaluation. It's about perceiving reality, understanding nuances, and weighing actions or attitudes based on moral or ethical standards. Discernment is integral to making informed judgments that respect individual circumstances and intentions—allowing for growth and learning from any errors people make without rash condemnation. It doesn't inherently promote condemnation but rather understanding and thoughtful, compassionate dialogue.

2. Condemnation: On the contrary, condemnation often results from snap judgments, fueled by prejudice, misunderstanding, hate, or a lack of empathy. It tends to be unproductive, divisive, and destructive. Such judgments often fail to consider complex circumstances, often leading to resentment, further division, or internal turmoil.

Thus, this scripture encourages discernment and wise, compassionate judgment, not their absence. It underscores the transformative power of forgiveness and the protection one receives by refraining from condemnatory attitudes—showing that these practices are as much about safeguarding oneself as they are about maintaining interpersonal harmony and kindness.

Put simply, while making moral assessments is part and parcel of ethical behavior, they must be done thoughtfully and empathetically, considering the humanity inherent in all individuals. Reflections on this scripture remind us that condemnation and judgment, especially unjust ones, can inflict harm on both the condemner and the condemned. Thus, wisdom lies in understanding, compassion, forgiveness, and the selective application of judgement, not complete abstention from it, as the latter can often lead to a lack of accountability.

To sum up, this passage isn't saying moral assessments don't matter, but urges us to use them responsibly, kindly, and fairly, steering us away from unfair judgements without compromising our sense of justice or fairness.
