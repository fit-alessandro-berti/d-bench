To answer this question, we first need to ground Ezekiel 36:26–27 in its original context: this promise is given to Judean exiles in Babylon, who had watched their temple, kingdom, and homeland be destroyed as punishment for generations of idolatry, oppression, and rejection of God’s call to repentance. The passage is a core promise of the New Covenant, addressing the root of Israel’s sin (not just their external circumstances of exile) and God’s plan for radical spiritual renewal.

### The Difference Between External Compliance and Receiving a “New Heart”
The contrast between these two states is central to the biblical critique of empty religiosity, and hinges on origin, motivation, and fruit:
1. **External compliance** refers to outward conformity to religious, moral, or covenantal obligations, driven by self-interest rather than genuine inner alignment with God’s will. For the pre-exilic Israelites, this looked like scrupulous temple ritual, Sabbath-keeping, and sacrifice, paired with hidden idolatry, exploitation of the poor, and rejection of God’s prophets (cf. Isaiah 29:13: “These people draw near with their mouth and honor me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me”). It is a human effort to meet external expectations, avoid punishment, or gain status, without changing inner desires or motivations. Outward obedience may occur, but it is performative, fragile, and often coexists with unrepentant sin and hypocrisy.
2. **The “new heart”** refers to a radical, God-wrought transformation of the entire core of a person. In biblical Hebrew thought, the *lev* (translated “heart”) is not just the seat of emotions, but the integrated center of a person’s intellect, will, affections, moral agency, and spiritual capacity. A new heart is not a minor behavioral adjustment or a product of human self-improvement: it is a unilateral gift of grace, where God replaces the sin-calcified, unresponsive core with a new nature that is alive, soft, and naturally oriented toward him. This transformation is paired with the indwelling of God’s Spirit, who empowers the person to obey God out of love and desire, rather than compulsion or fear.

The key distinction is that external compliance is about doing the right things for the wrong (or self-serving) reasons, while the new heart changes the person’s very identity and desires, so that obedience flows naturally from their relationship with God, rather than being a performance to earn favor.

### Why Hardness Is Described as a Material Condition to Be Replaced
The metaphor of a “stony heart” uses tangible, physical properties to describe otherwise abstract spiritual realities, and underscores the severity of sin’s effects and the necessity of divine intervention:
1. **The metaphor’s aptness**: Stone is unyielding, impervious to influence, unresponsive, and incapable of growth, feeling, or relationship—exactly the qualities of a heart hardened by sin. Generations of idolatry, rebellion, and rejection of God’s call had left Israel’s hearts calloused and unresponsive to God’s voice, unable to repent or turn to him on their own. The contrast with a “heart of flesh” (not a weak or sinful flesh, as the term is used in some New Testament contexts, but flesh contrasted with stone to signify aliveness, responsiveness, and capacity for relationship) makes this spiritual state concrete. For the exiles, who had just lost their entire national and religious infrastructure, the metaphor would also have evoked the dead, unresponsive stone idols they had worshipped: their hearts had become like the lifeless idols they served, rather than the living God who created them.
2. **Hardness cannot be softened by human effort**: Describing hardness as a material condition underscores that it is a deep pathology, not a minor lack of effort. Just as a broken bone cannot be healed by the patient’s own willpower, a sin-hardened heart cannot be repaired by human religiosity or moral striving. The need for replacement, rather than just softening, highlights that this transformation is a radical work of God’s grace, not a human achievement. God does not ask the exiles to “try harder” to obey: he promises to do the work of removing their stony hearts himself, as a free gift.
3. **It rejects the sufficiency of external religion**: The metaphor makes clear that Israel’s problem was not their external circumstances (exile, lack of a temple) but their inner condition. No amount of external ritual or law-keeping could fix a heart that was fundamentally opposed to God. The promise of a new heart signals that God’s restoration would not be a return to the old system of external covenantal obligations, but a radical internal renewal that would make true, lasting obedience possible for the first time.

This promise finds its ultimate fulfillment in the New Covenant, through the work of Christ and the indwelling of the Holy Spirit, who gives all who trust in him a new heart, writes God’s law on their inward parts, and enables them to walk in God’s ways (cf. Jeremiah 31:33; 2 Corinthians 3:3–6).
