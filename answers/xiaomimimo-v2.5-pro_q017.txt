This passage from John uses "light" and "darkness" as profound metaphors. To understand why light would be hated, we must explore the relationship between truth, exposure, shame, and the human desire for concealment.

**1. Light as Truth and Exposure**
Here, "light" symbolizes divine truth, moral clarity, and ultimate reality. It isn't just knowledge; it's an illuminating force that reveals things as they truly are. When light enters a room, it exposes what is hidden in the shadows. Similarly, moral and spiritual truth exposes the hidden motives, secrets, and corruption within human hearts and societies.

**2. The Psychological Weight of Exposure**
"Men loved darkness rather than light, because their deeds were evil." This points to a core human dilemma: the pain of exposure.
*   **Cognitive Dissonance:** We often build a self-image that justifies our actions. Truthful light can reveal the gap between our deeds and our self-concept, creating uncomfortable dissonance.
*   **Shame vs. Guilt:** Exposure can trigger shame—a painful feeling about *who we are* (corrupt, weak, deceitful) rather than just guilt about *what we did*. Shame threatens our fundamental sense of self-worth and belonging.
*   **Loss of Control:** Darkness allows us to maintain an illusion of control and autonomy. Truth, especially divine truth, asserts a higher standard and a reality we cannot manipulate. Accepting it means surrendering that control.

**3. The Flight from the Light**
"For every one that doeth evil hateth the light, neither cometh to the light, lest his deeds should be reproved."
The hated isn't the light itself, but its **consequence**: reproach, judgment, and the painful work of repentance. The passage describes a defensive posture:
*   **Avoidance:** To "not come to the light" is to deliberately avoid self-examination, accountability, or environments where truth is spoken.
*   **Rationalization:** In darkness, we can rationalize evil as necessary, harmless, or even good. Light dismantles these justifications.
*   **Preservation of Identity:** If my deeds are evil and I am attached to them, the light threatens my identity. The person defined by these deeds must "die" for the new, truthful self to emerge. This feels like annihilation.

**4. The Contrasting Truth-Seeker**
"But he that doeth truth cometh to the light..."
This person is not necessarily morally perfect. They are characterized by a commitment to truth ("doeth truth"), which includes integrity, transparency, and a willingness to be corrected. They come to the light not for condemnation, but for **clarification and healing**.
*   **The Goal is Different:** Their hope is that their deeds "may be made manifest, that they are wrought in God." They want their good works to be confirmed as coming from God's grace, and their flawed works to be purified. They see light not as a harsh spotlight of judgment, but as a healing fire that refines.

**In essence, the passage reveals a fundamental choice:**
*   **The choice of darkness** is the choice to prioritize the temporary comfort of unchallenged self-justification, the preservation of a false self, and freedom from immediate shame. It is the path of exile from reality.
*   **The choice of light** is the choice to embrace the painful process of exposure, repentance, and transformation. It is the path of integration, where one's life and deeds are aligned with a transcendent reality (God).

The "hatred" of the light, therefore, is the self-protective recoil of the unhealed soul from the very thing that could heal it. It is the short-term preference for the anesthesia of illusion over the surgery of truth. The passage suggests that our relationship with truth is ultimately a spiritual barometer: it reveals whether we are oriented toward self-preservation in the dark, or toward surrender and redemption in the light.
