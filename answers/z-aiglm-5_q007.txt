The difference between Luke 6:37 and the claim that "moral judgments never matter" is the difference between **withholding a sentence** and **denying that a crime exists**.

To understand this distinction, we must separate the act of *seeing* the truth (discernment) from the desire to *punish* or *exclude* based on that truth (the appetite to condemn).

### 1. Moral Judgments vs. Moral Relativism
If moral judgments "never mattered," it would imply that there is no objective good or evil, or that recognizing these distinctions is irrelevant. This is moral relativism or nihilism.

However, the biblical text presupposes that moral reality exists. You cannot "forgive" someone unless a wrong has actually been committed. Forgiveness implies that a moral standard was violated, but the offended party chooses to absorb the cost of that violation rather than demanding retribution.

*   **If judgments never mattered:** There would be no need for forgiveness, because nothing would be "wrong."
*   **Luke 6:37:** Acknowledges that wrongs exist (otherwise there would be nothing to forgive), but commands the listener to renounce the right to execute justice personally.

Therefore, the verse is not an argument for moral blindness; it is an argument against moral **vindictiveness**.

### 2. Discernment: The Eye of the Soul
Discernment is the cognitive ability to distinguish between truth and error, good and evil. It is a necessary faculty for survival and wisdom.

*   **Function:** Discernment is diagnostic. It asks, *"What is this?"*
*   **Example:** A surgeon discerns a tumor. A mechanic discerns a broken part. A parent discerns a child's dangerous behavior.
*   **Spiritual Context:** In the broader context of scripture, believers are actually commanded to be discerning. Later in Luke and other Gospels, Jesus tells his followers to be "wise as serpents" (Matthew 10:16). The goal of discernment is **remedial**—it aims to understand reality so that it can be navigated, healed, or corrected.

If one reads "Judge not" as a command to stop discerning, it would lead to foolishness. One would have to associate with false teachers, ignore abuse, or call evil "good."

### 3. The Appetite to Condemn: The Hunger for Verdicts
Condemnation is not a cognitive act; it is a volitional and emotional act. It is the desire to cast someone down, to sentence them, or to define them by their worst moment.

*   **Function:** Condemnation is punitive. It asks, *"How can I destroy or exclude this person?"*
*   **The "Appetite":** This phrase is crucial. Condemnation is often driven by an appetite—a hunger for self-righteousness, control, or schadenfreude (joy in another's misfortune).
*   **Mechanism:** When we condemn, we are not merely observing a fault; we are attempting to play God. We are seizing the gavel and pronouncing a final verdict on a person’s soul. It says, "You are your mistake. You are irredeemable."

### 4. The Critical Distinction
The difference can be summarized in the posture of the heart:

**Discernment sees the fault and moves toward the person.**
A discerning person looks at an addict and thinks, "This person is enslaved; they need help." They retain the moral judgment (addiction is bad) without condemning the person (the addict is trash).

**Condemnation sees the fault and moves against the person.**
A condemning person looks at the same addict and thinks, "This person is weak; they should be cast out." Here, the moral judgment is weaponized to elevate the self or exclude the other.

### Summary
Luke 6:37 does not ask us to pretend that the emperor has clothes when he does not. It does not ask us to suspend our moral faculties.

Rather, it commands us to renounce the **role of the judge**. It tells us that while we must see reality for what it is (discernment), we must not use that reality as a weapon to harm others (condemnation).

The "measure" spoken of in the verse is the measure of grace. If we view others through a lens of condemnation—defining them solely by their errors—we invite a world where we, too, are defined solely by our errors. If we view others through a lens of discernment—seeing the error but valuing the person—we create a space where forgiveness and redemption are possible.
