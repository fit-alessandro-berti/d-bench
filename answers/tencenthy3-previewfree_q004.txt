The trust described in Proverbs 3:5–6 is a **covenantal, whole-hearted reliance** upon the character and promises of God, specifically in moments where human reasoning hits its limits. It is a call to **humble self-distrust**, not a command to reject the intellect God gave us.

Here is the distinction between the two:

### 1. The Nature of the Trust: Humble Self-Distrust
The text says, *"Trust in the Lord with all thine heart."* In Hebrew poetry, "heart" refers to the center of the will, the emotions, and the mind. "All thine heart" implies an undivided loyalty.

However, the necessary corollary is found in the next phrase: *"and lean not unto thine own understanding."* The Hebrew word for "lean" (*sha`an*) means to support oneself upon something, to rely on it as a crutch or foundation.

**Why is self-distrust required?**
The verse does not say that human understanding is non-existent; rather, it implies that human understanding is ** insufficient** when operating in a vacuum.
*   **Limited Scope:** Our understanding is limited by time (we see the present, not the future), perspective (we see the surface, not the heart), and sin (our reasoning is often biased toward self-justification).
*   **The Nature of the Relationship:** This is a relational trust. A patient trusts a surgeon not because the patient lacks a brain, but because the surgeon has knowledge the patient does not possess regarding the body. Similarly, we distrust our own navigation because we acknowledge God’s omniscience.

**Humble self-distrust** means: *"I will use my mind to the fullest, but I will not worship my conclusions as the final word. I submit my logic to the One who sees the end from the beginning."*

### 2. What it is NOT: Anti-Intellectualism
Anti-intellectualism is the dismissal of reason, study, and intellectual pursuit as intrinsically hostile to faith. This is **not** what Proverbs 3:5–6 advocates. Here is why:

*   **The Proverbs Themselves:** The Book of Proverbs is essentially a textbook on developing "understanding" (*binah*) and "wisdom" (*chokmah*). If "lean not unto thine own understanding" meant "don't think," the entire Bible would be self-contradictory. God commands us to study, to learn, and to sharpen our minds (2 Timothy 2:15).
*   **The Definition of "Understanding":** The Hebrew word for "understanding" here is *tebunah*. In the context of the fall, "own understanding" often refers to **autonomous** reasoning—reasoning that decides what is right and wrong without reference to the Creator. Anti-intellectualism rejects the *tool* (the mind); Proverbs rejects the *autonomy* (the self as the ultimate authority).
*   **The Command to "Acknowledge Him":** Verse 6 says, *"In all thy ways acknowledge him."* The word "acknowledge" (*yada*) implies an intimate, experiential knowledge. You cannot acknowledge God "in all thy ways" (in every practical aspect of life—business, science, art, politics) without engaging the mind to see how His truth applies to those fields.

### 3. Distinguishing the Two

| Feature | Humble Self-Distrust (Biblical) | Anti-Intellectualism (Cultural/Heresy) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **View of the Mind** | A gift from God to be used, but not worshipped. | A liability or a source of pride to be suppressed. |
| **The Action** | **Submitting** logic to God’s revelation. *"I think deeply, but I hold my conclusions loosely."* | **Neglecting** logic in favor of feelings or blind leaps. *"I refuse to think."* |
| **The Goal** | To align one's finite mind with the Infinite Mind. | To maintain a "simple" faith that fears complexity. |
| **Relationship to Truth** | Truth is objective and found in God; we use our minds to seek it. | Truth is subjective or purely mystical; thinking muddies the water. |
| **Result** | A robust faith that can withstand questioning. | A fragile faith that often collapses when challenged by reality. |

### Summary
The trust in Proverbs 3:5–6 is the confidence that **God is a better navigator than I am.**

It is humble self-distrust because it admits that my personal reasoning is tainted by sin and limited by ignorance. It is *not* anti-intellectualism because it does not ask you to stop thinking; rather, it asks you to bring your thinking *captive to the obedience of Christ* (2 Corinthians 10:5).

As the Puritan Thomas Manton wrote: *"To trust in the Lord is not to neglect the use of means, but to use them with dependence on God... We must not lean on our own understanding, yet we must use it; we must not trust in it, yet we must exercise it."*
