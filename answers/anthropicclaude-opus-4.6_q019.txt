# The Contradiction of the Tongue

## The Inner Contradiction Exposed

James identifies something deeply incoherent: the same instrument directed toward the same God produces opposite moral acts. The contradiction is not merely behavioral inconsistency. It is **ontological incoherence** — a fracture in the very nature of the speaker.

Consider the logical structure carefully:

- To bless God is to honor Him as Creator
- Men are made *after the similitude of God*
- Therefore to curse men is to assault the very image you claim to honor
- The blessing becomes a lie retroactively

You cannot genuinely reverence the Artist while defacing His portrait. James is saying that cursing image-bearers **invalidates** the blessing of God, not merely *accompanies* it awkwardly. The two cannot coexist without one being false.

## Why Speech Reveals Moral Disorder So Quickly

Several reasons converge here:

**Speech is the closest externalization of the heart.** Action can be calculated, delayed, performed strategically. But the tongue moves at the speed of thought. It catches us before our self-management arrives. This is why Christ said that from the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks (Matthew 12:34).

**Speech requires less commitment than action, so we are less guarded.** A man might restrain himself from striking someone while freely destroying that person with words — precisely because speech *feels* cheaper. Yet James has already argued it is not cheaper at all.

**Speech simultaneously reveals and enacts.** It does not merely *report* inner disorder; it *performs* it. The curse is not evidence of a separate inner problem. The curse **is** the problem, occurring in real time.

## The Deeper Theological Point

The phrase "made after the similitude of God" is doing essential work. James grounds the wrongness of cursing not in the merit of the person cursed, but in **whose image they bear**. This means:

- The prohibition holds regardless of whether the person *deserves* harsh words
- It holds for enemies, for the wicked, for those who wrong you
- The image of God in man is not earned and cannot be forfeited

This is why James says "these things **ought not** so to be" — not merely "these things are regrettable." He uses the language of moral impossibility. A spring does not produce both fresh water and bitter from the same opening. If it does, something is **wrong with the spring itself**.

The tongue does not have a speech problem. The person has a **nature** problem. And speech, being the least filtered expression of that nature, reveals it first.
