The text moves from "cease to do evil" to "learn to do well" because it recognizes that simply stopping harmful behavior is not enough; rather, one must actively learn and practice doing good. This suggests that goodness is not just the absence of evil, but a positive and proactive form of love and justice.

The use of the word "learn" implies that doing well is a skill that must be acquired and developed over time, rather than something that comes naturally. It requires effort, dedication, and practice to cultivate habits of love, justice, and compassion.

By moving from "cease to do evil" to "learn to do well", the text implies that goodness is not just a matter of refraining from negative actions, but rather a matter of actively pursuing positive actions that promote the well-being of others, particularly the vulnerable and marginalized (as mentioned in the subsequent phrases: "seek judgment, relieve the oppressed, judge the fatherless, plead for the widow").

This understanding of goodness as a practiced form of love and justice is in line with the biblical concept of righteousness, which emphasizes not just moral rectitude, but also a commitment to justice, mercy, and compassion. It suggests that true goodness is not just about individual moral purity, but about actively working to create a more just and loving community.
