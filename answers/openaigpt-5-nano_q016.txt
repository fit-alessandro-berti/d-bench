Short answer: the elder brother’s deepest problem is pride in his own obedience — a self-righteous, merit-based mindset that treats obedience as a contract with the father and resents mercy shown to others.

Why that fits best:
- He declares, “these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment.” That sounds like a claim to earned reward, not a relationship of love.
- He complains, “thou never gavest me a kid…,” which reveals a sense of entitlement and envy about what he thinks he deserves.
- He calls the prodigal “this thy son,” and resents the father’s mercy toward him, implying a judgment that mercy toward others diminishes his own status.
- The father’s response, “Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine,” highlights that the relationship with the father is meant to be about gracious, generous belonging, not earned merit. The elder brother has not grasped that mercy is the father’s free gift, not a reward for long obedience.

In sum:
- Anger and envy are present, but they are symptoms.
- Wounded fairness is a factor, yet it stems from a deeper root: self‑righteous pride and a transactional view of the relationship with the father.
- Discomfort with mercy itself is related, but the core issue is that he does not recognize and celebrate grace, because he thinks his obedience already secures his place.

Pastoral takeaway: the parable challenges religious self-righteousness (the elder brother) to enter into the joy of grace, just as the prodigal is welcomed back.
