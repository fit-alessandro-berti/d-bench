The passage in Amos 5:21–24 rejects the worship not because it is excessive or unbalanced, but because it is actively fraudulent in a specific moral and theological sense. The language is deliberately totalizing—“I hate, I despise,” “I will not smell,” “I will not accept,” “I will not hear”—because the prophet is not describing a deficiency that needs supplementation; he is describing a counterfeit that must be exposed and discarded.

### The nature of the fraud
Amos addresses a northern kingdom in which religious observance was elaborate and well-funded. The people maintained the calendar of feasts, brought the required sacrifices (burnt offerings, grain offerings, peace offerings), and supported professional musicians. From the standpoint of cultic procedure, they were doing things correctly. What made this unacceptable was the simultaneous, systematic violation of the covenant’s ethical requirements.

The surrounding chapters make the connection explicit. The same people who offer sacrifices and sing psalms are:
- Selling the righteous for silver and the poor for a pair of sandals (Amos 2:6, 8:6)
- Perverting justice at the city gate (Amos 5:7, 10–12)
- Trampling the heads of the poor and pushing the afflicted out of the way (Amos 5:11)
- Treating the Sabbath and new moon observances primarily as interruptions to commerce rather than as occasions of loyalty to Yahweh (Amos 8:5)

The worship therefore functioned as a moral alibi. It allowed the participants to maintain the public claim that they were in proper relationship with God while their economic and judicial practices directly contradicted the character of the God they claimed to serve. In covenant terms, this is not imperfect worship; it is perjury. The rituals were being used to ratify a relationship that did not exist in practice.

### Why sharp rejection rather than “better balance”
A call for balance would presuppose that the rituals themselves still carried independent value and simply needed to be paired with improved ethics. Amos (and the other eighth-century prophets who use similar language) rejects that premise. Once the ethical foundation is removed, the rituals cease to be worship and become something else: an attempt to secure divine favor or social legitimacy through mechanical performance. God’s refusal to “smell” the offerings or “hear” the music is the refusal to participate in that transaction.

The positive demand—“let justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like an ever-flowing stream”—is not an additional requirement added to the cult. It is the actual content of the covenant relationship that the cult was supposed to express. When the stream is absent, the temple activities are not neutral or partially defective; they are lies told in God’s name.

### The specific kind of fraud
This is not generic hypocrisy or “doing bad things while going to church.” It is a more precise category: the instrumentalization of religious forms to obscure or legitimize injustice. The worshippers are not merely inconsistent; they are constructing a religious system in which God is expected to accept the role of a patron who overlooks exploitation as long as the correct ceremonies are performed. Amos treats this as a direct assault on God’s character. The God who delivered Israel from Egypt on the basis of justice and compassion will not be domesticated into a deity who ratifies oppression through ritual.

The sharpness of the language is therefore diagnostic rather than rhetorical excess. It is meant to make clear that the problem is not insufficient piety but corrupted piety—piety that has become a mechanism for moral self-deception.
