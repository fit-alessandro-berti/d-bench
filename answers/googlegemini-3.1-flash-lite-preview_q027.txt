The parable of the Pharisee and the Publican is a masterclass in the critique of religious performativity. To answer your question, it is not merely one of these elements that condemns the Pharisee, but rather how they synthesize into a singular spiritual dysfunction: **the replacement of God with the Self as the object of worship.**

Here is a breakdown of why the Pharisee is condemned and why the Tax Collector’s posture is the standard of "truer" spirituality.

### 1. What condemns the Pharisee?
It is a mistake to suggest that the Pharisee’s "doctrine" was false in the legalistic sense. He was likely factually correct: he probably *was* upright, he *did* fast, and he *did* tithe. His condemnation lies in the corruption of these virtues.

*   **Pride as a Filter:** The text notes he "prayed thus **with himself.**" His prayer never reached God because it was reflexive; it bounced off the walls of his own ego. His righteousness became a mirror rather than a window.
*   **Comparison as Validation:** The Pharisee does not define his righteousness by God’s holiness (the only true standard), but by the perceived failure of others ("...or even as this publican"). This is the hallmark of moral vanity: one’s standing is not based on inherent goodness, but on being "better than" someone else.
*   **Contempt as a Barrier:** By despising others, the Pharisee effectively claims he has no need of a Savior. If you are already the gold standard of humanity, you are inoculated against the grace of God. Contempt creates a psychological and spiritual "sealing" that grace cannot penetrate.
*   **Virtue as Currency:** He is attempting to "buy" God’s favor with a resume of good works. He treats God as a debtor, essentially saying, "I have done my part; now you owe me your approval."

### 2. Why is the Tax Collector’s posture "truer"?
The Tax Collector (Publican) captures the essence of what theologians call "the poverty of spirit." His posture is considered truer for three fundamental reasons:

*   **Acknowledgment of Objective Reality:** The Pharisee lives in a delusion of self-sufficiency. The Tax Collector lives in the reality of his own imperfection. Because God is Truth, a prayer rooted in the painful truth of one’s condition is inherently more "real" than a prayer rooted in the fabrication of one's own sanctity.
*   **The Shift from Merit to Mercy:** The Pharisee asks for nothing because he believes he has earned everything. The Tax Collector recognizes that he has no leverage. By shifting the request from "I deserve" to "be merciful to me," he acknowledges his total dependence on God. This is the prerequisite for "justification" (being made right with God).
*   **Sacrificial Humility:** By standing "afar off" and not daring to look up, he demonstrates an encounter with the "Wholly Other"—the holiness of God. The moment a person realizes they are in the presence of God, the appropriate response is not to recite their accomplishments, but to be struck by the gap between the Divine and the human. 

### Conclusion
The Pharisee is condemned not because his life was "wicked" in behavior, but because his heart was **closed.** His righteousness was an insulation that kept him from seeing his need for God.

The Tax Collector is justified because he is **open.** By acknowledging his state as a "sinner," he creates an empty vessel that God can actually fill. The parable teaches that the greatest obstacle to spiritual health is not sin itself, but the *arrogance of thinking one has already arrived.* The Pharisee is full of himself, so there is no space for God; the Tax Collector is empty of himself, making him the perfect vessel for mercy.
