This is one of the most important verses in the entire Old Testament. It's God's answer to a question every religious person asks.

Here is the full context:

> **Micah 6:6-8 [KJV]**
> Wherewith shall I come before the LORD, and bow myself before the high God? shall I come before him with burnt offerings, with calves of a year old?
> Will the LORD be pleased with thousands of rams, or with ten thousands of rivers of oil? shall I give my firstborn for my transgression, the fruit of my body for the sin of my soul?
> He hath shewed thee, O man, what is good; and what doth the LORD require of thee, but to do justly, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with thy God?

### The Courtroom Scene

Micah was a prophet to both Israel and Judah around 735 BC, a contemporary of Isaiah. In Micah 6, God sets up a courtroom. He calls the mountains and hills as witnesses and puts His own people on trial: "O my people, what have I done unto thee?"

The people respond like defendants trying to bribe the Judge: "What do you want from us? More extravagant worship? A thousand rams? Rivers of oil? Should we even sacrifice our own children?"

God's answer in verse 8 cuts through all of it. He doesn't want *more religion*. He wants a transformed life.

> "He hath shewed thee" - this isn't new information. It's already been told in the Torah.

And note who He is speaking to: "O man" - Hebrew *adam*. This isn't just for Israel. It's a universal human obligation.

He summarizes it in three verbs that cover every direction of your life:

### 1. To DO Justly - *asot mishpat*

**Mishpat** is the outward, public requirement. It means justice, fairness according to God's standard.

It's not just *feeling* that justice is good, it's *doing* it.

This is how you treat other people when you have power over them. In business, in court, when no one is watching. Do not rig the scales. Do not exploit. Defend the vulnerable. In Micah's day, the rich were stealing land, the prophets were prophesying for money, and the judges were taking bribes. 

**Justice looks outward to your neighbor.**

> Question: Where has God placed you to be fair when it would be easier to be unfair?

### 2. To LOVE Mercy - *ahavat chesed*

**Chesed** is one of the richest words in Hebrew. It's translated as mercy, loving-kindness, loyal love, covenant faithfulness. It's the love God shows to us - undeserved, tenacious, compassionate love.

Notice the difference in verbs. You *do* justice, but you must *love* mercy. Justice is an action, mercy is an affection.

Many people will *do* mercy out of duty but resent it. God asks you to love it. To delight in showing kindness, forgiveness, and generosity to people who don't deserve it - because that's what He's done for you.

Justice restrains you from harming others. Mercy compels you to help them.

**If justice is giving people what they are due, chesed is giving them more than they are due.**

> Question: Who in your life needs not just fairness, but kindness?

### 3. To Walk Humbly With Thy God - *hatznea lechet im Elohecha*

This is the inward, vertical requirement. And it's the hardest.

The Hebrew word for humbly, *hatznea*, only appears here in the entire Old Testament. It means to walk carefully, circumspectly, modestly, with a lowliness of self.

It's not a fast, flashy walk. It's a slow, attentive, intimate walk - like walking with a friend you don't want to get ahead of or fall behind.

This is the root that makes the other two possible. You can't sustain justice and mercy without humility before God. Without humility, your justice becomes self-righteous and harsh, and your mercy becomes condescending.

Humble walking means you remember you are a creature, not the Creator. You are dependent. You keep in step with Him.

**Justice looks outward. Mercy looks outward with love. Humility looks upward.**

> Question: Are you walking *with* God, or just walking *for* Him?

### Why it all holds together

Jesus said the same thing in different words:

> "Woe unto you... for ye pay tithe of mint and anise and cummin, and have omitted the weightier matters of the law, judgment, mercy, and faith." - Matthew 23:23

Micah 6:8 is the entire law in three phrases. It keeps us from two extremes:

*   Justice without mercy becomes cruel.
*   Mercy without justice becomes spineless.
*   Both without humility become performative.

God is not asking, "What will you *bring* to Me?" He is asking, "What will you *become* with Me?"

Not rivers of oil. A life of integrity, kindness, and humble communion.

What part of that trio is God highlighting for you right now?
