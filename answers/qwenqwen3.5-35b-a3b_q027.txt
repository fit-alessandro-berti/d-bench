Based on the text of Luke 18:9–14 and its immediate context (verse 9), the condemnation of the Pharisee is not caused by a single error, but by a **convergence of attitudes that inverts the proper relationship between God and man.**

To answer your specific question directly: **False doctrine is not the issue.** The Pharisee’s theology was orthodox by the standards of the Law (tithing and fasting were commanded or customary). The condemnation stems from **pride** (the root), expressed through **comparison**, **contempt**, and the **use of virtue as self-display.**

Here is a detailed breakdown of why these factors condemn him, and why the tax collector’s posture is considered spiritually true.

### 1. What Condemns the Pharisee?

The text provides the diagnosis in verse 9 before the story even begins: the parable was told to "certain which **trusted in themselves** that they were righteous, and **despised others**."

*   **False Doctrine (Not the Issue):** The Pharisee's religious claims were factually correct according to the Jewish Law of the time. He fasted (v. 12), gave tithes (v. 12), and thanked God. If the condemnation were about false doctrine, Jesus would have corrected his theology, not his posture.
*   **Pride (The Root Cause):** The prayer is fundamentally self-centered. He does not say, "Lord, help me," or "Lord, forgive me." He says, "God, **I thank thee**." While thanking God is good, here he thanks God for *his own achievements*. He views God not as a Father to approach, but as a celestial auditor verifying his scorecard. Jesus concludes the parable with the principle: "every one that exalteth himself shall be abased."
*   **Comparison (The Mechanism):** Righteousness is defined relationally. "I thank thee, that I am **not as other men are**." This is a "false righteousness" because it is comparative rather than absolute. He measures his standing against the "lowest" tier (extortioners, adulterers) rather than against the Law of God or the Holiness of God.
*   **Contempt (The Heart Attitude):** Verse 9 explicitly states he "despised others." In verse 11, he uses the phrase "even as this publican." He elevates his own standing by actively devaluing another human being. This violates the second commandment of the Law (love your neighbor), which Jesus taught was central to the Law.
*   **Virtue as Self-Display (The Form):** The Greek text says he prayed "with himself" (pros heauton). This can mean he was talking to himself, or that his prayer was a soliloquy for his own edification rather than a communion with God. He performs his piety ("I fast," "I give") as a claim to merit rather than a response to grace.

**Summary:** The Pharisee is condemned because he treats righteousness as a **possession he earns and displays**, rather than a status he receives by grace.

### 2. Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture "Spiritually Truer"?

The tax collector (publican) represents the antithesis of the Pharisee. His "truth" is not in his moral perfection, but in his accurate perception of his spiritual state and his reliance on God's character.

*   **Epistemological Honesty:** The Pharisee claims, "I am righteous." The tax collector admits, "I am a sinner." The tax collector has an accurate diagnosis of his soul. In spiritual matters, self-knowledge is the first step toward healing. He knows he has no merit to bring before God.
*   **The Direction of Grace:** The Pharisee tries to bring his good deeds to God to create a debt God owes him. The tax collector brings nothing but his need. He prays, "God be merciful." The Greek word used is *hilasqti*, meaning to make propitiation or atonement. He acknowledges he needs a sacrifice for his sin.
*   **The Physical Posture:**
    *   *Pharisee:* Stood "with himself" (likely erect, proud).
    *   *Tax Collector:* Stood "afar off" (recognizing he is separated from God by sin), would not "lift up his eyes" (shame, unworthiness), and "smote upon his breast" (a sign of deep grief and repentance).
    *   These physical actions align with the spiritual truth that approaching God requires humility and repentance.
*   **The Verdict of "Justification":** The most important word in the passage is "justified" (v. 14). In Jewish legal terminology, to be justified is to be declared right in the eyes of the judge.
    *   The Pharisee went up the temple looking **upward** to God, but he left the temple in the same state, perhaps with a heavier burden.
    *   The tax collector went down **to his house justified**. He received a legal standing of "righteousness" not because he was morally perfect, but because God accepted his plea for mercy.

### Theological Conclusion
The parable illustrates that **humility is the vessel that receives grace.**

The Pharisee condemns himself by attempting to stand as an equal to God, offering Him gifts that God does not need (His own good works). The tax collector is saved because he recognizes the gap between the holy God and the sinful man, and he jumps across that gap only by God's mercy.

The "spiritual truth" of the tax collector is that **righteousness is imputed (given) by God based on faith and repentance**, whereas the Pharisee treats righteousness as **imputed to himself based on works and performance.**
