• Luke 6:37 is not a ban on moral thinking; it’s a warning against condemnatory, self-righteous judging.

  Discernment and condemnation are different acts:

  1. Discernment is truth-seeking.

  - You observe, evaluate, and name what is good or harmful.
  - It can lead to protection, instruction, correction, and restoration.
  - It is motivated by love, humility, and a willingness to include yourself in the same moral standard.
  - Example: noticing a pattern of harm and helping someone get support or setting boundaries.

  2. Condemnation is a final verdict.

  - It is rigid labeling (“you are a bad person”).
  - It often ignores context, grace, and possibility of change.
  - It can humiliate, punish, or exclude rather than heal.
  - It carries the appetite to “be better than” and to punish.

  In that verse, Jesus says:

  - Don’t act as a judge who throws down a verdict from a place of moral superiority.
  - Don’t condemn, because the one who does will be met with the same spirit of condemnation.
  - Forgiveness is tied to the posture that opens the way for transformation.

  So moral judgment does matter; what is rejected is the spiritual posture of ruthless condemnation. Christian ethics
  often distinguishes:

  - seeing rightly (discernment),
  - from condemning finally (condemnation).

  That’s why you can say:

  - “This behavior is wrong” (discernment),
  - without saying “you are irredeemable” (condemnation).
