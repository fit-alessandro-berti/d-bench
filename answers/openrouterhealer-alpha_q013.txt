This is an excellent question that cuts to the heart of prophetic critique in the Hebrew Bible. The sharpness of Amos's rejection—using words like "hate" and "despise"—is precisely the point. It’s not a request for balance, but a devastating diagnosis of a spiritual condition. Here’s what’s being exposed:

### 1. The Fraud of Performative Piety
The religious acts described (feasts, sacrifices, songs) were commanded by the Law of Moses. They were, in theory, the very center of Israel’s relationship with God. Amos’s shock value lies in declaring that *even the prescribed rituals*, when performed by a society that is structurally unjust, are not just deficient—they are **repugnant** to God.

The fraud is one of **divorce between cult and ethics**. The people believed that ritual observance *per se* secured divine favor. They were essentially saying, “We are faithful covenant keepers” through their temple activities, while simultaneously:
- Crushing the poor in the courts (Amos 5:12)
- Taking bribes (Amos 5:12)
- Selling the righteous for silver and the needy for a pair of sandals (Amos 2:6)
- Trampling on the heads of the poor (Amos 2:7)

The worship had become a **religious screen** for a corrupt economy. It was a form of spiritual laundering—using the appearance of holiness to sanitize a life of exploitation.

### 2. The Fraud of Ritual as Substitution
This is the core of the moral outrage. The people were treating worship as a **transaction or a substitute** for righteousness. The assumption was: “We offer the fat of the beasts, the noise of our songs—therefore God is pleased with us.” Amos turns this completely on its head.

God, speaking through Amos, is saying: **I never wanted the ritual without the reality.** The sacrifices were meant to be symbols of a life given in devotion. When the symbol continues while the reality is abandoned, the symbol becomes a lie. The worship isn’t just “unbalanced”; it’s a **counterfeit faith**. It’s like an abusive spouse who brings flowers on anniversaries while committing ongoing cruelty—the gesture is not just meaningless, it’s offensive.

### 3. The Fraud of a Misplaced Center
Amos reveals that the people’s spiritual center of gravity was wrong. They had placed it on the **maintenance of the religious system** (feasts, sacrifices, songs) rather than on the **character of God** (“judgment and righteousness”).

The closing line—*“let justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like an ever-flowing stream”*—is the true liturgy God desires. This isn’t a metaphor for “a little more justice.” It’s a call for a **transformative, pervasive, unstoppable flood** of ethical living that would reshape the entire social landscape.

The fraud, then, is one of **spiritual misdirection**: the people were looking at the rituals as the goal, while God was looking at the society the rituals were supposed to produce. Their worship had become an end in itself, a spectacle of sound and smoke that insulated them from the cries of the oppressed.

### Why Not Just Ask for Balance?
Because a call for “balance” implies the foundation is sound. Amos argues the foundation—*the heart of the religious community*—is rotten. When the core motivation is corrupt (using religion for self-justification or social standing), adding more “justice activities” is just another layer of performance. God demands a **revolution of the heart that manifests as a revolution of society**.

In essence, Amos exposes a **spiritual fraud of the highest order**: the fraud of believing you can adore God while despising the people He loves. It’s the fraud of worship that comforts the wicked while neglecting the vulnerable. The intensity of the rejection is a measure of how serious God is about the integrity between devotion and justice.

This passage remains a piercing challenge: Does our spiritual life make us *more aware of* and *more committed to* the needs of the marginalized, or does it serve as a private comfort that blinds us to systemic harm? Amos insists on the former.
