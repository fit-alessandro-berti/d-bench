The elder brother’s deepest problem is **a fundamental misunderstanding of his relationship with his father**. While anger, envy, wounded fairness, pride, and discomfort with mercy are all present, they are symptoms of this core issue.

Let’s briefly address each symptom, then synthesize them:

1.  **Anger:** This is the most visible reaction. It stems from feeling disrespected and overlooked after years of faithful service.
2.  **Envy & Wounded Fairness:** He perceives the situation as a zero-sum game. His brother's celebration came at the cost of the elder son's own sense of reward and status.
3.  **Pride in Obedience:** His service was transactional—"I have served you... I never disobeyed your command." He sees himself as having *earned* his inheritance and favor through labor, not as having *received* them as a gift.
4.  **Discomfort with Mercy:** This is the most telling symptom. He cannot celebrate his brother's return because his worldview is built on *merit*, not *grace*. Unearned forgiveness and celebration for a notorious sinner is an affront to his sense of cosmic justice.

**The Synthesis: The Heart of the Problem**

All these symptoms flow from one root: **The elder brother does not understand the nature of sonship.** He views himself as a **"hired servant"** (see Luke 15:19) rather than a beloved son. This is the tragic irony of the parable.

*   **The Prodigal Son's Journey:** He started as a son but left, lived as a slave to his passions, hit rock bottom, and returned as a slave, expecting to be treated as a hired hand. But his father restores him to full sonship.
*   **The Elder Brother's Stance:** He stayed physically at home but **left the heart of sonship**. He worked like a slave to earn what he believed he deserved. He was **angry** because a celebration he felt he *bought* was being given freely to another. He was **envious** because he didn't understand that the father's love is not a finite resource divided among sons. He was **proud** because his identity was built on his own performance. He was **uncomfortable with mercy** because it undermines the very foundation of his self-righteousness.

His deepest problem is that **he has internalized the "works-righteousness" of the younger son.** Both brothers wanted to control the father's estate. The younger did it through demanding inheritance early; the elder did it through tireless labor. Neither simply *received* the father's generous love as an undeserved gift.

**The Theological and Application Insight:**

Jesus tells this parable primarily about God's reaction to **sinners** (the younger brother) and the **self-righteous** (the elder brother). The elder brother's sin is not primarily moral; it's **relational and spiritual.** It is the sin of the **Pharisees** (see Luke 15:2) who "trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others."

His deepest problem is the **absence of genuine love for his brother and trust in his father's goodness.** He cannot celebrate because he doesn't believe the father is just in forgiving. He stays outside the celebration, making his own cold calculation of fairness.

In the end, the father's plea, **"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine"** (Luke 15:31), is an invitation to reorient his heart. The father is saying, "Your position and inheritance are secure, not because of your work, but because of my love. Come inside and share my joy, for your brother was dead and is alive."

**Conclusion:** The elder brother's deepest problem is **a failure of understanding and receiving grace.** He is an elder son who, like the younger, has forgotten what it means to be a child in the father's house. His anger and self-righteousness are the tragic symptoms of a heart that has turned a gift into a wage.
