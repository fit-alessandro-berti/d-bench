Your question cuts to the theological and psychological core of Matthew 6:21. The text does not merely describe a static diagnosis; it describes a **dynamic spiritual law**. Here’s how it answers your two questions:

### 1. Does this text say treasure *reveals* the heart, or *trains* the heart?
**Primarily, it says treasure trains (or forms) the heart, though it naturally reveals what the heart is becoming.**

Linguistically and theologically, the key is in the Greek:  
> `γὰρ οὗ ἐστιν ὁ θησαυρὸς σου, κεῖσε ἔσται καὶ ἡ καρδία σου.`  
> *"For where your treasure is, there your heart will also be."*

- The conjunction `γὰρ` ("for") signals that verse 21 explains *why* Jesus commands store up heavenly treasure in verses 19–20. It’s not an add-on diagnosis; it’s a causal principle.
- The future tense `ἔσται` ("will be") is predictive and formative. Jesus isn’t saying, "Where your treasure happens to be, show me your heart." He’s saying, **"Your heart is migrating toward your treasure."** Over time, wherever you intentionally accumulate, secure, and depend on, your inner life will align.
- That said, treasure *does* reveal the heart once the alignment occurs. But the forward-looking grammar emphasizes **direction over diagnosis**. Jesus is warning against the gravitational pull of investment, not just describing existing devotion.

### 2. How do possession, attention, and love become tied together here?
Jesus outlines a psychological-spiritual mechanism that binds these three:

| Stage | Textual/Mechanistic Role |
|-------|--------------------------|
| **Possession** (`lays up treasure`) | `θησαυρίζω` means to deliberately accumulate, guard, and secure something valuable. This is intentional investment. In the ancient world, treasure wasn’t just wealth; it was **security, identity, and future hope**. |
| **Attention** (implied in the act of storing) | What you secure demands ongoing care, anxiety, planning, and vigilance. Earthly treasure corrupts and can be stolen; this creates chronic worry. Heaven’s treasure requires neither guard nor fear. Attention follows where you invest your resources and trust. |
| **Love/Heart** (`there will your heart be`) | In biblical anthropology, the `καρδία` (heart) is the center of will, allegiance, and worship. Attention, sustained over time, shapes desire. Desire crystallizes into loyalty. Loyalty becomes love. **The heart does not follow wealth; it follows what you make your ultimate trust.** |

**The tie together:**  
Possession anchors attention → Attention shapes desire → Desire fixes the heart → The heart loves what it trusts.  
This is a feedback loop Jesus presents as unavoidable: you cannot continuously store security in one place without your inner life migrating there. That is why verse 24 immediately follows: *"You cannot serve both God and wealth."* It’s not about morality alone; it’s about **devotional gravity**.

### Contextual Note
In first-century Mediterranean culture, "treasure" was synonymous with **where you place your hope of provision and safety**. Jesus contrasts two systems of security:
- Earthly: subject to decay (`moth, rust`) and theft → breeds anxiety, self-reliance, and temporal affection.
- Heavenly: immune to decay/theft → breeds peace, divine dependence, and eternal affection.

### In Short
Matthew 6:21 teaches that **treasure trains the heart by demanding attention, which slowly but surely captures affection and loyalty**. Possession is the starting point; attention is the process; love (in the sense of ultimate allegiance) is the destination. Jesus isn’t just describing a spiritual fact; He is issuing a call to **redirect investment in order to redirect devotion**.
