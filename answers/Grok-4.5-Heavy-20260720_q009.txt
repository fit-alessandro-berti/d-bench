**Victory over evil is defined as refusing to be mastered by it and instead conquering it through active good.**

The passage (quoting Proverbs 25:21–22) gives a concrete command: if your enemy is hungry, feed him; if thirsty, give him drink. The reason given is that “in so doing thou shalt heap coals of fire on his head.” Verse 21 then states the governing principle: “Be not overcome of evil, but overcome evil with good.”

This is not mere non-retaliation or passive endurance. It is proactive. Evil is overcome when the believer refuses to let it dictate the terms of the relationship—refuses to answer evil with evil, hatred with hatred, or harm with harm—and instead answers with tangible, costly kindness that meets real needs. The “victory” (the same root as the language of conquering elsewhere in the New Testament) is moral and relational: good asserts its superior power, the cycle of reciprocal evil is broken, and the person doing good remains free rather than being pulled into the enemy’s logic.

### The “coals of fire”
Interpretations of the image vary, but none of the major ones turn the command into weakness or manipulation:

- The most common reading in Christian commentary (Martyn Lloyd-Jones and many others) is that unexpected kindness produces a burning sense of shame and remorse in the enemy, which can lead to repentance. The pain is internal and potentially redemptive.
- Some (including John Piper) emphasize the Old Testament usage of coals/fire language for divine judgment. Kindness that is rejected heightens accountability; the believer still “overcomes” by leaving vengeance to God (see the preceding verse 19) while remaining free of personal bitterness.
- A minority view notes a possible Egyptian penitential practice of carrying coals as a sign of remorse; kindness would then be the means of prompting the enemy toward that posture. Others treat the phrase more generally as a proverbial way of saying the act will have a powerful, searching effect.

Crucially, the coals are presented as a *consequence* (“for in so doing”), not as the primary motive. The motive is the positive command to overcome evil *with good* and the broader ethic of Romans 12 (sincere love, blessing persecutors, living peaceably as far as it depends on you).

### Weak, manipulative, or genuinely strong?
It is **genuinely strong**.

- **Not weak.** Weakness would be avoidance, indifference, or mere non-resistance that leaves the situation untouched. Feeding and giving drink to an enemy requires deliberate self-mastery, courage, and often real cost. Retaliation is the instinctive, lower-effort response that actually cedes power to evil by accepting its terms. Active beneficence refuses those terms and demonstrates a higher order of strength.
- **Not primarily manipulative.** Manipulation would require the kindness to be a calculated facade whose real aim is psychological control or shaming. The text and its context (love without hypocrisy, leave room for God’s wrath, imitate the God who shows goodness to the undeserving) call for genuine good. Any shame, conviction, or heightened judgment is a secondary effect of an intrinsically right action, not the driving purpose. Doing what is good because it may also produce a searching effect is not the same as weaponizing a false kindness.
- **Strong because it is transformative and Christ-like.** It breaks the reciprocity of evil. It trusts that good is more powerful than evil. It leaves the final outcome (repentance or judgment) with God while the believer’s own character and strategy remain unconquered. In a world that often equates strength with dominance or “owning” the opponent, this is countercultural power—the same pattern the New Testament sees in the cross itself.

Modern ears can sometimes hear a passive-aggressive tone if the kindness is performed with a superior smirk. The surrounding commands (bless, do not curse, live peaceably, love sincerely) guard against that distortion. When practiced with integrity, the passage presents an ethic of remarkable moral and spiritual force: evil is not answered on its own terms and is thereby overcome.
