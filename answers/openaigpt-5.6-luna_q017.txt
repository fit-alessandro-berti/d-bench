In John, “light” is more than information. It is God’s self-revelation in Christ: reality as it truly is, including the truth about God, the world, and ourselves. Light is hated because it does not merely show *what has happened*; it shows *what we are doing*, why we are doing it, and whom our actions affect.

Several things make exposure painful:

1. **Light threatens concealment.**  
   Darkness permits a person to manage appearances. One can hide motives, maintain a respectable self-image, or keep incompatible parts of life separate. To come into the light is to lose control over the story one tells about oneself.

2. **Light threatens autonomy.**  
   Truth makes a claim upon us. If something is clearly seen as evil, we can no longer treat it as merely a preference, a private matter, or someone else’s fault. The light calls for judgment, confession, and change. Thus the issue is not simply that people lack evidence; they may resist the implications of what they already know.

3. **Exposure awakens shame.**  
   Shame says, “If I am truly seen, I will be rejected.” It therefore urges hiding: Adam and Eve hide in Genesis 3, and the impulse remains familiar. We conceal ourselves because we fear humiliation, loss of status, punishment, or the collapse of an identity we have constructed.

4. **Darkness can feel protective.**  
   Even destructive habits may offer temporary safety, pleasure, power, or belonging. A person may prefer the darkness not because it is good, but because it allows life to continue without confrontation. Darkness can become a shelter from truth, though eventually it also becomes a prison.

This helps explain the striking wording: “men loved darkness rather than light.” The problem is not merely intellectual blindness. It is a disordered affection. People can recognize enough of the truth to feel threatened by it and still prefer the concealment that protects their desires. The light is hated because it exposes not only deeds but loves.

Yet John does not present light as mere humiliation. In the Gospel, Christ is the light who gives life, grace, and salvation. His exposure is like the opening of a wound to a physician: it hurts, but the purpose is healing. The difference between the two responses is not that one group has sin and the other does not. Rather, one response hides from the light, while the other entrusts itself to it.

That is the meaning of “he that doeth truth.” It does not mean a person who has never done wrong. It means someone who lives honestly before God—who refuses to make peace with falsehood, receives correction, confesses sin, and allows life to be examined. Such a person “cometh to the light,” not to display personal goodness, but so that it may be seen that the deeds are “wrought in God.” Even the good revealed in that person is attributed to God’s work, not to self-righteousness.

So the desire not to be seen clearly is ultimately a desire to preserve a certain self: unjudged, self-defined, and in control. The invitation of the passage is not, “Make yourself worthy before appearing.” It is, “Come into the truth, where what is hidden can be healed.” Light condemns the refusal to be seen, but for the one who comes to it, the same light becomes mercy.
