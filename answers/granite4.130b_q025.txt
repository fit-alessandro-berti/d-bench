Matthew 6:1 highlights a tension between outward acts of kindness and the inner motives that drive them. The key point Jesus makes is not merely that **visibility** of good deeds is problematic; rather, it is the **inner desire to be seen by others**—the craving for human recognition—that turns even righteous actions into something morally risky.

**Why the craving matters more than visibility:**

1. **The Reward Shifts:**  
   - If we give or help *because* we want people to notice and applaud us, our “reward” becomes the praise of others. Jesus warns that in such a case we have *no reward from the Father* (the heavenly source of true commendation). The motivation has moved away from honoring God toward satisfying human ego.

2. **Hypocrisy vs. Authenticity:**  
   - Public goodness can easily become “hypocritical” when it is performed primarily for show. The verse uses the phrase *“to be seen of them,”* emphasizing that the act’s visibility serves a secondary, self‑serving purpose. If the motive were pure love or compassion, even a public display would not negate its moral worth.

3. **Internal Condition Determines Moral Quality:**  
   - Christian ethics (and many other moral frameworks) judge actions by intention as well as outcome. A deed done out of genuine humility and faithfulness to God remains good regardless of how many eyes witness it. Conversely, the same deed infused with a craving for accolades corrupts its ethical purity.

4. **The Danger Lies in the Heart:**  
   - Jesus repeatedly stresses that sin begins “in the heart” (e.g., Matthew 5:21‑22). By warning against *craving* to be seen, He points to an inward moral danger—pride, self‑exaltation, and reliance on human approval—that can poison any charitable act.

**Conclusion:**  
The verse’s concern is not that good deeds should never be observed; it warns that **the desire for visibility as a means of personal esteem makes public goodness morally dangerous**. When the motive shifts from serving God or others to seeking human admiration, the act loses its true righteousness and forfeits divine reward. Thus, the problem lies in *the craving to be seen*, not merely in the fact that people can see the good deed.
