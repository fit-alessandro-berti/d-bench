In Matthew 12:34–35, Jesus delivers a profound lesson in moral psychology, establishing a direct, causal link between human speech and the inner state of the soul. He uses two vivid metaphors to explain this connection: the **overflow of a vessel** (“abundance”) and the **withdrawal from a storehouse** (“treasure”). 

This passage demonstrates that words are not merely isolated, neutral sounds; rather, they are the currency of our inner life. Ultimately, the passage suggests that words are **all three: symptoms, choices, and revelations.**

Here is an analysis of how the passage connects speech to our hidden moral stockpiles, and how it embodies these three categories.

---

### 1. The Connection to the "Hidden Moral Stockpiles"
Jesus uses the Greek word *thesauros* (translated as "treasure" or "temple storehouse") to describe the heart. In the ancient world, a *thesauros* was a secure place where valuables—gold, grain, or armor—were kept. 

By applying this to the moral life, Jesus suggests that:
* **The heart is cumulative:** We do not remain morally neutral. Every thought, desire, reaction, and habit we indulge is a "deposit" into the spiritual warehouse of our heart. Over time, we amass either a "good treasure" or an "evil treasure."
* **Speech is the overflow (*perisseuma*):** The Greek word for "abundance" refers to a surplus, like a cup overflowing its brim. When the heart is packed to capacity with a certain moral quality, it cannot contain it. The pressure of this stockpile forces an outlet, and that outlet is the mouth. Speech is the inevitable leakage of our internal inventory.

---

### 2. Words as *Symptoms* (The Diagnostic View)
A symptom is an involuntary physical sign of an underlying, unseen condition. In this sense, **words are spiritual symptoms.**

* **The Inevitability of Leakage:** Jesus asks the Pharisees, *“How can ye, being evil, speak good things?”* He implies a spiritual impossibility. A corrupt heart cannot produce pure speech any more than a poisoned well can yield fresh water. 
* **The Unconscious Betrayal:** While a person can try to feign goodness, under pressure, the "abundance" of the heart will spill out. Anger, resentment, pride, or—conversely—grace, humility, and love will slip past our filters. In this framework, our words are diagnostic tools; they tell us, and the world, the actual health of our soul. 

### 3. Words as *Choices* (The Volitional View)
While the *overflow* of the heart is automatic, the *stockpiling* of the heart is a matter of free will. Therefore, **words are also choices.**

* **The Responsibility of Cultivation:** The "good man" actively and intentionally deposits "good things" into his heart. He chooses what to meditate on, what to love, and how to respond to life's circumstances. 
* **The Sowing and Reaping of Language:** We do not just choose our words in the moment of speaking; we choose them days, months, and years prior by what we allow to dwell in our minds. By choosing our internal focus (our "treasure"), we are choosing the words we will eventually speak. The act of "bringing forth" (*ekballei*—literally, "to hurl or cast out") implies an active agency.

### 4. Words as *Revelations* (The Epistemological View)
A revelation pulls back the curtain on something hidden. In Matthew 12, **words are revelations because they unmask hypocrisy.**

* **Exposing the Hidden Self:** Jesus was addressing the Pharisees, who were masters of religious theater. They looked righteous outwardly, but Jesus called them a "generation of vipers." Their polished theological arguments could not hide the venom in their hearts. 
* **The Ultimate Proof:** By tracing speech back to the "treasure of the heart," Jesus strips away pretension. You can lie about who you are, but your consistent pattern of speech—how you speak of others, how you react when insulted, what you boast about—will eventually reveal your true identity. Words are the medium through which the invisible soul becomes visible to the world.

---

### Conclusion
In Matthew 12:34–35, Jesus presents a holistic view of human communication. 

Words are **choices** because we are responsible for the moral inventory we build in our hearts. They are **symptoms** because they naturally and inevitably bubble up from our deep-seated spiritual health (or illness). And they are **revelations** because they ultimately strip away our pretenses, exposing the true nature of our hidden stockpiles to God and the world.
