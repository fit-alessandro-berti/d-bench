Based on Matthew 6:1, the moral danger of public goodness lies entirely in **the craving to be seen, not in the visibility itself.** 

Jesus is diagnosing a problem of *motive*, not a problem of *geography* (where the deed takes place). Here is a breakdown of what makes public goodness morally dangerous according to this verse:

### 1. The Distinction Between Visibility and Motive
To understand that visibility itself is not the problem, we only have to look one chapter earlier. In Matthew 5:16, Jesus commands: *"Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."* 

If visibility were inherently evil, these two verses would contradict each other. The key distinction is found in the phrase **"to be seen of them."** 
* In Matthew 5, the visibility acts as a window, drawing human eyes *through* the good deed to glorify God. 
* In Matthew 6, the visibility acts as a mirror, drawing human eyes to stop and glorify the *person* doing the deed. The danger is not that people are watching; the danger is doing the deed *because* you want them to watch.

### 2. The Danger of Spirituality as "Theater"
In the original Greek text, the phrase translated as "to be seen" is *theathēnai*, which is the root of the English word "theater" or "theatrical." 

The moral danger here is turning righteousness into a performance. When the craving to be seen takes over, a person’s spiritual life becomes an act of hypocrisy (a word that historically referred to stage actors wearing masks). Goodness ceases to be an act of genuine love toward God or neighbor, and instead becomes a tool for PR, reputation management, and ego-building. 

### 3. A Short-Circuited Spiritual Economy
Jesus warns: *"otherwise ye have no reward of your Father."* He outlines a strict spiritual economy. Every act of perceived goodness comes with a reward, but you only get to collect it from one source.

If your deep, internal motive is human applause, social capital, or a reputation for being a "good person," the praise of the crowd becomes your payment. Once you receive that human validation, the transaction is closed. God owes you nothing because you did not do it for Him; you did it to purchase human admiration.

### 4. The Idolatry of the Crowd
Ultimately, the craving to be seen makes public goodness dangerous because it displaces God as the primary audience of a person's life. 

If a person does their alms "to be seen of men," they are treating the opinions of humans as more valuable than the gaze of God. It shifts the center of moral authority away from the "Father which is in heaven" and hands it entirely to society.

**In summary:** Public goodness becomes morally dangerous when it is weaponized for self-glorification. The problem is not that an action is visible, but that the human heart has a fatal tendency to use "goodness" as a disguise for narcissism.
