Short answer: Proverbs 3:5–6 calls for wholehearted, relational trust in God — a submissive reliance on his wisdom, character and direction — not a crude rejection of reason or learning. “Do not lean on your own understanding” means don’t make your own limited, sinful, provisional judgment the final authority. It calls you to humble self-distrust (a disciplined humility that still uses mind and gifts) rather than to anti‑intellectualism (the refusal to think, learn, or weigh evidence).

How they differ

- Humble self-distrust
  - Recognizes human finitude, bias, and sinfulness; therefore seeks God’s wisdom and correction.
  - Uses reason, evidence, experience and gifts, but treats them as subordinate and provisional.
  - Is willing to change conclusions, repent, and submit plans when God (through Scripture, prayer, counsel, providence) directs otherwise.
  - Practices prayerful discernment, Scripture reading, and consultation with wise people.
  - Leads to responsible action: planning, study, and effort done in dependence on God (e.g., pray and then act; plan and then trust).
  - Example: a doctor who prays, consults colleagues and the literature, and humbly asks God for wisdom about a difficult case.

- Anti-intellectualism
  - Dismisses or distrusts reason, evidence, expertise and learning as either irrelevant or harmful.
  - Often substitutes slogans or pietistic impulses for careful thinking and responsibility.
  - Can become passive (“God will do it, so I need do nothing”) or irrational (“facts contradict my claim so I’ll ignore them”).
  - Tends to isolate faith from the disciplines God gave people for learning about the world.
  - Example: refusing proven medical treatment because one believes true trust requires avoiding human means.

Brief practical guide to living Proverbs 3:5–6 without becoming anti‑intellectual
- Pray for wisdom (James 1:5) and then study the matter carefully.
- Read Scripture to shape your basic assumptions and values (the fear of the Lord is the beginning of wisdom — Prov. 1:7).
- Seek expert advice and wise counsel, and weigh evidence soberly.
- Make plans and take steps, but hold them with humility and openness to God’s redirection.
- Repent and revise when your understanding proves partial or wrong; keep learning.

One-sentence summary: Trusting the Lord here is wholehearted dependence that puts God’s wisdom over your own fallible judgments while still using your mind, gifts and responsible means — not an anti‑intellectual refusal to think or learn.
