**Step‑by‑step analysis of Matthew 12:34–35**

1. **Context of the passage**  
   - Jesus is confronting the Pharisees who accuse Him of casting out demons by the power of Satan (vv. 22‑37).  
   - The immediate objection (vs. 34) is that the Pharisees, despite their outward piety, call Him “a gluttonous man and a drunkard, a friend of tax‑gatherers and sinners.”  
   - Jesus turns the criticism back on them, asking how they, “being evil,” can “speak good things.”

2. **The core metaphor – “heart” as a “treasure”**  
   - **“Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh.”**  
     - This phrase establishes a direct causal link: the interior disposition (the *heart*) determines what comes out of the *mouth*.  
   - **“A good man out of the good treasure of the heart bringeth forth good things: and an evil man out of the evil treasure bringeth forth evil things.”**  
     - The *heart* is likened to a storehouse (*treasure*). What is stored there is retrieved and expressed verbally.

3. **Speech as a **symptom** of inner condition**  
   - Because the mouth cannot speak “good things” if the heart is “evil,” the words we utter reveal the state of our inner moral stockpile.  
   - Example: A person who constantly uses sarcasm or gossip likely has an “evil treasure.” Their speech is **symptomatic** of that hidden malice.

4. **Speech as a **revelation** of hidden moral stockpiles**  
   - Jesus explicitly calls the Pharisees “a generation of vipers,” implying their hypocrisy is *discovered* through their words.  
   - The passage says the heart’s “treasure” is *brought forth* by the mouth, meaning our utterances **show** what has been accumulated (or neglected) within.  
   - Thus, speech functions **detectively**, exposing the moral inventory that otherwise remains concealed.

5. **Speech as a **choice** that reflects inner disposition**  
   - Even though the heart *produces* the words, humans still make *decisions* about whether to speak those words.  
   - The verse implies a two‑fold relationship: (a) the heart *predisposes* us toward certain kinds of speech, and (b) we *choose* to let that predisposition be expressed.  
   - Example: A person may feel angry impulses (evil treasure) but decide to respond calmly (a conscious choice). The initial feeling is the *stockpile*, the response is the *exercise of choice*.

6. **All three perspectives together**  
   - **Symptom:** Our words *show* the state of our heart.  
   - **Revelation:** They *expose* what we have stored (good or evil).  
   - **Choice:** Yet we inevitably *choose* to let that stored content emerge.  
   - Therefore, the passage teaches that speech is **simultaneously** an indicator, a disclosure, and an expression of a prior moral decision‑making process.

### Conclusion

Matthew 12:34–35 connects speech with hidden moral stockpiles in the heart by presenting the mouth as the conduit through which the heart’s “treasure” is manifested. It suggests that words serve **all three** functions: they are **symptoms** of an underlying moral condition, **revelatory** indications of what has been stored, and the **fruit of a choice** to let that stored material surface. In theological terms, the passage underscores the biblical principle that “out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh,” warning that genuine righteousness is not merely external conformity but is rooted in the interior righteousness of the heart.
