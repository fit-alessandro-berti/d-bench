The parable of the Pharisee and the tax collector (Luke 18:9–14) is a profound critique of religious self-righteousness and a celebration of humility before God. The Pharisee’s condemnation is not due to a single flaw but a combination of interrelated spiritual failures. Here’s a breakdown of what condemns him and why the tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer:

### **What Condemns the Pharisee?**
1. **Pride (Self-Righteousness)**
   - The Pharisee’s prayer is centered on *himself* ("I thank you that *I* am not like other men… *I* fast twice a week, *I* give tithes…"). His focus is on his own perceived moral superiority rather than on God’s mercy or grace.
   - Jesus introduces the parable by saying it is aimed at those who **"trusted in themselves that they were righteous"** (v. 9). The Pharisee’s confidence is in his own works, not in God’s justification.

2. **Comparison and Contempt for Others**
   - He defines his righteousness *negatively*—by how much better he is than others ("not like other men: extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even as this publican").
   - His prayer is not just self-congratulatory but *disdainful* toward the tax collector (a group despised in Jewish society). This violates the command to **"love your neighbor"** (Lev. 19:18; Matt. 22:39) and reflects a heart of **judgmentalism** (Matt. 7:1–5).

3. **Virtue as Self-Display (Hypocrisy)**
   - While his fasting and tithing are good in themselves, he uses them as **badges of honor** to impress God (and perhaps others). His religion is performative, not relational.
   - Jesus elsewhere condemns the Pharisees for doing righteous deeds **"to be seen by others"** (Matt. 6:1–6; 23:5–7). The Pharisee’s prayer is essentially a **monologue of self-praise**, not a dialogue with God.

4. **Lack of Repentance or Dependence on God**
   - The Pharisee does not ask for mercy, forgiveness, or help—he sees no need for it. His prayer is **self-sufficient**, whereas true prayer acknowledges dependence on God (cf. the Lord’s Prayer: "Forgive us our debts," Matt. 6:12).
   - By contrast, the tax collector’s prayer is a cry for mercy, recognizing his sinfulness.

5. **False Doctrine? (Indirectly)**
   - While the Pharisee’s *actions* (fasting, tithing) align with Jewish law, his **theology of justification** is flawed. He assumes righteousness is earned by human effort rather than received by grace (cf. Rom. 3:20; Gal. 2:16).
   - His error is not in the *practices* themselves but in his **misplaced trust** in them for salvation.

### **Why Is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?**
1. **Humility Before God**
   - He stands **"afar off"** (a sign of unworthiness, cf. Ezra 9:6) and **"would not lift up his eyes to heaven"** (a posture of shame and penitence, cf. Luke 15:21).
   - He **beats his breast** (a gesture of mourning and repentance, cf. Luke 23:48). His entire demeanor reflects **brokenhearted contrition** (Ps. 51:17).

2. **Honesty About Sin**
   - He does not minimize his guilt but confesses it openly: **"God, be merciful to me, a sinner!"** (v. 13). This aligns with Jesus’ teaching that **"those who are well have no need of a physician, but those who are sick"** (Mark 2:17).
   - His prayer echoes Psalm 51:1: **"Have mercy on me, O God, according to your steadfast love."**

3. **Faith in God’s Mercy (Not Self)**
   - Unlike the Pharisee, he does not trust in his own righteousness but **throws himself on God’s grace**. This is the essence of saving faith (cf. Rom. 4:5; Eph. 2:8–9).
   - Jesus declares him **"justified"** (v. 14)—a legal term meaning "declared righteous" by God—not because of his works, but because of his **faith in God’s mercy**.

4. **No Comparison or Contempt**
   - He does not measure himself against others; his focus is solely on his own need for God. This fulfills Jesus’ call to **"judge not"** (Matt. 7:1) and to **"take the log out of your own eye"** (Matt. 7:5).

### **Theological Implications**
- **Justification by Grace Alone**: The tax collector is justified not by his deeds but by **faith in God’s mercy**—a foreshadowing of Paul’s doctrine of justification by faith (Rom. 3:28; Gal. 2:16).
- **True Righteousness is Humble**: The Pharisee’s "righteousness" is external and self-centered; the tax collector’s is **internal and God-centered**.
- **God Opposes the Proud**: James 4:6 ("God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble") is illustrated here. The Pharisee’s pride leads to rejection; the tax collector’s humility leads to justification.

### **Conclusion**
The Pharisee is condemned primarily for **pride, self-righteousness, contempt for others, and performative religion**, while the tax collector is justified because of his **humility, repentance, and faith in God’s mercy**. The parable teaches that **true righteousness is not found in comparing ourselves to others or trusting in our own goodness, but in acknowledging our sin and casting ourselves on God’s grace**.
