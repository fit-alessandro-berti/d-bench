**Answer:**

The Pharisee is condemned not by any single factor alone, but by a *combination* of attitudes that reveal a fundamental spiritual flaw: **his reliance on self-righteousness and self-exaltation rather than on God’s grace**. Let’s examine the options you’ve offered:

1. **False doctrine** – Not really. The Pharisee appears to uphold correct religious practices (fasting, tithing, separation from sinners). His error is not in *what* he believes or teaches, but in *how* he views himself in relation to God and others.

2. **Pride** – Yes, this is central. His prayer is self-congratulatory: “I thank you that I am not like other people.” He positions himself as morally superior, judging others while ignoring his own need for mercy. Pride is the root of his spiritual problem.

3. **Comparison** – He compares himself to others, but not just casually—he uses their perceived immorality to elevate his own status. This self-comparison is not neutral; it’s a tool to inflate his self-image.

4. **Contempt** – He “despises others,” as the text says. His attitude toward the tax collector is one of disdain, which reveals a hardened heart. Contempt separates him from compassion and humility.

5. **Use of virtue as self-display** – This is perhaps the most precise insight. The Pharisee *does* perform religious virtues (fasting, tithing), but he presents them not as offerings to God, but as evidence of his own righteousness. His virtue becomes a mask for self-glory, not a sign of humility before God.

Thus, **the core issue is pride**, which manifests through **comparison**, **contempt**, and the **use of religious virtue as a means of self-display**. He prays *to himself*, not to God—his prayer is a monologue of self-praise, not a supplication for mercy.

In contrast, the **tax collector’s posture**—standing “afar off,” not lifting his eyes to heaven, but smiting his breast in a gesture of deep remorse—is spiritually truer because it reflects **humility and repentance**. He acknowledges his sinfulness and seeks God’s mercy, not his own merit. His posture is one of dependence on divine grace.

Jesus concludes: “This man went down to his house justified rather than the other.” The tax collector, though a sinner by societal standards, is declared righteous because he is *broken before God*. The Pharisee, though outwardly righteous, remains unjustified because he does not recognize his need for grace.

In short: **It is not virtue itself, but self-righteousness and pride in virtue, that condemns the Pharisee. True righteousness is marked not by self-exaltation, but by humility, repentance, and dependence on God’s mercy.**
