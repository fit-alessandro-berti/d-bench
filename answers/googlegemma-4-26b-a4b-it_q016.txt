The text suggests that while the elder brother exhibits all the symptoms you listed—anger, envy, and a sense of wounded fairness—these are merely the outward expressions of a much deeper, structural problem in his soul.

The most profound diagnosis is that the elder brother’s problem is **a lack of relational intimacy, masked by a righteousness based on merit.**

Here is an analysis of why his problem transcends the individual emotions you listed:

### 1. The Failure of "Service" vs. "Sonship"
The elder brother defines his relationship with his father through the lens of **contractual obligation** rather than **covenantal relationship**. He says, *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee..."* 

In the Greek, the word used for "serve" (*douleuo*) often carries the connotation of a slave serving a master. He does not say, "I have lived with you as a son"; he says, "I have worked for you as an employee." He views his obedience as a currency used to purchase specific rewards (the "kid"). Because he views himself as a servant earning a wage, he is outraged when the father grants a "gift" (the fatted calf) to someone who earned nothing.

### 2. Pride in Obedience (Legalism)
His problem is not that he is disobedient, but that his obedience is **self-serving**. He uses his sinlessness as a weapon. By saying, *"neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment,"* he is not expressing humility or gratitude for grace; he is presenting a ledger of his own merit. He believes his righteousness makes him superior to his brother, which creates a barrier between him and the father. He loves the father's *commands*, but he does not love the father's *heart*.

### 3. Discomfort with Mercy (The Theological Root)
The deepest psychological and spiritual problem is his **rejection of Grace.** 

Grace, by definition, is unmerited favor. The elder brother’s entire worldview is built on **meritocracy**: *I do good, I get good; he does bad, he gets bad.* When the father demonstrates mercy toward the prodigal, it invalidates the elder brother's entire way of life. If the father can be moved by nothing more than a repentant heart, then the elder brother's years of disciplined "service" lose their status as a means of leverage. He is uncomfortable with mercy because mercy threatens his sense of control and his sense of superiority.

### 4. The Tragedy of "Thou Art Ever With Me"
The father’s response reveals the ultimate tragedy: *"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine."*

The elder brother lived in the father's house, enjoyed the father's protection, and possessed all the father's wealth, yet **he lived like an orphan.** He felt he was "owed" something, failing to realize that he already possessed everything. He was physically present but relationally distant.

### Summary
While his **anger** is the symptom and his **envy** is the driver, his deepest problem is **the substitution of legalism for love.** He sought to be a "good servant" to avoid being a "vulnerable son," and in doing so, he became a stranger to the very joy (the feast) he claimed to be working for.
