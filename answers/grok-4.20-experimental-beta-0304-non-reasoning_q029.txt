**The moral posture required is humble, self-aware, restorative gentleness — not superiority, harshness, or detached judgment.**

### 1. What the verse actually requires

- **“Ye which are spiritual”**  
  The correctors must themselves be walking in the Spirit (cf. Galatians 5:16–25). This is not a claim to sinless perfection, but a description of people whose lives are increasingly marked by the fruit of the Spirit rather than the works of the flesh. Only they are qualified to restore.

- **“Restore such an one” (καταρτίζετε – *katartizete*)**  
  The verb is the same one used for mending fishing nets (Matthew 4:21) or setting a broken bone. The goal is not public shaming, expulsion, or score-settling; it is healing and reintegration. Restoration is the end in view, not condemnation.

- **“In the spirit of meekness” (πνεύματι πραΰτητος – *pneumati prautētos*)**  
  Meekness is not weakness. In the New Testament it is power under control — strength that is willingly restrained and submitted to God. It is the opposite of the “conceit,” “provoking,” and “envying” Paul has just condemned in 5:26. Meekness flows from the cross-shaped character of Jesus (Matthew 11:29; 2 Corinthians 10:1).

- **“Considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted”**  
  Literally: “keeping an eye on yourself” (σκοπῶν σεαυτόν – *skopōn seauton*). This is vigilant self-suspicion. The corrector must treat his own remaining vulnerability to the same sin (or a related one) with deadly seriousness. Temptation is not theoretical; it is a live possibility for the most “spiritual” person.

### 2. Why these three things are welded together

Paul binds **restoration**, **meekness**, and **self-suspicion** into a single moral posture because each one without the others quickly becomes deformed:

- Restoration without meekness becomes **harsh, surgical correction** — “I’m right, you’re broken, fix it or get out.” It wounds rather than heals.
- Meekness without restoration becomes **sentimental passivity** — “Who am I to say anything?” It tolerates sin and calls it love.
- Self-suspicion without restoration becomes **paralyzed introspection** — “I’m so flawed I can never speak to anyone else’s sin.” It abandons the brother.
- Restoration and meekness without self-suspicion become **self-righteous condescension** — “I’m spiritual, you’re not; let me fix you.” Pride always slips back in through the back door of “helping.”

By keeping them together, Paul creates a **cruciform posture**:

- I come as one beggar who has been fed telling another beggar where to find bread.
- I remember that the same sinful nature that tripped my brother still lives in me.
- I therefore approach with trembling hands and a gentle voice, because I know how fragile spiritual life is and how easily I could be the one lying in the ditch tomorrow.

This is the exact opposite of the “spiritual elitism” the Judaizers were promoting in Galatia — the attitude that “I am more advanced, therefore I look down on the weak.” Paul says the true mark of spiritual maturity is the ability to restore the fallen with meekness precisely because you never forget how easily you could fall.

### 3. Practical shape of this posture

- **Deepened self-knowledge**: The best correctors are those who have already been broken by their own sin and restored by grace. They carry the “brand-marks of Jesus” (Galatians 6:17) in their own story.
- **Grief over sin**: Both the sin of the brother and the potential sin in themselves produces sorrow, not smugness (see 2 Corinthians 7:8–11).
- **Prayerful dependence**: You cannot restore in the spirit of meekness while trusting in your own superior holiness. You must be praying, “Lord, apart from your grace I would be worse than he is.”
- **Hopeful patience**: Because you know how long it took God to bring *you* along, you are willing to invest time and relationship rather than demand instant change.

In short, the moral posture Galatians 6:1 demands is the posture of the gospel itself: we correct because sin is serious and fellowship is precious; we do it meekly because we stand only by grace; and we do it with trembling self-suspicion because we know that grace is our only hope too.
