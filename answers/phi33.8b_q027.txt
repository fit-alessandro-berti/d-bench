The Pharisee's trust in themselves and despising others is what condemns his attitude here in Luke 18:9–14. This self-righteener is guilty of false doctrine because he assumes that his own outward religious observances, such as fasting and tithing, sufficed to secure his favor in the eyes of God. He contrasts himself with the publican, whom he views as someone less holy. The tax collector, in contrast, displays a humility that recognizes his sinfulness and relies solely on God's mercy. Jesus criticizes the Pharisee's misguided focus on external righteousness as opposed to the genuine trust in God, which is evident in the tax collector's acknowledgment of his sins and his plea for God's mercy.

The Pharisee's self-righteousness is rooted in pride, and his focus on appearances distracts him from the truth of the Gospel. This prideful attitude indicates his misunderstanding that righteousness is measured by an external display of virtue in contrast to one’s internal moral condition. 

In contrast, the tax collector demonstrates contrition for his sins and realizes his unworthiness before God, showing that he recognizes his incapacity to come to God based on his sinful humanity – a quality that the Pharisee overlooks despite his outward show of religiosity. The publican, therefore, expresses a more reliable posture to Jesus, as he embraces God's mercy and acknowledges his need for God's grace and healing over the Pharisee’s empty exterior religiosity. 

Although the Pharisee might believe he's a pious man, the publican, despite not performing religious acts, is the one genuinely dependent on God, which is why Jesus affirms that this tax collector went down to his house justified. For the Pharisee's pride and hypocrisy, Jesus has a different view of who is truly justified.

The tax collector in Luke 18:12–15 is spiritually more aligned with Jesus, as he exemplifies the humility that Jesus espouses and reflects an awareness of his need for God's forgiveness. His self-deprecating statement indicates not a lack of faith, but rather a powerful and honest self-awareness, which is genuine. His contrition and reliance on mercy, unlike the Pharisee’s self-complacency, exemplify the spiritual truth sought here: that all who are seeking God earnestly, regardless of their outward religious practices, are at peace with God; the crux of the matter lies in one's trust in God, as Jesus confirms.

Jesus asserts that what matters is a transformed heart, aligned with God's heart, and a dependence on God's mercy. That's what matters: one's relationship with God over outward appearances or religious conformity. The Pharisee's self-deprecation, on the surface, might seem negative, but it's actually insincere; his humility was a facade.

In contrast, the publican's contrition and sincere recognition of God's kindness to a sinner underscores the spiritual focus Jesus advocates, one that invites him into God's kingdom. While Jesus does not condone sin, he advocates humility that leads to salvation. While Jesus did not condone sin, He did not condescend to dismiss the repentant publican's plea for mercy.

The Pharisee exhibits hypocrisy by boasting of his religious observances and feeling that he's not like sinners, and he does not realize God's compassionate mercy extended to him. In his oblivion, he refuses to rely on God's mercy toward an unworthy person like himself. His focus on justification through observances is misplaced – for it's not by works but solely by the grace and mercy of God that one is ultimately just or condemned. Therefore, he is truly unjustified, for he cannot see this in himself and therefore believes he's righteous in the eyes of God.

Jesus' point here is that spiritual assurance comes not from external religiosity or a self-deprecating facade but from faith and dependence on God’s mercy.

The publican, recognizing his depravity, recognizes that he should be grateful for God’s grace, who despite his sinful behavior has not rejected him, even as he did not expect salvation from God. He expresses his contrition by acknowledging God’s forgiveness – a reflection of God’s mercy – that he experiences.

In this parable, Jesus underscores the importance of humility, reliance on God's mercy, and a contrite heart rather than self-reliance and empty religious acts. He teaches that genuine humility is characterized by the honest acknowledgment of one's sinfulness, which results in seeking God’s grace. The religiously observant Pharisee, on the surface, might seem pious, but his self-deprecation and pride are indicative of a disconnect from Jesus' message of God’s mercy, reliance on God's grace, and a humble heart. The religious fervor of the Pharisee is misleading because he fails to understand that external religious practices alone cannot secure him salvation. 

This parable reflects the necessity of humility and reliance on God’s mercy, contrasting religious observance against genuine contrition and spiritual alignment toward God, underscoring the spiritual truth: that one's righteousness comes from faith, not from external actions alone. Thus, Jesus criticizes the misguided pride of the Pharisee and lauds the true faith of the publican, for it is his sincere repentance and humility that lead to his forgiveness and healing at God's hands.

In summary, a self-deprecating posture is misplaced when it's insincere, and hypocrisy when it's a guise for pride. Jesus commends the humble and contrite heart of the publican over his self-critique because it reflects the true spirit of Christianity: a recognition of sinful humanity, reliance on God’s mercy, and the acknowledgement that God will not reject sinners, but instead embrace them. This parable, then, condemns the self-deprecating Pharisee, and praises the humble acknowledging heart of the tax collector. To fully grasp this parable, it’s imperative to understand the necessity of humility, reliance on God's mercy, and a contrite heart, for them are the pathways to Jesus’s acceptance of the sinner.
