A thoughtful and important question.

The trust called for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is not a rejection of reason or intellect, but rather an invitation to humble dependence on God that acknowledges the limits of human understanding. It distinguishes itself from anti-intellectualism in several key ways:

1. **Humble Self-Distrust ≠ Disregard of Reason**  
   To “lean not unto thine own understanding” is not to despise thinking, learning, or wisdom—after all, the book of Proverbs itself is filled with exhortations to seek wisdom, knowledge, and discernment (e.g., Prov. 4:7). Rather, it is a warning against *autonomous reasoning*—the belief that human insight alone is sufficient to navigate life rightly apart from God’s guidance. Humble self-distrust recognizes that our perspectives are finite, fallen, and prone to error when divorced from divine truth.

2. **Trusting God Includes Using the Mind He Gave**  
   Authentic trust in the Lord does not bypass the intellect; it redirects it. A person who truly acknowledges God “in all [their] ways” (v. 6) will pray for wisdom (James 1:5), study Scripture (Ps. 119:105), seek counsel from others (Prov. 11:14), and engage carefully with creation and reason—all while submitting these efforts to God’s sovereign direction. Anti-intellectualism dismisses the pursuit of understanding; biblical faith sanctifies it under God’s lordship.

3. **The Contrast: Wisdom vs. Presumption**  
   Leaning on one’s own understanding reflects *presumption*—the assumption that we can see the whole picture or know the best path independently. In contrast, trusting God involves active humility: using our minds faithfully while acknowledging that only God sees the end from the beginning (Isa. 55:8–9). This posture doesn’t descend into passive ignorance but into obedient action grounded in reliance on God’s character and promises.

In summary, the trust Proverbs 3:5–6 calls for is a worshipful reliance on God that embraces humility without rejecting reason. It invites us to hold our conclusions loosely, submit them to God’s Word and Spirit, and walk forward in faith—even when we don’t have all the answers—knowing that He “shall direct [our] paths” (v. 6) far better than we could on our own.
