The image in Matthew 7:3-5 is sharp for several reasons:

1. **Vivid metaphor**: The comparison between a mote (a small speck of dust) and a beam (a large wooden plank) creates a stark contrast that grabs the listener's attention. The exaggeration highlights the absurdity of focusing on a minor flaw in someone else while ignoring a major issue in oneself.
2. **Irony and hypocrisy**: The passage critiques the hypocrisy of trying to correct someone else's minor flaw while being oblivious to one's own significant shortcomings. This irony makes the message more impactful and memorable.
3. **Personal application**: The use of "thy brother" and "thine own eye" makes the message personal and relatable, encouraging the listener to reflect on their own behavior and relationships.

The passage criticizes hypocrisy without forbidding moral correction altogether by:

1. **Targeting the motivation**: The criticism is aimed at the hypocritical motivation behind the correction, rather than the act of correction itself. It's not saying that we should never try to help others with their flaws, but rather that we should examine our own motivations and actions first.
2. **Encouraging self-reflection**: The passage encourages self-reflection and personal growth, suggesting that we should address our own major issues before trying to help others with their minor ones.
3. **Providing a solution**: The passage offers a solution, "first cast out the beam out of thine own eye," which implies that once we've addressed our own flaws, we'll be in a better position to help others with their flaws. This suggests that moral correction is not forbidden, but rather it should be done with humility, self-awareness, and a willingness to examine oneself first.

Overall, the passage uses a vivid and ironic image to critique hypocrisy and encourage self-reflection, while still allowing for moral correction and growth.
