This is one of the most striking passages in the prophetic literature, and its sharpness is intentional. Here's why the rejection isn't merely corrective but categorical:

## The Fraud Being Exposed: Substitutionary Religion

The core fraud is **using worship as a substitute for righteousness rather than an expression of it**. The people aren't genuinely trying to worship God but falling short — they're performing elaborate religious rituals *while actively perpetuating injustice*. Their worship functions as a transactional mechanism: "We offer sacrifices; God gives blessings; we continue exploiting the poor."

This is why the language is so contemptuous — "I hate, I despise" — not "I wish you'd adjust the balance." God isn't receiving flawed worship; He's receiving **fraudulent** worship, because its very purpose is to purchase divine indifference to injustice.

## Why "Better Balance" Would Miss the Point

Asking for balance would imply the worship and the injustice are *separate domains* that just need proportional attention. But Amos is saying they are **morally linked** — the worship *is itself* the problem because:

- It **crowds out** justice by giving people a false sense of having fulfilled their obligations
- It **inverts God's character** — treating YHWH as a deity who can be bribed rather than a God of justice
- It **soothes the conscience** of oppressors while doing nothing for the oppressed

## The Radical Final Verse

"But let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream" isn't a request to add justice to worship — it's a **replacement**. The torrent imagery suggests something overwhelming and all-encompassing. The prophet envisions a society where justice is the fundamental operating principle, and worship would naturally flow from that — not the other way around.

Amos essentially unmasks the oldest religious trick: believing you can treat God as a cosmic vending machine while treating your neighbor as disposable.
