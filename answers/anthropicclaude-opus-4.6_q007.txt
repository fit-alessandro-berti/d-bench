# Discernment and the Appetite to Condemn

This is one of the most misused verses in Scripture—deployed both by those who want to silence all moral reasoning and by those who quietly exempt themselves from its demand. Getting it right requires careful thought.

## What This Verse Cannot Mean

If Jesus were prohibiting all moral judgment, He would be contradicting Himself almost immediately. Within the same sermon, He says:

- "Why beholdest thou the mote that is in thy brother's eye, but perceivest not the beam that is in thine own eye?" — which *assumes you should eventually help remove the mote*. The instruction is not "ignore the mote" but "address yourself first."
- "Beware of false prophets" — which requires evaluating someone's teaching and character.
- "By their fruits ye shall know them" — an explicit command to *judge* the quality of what people produce.

A blanket prohibition on moral evaluation would also make nonsense of the entire prophetic tradition, the law, Paul's letters, and Jesus's own repeated practice of calling out hypocrisy, exploitation, and falsehood in the sharpest possible terms.

So something more precise is being said.

## The Distinction

**Discernment** is the capacity to perceive what is true, good, and right—and to recognize what is false, harmful, and wrong. It is a moral necessity. Without it, you cannot love your neighbor, because love requires knowing what actually helps and what actually harms. You cannot protect the vulnerable without identifying the predator. You cannot repent without recognizing sin.

**The appetite to condemn** is something altogether different. It is a posture of the soul—a readiness to pronounce final judgment on another person's worth, to position yourself as the authority who assigns them to their ultimate category. It often carries distinct marks:

- **It is eager.** Discernment is often reluctant and sorrowful. The appetite to condemn is energized, even pleasurable.
- **It is final.** Discernment says "this action is wrong" or "this pattern is dangerous." Condemnation says "this person is worthless" or "this person is beyond redemption."
- **It is self-serving.** Every person you condemn becomes evidence of your own righteousness. The condemner builds an identity out of the catalogue of others' failures.
- **It is selective.** It applies standards to others that it never turns inward. This is precisely the hypocrisy of the beam and the mote.
- **It forecloses mercy.** Discernment can coexist with compassion, patience, and hope. Condemnation has already closed the book.

## The Structure of the Teaching

Notice the precise architecture of Jesus's words. Three pairs:

| Do not... | And you will not... |
|---|---|
| Judge | Be judged |
| Condemn | Be condemned |
| — | — |
| **Forgive** | **Be forgiven** |

The third pair breaks the pattern—it moves from negative to positive. This reveals the direction of the whole teaching. It is not merely a prohibition; it is a redirection. The energy you might spend constructing cases against others is to be redirected toward mercy, because *you yourself stand in need of exactly the same mercy*.

This is not moral relativism. It is moral realism of the most devastating kind. It says: the standard you apply to others will be applied to you—and you will not survive it.

## Why the Difference Matters Practically

When discernment and condemnation are confused, two errors follow:

**First**, people abandon discernment in the name of tolerance and call it virtue. They refuse to name predatory behavior, false teaching, or injustice, because they have been told that any judgment is the same as the judgment Jesus prohibits. This is not kindness—it is abdication. It leaves the weak unprotected and calls cowardice love.

**Second**, people baptize their appetite to condemn by calling it discernment. They build entire identities around identifying who is wrong, who is out, who deserves punishment. They are fluent in the language of moral reasoning but strangers to self-examination. The Pharisees were not lacking in discernment. They were drowning in condemnation.

## The Test

Perhaps the simplest diagnostic: **Does your moral judgment about another person produce in you any grief?** Any recognition of shared frailty? Any impulse toward prayer or restoration?

Or does it produce satisfaction?

The answer to that question reveals whether you are exercising discernment or feeding an appetite. And Jesus is quite clear about what happens to those who feed that appetite: they will be measured by the same instrument they so eagerly applied to others.

The call is not to see less clearly. It is to see clearly while standing consciously under the same gaze—and to let that awareness transform judgment into something that serves redemption rather than destruction.
