This is a complex passage that touches on some deep theological and philosophical questions about human nature, sin, and self-knowledge. A few thoughts:

On one level, the passage does seem quite pessimistic about human nature, describing the heart as "deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked." This suggests an innate brokenness and propensity towards self-deception and sin in the human condition. We can't fully know or trust our own motives and desires.

At the same time, I don't think this is meant as a blanket condemnation of humanity as irredeemably evil. Rather, it's a sobering reminder of how susceptible we all are to deceiving ourselves, rationalizing our behavior, and being led astray by misguided desires. In that sense, it's a realistic assessment of the human struggle with sin and the limits of our self-understanding.

The idea that "only God can truly know the heart" is significant. It positions God as the ultimate judge who sees past our blind spots and hidden faults. We may be mysteries to ourselves, but we are fully known by God. This could be seen as a warning (our sins will be found out) but also a comfort (when our own conscience condemns us, God knows the full truth).

So what's the takeaway for how we talk about motives, given the unreliability of the heart? A few possibilities:

1) Speak humbly and tentatively about our own motives, acknowledging that we don't fully know ourselves. Avoid over-confident claims of purity.

2) Look for external signs of "fruit" - concrete behaviors and results that give evidence of the motives within, since examining feelings alone can mislead. 

3) Rely on Scripture, wise counsel, and God's Spirit to reveal blindspots and test our motives, rather than just our own introspection.

4) Ultimately, entrust the judgment of all people's motives to God, who alone can accurately weigh the inner workings of the heart.

In the end, this passage calls for honesty and humility about the human condition - owning up to the reality of inner darkness and self-deception, while hoping in a God who knows us better than we know ourselves. It's a call to "walk circumspectly" with self-suspicion, anchored in the grace of the One who searches all hearts.
