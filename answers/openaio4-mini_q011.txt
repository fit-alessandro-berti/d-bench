In Isaiah 1:16–17 the prophet pivots from negative injunctions (“Wash you, make you clean; … put away the evil of your doings … cease to do evil”) to positive commands (“Learn to do well; seek judgment; relieve the oppressed; judge the fatherless; plead for the widow”).  That shift is not merely rhetorical flourish, but makes an important theological and ethical point: true righteousness in YHWH’s sight is not exhausted by refraining from wrongdoing, but requires active engagement in justice and mercy.  

1. Linguistic and literary context  
  • The Hebrew verb translated “learn” (לָמֳדוּ, lilmodû) implies more than casual discovery; it carries the sense of apprenticeship or disciplined training.  Isaiah is urging the people to become students of “doing well,” as one might become proficient at a craft.  
  • The parallelism in Hebrew poetry often couples a prohibition with its complement: stop the bad, start the good.  But here the emphasis on “learning” the good underscores that generosity, advocacy, and fair judgment are skills to be cultivated.  

2. Why “cease” is not enough  
  • Ceasing to do evil is a necessary first step—it creates moral space by removing obstacles between the people and God (v. 15).  But Isaiah’s vision of covenantal life extends far beyond non-violence or mere legal compliance.  
  • In the prophetic corpus, YHWH’s outrage is repeatedly kindled by empty ritual (1:11–14), hypocrisy, and social exploitation.  Reform that stops at ritual purity or avoidance of wrongdoing merely maintains the status quo of oppression.  

3. Active goodness as practiced love and justice  
  • “Learning to do well” brings together two classic strands of Old Testament ethics:  
    – Wisdom tradition (e.g. Proverbs 1–9), where “learning” is the hallmark of the godly life.  
    – Prophetic concern for the marginalized: widows, orphans, the poor (cf. Exod 22:22–24; Amos 5:12; Micah 6:8).  
  • Goodness here is portrayed as a competency.  You don’t simply refrain from stealing; you practice generosity.  You don’t merely stop perverting justice; you train yourself in impartial judgment on behalf of the powerless.  
  • From a covenantal standpoint, Israel’s identity is bound up in ethical fidelity: “if you walk in my statutes … then I will dwell among you” (Lev 26:3–6).  “Walking” implies ongoing motion, not standing still.  

4. Theological implications  
  • Goodness is not merely negative (the absence of sin) but positive (the presence of virtue).  
  • Love of God overflows in love of neighbor.  As James 2:8–9 will later put it, fulfilling “the royal law” means active mercy, not just avoidance of harm.  
  • This patterned movement—from “stop” to “learn” to “act”—anticipates the New Testament emphasis on faith that works (e.g. Gal 5:6; 2 Cor 5:14–15).  

5. Practical take-aways  
  • Self-examination must lead to apprenticeship in righteousness.  We ask God to cleanse us, then pick up the tools of justice: advocacy, generosity, fair speech.  
  • Congregations and communities don’t simply issue moral prohibitions; they train one another in mercy ministries, fair treatment of the vulnerable, and public advocacy.  

In Isaiah’s economy, therefore, true “goodness” is neither passive nor automatic.  It is a formed, trained habit of love and justice.  One must first remove the barriers of sin, but then embark on a lifelong vocation of learning—and doing—what is well-pleasing to God.
