The elder brother’s deepest problem is not a single one of these elements, but rather the way they coalesce into a **fundamental misunderstanding of his relationship with his father.** 

While the anger, envy, and pride are the *symptoms* of his state, his true problem is a **transactional view of love.** 

Here is how these elements reveal that deeper issue:

### 1. The Transactional View (The Root Cause)
The elder brother views his relationship with his father as a contract, not a covenant. By saying, *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee... thou never gavest me a kid,"* he reveals that he believes his obedience earns him a debt—that the father "owes" him rewards for his good behavior. Because he views the father as a master/employer rather than a parent, grace (the fatted calf) feels like an injustice. If love is a wage to be earned, then mercy given to the undeserving brother feels like a theft from him.

### 2. The Isolation of Pride
His pride in his obedience (*"neither transgressed I at any time"*) has created a wall of self-righteousness. He does not see his brother as a brother; he refers to him as *"this thy son,"* deliberately distancing himself to underscore the perceived inequality of their treatment. His pride has made him "the elder brother" rather than "the brother," effectively exiling himself from the family circle even while living in the house.

### 3. Discomfort with Mercy (The Theology of Merit)
This is perhaps the most profound part of his problem. The elder brother is offended by mercy because mercy negates the "value" of his own hard work. If his wayward brother can be welcomed home without a period of probation or performance, then the elder brother's own strict adherence to the law feels trivialized. His discomfort with mercy stems from a belief that the world *should* be governed by strict justice. He cannot rejoice because he insists on a moral hierarchy where he is at the top and the "prodigal" is at the bottom.

### 4. The Loss of Intimacy
The father’s response, *"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine,"* highlights the tragedy: **The elder brother possessed the inheritance all along, but he was too busy keeping score to enjoy it.** 

His anger and envy blinded him to the fact that his "great reward" was not the fatted calf or the party, but the daily, uninterrupted intimacy of living with the father. He sacrificed the relationship for the sake of his own ego and his perceived "fairness."

### Conclusion
The elder brother’s deepest problem is **his inability to recognize that he is just as lost as the younger brother.** 

The younger brother was lost in his rebellion; the elder brother is lost in his own self-sufficiency. He has traded the father’s heart for a legalistic sense of entitlement. He cannot enter the party because the party is founded on grace—a concept that is fundamentally incompatible with his internal system of merit. **His real prison is that he cannot conceive of a love that is free; he only understands a love that is earned.**
