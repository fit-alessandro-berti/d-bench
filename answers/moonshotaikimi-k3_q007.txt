The verse forbids a posture, not a faculty. Reading it as "moral judgments never matter" confuses censoriousness with perception — and the text itself won't sustain that reading.

### What the verse actually forbids

Two features of the verse rule out the indifference interpretation.

**The pairing.** "Judge not... *condemn* not." The second verb (Greek *katadikazō* — to pass sentence, to declare finally guilty) narrows and clarifies the first. What's in view is not the noticing of good and evil but the rendering of verdicts on persons — the judicial act of closing someone's case.

**The third clause.** "Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven." Forgiveness is a morally discriminating act. You can only forgive what you have honestly named as wrong; a debt must be seen as a debt before it can be released. If "judge not" meant *make no moral assessments*, the command to forgive would be incoherent — there would be nothing to forgive. The verse's own logic requires discernment as the precondition of the mercy it commands.

The context confirms it. A few verses later comes the speck and the beam — and note how it ends: "first cast out the beam out of thine own eye, and *then* shalt thou see clearly to pull out the mote that is in thy brother's eye" (6:42). The speck is real; its removal remains the goal. What gets corrected is the order (self first) and the spirit (surgery, not spectacle). The same sermon insists trees are known by their fruit (6:43–44), and the Matthean parallel is followed almost immediately by "beware of false prophets... by their fruits ye shall know them." Jesus states it outright elsewhere: "Judge not according to the appearance, but judge righteous judgment" (John 7:24).

### Why "never matters" is a different — and lesser — claim

The indifference reading fails on several levels. It is self-refuting (it is itself a moral judgment about moral judgments). It dissolves the very sermon it's drawn from. And it substitutes something cheap for something costly: indifference is not mercy. Refusing to evaluate anything protects no one; the shepherd who declines to judge wolves is not being merciful, he's abandoning sheep. (It's also worth noting that "judge not" cannot be wielded as a solvent on all moral speech without becoming what it forbids — a verdict delivered with finality.)

But the deepest difference is this: the verse does not say there is no judgment. It says judgment is not yours. The reciprocity — "ye shall not be judged" — points to a real judgment in real hands. The ground of non-condemnation is not an empty courtroom but an occupied bench: a Judge who alone sees hearts and whole timelines ("judge nothing before the time, until the Lord come," 1 Cor. 4:5). Indifference says nothing matters enough to adjudicate. Jesus says: you are not on the bench; you are in the dock, and mercy is on offer. That word is far more demanding — it requires you to stay entangled with wrongdoers, neither excusing nor damning them, which is harder than either exit.

### Discernment versus the appetite to condemn

"Appetite" is the right word, because condemnation *feeds* the condemner. There is a small gratification in fault-finding — superiority, certainty, the quick bonding of a shared target. Condemnation pays. Discernment costs: once you've seen a wrong clearly and refused to write the person off, you inherit the harder verbs — admonish, bear with, restore, forgive (Gal. 6:1: "restore such an one in the spirit of meekness; considering thyself"). Condemnation is the cheap exit; it discharges the relationship.

The contrasts run along several lines:

- **Object.** Discernment addresses acts and patterns; condemnation addresses persons — their whole worth and final standing. "He lied" versus "he's a liar": the second collapses a life into a fault.
- **Affect.** Discernment grieves; condemnation savors. Jesus pronounces woes over Jerusalem and then weeps over it. Jonah, by contrast, is *angry* when Nineveh is spared — the appetite would rather keep its grievance than see a city restored. Condemnation needs the offender to stay guilty; discernment longs to be put out of work by repentance.
- **Self-relation.** Discernment begins at home ("first the beam"). Condemnation is structurally self-exempting, and often fiercest against the fault we secretly carry (Rom. 2:1). The beam-to-speck ratio isn't random; judging others is one of the oldest flights from self-knowledge.
- **Temporality.** Discernment is provisional: *this is what you are doing*. Condemnation is final: *this is what you are and will be*. Final verdicts require sight of the heart and the whole story — data we lack, for an office we don't hold.
- **Aim.** Removing a speck from an eye is surgery: it requires clarity, gentleness, and closeness, and no one operates in order to win. The condemner doesn't want the eye healed; he wants the speck to stay put, as evidence.

The diagnostic questions write themselves. Would I be glad to see this person restored? Have I searched for this fault in myself first? Am I speaking to heal or to win — and would I say it to their face, for their good, at cost to myself?

### The upshot

"Judge not" does not lower the moral bar; it relocates the gavel. Every question of right and wrong remains intact — the trees still bear fruit, the specks are still specks — while we are stripped of the one pleasure mercy cannot survive: the pleasure of the verdict. Discernment serves the person; condemnation feeds on them. The verse commands the first, starves the second, and then commands forgiveness — which is simply what discernment becomes when it refuses to turn into condemnation.
