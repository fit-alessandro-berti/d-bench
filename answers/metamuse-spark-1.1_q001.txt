In Genesis 4:6-7 we get the first definition of *sin - chatta't* in the Bible, and it is strikingly concrete.

The Hebrew is famously difficult, so a more literal rendering helps:

> "Why are you angry, and why has your face fallen? If you do well, will you not be lifted up - *se'et*? And if you do not do well, sin is a croucher - *chatta't rovetz* - at the door - *petach*. And to you is his desire - *teshuqah* - but you shall rule - *mashal* - over him."

There are three moves in the picture:

### 1. Sin is figured as a beast, not just a feeling

**Not impulse. Not habit. Not an abstract idea of wrong.**

The verb *rovetz* is almost never used for humans. It is the word used for an animal crouching, lying in wait for prey. The same participle is used for a lion or other predator ready to pounce. In some ANE texts, a *rovitz* is a demon crouching at the threshold of a house.

The threshold matters: *petach* - the door. Sin hasn't entered yet. It's not described as inside Cain. It's not his nature, his blood, or a habit he already has. It's outside, at the boundary between intention and act, waiting for permission to come in.

That is a very different anthropology from later ideas of total depravity or determinism. Evil is predatory, alive, personal, but still *outside*.

### 2. Sin has desire, but not authority

> "and unto thee shall be his desire"

The word *teshuqah* - desire, craving, urge to possess or control - is rare. It occurs only three times in the Torah: here, in Genesis 3:16 of the woman to/because of her husband, and in Song of Songs. In each case it implies a desire to overmaster, to claim.

The grammar is ambiguous: "his" could be sin [masculine in Hebrew Grammar despite *chatta't* being feminine - hence *rovetz* is masculine] or it could be Abel's. The earliest Jewish reading - LXX, Targum Onkelos, Rashi - takes it as sin's.

The picture is then inverted: Sin wants you, the way a predator wants prey, or a dependent wants to possess its master. It hungers for you. But hunger is not sovereignty.

### 3. Moral agency is retained even in emotional distress

This is the core of the passage. God does not say to Cain:

* "You were made this way"
* "You are already guilty so you will sin"
* "The Devil made you do it"

He starts with two questions: "Why are you angry? Why has your face fallen?" That is an appeal to self-examination. Cain's anger and shame - after his offering is not *sha'ah*, regarded with favor - are real, but they do not *cause* his next act. They are the condition in which choice happens.

Then the conditional:

**"If you do well - *im teitiv* - shall you not be accepted/lifted up?"** *Se'et* can mean both "acceptance of offering" and "lifting of the face" - the opposite of a fallen countenance. Doing well restores you.

**"If you do not do well..."** the beast is still at the door. It does not kick the door in.

Then the charge: *ve'atah timshal-bo* - "and you, you shall/must rule over him." The verb can be read as a promise - "you will rule" [you are able] - or as an imperative - "you must rule" [you are responsible]. Most Hebrew grammarians take it as both: You can, therefore you must.

> This is a picture of libertarian moral agency:
> **Temptation is external, intimate, and powerful, but not irresistible. Responsibility is internal, personal, and non-transferable.**

Cain is not portrayed as a victim of a habit he can't break, or of an impulse that overwhelms reason. He is portrayed as a threshold guardian. The beast can't enter unless he opens the door.

That is why this text became foundational in both Jewish and Christian ethics. In the rabbinic idiom, it is the first statement of the *yetzer hara* - the evil inclination - as a crouching dog that if you feed it, it grows, if you master it, it obeys. Augustine and later Aquinas cite it as proof that sin's power is desire, not necessity.

Evil here is not banal. It's not normal. It's predatory. And you are not prey, you are the ruler of the house.
