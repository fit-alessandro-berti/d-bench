In Matthew 6:1 the moral danger is not *visibility* itself but the *motive for visibility*—and what that motive does to the heart.

A few key points from the verse and its context:

1. **The wording targets intention, not mere publicity.**  
   The phrase is: “do not your alms **before men, to be seen of them**.”  
   The problem is *doing it in order to be seen*—turning good works into a performance whose goal is human attention and approval.

2. **Jesus elsewhere *commands* visible goodness.**  
   Just one chapter earlier (Matthew 5:16):  
   > “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father…”  
   So visibility can be right when the *end* is that God is honored, not the self.

   Put simply:  
   - Matthew 5:16: “Be seen — so that they glorify **God**.”  
   - Matthew 6:1: “Don’t do righteousness — in order that they glorify **you**.”

3. **Why public goodness is morally dangerous.**  
   Public acts of goodness:
   - Easily train us to seek **reputation instead of righteousness**.  
   - Turn people into an **audience**, and goodness into a stage.  
   - Shift our inner “reward system” from “What does God think?” to “What do they think?”

   Jesus sharpens this with the language of “reward”:  
   - If your true aim is *to be seen by others*, then their praise **is** your reward—“you have no reward from your Father.”  
   - You’ve already been “paid in full” in the currency you chose: human applause.

4. **So what is “morally dangerous” here?**  
   - Not that others *happen* to see your good deeds.  
   - But that you *want* them to see, so that they will think well of *you*.  
   - That desire then corrupts the act from worship and love into self-display.

In short:  
The problem in Matthew 6:1 is not visibility itself; it is the craving to be seen, and the way that craving turns acts of goodness into self-centered performances, cutting off the kind of God-centered sincerity that God rewards. Visibility becomes dangerous because it feeds, exposes, and often reshapes our motives.
