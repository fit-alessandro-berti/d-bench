This passage from Matthew 5:44–45 is one of the most radical ethical teachings in the New Testament, and the difficulty in taking it seriously arises from several layers: moral psychology, cultural expectations, and theological idealism.  

---

### **1. Psychological and cultural barriers**
- **Human inclination toward justice and reciprocity** – People naturally expect fairness, reward for good, and punishment for evil. Enemy-love seems to violate that basic sense of justice.  
- **Perceived as weakness** – In many cultural contexts, loving enemies is seen as naïve or submissive, not as strength.  
- **Emotional difficulty** – Hurt and anger are powerful emotions; to love those who harm us requires suppressing natural self-protective feelings.

---

### **2. Is it sentimental?**
Superficially, it can be misinterpreted as sentimental or passive, but in its biblical context it is not sentimental love (like a warm feeling) but a deliberate, active will to seek the good of the other—even the hostile other.  

---

### **3. Is it strategic?**
Some have read it as strategic:  
- **Breaking cycles of violence** by unexpected kindness.  
- **Gaining moral high ground** or social influence.  
But in the text, the primary reason given is not strategy but **imitation of God’s impartial benevolence**—“that you may be children of your Father in heaven.”

---

### **4. Deeper moral strength**
The passage frames enemy-love as an expression of **spiritual maturity and participation in God’s character**.  
- It reflects God’s common grace (sun and rain to just and unjust alike).  
- It requires transcending ego, fear, and retaliation—thus a higher moral achievement, not because it is “nice,” but because it mirrors the Divine perspective beyond human categories of friend/enemy.

---

**Conclusion:**  
It’s difficult to take seriously because it contradicts deep-seated human notions of justice and self-preservation. Yet in its religious context, it’s neither mere sentiment nor simple strategy—it’s a call to a supernatural virtue, reflecting the nature of God, which is the core of Christian ethical teaching.
