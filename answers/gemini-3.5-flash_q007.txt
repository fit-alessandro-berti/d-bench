Luke 6:37 is one of the most frequently quoted—and systematically misunderstood—verses in the New Testament. In modern secular discourse, "Judge not" is often wielded as a proof-text for absolute moral relativism, implying that moral judgments never matter, that all lifestyles and choices are equally valid, and that pointing out wrongdoing is the ultimate social sin.

However, a closer look at the text, its context, and the wider scope of Christian ethics reveals a profound difference between **moral relativism** (the belief that moral judgments do not matter) and **divine restraint** (the command to refrain from condemnation). 

The distinction lies entirely in the difference between **discernment** (an intellectual and spiritual necessity) and the **appetite to condemn** (a psychological and spiritual pathology).

---

### 1. Why Moral Judgments Still Matter
To claim that Luke 6:37 outlaws all moral judgments is logically and textually untenable. 

First, **the command to forgive requires moral judgment.** The verse concludes: *"forgive, and ye shall be forgiven."* Forgiveness is not a blank check of indifference; it is a transaction that *presupposes* a moral infraction. You cannot forgive someone unless you have first judged their action to be wrong. If there is no moral standard, there is nothing to forgive.

Second, **Jesus expects his followers to evaluate behavior.** Just a few verses later in the same chapter, Jesus tells his disciples to recognize trees by their fruit (Luke 6:43-45) and warns against blind guides. To distinguish between a "good tree" and a "bad tree," or a wise guide and a blind one, requires robust moral evaluation. 

Therefore, Luke 6:37 does not demand the abandonment of truth or the suspension of our cognitive faculties. It does not ask us to pretend that cruelty is kindness or that deceit is honesty.

---

### 2. Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn
The Greek word used for "judge" in this passage is *krinō*, which can mean to analyze, but in this context carries the heavy weight of passing final sentence or sitting in tribunal. The subsequent phrase clarifies the target: *"condemn not"* (*katadikazō*—to declare guilty and worthy of punishment).

To understand the difference between the two, we must contrast **discernment** with **the appetite to condemn**.

| Attribute | Discernment (The Physician’s Lens) | Condemnation (The Executioner’s Gavel) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Object** | Focuses on the *act* and its fruit. | Focuses on the *soul* and worth of the person. |
| **Motive** | Driven by love, truth, and the desire for restoration. | Driven by pride, ego, and the desire for superiority. |
| **Posture** | Humble, self-aware (knowing one's own flaws). | Self-righteous, hypocritical (the "log" in one's own eye). |
| **Outcome** | Leads to healing, boundary-setting, and redemption. | Leads to ostracization, shame, and destruction. |

#### Discernment: The Diagnostic Eye
Discernment is the ability to see things as they truly are. It is diagnostic. A doctor does not look at a patient’s tumor and say, "I must not judge this tumor." The doctor diagnoses the cancer precisely because they value the patient’s life. 

In the spiritual and moral realm, discernment identifies sin, injustice, and falsehood so that they can be corrected, resisted, or healed. Discernment is grieved by the wrong it sees because it loves the victim and desires the restoration of the wrongdoer.

#### The Appetite to Condemn: The Pharisaic Trap
The "appetite to condemn," however, is not interested in healing. It is a psychological defense mechanism and a spiritual drug. 

Human beings have an innate craving to feel righteous. Because achieving true righteousness is painful and requires deep repentance, we often opt for the cheap counterfeit: **feeling superior by pointing out the unrighteousness of others.** 

When we condemn, we project our own shadow onto the offender. We use their sins to distract ourselves from our own. The appetite to condemn is characterized by:
*   **Finality:** It writes the person off as irredeemable.
*   **Pleasure:** It finds a perverse satisfaction in others' moral failures (schadenfreude).
*   **Usurpation:** It attempts to sit in the seat of God, who alone knows the secrets of the human heart and has the authority to pass final judgment.

---

### 3. The Mirror of Reciprocity
Jesus anchors this teaching in a law of spiritual reciprocity: *"Judge not, and ye shall not be judged... for with the same measure that ye mete withal it shall be measured to you again"* (Luke 6:38).

This is both a divine promise and a psychological reality. If we build a life characterized by an appetite to condemn, we create a merciless world for ourselves. The person who lives by the sword of hyper-critical condemnation will eventually be destroyed by their own standard, trapped in the fear of being exposed. 

Conversely, the person who practices discernment tempered by mercy recognizes that they, too, are in need of grace. They see the "speck" in their brother’s eye, but because they have wrestled with the "log" in their own (Luke 6:41-42), their approach is marked by gentleness, not wrath.

### Conclusion
Luke 6:37 is not a call to intellectual or moral blindness. It is a warning against **usurping God's role as the ultimate Judge** and using the moral failings of others to fuel our own self-righteousness. 

Moral judgments matter immensely—without them, we cannot love justice or walk in truth. But while **discernment** seeks to diagnose the wound so that the Great Physician can heal it, **condemnation** seeks to execute the patient. Jesus commands us to put down the executioner’s gavel, keep our eyes wide open in discernment, and hold our hands out in forgiveness.
