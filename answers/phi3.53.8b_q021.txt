The proverb from Proverbs 16:18–19 highlights the consequences of pride, framing it not merely as a matter of ethical guilt but as a state of moral blindness that can lead to one's downfall. The contrast between the "proud" and the "lowly" serves to underscore the moral and psychological divide between humility and arrogance, illustrating the potential pitfalls of the latter.

In this verse, pride ("haughty spirit") is depicted not just as a moral failing but as a fundamental misjudgment that precludes clear judgment ("precedes destruction") and vulnerability ("precedes a fall"). Pride here is not merely about feeling good about oneself; it's a deeper, systemic flaw that colors one'don't see things as they are, leading to a flawed perception of reality, which ultimately culminates in one’s own downfall. This form of blindness suggests that a proud or arrogant character may become so wrapped up in their own sense of superiority that they misinterpret or misperceive their social position, circumstances, or the behavior of others. Such a perception can blind them to the risks and consequences of their actions, rendering them unable to make wise decisions or understand their eventual downfall.

The notion that it's better to share spoils with those seen as lowly or humble rather than conspiring with prideful individuals underscores the value the Bible associates with humility. It suggests that engaging with the lowly is not just a moral choice but a strategic one—to avoid the perils of hubris. The morality of arrogance, as depicted here, is not simply a flaw to be cleansed or purged; it's a destructive force that can blind not only oneself but also others in pursuit of gain, often leading to negative outcomes for all parties involved.

The moral psychology of arrogance, as illustrated in this text, hinges upon an inflated view of oneself that can close off a person to reality. Pride fosters a false sense of control or invulnerability, which ignores the human interdependence and the inevitable limitations of individual power. There's an inherent self-deception where one overestimates their abilities or importance and underestimates others. Such arrogance tends to lead to choices that benefit oneself at the expense of others, which is socially divisive and self-defeating.

The fact that the verse suggests pride could be tied to "blindness and collapse" implies that those who are proud are not seeing the world around them truthfully. They are failing to understand their place in the world or the consequences of their actions, which makes them susceptible to dramatic missteps or downfalls. The blindness comes from an inability to maintain perspective and reality-testing when interacting with others or making decisions. The collapse can be understood as a spiritual or moral failure when one's arrogance leads to actions that are not aligned with God's will or one'sin' capacity to maintain healthy relationships and navigate life's challenges as they should.

Moreover, pride can blind others as well, as prideful actions often result in unfairly taking credit or mistreating those perceived as beneath oneself. This can lead to increased social stratification and conflict, as well as personal isolation and dissatisfaction as one grows increasingly disconnected from those around them.

In sum, the proverb implies that arrogance comes with a false perception of autonomy and power, whereby the person is at risk of being both a victim of their own delusion and an oppressor to those considered lower in status. It's both a moral failing and a societal issue; it breeds division and mismanagement when pride is allowed to dictate one's judgment, which can result in loss and destruction. The contrast with the humble suggests a worldview that emphasizes interconnectedness, equity, and a grounded assessment of oneself within a larger social and spiritual context.

From a Christian perspective, as often indicated in biblical teachings, humility is a virtue that involves recognizing one'sin', depending on God, and being in right relationship with others. Arrogance, in turn, disrupts this balance and can be an obstacle to spiritual growth and moral integrity.

Thus, pride linked with blindness and collapse serves as a potent warning against the damaging effects of self-inflated thinking—not only can it be an isolating and self-destructive psychological state, but it also undermines relationships and the moral fabric of society. The contrast with the "lowly" serves as a reminder of the wisdom in aligning oneself with others, recognizing our shared humanity, and placing value on humility over arrogance for both individual well-being and the health of the community.
