Excellent question! Matthew 12:34–35 draws a profound connection between a person’s inner moral state ("the heart") and their spoken words.

### **Analysis of the Passage**
- **"For out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh."**
    - This metaphor suggests that words flow naturally and inevitably from whatever fills the heart, just as water might overflow from a full vessel.
- **"A good man out of the good treasure... an evil man out of the evil treasure..."**
    - This imagery pictures the heart as a storehouse or treasury stocked with either good or bad "treasure" (values, motives, attitudes).

### **Concepts within the Passage**
#### **Speech as a Symptom**
- The passage presents words as *symptoms* or outgrowths of one’s inner character. Just as fruit reveals the nature of a tree, words reveal what’s inside a person.
- Evil hearts yield evil words; good hearts yield good words.

#### **Speech as a Choice**
- The consistent analogy is less about deliberate choice than inevitable expression (overflowing abundance). However, the mention of "bringeth forth" does carry a sense of *agency*. It implies that what comes out is chosen from what’s within; one cannot choose to bring forth good if their inner “treasury” is full of evil, and vice versa.
- So, while momentary word choices are possible, sustained good or evil speech is determined by what the heart contains.

#### **Speech as a Revelation**
- Most clearly, the passage presents speech as *revelation*—an unveiling of the true state of one’s inner being (heart).
- Words uncover what is hidden, putting internal realities on public display.

### **Conclusion**
**This passage connects speech with hidden moral stockpiles in the heart by describing words as the natural and inevitable product of what we have accumulated internally.**  
It suggests that words are:
- **Symptoms** (outgrowth of inner character),
- **Revelations** (unveiling of hidden moral realities),
- **And to a lesser but real degree, Choices** (we draw out treasures that already exist within us).

**In summary:**  
Words are all three—symptoms, revelations, and choices—but most fundamentally, Jesus emphasizes them as *symptoms* and *revelations* of our inner moral "stockpile," for from the overflow of the heart the mouth speaks.
