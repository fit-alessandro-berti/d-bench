First, the required moral posture for correction in this passage is a humble, shared-vulnerability stance rooted in spiritual dependence, not self-righteous superiority. The "spiritual" people called to act are not framed as morally flawless leaders who stand above failure, but as people who ground their intervention in their own need for God’s grace, prioritizing the other person’s place in community over judgment of their fault.

Restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion are inextricably linked here because none of these elements work on their own to fulfill the passage’s redemptive purpose:
1. **Restoration is the only legitimate end of correction** for this Christian community. The goal is never shame, exclusion, or punishing a fault—it is to bring the person who stumbled back into right relationship with God and the church body. Any correction that does not center this goal is a violation of the verse’s mandate.
2. **Meekness is the only disposition that can make restoration possible**. Meekness here is not weakness, but a gentle, humble posture that refuses to leverage another’s failure to elevate one’s own status. An arrogant, judgmental approach would push the person who stumbled away, defeating the goal of restoration.
3. **Self-suspicion is the foundation of that meekness**. The call to "consider thyself, lest thou also be tempted" forces the person intervening to confront their own vulnerability to failure: they are not immune to sin, just because they have not stumbled in this specific way yet. This honest self-awareness eliminates the self-righteousness that would poison meekness, and keeps the focus on shared dependence on God’s grace, rather than a hierarchy of "perfect" vs. "failed" believers.

Separated, these elements become harmful: correction without restoration is shaming, correction without meekness is abuse, and correction without self-suspicion is hypocritical judgment. Tied together, they create a framework for correction that protects both the person who stumbled and the person intervening, keeping the community rooted in grace.
