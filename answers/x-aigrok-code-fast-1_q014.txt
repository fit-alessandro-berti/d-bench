### Interpretation of the Passage
Matthew 6:19–21 is part of Jesus' Sermon on the Mount in the Gospel of Matthew. It contrasts accumulating temporary "treasures" on earth (material possessions vulnerable to decay, theft, or loss) with building eternal treasures in heaven (spiritual investments like faith, generosity, or righteousness). The key verse (21) uses a causal principle: "For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also." This is not a prescriptive instruction but a descriptive observation about human nature, grounded in ancient wisdom traditions (e.g., echoing Proverbs 4:23, "Guard your heart above all else, for it is the source of life").

### Does the Text Say Treasure Reveals the Heart, or That It Trains the Heart?
The text states that treasure *reveals* the heart, not that it *trains* the heart. The wording ("where your treasure is, there will your heart be also") implies a revealing or indicative relationship: what you prioritize and invest in (your "treasure") mirrors your inner inclinations or affections. It's observational, showing that external actions (like storing wealth) expose internal motives, rather than instructing treasure as a tool to deliberately shape or "train" the heart's desires.

- **Evidence for "reveals":** The phrase uses "will your heart be also," suggesting inevitability and reflection. If treasure trained the heart, the verse would likely emphasize change or intentional development (e.g., "make your heart follow your treasure"), but it instead describes an alignment that humans experience passively.
- **Not "trains":** There's no language of habit formation or deliberate education here; it's akin to Jesus' broader teachings (e.g., in Matthew 12:34: "The mouth speaks from the overflow of the heart") where actions reveal character. Modern psychological parallels exist, like cognitive dissonance theory or behavioral economics (e.g., people allocate resources where emotions are invested), supporting that priorities often reflect, rather than forge, the heart.

### How Possession, Attention, and Love Tie Together
The verse ties possession, attention, and love through the concept of "treasure" as a unifying lens on human behavior and values. Here's how they interconnect:

- **Possession**: Refers to what you acquire or "lay up" (accumulate), whether material ("treasures on earth," like money or goods) or immaterial ("treasures in heaven," like spiritual gains). It emphasizes ownership and control—earthly possessions are fleeting and risky, urging a shift to non-perishable ones.
  
- **Attention**: Implies focus or investment; the act of "laying up" treasures involves directing time, energy, and thought. Earthly treasures consume attention due to their vulnerability (moths, rust, thieves), pulling focus from eternal matters. This contrasts with heavenly treasures, which might include acts like prayer, charity, or moral living, requiring sustained attention.

- **Love**: Represented by "your heart," which in biblical language denotes affections, desires, and loyalties. The verse posits that what you love (and thus prioritize) is revealed by where you invest possessively and attentively.

These elements form a cycle: Your love draws your attention to certain possessions, and those possessions reinforce where your heart remains. For instance, accumulating wealth (possession) might claim your attention (effort, worry), signaling that your heart's love is earthly, not divine. Reversing to heavenly treasures reorients love, but the text describes this as indicative rather than causal—your treasures don't "train" love; they expose it. This aligns with Jesus' call to rethink priorities, as seen in parables like the Rich Fool (Luke 12:15–21), where possessions can blind or distort affections.
