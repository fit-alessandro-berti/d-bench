This verse from 1 John 4:20 presents a profound theological and ethical assertion that love for others is inseparable from love for God. The premise supported here is that tangible love for those we can directly interact with reflects and influences our capacity to love God, whom we cannot see. The verse challenges the reader by treating the love of one's visible neighbor as a test of the genuineness of one’s professed love for God.

The key reasons and implications of this verse include:

1. **Concrete Evidence of Love**: Love, as described biblically, is concrete and manifest in actions (1 Corinthians 13). Therefore, if someone can’t love their brother whom they can see and interact with, it raises a question on their ability to love God sincerely, whom they have not seen. Practically, interactions with people provide numerous opportunities to demonstrate love through kindness, forgiveness, service, and compassion, which are also considered expressions of love for God.

2. **The Incarnation of Christ**: In Christian theology, God became visible and tangible in the person of Jesus Christ. The incarnation means that God has been seen, initially through Christ, who is the visible image of the invisible God (Colossians 1:15). Loving others, especially fellow believers, who are also the body of Christ (1 Corinthians 12:27), becomes a direct way of loving God in a visible, tangible manner. 

3. **Self-Deception Exposed**: The verse exposes a form of self-deception where individuals profess love for God but have unloving attitudes or behaviors towards others. This inconsistency reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of the nature of God and the essence of divine love, which encompasses all of humanity without discrimination. It suggests that one cannot compartmentalize love for God and disdain toward people, as true love is holistic and embodies all relationships.

4. **Ethical Consistency**: By insisting that love for others is a litmus test for love for God, the verse promotes an ethical consistency in the believer’s life. This teaching discourages hypocrisy and encourages a living faith that is expressed through loving deeds and attitudes toward others. It establishes a direct correlation between vertical love (toward God) and horizontal love (toward people), emphasizing that both are inseparable.

5. **Spiritual Maturity**: Engaging in loving relationships with others, especially within the complexities and challenges that sometimes occur, can foster spiritual growth and maturity. Such relationships often require forgiveness, patience, and humility, which are qualities consistently attributed to God. Thus, loving others can be seen as a practice ground for spiritual virtues that reflect the nature of God.

Overall, the teaching in 1 John 4:20 puts forth a practical gauge for assessing one’s love for God through their love for fellow human beings. It invites believers to scrutinize their hearts and align their actions with the teachings of Christ, ensuring their love for God is not merely abstract or theoretical, but demonstrated and verified in their daily interactions and relationships.
