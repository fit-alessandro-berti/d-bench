 This passage from Luke 6:37 is often misunderstood as an absolute prohibition against making moral judgments. However, it is essential to understand that this passage is not advocating for moral relativism or indifference. Instead, it is a call to avoid hypocrisy and judgmentalism in our interactions with others.

Jesus is not suggesting that we should never make moral judgments or that they are inherently wrong. Rather, he is warning against the danger of judging others harshly and condemning them without mercy while overlooking our own faults. The passage emphasizes the importance of practicing forgiveness and extending grace to others as we would want them to do the same for us.

Discernment and the appetite to condemn are two distinct concepts. Discernment refers to the ability to make sound judgments based on facts, wisdom, and experience. It involves evaluating situations and people with clarity and objectivity, recognizing right from wrong, and making wise decisions. The appetite to condemn, on the other hand, is a negative and destructive attitude that seeks to judge and criticize others harshly, often without sufficient evidence or understanding.

Jesus' message in Luke 6:37 is not about eliminating discernment but rather about eliminating the appetite to condemn. We should strive to make fair and just judgments based on facts and evidence, but we should also be mindful of our motivations and attitudes when doing so. We should avoid the temptation to judge others harshly or to condemn them without mercy, especially when we have not walked in their shoes or fully understood their circumstances.

In summary, the passage from Luke 6:37 is not a call to abandon moral judgments altogether. Instead, it is a call to avoid hypocrisy, judgmentalism, and harsh condemnation while practicing forgiveness and extending grace to others. We should strive to make sound judgments based on discernment and wisdom, but we should also be mindful of our motivations and attitudes when doing so.
