The deepest problem is **not anger by itself, but his refusal to enter into the father’s mercy**. His anger, envy, and sense of wounded fairness are expressions of a deeper posture: he understands his relationship with the father as a transaction based on service and reward rather than as sonship grounded in love.

Several details reveal this:

- **“These many years do I serve thee”** — he speaks like a servant earning wages, not like a son sharing his father’s life.
- **“Thou never gavest me a kid”** — his obedience has become a claim of entitlement.
- **“This thy son”** — he will not call the prodigal “my brother.”
- He is not merely upset that a sinner was forgiven; he is offended that the father’s joy is not distributed according to merit.

His complaint about fairness is understandable on the surface. The younger son has behaved shamefully, and the elder has remained faithful. The father does not deny that distinction or pretend the younger’s actions were harmless. But the elder assumes that mercy toward the younger diminishes the father’s love and his own standing. The father answers precisely that fear: **“Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine.”**

Thus the elder brother is also “outside”—not physically lost like the younger, but relationally alienated from the father’s heart. The younger wanted the father’s possessions without the father; the elder wants the father’s rewards without sharing the father’s compassion. Both are tempted to treat sonship as a means of getting what they want.

So the deepest issue is **self-righteousness expressed as resistance to grace**. Anger, envy, wounded fairness, and pride in obedience are all involved, but the central failure is that he cannot rejoice when mercy restores a brother. The parable ends without telling us whether he enters, leaving the question directed at hearers who may consider themselves faithful: **Will they come inside and share the Father’s joy?**
