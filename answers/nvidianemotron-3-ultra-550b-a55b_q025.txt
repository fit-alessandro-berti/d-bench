The short answer is: **The problem is the craving to be seen, not visibility itself.**

The grammar, context, and theology of Matthew 6 all converge on **intent** as the decisive factor. Here is the breakdown:

---

### 1. The Grammar: Purpose Clause vs. Circumstance
The Greek phrase *hopōs phthēte autois* ("**to be seen** by them" / "so that you may be seen by them") is a **purpose clause** (final clause), not a mere description of circumstance.

*   **If visibility were the problem**, the text would use a participle of circumstance ("while being seen") or a temporal clause ("when you are seen").
*   **Because craving is the problem**, the text uses *hopōs* + subjunctive, indicating **aim, goal, or motive**.

Jesus is not forbidding the *circumstance* of an audience; he is forbidding the *calculation* of an audience.

---

### 2. The Immediate Context: Matthew 5:16 (The "Counter-Verse")
This is the strongest internal evidence. Just verses earlier (in the same sermon), Jesus commands the exact opposite of "hiding":

> *"Let your light **so shine before men**, that they may **see your good works**, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."* (Matt 5:16)

*   **Matt 5:16:** Visibility is **mandatory** ("let it shine"). The *telos* (goal) is **God’s glory**.
*   **Matt 6:1:** Visibility is **incidental** (or avoided). The *telos* (goal) is **human applause**.

**The variable is not "seen/not seen." The variable is "for whose glory?"**

---

### 3. The Theological Logic: "Reward" as Receipt of Desire
Jesus contrasts two "rewards" (*misthos*):
1.  **Reward from men:** *Applause, reputation, social capital.* This is a **full payment** (*apechō* in v. 2, 5, 16 — "they have received their reward in full"). It is a closed transaction. You wanted to be seen; you were seen. Transaction complete.
2.  **Reward from the Father:** *Eternal inheritance, communion, resurrection life.* This is given to those whose motive was the Father, not the crowd.

The danger is **idolatry of the audience**. When you perform *for* the crowd, you make the crowd your god, and the crowd pays exactly what a false god pays: a fleeting, finite reward.

---

### 4. The "Hypocrite" Label (*hypokritēs*)
Jesus calls these performers *hypokritai* — **stage actors**.
*   An actor wears a mask (*prosōpon*) to *appear* as someone else.
*   The danger is **ontological**: You become the mask. You cease to be a "son of your Father" (who acts from identity) and become a performer (who acts for approval).
*   Public goodness becomes morally dangerous when it **disconnects the act from the heart**, turning virtue into theater.

---

### 5. Why Visibility *Feels* Like the Problem (And Why It Isn't)
Visibility is **the occasion of temptation**, not the essence of sin.
*   **Analogy:** Money is not the root of evil; *the love of money* is (1 Tim 6:10). Public goodness is not the root of hypocrisy; *the love of human praise* is (John 12:43).
*   Jesus prescribes **secrecy (vv. 3–4, 6, 17–18)** as a *spiritual discipline* (asceticism) to **cauterize the craving**, not because secrecy is inherently holier.
    *   "Do not let your left hand know what your right hand does" (v. 3) is hyperbole for **internal detachment from audience feedback**.
    *   The goal of the discipline is to reach a state where you *could* be seen, but **don't care** if you are — because the Father sees.

---

### Summary
| Factor | Visibility (Being Seen) | Craving (Intent to Be Seen) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Greek Syntax** | Circumstance (Participle) | **Purpose (*hopōs*)** |
| **Matt 5:16** | **Commanded** | Forbidden |
| **Reward Source** | Men (Finite) | **Father (Eternal)** |
| **Identity** | Actor (*hypokritēs*) | **Son/Daughter** |
| **Moral Status** | Neutral / Often Good | **Corrupting / Idolatrous** |

**Conclusion:** Public goodness is morally dangerous **only when the public becomes the *reason* for the goodness.** The verse condemns the *theatrical heart*, not the *public stage*.
