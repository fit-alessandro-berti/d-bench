The elder brother’s deepest problem isn’t primarily *anger* or *envy* (though both are real). Those are symptoms. His root problem is that he does not share the father’s heart—he relates to the father as a **master who owes wages**, not as a **father who gives grace**. In other words: **self-righteous, transactional sonship**.

### 1) He thinks like a servant, not a son
He says, “These many years **do I serve thee**… neither transgressed I… and yet **thou never gavest me**…” (vv. 29–30).  
That’s the language of performance and payback: *I worked, I obeyed, therefore I deserve.* He’s physically “in the house,” but inwardly he’s distant—he’s trying to secure joy as a wage rather than receive it as a gift.

The father’s reply exposes this: “**Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine**” (v. 31). The elder brother has been living like a hired hand while already possessing the status and inheritance of a son.

### 2) He resents mercy because it threatens his system of fairness
He can tolerate “justice” because it flatters his record; he cannot tolerate “grace” because it equalizes him with repentant sinners. That’s why he refuses to enter the celebration: he doesn’t just dislike his brother’s restoration—he dislikes what the father’s joy *means*.

Notice how he speaks:
- “**This thy son**” (v. 30), not “my brother.”  
He disowns relationship, because mercy to the undeserving feels like an insult to his moral capital.

### 3) He wants joy without communion
He complains, “that I might make merry **with my friends**” (v. 29), not “with you.” The father is inviting him into shared joy (“*we* should make merry,” v. 32). The elder brother wants the benefits of the household without the father’s values—especially the father’s delight in recovering the lost.

### 4) The parable’s target: the Pharisaic posture
Luke frames these parables with: “This man receiveth sinners” (Luke 15:1–2). The elder brother embodies the religious person who stays “obedient” yet cannot rejoice when God is merciful to the “wrong kind” of people. So the deepest issue is not emotional volatility; it’s a **misunderstanding of grace and a heart unaligned with God’s compassion**.

### Summary
- **Anger, envy, wounded fairness, pride**: real, but secondary.  
- **Deepest problem**: a **self-righteous, transactional relationship with the father** that makes him **unable to rejoice in mercy** and **unable to recognize his own need for grace**.

He is “lost” in a different way than the younger brother: not through rebellion, but through **resentful righteousness**.
