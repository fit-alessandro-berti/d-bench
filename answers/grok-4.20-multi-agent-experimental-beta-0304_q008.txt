**The verse treats love of the visible neighbor as the concrete, testable proof of love for the invisible God.**

John’s logic is both practical and theological:

1. **Visibility as the arena of genuineness**. It is *easier* to claim love for an invisible God. No immediate feedback, no daily friction, no risk of being contradicted by the person’s actual behavior. You can feel spiritual emotions, enjoy worship, hold orthodox doctrine, and still nurse resentment, bitterness, or indifference toward the people you actually see and live with. John calls this a lie because love is not primarily a feeling or a private spiritual experience; it is an active orientation of the will that shows itself in concrete relationships. If the love does not operate where it *can* be tested (the visible brother or sister), the claim that it operates where it *cannot* be tested (God) is self-refuting. “How can he…?” is a rhetorical question exposing impossibility, not mere difficulty.

2. **Theological connection between neighbor and God**. 
   - Every human being is made in God’s image (Genesis 1:27; James 3:9). To hate or persistently mistreat the image while claiming to love the One whose image it is is internally incoherent.
   - John has already tied love for God to love for others in this same chapter: “If we love one another, God abides in us” (4:12); “God is love, and whoever abides in love abides in God” (4:16). The same love that originates in God’s nature must flow through us to others. Cutting off the horizontal channel reveals that the vertical claim is false.
   - This echoes Jesus’ own teaching: the two great commandments are inseparable (Matthew 22:37–40), and final judgment turns on how we treated “the least of these my brothers” (Matthew 25:40, 45). John is simply applying that teaching with apostolic bluntness.

3. **The kind of self-deception being exposed**. 
   - It is the deception of *religious hypocrisy*—the ability to maintain a fervent “vertical” spirituality while living in practical unlove horizontally. This was a live danger in John’s churches. Some who claimed advanced spiritual knowledge or special anointing apparently felt superior to ordinary believers and were willing to break fellowship or withhold compassion (see 1 John 2:9–11; 3:14–18). They may have told themselves their love for God was so pure and “spiritual” that it transcended ordinary human relationships. John says this is not advanced spirituality; it is a lie.
   - It is also the deception of *emotional or intellectual substitute*. Many people confuse warm religious feelings, doctrinal correctness, or mystical experiences with love. John insists that love is known by its fruits (cf. 3:18—“not in word or talk but in deed and in truth”). Hatred, neglect, or bitterness toward a visible brother is decisive evidence that the love of God is not “abiding” in that person, no matter what they say or feel in prayer.
   - Finally, it exposes the *Gnostic-type dualism* that was already threatening the churches John wrote to: the idea that the spiritual realm is so superior that the material, visible, bodily realm (including actual human beings) doesn’t ultimately matter. John will have none of it. The incarnation itself (4:2–3) destroys that divide. God became visible in Christ; therefore love for the now-visible God must show itself in visible love for those He has redeemed.

**Summary**: The verse is a diagnostic test of the *authenticity* of our love for God. Because God is invisible, we are prone to self-deception—professing love while living in unlove. John cuts through the fog by saying: look at the brother or sister you *can* see. Your conduct toward them is the clearest evidence of whether the love of God is truly in you. Where there is ongoing hatred, neglect, or contempt for the visible image of God, the claim to love the invisible God is simply false. This is one of the most searching and practical tests of genuine Christianity in the entire New Testament.
