Short answer
- The “power” praised here is the power of self-giving love and divine obedience. It’s not force or domination. It’s the power that comes from voluntarily laying aside privilege to serve others, even to the point of suffering and death, in order to redeem.

More developed explanation
- What kind of power is being praised?
  - It’s the power of love expressed in kenosis (self-emptying). The Son, though in the form of God, does not cling to equality with God (Phil 2:6). Instead, he “empties” himself, taking the form of a servant, becoming like a human, and obediently going to the cross (Phil 2:7–8). This is powerful in a redemptive, transformational sense: through humble obedience, God accomplishes salvation and later exalts Jesus to the highest place (Phil 2:9–11).
  - The outcome of this power is not domination but reconciliation, service, and glory. The weakness of the cross becomes the means of true victory.

- Why is humility joined to greatness unsettling?
  - It subverts common measures of power. In many cultures, power means control, status, and visible strength. Here, greatness is displayed precisely in vulnerability, service, and obedience.
  - It challenges the idea that authority must be exercised over others. Jesus’ model shows authority exercised for others, in love, not over them.
  - The paradox is provocative: strength is revealed in weakness; victory comes through a cross; exaltation follows cruciform living.
  - For observers who prioritize results, efficiency, or coercive force, this path can feel disarming or disorienting because it flips expectations about how power ought to work.

Practical takeaways
- For believers: adopt the “mind of Christ” by pursuing servant leadership, humility, and obedience, especially when that means sacrificing personal advantage for others.
- In leadership or relationships: aim to build power that heals, unites, and lifts others, rather than power that suppresses or intimidates.
- Theologically: see the cross as the ultimate demonstration of power—God’s power to save and to transform hearts through love and sacrifice, not merely to dominate.

A note on interpretation
- Paul’s language uses the mystery of kenosis: Jesus remains truly God but, in his incarnation, does not exploit that divine prerogative for himself. The text then emphasizes God’s response—exaltation—showing that this humble path is the true way to glory. This is a foundational theme in Christian theology of power, leadership, and salvation.
