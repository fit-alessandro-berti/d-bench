The shift you’re observing—from “cease to do evil” to “learn to do well”—is morally instructive. “Cease” is restraint; it pulls us back from what is wrong or harmful. That correction is necessary, but is preliminary; stopping evil does not yet produce righteousness.

“Learn to do well” reframes goodness not as mere absence of wrongdoing (not doing harm) but as something active and disciplined that must be cultivated: seeking judgment, relieving the oppressed, defending the fatherless, pleading for the widow. These are forms of love expressed as justice and protection for the vulnerable.

So the text suggests goodness is not just withdrawal from sin; it is a practiced, habitual orientation toward love and justice, built through repeated action and moral training.
