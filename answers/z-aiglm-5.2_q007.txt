To understand Luke 6:37, it is vital to distinguish between the abandonment of morality and the surrender of our ego’s desire to play God. The command to "judge not" is often misinterpreted as a call to moral relativism—the idea that nothing is truly right or wrong. However, Jesus is not saying that moral judgments never matter; rather, He is diagnosing the human heart's tendency to weaponize morality for self-righteousness.

Here is an exploration of the difference between moral relativism, discernment, and the appetite to condemn.

### "Judge Not" vs. "Morality Doesn't Matter"

If Jesus were saying that moral judgments never matter, the entire verse would collapse into incoherence. To tell someone "you should not judge" and "you should forgive" is, itself, a moral judgment. It assumes a clear standard of right and wrong. 

When Jesus says, "Judge not," He is not obliterating the moral law; He is removing human beings from the ultimate judge's seat. There are several reasons why this is different from moral indifference:

1. **The Standard Remains:** The verse implies that there *is* a judgment to be faced ("and ye shall not be judged"). Jesus is teaching that God is the ultimate arbiter of justice. When we usurp that role, we invite God’s strict judgment upon ourselves. Morality matters so much that we are unfit to administer its final verdicts.
2. **The Goal is Transformation, Not Blindness:** In the broader context of Luke 6, Jesus tells His followers to remove the plank from their own eye before trying to remove the speck from their brother’s eye. The assumption is that there *is* a speck (a moral failing) that needs addressing, but the observer is too compromised by their own hypocrisy to address it accurately. 
3. **The Command to Forgive:** Forgiveness is impossible without a moral judgment having been made. You cannot forgive someone unless a wrong has actually been committed. Jesus assumes wrongs will be done, but He commands a response of grace rather than retaliation. 

### Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn

To navigate a world where morality matters but ultimate judgment belongs to God, we must differentiate between *discernment* and the *appetite to condemn*.

**Discernment** is the capacity to perceive reality accurately. It is the ability to look at an action, a behavior, or a system and say, "This is destructive," or "This is true." 
* Jesus frequently commanded His followers to practice discernment, telling them to "beware of false prophets" and to "know them by their fruits" (Matthew 7:15-16). 
* Discernment is rooted in clarity, wisdom, and love. It is observational and protective. A discerning person recognizes a thief not because they despise the thief, but so they can lock their door and protect their family. Discernment seeks to understand the difference between good and evil so that good can be chosen and harm avoided.

**The Appetite to Condemn**, on the other hand, is an emotional and spiritual drive. It is not primarily about discerning truth; it is about asserting dominance, enacting vengeance, or soothing one's own insecurities.
* Condemnation is final. It does not just say, "You did a bad thing"; it says, "You are worthless, and I delight in your ruin."
* The appetite to condemn is fueled by hypocrisy and self-righteousness. When we condemn, we often use the moral failings of others as a contrast to make ourselves look brighter by comparison. 
* Jesus targeted this appetite directly in His ministry, reserving His harshest words for the Pharisees—religious leaders who possessed great moral discernment but used it as a weapon to crush the vulnerable and elevate themselves.

### The Heart of the Verse

Luke 6:37 is a warning against the *appetite* to condemn. We are entirely capable of looking at a situation, discerning that it is morally bankrupt, and yet refusing to condemn the person involved. 

When Jesus says "condemn not," He is asking us to relinquish our desire for final, ruinous judgment against others. By doing so, we leave room for repentance and grace—both for the offender and for ourselves. The second half of the verse reveals the mechanism of the spiritual universe: the standard we apply to others is the standard God will apply to us. 

If we treat morality as if it doesn't matter, we invite chaos. But if we hold morality tightly while wielding it as a sword to condemn our neighbors, we invite the full, unmitigated weight of divine justice upon our own hidden flaws. The biblical path is a razor's edge: we must discern evil with clear eyes, but hold our neighbors with open hands of forgiveness, trusting the ultimate scales of justice to the only Judge who can see the human heart.
