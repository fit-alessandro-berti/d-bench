**The passage presents speech as the natural overflow and disclosure of the heart’s hidden moral stockpile (its “treasure”), making words primarily symptoms and revelations of that inner condition—while the surrounding context also treats them as responsible choices.**

### The core connection: heart as moral storehouse
Jesus addresses the Pharisees (“generation of vipers”) after they attribute his exorcisms to Beelzebul. He asks how people who *are* evil can speak good things, then explains:

- “Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh.”
- “A good man out of the good treasure of the heart bringeth forth good things: and an evil man out of the evil treasure bringeth forth evil things.”

The key imagery is the heart as a *θησαυρός* (treasure / storehouse / stockpile) that accumulates good or evil. From the *περισσεύμα* (abundance / overflow / surplus) of that store, the mouth speaks. In verse 35 the verb is *ἐκβάλλει* (“casts out / puts forth / ejects”), portraying speech as the active production or expulsion of what has been stored inside.

The heart is therefore the hidden moral reservoir; speech is what it necessarily yields when it overflows. The parallel in Luke 6:45 and the tree-and-fruit saying in Matthew 12:33 reinforce the same logic: nature determines produce. A bad tree cannot bear good fruit; an evil heart cannot genuinely produce good speech.

### Symptoms, revelations, or choices?
**Primarily symptoms and revelations.**  
The rhetorical question (“how can ye, being evil, speak good things?”) treats speech as diagnostic of the heart’s condition. Evil words are the symptom of an evil store; good words are the symptom of a good one. The mouth simply expresses “that which fills the heart.” Classic commentators (e.g., Matthew Henry) describe the heart as a treasury whose contents inevitably flow out—muddy water from a corrupt spring, good things from a good store. Speech therefore *reveals* the hidden moral stockpile that would otherwise remain invisible.

**Also choices, though secondarily and in a constrained sense.**  
The very next verses (12:36–37) declare that people will give account for every idle word and will be justified or condemned by their words. Speech is therefore a morally responsible human act, not a purely passive emission. The active verb *ἐκβάλλει* further implies agency in bringing the stored content forth. Yet the passage’s main thrust is that the *quality and content* of the speech are determined by the prior state of the heart’s treasure rather than by unconstrained momentary deliberation. One chooses to speak (and is accountable for it), but what comes out is constrained by—and symptomatic of—what has been stockpiled inside.

In short, the text presents words as **all three**:  
- **symptoms** of the heart’s moral condition,  
- **revelations** of its hidden treasure,  
- and **choices**—responsible acts of expression for which the speaker is judged—yet choices whose character is governed by the abundance already stored in the heart.

The practical implication Jesus draws is that reforming speech in isolation is futile; the heart itself must be changed, because “out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh.”
