To address this question, we first ground ourselves in the context of Philippians 2:5-8: this passage quotes an early Christian "Christ hymn" (a pre-Pauline confessional creed) that Paul invokes to confront the Philippian church’s internal status rivalry and competitive behavior, holding up the example of Jesus as a radical alternative to the hierarchical, domineering norms of both Roman imperial culture and standard human assumptions about power.
### What kind of power is praised here?
The power celebrated in this passage is a paradoxical, self-emptying (kenotic) power rooted in divine love, which stands in direct opposition to coercive, status-based domination.
Unlike worldly power, defined by the ability to claim privilege, impose one’s will, and assert superiority over others, this is the power of the one who, though "in the form of God" and fully equal with God (holding ultimate divine status, authority, and privilege), voluntarily chooses not to exploit or cling to that status for his own advantage. It is an active, intentional power: Christ "emptied himself, taking the form of a servant," and humbled himself "to the point of death, even death on a cross"—a deliberate renunciation of rightful privilege to enter into human solidarity, serve the marginalized, and redeem humanity from sin and death.
Its triumph is not achieved through force or subjugation: it conquers the powers of sin, death, and oppressive empire not by destroying enemies, but by bearing their shame and violence in love, and being vindicated by God’s resurrection (as the immediate following verses 9–11 confirm, where God exalts Christ precisely because of his humility). It is a power of mutual self-gift, not domination, and it is the pattern Paul calls the entire Christian community to embody, rejecting self-interest and status rivalry in favor of prioritizing the good of others.

### Why is humility joined to greatness more unsettling than open domination?
Open domination aligns with our most ingrained, culturally reinforced assumptions about how power works, making even unjust hierarchies feel predictable and "the way things are." We can easily categorize open dominators as "evil," position ourselves as innocent victims (or justified enforcers of the system), and avoid challenging the underlying logic of power itself. Humility joined to greatness, by contrast, upends these core assumptions in deeply uncomfortable ways:
1. **It breaks the assumed link between power and self-interest.** We are taught that greatness is measured by status, control, and the ability to advance one’s own position, and that people with power will naturally use it to protect their privilege. When a figure with the highest possible authority (in this case, the divine Christ) voluntarily renounces that privilege to serve and sacrifice, it exposes the moral emptiness of status-seeking and domination as natural or necessary. It reveals that true greatness is not about status, but about love, which destabilizes the legitimacy of every hierarchical system that relies on the claim that the powerful are inherently more deserving of authority.
2. **It eliminates the safe moral distance domination affords.** With open oppression, we can externalize evil, blame the "bad" individual dominator, and avoid examining our own complicity in unjust systems or our own quiet desire for status, control, or comfort. But humility joined to greatness holds up a mirror to our own choices: if the most powerful being in the universe can choose to serve rather than dominate, then our own choices to exploit others, seek status, or benefit from oppression are not inevitable—they are moral failures. There is no one else to blame, forcing a confrontation with our own participation in systems of harm.
3. **It cannot be engaged with on familiar terms of power.** Open domination can be resisted with force, counter-domination, or rebellion, all of which operate within the same hierarchical logic of power as the oppressor. But the power of self-giving love does not respond to force with force, hate with hate, or oppression with counter-oppression. Instead, it responds with forgiveness, service, and sacrifice, short-circuiting our usual strategies for navigating power. It does not demand compliance through threat; it invites a free response of love and self-examination, which is far more demanding than the passive compliance or active rebellion that domination elicits.
4. **In its original context, it directly subverted the ultimate symbol of imperial power.** For first-century Roman audiences, the cross was not a symbol of love, but the ultimate tool of imperial terror: a public, shameful death reserved for rebels and enslaved people, designed to crush dissent and assert Rome’s absolute dominance over its subjects. Christ’s voluntary submission to the cross turned that symbol of imperial domination into the ultimate symbol of a different, greater power: the power of love that overcomes even the empire’s most brutal instrument of control. For Roman authorities and those invested in imperial power, this subversion was a direct existential threat; for Jewish audiences expecting a conquering Messiah who would overthrow Roman rule, a Messiah who humiliates himself to a criminal’s death was a shocking scandal that upended all their expectations of what divine power should look like.
In short, open domination is unsettling only when we are its targets, but it is familiar in its logic. Humility joined to greatness is unsettling for everyone, because it exposes the moral failure of our shared assumptions about power, and calls us to a way of being that rejects domination entirely—even when we are the ones with power.
