The rejection of worship in Amos 5:21-24 is rooted in a profound critique of hypocrisy and empty ritualism. Here's an explanation:

**Context**
The book of Amos is set against the backdrop of the northern kingdom of Israel in the 8th century BC. Israel was enjoying economic prosperity, but this wealth was accompanied by widespread social injustice and moral corruption. The people were religiously devout, performing all the appropriate external rituals of worship—feast days, burnt offerings, peace offerings, singing, and music. However, their religious practices were not accompanied by a corresponding commitment to justice and righteousness.

**Why the Sharp Rejection**
God, through the prophet Amos, rejects their worship so sharply not because worship itself is inherently bad, but because their worship was devoid of true repentance and ethical living. They were going through the motions of religion without changing their hearts or their behavior. They believed that their prayers, sacrifices, and religious ceremonies would earn them God's favor, even while they were oppressing the poor, exploiting the vulnerable, and engaging in unethical business practices.

This rejection is not a call to abandon worship entirely but a demand for genuine commitment to the values of justice and righteousness. God is more interested in a relationship with his people based on mutual trust and obedience than in superficial acts of worship.

**Moral or Spiritual Fraud Exposed**
The moral or spiritual fraud being exposed here is the hypocrisy of worship without justice. The Israelites were using their religious practices as a shield to cover their sins. They thought that by performing the external rituals, they could ignore their social and moral obligations. They were not necessarily rejecting God, but they were misunderstanding the nature of true worship. True worship, as God desires it, involves a wholehearted commitment to loving God and loving one's neighbor.

The passage contrasts the rejected worship with the command to "let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream" (Amos 5:24). This emphasizes that justice and righteousness are not optional add-ons to worship but are integral to it. False worship occurs when people use religion to avoid confronting their social and moral responsibilities.

In summary, the rejection of worship in Amos 5:21-24 is a sharp condemnation of hypocrisy and empty ritualism. It exposes the moral and spiritual fraud of using religious practices to cover up unethical behavior and underscores the importance of justice and righteousness as the foundation of true worship.
