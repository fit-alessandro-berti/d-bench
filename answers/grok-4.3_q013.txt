The sharp rejection in Amos 5:21–24 is not a rejection of worship itself, but a judgment on worship that has become a form of religious performance detached from justice. The language is deliberately severe—“I hate, I despise,” “I will not smell,” “I will not accept,” “I will not hear”—because the prophet is exposing a deeper problem than mere imbalance.

### The Fraud Being Exposed

The core issue is **hypocrisy that weaponizes religion**. The people of Israel were maintaining an active cultic life—feasts, burnt offerings, grain offerings, peace offerings, songs, and instrumental music—while simultaneously practicing systemic oppression. Earlier in the chapter (and throughout Amos), the prophet describes land seizures, bribery in the courts, selling the poor into debt slavery for trivial amounts, and sexual exploitation. The religious activity was not simply coexisting with injustice; it was functioning as its cover.

In this context, the rituals had become a moral anesthetic. By offering sacrifices and singing hymns, the worshippers could maintain the appearance of devotion and covenant loyalty while their daily conduct violated the very ethical demands of the covenant. The worship was not neutral; it was fraudulent because it allowed them to feel religiously secure while they defrauded the vulnerable. God’s refusal to “smell” the offerings or “hear” the music is a statement that the ritual had become an abomination precisely because it was being used to mask evil.

### Why Not “Better Balance”?

A call for “better balance” would assume that the worship and the injustice were two separate spheres that simply needed to be brought into proper proportion. Amos rejects that framing. The problem was not insufficient religious activity alongside some injustice; the problem was that the religious activity had become spiritually and morally corrupt *because* of the injustice. 

In the prophetic worldview (shared by Isaiah, Micah, and Hosea), genuine worship is not a distinct compartment of life. When justice is absent, the worship itself becomes a lie. The rituals were not being offered in ignorance or weakness; they were being offered by people who had “turned justice into wormwood” (Amos 5:7) and “trampled on the poor.” Therefore, God does not ask for more offerings or better music. He demands that the substitute be abandoned. The famous line in 5:24—“let justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like an ever-flowing stream”—is not an addition to their worship; it is presented as the true alternative to it.

This is why the rejection is total rather than corrective. The worship had crossed a line from imperfect devotion into active deception—both self-deception and an attempt to deceive God. In that situation, more religious activity would only deepen the fraud.
