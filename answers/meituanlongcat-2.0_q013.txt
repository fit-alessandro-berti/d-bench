To understand why Amos delivers such a sharp, total rejection of Israel’s worship—rather than a gentle call for "better balance"—we have to look at the nature of the covenant relationship between God and Israel, and the specific social context of the 8th century BC. 

In the modern Western mindset, we often view religion as a matter of "balance": a little bit of ritual, a little bit of ethics, a little bit of spirituality. But in the ancient Near Eastern covenant framework, worship and ethics were not two separate categories that needed balancing; they were inextricably linked. 

Here is why the passage rejects worship so sharply, and the specific moral and spiritual fraud it exposes.

### Why Sharp Rejection Instead of "Better Balance"?

**1. The worship was functioning as a substitute, not a supplement.**
If the Israelites were simply neglecting justice while trying their best, a call for "balance" would be appropriate. But the text implies they were using their worship *as a substitute* for justice. They were offering the "fat beasts" and singing loud songs precisely so they could continue exploiting the poor without guilt. You cannot "balance" a bribe; you must reject it. 

**2. The worship was an active offense to God.**
God says, "I hate, I despise... I will not smell." The language is visceral. In the ancient world, the smoke of sacrifices was a "soothing aroma" to the deity. By saying He refuses to smell it, God is saying their worship is not just ineffective; it is repulsive. It is the spiritual equivalent of bringing a bouquet of flowers to a murder victim's funeral. The flowers are not "out of balance" with the tragedy; they are an obscene insult to it.

**3. The "But" of verse 24 demands a complete paradigm shift.**
The Hebrew word translated "But" (*v'eem*) is a strong adversative. It signals a total break. God is not saying, "Add justice to your worship." He is saying, "Stop the worship, and start the justice." The metaphor of waters and a mighty stream implies that justice is not a secondary ingredient; it is the very current of life. Without it, the riverbed of religion is dry.

### The Moral and Spiritual Fraud Being Exposed

Amos is exposing a profound, multi-layered fraud. The Israelites were committing what we might call **sacerdotal fraud**—the belief that the mechanics of religion can override the demands of morality. 

**1. Transactional Religion (The Divine Bribe)**
The fraud here is treating God like a mafia don or a corrupt judge. The people believed that if they paid the "tariff" of sacrifices and songs, God was contractually obligated to bless them and overlook their sins. They were trying to purchase God's favor with animal blood to avoid the cost of repentance. God rejects this because He is not a mercenary; He is a covenant King who demands loyalty, not just tribute.

**2. Spiritual Bypassing (Vertical Piety to Evade Horizontal Cruelty)**
This is the most damning fraud. Throughout the book of Amos, God details how the wealthy are trampling the poor, taking bribes, and perverting justice (Amos 2:6-8, 5:11-12). The spiritual fraud is using the vertical relationship (worshiping God) to absolve the horizontal relationship (oppressing neighbor). They were singing hymns to the God of justice while actively committing injustice. This is the ultimate hypocrisy: claiming to love the Creator while despising the created.

**3. The Illusion of Substitution (Offering the Symbol Instead of the Reality)**
God did not ask for the "noise of thy songs" or the "melody of thy viols." He asked for a broken and contrite heart, and a just society. The fraud is offering God the *symbol* of devotion (music, incense, fat animals) instead of the *substance* of devotion (obedience, mercy, justice). It is the same fraud Jesus later condemned when He quoted Isaiah: "This people honors me with their lips, but their heart is far from me" (Matthew 15:8). 

**4. Performative Religion as a Shield Against Accountability**
By filling the temple with noise and smoke, the religious establishment created a smokescreen. As long as the rituals were happening, the people felt secure. "We have a temple, we have sacrifices, we have worship bands—surely God is with us!" The fraud was using religious activity as a psychological and social shield to avoid the terrifying reality that God was actually against them because of how they treated the vulnerable. 

### Summary

Amos does not ask for better balance because the worship of Israel was not a well-intentioned effort that had simply lost its way. It was a calculated, fraudulent system designed to give the illusion of piety while the powerful continued to devour the weak. 

The sharp rejection is necessary because **worship divorced from justice is not just incomplete; it is blasphemy.** It takes the name of the Holy One and uses it to sanctify human cruelty. Therefore, God demands the noise to stop, the sacrifices to cease, and the mighty, unstoppable river of justice to finally flow.
