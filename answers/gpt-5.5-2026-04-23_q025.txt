In Matthew 6:1, the moral danger is **not visibility itself**, but **the desire to be visible in order to gain human praise**.

Jesus says:

> “Take heed that ye do not your alms before men, **to be seen of them**…”

The key phrase is **“to be seen of them.”** The issue is not simply that others notice the good deed, but that the deed is performed *for the sake of being noticed*. Public goodness becomes dangerous when it turns into self-advertisement.

This matters because Jesus has already said:

> “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven.”  
> — Matthew 5:16

So there is a kind of visible goodness that is right: when people see the good and are led to glorify God. But in Matthew 6:1, the aim is different: the person wants others to admire *him*. The good act becomes a performance.

The danger is that outward righteousness can become inward vanity. Almsgiving, prayer, and fasting—good things in themselves—can be corrupted by the craving for reputation. That is why Jesus later speaks of “hypocrites” who sound trumpets, pray conspicuously, and disfigure their faces so others will notice them.

So the contrast is:

- **Matthew 5:16:** visible good works that point people to God.  
- **Matthew 6:1:** visible good works designed to draw praise to oneself.

Public goodness is morally dangerous because it can subtly shift the “audience” from God to people. Once human admiration becomes the desired reward, Jesus says, that reward is all one receives:

> “Otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven.”

So the problem is not being seen. The problem is **craving to be seen**, and doing good as a way of purchasing admiration.
