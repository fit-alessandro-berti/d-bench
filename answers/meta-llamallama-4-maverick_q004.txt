The kind of trust being asked for in Proverbs 3:5-6 is not about abandoning one's intellect or critical faculties but rather about cultivating a humble self-distrust. This passage encourages the reader to trust in the Lord with all their heart, rather than relying solely on their own understanding.

**Humble Self-Distrust vs. Anti-Intellectualism**

To understand the difference between humble self-distrust and anti-intellectualism, let's examine the two concepts:

1.  **Humble Self-Distrust:** This involves recognizing the limitations of one's own understanding and being open to guidance and wisdom from a higher source. It acknowledges that human knowledge is finite and that there may be perspectives or insights beyond one's own comprehension. Humble self-distrust is about being aware of one's own biases, blind spots, and the potential for error, and being willing to learn and be corrected.

2.  **Anti-Intellectualism:** This is a rejection of knowledge, critical thinking, or intellectual pursuits. Anti-intellectualism often involves a disregard for evidence, reasoning, or expertise, and can manifest as a simplistic or uncritical approach to complex issues.

The trust encouraged in Proverbs 3:5-6 is rooted in humble self-distrust, not anti-intellectualism. The passage does not dismiss the value of understanding or intellectual pursuits but rather cautions against relying solely on one's own limited perspective.

The phrase "lean not unto thine own understanding" suggests that one's own understanding is insufficient or potentially flawed. Instead, the passage encourages the reader to "acknowledge him" (the Lord) in all their ways, implying a willingness to seek guidance and wisdom from a higher source.

In essence, the trust being asked for is a trusting humility, where one acknowledges the limitations of their own understanding and is open to guidance from a higher source, while still valuing knowledge and critical thinking.
