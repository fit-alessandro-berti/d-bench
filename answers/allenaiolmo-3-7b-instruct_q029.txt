
Great question! Let's break down **Galatians 6:1** and your query about the *moral posture for correction*, as well as the *relationship among restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion*.

---

### **1. What kind of moral posture is required for correction here?**

The verse says:  
> “Brethren, if a brother be overtaken in a fault… ye which are spiritual, restore such an one in the spirit of meekness…”

**Required moral posture:**

- **Humility and Compassion (Meekness):**  
  The key moral posture is **meekness**—not pride, not harshness, but gentle and patient love. As spiritual brothers and sisters, the corrector must approach the one in fault with humility and a desire to help, not shame or condemnation.
  
- **Empathy and Forgiveness:**  
  Meekness here implies putting the other person’s feelings and dignity first. Correction is not about humiliating or wounding, but about restoring—helping the person return to right standing, with gentleness.
  
- **Self-restraint and Awareness (Self-suspicion):**  
  The phrase “considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted” means the corrector must be self-aware, recognizing their own potential to sin or fall into the same temptation. This self-suspending (suspending judgment, not acting out of pride or self-righteousness) is part of the posture.
  
In summary, the posture is **one of humility, love, patience, self-awareness, and a desire to restore rather than judge or shame.**

---

### **2. Why are restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion kept together in this verse?**

Paul is giving a holistic approach to correcting a brother’s fault. Here’s why these elements are connected:

#### **a. Restoration**
- **Purpose of correction:** The ultimate goal is **restoration**—helping the brother return to the community in right relationship with God. It’s not just about pointing out the error but actively working to bring the person back to spiritual health.
  
#### **b. Meekness**
- **How it’s done:** Restoration is done “in the spirit of meekness.” Meekness is the *method*—gentle, non-confrontational, non-accusatory. It’s about love, not power or authority for its own sake.
  
#### **c. Self-suspicion (self-awareness)**
- **Why it matters:** The corrector must not act from pride or personal resentment. By “considering thyself,” they are being warned to pause and reflect: “Am I just as guilty? Could this happen to me?” This self-suspension of judgment prevents the correction from becoming a source of pride, defensiveness, or hypocrisy.
  
**They are kept together because:**
- Effective restoration is not a one-sided act but a relational one, done with love and humility (meekness).
- Without self-suspicion (self-awareness), the correction could easily turn into a display of power or judgment, undermining the very goal of restoration.
- Paul is teaching that the spiritual community’s correction is a demonstration of the very virtues it aims to uphold: love, humility, and the avoidance of pride.

In essence, the three elements form a balanced approach:
- **Restoration** (the *goal*)
- **Meekness** (the *manner*)
- **Self-suspicion** (the *guardrail*, to ensure humility and avoid sinning by correction)
