This is a profound question that goes to the heart of the Parable of the Prodigal Son (Luke 15:11–32). Let’s examine the elder brother’s words and actions in verses 28–32 and consider what his real problem is.

**What does the elder brother say?**
He says to his father:
- He has served faithfully for many years, never breaking a commandment.
- Despite this, he was never given a celebration (a kid to make merry with friends).
- Now, when the younger son returns after wasting his inheritance with "harlots," the father throws a lavish feast for him.
- He feels slighted, as if the father’s actions are unfair.

**Possible interpretations of his problem:**

1. **Anger:**  
   He is clearly angry, but anger is a symptom, not the root problem.

2. **Envy:**  
   He resents his brother’s return and the celebration, which suggests envy of his brother’s restored status and the father’s favor.

3. **Wounded fairness / Sense of injustice:**  
   He feels that his years of faithful service have gone unrecognized, and that the father’s actions are unfair. He sees himself as the one who deserved the celebration, not the wayward brother.

4. **Pride in obedience:**  
   He takes pride in his own obedience and sees it as entitling him to reward and recognition. His complaint is, in effect, “I deserve this more than he does.”

5. **Discomfort with mercy itself:**  
   He seems disturbed not just by the lack of reward, but by the father’s willingness to show mercy and joy over the returning son. There’s a sense that mercy feels undeserved or misapplied.

---

**What is the deepest problem?**

The text suggests that the elder brother’s problem is not simply anger or envy, but a combination of wounded fairness and pride in his own righteousness. He is hurt that his obedience has not been publicly honored, and he feels that the father’s mercy toward the prodigal son is misplaced or even offensive.

However, Jesus, through the parable, points to something deeper: the elder brother’s inability to rejoice in the mercy and forgiveness shown to another. His self-righteousness blinds him to the joy of the kingdom. He is more concerned with what he deserves than with celebrating the restoration of a lost sibling.

**Therefore, the elder brother’s deepest problem is a combination of:**

- **Pride in his own obedience**
- **A wounded sense of fairness**
- **An inability to rejoice in the mercy shown to others**

This is not merely envy or anger, but a spiritual blindness—a failure to enter into the joy of forgiveness and reconciliation because of his own self-focus and sense of superiority.

**In summary:**  
The elder brother’s deepest problem is **pride in obedience and a wounded sense of fairness**, which prevents him from sharing in the father’s mercy and joy. He is more concerned with what he deserves than with celebrating the restoration of the lost.
