While all the options you listed are present in the text, they function at different levels of depth. To identify the **deepest** problem, we must distinguish between symptoms, motivations, and the underlying spiritual condition.

Here is an analysis of the layers, moving from the surface to the root:

### 1. The Symptoms: Anger and Envy
*   **Anger:** Verse 28 states explicitly, "he was angry." However, anger is rarely a root cause; it is almost always a secondary emotion protecting something else.
*   **Envy:** The elder brother is jealous of the "fatted calf" and the celebration. Yet, his envy is specific: he is not envious of the brother's freedom, but of the *reward* given despite the brother's sin.
*   **Verdict:** These are the visible **fruits** of the problem, not the root.

### 2. The Mindset: Wounded Fairness
*   The elder brother operates on a strict system of merit. In verse 29, he presents his resume: "these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment."
*   He views the Father's love as a wage to be earned rather than a gift to be received. When the younger brother receives a reward without the work, the elder brother's internal ledger is thrown off. He feels the universe is unjust.
*   **Verdict:** This is the **intellectual justification** for his anger, but it begs the question: *Why* is his sense of self-worth tied so tightly to fairness?

### 3. The Identity: Pride in Obedience
*   This is the engine driving the wounded fairness. The elder brother believes he is "good" and his brother is "bad." His identity is built on moral superiority ("neither transgressed I at any time").
*   Because he trusts in his own obedience, he feels he has leverage over the Father. He speaks to the Father not as a son to a dad, but as a worker to a boss ("serve thee").
*   **Verdict:** This is the **psychological root**. His pride prevents him from seeing his own need for grace.

### 4. The Spiritual Condition: Discomfort with Mercy Itself
*   This is the **deepest spiritual problem**. The parable is ultimately about the nature of God (the Father). The Father represents grace that disrupts human systems of justice.
*   The elder brother cannot tolerate a world where love is not transactional. If the Father can love the "wasteful" son freely, then the elder brother's "goodness" purchases nothing special. Mercy renders his pride obsolete.
*   By refusing to go in (v. 28), he rejects the Father's invitation to grace. He would rather stay outside in the cold of his own "justice" than enter the warmth of the Father's "mercy."
*   **Verdict:** This is the **theological root**.

### Conclusion
The elder brother's deepest problem is **discomfort with mercy itself**.

While **pride in obedience** is the mechanism that creates his hardness of heart, the ultimate barrier between him and the Father is his inability to accept that love is a gift, not a wage. He wants a Master who rewards performance, but the Father wants a relationship based on sonship.

As the Father says in verse 31, *"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine."* The inheritance was already his by right of birth, not by right of works. The elder brother's tragedy is that he was so focused on earning what he already possessed that he missed the joy of simply belonging. He is just as "lost" as the younger brother, but he is lost in the house, blinded by his own righteousness.
