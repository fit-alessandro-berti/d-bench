### **What kind of self is being denied here?**

In these verses, Jesus says:

> “Whosoever will come after me, let him deny himself, and take up his cross, and follow me.”

The phrase **“deny himself”** (Greek: *sukinoi hēmēn*) is key here. In a biblical context, “to deny oneself” usually means to set aside personal desires, ambitions, pride, or even one’s identity in favor of something higher—namely, following Christ.

This is not about denying one's existence or basic humanity (which would be self-annihilation), but rather **denying one's own interests, status, or claims to self-sufficiency**. It means letting go of what you want, what you think is important, or what gives you a sense of importance or comfort. In other words:

- **Denial of personal ambition or ego:** You stop seeking recognition, power, or comfort.
- **Surrender of personal priorities:** You put aside your own needs, desires, or even your “life plan.”
- **Identifying with the “lower self”** (the one that wants to be in control) and instead identifying with the “higher calling” (to follow Jesus and his mission).

This is similar to Paul’s teaching in Philippians 2:7: “And being found in human form, he had nowhere to lay his head, and was hungry and thirsty...”

So, the “self” being denied is the **self that is self-focused, proud, or seeks to control one’s circumstances through one’s own means**.

---

### **Why does this sound liberating to some and offensive to others?**

#### **Liberating to Some:**

For many, this is liberating because:

1. **Freedom from Self-Imposed Limitations:** If you are held back by pride, selfishness, or a desire for control, letting go of that “self” can feel like true freedom. You stop being shackled by your own desires.
2. **Call to Radical Commitment:** It promises a deeper, more meaningful life—where your identity is not tied to your own success or comfort, but to something greater (God’s will, the gospel).
3. **Authentic Living:** Some see it as a call to live authentically, not as a “self-made” person but as one who is transformed by Christ.
4. **Divine Purpose:** Denying oneself can be understood as making room for God’s purpose in your life, rather than your own agenda.

#### **Offensive to Others:**

For others, this can seem problematic because:

1. **Moral Absolutism:** Some people feel that “denying yourself” could mean suppressing normal human needs, emotions, or even expressing themselves. It can sound like suppression or self-denial to an extreme.
2. **Pressure and Burden:** The phrase “take up your cross” can feel like an unbearable burden—suggesting that only those prepared for suffering should follow Jesus. This can be interpreted as demanding total self-sacrifice, which some find intimidating or even unfair.
3. **Individualism vs. Collectivism:** In a culture that values personal achievement and autonomy, the idea of abandoning one’s personal goals for a greater cause can feel oppressive or even morally suspect.
4. **Misunderstanding of the Command:** Some may interpret “denying oneself” as rejecting one’s identity or worth as a human being, which is not what Jesus means. He means renouncing self-centeredness, not annihilation.
5. **Religious Pressure:** The message can be misused by preachers who encourage extreme self-denial that leads to unhealthy depression or repression.

---

### **In Summary**

- **What’s being denied:** The self that is self-willed, ambitious, and focused on personal comfort or status.
- **Why liberating:** It offers freedom from the tyranny of self-interest and the possibility of a life oriented around something higher.
- **Why offensive:** It can feel demanding, suppress personal needs, or be misunderstood as requiring total self-erasure rather than transformation.

**Jesus is not calling for self-destruction, but for transformation—the kind that turns self-interest into self-giving love for God and others.**
