Galatians 6:1 emphasizes the attitude and approach necessary when correcting someone caught in a fault. The verse says:

> "Brethren, if a man be overtaken in a fault, ye which are spiritual, restore such an one in the spirit of meekness; considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted."

### The Moral Posture Required for Correction

1. **Spiritual Maturity and Sensitivity**  
   The correction is to be done by those "which are spiritual," implying individuals who have a mature, Christlike understanding and sensitivity. This isn’t for the arrogant or self-righteous, but for those who know the seriousness of sin and the grace available through God.

2. **Restoration, Not Condemnation**  
   The goal is to "restore," which suggests healing, bringing back, and reconciliation rather than punishment or humiliation. The posture is one of helping a brother or sister regain their footing rather than casting them down.

3. **Spirit of Meekness**  
   Meekness here refers to humility and gentleness, not weakness. The one who corrects must do so gently, patiently, and lovingly, recognizing their own limitations and vulnerabilities. Meekness prevents pride and harsh judgment.

4. **Self-Consideration or Self-Suspicion**  
   "Considering thyself" means reflecting on one’s own weaknesses and the possibility that the same temptation or fall could happen to them. It cultivates empathy, humility, and caution, helping to avoid a proud or condemning spirit.

### Why Are Restoration, Meekness, and Self-Suspicion Kept Together?

- **They balance each other to ensure positive and effective correction.**  
  Restoration without meekness can become harsh correction; meekness without restoration could mean tolerating sin without accountability; and without self-reflection, the person correcting may become prideful or blind to their own vulnerabilities.

- **They guard against abuse of authority or superiority.**  
  Each concept protects against a different wrong way of correcting: judgmentalism, severity, or hypocrisy.

- **They reflect the heart of Christ’s correction.**  
  Christ’s approach to sinners was always one of restoring them with humility and a deep awareness of human weakness, never coming from pride or self-righteousness.

### Summary

The moral posture required is one of humble, spiritually mature gentleness, motivated by love and a self-aware spirit that recognizes personal vulnerability to sin. Restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion work together to create a corrective spirit that heals rather than condemns and that binds the community in mutual care and accountability rather than division or pride.
