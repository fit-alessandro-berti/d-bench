**Wholehearted, humble trust that places God as the ultimate authority and guide, while still valuing and using the intellect under His lordship—this is humble self-distrust, not anti-intellectualism.**

### The Trust Commanded
Proverbs 3:5–6 calls for *complete, undivided trust in the Lord* (“with all thine heart”). This is relational reliance: confident dependence on God’s character, wisdom, promises, and sovereignty rather than treating Him as one advisor among equals.  

- “Lean not unto thine own understanding” means refusing to rest the weight of life’s decisions, ethical judgments, and ultimate security primarily or exclusively on unaided human insight, experience, or reasoning. Our understanding is real and valuable, yet finite, partial, fallen (affected by sin, bias, limited knowledge, and self-interest), and therefore unreliable as a final foundation.  
- “In all thy ways acknowledge him” requires consciously recognizing God’s presence, will, and ways in every sphere—seeking His guidance through Scripture, prayer, providence, and wisdom community rather than autonomous self-direction.  
- The promise—“and he shall direct thy paths”—is that God will make the way straight (provide moral and practical guidance), not that He will bypass the mind or cancel planning.

This is the trust of a creature who knows the Creator is infinitely wiser and good: total-hearted submission that still engages the heart *and* mind.

### Humble Self-Distrust vs. Mere Anti-Intellectualism
**Humble self-distrust** is the posture Proverbs actually teaches:  
- It recognizes creaturely limits and the noetic effects of sin. We *can and should* think carefully, study, plan, and reason (Proverbs itself is wisdom literature that repeatedly praises “understanding,” knowledge, counsel, and discernment—see 1:5; 2:2–6; 4:5–7; 18:15). Yet we refuse to treat those faculties as autonomous or ultimate.  
- It subordinates intellect to revelation and relationship: reason is a gift to be used vigorously *under* the fear of the Lord (Prov 1:7; 9:10), which is the beginning of wisdom. “Acknowledge him” in all ways includes using our minds to understand Scripture, apply principles, weigh evidence, and steward creation, always open to correction by God’s superior wisdom.  
- Historical Christian reading (e.g., faithful Protestant and Catholic exegesis) has consistently linked this to *fides quaerens intellectum*—faith seeking understanding—rather than faith abandoning understanding. Jesus and the apostles model rigorous argumentation, citation of evidence, and love of God with the mind (Matt 22:37; Acts 17; etc.).  

**Mere anti-intellectualism** is a distortion that the text does *not* support:  
- It would treat “lean not” as a command to distrust or discard careful thought, education, science, evidence, or human counsel altogether—embracing irrationalism, fideism without reason, or hostility to learning.  
- That reading collapses under the rest of Proverbs (and the canon): wisdom is to be sought diligently, fools despise knowledge, and God’s people are called to renew their minds (Rom 12:2). Relying on “gut” or superstition instead of thoughtful discernment is closer to the folly Proverbs condemns than to the trust it commands.  
- Anti-intellectualism often masks pride (claiming “I just trust God” while ignoring revealed means or hard thinking) or laziness, not humility. True humble self-distrust *uses* understanding more carefully precisely because it does not deify it.

In short: the trust asked for is the humble, whole-person confidence that says, “My best reasoning is a gift and a responsibility, but I will not rest my identity, ethics, or security on it alone. I submit my mind and ways to the Lord who alone sees perfectly and directs faithfully.” This produces neither mindless irresponsibility nor self-sufficient rationalism, but wise, God-dependent living. 

(The verse itself warns against both extremes by pairing “do not lean on your own understanding” with active acknowledgment that still engages the whole person.)
