**Isaiah 55:6–7** pairs “way” and “thoughts” on purpose. The text is making a claim about what repentance actually is, not just what it looks like.

### 1. Why both “way” and “thoughts”?

**“Way” = outward conduct**  
In Hebrew, *derek* literally means road or journey. It’s the patterns of behavior, habits, and choices you can observe. The “wicked” are told to forsake their *way* — the lived direction of their life.

**“Thoughts” = inward imagination**  
*Machashabah* refers to plans, schemes, intentions, and the inner dialogue. The “unrighteous man” must forsake his *thoughts* — the mental world where sin is first imagined, justified, and rehearsed.

The text refuses to split the two. You can change behavior without changing desire, and you can feel guilty without changing direction. Isaiah says true turning, *shub* “return”, requires both.

### 2. What that implies about conduct and imagination

| Aspect | If only this changes | What remains unchanged |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Outward conduct** | You get moral reform, behavior management | The heart can still be running old scripts. Think of the Pharisee in Luke 18: he did the right “ways” but his inner *thoughts* were self-righteous. |
| **Inward imagination** | You get guilt, internal conflict | The “way” keeps expressing the un-renounced thoughts. James 1:14–15 lays out the sequence: desire → enticement → sin → death. |

So the passage diagnoses sin as holistic. It’s not just bad deeds, and not just bad daydreams. It’s a person oriented away from God in both head and hands. Repentance, then, isn’t behavior modification or emotional regret. It’s reorientation at both levels.

### 3. What this says about the possibility of mercy

The order matters: “forsake… return… and he will have mercy.”  

1. **Mercy is not the reward for perfection**  
   God doesn’t say “become sinless in thought and deed, then come.” He says “forsake and return.” The turning itself is met with mercy, not the completed transformation. The verbs imply process, not prerequisite achievement.

2. **“Abundantly pardon” matches the depth of the problem**  
   Because sin is doubled — way *and* thoughts — the pardon is also emphasized: *yarbeh lisloach*, literally “he will multiply to forgive.” The breadth of the sin is met with surplus mercy. God’s forgiveness isn’t reluctant or rationed. It over-answers the problem.

3. **Urgency frames the offer: “while he may be found”**  
   The passage opened with time pressure. Mercy is abundant, but not automatic or indefinite. The pairing of way + thoughts shows how total the return must be, and the urgency shows why now matters. God is near and pardoning, but the text assumes there’s a window where response is possible.

**Bottom line in one sentence**  
Isaiah 55:6–7 treats repentance as the whole person coming home — feet and imagination both — because sin hijacks both, and mercy is offered to the whole person precisely at the point of turning, not at the point of arrival.
