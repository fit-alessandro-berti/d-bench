The core tension in Matthew 6:1 is often misunderstood. Many read it as a prohibition against *private* charity or *public* charity. However, a careful reading shows that Jesus is addressing the **internal orientation** behind the action, rather than the external visibility of that action.

To answer your specific dilemma: **The problem is not visibility itself, but the craving to be seen.**

Here is a breakdown of why public goodness becomes morally dangerous when motivated by a craving for recognition, even in the context of this verse.

### 1. The Nature of the Clause (The "To Be Seen")
The Greek text uses the phrase *pro* eleosomen* (before men) as the setting, followed immediately by *util* an inei* (to be seen by them). The phrase "to be seen" is not merely descriptive of the location; it is a **teleological clause**. It indicates purpose.

In grammar and logic, this structure implies: "Do not do this **in such a way that** you are seen."

If a person gives alms in a public square but intends for it to be a private expression of piety, the visibility is incidental, not the point. If they give in a public square, but the point of the action is to signal their own piety to the observers, the morality of the act is compromised. The danger lies in the inversion of the goal: **The act is performed for the观察者 (watcher), not for the suffering person or God.**

### 2. The Corruption of Identity
When virtue becomes a performance, it ceases to be an act of love and becomes an act of **self-creation**.

In this verse, Jesus identifies a specific form of idolatry. To "do your alms before men to be seen" is to say, "My goodness counts." It asks the world to validate your virtue.
*   **The Reward of Men:** This is temporary, social capital. It buys approval, respect, and status.
*   **The Reward of the Father:** This is eternal, divine validation.
*   **The Danger:** When a person seeks the first, they implicitly deny the second.

The moral danger is that the person stops giving *to the needy* (living out God's love) and starts *giving to their reputation* (proving they are holy). In this dynamic, the needy person becomes a prop in the giver's diaspora.

### 3. The Transaction Trap
Public charity without selfless intent turns morality into a transaction.
*   **Sincere Public Goodness:** Feeding the hungry despite knowing the crowd will praise you (e.g., when a farmer helps a stranger even though it embarrasses).
*   **Dangerous Public Goodness:** Feeding the hungry *in order that* the crowd can praise you.

If you give and then announce it, you are engaging in a "tit-for-tat" bargain. You give $100. You get 100 seconds of applause. You receive the reward of human approval, and Jesus argues that this cancels your eligibility for divine reward. The danger is that it renders the good work "paid for," rendering it no longer a gift of grace.

### 4. Does Jesus Forbid Public Giving?
To understand the moral danger, we must distinguish between *visibility* and *theater*.
*   **Jesus condemned hypocrisy**, not visibility. In Matthew 23:5-6, He describes the Pharisees who ff identify themselves *as individuals* by their titles to be seen.
*   **Publicity can be virtuous:** Later in the Acts of the Apostles (Acts 20:35), Paul warns against working against the "fruity" work of God because of "glory and honor." Furthermore, public acts of kindness often act as a testimony to the Gospel (Acts 14).

Therefore, the visibility itself is not the problem. If a person gives in public to show they are a god, they are committed to the secret of the Father.

### Summary
The verse warns against **theatrical religiosity**. The moral danger is not that people can see the giver; the danger is that the giver can calculate the applause.

*   **The Visibility:** The medium.
*   **The Craving:** The poison.

The verse does not say, "Do not give in the open." It says, "Do not do what makes the people your audience rather than God your witness." The act must be directed at the Father, and the consequences of the event are left to God's judgment, not the crowd's applause.
