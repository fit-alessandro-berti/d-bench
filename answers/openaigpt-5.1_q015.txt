The image is “sharp” in two ways: visually and morally.

### 1. Why the image is so sharp

- **Absurd contrast:**  
  A *mote* is a tiny speck (like sawdust); a *beam* is a load‑bearing timber. The picture of someone with a huge plank stuck in their eye offering delicate “eye surgery” to remove a speck from someone else is deliberately ridiculous. It’s meant to make you laugh—and then wince.

- **Vivid, concrete, and personal:**  
  The eye is one of the most sensitive parts of the body. Anyone picturing this feels the absurdity and danger of a “blind” person trying to work on someone else’s eye. The image sticks in the imagination and gets past defenses.

- **Targets self‑deception, not just error:**  
  The point isn’t just, “You’re a sinner too.” It’s: “You are *worse* off than the one you’re criticizing and somehow don’t see it.” The “beam” is willful blindness to one’s own major fault while focusing on another’s minor one. That’s why Jesus uses the word “hypocrite” (actor, pretender).

### 2. How it criticizes hypocrisy without banning moral correction

The key is the word *first* in verse 5:

> “First cast out the beam out of thine own eye; **and then** shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote out of thy brother’s eye.”

So:

- **Correction is not forbidden; it’s conditioned.**  
  Jesus doesn’t say, “Forget about your brother’s mote.” He says:  
  1. Start with rigorous self‑examination and repentance.  
  2. Only *then* are you in a position to help someone else.

- **The problem is not moral discernment, but moral inconsistency.**  
  Hypocritical judging:  
  - Magnifies small faults in others.  
  - Minimizes or ignores larger, similar faults in oneself.  
  - Uses others’ sins as a distraction from one’s own.

  Faithful correction, by contrast, comes from:
  - Someone who has faced their own sin first (“beam” removed or being removed).  
  - Humility and love, not superiority.  
  - A desire to heal, not to condemn.

- **“Judge not” (v.1) isn’t moral relativism.**  
  In the very same sermon Jesus tells his followers to:
  - Discern false prophets (7:15–20).  
  - Make hard calls about what is holy and what is not (7:6).  

  So “judge not” means: don’t take God’s place as condemning judge, and don’t judge others by a standard you won’t apply to yourself (vv. 1–2). But moral clarity and loving correction remain necessary.

In summary: the image is sharp because it’s comically exaggerated and painfully concrete, forcing us to see our own blindness. And the passage doesn’t outlaw moral correction; it demands that correction begin with honest self‑judgment, so that we help others from a place of humility, not hypocrisy.
