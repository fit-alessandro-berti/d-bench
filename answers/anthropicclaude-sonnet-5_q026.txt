# Speech as the Overflow of the Heart's "Treasure"

This passage constructs a striking economic metaphor: the heart is a storehouse (θησαυρός/*thesauros*, "treasury"), accumulating either good or evil "treasure" over time through repeated choices, meditations, and experiences. Speech becomes the outward transaction that reveals what's been deposited within.

## Unpacking "Abundance" (περίσσευμα)

The Greek word suggests overflow—when a container is filled beyond capacity, it spills over involuntarily. This is key: Jesus isn't describing a mechanical, one-to-one relationship between specific thoughts and specific words, but rather a *saturation point*. The heart accumulates moral character over time, and speech is what happens when that accumulated character exceeds containment.

## Symptom, Choice, or Revelation? — The Case for "All Three"

**As Symptom:**
Like a fever indicates infection, corrupt speech indicates a diseased moral condition. The Pharisees' accusations against Jesus (the immediate context, v. 24) aren't isolated errors—they're symptomatic eruptions of settled hostility. You don't need to diagnose every word; the pattern reveals the underlying condition.

**As Choice:**
Yet the passage retains moral responsibility. Jesus doesn't say "you speak evil because you cannot help it"—he asks "*how can ye*, being evil, speak good things?" This is almost rhetorical exasperation, implying speech remains an act for which one is accountable (compare v. 36-37: "every idle word... men shall give account"). The storehouse doesn't fill itself passively; it's stocked through cumulative choices—what one dwells on, desires, and does.

**As Revelation:**
Perhaps most fundamentally, speech functions *epistemically*—it's how hidden interior states become knowable, both to others and paradoxically to the speaker. Words don't just express the heart; they *disclose* it, sometimes involuntarily exposing what one might prefer to conceal. This is why Jesus can use speech as diagnostic evidence against his opponents—their words betray their hearts even when their intentions were to trap him.

## The Synthesis

These three dimensions aren't competing but *sequential and cyclical*:

1. **Choices** (dwelling on good/evil) → accumulate as **treasure** in the heart
2. This treasure, once saturated, produces speech as **symptom** (involuntary overflow)
3. That speech serves as **revelation** (evidence) of the heart's true state—to others and in divine judgment

The passage thus refuses a simple mechanistic view. Speech isn't merely caused by the heart (mere symptom) nor merely chosen in isolation (mere decision)—it's the *visible verdict* on an invisible moral history, accountable precisely because it was cultivated through freely repeated choices, yet functioning diagnostically because that cultivation eventually exceeds conscious control.

This is likely why Jesus escalates immediately to *final judgment* (v. 36-37)—if speech is symptom, choice, and revelation simultaneously, it becomes the most honest testimony available about who a person actually is, regardless of who they claim to be.
