The passage from Luke 15:28–32 depicts the elder brother's reaction to the return of his prodigal younger brother. To identify his deepest problem, we can analyze his words and emotions:

1. **Anger**: The elder brother is described as being “angry,” which is a prominent emotion in his response. He refuses to enter the house (v. 28), indicating a strong sense of displeasure.

2. **Envy**: He complains that the father never gave him a fatted calf for his years of faithful service, suggesting a sense of resentment or envy toward his brother who is now lavished with celebration (v. 29).

3. **Wounded Fairness**: He emphasizes his own obedience, stating, “Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment” (v. 29). This highlights a feeling that his own righteousness and the father's fairness are being disregarded. He feels wronged by the father’s generosity.

4. **Pride in Obedience**: His focus on his diligent service and the lack of recognition for it reflects pride in his own obedience compared to his brother’s reckless behavior. He values the external acknowledgment of his dutifulness.

5. **Discomfort with Mercy**: The father’s response, “Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine. It was meet that we should make merry, and be glad: for this thy brother was dead, and is alive again; and was lost, and is found” (v. 31‑32), directly addresses the elder brother’s discomfort with the father’s merciful attitude. The father’s insistence that celebration is appropriate because the lost brother has returned reveals that the elder brother is uneasy with such a display of mercy toward someone he perceives as unworthy.

**Conclusion**: While the elder brother experiences anger, envy, and a sense of wounded fairness, his **deepest problem** is his **discomfort with mercy itself**. His primary issue is not merely his anger or jealousy but his inability to accept the father’s compassionate and forgiving response to his wasteful and wayward brother. This discomfort with the father’s mercy challenges his own rigid sense of justice and righteousness, making it the core of his underlying struggle.
