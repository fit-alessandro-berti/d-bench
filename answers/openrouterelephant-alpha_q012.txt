These two verses present **both** a diagnosis and a divine solution. The tension is not "pessimism vs. optimism," but rather a realism about *epistemic limitation* combined with a claim about *divine omniscience*.

Here is how to understand the passage and how to speak about motives in light of it.

### 1. It is realistic, not merely pessimistic
Calling the passage "mainly pessimistic" implies a defeatist attitude. A more accurate reading is **diagnostic realism**.

*   **The Problem (The Deceit):** The Hebrew word translated "deceitful" (*’akal*) implies something that is *cannibalistic*—it devours its owner. The heart is described as "desperately wicked" (*’alâlā*, meaning insolent, corrupt, or desperately sick). The text acknowledges that humans are experts at self-rationalization. We are not merely mistaken; we are often the architects of our own self-deception.
*   **The Limitation (Who can know it?):** The rhetorical question "Who can know it?" highlights the human *epistemological boundary*. We lack the objective, third-person perspective to accurately audit our own motives. We are too close to the machine to see the rust.

Therefore, the passage is not saying "Humans are doomed to be evil," but rather "Humans are incapable of perfectly self-observing."

### 2. The Ground of Confidence (The Search)
If the passage were only pessimistic, it would end with "who can know it?" (a question of despair). Instead, it pivots to a statement of divine competence:

> *"I the Lord search the heart, I try the reins…"*

The text shifts the burden of verification from the **subjective** (the unreliable self) to the **objective** (the sovereign Observer).

*   **The Reins (Kidneys):** In ancient anatomy, the kidneys (*qidrayim*) were thought to be the seat of desire, conscience, and deep emotion. God is not just checking the surface; He is examining the deep appetites and latent motives.
*   **The Standard:** God does not judge by outward appearances or cultural norms ("looks good to me"). He judges by the "fruit of his doings"—the tangible evidence of a life (Matthew 7:16).

### 3. How to Talk About Motives (The Practical Implication)
If the heart is an unreliable witness, how should we discuss—ourselves or others—motives, intentions, and character?

#### A. Adopt a "Verificationist" Approach (Fruit over Feeling)
Since internal feelings are malleable and prone to bias, we must anchor our understanding of motive in external, observable reality.

*   **Talk about actions, not just affirmations.** Instead of saying, "I *feel* I am a good person," one should say, "Let us look at the pattern of my choices."
*   **Use the language of consistency.** Jesus warned against the gap between lips and life (Matthew 15:8). A "true" motive is verified by consistency over time and across circumstances. If someone claims a motive of love but consistently acts in domination, the fruit contradicts the claim.

#### B. Practice Confession as Diagnostic, Not Definitional
Because the heart is deceitful, the practice of honest confession is vital.

*   **Name the rationalizations.** When talking about motives, we should be willing to say, "I thought I was doing this for X, but upon reflection, it was really Y (pride, fear, or selfishness)." The goal is not to condemn the self, but to map the deceit.
*   **Seek external counsel.** Because we are poor witnesses for ourselves, we need trusted community to help interpret our "fruit" and expose our blind spots (Proverbs 27:17).

#### C. Embrace "Motivic Humility"
If God searches the heart, we must be cautious about assigning final motives to others.

*   **Avoid definitive interior diagnoses.** We cannot see the *reins*; therefore, we cannot be certain of another person's ultimate intent. We can see the fruit, but the heart remains the Lord's domain (1 Samuel 16:7).
*   **Focus on stewardship, not speculation.** Rather than getting stuck in "Why did they do that?" (which invites projection), focus on "What is required of me now?"

### Summary
The passage is **realistic** rather than pessimistic. It soberly diagnoses the human condition as self-deceptive, but it counter-intuitively offers relief: the burden of verification does not rest on our flawed self-surveillance, but on God’s thorough examination.

**How to talk about motives:** Shift the conversation from *internal assertions of goodness* to *external evidence of fruit*. Be honest about the capacity for self-deception, but anchor your identity and your judgments not in your feelings about yourself, but in the observable trajectory of your life under a standard that searches deeper than the self.
