In the passage of Psalm 51:10-12, David, the author, is seeking much more than just a pardon from his sins. He is essentially asking for the transformation of his inner self. This is evident in his plea for a clean heart, which is indicative of his desire for a heart that is transformed and renewed, not merely forgiven. A pardon or forgiveness may relieve guilt and end disciplinary measures, but it does not innately bring about inner change – a transformation of character, thought patterns and behavistatic patterns.

Guideline 

In the context of biblical teachings, pardon and forgiveness refer to God’s grace towards the sinner and are usually associated with the absolution of sins, usually involving release from guilt and punishment. They are vital components of salvation but may not address the root of a problem, which, according to biblical principles, are often tied to one's character, attitude, and approach towards life. They do not necessarily ensure that an individual will not fall into sin again. In other words, forgiveness does not always guarantee personal growth or transformation – the result of which would be a clean heart, freeing one from the corruption of sin.

Contrastingly, inner change relates to a long-sought spiritual maturity, a change in ways of thinking and behaving. Asking for a clean heart implies a desire to become a morally upright and righteous person – embodying God's values – as mentioned in 1 John 1:9 and Romans 2:20-23. This involves desiring a right spirit or a new disposition towards life in line with God's wisdom, which will inevitably stem from a restored joy of salvation. Therefore, by asking for a clean heart, David is essentially calling for divine intervention that would help him live out his life according to God’s holy laws. His prayer reflects his yearning to experience godly joy, a state of being that will result from a right spirit, characterized by godly love, humility, obedience, and peace (Psalm 51:12).

An individual with a clean heart will not continuously fall into the cycle of sin and guilt because of inner change. This inner transformation will naturally extend from a joy that comes from a regained relationship with God as the clean heart is associated with renewed life.

Guideline

Mention of the "free spirit" as a result of upholding the joy of salvation can be understood as a testament to the empowerment of God’s spirit within. Freedom from sin and guilt implies God's grace and mercy, providing assurance against worldly temptations, which ultimately allows one to fully enjoy the blessings of God’ enduring spirit. Freedom thus implies more than just mere escape from worldly sins, it suggests personal growth, the ability to resist future temptations and the embodiment of God’s character.

From your question about wanting escape from guilt and seeking inner change, two important concepts to keep in mind are repentance and sanctification. Repentance is essential to escape guilt but as it involves sorrow for sins committed and a resolve to change, it should eventually result in a transformed heart. This transformation, in turn, leads to a sanctified life, free from sin and led by God's free spirit within one's heart.

Conclusion

The difference highlighted here is that asking for a clean heart goes beyond the desire to escape guilt, which can be temporary, while seeking inner change invites divine intervention for a permanent, joyful, and spiritually empowered life. The request for a clean heart represents such a desire for substantial positive change, emphasizing character renewal, a right spirit, and Godly joy as a testament to a life of holiness and obedience to God's holy laws.

Psalm 51:11 further reinforces this idea as David appeals for a constant remembrance of God's judgments and the sin nature that was in his heart, urging for God's transformation of his spirit. His prayers thus serve not only as a means of repentance but also as a longing for an overarching transformation.
