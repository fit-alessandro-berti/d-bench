## The Architecture of the Metaphor

Jesus uses two interlocking images here: **abundance** (the Greek *perisseuma* suggests overflow, surplus) and **treasure** (*thēsauros*, a storehouse or stockpile). Together they create a hydraulic picture of moral life: the heart is a reservoir that fills over time, and speech is what spills out when the reservoir exceeds capacity. Words, on this view, are not generated at the moment of speaking—they are *drawn from inventory*.

The verb in verse 35 reinforces this: the person "bringeth forth" (*ekballei*, literally "casts out") good or evil things, the way a merchant pulls goods from a warehouse. You can only bring out what you have previously stored up.

## Symptoms, Choices, or Revelations?

The passage seems to hold all three together, and the tension between them is part of its force.

**Words as symptoms.** The rhetorical question—"how *can* ye, being evil, speak good things?"—implies a kind of moral impossibility. Speech follows character the way fruit follows the tree (the surrounding context in Matthew 12:33 makes this explicit). A symptom is involuntary: the fever doesn't choose to appear. Jesus suggests that over time, your speech *will* betray your inner condition whether you intend it to or not. Hypocrisy can manage individual sentences, but the "abundance" eventually overflows.

**Words as revelations.** This is the diagnostic edge of the passage, aimed at the Pharisees ("generation of vipers"). They had just attributed Jesus' healing to demonic power, and He treats that utterance as evidence—an audible biopsy of the heart. Words function here as testimony admissible in judgment, which is exactly where the passage goes next: "by thy words thou shalt be justified, and by thy words thou shalt be condemned" (v. 37). Speech reveals what no one could otherwise see.

**Words as choices.** Yet the treasure metaphor preserves moral responsibility, because *stockpiles are accumulated*. No one fills a storehouse by accident. The "treasure of the heart" is built up through countless prior choices—what one dwells on, loves, rehearses, consents to. So even if a given utterance erupts spontaneously, it is the product of a long history of choosing. The choice happens upstream of the speech.

## The Synthesis

The passage's deepest claim is that this is a false trichotomy: words are symptoms *because* they are revelations of accumulated choices. The moment of speech may feel involuntary—especially unguarded, angry, or careless speech—but what it discloses is precisely the self we have been constructing all along.

This is why the passage resists the common excuse "I didn't mean what I said." Jesus' anthropology suggests nearly the opposite: it is our *unmeant* words, the overflow, that most accurately mean us. Polished, deliberate speech can be managed; abundance cannot.

The practical implication is striking: if you want to change your speech, the passage says, working on your mouth is futile. You must change what you are stockpiling. The tongue is downstream of the treasury.
