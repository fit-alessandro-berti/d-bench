Short answer: Luke 6:37 is not a slogan that “moral judgments don’t matter.” It’s a warning against two things: (1) judging in a way that condemns people without self-examination, and (2) a judgmental posture that lacks mercy. It does not cancel discernment or calls to righteousness; it redirects judgment toward humility, mercy, and restoration.

What Luke 6:37 actually says
- “Judge not, and you shall not be judged; condemn not, and you shall not be condemned; forgive, and you shall be forgiven.”
- This emphasizes the heart and motive behind judgment: avoid hypocritical or vindictive condemnation; practice forgiveness and mercy.
- The verse is paired with “Be merciful, even as your Father is merciful,” suggesting a relational, grace-filled posture rather than a punitive one.

Discernment vs condemnation
- Discernment (moral discernment, righteous judgment)
  - What it is: evaluating actions, beliefs, and outcomes against a genuine standard (truth, justice, love, human flourishing).
  - What it aims for: truth, protection, accountability, correction when needed, and ultimately restoration.
  - Biblical support: Jesus’ call to judge with right judgment (John 7:24); Paul’s exhortations to test everything (1 Thessalonians 5:21); Hebrews 5:14 about discerning good from evil; 1 Corinthians 5:12–13 (addressing sin in the community) shows accountability within a framework of love.
  - How it looks in practice: identifying harmful behavior, calling it out, setting boundaries, offering correction, and seeking reconciliation where possible.

- Condemnation (a punitive, final verdict)
  - What it is: pronouncing someone utterly guilty and deserving punishment, often with self-righteous pride or without proper evidence.
  - What it leads to: alienation, bitterness, cycles of retaliation, and a failure to allow repentance or change.
  - It’s the kind of judgment Jesus warns against when he condemns hypocrisy and a harsh, unmerciful spirit.

- The danger of a permissive stance
  - Saying “moral judgments never matter” can slide into tolerating harm or enabling wrongdoing, especially if it becomes careless about truth or accountability.
  - Luke 6:37 does not free us from moral discernment; it challenges the motive and the form of that discernment.

How to practice discernment without condemning
- Start with self-examination: “Why do I judge this person? Do I project my own faults onto them?” (Matthew 7:3–5).
- Use a fair standard: judge actions against a consistent, good standard, not who you like or dislike.
- Aim for restoration: when possible, address the issue in a way that seeks reconciliation and growth (Matthew 18:15–17; Galatians 6:1).
- Separate action from identity: distinguish a person’s conduct from their inherent worth as a person.
- Balance truth with mercy: speak truth plainly but with gentleness and humility (Ephesians 4:15; Colossians 4:6).
- Avoid contempt or exclusion: avoid a posture that writes people off; pursue accountability in a way that leaves room for repentance and change.
- Remember the reciprocity in Luke 6:38: “the measure you use, it will be measured to you” and “forgive, and you will be forgiven.” Mercy tends to invite mercy; harsh condemnation tends to invite more hardness.

Bottom line
- Luke 6:37 invites humility, mercy, and forgiveness in place of harsh, hypocritical condemnation.
- It affirms the need for discernment and accountability, but warns against a judgmental, punitive spirit.
- The goal is to discern rightly, correct when necessary, and foster reconciliation—never to pretend that moral discernment doesn’t matter, or that every judgment should be made with a rigid, unforgiving heart.
