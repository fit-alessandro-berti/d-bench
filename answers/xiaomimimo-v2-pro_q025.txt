# The Motive Behind the Visibility

This is a beautifully precise question, and I think the verse itself points clearly toward the answer.

## The Craving, Not the Seeing

The critical phrase is **"to be seen of them"** — which in the Greek carries a sense of purpose (*pros to* — "with the aim of"). Jesus isn't describing accidental visibility. He's describing a specific *intent*: doing good **for the sake of** being observed.

Consider what Jesus does *not* say. He doesn't say "hide your light" — in fact, just one chapter earlier he says a city set on a hill **cannot be hidden** (Matt. 5:14-16). Goodness that naturally catches the eye is fine. Goodness staged for applause is not.

## Why This Distinction Matters

The problem isn't optics — it's idolatry. When the craving for human approval becomes the engine driving your virtue:

- **Your audience is the crowd**, not God
- **Your reward is their praise**, which Jesus says is received *in full* ("they have their reward" — v.2)
- **Your goodness becomes hollow** — theater rather than transformation

## A Helpful Analogy

A musician playing beautifully in a park isn't doing anything wrong. But if she plays *only* so passersby will admire her, the music has become a costume. The notes may sound the same, but something essential has curdled.

So visibility is the *stage*. The craving for the spotlight is what Jesus calls morally dangerous.
