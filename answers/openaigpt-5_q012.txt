Short answer: Jeremiah is being realistic about how easily we fool ourselves, not fatalistic about humanity. The point is that God alone sees through our rationalizations, so trust in him rather than in your own inner certainties.

Key observations
- Context: Jeremiah 17 contrasts the person who “trusts in man” with the one who “trusts in the Lord.” The “deceitful” heart sits in a passage about misplaced trust and false security.
- Language: “Heart” (lev) is the inner person—thoughts, will, affections. “Reins” (kidneys) were the hidden seat of emotions. “Deceitful” (aqov) and “desperately sick/incurable” (’anash) can mean morally crooked and diagnostically unreliable. The stress is on self-deception.
- God’s role: “I the Lord search… test… give according to… fruit.” Divine scrutiny and visible fruit, not our self-report, provide the verdict.

So the text isn’t saying, “You are irredeemably bad so introspection is pointless,” but, “Your inner life is opaque and biased; let God test it, and look to the fruit.”

How to speak about motives when the heart is an unreliable witness

For your own motives
- Be humble and provisional: “As far as I can tell,” “I want to do this for X, but I see Y in me too.”
- Invite God’s search: Pray Psalm 139:23–24; let Scripture and the Spirit expose motives (Heb 4:12–13).
- Check the fruit: What outcomes and patterns follow (Gal 5:19–23; Matt 7:16)? Are love, joy, peace growing?
- Use community: Submit plans to trusted people who can ask, “Why this? Why now? What if no one knew you did it?”
- Expect mixed motives: Act in obedience anyway; confess the mixture and ask God to purify it (Ps 51; Phil 1:9–11).

Helpful self-diagnostics
- Would I still do this if it cost me or stayed unseen?
- Do I become defensive when questioned?
- Am I using “principle” to mask fear, pride, or comfort-seeking?
- What am I most afraid to lose here—God’s pleasure or reputation/control?

When speaking about others’ motives
- Be slow to ascribe motives. Scripture warns against presuming to know hearts (1 Cor 4:5).
- Address actions and effects you can see (“fruit”), not hidden intentions. Ask clarifying questions rather than asserting inner reasons.
- Use charitable interpretations while holding people accountable to observable behavior.

Don’t be paralyzed
- God “gives according to ways… fruit.” You are responsible for choices and their outcomes, not for achieving perfect self-transparency first.
- The passage drives you to trust God, not to despair: immediately after, Jeremiah prays, “Heal me… save me” (17:14). In the broader biblical arc, God promises a renewed heart (Jer 31:33; Ezek 36:26) and is greater than our condemning hearts (1 John 3:20).

In sum: Jeremiah 17:9–10 is sober about self-deception and hopeful about God’s ability to search, judge, and transform. Speak of motives with humility, invite testing by God’s Word and community, attend to fruit, and keep moving in faithful obedience.
