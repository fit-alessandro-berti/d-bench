In Eph 4:31–32 Paul is doing a tight **“put off / put on”** contrast (a pattern running through 4:22–24, 4:25–29): remove the old-community ways that fracture the body of Christ, and replace them with the new-community ways that express the gospel.

### 1) “Bitterness” is treated as the *root posture* that blocks forgiveness
The Greek *pikria* (“bitterness”) is more than a momentary feeling of hurt. It’s the **settled sharpness of resentment**—an inner stance of “I will hold this against you.” That’s why it stands at the head of the chain:

- **bitterness** (the internal, nursed resentment)
- then its eruptions: **wrath/anger**
- its social forms: **clamor** (public quarrelling), **evil speaking** (slander)
- with the underlying intent: **malice** (the desire to harm)

So bitterness functions like a **source term**: it’s the inward condition that makes the outward sins likely, and it is precisely what makes forgiveness psychologically and morally “unthinkable.” If I am committed to keeping the offense “alive,” I cannot simultaneously release the offender.

### 2) Forgiveness is the direct opposite because it is “grace” applied to an offender
“Forgiving” here is *charizomenoi*—from *charis* (“grace”). It’s not merely “stopping being mad,” but **granting favor where repayment could be demanded**. Paul grounds it explicitly: “as God in Christ forgave you.” That makes forgiveness not optional conflict-management but **gospel-imitation**.

So the direct opposition is logical:

- **Bitterness** = keeping a debt on the books; preserving the offense as a claim.
- **Forgiveness** = canceling the claim; treating the other with grace because God has treated you with grace.

### 3) Is bitterness a feeling, a habit, a chosen posture, or contagious?
In Paul’s framing it’s **all of the above, but chiefly a cultivated posture**:

- It may begin as a **feeling** (hurt, disappointment).
- It becomes **bitterness** when it is *kept*, rehearsed, justified—i.e., a **chosen and sustained stance**.
- It then turns into a **habit**: a settled way of interpreting people and injuries.
- And it is **contagious in a community**: the list shows it doesn’t stay internal; it spills into speech (“clamor,” “slander”) and motivates actions (“malice”), infecting relationships and unity (cf. 4:3, 4:29–30).

That’s why Paul places bitterness and forgiveness so directly against each other: bitterness is the inner commitment to retain and spread relational debt; forgiveness is the Spirit-shaped, Christ-modeled practice that cancels the debt and protects the unity of the church.
