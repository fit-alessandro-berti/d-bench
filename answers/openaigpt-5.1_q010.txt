The passage is praising a very particular kind of power:  

1. **Power as self-giving, not grasping**  
   - “Being in the form of God” and “equal with God” means Christ possesses true, highest authority and status.  
   - Yet he “made himself of no reputation” and took “the form of a servant.”  
   - So the power on display is not **coercive power** (forcing others), but **kenotic power**—the power to *freely* lay aside privilege, to descend, to absorb wrong rather than inflict it.  
   - This is power expressed as *freedom to love at cost to oneself*. It is greatness that chooses the lowest place.

2. **Power that is shown in obedience and vulnerability**  
   - “He humbled himself, and became obedient unto death, even the death of the cross.”  
   - The cross is the opposite of worldly glory; it’s shame, weakness, exposure.  
   - Yet the text insists that in precisely this humiliation, Christ is exercising divine authority and purpose.  
   - The power praised is thus **moral and spiritual authority**: the right and ability to bear sin, to forgive, to reconcile, to triumph through apparent defeat.

---

### Why humility joined to greatness is more unsettling than open domination

1. **You can’t dismiss it as weakness.**  
   - When the powerless act humbly, we can (wrongly) chalk it up to necessity: “They have no choice.”  
   - When someone *who clearly has power* voluntarily takes the lowest place, that exposes our instincts. We see:  
     - This humility is not forced.  
     - They could have asserted themselves and didn’t.  
   - That kind of humility is a *choice*, and so it becomes a **judgment** on our pride and our grasping.

2. **It removes our usual defenses.**  
   - Open domination is easy to recognize and resist:  
     - We can fight it.  
     - We can resent it.  
     - We can feel morally superior to it.  
   - But humility from someone greater disarms us. There is nothing obvious to fight. Instead of being oppressed, we feel **obligated**, exposed, called:  
     - “If they act like this, what excuse do I have for my pride?”  
   - It leaves us unshielded before a standard of goodness that is not abstract but *embodied*.

3. **It redefines what greatness is.**  
   - If the greatest One serves, then greatness itself is service.  
   - That’s unsettling because it overturns our internal value system:  
     - We tend to associate greatness with being above others;  
     - This text associates greatness with going beneath others.  
   - If God’s own life is self-emptying love, then our hunger for status, control, and recognition is revealed as deeply out of tune with reality.

4. **It exerts a deeper kind of authority.**  
   - Domination controls your body, your circumstances.  
   - Humble greatness reaches your conscience. It quietly says, “Follow me.”  
   - Christ on the cross does not coerce, yet his self-giving love claims us more profoundly than raw power ever could. That interior claim can feel more threatening than external force, because it addresses our freedom:  
     - “Will you become like this? Will you let your life be shaped by this same mind?”

In sum, Philippians 2 praises **cruciform power**: the power of one who has all rights, and yet chooses the lowest place in love. It feels more unsettling than domination because it is both undeniably great and undeniably humble—and that combination exposes our pride, redraws our idea of glory, and quietly insists that *this* is what we are meant to become.
