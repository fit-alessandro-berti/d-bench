The passage from Philippians 2:5-8 is a profound reflection on the nature of power, humility, and greatness as exemplified by Jesus Christ. The power being praised here is not the kind of power that is characterized by domination, control, or force. Instead, it is a power rooted in humility, selflessness, and service. This type of power is often referred to as "servant leadership" or "kingdom power."

### Why is this kind of power praised?

1. **Humility**: The passage begins by stating that Jesus, who was in the form of God (possessing divine attributes), did not consider it robbery (or theft) to be equal with God. Instead, He chose to humble Himself. This humility is a powerful attribute because it demonstrates a willingness to put others before oneself, a principle that is foundational in many ethical and spiritual teachings.

2. **Servitude**: Jesus chose to take on the form of a servant. This act of servitude is a powerful form of power because it shows a commitment to the well-being and dignity of others. Servitude is not about domination or control; it is about empowering others and serving their needs. This kind of power is often more unsettling than open domination because it requires individuals to relinquish control and embrace vulnerability.

3. **Obedience**: The passage highlights Jesus' obedience, even to the point of death on a cross. This obedience is a demonstration of ultimate commitment and sacrifice. It shows that true power is not about asserting one's will at all costs, but about being willing to endure hardship and sacrifice for a greater purpose.

### Why can humility joined to greatness feel more unsettling than open domination?

1. **Challenge to Authority**: Open domination is often associated with authority figures who assert their power through control, fear, or intimidation. This type of power can be unsettling because it often creates an environment of fear and submission rather than mutual respect and cooperation. In contrast, humility joined to greatness challenges traditional notions of authority by suggesting that true power lies in service and selflessness.

2. **Vulnerability**: Humility involves vulnerability. When someone is humble, they are open to being seen, criticized, and challenged. This vulnerability can be unsettling because it requires individuals to be open to feedback and to admit their limitations. Open domination, on the other hand, is often characterized by a facade of strength and invulnerability.

3. **Mutual Growth**: Servant leadership fosters an environment of mutual growth and development. When leaders prioritize the needs of their followers, it creates a culture of collaboration and mutual respect. Open domination, however, tends to create a hierarchical structure where the leader's needs and desires are prioritized over those of others. This can lead to resentment and disengagement among followers.

4. **Ethical and Spiritual Values**: The power exemplified in Philippians 2:5-8 is deeply rooted in ethical and spiritual values. It reflects a commitment to justice, compassion, and love. This kind of power can be unsettling because it challenges individuals to align their actions with higher moral and spiritual principles, rather than pursuing power for personal gain or status.

5. **Cultural and Societal Norms**: In many cultures and societies, domination and control are often seen as signs of strength and success. Humility and servitude, on the other hand, may be viewed as signs of weakness or incompetence. This cultural resistance to humility can make the idea of servant leadership feel unsettling, as it goes against deeply ingrained norms and expectations.

In summary, the power being praised in Philippians 2:5-8 is a form of leadership that is characterized by humility, servitude, and obedience. This kind of power is unsettling compared to open domination because it challenges traditional notions of authority, requires vulnerability, fosters mutual growth, aligns with ethical and spiritual values, and often conflicts with cultural and societal norms that prioritize domination and control. Understanding and embracing this form of power can lead to more equitable, compassionate, and sustainable relationships and communities.
