This is an important question, because a superficial reading of Luke 6:37 can indeed make it sound as if Jesus is forbidding all moral evaluation. The key is to see that Jesus is targeting a specific posture of the heart, not invalidating the concept of moral judgment itself.

Here’s how the verse differs from saying moral judgments never matter, along with an exploration of the difference between discernment and the appetite to condemn.

---

### 1. How Luke 6:37 Differs from “Moral Judgments Never Matter”

If the verse meant “moral judgments never matter,” it would contradict the very sermon it comes from (the Sermon on the Plain/Mount), where Jesus constantly makes and demands moral judgments:
- He distinguishes between good and bad fruit (Luke 6:43–45).
- He warns against false prophets (Matt. 7:15–20).
- He calls certain behaviors “hypocrisy” (Luke 6:42).

So Jesus is not banning the act of judging between right and wrong. Rather, He forbids a *condemning, unforgiving posture* that sets oneself up as the final judge of another person’s soul, motive, or eternal standing. The difference hinges on the Greek verb used: *krínō* (to judge) in this context carries the sense of “to pronounce final condemnation” or “to enact a verdict that writes someone off.” Jesus commands us not to assume the role of Judge that belongs to God alone (see James 4:11–12).

What the verse is *not* saying:
- **Not** that we cannot identify sin or call something wrong.
- **Not** that we must affirm every action as morally neutral.
- **Not** that we lose the capacity to discern truth from falsehood.

What it *is* saying:
- We must not hand down ultimate condemnation on a person.
- We must not carry a hostile, unforgiving, or dismissive spirit.
- We must measure others with the same grace we hope to receive from God.

Thus, the statement is not a manifesto for moral relativism; it is a boundary marker for how finite, sinful humans treat one another under God’s ultimate justice.

---

### 2. Discernment vs. the Appetite to Condemn

The confusion arises because both discernment and condemnation involve recognizing something is wrong. But they differ entirely in their source, manner, and purpose.

| Element | Discernment | Appetite to Condemn |
|--------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Source** | Flows from love, humility, and a desire for truth. | Flows from pride, self-righteousness, or wounded ego. |
| **Goal** | Restoration, protection, and healing. | To diminish, punish, or elevate oneself above another. |
| **Posture** | “I see a problem; how can I help, warn, or restore?” | “I see a problem; this person is unacceptable, worthless, or beyond hope.” |
| **Relation to self** | Recognizes “I too am capable of that sin; I first examine myself.” (Luke 6:41–42) | Ignores the log in one’s own eye; focuses relentlessly on the speck in the other’s. |
| **Tone & timing** | Speaks truth gently, appropriately, and often after listening. | Delivers verdicts harshly, hastily, and permanently. |
| **Treatment of the person** | Separates the person from their action; honors their image-bearing dignity. | Equates the person with their worst action; dehumanizes or despises. |
| **Fruit** | Redemptive boundaries, warnings, or correction that opens the door to change. | Division, shame, bitterness, and a closed door to relationship. |

#### Discernment in practice:
- **Jesus with the woman caught in adultery (John 8:1–11).** He discerns her sin clearly (“Go and sin no more”), but He refuses to condemn her. He calls out the hypocrisy of the accusers and opens a path to new life.
- **Paul confronting Peter (Galatians 2:11–14).** Paul publicly discerns that Peter’s behavior is hypocritical and damaging to the gospel. He doesn’t write Peter off; he corrects him for the sake of truth and unity.
- **Church discipline (Matthew 18:15–17; 1 Corinthians 5).** The church is called to judge serious, unrepentant sin within its community, but always with the goal of restoration (“gain your brother”), not vindictive punishment.

#### The appetite to condemn in practice:
- It rejoices in the exposure of someone’s failure rather than grieving it.
- It uses moral truth as a weapon, not as a medicine.
- It refuses the possibility of repentance or forgiveness, treating the other as irredeemable.
- It often masquerades as “righteous anger” but is actually a hunger for superiority or revenge.

---

### 3. Why This Matters

If we mistake Jesus’ command and say “moral judgments never matter,” we lose the ability to:
- Name injustice and advocate for victims.
- Grow in holiness and help others do the same.
- Distinguish between a safe teacher and a spiritually dangerous one.

But if we ignore Jesus’ warning and develop an appetite to condemn, we become:
- Phantoms of the Pharisee, whom Jesus rebuked most sharply.
- People who demand mercy for ourselves but extend only scrutiny to others.
- Abusers of truth, using it to fracture rather than to reconcile.

### In Summary
Luke 6:37 does not erase moral judgment; it fundamentally reorients it. The question is not whether we will make judgments—we inevitably do—but whether those judgments will be acts of **humble, restorative discernment** or expressions of a **proud, finalizing condemnation**. True discernment never tries to occupy God’s seat; it kneels at His feet, applies His truth carefully, and always leaves the door open for grace, because that’s the door we ourselves depend on every day.
