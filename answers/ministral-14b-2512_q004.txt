Proverbs 3:5–6 calls for a **trust in the Lord that is both profound and discerning**, not a blind rejection of reason or human understanding. To distinguish this from mere anti-intellectualism, we must unpack the passage carefully:

### **1. Trust in the Lord with All Your Heart (Not Blind Faith)**
The verse does not demand that we abandon reason or suppress our intellect. Instead, it calls for:
- **A primary, foundational reliance on God**—not as an alternative to wisdom but as the ultimate source of wisdom.
- **Wholehearted commitment**—meaning our trust must be **prioritized** above self-reliance, not that we ignore human insight entirely.
- **Acknowledgment of God’s sovereignty**—recognizing that His wisdom transcends our limited understanding.

This is not anti-intellectualism but **humble dependence**—trusting God while still using the mind He gave us.

### **2. Lean Not to Your Own Understanding (Not Rejecting Reason)**
The phrase *"lean not unto thine own understanding"* does not mean:
- **"Ignore all human wisdom"** (e.g., science, philosophy, or practical knowledge).
- **"Act against your conscience"** (e.g., reckless decisions without thought).

Instead, it means:
- **Do not make your own limited understanding the final authority**—because human reasoning is flawed (Eccl. 7:29; Jer. 17:9).
- **Do not trust in your own plans as if they were infallible**—because God’s ways are higher (Isa. 55:8–9).
- **Do not rely on self-sufficiency**—because true wisdom comes from God (Col. 2:3).

### **3. Humble Self-Distrust vs. Anti-Intellectualism**
| **Humble Self-Distrust** | **Anti-Intellectualism** |
|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| Recognizes human limitations but still engages with reason. | Rejects reason as inherently evil or useless. |
| Trusts God’s guidance while using wisdom where possible. | Dismisses all human knowledge as inferior to faith alone. |
| Seeks God’s direction but remains open to learning. | Dismisses education, science, or critical thinking. |
| *"I don’t know everything, but I trust God’s wisdom more."* | *"All human knowledge is worthless; only faith matters."* |

### **4. The Balance: Trusting God While Using Wisdom**
The passage does not contradict human effort but **subordinates it to divine guidance**. For example:
- A doctor may trust God for healing while still using medical knowledge.
- A leader may seek God’s will while still consulting wise counsel.
- A student may rely on God’s direction while studying diligently.

### **Conclusion**
Proverbs 3:5–6 calls for **humble self-distrust**—not in the sense of rejecting reason but in recognizing that **true wisdom begins with God**. It is a call to:
1. **Prioritize God’s wisdom** over self-reliance.
2. **Use human understanding** but not as an idol.
3. **Acknowledge God in all decisions**, trusting Him to guide even when our understanding is limited.

This is the opposite of anti-intellectualism—it is **faith-informed wisdom**, where trust in God and engagement with reality work together.
