That verse gets misread a lot — it’s not a gag order on moral judgment. It’s a warning about *how* and *why* we judge. 

### **The core difference: Discernment vs. Condemnation**

|  | **Discernment** | **Appetite to condemn** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Goal** | See clearly so you can act wisely | Declare someone guilty and enjoy their “sentence” |
| **Posture** | “Is this true? Is this good? What’s the wise move here?” | “You’re bad. I’m better. You deserve punishment.” |
| **Focus** | The *action* and its consequences | The *person* and their worth |
| **Fruit** | Protection, wisdom, course-correction | Contempt, self-righteousness, relational rupture |
| **Jesus’ own example** | “Go and sin no more” to the woman caught in adultery — he named the sin but refused to stone her | The Pharisees already had rocks in hand before she spoke |

**1. “Judge not” ≠ “Never evaluate”** 

The Greek word for “judge” here is *krinō* — it carried a courtroom sense: to sentence, to hand down a final verdict on a person’s worth. Jesus himself made constant moral evaluations: “Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees,” “Beware of false prophets,” “By their fruits you will know them.” 

So Luke 6:37 isn’t forbidding discernment. You *have* to discern to live: choosing friends, raising kids, voting, hiring, even forgiving. You can’t forgive what you haven’t first recognized as wrong.

**2. The problem is the appetite** 

The passage sits in a sermon about loving enemies and extending mercy. The target is a specific heart condition: the relish we get from condemning. It’s the part of us that likes to sit on the bench, bang the gavel, and feel superior. That appetite blinds us — “why do you see the speck in your brother’s eye, but do not notice the log in your own?” comes just a few verses later in Luke 6:41-42.

Condemnation says: “You are your worst moment, and I get to say so.”  
Discernment says: “That action is destructive, and I need to respond to reality.”

**3. Why the distinction matters**

Moral judgments *do* matter. If they didn’t, “forgive” in the same verse would be meaningless — you can only forgive a debt you first recognize. Jesus isn’t telling us to pretend evil is fine. He’s telling us to refuse the role of final executioner.

Think of it like this:  
- **Discernment** is a surgeon’s scalpel. You use it to cut out what harms, so healing can happen.  
- **Condemnation** is a sledgehammer. You use it to crush, and nothing heals after.  

The verse ends with “forgive, and ye shall be forgiven.” That’s the telos. The kind of judgment Jesus warns against is the kind that blocks forgiveness — the kind that says “I’ll never release you from this.” Discernment leaves the door open for repentance and restoration. Condemnation nails it shut.

So no, moral judgments aren’t canceled. What’s canceled is the power trip we get from delivering verdicts. You’re still called to name what’s good and evil. You’re just not called to enjoy playing God while you do it.
