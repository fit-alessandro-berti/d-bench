The trust described in Proverbs 3:5–6 involves a deep, humble reliance on God rather than a rejection of intellectual engagement. This passage asks for a kind of trust that acknowledges the limits of human understanding—not by dismissing reason or intellect, but by recognizing their inherent boundaries and potential for error.

Humble self-distrust is a thoughtful recognition of one's finite wisdom, prompting an openness to divine guidance. It's not about abandoning reason but about placing reason within the larger context of faith, virtue, and spiritual discernment. This humility corrects our tendency toward prideful self-sufficiency.

In contrast, mere anti-intellectualism is an unreflective dismissal of reason itself, a distrust of learning, questioning, and thoughtful inquiry. The passage doesn't promote ignorance or discourage understanding; instead, it reminds us that human wisdom alone, unaided by spiritual insight, may lead to incomplete or flawed conclusions.

Therefore, Proverbs invites a trust that actively engages the mind, yet willingly submits to the higher wisdom and direction of God, aligning reason with faith in a way that enriches rather than diminishes intellectual pursuit.
