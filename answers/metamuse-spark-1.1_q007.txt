That verse is often read as: "Never say anything is right or wrong." But that's not what Jesus is saying - in fact, that reading would contradict almost everything else he says in the same sermon.

In Luke 6, he just told his listeners to *love your enemies, do good, bless.* Those are all moral judgments. He calls out greed, hypocrisy, blindness. Later he says "a good tree does not bear bad fruit" - which requires you to look at the fruit. And "a blind man cannot guide a blind man."

So he assumes you *will* make moral evaluations. The question is *how* and *from what position*.

There is a difference in Greek, and in English, between two things we collapse into the word "judge."

### 1. Discernment is: "This is true, this is false. This is harmful, this is healing."

### 2. Condemnation is: "Because of this, you are worthless. You are written off. I sit above you as your final judge and executioner."

Luke 6:37 actually uses two different words to make this clear:

> **"Judge not [krino]** - don't play final judge - **and ye shall not be judged: condemn not [katadikazo]** - don't pass final sentence - **and ye shall not be condemned"**

*Krino* was the word for a magistrate pronouncing a verdict. *Katadikazo* is even stronger - to condemn to punishment, to deem guilty with no appeal.

Discernment evaluates an action. Condemnation evaluates a person *and closes their case.*

Here's how they differ in practice:

**Discernment says:** "What was done is wrong."
**The appetite to condemn says:** "Therefore you are *only* wrong, and always will be."

**Discernment is humble.** It remembers "I'm looking at you through my own flaws." That's why in Matthew's parallel account, Jesus immediately adds: "First remove the log from your own eye, *then* you will see clearly to remove the speck from your brother's eye." The point isn't *never* help with the speck. It's *see clearly first.*

**The appetite to condemn is self-exalting.** It *enjoys* the feeling of being above someone. It needs someone to be lower to feel righteous. Psychologically, it feels good - a little hit of superiority.

**Discernment aims to restore.** Its question is: "What is true, and what would heal?"
**Condemnation aims to dispose.** Its question is: "How can I punish, exile, or prove myself right?"

**Discernment can hold complexity.** It can say: "You did something destructive, and you are still a human made in the image of God, still capable of more than this."
**Condemnation flattens a person into their worst moment.** It dehumanizes.

If "judge not" meant "moral judgments never matter," then love would be impossible. Love has to be able to say "this is harming you" and "this is harming others." A parent who never says a road is dangerous isn't loving. A friend who never says "this addiction is killing you" isn't loving. Indifference is not forgiveness.

What Jesus is forbidding is the *appetite*. The constant scanning for faults, the pleasure in pronouncing people guilty, the finality.

And he points to why we do it: we want to set the standard for how we will be treated.

The next verse makes it explicit: "for with the same measure that ye mete withal it shall be measured to you again." If you live by a microscope that looks for guilt, you will experience the world as constantly guilty, including yourself. If you live by a measure of mercy - which still sees clearly, but wants to forgive what can be forgiven - you create space to breathe.

Forgiveness in this verse - *apolyete, release* - doesn't mean "pretend it never happened." It means "loose the debt. Don't make them keep paying you in your mind forever."

A simple self-check when you feel that pull:

1.  **Am I seeking to understand or to win?** Discernment asks questions. Condemnation has already written the closing argument.
2.  **If this person changed, would I be glad or disappointed?** Condemnation is disappointed when the villain becomes human again.
3.  **Am I willing to apply the same standard to myself today?** Not in general, as a theoretical sinner - but for this exact tendency.

Moral clarity matters. Without it we become numb. But condemnation hardens the heart of the one doing it far faster than it changes the one receiving it.

Discernment says: "This is wrong, therefore we need truth and healing."

Condemnation says: "You are wrong, therefore you are disposable."

Jesus is inviting you out of the second job entirely, so you can do the first well.
