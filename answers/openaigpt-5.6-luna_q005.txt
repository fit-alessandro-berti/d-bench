Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it confronts several powerful instincts at once: self-protection, the desire for retaliation, loyalty to one’s group, and the conviction that people who do evil have forfeited any claim on our goodwill. It can sound especially implausible when the enemy has caused real trauma or continues to pose a threat.

In Jesus’ teaching, however, “love” does not primarily mean affection or approval. To love an enemy is to refuse to make hatred the governing principle of one’s action. “Bless,” “do good,” and “pray” make love concrete: one seeks the enemy’s good, even while naming the wrong truthfully and resisting it where necessary. A person may love an enemy without trusting that person, reconciling immediately, surrendering legal protection, or remaining in an abusive situation.

It is not merely sentimental. Sentiment says, “Everyone is really harmless,” or suppresses anger in the name of niceness. Jesus does neither. The command presupposes that enemies really curse, hate, misuse, and persecute. Enemy-love takes evil seriously enough to refuse reproducing it. Nor is it merely strategic, although it may have strategic consequences: it can interrupt cycles of revenge, disarm hostility, and make social reconciliation possible. But Jesus grounds it not in its usefulness, but in the character of God, who gives sun and rain to both the just and unjust.

The phrase “that ye may be the children of your Father” means that enemy-love is an act of resemblance. God’s generosity is not restricted to those who deserve it. To love an enemy is therefore to participate in a wider moral vision than reciprocity: goodness is given not only because it has been earned, but because the giver refuses to let another person’s evil dictate his or her own character.

That makes enemy-love a form of moral strength. It requires enough inner freedom to distinguish justice from revenge, enough courage to confront wrongdoing without dehumanizing the wrongdoer, and enough humility to remember that one’s own moral standing is also received rather than self-created. It is not passivity. One may oppose an enemy, expose injustice, use lawful protection, or prevent further harm—and still refuse to desire the enemy’s destruction for its own sake.

For those who have been seriously harmed, this command should not be used to demand instant feelings, unsafe contact, or premature reconciliation. Enemy-love may begin simply as a refusal to let another person’s violence colonize one’s soul. It can include grief, anger, boundaries, and a prayer that justice be done and that the enemy be changed. In that sense, it is neither softness nor calculation, but a disciplined participation in God’s indiscriminate goodness.
