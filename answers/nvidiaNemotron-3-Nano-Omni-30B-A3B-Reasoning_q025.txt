**Answer:**

The moral danger in Matthew 6:1 is **the craving to be seen**—the desire for human approval—rather than visibility per se.

1. **Visibility as a means, not the end**  
   - The verse warns against “doing your alms before men, to be seen of them.”  
   - The act of giving itself is not condemned; the problem is the *motivation* behind it.

2. **Craving for recognition corrupts the intention**  
   - When the purpose shifts from “helping those in need” to “receiving praise, admiration, or social status,” the act becomes self‑serving.  
   - This self‑centered motive nullifies the spiritual reward that comes from God (“no reward of your Father which is in heaven”).

3. **Visibility itself isn’t inherently sinful**  
   - Jesus elsewhere commends public deeds that glorify God (e.g., preaching the gospel, public service).  
   - What makes the act “dangerous” is the *intent* to use visibility as a badge of righteousness.

**In short:** The verse condemns the *inner desire* for human applause, because that desire replaces genuine devotion to God with a performance for people. The moral fault lies in the craving to be seen, not in the fact that the act is public.
