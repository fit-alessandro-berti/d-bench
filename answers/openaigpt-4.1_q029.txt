This verse from **Galatians 6:1** lays out a profound approach to how believers should respond when someone among them falls into sin or error. Let’s break down the *moral posture* required and why *restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion* are united in Paul’s instruction.

---

### 1. **The Moral Posture Required**

- **Spirituality:** “Ye which are spiritual…”  
  Correction is to be attempted by those who are spiritually mature, implying humility, wisdom, and reliance on the Spirit.

- **Restorative Purpose:** “…restore such an one…”  
  The goal is not punishment or condemnation, but *restoration*—to bring the person back, to heal, to help them recover.

- **Meekness:** “…in the spirit of meekness…”  
  The correction must be gentle and humble, avoiding harshness, superiority, or self-righteousness.

- **Self-suspicion (Introspection):** “…considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted.”  
  The one correcting should be aware of their own fallibility and vulnerability to sin, preventing pride or arrogance.

---

### 2. **Why Restoration, Meekness, and Self-Suspicion Go Together**

- **Restoration** is the purpose:  
  Correction is not to expel, embarrass, or hurt, but to *restore* someone to spiritual health and fellowship.

- **Meekness** is the manner:  
  True restoration only happens when the corrector acts gently. A harsh or proud attitude can wound further or drive someone away. Meekness (“gentleness” in some translations) ensures that the correction is done out of love and concern, not haughtiness.

- **Self-suspicion (Humility)** is the safeguard:  
  Being aware of one’s *own* susceptibility to error guards the corrector from becoming self-righteous, judgmental, or hypocritical. It keeps the corrector humble, knowing that anyone—including themselves—could fall into similar faults.

---

### 3. **Summary**

The three are deeply **interconnected**:

- **Without restoration**, correction becomes mere judgment.
- **Without meekness**, restoration becomes patronizing or hurtful.
- **Without self-suspicion**, meekness evaporates and pride enters.

Paul’s wisdom here is that true, loving correction (restoration) must be offered with *gentleness* (meekness) and with *humility* (self-suspicion), reflecting not only the heart of Christ but also fostering genuine community health.

#### **In essence, real correction is done not with a pointed finger, but with an outstretched hand.**
