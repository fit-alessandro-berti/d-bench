Short answer  
Because it runs directly against the instinct for reciprocity (“pay back what I got”), enemy–love feels unrealistic.  It is not a matter of sweet sentiment (“be nice”) and it is more than a shrewd tactic (“kill him with kindness”).  In the Sermon on the Mount Jesus presents it as the distinctive moral energy that comes from sharing God’s own life – a strength that can afford to absorb hostility instead of echoing it.

Expanded answer  

1.  Why it sounds impossible  
   a.  Biological wiring.  Retribution, group-loyalty and self-protection are built into our nervous system.  Neurologically anger feels empowering and fear makes us vigilant; love makes us vulnerable.  
   b.  Social conditioning.  Most cultures praise loyalty to friends and punishment for foes.  Honour, patriotism and even some theories of justice tell us that to “let the enemy win” is weakness.  
   c.  Religious reflexes.  Many believers equate God’s righteousness with symmetrical pay-back: the righteous must prosper and the wicked must be crushed.  A command to bless persecutors collides with this reflex.  

2.  Not sentimental  
   •  The Greek imperative agapᾶτε (Mt 5:44) denotes agapē, an act of the will: to seek the other’s good, not to conjure pleasant feelings.  You cannot be ordered to feel; you can be ordered to do.  
   •  Jesus specifies deeds (bless, do good, pray).  None require liking the person, only treating him as a neighbour.  
   •  In Luke’s parallel (6:35) enemy-love is linked with “expecting nothing in return”; this rules out the syrupy hope that “if I’m nice enough everyone will like me”.  

3.  Not merely strategic  
   •  Yes, benevolence can melt hostility (Prov 25:21-22; Rom 12:20) and non-violent movements from Gandhi to King have used that power.  But the Sermon does not appeal to likely success; it appeals to the character of God:  
        “so that you may be sons of your Father … for he makes his sun rise on the evil and on the good.”  
   •  God’s sunshine often fails to reform the “evil”; yet God keeps shining.  The love is therefore principled, not results-based.  

4.  A deeper moral strength  
   a.  Identity: “children of your Father.”  The command flows from a new identity, not merely from ethical calculus.  
   b.  Freedom from mimicry: Violence feeds on imitation (René Girard).  Refusing to mirror the aggressor breaks the cycle; that takes more inner strength than retaliation.  
   c.  Eschatological trust: God, not I, will deliver final justice (Rom 12:19).  Because judgment is handed over, I am free to will the enemy’s good now.  
   d.  Creative possibility: To treat a foe as convertible into a friend is to open a future that vengeance forecloses.  That is why King called agapē “overflowing good will even for those who seek to harm you.”  

5.  Illustrations of the three levels  
   •  Sentimental – “Just smile and be nice.”  Works until the other side draws blood.  
   •  Strategic – Gandhi’s satyagraha or the U.S. civil-rights sit-ins.  Even when it fails, the method is judged by outcome.  
   •  Transformative moral power – Jesus on the cross (“Father, forgive them”), Stephen praying for his murderers, or the Truth-and-Reconciliation process in South Africa.  The action is right even if the offender never repents, because it witnesses to a different reality.  

Conclusion  
Enemy-love is hard to take seriously because it contradicts our primal sense of fairness and self-preservation.  Yet in the Gospel it is neither mushy feeling nor mere tactic; it is the robust, God-like capacity to will the good of the other without surrendering commitment to justice.  It is the moral strength that refuses to let somebody else’s evil dictate one’s own behaviour, thereby breaking the chain of reciprocal harm and showing what it means to be “children of your Father in heaven.”
