This is one of the strongest ethical commands in the New Testament, and it does not define victory the way we normally do.

To understand it, you have to read verses 19-21 together:

> 19 Dearly beloved, avenge not yourselves, but rather give place unto wrath: for it is written, Vengeance is mine; I will repay, saith the Lord.
> 20 Therefore if thine enemy hunger, feed him; if he thirst, give him drink: for in so doing thou shalt heap coals of fire on his head.
> 21 Be not overcome of evil, but overcome evil with good.

Paul is quoting Proverbs 25:21-22.

### 1. How does it define victory over evil?

Paul defines victory in two moves:

**a) Victory is internal first: "Be not overcome of evil."**
Evil wins not when you are harmed, but when you become like the one who harmed you. If you repay hatred with hatred, contempt with contempt, the evil has replicated itself in you. You have been overcome.

**b) Victory is external second: "but overcome evil with good."**
The Greek word for overcome is *nikao* — to conquer, to vanquish. You don't win by matching evil, you win by breaking its logic and ending its cycle. You refuse to be a link in the chain.

So victory is not: "my enemy suffers." Victory is: "evil itself fails to spread, and may even be transformed."

### 2. Is this weak?

It looks weak because it refuses retaliation, which we associate with power. In Roman culture — and ours — feeding an enemy when you have the power to hurt him looked like cowardice.

It is actually far harder than retaliation. Retaliation is instinctive and requires no self-control. To remain kind, just, and merciful when you are wronged requires:

* self-mastery over anger
* trust that justice is not abandoned, but left to God — "Vengeance is mine; I will repay"
* enough security in your own identity that you don't need revenge to feel validated

That is not weakness. It is restraint, which is a higher form of strength.

### 3. Is it manipulative — "heap coals of fire on his head"?

This is the confusing phrase. If you do good *in order to* make God punish your enemy more, that would be manipulation — doing good as a covert form of revenge.

That interpretation contradicts the entire context. Verse 14 says "Bless them which persecute you: bless, and curse not." Verse 17-18 says "Live peaceably with all men." You cannot bless and live peaceably while secretly trying to increase someone's punishment.

Scholars point to three overlapping backgrounds for "coals of fire":

**1. Shame that leads to repentance.** In the ancient world, fire on the head was a picture of intense shame or pangs of conscience. Your unexpected kindness burns away his justification for hating you. The pain is not torture, but the moral discomfort that can cause a person to reconsider.

**2. An act of life-preservation.** In the ancient Near East, keeping your home fire lit was essential. If your fire went out, you had to go to a neighbor for coals to relight it. To give an enemy coals was literally to give him the means to live through the night — to sustain his life.

**3. An Egyptian ritual of repentance.** There was an ancient custom where a person showing public remorse would carry a pan of burning coals on his head. To "heap coals" would mean to bring someone to the point of contrition.

All three point to the same end: your goodness does not destroy your enemy, it may restore him.

The "coals" are not God's extra punishment. The punishment part was already handled in verse 19 — God will handle justice. Your job is different.

**Conclusion:**

Paul is not saying: Be nice so God will hurt them worse.

He is saying: Leave justice to God. Your task is to refuse to become evil yourself, and to give your enemy an experience of unearned good that exposes evil for what it is, breaks the cycle of retaliation, and if possible, opens the door for change.

That is why verse 21 is the summary: Evil is not overcome by more evil. A fire is not put out by more fire. It is overcome only by something of a completely different nature — good.

It is not weak, and it is not manipulative. It is genuinely strong, because only good has the power to actually end evil rather than just perpetuate it.
