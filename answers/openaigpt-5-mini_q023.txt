A short answer: Isaiah is insisting that true repentance is a whole‑person turn to God. By naming both “way” (derek) and “thoughts” (machshavah), the prophet shows that God’s call addresses external behavior and the inward will and imagination. Mercy is offered to anyone who truly turns — not merely to outward reformation, nor to private remorse without change — because God forgives and renews the whole person.

Why both words?
- “Way” (Heb. derek) pictures conduct, choices, patterns of life — what you do, where you walk. Turning from your “way” means breaking sinful habits and changing observable actions.
- “Thoughts” (Heb. machshavot) reaches the inner life: intentions, plans, fantasies, the motives and imaginations that shape behaviour. Scripture elsewhere (e.g. Mark 7:21–23; Psalm 51) treats the heart’s orientation as the root of sin.

What that implies about outward conduct and inward imagination
- Sin is not only external; it issues from the mind and will. So a merely external reform (better behavior without changed motives) is incomplete, and inner repentance that doesn’t result in changed conduct is likewise incomplete. Genuine repentance (Hebrew teshuvah, “return”) must involve both a change of heart and a change of life.
- The text also affirms that God cares about the inner life. He judges thoughts and imagination, and he invites their transformation (see Jeremiah 31:33; Romans 12:2).

What this says about the possibility of mercy
- The promise follows the call: “let him return… and he will have mercy on him.” That shows God’s readiness to pardon when a person truly turns. “Abundantly pardon” signals that God’s forgiveness reaches even inward sins — his mercy is sufficient for the whole person.
- The emphasis on both way and thoughts is not a barrier but an invitation: God will forgive and renew both outward deeds and inward motives when we seek him sincerely. Practically, that means confession, asking God to change your mind and heart, and letting that inward change shape new choices.

Practical application: examine both what you do and what you want; confess and forsake sinful actions and desires; ask God to re‑form your imagination and motives; trust the promise that he abundantly pardons those who truly return.
