
The elder brother’s deepest problem is **discomfort with mercy itself**. Here’s why:

1. **His anger and envy are symptoms, not the root**: While his anger and resentment are evident, they stem from a deeper failure to understand or accept the father’s radical, unmerited grace. He focuses on his sibling’s unworthy behavior (e.g., "devoured thy living with harlots") and feels unjustly overlooked in comparison, but these emotions arise from his inability to see beyond a transactional view of his relationship with his father.

2. **Wounded fairness and pride in obedience are secondary**: He emphasizes his obedience ("neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment") and feels wronged by the lack of recognition for his faithful service. This reflects a legalistic mindset, where he expects rewards proportional to his efforts. However, the father redirects him to the deeper truth: his identity is not based on what he *does* but on his relationship with the father ("all that I have is thine"). His pride in his own righteousness blinds him to the father’s joy in restoration, which overshadows merit.

3. **The father’s response reveals the core issue**: The father’s words—"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine"—highlight that the elder brother has always enjoyed the father’s provision and presence. Yet he fixates on what he perceives as *lack* (no "kid" for a celebration) instead of gratitude for his secure inheritance. This suggests he struggles to embrace the father’s generosity toward others, especially those who have "sinned" (in his view). 

4. **Mercy as the central tension**: The parable hinges on the father’s mercy toward the repentant younger son (representing God’s unconditional love for sinners). The elder brother’s inability to rejoice in this mercy—"It was meet that we should make merry"—reveals his discomfort with grace that defies his sense of justice. He would rather see the father’s resources spent on someone "worthy," not someone who has "devoured [his] living." His resentment is rooted in a worldview where merit and judgment supersede compassion and reconciliation. 

In short, the elder brother embodies those who, like the Pharisee in Luke 18:11–12, trust in their own righteousness and view sinners with suspicion. His tragedy is that he fails to grasp that the father’s love and joy are not limited or diminished by mercy but are the very heartbeat of the kingdom. His deepest struggle is reconciling grace with his own self-righteousness.
