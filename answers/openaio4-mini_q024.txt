In Ezekiel 36:26–27 God promises not merely that his people will learn to obey from the outside (“external compliance”) but that he will actually re-make their inner selves so that obedience flows naturally out of new desires and affections.

1.   External Compliance versus a New Heart  
    • External Compliance  
      – You obey because you’re afraid of punishment, or because someone taught you, or out of social convention.  
      – Your “heart” (your will, your affections, your thought‐life) hasn’t really changed—it’s the same old desires, only you put on a religious veneer.  
      – Paul insists in Romans 2:28–29 that true Jewishness is not a matter of outward rite (circumcision) but of “a circumcision of the heart, by the Spirit.”  
    • A “New Heart”  
      – The “heart” in Hebrew thought is the center of will, emotion, and understanding. To change the heart is to reconfigure a person’s very motivation.  
      – God “takes away the stony heart” (v. 26), implants “a heart of flesh,” and “puts his Spirit within” (v.27). The result is not mere rule‐keeping but delight in God’s ways, personal appropriation of his values, and inward empowerment to live them out.  
      – In the New Testament Jesus echoes this in the promise of the New Covenant (Jer. 31:33; cf. Luke 22:20; 2 Cor. 3:3–6): the law written on hearts, rather than tablets of stone.

2.   Why Is Hardness Pictured as Material?  
    • Vivid Imagery of Transformation  
      – “Stony heart” versus “heart of flesh” is a shockingly concrete picture. We all know how unfeeling, unresponsive stone is, and how soft, living flesh can be wounded, healed, warmed, changed.  
      – Ezekiel’s audience needed to see that God was not making cosmetic adjustments; he was removing a heart that resisted, blocked, and repelled him, and giving one fully alive and pliable.  
    • Symbol of Obstinacy and Spiritual Insensitivity  
      – In Scripture, rocks and flint are often metaphors for stubbornness (e.g. Jer. 4:4; Acts 7:51). A “stony” heart will not hear God’s voice, will not absorb his truth, will not yield.  
      – Flesh, by contrast, is permeable, capable of growth, capable of feeling (Ps. 51:8–10). A “heart of flesh” responds to God’s Word, repents, loves, obeys.

3.   Theological Significance  
    • Regeneration, Not Rehabilitation  
      – External compliance is like trying to repaint rusted metal. Regeneration is replacement of the metal itself.  
      – Only a heart remade by the Spirit can truly “walk in [God’s] statutes” and “keep [his] judgments” with joy (v.27).  
    • Assurance and Identity  
      – When God himself destroys the old heart and implants a new one, we can be confident that the work will succeed. Our obedience is no longer a fragile, self-supported effort but the fruit of a divine work of grace.  
      – Our identity shifts from “I try to obey” to “I am the person God has made eager to obey.”

In short, external compliance is behavioral, partial, and precarious; a “new heart” is a wholesale, inner makeover by God’s Spirit, pictured in language of replacing one material reality (stone) with another (living flesh) to underscore just how radical and thorough the change is.
