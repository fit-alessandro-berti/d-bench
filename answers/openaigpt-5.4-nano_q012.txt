Jeremiah 17:9–10 is mainly **realistic about self-deception**, and only secondarily “pessimistic” in the sense that it expects that self-justifying deception will be present.

- **“The heart is deceitful… and desperately wicked”** points to an inner unreliability. The problem isn’t only that humans do wrong; it’s that the *inner narrative* that claims to explain motives is untrustworthy.
- **“I the Lord search the heart, I try the reins”** shifts the focus from human introspection-as-proof to **God’s ability to see truly**. The point is not “don’t think,” but “don’t treat your self-diagnosis as final evidence.”

So the passage reads like: *human motives are often hidden—even from ourselves—therefore judgment and genuine discernment belong to God.*

### How should someone talk about motives if the heart is unreliable?
In a biblically faithful way, you can speak about motives, but you have to speak about them with **humility, testing, and accountability**, not as ultimate certainty.

1. **Use motive-language cautiously**  
   Don’t assume “I know why I did it” is definitive. You can say things like: *“I think my motive may be…”* rather than *“My motives are pure/impure with certainty.”*

2. **Expect mixed motives**  
   The Bible often treats motives as composite (not one clean cause). Jeremiah 17 supports the idea that self-understanding can be partial or masked.

3. **Let behavior and fruit be part of the assessment**  
   Jeremiah connects heart scrutiny with outcomes: God gives “according to… the fruit of his doings.” That implies that actions (and their patterns) can provide more reliable data than inner claims alone—still not perfect, but less easily self-manipulated than feelings.

4. **Submit motives to God’s “searching” rather than to your own courtroom**  
   The passage models the correct epistemology: the decisive “inspection” is divine (“I the Lord search…”). In practice that means confession, prayer, and asking for correction—especially from trustworthy others.

5. **Accept correction from outside observers**  
   Since the heart is deceitful, other people may perceive what you cannot. That fits with communal discernment and the New Testament theme of examining oneself with guidance, not in isolation.

6. **Speak constructively—motive-talk as repentance, not self-certainty**  
   Motive language should function to orient you toward truth and change (“I may be deceiving myself; help me see”), rather than to win arguments (“I’m definitely motivated by X”).

**In short:** Jeremiah 17:9–10 doesn’t eliminate motive-talk; it warns against taking the heart’s testimony as final. Real faithfulness is to speak about motives *provisionally*, look to *fruit*, and submit inner claims to *God’s searching* (often mediated through Scripture, prayer, and trustworthy counsel).
