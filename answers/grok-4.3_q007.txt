This verse does not teach that moral judgments are irrelevant or that we should suspend our ability to distinguish right from wrong. Instead, it addresses a particular *posture* toward judgment—one rooted in condemnation rather than truth-seeking or mercy.

### The Targeted Error

The command "Judge not" (Greek: *krinete*) in this context does not primarily mean "never evaluate actions" or "treat all behavior as morally equivalent." Jesus frequently makes moral distinctions in the same gospel—calling out hypocrisy, warning about sin, and identifying fruit by its nature (see Luke 6:43-45 immediately following). The issue is not evaluation itself but the *spirit* in which it is exercised.

The surrounding verses (Luke 6:37-42) make this clear. The warning against judging is immediately followed by the command to examine one's own life first ("take the plank out of your own eye"). This suggests the prohibition targets a specific failure: the tendency to use moral standards as a weapon of superiority rather than as a tool for understanding or correction. The parallel in Matthew 7:1-5 makes the same point even more explicitly.

### Discernment vs. the Appetite to Condemn

The distinction that matters is between two different activities:

**Discernment** involves the honest assessment of reality. It asks: *What is actually happening here? What are the likely consequences? What does wisdom require?* This faculty is necessary for any functional moral life. Without it, one cannot identify evil, protect the vulnerable, or offer meaningful guidance. Discernment can be exercised with varying degrees of certainty and should remain open to correction, but it does not require moral neutrality. A parent discerning that a child's behavior is harmful, a friend noting a pattern of self-destruction, or a society identifying corruption are all engaging in discernment.

**The appetite to condemn** is different in both motive and method. It is less interested in accurate perception than in the satisfaction of pronouncing guilt. Its characteristics include:
- Selective outrage that ignores comparable or worse behavior in oneself or one's group.
- A preference for harsh conclusions over patient investigation.
- The use of moral language primarily to diminish or exclude rather than to correct or restore.
- A resistance to extending the same standards of scrutiny to one's own conduct.

The verse is not saying "do not notice when someone is doing harm." It is warning against the human tendency to derive a sense of righteousness from the act of condemning others while remaining blind to one's own more substantial failures. This is why the teaching immediately connects non-condemnation with forgiveness: the posture that refuses to condemn others is the same posture that recognizes its own need for mercy.

### Why This Matters

If the verse meant that moral judgments never matter, it would be incoherent with the rest of Jesus' teaching, which repeatedly distinguishes between good and evil fruit, between the narrow and wide paths, and between genuine discipleship and self-deception. The command is not an endorsement of moral indifference but a restriction on a particular vice: the prideful use of moral categories to elevate oneself.

In practice, this means one can still identify that an action is wrong, harmful, or foolish while refusing the internal posture that says, "I am the kind of person who would never do that." The first is often necessary. The second is what the verse targets.
