The parable does not present a single, isolated "gotcha" flaw in the Pharisee; rather, it diagnoses a **fundamental inversion of the relationship between God, self, and neighbor**. The condemnation is structural: the Pharisee has made *himself* the center of the spiritual universe, using God as a mirror for his own glory and the neighbor (the publican) as a foil for his superiority.

Here is a breakdown of the specific dynamics at play.

---

### 1. What Condemns the Pharisee?
**The short answer: It is not *what* he does (virtue), but *how* he relates to it (autonomy/pride).** The options you listed are not mutually exclusive; they are concentric circles describing the same root sin: **Justification by Sight (Works) rather than Justification by Faith (Mercy).**

#### A. The "False Doctrine" is Functional, Not Necessarily Creedal
The Pharisee likely holds *correct* theology: God is holy, the Law is good, fasting and tithing are commanded/commended. His heresy is **soteriological** (doctrine of salvation). He operates on a **contractual metaphysic**: *I perform → God owes → I am justified.*
*   **The Error:** He treats righteousness as a *possession* he has achieved and can display, rather than a *gift* he receives. He confuses the *fruit* of righteousness with the *root* of acceptance.

#### B. Pride as "Autonomy" (The Theological Root)
The Greek phrase *pros heauton* (v. 11)—"prayed **with/by/to himself**"—is the diagnostic key.
*   He addresses God, but the *content* and *orientation* are entirely self-referential.
*   Pride here is not merely "feeling good about yourself"; it is the claim to **self-sufficiency before God**. He does not *ask* for anything (mercy, grace, help to obey). He only *reports*. He treats the temple not as a place of atonement, but as a stage for exhibition.

#### C. Comparison & Contempt as the Necessary Fruit
You cannot sustain a religion of *achievement* without a grading curve.
*   **Comparison:** "I am not as other men are." Righteousness-by-works is inherently comparative. If I am saved by my score, I *must* know the average to know if I pass.
*   **Contempt (Exoutheneō):** The text says he "despised others" (v. 9) and explicitly distances himself from "this publican" (v. 11).
*   **Why this condemns him:** Contempt for the neighbor is proof of alienation from God. As 1 John 4:20 later formalizes: "He who does not love his brother whom he has seen cannot love God whom he has not seen." The Pharisee’s vertical posture (standing tall) necessitates a horizontal posture (looking down).

#### D. Virtue as Self-Display (The Liturgical Idolatry)
"I fast twice a week; I give tithes of all I possess."
*   The Law required *one* fast a year (Day of Atonement) and tithes on *agricultural produce*. He exceeds the Law (voluntary supererogation).
*   **The Sin:** He weaponizes obedience. Virtue becomes **liturgical theater**—performed *coram hominibus* (before men) under the guise of *coram Deo* (before God). He has turned the means of grace (prayer, fasting, giving) into merits of merit.

**Summary:** The Pharisee is condemned because he **presents his resume at the judgment seat and calls it a prayer.** He seeks *justification* (a forensic verdict of "Righteous") but brings *sanctification* (his moral progress) as the grounds. He wants the verdict *because of* the evidence, not *despite* it.

---

### 2. Why Is the Tax Collector’s Posture "Spiritually Truer"?
The Publican (Tax Collector) is not justified because he is "humble" in a generic, virtue-ethics sense (as if humility is just another work he performs better than the Pharisee). He is justified because his posture **corresponds to ontological reality.**

#### A. Physical Posture as Theological Confession
*   **Standing "afar off" (makrothen):** He recognizes the chasm between God’s holiness and his sin. He does not presume access.
*   **Eyes cast down:** In the Ancient Near East, lifting eyes to heaven was the standard prayer posture (cf. Jesus in John 17:1). Refusing to look up is a bodily admission of **shame and unworthiness**. He knows he has no "face" to show God.
*   **Beating the breast (etupton to stēthos):** A rare, violent gesture of extreme grief (cf. Luke 23:48 at the crucifixion). It signifies that the *heart* (the seat of the will/intellect) is the source of the corruption. He does not blame environment or genetics; he strikes the source.

#### B. The Content: "The God, be propitiated to me, THE sinner" (*Ho theos, hilasthēti moi tō hamartōlō*)
This is the theological engine of the parable.
1.  **"The God" (Ho Theos):** Definite article. He addresses the *Covenant Lord*, the Judge of all earth.
2.  **"Be propitiated/merciful" (Hilasthēti):** **Aorist Passive Imperative.** He does not say "forgive me" (aphēson) generically. He uses the technical cultic term related to the **Mercy Seat (Hilastērion)**—the lid of the Ark where blood was sprinkled on Yom Kippur.
    *   *He is asking for atonement.* He knows he brings no merit; he asks God to provide the covering for wrath *on the basis of a substitute*.
3.  **"To me, THE sinner" (Moi tō hamartōlō):** The article + singular. Not "a sinner among many," but **"The Sinner"—the representative guilty one.** He stands in the dock alone, with no comparison class.

#### C. Why This Justifies (v. 14: *dedikaiōmenos*)
Jesus uses the **Divine Passive** ("was justified" — God did it).
*   The Pharisee *attempted* to justify himself (active voice implied in his prayer: "I thank you that I *am* righteous").
*   The Publican *begged* to be justified (passive voice: "You make it so").
*   **The Great Reversal:** "Everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, but he who humbles himself will be exalted."

The Publican’s posture is "truer" because **it is the only posture that allows God to be God (Justifier) and man to be man (Sinner).** The Pharisee’s posture tries to make Man the Justifier and God the Auditor.

---

### The Hermeneutical Trap (Application)
The parable sets a trap for the reader.
*   If you read this and think, *"Thank God I am not like that Pharisee,"* **you have just prayed the Pharisee’s prayer.**
*   The only escape is to stand *with* the Publican: *"God, be propitiated to me, the Pharisee."*
