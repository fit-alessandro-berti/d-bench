The text is not merely saying that treasure *reveals* the heart, but that it *trains* and *orients* the heart. The logic is sequential and causal:

1. **Treasure as investment** — The imperative "lay up" implies deliberate, repeated action. This is about where we choose to place our resources and attention.
2. **Heart follows investment** — "For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also." The heart (in Hebraic thought, the center of will, desire, and emotion) follows the treasure. This is not a static observation but a principle of spiritual and psychological gravity: we become oriented toward what we value, protect, and cultivate.

**Possession, attention, and love are intertwined in a cycle:**

- **Possession** creates *stewardship* — what we own requires care (against moth, rust, theft).
- **Stewardship demands *attention*** — we must think about, manage, and worry over it.
- **Attention cultivates *attachment*** — over time, what we attend to shapes our affections and identity.

Jesus is warning that earthly treasure pulls the heart into a cycle of worry, corruption, and loss — because its nature is to fade, be stolen, or decay. In contrast, heavenly treasure (acts of love, faith, righteousness) draws the heart toward the eternal, incorruptible, and secure. The heart is being *trained* by what it serves.

In short: **We do not just reveal our hearts by our treasures; we form our hearts by them.**
