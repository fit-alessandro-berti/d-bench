At first glance, Luke 6:37 might sound like an absolute prohibition on moral evaluation—suggesting that right and wrong are human constructs that should never be spoken of. However, this is a profound misreading. If moral judgments never mattered, there would be no such thing as forgiveness; you cannot forgive someone for whom there is no offense, no moral breach. 

To understand Luke 6:37, we must distinguish between the human calling to **discern** and the human tendency to **condemn**. They are not the same, and understanding this difference is the key to navigating morality with both clarity and compassion.

### The Error of Abandoning Moral Discernment
If we interpreted this verse to mean "moral judgments never matter," we would be left morally blind. The very next verse in Luke’s Gospel (6:43-45) says, *"For a good tree bringeth not forth corrupt fruit; neither doth a corrupt tree bring forth good fruit... A good man out of the good treasure of his heart bringeth forth that which is good."* 

Jesus is explicitly asking us to judge the "fruit"—to evaluate actions and character. Later, in Matthew 7:5, after telling the crowd not to judge, He tells them to first remove the log from their own eye so they *can* see clearly to remove the speck from their brother's eye. The goal of removing the **condemning spirit** is to make way for **clear-eyed discernment**, not to abandon our ability to tell right from wrong.

### Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn

The distinction between discernment and condemnations lies in their **origin**, their **posture**, and their **goal**.

**1. The Origin: Clarity vs. Insecurity**
*   **Discernment** originates from a commitment to truth. It is an objective assessment of reality. When a discerning person evaluates an action, they are seeking to understand what is true, what is harmful, and what is redemptive.
*   **The Appetite to Condemn** originates from a need to elevate oneself. As Jesus notes in Matthew 7, the condemner usually creates a distorted standard—ignoring their own massive "log" while focusing on another's minor "speck." Condemning feels good because it provides a temporary rush of moral superiority, but it is rooted in insecurity, not truth.

**2. The Posture: Humility vs. Sovereignty**
*   **Discernment** operates with the posture of a peer—a fellow pilgrim who stands under the same law it enforces. It says, "We all fall short; let me see if I can help you navigate this." 
*   **The Appetite to Condemn** operates with the posture of a judge on a bench, wielding absolute authority. Condemnation assumes the final say over someone’s worth and destiny. When we condemn, we step outside our jurisdiction. We declare not just that an action was bad, but that the *person is irredeemably bad*. Only God holds the authority to make final assessments of the human heart.

**3. The Goal: Restoration vs. Destruction**
*   **Discernment** is fundamentally pro-life and pro-restoration. It identifies wrong behavior to protect the weak, to bring justice, and to offer a path toward healing. It is surgical—it cuts out the disease to save the patient.
*   **The Appetite to Condemn** is fundamentally destructive. It identifies wrong behavior to justify the destruction of the person. It is not interested in the restoration of the offender; it is satisfied only by their punishment and exclusion. As the proverb says, "An angry man stirs up strife, and a hot-tempered man leads to transgression." Condemnation is nearly always fueled by an unforgiving anger.

### Forgiveness as the Antidote to Condemnation
The third clause of Luke 6:37 ties it all together: *"forgive, and ye shall be forgiven."* 

This reveals that the reason we must "condemn not" is that we are all standing in desperate need of a pardon we cannot earn. Condemning others is a way of pretending we need no forgiveness ourselves. 

When you truly grasp that you have been shown mercy, two things happen:
1.  **You retain moral clarity:** You recognize that an action is deeply wrong and offensive, just as your own sins were offensive.
2.  **You lose your appetite for condemnation:** Because you know the cost of sin, and you know the depth of the grace you received, you simply cannot look at a sinner and declare them beyond hope.

### Summary
Luke 6:37 is not a command to abandon morality; it is a command to abandon self-righteousness. It calls us to be deeply discerning people who can clearly identify sin, injustice, and danger, but who are utterly devoid of the cruel, self-serving appetite to condemn. 

We are called to hate the sin, to fight for justice, to speak the truth—but to love the sinner so fiercely that we would rather die than condemn them, trusting that the final verdict belongs to a God who is simultaneously perfectly just and infinitely merciful.
