This is a rich and subtle question, and these two verses from Ephesians do lay out a striking opposition—**put away bitterness… be kind and forgiving**—which reflects a deep understanding of human relationships.

### 1. **Bitterness and Forgiveness: Why They’re Linked**

Bitterness and forgiveness are paired in opposition because they are, at heart, emotional and relational responses to being wronged. Bitterness is what happens when hurt or offense is *not* released; forgiveness is the act that releases it. The presence of one usually means the absence of the other.

### 2. **How Does the Text Portray Bitterness?**

Let’s look at the text’s literary and psychological cues:

- **“Let all bitterness, wrath, anger, clamor, slander be put away from you, along with all malice.”**  
  The way Paul lists these terms *together* suggests they are all part of a family of destructive responses: bitterness leads to wrath (explosive anger), which can lead to clamor (fighting), slander (speaking against others), and malice (settled ill will).

So, how is bitterness viewed?

#### a. **A Chosen Posture or Habit**
  - The command “Let… be put away” implies agency—bitterness is something you can hold onto or lay down. It’s not a passing feeling but becomes, if habitual, a settled disposition or *posture* toward others.
  - In context, the Greek word for bitterness ('pikria') was used of enduring resentment—a spirit soured by brooding over offenses.

#### b. **Contagious and Community-Oriented**
  - Bitterness isn’t just personal; it spreads. See Hebrews 12:15: “that no root of bitterness springs up and causes trouble, and by it many become defiled.”  
    The New Testament often warns about bitterness infecting the community, eroding unity and kindness.

#### c. **A Poison of the Heart**
  - It is a long-lived, internalized emotional state—a “smoldering resentment” (as some commentators put it)—that expresses itself in the other behaviors listed: wrath, anger, evil speaking.

### 3. **In Contrast: Forgiveness as the Remedy**

- Forgiveness is not only required but *modeled* (“as God in Christ forgave you”).
- It’s the chosen release of resentment and the active move toward reconciliation, even when forgiveness is costly.
- Forgiveness, like bitterness, can become a posture—a “tenderhearted” stance.

### 4. **Why Does This Matter?**

- If you let bitterness take root, it’s almost impossible to be truly kind, compassionate, or forgiving.
- Bitterness *feels* like self-protection, but it corrodes your own heart and relationships, whereas forgiveness is brand-new life for both the forgiver and the forgiven.

### **Summary Table**

| Bitterness                           | Forgiveness                       |
|---------------------------------------|------------------------------------|
| Tend toward brooding, internal poison | Opens the way for healing          |
| Habitual/learned/cultivated           | Chosen act and ongoing stance      |
| Spreads through relationships         | Restores relationships             |
| Expresses itself in anger/words/deeds | Expresses kindness, tenderness     |
| A closed loop (past-focused)          | An open door (future-focused)      |

**In sum:**  
Bitterness, as Paul uses it, is more than fleeting emotion—it’s a disposition, even a “social disease,” that both poisons and spreads. The text treats it as a posture you can nurture or reject. Forgiveness—commanded and modeled by Christ—is the only sure antidote. Their direct opposition in the passage is not accidental, but essential: only by actively rejecting bitterness can true kindness and compassion thrive.
