Below, I'll provide a thoughtful analysis of Matthew 12:34–35 (using the King James Version you quoted, for consistency) in response to your question. This passage is part of Jesus' rebuke of the Pharisees, who had accused him of casting out demons by the power of Beelzebub (the "prince of devils"). Jesus uses vivid metaphors to link speech to the inner moral state of a person. I'll break it down step by step, drawing on the text's language, context, and broader biblical themes, while directly addressing how speech connects to "hidden moral stockpiles" in the heart and whether words function as symptoms, choices, revelations, or all three.

### 1. **The Connection Between Speech and "Hidden Moral Stockpiles" in the Heart**
The passage employs two key metaphors to describe the heart as a repository of moral character:
- **Abundance (or "overflow") of the heart**: This suggests the heart is like a overflowing container or storehouse. Whatever is stored up inside—good or evil—naturally spills out through speech. The Greek word translated as "abundance" (perisseuma) implies excess or surplus, emphasizing that speech isn't random but an inevitable outflow of what's already amassed within.
- **Treasure of the heart**: This evokes the idea of a hidden vault or stockpile (the Greek thesauros means "treasure" or "storehouse"). A "good man" draws from a treasury of goodness, producing good words and actions, while an "evil man" draws from a treasury of evil, producing harmful ones. The heart's "treasure" is hidden from outward view but is revealed through what emerges from it.

In essence, Jesus is teaching that speech is not isolated or superficial—it's deeply rooted in the core of one's being. The Pharisees' blasphemous words (accusing Jesus of demonic power) expose their inner corruption, likened to the venom of "vipers" (a generation of snakes, echoing Old Testament imagery like Isaiah 59:5 or Psalm 140:3, where vipers symbolize deceit and poison). This isn't just about what people say in the moment; it's about how words betray the accumulated moral (or immoral) reserves built up over time through thoughts, desires, and habits. The heart, in biblical terms, represents the seat of emotions, will, intellect, and spirituality (e.g., Proverbs 4:23: "Keep thy heart with all diligence; for out of it are the issues of life"). Thus, speech acts as a window into these concealed stockpiles, showing that external expressions are symptomatic of internal reality.

This idea aligns with other New Testament teachings, such as James 3:5–6, which compares the tongue to a fire sparked by inner forces, or Luke 6:45 (a parallel to this passage), which reiterates that "out of the good treasure of his heart the good man produces good, and out of the evil treasure the evil man produces evil."

### 2. **Are Words Symptoms, Choices, Revelations, or All Three?**
The passage portrays words as multifaceted—tied to the heart's condition in ways that encompass symptoms, revelations, and even choices. However, the emphasis leans toward symptoms and revelations, with choices implied more subtly. Let's unpack this:

- **Words as Symptoms**: Yes, strongly. The phrasing "out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh" presents speech as a natural, involuntary symptom of the heart's state, much like how a fever indicates an underlying illness. Evil people can't consistently "speak good things" because their inner "evil treasure" predisposes them to produce evil words—it's an organic outflow, not something they can fully suppress. The viper metaphor reinforces this: just as a snake's bite delivers venom because that's its nature, corrupt speech is a symptom of a corrupt heart. This doesn't mean every word is predestined, but it suggests that habitual speech patterns diagnose the heart's health (or sickness). In modern terms, it's like Freudian slips or body language betraying hidden motives—words are indicators of deeper issues.

- **Words as Revelations**: Absolutely. The act of "bringing forth" good or evil things reveals the hidden treasure. What comes out exposes what's inside, making speech a revelatory tool. Jesus is essentially saying, "Your words give you away." This is why he warns in the next verse (Matthew 12:36–37) that people will be judged by their words—they justify or condemn based on what they reveal about the heart. In a broader biblical sense, this echoes the idea that God sees the heart (1 Samuel 16:7), but humans often judge by outward signs like speech, which serve as evidence of inner truth. For the Pharisees, their accusatory words revealed their hypocrisy and rejection of God's work, unmasking their "evil treasure."

- **Words as Choices**: Yes, but more implicitly and secondarily. While the passage stresses the heart's determinative role (suggesting speech flows from it almost automatically), it doesn't negate human agency. Speaking involves deliberate choice—what we say, how we say it, and when. The "good man" chooses to draw from his good treasure, implying moral discipline and intentionality (e.g., cultivating a heart aligned with God's will through prayer, obedience, and renewal, as in Romans 12:2). Conversely, the "evil man" might choose to indulge his evil inclinations, leading to harmful speech. However, Jesus' rhetorical question ("how can ye, being evil, speak good things?") implies that true goodness requires a transformed heart, not just willpower—choices are shaped by the heart's stockpiles. Without inner change (e.g., through repentance and the Holy Spirit), attempts to "speak good" will ring hollow or fail over time.

In summary, the passage suggests **all three**, but with a hierarchy: words are primarily **symptoms** and **revelations** of the heart's hidden moral stockpiles, with **choices** playing a supporting role. Speech isn't just a neutral tool—it's a moral barometer. A consistently good (or evil) heart will produce corresponding words, but individuals can choose to align their hearts with God, leading to transformed speech (e.g., Psalm 19:14: "Let the words of my mouth, and the meditation of my heart, be acceptable in thy sight").

### Broader Implications and Application
This teaching challenges superficial piety: you can't fake goodness forever because your words will eventually betray your heart. It calls for self-examination and heart-level change, not just better "word choice." In practical terms, it might encourage practices like guarding one's thoughts (Philippians 4:8) to build a "good treasure."
