Isaiah deliberately pairs “way” and “thoughts” to show that repentance must be whole‑person: both visible behavior and hidden inner life must turn.

### 1. “Way” and “thoughts” in Hebrew

- **“Way” (דֶּרֶךְ, derek)**  
  Often means one’s path, manner of life, habitual conduct—how you actually live, act, choose, relate.
  
- **“Thoughts” (מַחְשְׁבוֹת, machashavot)**  
  Not just fleeting ideas, but plans, designs, inner reasonings, moral imagination—what the heart is aiming at and plotting.

The paired lines are Hebrew parallelism:

> Let the **wicked** forsake his **way**,  
> and the **unrighteous man** his **thoughts**…

This shows that “wickedness” is not only in what one *does* (way) but also in what one *intends, desires, and devises* (thoughts).

### 2. Outward conduct and inward imagination

By requiring both to be forsaken, the text rules out:

- **Mere external reform**:  
  Changing behavior while continuing to cherish unrighteous fantasies, resentments, idolatries, or self‑exalting plans.

- **Mere internal sentiment**:  
  Feeling regret or having “spiritual” thoughts while refusing to alter your actual way of life.

Repentance is not cosmetic. It is a re‑direction of:

- The **path you walk** (your practices, relationships, ethics, use of time and body), and
- The **world you live in inwardly** (your loves, ambitions, story you tell yourself, how you imagine the good life).

Isaiah reinforces this in the next verses:

> “For my thoughts are not your thoughts,  
> neither are your ways my ways, saith the Lord.” (vv. 8–9)

To repent is to *exchange* our thoughts and ways for His—letting God contradict and overturn both our behavior and our inner script.

### 3. What this implies about mercy

Notice the sequence:

1. **Seek / call** – There is a real, open, gracious invitation:  
   “Seek ye the Lord while he may be found, call ye upon him while he is near.”

2. **Forsake / return** – Repentance is described as:
   - Forsaking way and thoughts (a decisive break),
   - Returning to the Lord (the covenant word *shuv*—to turn back, like a straying child coming home).

3. **Mercy / abundant pardon** – The promise meets the whole person:
   - “He will have mercy upon him” – God’s posture is compassionate, not reluctant.
   - “He will abundantly pardon” – Not a thin, grudging forgiveness but overflowing, more than enough to cover both corrupt deeds *and* twisted imaginations.

So:

- **No one is too far gone**, because God’s mercy is as comprehensive as the sin it addresses—He pardons the outward record and the inward corruption.
- **No one truly repents halfway**—God calls for, and gives grace for, a turning that reaches both what everyone sees and what only you and God know.

In sum, Isaiah 55:6–7 teaches that God’s mercy is wide enough to cover both our ways and our thoughts, but repentance must also reach both. Outward conduct must change, inward imagination must be surrendered, and in that full turning to Him we meet not condemnation, but “abundant pardon.”
