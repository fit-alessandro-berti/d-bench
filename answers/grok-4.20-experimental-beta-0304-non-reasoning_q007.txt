**The Difference Between "Judge Not" and "Moral Judgments Never Matter"**

Luke 6:37 is not a blanket prohibition against all moral evaluation. It is a warning against a particular *spirit* and *posture* toward other people: the spirit of censoriousness, personal condemnation, and the lust to pronounce final verdicts on someone’s worth or eternal standing. Jesus is not saying “never discern right from wrong,” but “do not set yourself up as the ultimate judge and executioner of your brother.”

### 1. What “Judge not / condemn not” actually prohibits
- The appetite to **condemn**: the eager, self-righteous impulse to write people off, to humiliate, to savor their moral failure, or to treat them as irredeemable.
- Hypocritical or disproportionate judgment (see the immediately following verses, Luke 6:41–42, about the speck and the log).
- The assumption that we occupy God’s seat. Only God knows the full heart, the mitigating factors, the degree of knowledge, the pressures, and the final trajectory of a soul. To “condemn” in the biblical sense is often to usurp that role.
- The refusal to forgive. The verse links condemnation with unforgiveness and promises that the measure we use will be used on us.

Jesus himself makes many sharp moral judgments (calling Pharisees “whitewashed tombs,” “brood of vipers,” “hypocrites”; overturning tables; warning of hell). Paul tells the Corinthian church to judge and even expel a sexually immoral member (1 Cor 5). So the command cannot mean “never name sin” or “never evaluate behavior.”

### 2. Discernment vs. the Appetite to Condemn

| Aspect                  | Discernment (biblical, necessary)                          | Appetite to Condemn (what Jesus forbids)                     |
|-------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Goal**                | Protect, restore, love, pursue holiness and truth           | Punish, humiliate, self-justify, feel superior                |
| **Posture**             | Humble, sorrowful, self-aware (“first take the log out…”)  | Self-righteous, eager, savoring the failure of the other      |
| **Scope**               | Specific actions or teachings (“this behavior is sinful”)   | Totalizing verdict on the person (“he is worthless / damned”) |
| **Emotion**             | Grieved by sin, hopeful for repentance                      | Angry glee, contempt, schadenfreude                           |
| **Authority claimed**   | Limited, delegated, accountable to Scripture and community  | God-like finality and superiority                             |
| **Outcome sought**      | Restoration if possible (Gal 6:1; Matt 18:15–17)            | Exclusion, shaming, or destruction                            |
| **Relation to self**    | “There but for the grace of God go I”                       | “Thank you that I am not like other men” (Luke 18:11)         |

Discernment is a form of love. It says, “This path leads to death; I want you to live.” The appetite to condemn is a form of pride and often cruelty. It says, “I need you to be worse than me so I can feel safe and righteous.”

### 3. Why the distinction matters
- If “Judge not” meant “moral judgments never matter,” then preaching, church discipline, parental correction, doctrinal boundaries, and even calling a friend to repentance would all be forbidden. That would collapse the moral universe into relativism.
- Conversely, if we ignore the “Judge not” warning, we become the very Pharisees Jesus denounced: quick to load burdens on others while exempting ourselves, eager to expose rather than restore.
- The verse is therefore about *measure* and *motive*. The same moral standard must be applied to ourselves first, and the goal must be mercy and restoration whenever possible (“forgive, and you will be forgiven”).

### 4. Practical outworking
- You can say, “What you are doing is objectively wrong and harmful,” without adding, “Therefore you are trash and beyond redemption.”
- You can hold someone accountable in a church, marriage, or friendship while still hoping for and working toward their repentance rather than their ruin.
- You can vote, set boundaries, protect the vulnerable, and name evil publicly while refusing the secret pleasure of seeing the wicked suffer for its own sake.

In short:  
**Discernment** is the sober, humble recognition of moral reality for the sake of good.  
**The appetite to condemn** is the proud, vindictive urge to play God and destroy.

Luke 6:37 kills the second while leaving the first not only intact but strengthened, because only those who have first judged themselves are safe to help others. The command is not “never evaluate,” but “evaluate with the same merciless scrutiny you would want applied to your own hidden sins, and always leave room for the mercy you yourself desperately need.”
