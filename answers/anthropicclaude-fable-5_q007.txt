The strongest evidence that Luke 6:37 is not moral relativism comes from the verse itself: it ends with a command to *forgive*. Forgiveness is incoherent without judgment. You cannot forgive what you have not first discerned to be wrong. If moral evaluations never mattered, there would be nothing to forgive—only things to overlook or redescribe. So the verse presupposes the very discernment it is sometimes taken to abolish. The question is what kind of judging it forbids.

**The structure of the saying gives a clue.** It moves from *krinein* (to judge) to *katadikazein* (to condemn, to pronounce sentence against). The escalation suggests the target: not the act of moral perception, but the act of rendering a verdict—taking the seat of the final judge and pronouncing on a person's worth, foreclosing their story. Discernment says, "what you did was wrong." Condemnation says, "and that is what you *are*, and that is the end of the matter." The first evaluates an act; the second disposes of a soul.

**Context confirms this.** A few verses later Jesus tells the parable of the mote and the beam—and notably, the punchline is not "leave the mote alone." It is: first remove the beam from your own eye, *then* you will see clearly to remove the mote from your brother's. Moral correction is the assumed endpoint. What is condemned is the sequence—judging others as a substitute for self-examination—and the blindness that results. Elsewhere the same Jesus says "judge with righteous judgment" (John 7:24) and "by their fruits ye shall know them" (Matthew 7:16), the latter coming immediately after the parallel "judge not" passage. Whatever "judge not" means, it cannot mean "make no moral distinctions," or the surrounding teaching collapses.

**The deeper distinction is one of appetite.** Discernment and condemnation can reach the same verdict but come from opposite hungers. Condemnation is *pleasurable*. It manufactures innocence by contrast: every fault I locate in you is a small certificate of my own righteousness. This is why gossip is delicious and confrontation is dreadful—gossip is condemnation enjoyed at a safe distance, while genuine moral discernment implicates the discerner. The person who truly sees a friend's addiction or cruelty clearly is usually grieved by it, because seeing it clearly means seeing the friend, the circumstances, and one's own complicity or resemblance. The appetite to condemn wants the verdict; discernment wants the person back.

Several practical tests fall out of this:

- **Direction of attention.** Discernment looks at the deed in order to understand it; condemnation looks at the deed in order to be done with the person. One opens a future ("this can be repaired, repented of, restored"); the other closes it.
- **Self-inclusion.** Discernment uses a standard the judge accepts for himself—hence "with what measure ye mete, it shall be measured to you again." This is not a threat against having standards; it is a warning that standards are reflexive. The condemning person always assumes an exemption.
- **Epistemic humility.** Discernment knows what it cannot see: the heart, the history, the ending of the story. It judges acts provisionally and persons not at all. Condemnation claims a God's-eye finality that no human evidence can support.
- **Cost.** Discernment is willing to be present afterward—to help remove the mote, to bear the awkwardness of restoration. Condemnation is cheap; it walks away satisfied.

**Why the reflexive warning—"ye shall be judged"—matters.** One reading is simply divine recompense. But there is also a psychological truth in it: the habit of condemnation builds the inner courtroom in which one eventually stands oneself. A person who relates to others through verdicts comes to experience their own failures as verdicts—merciless, final, identity-defining. The forgiving person, by contrast, lives in a moral world where wrongdoing is real *and* survivable. Luke 6:37 is, among other things, an instruction about which moral universe you want to inhabit.

So the verse is not an exemption from moral seriousness but a demand for more of it. Relativism is actually the *easy* path—it spares us both judgment and forgiveness. Luke 6:37 asks for the harder thing: to see wrong clearly enough to name it, while refusing the satisfaction of the gavel; to hold the verdict on the act and release the verdict on the person. Condemnation is judgment in the service of the self. Discernment is judgment in the service of love—and only the second can end in forgiveness, which is where the verse insists on ending.
