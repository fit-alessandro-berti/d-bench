The posture required here is **a sustained double attention**: outward toward the broken brother with restorative intent, and inward toward the self with sober suspicion. None of the three terms can be detached without the whole ethic collapsing into either cruelty or indifference.

### 1. Restoration (not exposure)
The verb *katartizō*—used here for “restore”—describes the mending of a net, the setting of a dislocated joint, the equipping of a vessel for use. It assumes the person is still *usable*, still part of the fabric, and that the community possesses the skill and patience to make him whole again. The moral posture is thus **craftsman-like**: careful, knowledgeable, committed to function rather than display. It rules out the moralism that wants to freeze the fault as evidence of irredeemable character, and it rules out the easy tolerance that leaves the fracture untreated.

### 2. Meekness (not weakness)
*Prautēs* is not gentleness as passivity but as **power held in readiness, not deployed**. It is the posture of someone capable of force (the “spiritual” person has discernment, authority, leverage) who chooses to approach the wound without adding new bruising. In the logic of this verse, meekness is the only atmosphere in which restoration is physiologically possible: a cornered animal cannot be stitched; a shamed soul cannot be knit. The moral posture here is calibrated strength—enough to enter the danger, not so much as to overpower the person’s agency under God.

### 3. Self-suspicion (not self-congratulation)
“Considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted” installs a **standing suspicion of one’s own stability**. The corrector is forbidden the clean hands of the observer. This is not a psychological ploy to manufacture humility, but a recognition that *the same physics that broke him tug at you*. Moral gravity is shared; therefore, superiority is an optical illusion. The posture is thus **non-identical solidarity**: you are not *in* his fault, but you are not *outside* the conditions that produce it. This suspicion keeps the restorer leaning, not standing; listening, not pronouncing.

---

### Why the triad?
These three are kept together because each corrects the excesses of the others:

- **Restoration without meekness** becomes spiritual surgery without anesthetic— technically successful, relationally fatal.  
- **Meekness without restoration** becomes mere sympathy that refuses to unsettle the status quo; it comforts but abandons the *katartismos* mandate.  
- **Both without self-suspicion** convert the whole operation into a performance of moral superiority, where the “spiritual” person secures his identity by fixing the “less spiritual” one.

Conversely, **self-suspicion alone** paralyzes into silence; **meekness alone** sentimentalizes the fault; **restoration alone** engineers compliance without heart-change.

Together they produce what we might call **penitent strength**: the capacity to enter another’s failure without worshipping it, to oppose it without humiliating it, and to labor without believing yourself the main actor. The text therefore sketches a moral stance that is simultaneously *engaged* and *unsettled*—the only stance from which a cracked thing can be held without being crushed, and from which the holder can walk away without being broken.
