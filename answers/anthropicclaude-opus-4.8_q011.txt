The movement from "cease to do evil" to "learn to do well" carries real weight, and your instinct to press on the word "learn" is the right one.

**The two are not the same act.**

Ceasing evil is, in a sense, a stopping—a withdrawal of the hand. But "learn to do well" introduces something the first phrase doesn't: *acquisition*. You don't learn to stop; you simply stop. But you do learn to do good, which means goodness here is treated as a skill, a discipline, something gained over time through practice. The text refuses to let righteousness be merely the *absence* of wrongdoing.

**Why this matters morally.**

If goodness were only restraint, then a person who harmed no one would be counted righteous. But the prophet immediately fills the space after "learn" with active verbs: *seek* judgment, *relieve* the oppressed, *judge* the fatherless, *plead* for the widow. None of these is a "stopping." Each one requires you to move *toward* someone—to take up the cause of those who cannot defend themselves. The fatherless and the widow are the classic biblical figures of social powerlessness; they have no patron, no advocate. To "plead for the widow" is to insert yourself into a situation where you gain nothing.

So the text suggests that to merely refrain from oppression is not yet goodness—it is only the clearing of the ground. Real goodness is shown by what you build on that cleared ground.

**On "learn."**

The choice of a verb of education implies that justice does not come naturally or all at once. It is habituated, like Aristotle's claim that we become just by doing just acts. The recently-washed conscience is not yet a practiced one. You have to *practice* relieving the oppressed before it becomes who you are.

**A caution worth holding.**

One could read this as a strict sequence—first cease evil, *then* learn good, as if morality were a ladder. But notice the structure may also be saying these are two faces of one turning: the same washing that removes evil is meant to flow into active love. The "ceasing" without the "learning" would leave a person clean but inert—which the prophets elsewhere treat as its own kind of failure (the empty ritual purity Isaiah condemns just verses earlier, vv. 11–15).

**So, to your question directly:** the text comes down on the side of goodness as *a practiced form of love and justice*, not mere restraint. Restraint is the threshold; the house itself is built by seeking, relieving, judging, and pleading—the labor of turning toward the vulnerable.
