# The Heart as Storehouse: Speech in Matthew 12:34–35

## The Central Metaphor: *Thēsauros*

The Greek word translated "treasure" is **θησαυρός** (*thēsauros*)—a treasury, storehouse, or repository. Jesus is not speaking of isolated moments of virtue or vice but of **accumulated moral inventory**. The heart is pictured as a warehouse whose stock has been gathered over time, through a thousand small depositings of thought, desire, and habit.

From this image, speech becomes the act of **withdrawal**. What comes out of the mouth is what was already inside, reached for because it was nearest to hand.

## Speech as Symptom, Choice, and Revelation

Your question rightly senses that these categories overlap in the passage. Let me take them in turn.

### 1. Symptom

Jesus frames speech diagnostically: *"out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh."* The preposition "out of" (ἐκ) is causal—speech flows **from** an internal condition the way a cough flows from a disease. The Pharisees' slander (they had just attributed Christ's miracles to Beelzebub, v. 24) is not their real problem; it is evidence of a deeper one. Words betray the state of the inner man the way a fever betrays infection.

### 2. Choice

Yet Jesus holds them morally accountable—"how can ye, being evil, speak good things?"—which would be incoherent if speech were merely involuntary symptom. The good man **"bringeth forth"** (προφέρει) good things; the verb implies deliberate production, not reflex. Each word is an act. The treasure did not fill itself; it was stocked, and it is now being spent. Choice operates both in the long accumulation and in the momentary utterance.

### 3. Revelation

Because speech is drawn from the storehouse, it **discloses** what the storehouse contains—often contents the speaker would prefer to hide. This is why Jesus, just two verses later, says "by thy words thou shalt be justified, and by thy words thou shalt be condemned" (v. 37). Words are admissible evidence in the final court because they are unfalsifiable testimony about the heart.

## All Three, Because the Heart Is Integrated

The passage resists a tidy separation because Jesus' anthropology resists it. In Scripture the **heart** (לֵב / καρδία) is not merely emotion but the unified center of thought, will, and affection. So:

- Words are **symptoms** because the heart's condition produces them.
- Words are **choices** because the heart's condition was shaped by prior choices, and each utterance is a fresh act of the will.
- Words are **revelations** because the heart, though hidden, cannot help publishing itself through the tongue.

## A Sobering Implication

The passage dismantles the common self-defense, *"I didn't mean it."* Jesus' logic runs the other way: you said it *because* you meant it—somewhere, in some deposit of the heart you may not have inspected lately. Careless words (v. 36) are especially revealing precisely because carelessness bypasses the editor. What escapes unguarded is what was actually stored.

The remedy, then, is not tighter speech but a restocked heart—a point Jesus makes elsewhere when he says the tree must be made good before the fruit can be (Matt. 12:33). Speech reform without heart reform is only more careful hypocrisy; it changes the display window but not the inventory.
