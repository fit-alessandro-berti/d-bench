In this passage, Jesus is asking his followers to deny the "self" that is driven by sinful desires, ego, and self-preservation. This self is often referred to as the "old self" or the "flesh" in Christian theology. It is the part of us that prioritizes our own interests, comfort, and security above all else, often leading to selfishness, pride, and a focus on temporal pleasures.

Denying this self means surrendering our need for control, recognition, and self-aggrandizement. It involves letting go of our attachment to our own strengths, abilities, and accomplishments, and instead, acknowledging our weaknesses and limitations. This denial of self is not about self-annihilation or self-hatred, but rather about surrendering our self-centeredness to God's will and purposes.

This concept can sound liberating to some because it offers freedom from the burden of self-reliance and the pressure to constantly perform and achieve. By denying our old self, we can experience freedom from the weight of our own expectations and the opinions of others. We can find relief from the constant need to prove ourselves and instead, find our identity and worth in Christ.

On the other hand, this concept can sound offensive to others because it challenges the modern values of self-expression, autonomy, and individualism. In today's culture, we are often encouraged to prioritize our own needs, desires, and happiness above all else. The idea of denying ourselves and taking up a cross (a symbol of sacrifice and suffering) can be seen as oppressive, restrictive, or even masochistic.

Moreover, some people may interpret Jesus' words as a call to self-denial in a way that is unhealthy or damaging, such as suppressing one's emotions, desires, or needs. However, this is a misinterpretation of Jesus' teachings. The denial of self that Jesus calls for is not about self-negation, but about self-transformation. It is about surrendering our old, sinful self to be transformed into a new creation, one that is characterized by humility, love, and obedience to God.

Ultimately, the concept of denying oneself is liberating because it allows us to find our true identity and purpose in Christ, rather than in our own strengths, achievements, or self-efforts. By letting go of our old self, we can experience the freedom to live a life that is not driven by selfish desires, but by a desire to follow Jesus and serve others.
