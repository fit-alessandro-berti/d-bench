This is one of the most challenging and radical teachings in the moral tradition. The difficulty in taking it seriously often stems from the clash between its divine logic and our deeply human, self-protective instincts.

Let's break down the layers of that difficulty:

### 1. The Emotional Hurdle (Against Sentimentality)
It is not sentimental in the shallow sense. Sentimentality often involves a pleasant, warm feeling that requires no sacrifice and doesn't threaten our safety or ego. Enemy-love is the opposite. It asks us to generate goodwill toward a source of genuine threat, insult, or harm. It feels emotionally impossible because our nervous systems are wired for self-preservation and tribal loyalty. To love an enemy requires a conscious, willed override of these instinctive reactions—a form of moral discipline, not a pleasant emotion.

### 2. The Strategic Puzzle
On the surface, it seems like terrible strategy. In a zero-sum, competitive world, empowering or blessing an adversary appears to be self-sabotage. However, the passage hints at a different kind of strategy—the "strategy" of imitating God. The text offers a **theological reason**, not a tactical one: "That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven." The aim is not to win a conflict but to transform one's own character to align with a divine nature that is radically impartial ("he maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good").

If there is a "strategy" here, it is the long-game strategy of breaking cycles of hatred. It seeks to disarm enmity not by overpowering it, but by absorbing and transforming it. History shows this can be phenomenally powerful (e.g., figures like Gandhi or King), but its success is never guaranteed in the short term, which is why it requires immense faith.

### 3. The Core: A Deeper Moral Strength
This is where the teaching finds its true footing. Enemy-love is presented not as an effective tactic, but as the very **evidence of a transformed heart**. It is the ultimate test of moral and spiritual maturity because:
- **It is disinterested:** It expects no reward, reciprocity, or even changed behavior from the enemy. The good is done for its own sake, as a reflection of a greater Good.
- **It asserts human dignity:** It refuses to reduce the enemy to *only* their worst actions. It acknowledges that they, too, stand under the same sun and rain—participants in a shared human condition.
- **It is an act of freedom:** To love an enemy is to free oneself from being defined and poisoned by the hatred of another. It is to claim one's own moral agency.

### Conclusion: Why It's Hard to Take Seriously
We struggle to take it seriously because we instinctively evaluate it by worldly standards of fairness, justice, and effectiveness. By those standards, it can seem naive, weak, or self-destructive.

But the teaching operates on a different plane. It is not a description of how humans naturally are, but a prescription for how they could become. It is "serious" in the way a summit of a mountain is serious—it's not where most of life happens, but it is the highest point toward which the path climbs. Its difficulty isn't a flaw in the teaching; it is the very proof of its depth and the radical re-orientation it demands.

It asks: What if your highest moral strength was measured not by how you treat those who treat you well, but by how you respond to those who do not?
