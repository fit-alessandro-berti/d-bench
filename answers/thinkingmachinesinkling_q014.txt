The short answer is: **primarily that treasure *trains* (directs) the heart—though it also *reveals* it.** In this passage Jesus is not offering a neutral observation about human psychology; he is giving a reason for an command. The logic runs like this: **choose where to put your treasure, because your heart will inevitably follow.**

Here is how the pieces fit together.

### 1. The grammar points forward, not just backward
The key clause is future-tense in Greek:  
*“ὅπου γάρ ἐστιν ὁ θησαυρὸς σου, ἐκεῖ ἔσται καὶ ἡ καρδία σου”*  
—“For where your treasure is, **there your heart will be also**” (ἔσται = “it will be”).

Jesus has just issued two imperatives:
- **Negative:** “Do not store up treasures on earth…”  
- **Positive:** “But store up treasures for yourselves in heaven…”

Then he adds “For…” (γάρ). That “for” explains *why* you should obey. The reasoning is consequential: **if you deposit your wealth in heaven, your heart will end up there too.** The heart is the follower; the treasure is the leader. So the text is being used prescriptively—Jesus tells his disciples to relocate their investments precisely so that their affections will be relocated with them.

That said, the statement works in reverse as well. If someone finds their heart hopelessly entangled with earthly security, that reveals where their treasure already lies. So it is **diagnostic** (it exposes the heart) and **formative** (it shapes the heart). But in Matthew 6:19–21 the emphasis is on the latter: **you can steer your heart by steering your wallet, your time, and your security.**

### 2. Possession, attention, and love become one loop
| Element | What it means in the text | How it binds to the others |
|---|---|---|
| **Possession** (θησαυρός, “treasure”) | Not just money, but whatever we accumulate, protect, and treat as our security—wealth, status, reputation, even religious merit. | We do not merely “have” treasure; we *identify* with it. “My” treasure becomes an extension of “me.” |
| **Attention** | Earthly treasure demands vigilance: moth, rust, thieves. Heavenly treasure demands a different kind of watchfulness—prayer, generosity, obedience. | Attention is love in action. Whatever we guard, calculate, worry about, or rejoice in reveals where our mental and emotional energy is anchored. Jesus says the heart lives where the guard-duty is. |
| **Love / Heart** (καρδία) | In biblical usage, the heart is not just emotion but the center of will, desire, and commitment—the “control center” of the person. | Because the heart is the seat of desire, it naturally gravitates toward what it perceives as valuable. Once possession and attention are directed somewhere, love congeals around that object. |

Thus the three are tied in a **causal chain that becomes a cycle**:
1. You **possess** (invest, store up) in a particular place.
2. You therefore **attend** to it (protect it, think about it, fear losing it).
3. Your **heart** (love, will, identity) migrates to that location to be with what you value.

Jesus warns that this loop can be destructive: if the treasure is on earth, the heart becomes earthbound—anxious, grasping, divided. But he also offers the remedy: **break the loop by deliberately placing treasure in heaven** (through generosity, mercy, and seeking God’s kingdom). Then the heart is pulled upward, freed from corruption and theft, and aligned with God.

### Bottom line
The text does not say *only* “check your heart to see where your treasure is.” It says, **“Put your treasure in heaven, for then your heart will be there too.”** Treasure trains the heart—shaping what we love by determining what we hold dear, guard, and pursue. At the same time, the state of our treasure never lies; it always reveals where our heart truly resides. In Jesus’ teaching, possession, attention, and love are not separate compartments; they are a single movement of the soul, and he calls his disciples to direct that movement heavenward.
