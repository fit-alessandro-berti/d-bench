In these two verses Paul is calling believers not to repay wrong with wrong or to meet violence on violence, but rather to meet an enemy’s need with unexpected kindness.  Victory over evil, as Paul sees it, is not a matter of military or legal triumph but a matter of moral and spiritual conquest:  

1.  “Be not overcome of evil, but overcome evil with good.”  
    ­–  To be “overcome of evil” is to allow someone else’s malice to set your heart’s agenda.  To “overcome evil with good” is to break that cycle by refusing retaliation and instead doing what is genuinely helpful.

2.  Feeding the hungry enemy and giving drink to the thirsty enemy (v. 20)  
    ­–  These actions are concrete symbols of love and generosity.  Paul borrows the language of Proverbs (“heap coals of fire on his head”) to suggest that such kindness brings conviction and shame to the one who meant you harm, not by trickery but by the undeniable power of grace.

Why this is genuinely strong rather than weak or manipulative:

•  It takes more resolve to bless one’s enemy than to curse or fight back.  You must master your own anger, fears, and desire for revenge.  
•  It refuses to “win” by inflicting pain or asserting dominance; instead it draws the opponent out of hostility into conscience, gratitude, perhaps even repentance.  
•  It trusts God’s justice rather than demanding your own.  You are not abdicating power but relinquishing the right to pay evil back in its own currency.  
•  It mirrors Christ’s own example (see Romans 5:8; 1 Peter 2:23): the strongest moral witness is sacrificial love under pressure.

Far from endorsing passivity or emotional manipulation, Paul is issuing a radical call to courageous, counter-cultural love.  In his view, the only way to “win” definitively over evil is to replace its currency—retaliation—with the higher currency of self-giving goodness.
