Amos is not criticizing an overly liturgical religion that needs a little more social concern added to it. He is declaring the worship **false in its very meaning** because Israel’s public life contradicts the God being worshiped.

The surrounding accusations are crucial. Israel tramples the poor, takes bribes, perverts justice in the courts, exploits debtors, and silences those who tell the truth (Amos 2:6–8; 5:7, 10–12). Yet the people continue festivals, sacrifices, and music as though these guaranteed God’s favor.

The fraud has several dimensions:

- **Substitution:** ritual observance is used in place of obedience.
- **Compartmentalization:** God is honored ceremonially while his demands are denied economically and judicially.
- **Presumption:** worshipers assume sacrifices can secure divine approval despite unrepented injustice.
- **Concealment and legitimation:** splendid worship gives a sacred appearance to a society built on exploitation.
- **Self-deception:** Israel expects “the day of the Lord” to vindicate it, when that day will instead expose and judge it (Amos 5:18–20).

This explains the violent language: “I hate,” “I will not accept,” “I will not hear.” God refuses to be treated as a deity who can be flattered, bribed, or managed through ceremony. “Your feasts” also creates distance: observances outwardly directed to God have become merely *theirs*, because they no longer express covenant faithfulness.

“Judgment” in the KJV here means **justice**—especially honest courts and right social order. “Righteousness” is not only private virtue but faithful conduct within the community. The image of waters and a mighty stream calls for justice that is public, continuous, and life-giving, not occasional charity appended to religious ceremony.

So the passage does not reject worship as such. It rejects worship that has become a lie. The issue is not achieving a better balance between worship and ethics; it is restoring their integrity. Genuine worship of the just God must take social form in justice and righteousness. Without that, the songs and sacrifices do not merely fall short—they bear false witness about God.
