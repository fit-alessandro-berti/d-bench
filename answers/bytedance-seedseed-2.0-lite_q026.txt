First, this passage directly ties speech to the heart’s hidden moral stockpile (framed as a "treasure") through the core claim that speech cannot originate anywhere outside of what is stored in that internal storehouse. Jesus uses the metaphor of a treasure chest to describe the heart as an accumulated stockpile of core moral orientations, desires, beliefs, and commitments—this is the hidden internal foundation that no one can see on its own. He argues that it is impossible for someone with an evil, corrupted heart stockpile to produce genuinely good speech, just as you cannot pull a valuable legitimate treasure from a chest full of stolen, corrupted goods. Speech is only ever a reflection of what has been stored up in the hidden heart.

This passage frames words as all three: symptoms, revelations, and choices, rooted in the context of Jesus rebuking the Pharisees (who had falsely accused him of working through Satan’s power to cast out demons):
1. **Symptoms**: Speech is an inevitable, reliable symptom of the heart’s state. Jesus states that "out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh"—the overflow of a full stockpile naturally spills out in speech, just as excess liquid flows from an overfilled container. Evil words are not random accidents; they are the natural, unavoidable symptom of a corrupted hidden heart storehouse, and good words are the same symptom of a good heart.
2. **Revelations**: Speech uncovers the hidden stockpile that would otherwise remain secret. The Pharisees presented themselves as pious, morally upright religious leaders to the public, but their hostile, false accusations of Jesus revealed their actual hidden evil treasure. Their words pulled back the curtain on the internal state they had concealed, exposing what their public performance had hidden.
3. **Choices**: Speech is also a moral choice for which people are accountable. Jesus does not dismiss the Pharisees’ evil words as an involuntary reflex; he rebukes them as a "generation of vipers," holding them responsible for what they have chosen to say. The passage frames people as active agents who "bringeth forth" things from their heart’s treasure—they draw intentionally from their internal stockpile to speak, rather than just spilling over accidentally. Their choices are constrained by what they have stored up in their hearts, but they are still morally culpable for those words, as the words flow from the corrupted identity they themselves cultivated.
