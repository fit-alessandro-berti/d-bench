The passage in **Luke 6:37** is not a blanket prohibition against all forms of moral judgment but rather a warning against hypocritical, self-righteous, or harsh condemnation. The distinction lies in the difference between **discernment** (a necessary and virtuous ability to distinguish right from wrong) and the **appetite to condemn** (a sinful desire to judge others harshly, often from a place of pride or malice). Let’s explore this difference in depth.

---

### **1. The Context of Jesus’ Teaching**
Jesus’ words in Luke 6:37 come in the midst of the **Sermon on the Plain** (Luke 6:17-49), where He contrasts the way of His kingdom with the ways of the world. He is not abolishing moral discernment but rather exposing the **hypocrisy** of those who judge others while ignoring their own sins (cf. **Matthew 7:1-5**, where He says, *"Why do you see the speck in your brother’s eye but not the log in your own?"*).

Key themes in this passage:
- **Mercy over legalism** – Jesus prioritizes compassion and forgiveness over rigid, self-righteous judgment.
- **Reciprocity** – The measure you use to judge others will be the measure used against you (Luke 6:38).
- **Grace as a foundation** – Forgiveness is tied to our own reception of God’s forgiveness (cf. **Matthew 6:12, 14-15**).

Thus, Jesus is not saying moral judgments are irrelevant but that **our posture toward others must be humble, merciful, and self-aware**.

---

### **2. Discernment vs. Condemnation**
#### **A. Discernment (Biblical Judgment)**
Discernment is the **ability to recognize good and evil, truth and falsehood**, as commanded in Scripture:
- **1 Corinthians 2:15** – *"The spiritual person judges all things, but is himself to be judged by no one."*
- **1 John 4:1** – *"Beloved, do not believe every spirit, but test the spirits to see whether they are from God."*
- **Proverbs 14:8** – *"The wisdom of the prudent is to discern his way."*

**Characteristics of Godly Discernment:**
- **Rooted in truth** – Based on God’s Word, not personal bias.
- **Motivated by love** – Seeks restoration, not destruction (Galatians 6:1).
- **Self-examining** – Acknowledges one’s own sinfulness (Matthew 7:5).
- **Humble** – Does not assume superiority over others.

**Example:** A parent correcting a child out of love, or a church leader addressing sin in a spirit of restoration (Matthew 18:15-17).

#### **B. Condemnation (Ungodly Judgment)**
Condemnation is a **harsh, self-righteous, or malicious** form of judgment that:
- **Lacks mercy** – It seeks to punish rather than restore.
- **Is hypocritical** – It ignores one’s own faults while magnifying others’ (Romans 2:1).
- **Is motivated by pride** – It assumes moral superiority.
- **Is often based on appearances** – It judges the heart without true knowledge (1 Samuel 16:7).

**Examples:**
- The Pharisees who condemned Jesus for healing on the Sabbath (Luke 6:6-11).
- A person who publicly shames a struggling believer without seeking to help them.
- Social media "cancel culture," where people are permanently labeled and ostracized without opportunity for redemption.

**Jesus’ Own Example:**
- He **discerned** the hypocrisy of the Pharisees (Matthew 23) but did so with sorrow, not glee.
- He **condemned sin** (e.g., the money changers in the temple, John 2:13-16) but **showed mercy to sinners** (e.g., the woman caught in adultery, John 8:1-11).

---

### **3. Why the Distinction Matters**
If we interpret **Luke 6:37** as a prohibition on all moral judgment, we fall into **moral relativism**—the idea that nothing is truly right or wrong. But Scripture clearly teaches that **some things are sinful and must be named as such** (e.g., 1 Corinthians 5:1-5, where Paul rebukes a man in sexual immorality).

However, if we **only condemn and never show mercy**, we become like the elder brother in the **Parable of the Prodigal Son** (Luke 15:25-32)—self-righteous and lacking in love.

**The Balance:**
- **We must discern right from wrong** (to protect, guide, and correct).
- **But we must not condemn with a heart of pride or hatred** (for that is God’s role alone, Romans 12:19).

As **James 4:12** says:
> *"There is only one lawgiver and judge, he who is able to save and to destroy. But who are you to judge your neighbor?"*

This does not mean we never identify sin, but that we do so **with humility, recognizing that we too are sinners in need of grace**.

---

### **4. Practical Application**
**How do we practice discernment without condemnation?**
1. **Examine your own heart first** (Matthew 7:5) – Are you addressing this sin out of love or pride?
2. **Speak the truth in love** (Ephesians 4:15) – Correction should aim for restoration, not punishment.
3. **Leave room for grace** – Remember that God’s mercy is greater than our judgments (James 2:13).
4. **Pray for wisdom** – Discernment is a gift from God (James 1:5).
5. **Avoid gossip and slander** – Condemnation often spreads through ungodly speech (Ephesians 4:29).

**Example from Scripture:**
- In **Galatians 6:1**, Paul says:
  > *"Brothers, if anyone is caught in any transgression, you who are spiritual should restore him in a spirit of gentleness. Keep watch on yourself, lest you too be tempted."*
  Here, correction is **necessary** (discernment), but it must be done **gently and humbly** (not with condemnation).

---

### **Conclusion: The Heart Behind the Judgment**
The key difference between **discernment** and **condemnation** is the **motivation and posture of the heart**:
- **Discernment** says: *"This is wrong, and I must address it with love and truth."*
- **Condemnation** says: *"This person is wrong, and they deserve my scorn."*

Jesus calls us to **truth without hypocrisy, and justice without cruelty**. We are to **hate sin but love the sinner**, just as He did. As **Micah 6:8** puts it:
> *"He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the Lord require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God?"*

Thus, **moral judgments do matter**—but they must be made with **humility, mercy, and a deep awareness of our own need for grace**.
