The trust described in **Proverbs 3:5–6** is a **radical, wholehearted reliance on God** that involves both **humble self-distrust** and **active acknowledgment of His lordship**—without descending into **anti-intellectualism**. Let’s break this down:

---

### **1. The Nature of the Trust Being Asked For**
The Hebrew word for **"trust"** (*בָּטַח, *batach***) conveys **bold confidence, security, and refuge**—like a child clinging to a father’s hand in a storm. This is not passive fatalism but an **engaged, personal dependence** on God’s character, wisdom, and sovereignty. Key aspects:
- **With all thine heart** (*בְּכָל-לִבֶּךָ*): Total, undivided commitment (cf. Deut 6:5). This is not partial trust but a **posture of surrender** in every area of life.
- **Lean not unto thine own understanding** (*וְאֶל-בִּינָתְךָ אַל-תִּשָּׁעֵן*): The warning is against **self-sufficiency**—the assumption that human reasoning alone can navigate life’s complexities. It does *not* reject reason itself (Proverbs elsewhere exalts wisdom, e.g., 2:6; 4:7) but **idolatry of human autonomy**.

---

### **2. Humble Self-Distrust vs. Anti-Intellectualism**
#### **A. Humble Self-Distrust (Biblical Trust)**
This is the recognition that:
- **Human understanding is limited** (1 Cor 1:25; Isa 55:8–9). We see "through a glass darkly" (1 Cor 13:12).
- **Sin distorts our judgment** (Jer 17:9; Prov 14:12). Even our best reasoning can be tainted by pride, fear, or bias.
- **God’s ways are higher** (Prov 3:7: "Fear the Lord and turn away from evil"). Trusting Him means **submitting our intellect to His revelation**, not abandoning reason but **redirecting it**.

**Examples:**
- A scientist trusts God’s order in creation while studying it (Ps 19:1–2; Col 1:17).
- A parent seeks wisdom in Scripture *and* counselors when raising children (Prov 11:14; 15:22).
- A leader makes plans but holds them loosely, praying "If the Lord wills" (James 4:15).

#### **B. Anti-Intellectualism (A Distortion)**
This would be:
- **Rejecting reason altogether** (e.g., dismissing science, history, or critical thinking as "worldly").
- **Spiritualizing faith into irrationality** (e.g., "Just pray, don’t think").
- **Misapplying "lean not on your own understanding"** to justify laziness or superstition.

**Contrast with Scripture:**
- Proverbs **celebrates wisdom** (Prov 8:1–36; 9:10).
- Jesus commands **loving God with all your mind** (Matt 22:37).
- Paul **reasons** with audiences (Acts 17:2–4; 18:4) and expects believers to **test everything** (1 Thess 5:21).

---
### **3. "Acknowledge Him in All Your Ways" (v. 6)**
This is the **practical outworking of trust**:
- **Hebrew *יָדַע* (*yada*)**: To know *relationally*, not just intellectually. It implies **recognition of God’s presence and authority** in every decision (cf. Ps 1:6; Amos 3:2).
- **"All thy ways"**: No compartmentalization—finances, relationships, ethics, vocation, etc. (Col 3:17).
- **Result**: **"He shall direct thy paths"** (*יַיְשֵׁר אֹרְחֹתֶיךָ*). The verb *yashar* means to **make straight, smooth, or right** (like a path through a wilderness, Isa 40:3). This is **divine guidance**, not necessarily easy paths but *right* ones.

---
### **4. Key Distinctions**
| **Humble Self-Distrust** | **Anti-Intellectualism** |
|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| Recognizes human limits but uses reason as a gift from God. | Rejects reason as inherently corrupt. |
| Submits intellect to Scripture, not to blind emotion. | Elevates feeling or "spiritual vibes" over truth. |
| Seeks wisdom from God *and* wise counsel (Prov 13:20). | Isolates from input, claiming "God told me" without testing. |
| Acknowledges mystery (Deut 29:29) but pursues understanding. | Dismisses questions as "lack of faith." |
| Like a student trusting a teacher’s expertise while still studying. | Like a student refusing to read the textbook, calling it "distraction." |

---
### **5. Practical Application**
- **In Decision-Making**:
  - *Not*: "Don’t think, just feel."
  - *But*: "Pray, study Scripture, seek counsel, then trust God with the outcome" (Prov 15:22; Phil 4:6–7).
- **In Suffering**:
  - *Not*: "God’s ways are illogical, so stop asking why."
  - *But*: "I don’t understand, but I trust His character" (Job 13:15; Hab 3:17–19).
- **In Intellectual Pursuits**:
  - *Not*: "Faith means checking your brain at the door."
  - *But*: "All truth is God’s truth" (Augustine). Science, philosophy, and art can glorify Him when submitted to His lordship.

---
### **Conclusion**
Proverbs 3:5–6 calls for a **trust that is intellectually engaged but spiritually dependent**—a **humble agnosticism** about our own sufficiency paired with **confident knowledge** of God’s goodness. It’s the difference between:
- A **child** who trusts a parent’s guidance while still learning to walk (humble trust).
- A **rebel** who refuses to walk at all, calling it "legalism" (anti-intellectualism).

As Kierkegaard put it: *"Faith is the highest passion in a human being. Many in every generation may not come that far, but none comes further."* This is the **reasoned, worshipful trust** the proverb invites us into.
