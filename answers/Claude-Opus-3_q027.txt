The parable of the Pharisee and the tax collector is a powerful lesson about the nature of true righteousness and humility before God. Several factors contribute to the condemnation of the Pharisee's prayer:

1. Pride and self-righteousness: The Pharisee's prayer is full of self-congratulation and a sense of moral superiority. He views himself as exceptionally virtuous compared to others, taking pride in his religious practices and moral standing. This prideful attitude is contrary to the humility God desires.

2. Comparison and contempt: The Pharisee looks down on others, especially the tax collector, considering them inferior. He defines his righteousness in comparison to the perceived shortcomings of others. This contemptuous comparison reveals a lack of love and compassion for his fellow man.

3. Self-justification: The Pharisee's prayer is an attempt to justify himself before God based on his own merits and actions. He relies on his fasting, tithing, and avoidance of obvious sins as the basis for his righteousness, rather than humbly acknowledging his need for God's mercy and grace. 

4. Misplaced focus: The Pharisee's prayer is centered on himself and his own virtues, rather than on God and his glory. His religious practices have become a source of pride and self-display, obscuring the true purpose of worship and prayer.

In contrast, the tax collector's posture is treated as spiritually truer because:

1. Humility: The tax collector acknowledges his sinfulness and unworthiness before God. He does not try to justify himself but instead appeals to God's mercy. This humble recognition of his need for divine grace aligns with the contrite heart that God values (Psalm 51:17).

2. Authentic repentance: The tax collector's prayer is a genuine expression of repentance and sorrow for his sins. He beats his breast, a sign of deep remorse and self-reproach. This authentic contrition is more pleasing to God than the Pharisee's self-righteous display.

3. Dependence on God: By asking for mercy, the tax collector acknowledges his complete dependence on God's grace for salvation. He does not trust in his own merits but instead relies entirely on God's compassion and forgiveness.

The parable teaches that true righteousness is not about outward displays of piety or comparing oneself to others. Rather, it is a matter of the heart - a humble, repentant spirit that recognizes its need for God's mercy and grace. The tax collector's prayer exemplifies the attitude that God desires and rewards, while the Pharisee's self-exalting prayer reveals the dangers of spiritual pride and self-righteousness.
