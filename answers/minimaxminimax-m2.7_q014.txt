**Matthew 6:19‑21 in its literary and theological setting**

In the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus is urging his listeners to reorder their priorities.  
The three verses form a tiny “mini‑sermon” that moves from an *imperative* (“lay not up… but lay up…”) to a *descriptive* statement (“for where your treasure is, there will your heart be also”).  

The key words are:

| Greek term | English rendering | Core idea |
|------------|-------------------|-----------|
| **θησαυρός** (*thesauros*) | “treasure” (a store, a hoard) | Anything we consider valuable enough to **accumulate** and **protect**. |
| **καρδία** (*kardia*) | “heart” | The inner centre of a person—will, affections, desires, moral orientation. |
| **ἀγάπη** (*agápē*) | “love” (often implied) | The deepest affection that drives us to act. |

The passage does **not** isolate any one of these three ideas; it weaves them together.

---

## 1. Does the text say treasure **reveals** the heart, or **trains** it?

Both readings are present, and the verse itself suggests a **bidirectional relationship**.

### a) Treasure as a *revelation* of the heart

> “For **where your treasure is, there will your heart be also.**”

The wording is declarative. It tells us that the *location* of our treasure tells us where our heart already is. In other words:

* What we **value** (the treasure we have chosen to keep) is a mirror of what we **love**.
* Because the heart is the seat of desire, it naturally follows the objects of our devotion.

From this angle, the text functions **diagnostically**: if a person’s treasure is “on earth” (wealth, status, possessions that rust and thieves steal), that person’s heart is earthly‑oriented; if the treasure is “in heaven,” the heart is oriented heavenward.

### b) Treasure as a *training* of the heart

> “Lay **up** for yourselves treasures **in heaven**…”

The imperative “lay up” is a call to **active, intentional behavior**. By deliberately **choosing** to store treasure in heaven (righteous deeds, generosity, devotion to God), a person **shapes** the heart over time.

* The act of **storing** heavenly treasure is a habit‑forming practice. Repeatedly giving, serving, and praying rewires the affections.
* In this sense the text functions **prescriptively**: the heart can be **trained** by the very treasures we decide to accumulate.

### Synthesis

The two readings are complementary:

| Direction | What the text says |
|-----------|--------------------|
| **Heart → Treasure** | The heart’s current orientation tells us where we have placed our treasure. |
| **Treasure → Heart** | By deliberately placing treasure in heaven we cause the heart to follow and be reshaped. |

Thus the verse simultaneously **reveals** (diagnostic) and **trains** (prescriptive). The causal arrow points both ways, forming a feedback loop: what we love determines what we store; what we store deepens what we love.

---

## 2. How do **possession**, **attention**, and **love** become tied together here?

The three concepts interlock in a simple logical chain:

1. **Possession (what we have)**  
   * “Lay not up for yourselves treasures **on earth**… but **lay up** for yourselves treasures **in heaven**.”  
   * Possession is not neutral; it is the *object* we decide to keep, protect, or accumulate.

2. **Attention (where we focus)**  
   * The act of “laying up” implies **mental and material effort**: we think about, plan, and safeguard the treasure.  
   * Attention is the **means** by which possession becomes a *priority*. The heart’s focus follows the object that receives our mental energy.

3. **Love (the affection behind the focus)**  
   * “For **where your treasure is, there will your heart be also**.”  
   * Love is the *internal engine* that drives both possession and attention. If we love something, we will possess it (or seek to) and give it our attention.

### A practical illustration

| Scenario | Possession | Attention | Love | Resulting heart |
|----------|------------|-----------|------|-----------------|
| **Earthly focus** | Money, designer clothes, luxury items | Checking the stock market, scrolling ads, worrying about loss | “I love comfort and status” | Heart anchored in material security |
| **Heavenly focus** | Righteous deeds, generosity, prayer | Serving the poor, investing time in Scripture, remembering the needy | “I love God and neighbor” | Heart oriented toward the kingdom of God |

The text warns that **earthly treasure is unstable** (moth, rust, thieves) and thus creates a **fragile heart**. In contrast, **heavenly treasure endures** and **produces a resilient, loving heart**.

---

## 3. Theological depth: what the “heart” means in Scripture

In biblical anthropology the *heart* (Hebrew *lev*, Greek *kardia*) is the **center of the whole person**:

* **Cognitive** – what we think, understand, and consider important (Proverbs 23:7).
* **Volitional** – what we choose and pursue (Jeremiah 17:10).
* **Affective** – what we love, desire, and delight in (Psalm 37:4).

When Jesus says “where your treasure is, there will your heart be also,” he is describing how these three dimensions converge on the same object.

---

## 4. Practical takeaways (without prescribing a particular doctrinal stance)

1. **Self‑examination** – Ask: *Where am I actively “laying up” treasure?* The answer will pinpoint the current orientation of your heart.
2. **Intentional re‑orientation** – If the treasure is earthly, deliberately shift accumulation toward “heavenly” goods: generosity, compassion, spiritual disciplines. Over time, the heart will follow.
3. **Guarding against “dual loyalty”** – The next verse (Matthew 6:24) makes clear that we cannot serve both God and wealth. The treasure‑heart link shows why that is true: serving two masters splits affection.
4. **Understanding the “feedback loop”** – Recognize that the heart‑treasure relationship is not static. Changing our **behavior** (possession, attention) can gradually **re‑shape our loves**.

---

## 5. Summary

* **Matthew 6:19‑21** tells us that **treasure both reveals and trains the heart**. It is a diagnostic tool (what we already love shows up in what we store) and a prescriptive tool (what we deliberately store can reshape what we love).
* **Possession, attention, and love** are three facets of a single dynamic: we **possess** what we love, we **attend** to what we love, and the act of **possessing** and **attending** reinforces the love.
* The passage frames the Christian life as a continual **re‑allocation of treasure**—from the perishable (earthly) to the imperishable (heavenly)—and in doing so, **re‑orients the heart** toward lasting values.
