1. The point of the oracle  

Jeremiah 17:1-10 is a single unit.  
• vv. 1-4 – Judah’s sin is “engraved with an iron stylus”; judgment is coming.  
• vv. 5-8 – Two alternatives are set side by side:  
 a) the person who “trusts in man” (cursed, like a bush in the desert)  
 b) the person who “trusts in the LORD” (blessed, like a tree by water).  
• vv. 9-10 – a diagnosis that explains why people so often make the wrong choice: the human heart is untrustworthy, but the LORD can read it and will reward accordingly.

So vv. 9-10 are not an isolated pronouncement about anthropology; they are the punch-line of a sermon whose aim is to move the hearer from self-confidence to God-confidence.

2. “Deceitful” and “desperately sick”  

Hebrew:  
‘āqōb = crooked, slippery, deceitful (same root as the name “Jacob”).  
’ānāš = incurable, desperately sick.

The verse therefore does not merely say the heart is “evil”; it says it is,
1) bent—it twists information to suit itself, and  
2) terminally ill—unable to cure itself.

3. Pessimistic or realistic?  

Pessimism would mean, “there is no hope.” Jeremiah does not say that. The verses that follow (17:12-14) include a prayer for healing and salvation, and later Jeremiah promises a “new covenant” in which the law will be written on the heart (31:31-34). The passage is therefore:

• Realistic about self-deception: the heart habitually mis-reads its own motives.  
• Sober about human nature: left to itself the heart cannot fix this condition.  
• Hopeful: God both understands the heart and offers to re-shape it.

4. How then can we speak about motives?

a. With humility  
Instead of “My motives are pure,” say “As far as I can tell my motive is X, but I ask God to expose anything I am not seeing” (cf. Psalm 139:23-24).

b. By inviting divine scrutiny  
The rhetorical question “Who can know it?” is answered immediately: “I, the LORD, search the heart.” Prayer that consciously opens the motives to God’s inspection is part of the remedy.

c. By checking the “fruit”  
Verse 10 says God repays “according to the fruit of his doings.” Motives eventually externalise themselves. If the observable fruit contradicts the professed motive, the heart has been lying.

d. Through community feedback  
Because I may be blind to my own heart, I can allow trusted brothers and sisters to question me (Heb 3:13). Their vantage-point can expose self-deception I cannot see.

e. By holding our self-assessments loosely  
James 4:13-16 warns against confident self-projection. Similarly, we state motives tentatively, aware that later evidence (including inner conviction) may show us we were fooling ourselves.

f. In the language of dependence, not autonomy  
Jeremiah contrasts “trust in man” with “trust in the LORD.” When we discuss motives, we do well to frame them in dependence: “I want to serve the Lord here, and I’m asking him to purify the motives I can’t read.”

5. Summary  

Jeremiah 17:9-10 is not cynical fatalism but a theological realism: left to itself the human heart ﻿will﻿ deceive even its owner; yet God both perceives that heart perfectly and offers to heal it. Therefore, when speaking about motives we should do so:

• Humbly – recognising our limited self-knowledge.  
• Prayerfully – inviting God’s searching light.  
• Observantly – paying attention to the outward fruit.  
• Communally – allowing others to test what we cannot see.

In short, the text pushes us away from naïve confidence in our own introspection and toward an honest, God-dependent transparency that can finally lead to a heart renewed rather than simply exposed.
