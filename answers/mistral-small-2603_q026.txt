This passage from **Matthew 12:34–35** is a profound reflection on the relationship between speech, the heart, and moral character. Jesus’ words here suggest that **words are not merely superficial expressions but are deeply connected to the hidden moral condition of the heart**. Let’s break this down:

### **1. Words as Symptoms (Revelations of the Heart)**
Jesus calls the Pharisees a **"generation of vipers"**—a metaphor for their deceitful and corrupt nature. He then asks:
> *"How can ye, being evil, speak good things?"*

This implies that **speech is a symptom of the heart’s condition**. Just as a tree is known by its fruit (Matt. 7:16–20), a person’s words reveal their true moral state. If someone’s heart is filled with evil, their speech will inevitably expose it. Conversely, a good person’s words will reflect goodness.

- **Example:** If someone habitually lies, gossips, or curses, it suggests a heart that is not aligned with truth or love.
- **Contrast:** A person who speaks with kindness, wisdom, and truth likely has a heart that values those virtues.

Thus, **words are symptoms**—they reveal what is already inside.

### **2. Words as Choices (Active Expressions of the Heart)**
Jesus also frames speech as a **deliberate choice** tied to moral stockpiles:
> *"A good man out of the good treasure of the heart bringeth forth good things: and an evil man out of the evil treasure bringeth forth evil things."*

Here, the heart is described as a **"treasure"** (Greek: *thēsauros*), a storehouse of moral and spiritual resources. What a person **chooses to treasure** (love, truth, righteousness vs. deceit, malice, impurity) will determine what they **bring forth in speech**.

- **Active Process:** The heart is not passive; it **stockpiles** moral deposits (through habits, thoughts, and choices), and speech is the **outworking** of those deposits.
- **Responsibility:** Since speech flows from the heart’s treasure, **guarding what we store in our hearts** (Phil. 4:8) is crucial for controlling our words.

Thus, **words are choices**—they are the active expression of what we have cultivated in our hearts.

### **3. Words as Revelations (Unmasking the Hidden Self)**
Jesus’ critique of the Pharisees suggests that **their words were not just symptoms but revelations of their true selves**:
> *"For out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh."*

- **Hidden Motives Exposed:** Their hypocritical speech (e.g., condemning Jesus while plotting murder, Matt. 12:14) revealed that their hearts were full of **self-righteousness, malice, and unbelief**.
- **Divine Judgment:** Jesus’ words imply that **God sees beyond outward piety**—He judges the heart, and speech is one of the clearest indicators of its state.

Thus, **words are revelations**—they unmask what is hidden in the heart, both to others and to God.

### **Conclusion: Words as Symptoms, Choices, and Revelations**
This passage suggests that **speech is all three**:
1. **Symptoms** – Words reveal the true condition of the heart (like a thermometer measuring internal temperature).
2. **Choices** – Words are the active outflow of what we have stored in our hearts (like a farmer harvesting what he has sown).
3. **Revelations** – Words expose hidden motives, exposing what is truly inside (like a mirror reflecting the soul).

**Practical Implications:**
- **Self-Examination:** If our words are often negative, critical, or unkind, we must ask: *What am I storing in my heart?*
- **Guard Your Heart:** Proverbs 4:23 says, *"Keep thy heart with all diligence, for out of it are the issues of life."* What we **consume** (media, conversations, influences) shapes what we **speak**.
- **Transformation:** True change begins in the heart (Ezek. 36:26), which then transforms speech (Col. 4:6).

In short, **Jesus is teaching that speech is not just noise—it is a window into the soul.** What we say (and how we say it) reveals whether our hearts are aligned with God’s goodness or corrupted by evil.
