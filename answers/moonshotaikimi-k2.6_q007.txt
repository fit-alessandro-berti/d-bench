The command in Luke 6:37 is not a ban on moral perception; it is a surgery on the *soul*. Jesus does not say, “Discern not,” “Evaluate not,” or “Distinguish not.” He says, “Judge not... condemn not,” using the language of the courtroom (*krinō*, to try or sentence; *katadikazō*, to declare guilty and punish). The difference between this and the claim that “moral judgments never matter” is the difference between a physician diagnosing a disease and a executioner presiding over a hanging. One seeks healing; the other savors power.

Here is how to understand the distinction between the discernment Jesus requires and the condemnation He forbids.

### 1. The Object: Action vs. Person
**Discernment** judges *acts* and *fruits*. It looks at a deed, a policy, a habit, or a doctrine and names it—truth or falsehood, justice or cruelty, wisdom or folly. It is directed outward at reality, not downward at a person’s ultimate worth. Scripture repeatedly commands this kind of discrimination: “Test the spirits” (1 John 4:1), “By their fruits you will know them” (Matthew 7:20), “Do not judge by appearances, but judge with right judgment” (John 7:24).

**The appetite to condemn** judges *persons* as categories. It moves from “That is wrong” to “You are trash.” It assigns a final status to a soul, writing someone off as irredeemable, unworthy of patience, or beyond the reach of grace. It treats the sinner as a fixed specimen rather than a human being in motion toward either repentance or ruin.

### 2. The Posture: Humility vs. Superiority
True **discernment** is haunted by self-knowledge. It proceeds from the awareness that “there but for the grace of God go I,” and often from the memory of one’s own failures. It can be severe about an evil while remaining tender toward the evildoer, because it knows the complexity of temptation and the darkness in its own heart.

**Condemnation** feeds on superiority. It is not satisfied merely to recognize wrong; it needs to feel *elevated* by the recognition. The appetite to condemn is a kind of spiritual gluttony—it consumes the faults of others to fill an emptiness in the self. It delights in the downfall of the wicked not because justice is served, but because the downfall confirms the judge’s own imagined innocence.

### 3. The Purpose: Restoration vs. Exclusion
**Discernment** is instrumental. It aims at repair, protection, or truth-telling. A parent disciplining a child, a friend warning a friend, a society punishing a crime to restore order—these are acts of moral judgment, but their telos is the good of the one being judged or the community being defended. Even when it must exclude (as in removing an unrepentant member from fellowship, per 1 Corinthians 5), the goal is salvific shock, not annihilation.

**Condemnation** is terminal. It wants the other person *gone*, silenced, or permanently diminished. It uses moral language as a weapon of exclusion rather than a tool of restoration. Where discernment says, “This must stop for your sake and ours,” condemnation says, “You are finished.”

### 4. The Standard: Mercy vs. Strictness
Jesus embeds His command in a principle of reciprocity: “Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven... with the measure you use, it will be measured to you” (Luke 6:37–38). This reveals that the issue is the *standard* by which we hold others. 

**Discernment** applies truth with the lubricant of mercy. It remembers that it does not possess the full ledger of another’s life—their wounds, their ignorance, their hidden struggles. It leaves room for context, for repentance, for the mystery of another’s conscience before God.

**Condemnation** applies a rigid, abstract standard, often while making exceptions for itself. It is the “speck in your brother’s eye” while ignoring the “plank” in one’s own (Matthew 7:3). It demands perfection from others and grants indulgence to self.

### 5. The Authority: Delegated vs. Usurped
Ultimately, **discernment** acknowledges that final judgment belongs to God. It speaks with the authority of truth, but not with the authority of the Last Day. It can say, “This is evil,” but it cannot say, “You are damned.” It knows the difference between a creature and the Creator, between a witness and the Judge.

**The appetite to condemn** usurps God’s throne. It takes a seat in the divine tribunal and hands down eternal verdicts. It forgets that the same law it wields against others is a mirror in which it will one day see itself.

### Why This Matters
To say that “moral judgments never matter” is to surrender the capacity for justice, love, and truth. A world without discernment cannot protect the vulnerable, correct the erring, or even choose friendship over treachery. But a world ruled by the appetite to condemn becomes a hell of mutual surveillance, where every fault is a weapon and no one is safe from the mob.

Jesus is not asking us to become morally blind. He is asking us to become *merciful*. The eye that discerns without the heart that forgives becomes a predator. But the heart that forgives without the eye that discerns becomes a fool. The command in Luke 6:37 holds these in tension: see clearly, but sentence slowly; know the truth, but speak it with the trembling knowledge that you, too, will need mercy when the books are opened.
