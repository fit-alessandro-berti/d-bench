### 1. **Does the Text Say Treasure *Reveals* the Heart, or That Treasure *Trains* the Heart?**
The text doesn't explicitly use words like "reveal" or "train," but its logic and structure lean toward both ideas, with a stronger emphasis on treasure *directing* or *shaping* the heart (which aligns more closely with "training" than pure revelation). Let's break it down:

- **The Core Statement**: The passage culminates in verse 21: "For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also." This is presented as a causal or consequential principle ("For" indicates it's explaining the previous verses). It implies that the location of your "treasure" (what you value, invest in, or accumulate) determines the location of your "heart" (your deepest affections, desires, loyalties, or spiritual focus).
  
- **Treasure as a *Revealer* of the Heart**:
  - On one level, the verse functions diagnostically: Your treasures expose or reveal what's already in your heart. If you're hoarding earthly wealth (e.g., money, possessions, status), it shows your heart is oriented toward temporary, corruptible things. Conversely, if you're investing in "heavenly" treasures (often interpreted as spiritual acts like charity, faith, prayer, or moral living), it reveals a heart aligned with eternal values.
  - This "revealing" aspect is supported by the broader context in Matthew 6 (e.g., warnings against hypocrisy and materialism). Many commentators (e.g., Augustine or modern scholars like N.T. Wright) see it as a mirror: Treasures don't *create* your heart's state but make it visible. It's like saying, "Show me your bank account or daily priorities, and I'll show you what you truly love."

- **Treasure as a *Trainer* of the Heart**:
  - The passage more actively suggests that treasure *influences* or *trains* the heart by directing it. The imperatives ("Lay not up... But lay up...") are commands to *choose* where to store your treasures, implying agency: By deliberately pursuing heavenly treasures, you can redirect your heart away from earthly distractions. Over time, this shapes your affections—like training a muscle through repeated action.
  - This aligns with the idea that habits form character. For example, if you "lay up" treasures on earth, your heart gets pulled there (trained toward anxiety over loss, as moths/rust/thieves threaten them). But heavenly treasures are secure, so investing there trains the heart toward peace and eternal focus. Thinkers like John Chrysostom (an early Church Father) emphasized this formative aspect, noting that consistent choices "educate" the soul.

- **Which Is Primary?** In the text's logic, it's not an either/or—treasure both reveals *and* trains the heart, but the emphasis is on training through choice. The verse isn't passive (e.g., "Your heart shows your treasure"); it's directional ("Where your treasure is, *there will* your heart be"). It's a warning and invitation: Don't let earthly treasures pull your heart down; actively build heavenly ones to elevate it. This fits Jesus' Sermon on the Mount style, which often blends diagnosis with prescription.

If we had to pick one, "trains" captures the proactive tone better, as the passage urges behavioral change to influence the heart. However, interpretations vary—some (e.g., Reformed theologians) stress revelation (treasures as symptoms of inner state), while others (e.g., Wesleyan or Catholic views) highlight training (treasures as means of spiritual growth).

### 2. **How Do Possession, Attention, and Love Become Tied Together Here?**
The passage weaves possession (what you own or accumulate), attention (what you focus on or invest energy in), and love (your heart's deepest attachment) into an interconnected web. They're not separate; each feeds into the others, creating a cycle that either anchors you to earth or lifts you to heaven. Here's how they tie together based on the text:

- **Possession as the Starting Point**:
  - "Treasures" refer to what you possess or "lay up" (accumulate). On earth, these are tangible things like wealth or goods, vulnerable to decay ("moth and rust doth corrupt") or loss ("thieves break through and steal"). In heaven, they're intangible and eternal (e.g., virtues, relationships with God, acts of kindness—things not subject to corruption).
  - Possession isn't neutral; it implies ownership and investment. The text assumes that what you possess claims a piece of you—it's not just "stuff," but something you've chosen to value and protect.

- **Attention as the Bridge**:
  - Possessing something naturally draws your *attention*—your mental, emotional, and practical focus. Earthly treasures demand vigilance (guarding against moths, rust, thieves), which consumes energy and creates anxiety (see the surrounding verses in Matthew 6:25–34 about worry). Heavenly treasures, being secure, free up attention for higher pursuits.
  - This ties into biblical psychology: The "heart" in Hebrew thought (lev) isn't just emotion but the center of will, thought, and decision-making. Attention is how possession influences the heart—whatever you treasure gets your time, thoughts, and efforts, reinforcing its hold.

- **Love as the Endpoint (the Heart)**:
  - The heart represents love or ultimate allegiance ("there will your heart be also"). Possession and attention culminate in love: What you own and attend to, you end up loving (or becoming attached to). It's a psychological and spiritual truth—humans bond with what they invest in (a concept echoed in modern psychology, like the "endowment effect" where we overvalue what we own).
  - The tie is cyclical and reinforcing:
    - **Possession → Attention → Love**: Buying a luxury car (possession) leads to maintaining it (attention), which fosters affection (love) for it over, say, charitable giving.
    - **Love → Attention → Possession**: If your heart loves status, you'll attend to opportunities for wealth and end up possessing more of it.
    - In the passage, this cycle is why earthly treasures are dangerous—they corrupt the heart by tying love to the temporary. Heavenly treasures break the cycle, tying love to the eternal.

- **Overall Integration**: These elements aren't isolated; they're presented as a unified dynamic. The text uses metaphors of decay and theft to show how earthly ties lead to heartbreak (literally, a corrupted heart), while heavenly ties lead to security. It's a holistic view: Your possessions shape your attention, which trains your loves, revealing (and forming) your true self. This echoes other biblical ideas, like idolatry (loving created things over the Creator) or Jesus' teaching that "No one can serve two masters" (Matthew 6:24, right after this passage).
