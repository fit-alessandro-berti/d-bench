Amos 5:21–24 is one of the most abrasive passages in the Hebrew Bible. The rejection of worship is not a call for "better balance" because, in the eyes of the prophet, the worship being offered was not merely insufficient—it was **toxic**.

The sharp rejection stems from a specific type of moral and spiritual fraud that can be broken down into three categories:

### 1. Ritual as an Alibi (Moral Fraud)
The Israelites were using religious precision to mask social oppression. The historical context of Amos’s ministry was a period of great economic prosperity for the elite, built on the systemic exploitation of the poor (Amos 2:6-7, 5:11). 

By bringing "fat beasts" and performing "solemn assemblies," the people were attempting to use ritual as a legalistic cover-up. They believed that if the technical requirements of the Law (sacrifices and songs) were met, God was obligated to bless them. Amos exposes this as **moral fraud**: you cannot use the smoke of an altar to screen the sight of a starving neighbor from God’s eyes. To God, the ritual was not "unbalanced"; it was a lie.

### 2. The "Stench" of Hypocrisy (Spiritual Fraud)
The language used is visceral: "I hate," "I despise," "I will not smell." In the ancient world, the "soothing aroma" of a sacrifice was believed to please the deity. Amos reverses this imagery. 

The spiritual fraud here is the belief that **sacred space can be separated from common space.** The Israelites believed they could be holy in the Temple while being predatory in the marketplace. God’s rejection signifies that worship is not a vacuum. If the worshiper’s hands are stained with injustice, the "melody of the viols" is repurposed by God’s ears into "noise." The rejection is sharp because God refuses to be a "customer" of a religious industry that ignores His character.

### 3. The Definition of True Piety
The passage rejects worship because the people had mistaken the *means* for the *end*. 
*   **The Means:** Feast days, burnt offerings, songs.
*   **The End:** A society defined by *mishpat* (justice/judgment) and *tsedeqah* (righteousness).

When Amos calls for justice to "run down as waters," he is using the imagery of a perennial stream (a "mighty stream") in a desert land. Rituals are seasonal and episodic (feast days), but justice is meant to be a constant, life-sustaining flow. By rejecting the temporary "peaks" of religious fervor, God is demanding a "floor" of basic human decency.

### Conclusion: Why not balance?
The reason Amos doesn't ask for "balance" is that **false worship is worse than no worship.** 

If you provide a 50/50 balance of "piety" and "oppression," the piety simply serves to soothe the conscience of the oppressor, making the oppression more sustainable. God rejects the worship entirely to strip away the people's false sense of security. He is telling them that until "justice rolls down," their religion is not merely "unbalanced"—it is an offense to the very Being they claim to honor.
