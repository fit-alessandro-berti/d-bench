Isaiah couples “way” with “thoughts” because true repentance must be whole. In biblical Hebrew, “way” (*derek*) refers to one’s external conduct—visible habits, life-direction, and patterns of behavior—while “thoughts” (*machashabah*) points to the hidden world of intentions, imaginations, plans, and inner values. By demanding that both be forsaken, the prophet removes all hiding places. It is possible to reform outward actions while inwardly still treasuring sin, or to nurture pious thoughts without changing one’s lifestyle. God’s call surgically addresses both the fruit and the root.

**Outward conduct** – Forsaking one’s “way” means a tangible break with sinful behaviors. The thief stops stealing, the liar speaks truth, the adulterous relationship ends. Repentance becomes visible and communal, not merely a private sentiment.

**Inward imagination** – Forsaking “thoughts” goes further, targeting the workshop where sin is first conceived (cf. James 1:14–15). Grievance, lust, covetous scheming, and proud fantasies are renounced. This implies that God claims the mind and imagination, not just the hands and mouth. Biblical repentance is a renovation of the heart’s deepest designs (Romans 12:2), a turning of the entire inner life back toward God.

**The possibility of mercy** – Immediately after the call to forsake both way and thoughts comes the promise: “he will have mercy… he will abundantly pardon.” The pairing therefore magnifies grace. If repentance must be this radical, the assurance of pardon must be equally radical. No sin is too embedded, no thought-life too twisted, to exhaust God’s willingness to forgive. The double forsaking faces the double depth of human brokenness, and the “abundant” pardon reveals a mercy deeper than both. Mercy is not earned by the completeness of our turning; rather, the invitation to turn completely is itself evidence that mercy already stands ready. God’s nearness in the opening line (“while he is near”) signals that the window of grace is open, and the only thing that closes it is refusal to seek, call, and turn.
