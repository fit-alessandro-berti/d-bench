The core moral danger highlighted in Matthew 6:1 lies not in the visibility of the act itself, but in the **intention** behind it—the craving to be seen and admired by others.

Jesus is addressing a specific spiritual pitfall: when doing good (almsgiving, prayer, fasting) is performed primarily for social approval, the act becomes corrupted. The problem is the **motivation**, which shifts from genuine compassion or devotion to self-promotion.

To break it down:

1.  **Visibility as a Test, Not an Evil:** Public acts of goodness are not inherently wrong. They can inspire, teach, and serve the community. The danger is that visibility becomes a temptation, turning a vertical relationship (with God) into a horizontal one (seeking status before people).

2.  **The Craving for Recognition:** This is the heart of the warning. If the primary reward sought is human praise, then that praise *is* the full reward—the act is "paid in full" by the applause, and it carries no eternal or spiritual merit. The craving empties the deed of its transcendent value.

3.  **The Contrast in "Reward":** The verse sets up two mutually exclusive reward systems:
    *   **Reward of men:** Temporary social recognition, reputation, and honor.
    *   **Reward of your Father:** The internal, spiritual reward of communion with God and alignment with His kingdom values.

In essence, the **craving to be seen** transforms an act of love or worship into a transaction for social capital. The good deed is hijacked by the ego. The verse urges a shift in audience—from the crowd to God—so that the motive remains pure and the reward becomes one of relationship rather than reputation.

**So, to answer directly:** The problem is the craving to be seen. Visibility is the context in which this dangerous motive can flourish, but it is not the root cause. The root cause is the human heart seeking its glory from others rather than from God.
