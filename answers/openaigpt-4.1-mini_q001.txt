The passage from Genesis 4:6–7 presents a richly nuanced picture of moral agency, particularly in the context of Cain’s emotional and ethical state.

### Key elements in the text:
- **Cain’s emotional state:** "Why art thou wroth? and why is thy countenance fallen?"  
  This suggests Cain is experiencing anger and a downcast demeanor—an internal emotional struggle.
  
- **Moral performance and acceptance:** "If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?"  
  This implies a clear opportunity for positive moral action—there is a path of doing “well” that leads to acceptance, indicating choice and responsibility.

- **Warning about sin:** "if thou doest not well, sin lieth at the door."  
  Here, sin is personified as “lying at the door” — an external force patiently waiting, ready to seize an opportune moment. 

- **Desire and mastery:** "And unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him."  
  This suggests a relationship of control or domination: sin desires to overpower or consume Cain, but Cain is given the empowering promise that he *can* "rule over" sin.

---

### How is evil pictured?

1. **Temptation as an external, lurking force:**  
   The phrase “sin lieth at the door” conveys evil as something external to Cain that watches and waits—it is not merely an internal impulse or habit but an active, anticipatory presence, like a predator at the threshold. This emphasizes the *danger of succumbing* and the fact that evil seeks to invade or possess the individual.

2. **Evil as desire seeking to dominate:**  
   Sin’s “desire” for Cain indicates that sin is almost like a rival claimant to Cain’s agency, wanting to take control. This suggests evil is not simply a passive background condition but a force with intention and will.

3. **Evil as subject to human mastery:**  
   The promise “thou shalt rule over him” places a heavy responsibility and capacity on Cain—it presents sin as something that can be controlled, dominated, or resisted by human will.

---

### Responsibility of the person:

- **Moral choice is real and consequential:** The text shows that Cain has a decision to make in how he responds to his feelings and situation—whether to "do well" or not.
- **Awareness of danger:** Cain is warned explicitly about the presence of sin at his door, implying an awareness of moral challenges is necessary.
- **Capacity for mastery:** Cain bears responsibility because he is promised he can rule over sin, which presupposes the ability to resist or overcome evil.

---

### Summary:

- **Evil is presented primarily as an external **temptation or adversary**, lying in wait to take advantage. It is depicted like a beast at the door, desiring to dominate the person but not inherently uncontrollable.  
- **Cain’s moral agency involves vigilance, choice, and the potential to overcome this evil.** Responsibility is emphasized: Cain is accountable for his response, with the possibility and power to master the sinful impulse or temptation.

This framing balances the reality of temptation and the seriousness of sin with the hopeful, empowering prospect of human dominion over evil impulses or forces.
