 Enemy-love, as described in Matthew 5:44-45, is a challenging concept for many people because it goes against our natural instincts and cultural norms. It is not an easy command to follow, and there are several reasons why it may be difficult to take seriously.

First, enemy-love may be seen as sentimental or unrealistic. In a world where conflict and violence are all too common, the idea of loving those who hate us or harm us may seem naive or impractical. It may be difficult to see how such love could make a difference in the face of real harm or injustice.

Second, enemy-love may be seen as strategic or manipulative. Some people may view enemy-love as a way to gain an advantage over their enemies or to turn them into friends. While there may be some strategic benefits to loving one's enemies, this is not the primary reason for doing so according to Jesus' teachings.

Third, enemy-love is a deeper form of moral strength. It requires a level of self-control, empathy, and compassion that goes beyond our natural reactions to harm or injustice. It requires us to see our enemies as human beings deserving of love and respect, even if they have harmed us or acted against our interests. It is a challenging call to rise above our emotions and to act in a way that reflects the love and compassion of God.

Ultimately, enemy-love is a call to imitate the love and forgiveness of God, who sends sun and rain on the evil and the good alike. It is a reminder that every person, no matter how much they may have harmed us or acted against us, is deserving of love and respect as a child of God. It is a difficult command, but one that can bring us closer to God and to the deepest truths of the human spirit.
