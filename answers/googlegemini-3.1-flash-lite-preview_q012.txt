Jeremiah 17:9–10 sits at the intersection of existential psychology and theology. To address your question, one must look at how these verses function both as a diagnosis of the human condition and as a framework for personal accountability.

### 1. Pessimism vs. Realism
Whether this passage is "pessimistic" or "realistic" depends largely on whether you view human nature as inherently neutral or inherently fragmented.

*   **The Argument for Pessimism:** The language—"deceitful above all things" (*aqob*) and "desperately wicked" (*anash*)—is undeniably harsh. It suggests that the human inner world is not just prone to error, but actively prone to masquerading self-interest as virtue. If one believes that human beings are fundamentally "good" and only corrupted by external circumstances, this passage is profoundly pessimistic, as it locates the problem *inside* the individual rather than *outside* in society.
*   **The Argument for Realism:** From a psychological perspective, this is a sophisticated observation of the "motivated reasoning" that modern cognitive science confirms. The "heart" in Hebraic thought represents the seat of the intellect, will, and emotions. By calling it "deceitful," Jeremiah is noting that the human ego is a master of compartmentalization and rationalization. If you define "realism" as an honest appraisal of human cognitive bias, this passage is highly empirical: it asserts that we are the last people to understand our own true motivations.

Ultimately, it is **pessimistic about human self-sufficiency** but **realistic about human psychology.** It suggests that because we have a conflict of interest regarding our own actions, we are structurally incapable of being objective judges of our own moral state.

### 2. How to talk about motives when the heart is an unreliable witness
If the heart is an "unreliable witness," addressing one’s own motives requires a shift from **introspection** (looking inward, which Jeremiah warns is corrupted) to **external evaluation**. Here is how one might approach the problem of motive in light of this passage:

#### A. Shift from "Why?" to "What?"
Because we lie to ourselves about *why* we do things, asking "What were my true motives?" often leads to endless, recursive self-delusion. A more helpful approach is to focus on the **"fruit of the doings"** mentioned in verse 10. Instead of asking, "Was I being pure in my actions?" (the intent), look at the consequences: "What did this action produce in the lives of those around me?" If the fruit is bitterness, pride, or division, the initial motive—regardless of how "pure" it felt—was likely tainted.

#### B. The Need for "Community Auditing"
If the heart is deceitful, it needs external data. Blind spots, by definition, cannot be seen by the one who has them. Talking about motives requires a community of people who have permission to speak hard truths. We cannot "know" our hearts, but we can allow trusted others to hold up a mirror. Accountability is not just about keeping rules; it is about having someone else interpret your own behavior for you.

#### C. Intellectual Humility regarding Moral Certainty
If one accepts Jeremiah’s diagnostic, the immediate result should be a profound reduction in moral self-righteousness. If I know that my own heart is capable of deceiving me, I should be wary of my own certainty. When someone says, "I am doing this because I am right and they are wrong," the person who understands Jeremiah 17 will counter with: *"Is that true, or is this my own bias convincing me that my heart is pure?"*

#### D. The "Trial" of the Reins
Verse 10 introduces the "Lord" as the searcher of the heart. In a non-religious context, this can be translated as an appeal to **transcendent standards or objective realities.** To get past your own deceit, you must subject your motives to a standard that exists outside of your own desires (a code of ethics, a commitment to truth, or the wellbeing of others). You treat your motives as a defendant in a trial, rather than as the judge of the case.

### Summary
Jeremiah 17:9–10 suggests that **subjectivity is our default setting.** To talk about motives, we must stop trusting our internal narrative and start looking at the objective markers of our behavior—the "fruit"—while inviting outsiders to pressure-test the stories we tell ourselves. You cannot solve the problem of a deceitful heart by trying to "think harder" about yourself; you solve it by submitting your actions to the light of external truth and community feedback.
