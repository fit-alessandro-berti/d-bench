To understand Luke 6:37, one must distinguish between the **posture of the heart** (condemnation) and the **function of the mind** (discernment).

The passage is frequently misconstrued as a mandate for moral relativism—an instruction to never define an action as right or wrong. However, when read within the broader context of the Sermon on the Plain, it is clear that Jesus is not advocating for the abolition of moral standards, but for the reformation of the moral actor.

Here is an exploration of the difference between moral judgment and the appetite to condemn.

### 1. Discernment vs. Condemnation
The biblical command to avoid "judging" does not preclude the ability to recognize evil; in fact, the Bible elsewhere commands believers to "test everything" (1 Thessalonians 5:21) and to recognize "wolves in sheep’s clothing" (Matthew 7:15). 

The distinction lies in the direction of the energy and the intent of the heart:

*   **Discernment is objective and protective.** It is the intellectual and spiritual capacity to perceive the nature of an action or a choice. To discern is to say, "That action is harmful to the community or the individual." Its purpose is to foster wisdom, set boundaries, and protect the truth. It is a "looking at" a behavior.
*   **Condemnation is subjective and destructive.** To condemn is not just to identify an error; it is to pronounce a final verdict on the *person*. It is an act of "looking down" upon someone. When one harbors an appetite to condemn, they are assuming the role of the ultimate Judge, effectively closing the book on an individual’s capacity for repentance or change.

### 2. The Trap of the "Superiority Complex"
The danger Jesus addresses in Luke 6:37 is the psychological need for moral superiority. Often, the appetite to condemn is a defensive mechanism. We condemn others to amplify the distance between our perceived "virtue" and their "vice." 

By passing final judgment ("that person is irredeemable," or "they are beneath me"), we freeze that person in time. We ignore the reality that humans are dynamic, capable of growth, and desperately in need of the same mercy we hope to receive. 

**Moral judgment without condemnation** admits: "This act is wrong, but I recognize my own capacity for darkness, and I treat this person as a fellow traveler in need of correction."
**Condemnation** claims: "I am the standard of righteousness, and you have fallen short of it."

### 3. The Functional Difference
If moral judgments never mattered, there could be no concept of justice, forgiveness, or growth. 

*   **Forgiveness requires judgment:** One cannot forgive a person if one does not first admit an offense occurred. Forgiveness is the act of acknowledging that a moral boundary was crossed, but choosing not to demand payment for the injury. If moral judgments didn't matter, forgiveness would be meaningless—there would be nothing to forgive.
*   **Discernment is a prerequisite for mercy:** To love someone well, you must discern their struggles. If you lack discernment, you become naive; if you lack mercy, you become a tyrant. 

### 4. The Mirror of the Passage
The promise in the verse—"and ye shall not be judged"—suggests a principle of reciprocity in the human experience. When you position yourself as a condemnatory judge of others, you adopt a framework where life is a courtroom. By doing so, you invite others to view you through that same lens. 

To "not condemn" is to dismantle the courtroom dynamic in your relationships. It does not mean you stop observing the difference between right and wrong; it means you stop using your moral observations as a weapon to dehumanize others.

### Summary
The difference is that **discernment acts as a compass, while condemnation acts as a guillotine.** 

Discernment helps us navigate reality; condemnation seeks to eliminate the opposition. Luke 6:37 is not an invitation to silence our moral conscience; it is an invitation to recognize that our conscience is intended to guide us toward restoration, not to empower us to act as the final, unforgiving judge of our neighbors.
