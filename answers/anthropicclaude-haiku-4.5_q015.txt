# The Sharpness of the Image

The passage's power comes from its **extreme disproportion**: a mote (speck of dust) versus a beam (wooden plank). This isn't subtle—it's absurd, almost comical. The image works because:

- It's **visually impossible** to miss what you're ignoring (you can't see a beam in your own eye while obsessing over specks in others')
- The **reversal is humbling**: the critic becomes the blind one
- It moves from the particular failure to the universal problem—*hypocrisy itself*

## Criticizing Hypocrisy Without Forbidding Correction

The passage doesn't say "never correct anyone." Instead, it distinguishes between:

| **Forbidden** | **Permitted** |
|---|---|
| Correction from a position of moral superiority | Correction from self-awareness |
| Ignoring your own failures while policing others | Addressing your own faults first |
| Assuming you see "clearly" when you don't | Seeking clarity before helping others |

**The key move**: "First cast out the beam...***then*** shalt thou see clearly." 

Moral correction becomes *possible* only after self-examination. The passage assumes you'll eventually help your brother—but from humility, not judgment. You're qualified to help once you've acknowledged your own blindness.

This preserves both **accountability** (moral correction is real) and **humility** (it requires self-knowledge).
