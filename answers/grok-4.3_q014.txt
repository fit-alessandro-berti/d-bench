The text primarily presents **treasure as revealing the heart**, while the surrounding commands imply that treasure also **trains** or directs the heart. The two are not in competition; they are connected.

### The Core Statement

The key line is: “For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also.”

This is not merely a neutral observation. Jesus states it as a spiritual law: the heart (affections, attention, and ultimate allegiance) moves toward whatever a person treats as their treasure. The heart does not remain neutral or independent. It is drawn to and eventually resides with the object of one’s accumulated investment.

### Revelation vs. Training

- **Reveals**: The location of your treasure functions as a diagnostic. What you are actively accumulating (whether money, security, reputation, comfort, or influence) shows what you truly value. You can claim to love God or value eternal things, but if your practical investments are overwhelmingly directed toward earthly security and status, your heart is exposed as being tethered to those things. The heart follows the money and the attention.

- **Trains**: Because Jesus gives this statement in the context of a command (“Lay not up… but lay up…”), He is also teaching that the direction of treasure can be chosen. By deliberately redirecting one’s resources and efforts toward “heaven” (righteousness, generosity, eternal purposes), a person can pull their heart in that direction. The act of investing in something changes what one cares about. This is why the command comes first and the principle follows. Jesus is not just describing reality; He is giving a mechanism for reorienting the heart.

### How Possession, Attention, and Love Become Tied Together

The passage links three things that modern people often try to separate:

1. **Possession** (what you accumulate or invest in)  
2. **Attention** (what occupies your thoughts, energy, and worry)  
3. **Love/Affections** (what your heart ultimately clings to)

Jesus assumes these are not independent. What you possess and protect necessarily receives your attention. What receives sustained attention begins to capture your affections. Over time, your heart comes to rest where your treasure is stored. This is why earthly treasure is dangerous—not just because it can be lost, but because it trains the heart to find its security and joy in things that are corruptible and temporary.

This is why the warning is so sharp. It is not simply “don’t be greedy.” It is a warning that your investments are shaping your soul. Where you put your resources is where your heart will eventually live.

### Summary

The text teaches that treasure both **reveals** and **forms** the heart. The heart follows the treasure, which means that the deliberate choice of where to lay up treasure is one of the primary ways a person directs their affections. Possession and attention are not neutral; they are the mechanisms by which love is either corrupted by earthly things or reoriented toward God.
