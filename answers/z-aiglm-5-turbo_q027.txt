To answer accurately, we must not treat the options provided—false doctrine, pride, comparison, contempt, and virtue as self-display—as mutually exclusive. Rather, in the Pharisee, they form a single, fatal syndrome. However, the primary engine of his condemnation is **pride**, which inevitably births the other sins. 

Here is a breakdown of exactly what condemns the Pharisee, followed by why the tax collector’s posture is deemed spiritually true.

---

### Part 1: What Condemns the Pharisee?

**1. It is not primarily "false doctrine" (in the traditional sense).**
Ironically, the Pharisee’s facts about the Law are largely correct. Fasting and tithing were good, biblically mandated practices (even if he exaggerated them for effect). His condemnation does not rest on theological error regarding *what God commands*, but on a heretical *doctrine of justification*—he believes his moral performance earns him a righteous standing before God.

**2. Pride (The Root)**
Luke introduces the parable by stating Jesus spoke it to those who "trusted in themselves that they were righteous" (v. 9). The Pharisee’s fatal flaw is that his confidence is placed in the wrong object. Instead of trusting in the mercy of God, he trusts in his own moral resume. 

**3. Comparison (The Method)**
The Pharisee does not measure himself against the holiness of God; he measures himself against the sinfulness of other people. His prayer is entirely relative: *"I thank thee, that I am not as other men are..."* By lowering the standard of comparison to "extortioners, adulterers," and the tax collector, he creates a cheap, artificial standard that he can easily surpass. 

**4. Contempt (The Relational Outcome)**
The text explicitly states that this parable is aimed at those who "despised others" (v. 9). The Pharisee’s theology of self-salvation requires him to look down on others. If his righteousness is based on being "better than," then he *must* find people to be worse than. His gratitude to God is actually a disguised weapon used to bludgeon the tax collector. 

**5. Virtue as Self-Display (The Performative Act)**
Luke includes a brilliantly devastating grammatical detail: the Pharisee "stood and prayed thus *with himself*" (v. 11). The Greek phrase (*pros heauton*) can mean he prayed "to himself" or "by himself," but the implication is that his prayer never actually reached God. It was a monologue of self-congratulation. He is using the temple, the act of prayer, and his virtues not to connect with God, but to build a monument to himself. His virtues are not fruits of the Spirit; they are accessories to his ego.

---

### Part 2: Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture "Spiritually Truer"?

The tax collector’s physical posture—standing afar off, refusing to lift his eyes, beating his breast—is treated as spiritually truer because **it is the only posture that accurately corresponds to objective reality.** 

In theology, truth is not just conceptual; it is ontological (it aligns with the way things actually are). The Pharisee’s posture was a lie about who he was and who God is. The tax collector’s posture was the truth, for three reasons:

**1. It accurately assesses the human condition.**
The tax collector does not compare himself to the Pharisee; he looks at himself and simply says, "sinner" (*hamartōlos*). He recognizes that he has fallen short of God's glory. He does not try to negotiate, excuse, or contextualize his sin. His posture of beating his breast was a first-century cultural expression of deep grief, guilt, and unworthiness. It was spiritually true because he was, in fact, guilty.

**2. It accurately assesses the nature of God.**
By standing "afar off" and refusing to lift his eyes to heaven, the tax collector acknowledges the vast, unbridgeable chasm between a holy God and a sinful man. He understands that he has no right to approach God boldly. He recognizes God's absolute purity. 

**3. It accurately assesses the only mechanism of salvation.**
The Pharisee came to God with a list of his achievements; the tax collector came empty-handed, asking for *mercy*. The Greek word he uses is *hilasthēti* (from *hilaskomai*), which is the verb form of "propitiation" or "expiation"—it means to make atonement, to be appeased, or to cover sin. He is essentially saying, "God, let the sacrifice cover *me*, a sinner." 

### The Ultimate Contrast
Jesus declares the tax collector "justified" (declared righteous) rather than the Pharisee. Why? Because the Pharisee came to God as a creditor, demanding payment for his good works; the tax collector came as a bankrupt beggar, asking for a gift. 

The tax collector's posture is "spiritually truer" because it is the exact posture that the Gospel requires of every human being. As Jesus concludes elsewhere, "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven." The Pharisee was too full of himself to receive God's grace; the tax collector was empty enough to hold it.
