Interpretations of Luke 6:37 vary widely across Christian traditions and biblical scholarship, but the verse’s immediate narrative context and historical usage make clear it does not amount to a claim that moral judgments never matter. To unpack this, we can first ground the verse in its original text, then distinguish between the prohibited impulse to condemn and the faithful practice of discernment.

First, the verse appears in Luke’s Sermon on the Plain, and it is immediately followed by a rebuke of hypocrisy: “Why do you see the speck that is in your brother’s eye, but do not notice the log that is in your own eye? How can you say to your brother, ‘Brother, let me take out the speck that is in your eye,’ when you yourself do not see the log that is in your own eye? You hypocrite, first take the log out of your own eye, and then you will see clearly to take out the speck that is in your brother’s eye” (Luke 6:41-42). This follow-up clarifies that the “judging” and “condemning” prohibited in verse 37 is self-serving, hypocritical judgment: the act of applying a harsh, unforgiving standard to others while exempting oneself, to elevate one’s own status rather than uphold collective good. Biblical scholars also note that the original Greek term for “judge” in this verse refers specifically to censorious, punitive condemnation, not all forms of moral assessment.

This distinguishes the verse’s command from the claim that moral judgments never matter. It does not require ignoring harm, rejecting boundaries, or abandoning moral standards entirely. Instead, it demands that any moral assessment be rooted in humility, not pride. This is where the line between discernment and the appetite to condemn becomes clear:
- Discernment is a humble, restorative practice of moral assessment. It requires first examining one’s own flaws before addressing others’ harm, and it prioritizes repair over retribution. For example, discerning that someone has harmed a vulnerable community member would lead to action to protect the harmed person, hold the harmful actor accountable to make amends, and create conditions for their repentance and restoration—all while acknowledging one’s own history of harm and need for forgiveness. This aligns with other biblical calls for community accountability (such as Matthew 18’s instruction to address a peer’s sin privately first, before escalating consequences) that require thoughtful moral judgment to uphold collective safety.
- The appetite to condemn, by contrast, is a self-aggrandizing impulse. It uses moral assessment to elevate one’s own righteousness by punishing or casting out another, with no interest in their restoration or accountability to the same standard one applies to others. This is the hypocritical judgment Luke’s verse prohibits: it prioritizes personal status over the well-being of all members of the community, and ignores the verse’s paired call to practice forgiveness, as one hopes to receive it.

In short, Luke 6:37 does not erase the need for moral judgment; it transforms its purpose. Rather than abandoning moral discernment, it demands that discernment be rooted in humility and a commitment to collective care, not a desire to tear others down to lift oneself up.
