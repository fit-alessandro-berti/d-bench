# The Self That Must Be Denied

## What Kind of "Self" Is Being Denied?

The Greek verb here — *aparneomai* — means to **disown** or **renounce utterly**. It is the same word used later when Peter *denies* Jesus. So this is not a gentle suggestion to be less selfish. It is a radical disowning.

But *which* self?

The context is critical. Just verses earlier, Peter rebuked Jesus for predicting his own suffering and death. Jesus responded by calling Peter "Satan," saying his mind was set on *"the things of men, not the things of God"* (8:33). The "self" being denied is the self that Peter was just embodying:

- **The self-protective self** — the instinct to avoid suffering, preserve comfort, and secure one's own safety at all costs.
- **The self-glorious self** — the ego that needs to be recognized, validated, and placed at the center.
- **The self-determining self** — the insistence on scripting one's own story, on one's own terms, apart from any higher calling.

Notably, Jesus is **not** calling for the annihilation of personhood. The very next verse assumes that you *have* a life (psyche) worth saving. The paradox only works if there is a true self to be found on the other side of the false self being surrendered. You deny the smaller self to find the larger one.

Think of it this way: the self being denied is the **clenched fist** — the self that grips its own life so tightly it strangles it. The self being found is the **open hand**, free to receive and to give.

---

## Why It Sounds Liberating

For many, these words hit like a cool drink of water, and for deeply human reasons:

- **The exhaustion of self-maintenance.** Modern life demands relentless self-promotion, personal branding, image management, and optimization. The ego is a tyrannical master. Being told you can *set it down* is profoundly relieving.
- **Freedom from anxiety.** The self-protective project is inherently anxious — there is always something to lose. Letting go of that project can feel like stepping out of a cage.
- **The paradox rings true.** Most people, on honest reflection, recognize that their most alive moments were not moments of self-gratification, but moments of self-forgetfulness — absorbed in love, in a cause, in wonder, in service.
- **Liberation from the false self.** For people trapped in shame, addiction, or compulsive self-obsession, the invitation to "deny yourself" is not a burden — it is a door. It says: *you are not the prison you've built.*

The saints and mystics across traditions describe this as a kind of death that is actually a birth. Thomas Merton wrote that we are born into a world of alienation and must die to the false self to discover who we actually are.

---

## Why It Sounds Offensive

For others, these words trigger alarm bells — and the reasons are not always shallow:

- **The weaponization of self-denial.** This is the most serious charge. Historically, "deny yourself and take up your cross" has been preached *downward* — told to wives to endure abusive husbands, to slaves to endure masters, to the poor to accept their lot, to marginalized people to stop complaining. In the mouths of the powerful, self-denial for the oppressed became a tool of control. The cross was placed on shoulders that did not choose it.
- **The culture of self-actualization.** Post-Enlightenment thought, and particularly modern psychology, has (rightly, in many ways) identified the repression of the self as a source of neurosis and harm. "Be true to yourself" is not mere narcissism — it was historically a corrective to cultures where identity was wholly imposed by family, class, or institution.
- **It sounds like self-hatred.** To a generation steeped in the language of self-care, self-love, and boundaries, "deny yourself" can sound indistinguishable from self-contempt. The distinction between the *false* self and the *true* self is not always clearly taught.
- **Surrender of autonomy.** To secular ears, "follow me" and "deny yourself" can sound like a demand for uncritical obedience — the dissolution of the independent mind into a collective.
- **The glorification of suffering.** "Take up your cross" has sometimes been twisted into a theology that suffering is inherently virtuous, which can keep people in destructive situations and baptize their pain rather than relieve it.

---

## The Tension, Held Together

The most honest reading holds both responses in tension. Jesus' words are **both** liberating **and** dangerous — liberating when understood as an invitation to shed the ego's tyranny, dangerous when wielded as a weapon to crush the vulnerable.

The key may be in the phrase *"for my sake and the gospel's."* The denial is not self-denial for its own sake, nor for the sake of any human power structure. It is self-denial oriented toward a specific end — the gospel, the good news of God's kingdom. If the "self-denial" being preached does not lead to greater love, freedom, and life, it has been severed from its source.

Jesus lost his life — and in doing so, defeated death itself. The self-denial he offers is not a path to less life, but to *more*. The question it leaves each reader is: **Which self are you clinging to, and what is it costing you?**
