**The text moves from “cease to do evil” to “learn to do well” because mere renunciation of wrongdoing is necessary but incomplete.** True repentance and righteousness require both a turning *from* evil and a deliberate turning *toward* the good, expressed in active justice and care for the vulnerable.

Isaiah 1 opens with a fierce indictment of Judah and Jerusalem. The people continue their sacrifices, assemblies, and prayers, yet God rejects them as empty because their hands are “full of blood” and their society is marked by injustice (vv. 10–15). The call in vv. 16–17 is therefore a summons to genuine repentance that matches the problem: not more ritual, but moral and social transformation.

The structure is deliberate and progressive—a series of imperatives:

- Wash / make clean / put away the evil of your doings / cease to do evil (negative movement: purification and cessation).
- Learn to do well; seek judgment, relieve the oppressed, judge the fatherless, plead for the widow (positive movement: acquisition and practice).

“Cease to do evil” removes the corruption from before God’s eyes. But the text refuses to stop there. “Learn to do well” (Hebrew לִמְד֥וּ הֵיטֵ֛ב, *limdû hêṭêḇ*) uses the verb *lāmad*, which carries the sense of training, becoming accustomed to, or acquiring skill through disciplined practice—not merely deciding or knowing in the abstract. Goodness is treated as something that must be learned and habituated.

The content of that “well/good” is then immediately specified: pursue justice (*mishpat*), correct oppression, defend the orphan, and plead the widow’s cause. These are not abstract virtues or private piety. In the social world of 8th-century Judah, orphans and widows were paradigmatic of the defenseless—those without a male protector or economic power. Defending them was concrete, public advocacy. In the prophetic tradition this is the practical expression of covenant loyalty and love of neighbor (cf. the parallel calls in Amos, Micah 6:8, Isaiah 58).

Matthew Henry captures the logic cleanly: “It is not enough that we cease to do evil, but we must learn to do well. (1.) We must be doing, not cease to do evil and then stand idle. (2.) We must be doing good…” The movement prevents a purely negative or legalistic understanding of holiness. Stopping harm is required, but it leaves a vacuum; that vacuum is to be filled by cultivated practices of justice and protective care.

Thus the text suggests that goodness is not mere restraint. Restraint is the necessary first movement—removing what offends God’s eyes—but the goal and the substance of righteousness is a practiced form of love and justice: an active, learned, habitual orientation toward the vulnerable that embodies what God himself is like. Empty religion can coexist with the cessation of certain overt evils; it cannot coexist with this kind of practiced goodness.
