Short answer: it’s not merely sentimental, and it isn’t only a clever strategy. It is a radical, costly form of moral strength that springs from imitating God. It challenges natural instincts, social norms, and even one’s sense of justice, yet it points to a transformed way of living that Jesus says marks true children of God.

What makes enemy-love so hard

- Human instinct and social law of retaliation: People tend to respond to harm with anger, revenge, or withdrawal. Honor codes, trauma, and fear reinforce a cycle of retaliation. Loving an enemy requires breaking that cycle.

- Emotional and psychological work: “Loving” an enemy doesn’t mean liking them or endorsing their wrong. It means choosing goodwill in intention and action—blessing, doing good, praying for them—even when the feelings haven’t caught up yet. That inner reorientation takes time and grace.

- The scale of the command: Jesus broadens the circle from “neighbors” to “enemies.” In the context of first-century Israel under Roman rule, that meant a deeply disorienting call to bless or do good to oppressors and persecutors, not just friends or family.

- The cost of practical application: Blessing and praying for someone who has harmed you can be costly and risky. It may require setting boundaries, seeking justice, and choosing forgiveness without excusing wrongdoing. The call is not naive tolerance; it is a costly commitment to goodness.

Is it sentimental, strategic, or a deeper form of moral strength?

- Not primarily sentimental: The Greek term often translated as “love” here is agapē—a love of choosing what is best for the other, not a warm feeling. In this passage, Jesus specifies concrete acts: bless, do good, pray. Those are decisive, costly actions, not sentimental sentiment.

- Not merely strategic: There is a strategic dimension—reducing cycles of vengeance, bearing witness to a different kind of social order, breaking the logic of “eye for an eye.” But the motive isn’t primarily efficiency or pragmatism; it’s alignment with God’s character and the surrounding goal of transformation. The world’s best outcomes can result, but the aim is fidelity to God, not utilitarian success.

- A deeper form of moral strength: The most sustained interpretation is that this reveals a deeper strength of character shaped by grace. It echoes the imitation of God who is “kind to the ungrateful and evil” (Luke 6:35; Matt. 5:45). It is a sign of being “children of your Father” who shows mercy even to the undeserving. In the New Testament, this is linked to the fruit of the Spirit (Gal. 5:22–23) and to a life transformed by love that can endure wrong without becoming bitter.

How to think about and live this out

- Distinguish love from approval or naiveté: Loving enemies does not mean approving abuse or letting injustice go unchecked. It means choosing a posture of goodwill, seeking the other’s good, and trusting justice to God, while also protecting the vulnerable and pursuing right action.

- Practice in small steps: 
  - Pray for someone who has harmed you.
  - bless them privately (or say a kind word, if appropriate).
  - Do a kind deed toward them when an opportunity arises.
  - Choose not to retaliate in speech or action when provoked.

- Recognize the role of God’s grace: This kind of love is not something you conjure up by sheer will. It is a fruit that grows as you depend on God and imitate Jesus, who showed love even in the face of crucifixion.

- See the broader biblical pattern: Luke 6:27–36, Romans 12:17–21, and 1 John 4:20–21 echo the same call and show how this love operates in personal, communal, and structural ways.

Bottom line

Enemy-love in Matthew 5:44–45 is best understood as a transformative demand: a costly, deliberate practice of goodwill that exposes the moral strength of a person formed by God’s redemptive love. It’s not mere sentiment, nor a clever tactic alone, but a lifelong orientation that embodies what it means to be “children of the Father in heaven.” That said, it must be lived with wisdom, justice, and safety in mind, never as a blanket endorsement of harmful behavior.
