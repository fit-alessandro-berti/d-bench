This verse calls for a **posture of humble, self-aware compassion**—a moral stance that blends moral responsibility with deep personal vulnerability.  

The three elements—**restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion**—are kept together because they form a **triad of spiritual integrity** in the act of correcting others.  

Here’s why they belong together:

1. **Restoration (the goal)**  
   The aim is not punishment or exposure, but healing and reconciliation. The “spiritual” person sees the fault as a wound to be mended, not merely a rule broken.

2. **Meekness (the tone and method)**  
   Meekness here is not weakness, but strength under control—like a surgeon’s gentle hands. It means approaching the person with gentleness, patience, and respect, avoiding harshness or self-righteousness.

3. **Self-suspicion (the inner guard)**  
   The phrase “considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted” introduces **self-examination** before correction. It implies:  
   - Recognizing one’s own capacity for failure.  
   - Guarding against pride or a sense of moral superiority.  
   - Seeing the person in fault as a fellow frail human, not a lesser being.

The connection between them reveals a **profound psychological and spiritual wisdom**:  
If we try to restore without meekness, we risk harming further.  
If we act without self-suspicion, we risk hypocrisy.  
Meekness without a goal of restoration might just be passivity.  
Self-suspicion without meekness could lead to paralysis or excessive guilt.

In essence, this posture is **other-focused yet deeply self-aware**—a beautiful balance where care for another is tempered by the remembrance of one’s own fragility. It treats moral failure not as a occasion for condemnation, but as a shared human vulnerability where grace must be both given and received with trembling hands.
