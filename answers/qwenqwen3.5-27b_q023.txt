In Isaiah 55:6–7, the prophet presents a profound definition of repentance (Hebrew: *shuv*, "to return"). By explicitly linking the "way" (outward conduct) with "thoughts" (inward imagination), the text establishes that true repentance is **holistic**. It is not merely a change in behavior, nor is it merely a change in opinion; it is a total reorientation of the human person toward God.

Here is an analysis of why both are required and what this implies about conduct, imagination, and mercy.

### 1. Why Both "Way" and "Thoughts"?
In Hebrew poetry, parallelism is often used to reinforce a single truth from two angles. However, in this verse, the two terms represent the **root and the fruit** of human existence.

*   **The "Way" (*Derek*):** This refers to the path one travels, one’s lifestyle, habits, and visible actions. It is the external manifestation of a life.
*   **The "Thoughts" (*Machashavah*):** This refers to plans, intentions, counsels, and the internal reasoning of the mind. It is the volitional engine that drives the "way."

**The Necessity of the Pair:**
If a person forsakes their "way" but keeps their "thoughts," they are merely suppressing symptoms while the disease remains. This leads to hypocrisy or eventual relapse. Conversely, if a person changes their "thoughts" but does not forsake their "way," their faith is dead (James 2:17). God, who searches the heart (1 Samuel 16:7), demands integrity between the inner man and the outer life. Sin originates in the imagination (James 1:14–15); therefore, repentance must address the imagination before it can fully address the behavior.

### 2. Implications for Outward Conduct
The command to "forsake his way" implies that **repentance is tangible.**

*   **Trajectory Change:** A "way" implies direction. Repentance is not just stopping a bad action; it is changing one’s trajectory. It requires the abandonment of a lifestyle that is incompatible with God’s character.
*   **Accountability:** Because conduct is visible, the "way" serves as the evidence of the "return." One cannot claim to have returned to the Lord while continuing to walk the path of the wicked.
*   **Active Effort:** The text uses the imperative "forsake." This suggests that repentance involves active discipline. It is not a passive waiting for feelings to change, but a deliberate turning away from established habits.

### 3. Implications for Inward Imagination
The command to forsake "thoughts" implies that **repentance is cognitive and volitional.**

*   **The Seat of Sin:** The "unrighteous man" is defined not just by what he does, but by what he *plans* and *desires*. The text suggests that the wicked often justify their actions through their thoughts (e.g., rationalizing greed as ambition, or violence as protection). Repentance requires dismantling these internal justifications.
*   **Renewal of the Mind:** To forsake one's thoughts is to surrender one’s autonomy over how they interpret reality. It implies a shift from self-centered reasoning to God-centered reasoning. It aligns with the New Testament concept of being "transformed by the renewing of your mind" (Romans 12:2).
*   **Hidden Holiness:** This aspect of repentance deals with the private self. It implies that God cares about the secret chambers of the mind just as much as the public square.

### 4. The Possibility of Mercy
The connection between this dual repentance and God’s mercy is the climax of the passage.

*   **The Condition:** The text links the mercy ("he will have mercy") directly to the return ("let him return"). However, this is not a transaction where human effort *buys* mercy. Rather, the "return" is the posture required to *receive* it. One cannot receive mercy while clinging to the very things that separate them from the Merciful One.
*   **The Scope of Mercy:** The text promises that God will "abundantly pardon." The Hebrew word for "abundantly" (*rabbah*) suggests a measure that exceeds the measure of the sin.
    *   By requiring both "way" and "thoughts" to be forsaken, God acknowledges the depth of the human problem (total corruption).
    *   By promising "abundant pardon," God acknowledges the depth of His grace (total restoration).
*   **The Urgency:** Verse 6 ("while he may be found") adds urgency. The implication is that the window for this holistic return is open now. The "nearness" of God is the opportunity, but the "return" is the response.

### Summary
Isaiah 55:7 teaches that **repentance is the alignment of the heart and the feet.**
*   **Outward Conduct** must change because actions reveal allegiance.
*   **Inward Imagination** must change because thoughts reveal identity.
*   **Mercy** is available to the whole person, but it requires the whole person to turn.

The verse implies that God does not want a partial surrender. He offers "abundant pardon" not to those who merely clean up their appearance, but to those who fundamentally abandon their rebellion in both their private reasoning and their public life.
