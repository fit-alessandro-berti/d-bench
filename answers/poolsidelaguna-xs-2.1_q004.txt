The verse Proverbs 3:5–6 calls for a specific kind of trust—one that is **humble reliance on God’s sovereignty and wisdom** rather than a rejection of human reason or intellect. This distinction is crucial in differentiating **humble self-distrust** from mere **anti-intellectualism**.

---

### **1. What Kind of Trust Is Being Asked For?**
The verse emphasizes **trust in the Lord with all your heart** and **acknowledging Him in all your ways**. This is not a passive or blind faith but an active, wholehearted commitment to God’s guidance. It involves:
- **Recognizing the limitations of human understanding**: Our finite minds are prone to error, bias, and pride. Trusting God means acknowledging that our reasoning, while valuable, is incomplete without divine revelation and wisdom.
- **Subordinating reason to faith**: The call to "lean not unto thine own understanding" does not reject intellectual rigor but warns against placing ultimate confidence in our own reasoning as a substitute for seeking God’s will. Trust in God means allowing His truth to inform and direct our thoughts and decisions.

---

### **2. Humble Self-Distrust vs. Anti-Intellectualism**
#### **Humble Self-Distrust**
- **Definition**: A recognition of human fallibility and the need for divine grace. It involves using reason and knowledge while humbly admitting their limitations and submitting them to God’s authority.
- **Example**: A student who studies diligently but also prays for wisdom, acknowledging that their understanding is incomplete and dependent on God’s guidance.
- **Biblical Alignment**: Proverbs 3:5–6 aligns with this. It encourages using one’s mind (Proverbs 2:6 says "the Lord gives wisdom") while trusting God’s ultimate wisdom over our own.

#### **Anti-Intellectualism**
- **Definition**: A rejection of intellectual inquiry, reason, or human knowledge. It dismisses the value of education, critical thinking, or empirical investigation.
- **Example**: Someone who refuses to read, study, or engage in dialogue, claiming that "faith alone" is sufficient, even when practical decisions require discernment.
- **Conflict with Scripture**: The Bible does not endorse anti-intellectualism. Jesus and the prophets often used reason and logic (e.g., Acts 17:26, where Paul engages in philosophical debate). The Proverbs themselves celebrate wisdom, diligence, and discernment (Proverbs 1:7, 3:13).

---

### **3. The Balance in Proverbs 3:5–6**
- **Trust with "all thine heart"**: This means emotional and volitional commitment to God, not merely intellectual assent.
- **"Lean not unto thine own understanding"**: A warning against pride, overconfidence in one’s own reasoning, or substituting human reason for divine revelation.
- **"Acknowledge Him in all thy ways"**: This implies active engagement of the mind, but under the banner of God’s lordship. It calls for integrating faith and reason, not abandoning reason.

---

### **4. Practical Implications**
- **Use your brain, but submit it to God**: Engage in study, reflection, and critical thinking, but recognize that true wisdom ultimately comes from God (James 1:5).
- **Seek guidance**: Counsel, Scripture, and community can help align your understanding with divine truth.
- **Avoid two extremes**: 
  - **Pride** (relying solely on human reasoning). 
  - **Rejection of reason** (dismissing intellectual effort entirely).

---

### **Conclusion**
The trust demanded in Proverbs 3:5–6 is **not anti-intellectual** but **humble self-distrust**—a balanced posture that uses reason while recognizing its limitations and surrendering decisions to God’s wisdom. It invites believers to "think on the things of God" (Philippians 8:16), integrating intellect with faith rather than choosing one over the other.
