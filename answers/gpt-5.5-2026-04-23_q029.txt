Galatians 6:1 requires a posture of **humble, gentle, restorative self-watchfulness**.

Paul is not describing correction as an act of moral superiority. He says:

> “Ye which are spiritual, restore such an one in the spirit of meekness; considering thyself…”

So the corrector must be:

1. **Spiritual** — not merely “right,” but governed by the Spirit.  
   In Galatians, to be spiritual means to walk by the Spirit, whose fruit includes “meekness” or gentleness. The person correcting must not be acting out of irritation, pride, rivalry, or the desire to expose.

2. **Restorative** — the goal is to heal, not humiliate.  
   “Restore” carries the idea of setting something right, like mending what is broken or putting a dislocated limb back in place. The fallen person is not to be discarded but recovered.

3. **Meek** — correction must be gentle, not harsh.  
   Meekness does not mean moral weakness or indifference to sin. It means strength under control: truth spoken without cruelty, firmness without contempt.

4. **Self-suspicious** — “considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted.”  
   The corrector must remember his own vulnerability. He too can fall—perhaps into the same sin, or into the sins that often accompany correction: pride, harshness, impatience, hypocrisy, or self-righteousness.

These three—**restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion**—belong together because correction is spiritually dangerous.

If correction is not restorative, it becomes punishment or shaming.  
If it is not meek, it wounds the person further.  
If it lacks self-suspicion, it becomes prideful and exposes the corrector to temptation.

Paul’s point is that Christian correction must be carried out by someone who sees the sinner not as an enemy to defeat, but as a brother to recover—and who knows, “I am not above falling either.”
