In Luke 18:9, Luke gives you the interpretive key before the parable even starts:

> "unto certain which trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others"

That is what condemns the Pharisee. It is not false doctrine. Everything he claims is probably true.

Pharisees only fasted once a year by Law - on the Day of Atonement. This man fasts twice a week. The Law required tithes on grain, wine and oil. This man tithes *all that he possesses*. By external standards, he is exemplary.

What is wrong is the *orientation* of that virtue. All the options you list are true, because they are all facets of the same root sin:

**1. Pride - the root.** `pepoithotas eph' heautois` - trusting upon themselves. His confidence is self-sourced.

**2. Comparison - the mechanism.** `that I am not as other men are, extortioners, unjust... or even as this publican.` His righteousness only exists in contrast. He needs someone lower in order to be high. He is not asking "what am I before God?" but "what am I compared to him?"

**3. Contempt - the fruit.** `and despised others - exouthenountas`. The verb literally means to make someone nothing, to count as zero. His self-elevation requires another's annihilation. He has turned the Temple, the place of atonement for all Israel, into a theatre for branding another person as irredeemable.

**4. Virtue as self-display - the mode.** Luke is surgically precise: 

> "The Pharisee stood and prayed *thus with himself* - pros heauton"

It can be translated "he prayed to himself" or "he prayed about himself, to himself." Grammatically it is ambiguous, and theologically it is intentional. Though physically in the Temple, his prayer never leaves his own circle. God is addressed as an audience for a resume: "God, I thank thee that I am..." It is not fellowship, it's a performance review.

So God does not condemn him for fasting and tithing. He condemns him because virtue has become a mirror instead of a window. When virtue is used to prove that you do not need mercy, it ceases to be virtue.

### Why is the tax collector truer?

The publican's posture is truer because it corresponds to reality.

**1. He is in the right place, spatially and spiritually.** "Standing afar off" - not performing in the center. He knows the Temple system is for sinners to approach a holy God through sacrifice. He places himself where a sinner belongs.

**2. His body tells the truth.** "Would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven, but smote upon his breast." In that culture, beating the chest was a sign of mourning and contrition. He expresses what the Pharisee hides: grief.

**3. His prayer asks for what can actually be given.** 

> "God be merciful to me a sinner - ho Theos hilastheti moi to hamartolo"

The verb *hilastheti* does not just mean "be nice to me." It means "be propitiated, make atonement for me." It's the language of the mercy seat in the Temple. He is asking for what the Temple was built to provide: atonement he cannot provide for himself.

The Pharisee brings his achievement to God. The tax collector brings his need for God.

That is why Jesus can say:

> "this man went down to his house justified - dedikaiomenos - rather than the other"

Justified does not mean "made to look better." It means declared righteous, put in right standing. The Pharisee went up trusting that he *was* righteous, and so he received nothing. The tax collector went up confessing that he was *not* righteous, and so he could receive righteousness as a gift.

The parable closes with the principle: "every one that exalteth himself shall be abased; and he that humbleth himself shall be exalted."

The first man was high and needed to stay high. The second man was low and was willing to tell the truth about being low. In the economy of grace, only the second position can be lifted.
