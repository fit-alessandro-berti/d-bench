The juxtaposition of bitterness and forgiveness in this passage from Ephesians draws a stark contrast between two opposite attitudes and ways of relating to others. By placing the command to "put away" bitterness, wrath, anger, clamor, slander and malice immediately before the exhortation to be kind, tenderhearted, and forgiving, the text suggests that bitterness is incompatible with a spirit of Christlike grace and love.

While bitterness may start as a feeling of resentment or anger over a wrong suffered, the text seems to treat it as something more than a fleeting emotion. The grouping with wrath, slander and malice indicates that bitterness here is a poisonous root that gives rise to vengeful words and actions. It's portrayed almost as a posture or orientation of the heart that keeps a record of wrongs and lashes out rather than letting go.

Moreover, the fact that "all bitterness" is to be completely put away suggests that it can become a entrenched habit or disposition if left unchecked. Like a spiritual cancer, bitterness has the potential to spread and morph into cruelty, strife and vilification of others ("evil speaking"). This hints that bitterness is contagious in a sense - it breeds more of itself, perpetuating cycles of retaliation and rancor.

Against this, forgiveness is enjoined as the Christlike alternative - a chosen mindset of graciousness and mercy based on the lavish forgiveness believers have received from God. Just as harbored bitterness poisons relationships, forgiveness has the power to heal and restore. The text locates the ultimate basis for human forgiveness in the divine forgiveness extended "for Christ's sake." 

In summary, this passage treats bitterness as a toxic spiritual state that gives birth to angry words and deeds while corroding the one who carries it. It's a stubborn condition of the heart more than a momentary feeling, one that requires an intentional decision to "put away" and replace with compassionate, reconciling forgiveness rooted in the gospel. The stark contrast highlights the fundamentally incompatible nature of unforgiving bitterness with the way of Christ.
