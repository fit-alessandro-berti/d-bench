This passage from Isaiah highlights the importance of both outward actions ("way") and inward disposition ("thoughts") in the process of repentance and seeking God's mercy.

Outward conduct: The call for the "wicked" to "forsake his way" indicates that repentance requires a change in external behavior. This implies turning away from sinful actions, habits, and lifestyle choices that are contrary to God's will. 

Inward imagination: The "unrighteous man" is also urged to abandon "his thoughts," suggesting that repentance involves more than mere outward conformity. It necessitates a transformation of the inner life—the mind, heart, and will. Sinful thoughts, attitudes, and desires must be recognized and forsaken.

The mention of both external and internal dimensions underscores the comprehensive nature of true repentance. It is not enough to modify one's conduct while harboring unrighteous thoughts, nor is it sufficient to mentally assent to divine truth without a corresponding change in action. Genuine repentance requires a fundamental reorientation of both behavior and thinking.

The possibility of mercy is held out to those who repent in this holistic sense. The passage assures that God "will have mercy" and "will abundantly pardon" those who sincerely turn to Him. This emphasizes the graciousness and readiness with which God forgives and restores those who humbly seek Him in repentance.

In essence, this passage presents repentance as a wholehearted turning from sin in both deed and thought. By forsaking wicked ways and thoughts, and turning to God in faith, one can find mercy and abundant pardon. The implication is that God looks at the totality of a person's life—both outward and inward—and extends forgiveness to those who authentically return to Him.
