### 1. The immediate context – “putting off” and “putting on”

In **Ephesians 4:22‑24** Paul tells the believers that they **“have put off the old man with his deeds”** and **“have put on the new man, which is renewed in knowledge after the image of him that created him.”**  

The two imperatives that follow—**“Let all bitterness … be put away from you”** (v. 31) and **“be ye kind … forgiving one another”** (v. 32)—are the concrete, ethical counterpart to that doctrinal “putting off/putting on.”  

In other words, the apostle is not merely giving a list of sins to avoid; he is describing what it looks like to **live out the new identity** that believers already possess in Christ.

---

### 2. What “bitterness” (πικρία) means in the biblical lexicon

| Greek term | Typical range of meaning | Biblical occurrences |
|------------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| **πικρία (pikría)** | *Sharpness, harshness, a bitter disposition*; can describe a *deep‑seated resentment* (the “root” of a person’s actions). | Eph 4:31; Heb 12:15 (τῆς πικρίας ῥίζα – “root of bitterness”); Rom 3:14 (mouth “full of cursing and bitterness”); James 3:14 (bitter envy). |

The word is not limited to a fleeting emotion. In the **Septuagint** it frequently translates the Hebrew **מר** (*mar*), which can denote **bitter water**, **bitter speech**, or a **bitter (i.e., hostile) attitude** (e.g., Deut 29:18 “a root that yields bitter poison”).  

In the New Testament the phrase **“root of bitterness”** in **Heb 12:15** explicitly shows that πικρία can *spread* and *defile many*—a clear hint of a **contagious** quality.

Thus, when Paul uses πικρία here he is pointing to **a settled, internal disposition that expresses itself in hostile speech and action**, not simply a momentary feeling of annoyance.

---

### 3. Why the direct contrast with forgiveness?

1. **Incompatible relational orientations** – Bitterness holds a grudge, seeks payback, and builds walls; forgiveness releases the offense, restores relationship, and builds bridges. The two cannot coexist in the same interpersonal space.

2. **The indicative‑imperative pattern** – Paul grounds the imperative (“be kind, forgiving”) in the indicative (“*as* God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you”). The **forgiveness already received** becomes the **model** for the forgiveness we extend. Hence the contrast is theological as much as ethical: because you have been forgiven, you must put away bitterness.

3. **Community‑building language** – The surrounding verses stress the **unity of the body** (Eph 4:1‑16). Bitterness is the chief engine of division; forgiveness is the chief engine of reconciliation. Placing them side‑by‑by underscores that **the health of the fellowship depends on the removal of the former and the embrace of the latter**.

---

### 4. How does the text treat bitterness? (Feeling, habit, posture, or contagion?)

| Option | How the text addresses it |
|--------|--------------------------|
| **A fleeting feeling** | The text does **not** say “do not feel bitter.” It says “let all bitterness … be **put away**.” The verb *ἀπόθεσθε* (to put off, lay aside) implies a **decisive removal**, not merely a suppression of an emotion. |
| **A habit / deep‑seated disposition** | The word πικρία itself carries the nuance of **a settled attitude** (the “root” metaphor). The list pairs bitterness with “wrath, anger, clamour, evil‑speaking, malice”—all of which describe **patterns of behavior**, not isolated feelings. |
| **A chosen posture** | The imperatives “put away” and “be kind” are both **active choices**. The believer is exhorted to **choose** to adopt the posture of forgiveness, just as Christ chose to forgive. |
| **Something “contagious”** | The “root of bitterness” in **Heb 12:15** (which Paul echoes) shows that bitterness can **spread** through a community and defile others. While the primary thrust in Eph 4 is personal removal, the underlying image hints at a **social infection** that must be stopped. |

In short, **the text treats bitterness primarily as a habit/chosen posture (a deep, relational stance) that must be actively rejected**, while acknowledging—through the “root of bitterness” motif—that it can also function as a **contagious influence** if left unchecked.

---

### 5. Theological and practical implications

| Aspect | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Identity** | Because believers are **“forgiven in Christ,”** they now **share Christ’s forgiving character**. The opposite of that character—bitterness—is incompatible with the new identity. |
| **Action** | Forgiveness is not merely a feeling; it is a **gracious act** (the verb χαρίζομαι means “to grant freely, to pardon”). Likewise, putting away bitterness is a **relational decision** that manifests in speech and conduct. |
| **Community** | Bitterness **divides**; forgiveness **reconciles**. In a local church, a single “bitter root” can contaminate the whole fellowship (cf. Heb 12:15). Hence the urgent call to “put it away” is also a **protecting measure** for the body. |
| **Spiritual growth** | Practising forgiveness is a **means of sanctification**: it mirrors God’s love and progressively transforms the believer into the image of Christ (Col 3:10). |

---

### 6. Bottom line

- **Why they are paired:** Because forgiveness is the *antithetical* relational stance to bitterness; the one is the outward expression of the new self, the other is the residual posture of the old self. The pairing is also theologically grounded: **God’s forgiveness in Christ is the pattern we are to emulate**.

- **What the text treats bitterness as:** Not simply a fleeting feeling, but a **deep‑seated, habitual disposition (a “posture”)** that must be deliberately **removed** (“put away”). The biblical metaphor of a “root of bitterness” adds a **contagious** dimension—bitterness can infect others if it remains—but the primary emphasis is on the **individual’s choice** to reject it and to adopt the forgiving posture that flows from being forgiven by God.

In practice, this means that when a Christian says, “I am hurt and I feel bitter,” the Scripture’s call is **not** to *suppress* that feeling but to **lay it aside**—to *choose* the new posture of kindness and forgiveness, just as Christ chose to lay aside the weight of sin and extend grace. The contrast is sharp because the two ways of relating are fundamentally incompatible; the believer is called to live out the latter, for the sake of personal holiness and the unity of the body.
