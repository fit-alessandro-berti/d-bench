This passage from Matthew 12:34–35 presents a profound psychological and theological insight into the relationship between internal character and external expression. Jesus uses the metaphor of a "treasure" (or storehouse) to explain that speech is not an isolated act, but rather the inevitable overflow of what has been accumulated within.

Here is how the passage connects speech with hidden moral stockpiles, and an analysis of whether words are symptoms, choices, revelations, or all three.

### The Connection: Overflow from Abundance

The key phrase in verse 34 is **"out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh."** The Greek word for "abundance" (*perisseuma*) implies an overflow or surplus. Just as a cup overflows when it is filled beyond capacity, speech is portrayed as the natural spillover of what fills the inner person.

The "heart" in biblical anthropology is not merely the seat of emotions, but the center of the will, intellect, and moral character. It is the repository where values, beliefs, desires, and intentions are stored—the "treasure." Therefore, the connection is causal and diagnostic:
*   **Input:** What one dwells on, loves, and stores up internally (good or evil).
*   **Output:** What naturally emerges in speech when pressure is applied or when the heart is full.

Jesus argues that you cannot consistently produce good speech if your internal reservoir is filled with evil. The nature of the output is determined by the nature of the source.

### Are Words Symptoms, Choices, Revelations, or All Three?

The text suggests that words function as **all three**, though they emphasize different aspects of human agency and identity.

#### 1. Words as Symptoms (Diagnostic Function)
A symptom is an outward sign of an inward condition. Jesus explicitly frames speech this way: *"How can ye, being evil, speak good things?"* He implies that if the root is corrupt, the fruit must be corrupt. Speech acts as a diagnostic tool that reveals the health (or sickness) of the soul.
*   **Evidence:** The analogy of the tree and its fruit (used earlier in Matthew 7 and 12) supports this. You identify a tree by its fruit; you identify a person’s spiritual state by their words. If someone speaks malice, it is a symptom of a malicious heart.

#### 2. Words as Revelations (Unmasking Function)
Speech reveals what is hidden. The "treasure" is described as something stored away—often unseen by others. But speech brings it into the light. In moments of stress, anger, or casual conversation, people often reveal their true priorities and beliefs.
*   **Evidence:** The phrase "bringeth forth" suggests drawing out something that was previously concealed. The Pharisees’ accusation against Jesus (that He cast out demons by Beelzebul) revealed their own hardened hearts and rejection of God’s work, even though they may have appeared religious on the surface. Their words unmasked their true allegiance.

#### 3. Words as Choices (Moral Responsibility)
While speech flows from the heart, Jesus holds people accountable for their words (see Matthew 12:36–37, immediately following this passage: *"But I say unto you, That every idle word that men shall speak, they shall give account thereof in the day of judgment"*). This implies that while the heart influences speech, individuals are still responsible for what they allow to fill their hearts and what they choose to express.
*   **Evidence:** The distinction between the "good man" and the "evil man" suggests a moral trajectory shaped by repeated choices. One does not accidentally become a "good man"; one cultivates a good treasure through deliberate alignment with truth and love. Conversely, one allows evil to accumulate through neglect or active rebellion. Thus, speech is both a result of past choices (what we’ve stored up) and a present choice (what we decide to utter).

### Conclusion: All Three Interwoven

The passage best supports the view that words are **all three**:

*   They are **symptoms** because they indicate the current state of the heart.
*   They are **revelations** because they expose what is otherwise hidden in the private realm of the mind and spirit.
*   They are **choices** because humans are morally accountable for both cultivating their inner treasure and governing their tongues.

Jesus’ point is not to excuse bad speech as involuntary ("I couldn’t help it; my heart made me do it"), but to convict the listener that **transformation must begin internally**. You cannot fix the mouth without fixing the heart. To change what comes out, you must change what goes in and what is stored up.
