**External compliance** involves outward actions that match rules or appearances without a transformed inner disposition—behavior that can be performed (and often fails) while the will, affections, and motives remain unchanged or resistant. Receiving a **new heart** (with God’s Spirit) is an internal, God-wrought replacement of that core disposition so that desire, responsiveness, and enablement for true obedience arise from within.

### External Compliance vs. New Heart
In the flow of Ezekiel 36 (post-judgment restoration language for Israel), God promises action “not for your sake” but for the sake of His name, purifying, gathering, and then renovating the people so they can live rightly in the land (vv. 22–28). External compliance alone had already proven insufficient: the law and statutes could be known and even approximated externally, yet the people repeatedly broke covenant, pursued idols, and suffered exile. That is the classic pattern of effort under external command (law written on tablets/stone, social or ritual pressure, fear of consequences) without corresponding desire or power.

- External compliance can produce temporary conformity or hypocrisy (going through the motions, keeping judgments on paper). It does not alter the underlying “heart” that wants otherwise, resists, or calcifies. It leaves people able to check boxes while remaining unresponsive to God.
- The “new heart” and “new spirit” (plus Spirit put within) reverse this. The “heart of flesh” is living, sensitive, and pliable—able to respond to God rather than remaining rigid. Obedience is no longer merely compelled from outside; God “causes you to walk in my statutes” (v. 27) by indwelling enablement. Desire and capacity align with the command. This is why later Scripture picks up the same imagery for new-covenant reality: the law written on hearts (Jer 31; Heb 8/10), regeneration or new creation, being given a heart to know God, and life “by the Spirit” rather than mere letter.

In short: external compliance manages (or fails to manage) the surface. The new heart recreates the source so that statutes are kept from renewed affection and God-given power rather than struggle against an unconverted core.

### Why “Stony” Hardness as an Almost Material Condition That Must Be Replaced
The language is deliberately concrete and surgical. “Heart” is the standard biblical metaphor for the control center of a person (thinking, willing, desiring, deciding). Calling it “stony” portrays a total, stuck condition: unresponsive, calloused, unyielding, dead to God’s voice—the opposite of flesh that feels, bleeds, grows, and can be shaped. You cannot sand, polish, or partially reform stone into living tissue; the figure emphasizes that mere education, better resolution, or incremental self-reform will not suffice. The hardness must be *taken away* and replaced with a “heart of flesh.”

This is not a medical textbook claim; it is vivid prophetic imagery of radical divine initiative. Sin and long rejection of God produce (and reveal) this hardness; humans cannot manufacture their own cure. God performs the transplant and implants His Spirit. The material-sounding language therefore underscores:
-depth and totality of the problem (not surface defect but corneous core),
-necessity of replacement rather than repair or external armor,
-dependence on God’s creative act (echoing “new” creation motifs),
-and the resulting living responsiveness that makes obedience congruent rather than coerced.

The same chapter pairs this heart-work with cleansing from idols and a restored relationship (“ye shall be my people, and I will be your God”), showing the new heart is not abstract mysticism but covenant fidelity made real from the inside out.

**Summary**: External compliance leaves an untransformed, often hardened, center that can mimic obedience while remaining resistant. A new heart of flesh + the Spirit is God’s promised interior renovation that produces willing, Spirit-caused keeping of His ways. The stony-to-flesh figure drives home that the change is a divine replacement of an unresponsive core, not human polishing of the same rock.
