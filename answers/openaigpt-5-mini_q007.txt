Short answer: Luke 6:37 is not telling us that moral judgments never matter. It is forbidding a particular posture—self-righteous, final, shaming condemnation—and calling us instead to humble, merciful discernment that seeks restoration and justice rather than pleasure in another’s downfall.

Why they’re different

- The phrase “Judge not” targets a condemning attitude, not the ability to tell right from wrong. The Bible repeatedly calls people to evaluate actions and protect the vulnerable (e.g., John 7:24; 1 Cor. 5; Matt. 18:15–17). Jesus’ concern is the spirit in which judgment is made: hypocritical, harsh, and unforgiving.
- Condemnation (katakrínō) implies pronouncing an irrevocable, punitive verdict and often enjoying another’s disgrace. Discernment (krínō, in its responsible sense) is careful moral assessment aimed at truth, correction, and care.
- The command to “forgive” and the promise of being forgiven reverse an appetite to punish and exclude. It invites mercy and humility: remember your own faults before you pronounce final judgment on others.

How discernment differs from an appetite to condemn

- Aim: Discernment aims at truth, healing, and justice. Appetite to condemn aims at punishment, exclusion, or self-exaltation.
- Method: Discernment seeks facts, context, motive, and proportion. Condemnation leaps to worst explanations, exaggerates, and ignores nuance.
- Outcome: Discernment may lead to corrective action (reproof, discipline, legal accountability, protection of others) done with charity and the hope of restoration. Condemnation seeks to shame, humiliate, or annihilate reputation.
- Stance toward the person: Discernment separates the act from the person and leaves room for repentance. Condemnation defines the person by the act and treats them as beyond help.

Biblical pattern that balances both
- Jesus refuses to condemn the woman caught in adultery (John 8) yet tells her to “sin no more.” That models mercy without moral permissiveness.
- Scripture teaches church discipline (1 Cor. 5) and calls to correct one another (Gal. 6:1), showing that accountable moral judgment is expected—but done with humility and restoration in view.

Practical guidance for living this through
- Check your motives: are you protecting others or feeding your ego?
- Gather facts, listen, and avoid instant, public shaming.
- Aim for restoration: correct where needed, allow repentance, support change.
- Hold people accountable when necessary, but avoid permanent defining condemnation.
- Be willing to be corrected yourself.

Bottom line: Luke 6:37 calls us away from punitive, self-righteous judging and toward humble, truthful discernment—one that protects the innocent, seeks restoration, and refuses to take pleasure in condemning others.
