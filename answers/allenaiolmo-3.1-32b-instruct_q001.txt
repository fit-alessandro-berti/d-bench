This passage from Genesis 4:6–7 is foundational for understanding the biblical view of moral agency, especially in the context of the first human siblings, Cain and Abel.

Let’s break down the passage:

> “And the Lord said unto Cain, Why art thou wroth? and why is thy countenance fallen? If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted? and if thou doest not well, sin lieth at the door. And unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him.”

#### The Picture of Moral Agency

**1. Direct Address and Moral Choice:**
God speaks directly to Cain, asking why he is angry and downcast. This suggests that Cain is capable of self-reflection and emotional response. God’s question implies that Cain’s emotions and actions are not inevitable or controlled by external forces, but are the result of his own choices and attitudes.

**2. Conditionality of Acceptance:**
God says, “If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted? and if thou doest not well, sin lieth at the door.” This is a clear statement of conditionality: acceptance by God depends on the moral quality of Cain’s actions. The phrase “sin lieth at the door” (i.e., is present and waiting) suggests that the opportunity and responsibility to commit sin rests with the individual. Sin is not a force that takes over Cain, but a possibility that arises from his own actions.

**3. Responsibility and Authority:**
The last sentence, “And unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him,” refers to God’s earlier statement that Cain’s offering was accepted and that Abel would bring his offering “behind” Cain’s (Genesis 4:4). Now, Cain is told he will have authority over Abel. This reinforces the idea that Cain is responsible for how he uses his authority and freedom.

#### How is Evil Portrayed?

- **Not as an impulse, beast, or external force:** The text does not depict evil as an overwhelming impulse or as something that possesses Cain from outside. It is not a “beast within” or a supernatural temptation in the sense of later traditions.
- **Not as mere habit:** There’s no indication that Cain’s wrongdoing is a result of a habitual pattern or social influence.
- **Not as pure temptation:** While there may be temptation (implied by the contrast between doing well and not well), the focus is on Cain’s responsibility to choose between right and wrong.

Instead, **evil is portrayed as a choice or an action that arises from the exercise of free will.** Sin “lieth at the door”—it is present as a possibility, waiting for the individual to act upon it. The responsibility for moral action is squarely on Cain.

#### Responsibility According to the Text

The text places full responsibility on the individual:
- Cain is accountable for his emotions (“Why art thou wroth?”)
- Cain is accountable for his actions (“If thou doest well… if thou doest not well…”)
- Cain is accountable for how he uses his authority (“thou shalt rule over him”)

God does not absolve Cain of responsibility; instead, God calls him to account for his choices. The text presents moral agency as the capacity to choose between good and evil, with the onus on the person to make the right choice.

#### Summary Table

| Aspect | How is it presented in Genesis 4:6–7? |
|--------|--------------------------------------|
| Nature of Evil | Not an impulse, beast, or external force, but a possible outcome of one’s choices |
| Source of Evil | Arises from human action (“sin lieth at the door”) |
| Responsibility | Full responsibility rests with the individual; God holds Cain accountable for his actions and choices |
| Moral Agency | Defined as the ability and responsibility to choose between good and evil |

#### In Short

**Evil in this passage is not depicted as an irresistible force or an external temptation, but as a possibility that arises from the exercise of free will. The responsibility for moral action is placed entirely on the individual. Cain is accountable for his choices, and God holds him to that accountability.**
