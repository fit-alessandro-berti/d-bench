This passage from Mark 8:34–35 is a profound statement by Jesus about what it means to follow him. Let’s break down the meaning of “deny oneself” and why this teaching is interpreted in such different ways.

#### What kind of self is being denied here?

When Jesus says, “let him deny himself,” he is not talking about denying one’s existence or suppressing one’s identity in a harmful way. Instead, he’s talking about letting go of the self-centered life—the desire to put oneself first, to live for personal ambition, comfort, or security. In the context of first-century Jewish society (and in many cultures since), “self” often meant the ego, one’s own desires, or one’s attachment to the things of this world.

So, the self being denied here is the **self that is centered on oneself—ego, pride, self-interest, and the pursuit of personal gain or preservation**. Jesus is calling people to shift their focus from “me” to “God” and to others. To “take up one’s cross” means to be willing to suffer or sacrifice for a greater purpose, just as Jesus did.

This kind of self-denial is closely tied to discipleship: it means being willing to set aside one’s own agenda for the sake of following Jesus and serving others.

#### Why does this sound liberating to some and offensive to others?

**To some, it is liberating because:**
- **Freedom from Self-Imposed Chains:** Letting go of the relentless pursuit of one’s own desires, status, or security can be freeing. Many people feel trapped by the pressure to succeed, to look a certain way, or to meet society’s expectations. Jesus’ teaching offers the possibility of freedom from these internal pressures.
- **Purpose Beyond Self:** By denying oneself in this sense, people can find meaning in something larger than themselves—love, service, faith, or justice. This can bring deep fulfillment and peace.
- **Transformation:** The idea is that by dying to self-centeredness, one can become more authentic, compassionate, and whole.

**To others, it sounds offensive because:**
- **Misinterpretation as Self-Rejection:** Some hear “deny yourself” as a call to self-loathing, repression, or erasing one’s identity. This can be psychologically harmful and is not what Jesus intended.
- **Loss of Autonomy:** In cultures that highly value individualism and personal achievement, the idea of denying oneself can feel like a threat to personal freedom or identity.
- **Coercion or Manipulation:** Some might feel that religious or authoritarian figures use this teaching to control people by demanding unquestioning self-sacrifice or obedience, rather than genuine, voluntary transformation.
- **Cultural or Philosophical Clash:** People from backgrounds that emphasize self-actualization or personal fulfillment may find this teaching at odds with their values.

#### In summary

Jesus is not calling for the destruction or denial of the person, but for the transformation of the self—from being self-centered to being Christ-centered and others-centered. This can be experienced as liberating for those who feel burdened by selfishness or the pressure to succeed, but as oppressive for those who feel their identity or freedom is being attacked.

A healthy understanding of this teaching involves recognizing that true self-denial is not about erasing oneself, but about letting go of the false self (the ego, the selfish desires) in order to become the true self—one aligned with love, purpose, and God.
