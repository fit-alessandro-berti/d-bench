**Inner contradiction exposed (James 3:9–10):**  
James points to a split within the speaker: the same mouth is used at once (or in the same habitual pattern) for worship and for harm. The person can *call God “Father”* in one breath (blessing God) and then *turn around and curse other people*—people made in God’s likeness. In other words, the lips claim allegiance to God while simultaneously acting in opposition to what God’s character and image in others require. The contradiction is not merely that two different words are used, but that the speaker treats both as compatible—blessing God and cursing humans who bear God’s likeness.

**Why speech reveals moral disorder so quickly:**  
Because speech is fast, direct, and unfiltered. It comes out immediately from the heart’s settled orientation and from the real motivations beneath religious language.

- **It “drags the heart to the surface.”** One cannot easily hide what one truly values or what one truly wants to do. Cursing quickly exposes anger, contempt, resentment, or hostility.  
- **It exposes inconsistency in practice.** James’ point is that moral disorder often shows up as soon as the tongue is tested: the mouth can sound religious while the impulses behind it remain hostile.  
- **It is a compact action with consequences.** Words may look small compared to “big” deeds, but they are capable of harming relationships immediately—so moral character is revealed early in how one speaks.  
- **It reveals what you’re willing to do right away.** The tongue often reflects the immediate reflex of the person, not the carefully managed image.

So the “contradiction” is: *honoring God with the mouth while dishonoring God’s image in people with the same mouth.* Speech reveals this so quickly because it is the quickest outlet of inner allegiance—showing whether the heart is truly aligned with God or merely using religious words while betraying them in behavior.
