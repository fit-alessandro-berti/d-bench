You've identified the core tensions of this parable with precision. Let's dissect them.

**What condemns the Pharisee is not one single thing, but the integrated posture of his heart, which turns all his virtues into instruments of condemnation.** The elements you list are interconnected strands of that posture:

1.  **Pride & Self-Trust:** This is the foundational flaw. His opening—"God, I thank thee that I am not as other men are"—is a prayer of self-justification. He trusts in his own resume. The parable's intro states its target: "those who were righteous in their own eyes." His entire prayer is an exercise in affirming that self-view.

2.  **Comparison & Contempt:** His righteousness is defined *negatively* by others' failures. He doesn't say, "Thank you for my capacity to give." He says, "Thank you that I'm not like that extortioner, that adulterer... or *that publican*." His virtue is measured by the gap between himself and those he deems worse. This breeds contempt, specifically aimed at the humble man beside him.

3.  **Virtue as Self-Display:** His practices (fasting, tithing) are listed not as gifts to God or expressions of devotion, but as credentials to present *to himself and to God* in a cosmic court case he believes he's winning. The virtue becomes a trophy, not a sacrament. His prayer is a performance, even if the audience is God and his own ego.

**The "false doctrine" angle is subtle.** He isn't denying God's existence or twisting scripture. His error is theological *and* practical: he fundamentally misunderstands the nature of righteousness. He treats it as a **transactional merit**—a scorecard that earns divine favor—rather than a **relational state** of right-standing before a holy God. This doctrine of self-righteousness is what poisons his piety.

**Why is the tax collector's posture spiritually truer?**

Because his posture aligns with the foundational truth the Pharisee's doctrine obscures: **all righteousness is derivative, and all true prayer begins with need.**

*   **Honest Epistemology:** He has no illusions about his spiritual state. He doesn't come with a resume; he comes with a disease ("a sinner"). His knowledge of self is accurate.
*   **Correct Orientation:** "Standing afar off," he doesn't dare presume intimacy. His eyes are downcast, his gesture (beating his breast) is one of sorrow and repentance. His entire being is oriented *toward* God as the only source of help, not *at* God as an appreciative audience for his life.
*   **Prayer of Faith, Not Merit:** His plea—"God, be merciful to me"—is an act of faith. He believes in a mercy that exists independently of his worth. He trusts in God's character, not his own conduct. In Christian terms, he receives the gift of grace, while the Pharisee demands a wage.

In essence, the parable contrasts two economies:

*   **The Pharisee's Economy:** A **merit-based system** where virtue is accumulated and displayed to earn justification. The self is the subject; righteousness is the object.
*   **The Tax Collector's Economy:** A **grace-based system** where brokenness is acknowledged and mercy is sought. God is the subject; mercy is the object.

The tax collector goes home justified because he entered the temple already aware that justification is a gift to be received, not an achievement to be declared. His posture is truer because it matches reality: we stand not because we are tall, but because we are upheld.
