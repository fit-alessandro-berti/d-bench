Based on the text of Matthew 6:1 and its broader context within the Sermon on the Mount, the moral danger lies **not in visibility itself, but in the craving to be seen.** 

The verse targets the internal motive of the heart rather than the external location of the act. Here is a breakdown of why this is the case, supported by linguistic, contextual, and theological evidence from the text.

### 1. The Linguistic Clue: The "Theatrical" Motive
The phrase "to be seen of them" in the Greek text is *pros to theathēnai autois*. The root word is *theaomai*, which means to gaze upon, behold, or look at. This is the same root from which we get the word "theater." 

Jesus is not merely describing a public setting; He is describing a **performance**. The moral danger is turning an act of charity into a theatrical production where the giver is the star and the public is the audience. The problem is the *purpose* clause ("in order to be seen"), which reveals a heart seeking human applause rather than divine approval.

### 2. The Contextual Proof: Matthew 5:16
If visibility itself were the problem, Jesus would be contradicting Himself. Just a few verses earlier in the same sermon, Jesus commands: 
> *"Let your light so shine before men, **that they may see your good works**, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."* (Matthew 5:16)

This creates a clear contrast in motives:
*   **In Matthew 5:16:** Visibility is commanded so that God gets the glory.
*   **In Matthew 6:1:** Visibility is condemned when the giver uses it so that *they* get the glory. 
Therefore, the physical act of being seen is morally neutral; it is the direction of the glory (toward self or toward God) that determines its morality.

### 3. The Nature of the Danger: Corrupted Virtue and Stolen Reward
Jesus explains the danger by pointing to the "hypocrites" who sound trumpets when they give (Matthew 6:2). The moral danger of public goodness driven by vanity is threefold:
*   **It corrupts the virtue:** Alms-giving is meant to be an act of love and sacrifice directed toward the needy and toward God. When the craving to be seen takes over, the needy person is reduced to a mere prop in the giver's quest for social status. 
*   **It results in immediate, empty payment:** Jesus notes that these people "have their reward" (v. 2). The danger is that the craving for human praise is satisfied by human praise. Once the audience claps, the transaction is complete. 
*   **It forfeits the eternal:** Because the giver has already extracted their payment from earth (human admiration), they have "no reward of your Father which is in heaven." The craving for earthly visibility cannibalizes eternal reward.

### 4. The Remedy: The "Secret" is About the Heart, Not Geography
When Jesus says, "do not let your left hand know what your right hand is doing, so that your giving may be in secret" (Matthew 6:3-4), He is using a hyperbolic idiom, not necessarily a strict geographical rule. 

The point is not that you cannot hand a dollar to a homeless person on a busy street (which is inherently visible). The point is that your mental focus should be so entirely on God and the recipient that you are practically unaware of your own "performance." 

### Summary
The moral danger in Matthew 6:1 is **idolatry of the audience**. When the craving to be seen drives a good deed, the deed ceases to be an act of worship toward God and becomes an act of self-worship. Visibility is only dangerous when it serves as a mirror for the ego, rather than a window reflecting the character of God.
