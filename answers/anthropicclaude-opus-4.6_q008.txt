# Love of Neighbor as Test of Love for God

## The Logic of the Argument

The verse presents what is essentially an *a fortiori* argument — from the lesser to the greater:

**If you cannot love what is right before you** — a brother you can see, hear, touch, and know — **how can you credibly claim to love what infinitely exceeds your perception?**

This is not arbitrary. It reflects something about the *structure* of love itself. Loving a visible person already demands patience, forgiveness, sacrifice, humility, and the overcoming of selfishness. Loving the invisible God would demand all of this and more, since God makes even greater claims on the whole self without offering the immediate reciprocity of human relationship.

So the person who cannot manage the lesser task while claiming the greater one is exposed as **not understanding what love actually is**.

## The Self-Deception Being Exposed

This is a very specific and dangerous form of self-deception:

**Substituting a religious sentiment for the actual substance of love.** The person genuinely believes they love God because they experience devotion, worship, reverence, or theological conviction. But these feelings cost them nothing in relation to the difficult person standing in front of them.

What makes this deception so stubborn is that:

- **Love directed at the invisible can be entirely on one's own terms.** You can craft an image of God that never irritates you, never needs anything inconvenient from you, never offends your pride. It becomes a relationship with your own projection.
- **Love directed at a visible brother cannot be so controlled.** The real person resists your preferences, exposes your selfishness, and demands concrete action — not merely feeling.
- **Hatred of the brother reveals what the person's will actually chooses** when love becomes costly. The invisible devotion was never tested in the way the visible relationship tests it.

## The Deeper Principle

The verse implies that love is not fundamentally a feeling or a declaration but a **practice with a recognizable shape** — it looks like something when exercised toward real people. A person who hates his brother has demonstrated that this shape is absent from his character. His claim to love God is therefore not just unproven but *positively refuted* by the available evidence.

The self-deception is, in essence: **mistaking the comfort of religious experience for the costly reality of love, and using the invisibility of God as a hiding place from the demands that love actually makes.**
