The command to "love your enemies" as found in Matthew 5:44-45 is one of the most challenging teachings in the Christian faith. It is often considered difficult to take seriously for several reasons:

1. **Sentimental View**: Some people might view this command as overly sentimental or idealistic. They might argue that it's unrealistic to genuinely love someone who is actively causing harm or suffering. This perspective often stems from a misunderstanding of what "loving" means in this context. In Christian theology, "loving" does not necessarily mean feeling affection in the traditional sense, but rather choosing to act in a way that is beneficial and compassionate towards someone, even if they are your enemy.

2. **Strategic View**: From a strategic standpoint, this command can be seen as a form of counter-intuitive wisdom. In many cultures and historical contexts, showing love or compassion to one's enemies could be perceived as a weakness or a sign of naivety. It might seem like a strategic mistake because it could be seen as an invitation to further harm or exploitation. However, from a Christian perspective, this command is not about being naive or weak, but about demonstrating a higher form of moral strength. It's about embodying the values of love, forgiveness, and compassion, which are seen as powerful tools for transformation and reconciliation.

3. **Deeper Form of Moral Strength**: On a deeper level, this command represents a profound moral strength. It requires individuals to transcend their natural inclinations towards self-preservation and retaliation. It calls for a radical shift in perspective, moving from a mindset of fear, anger, or resentment to one of love, forgiveness, and benevolence. This transformation requires immense inner strength and often involves a deep spiritual connection or understanding. It's a call to embody the very nature of God, who is described in the Bible as loving and merciful towards all, even those who are hostile towards Him.

In summary, while the command to love one's enemies might seem difficult or even sentimental, it is fundamentally a call to moral strength. It challenges individuals to rise above their natural instincts and respond to harm with compassion and forgiveness, embodying the very nature of God. This form of love is not sentimental or strategic in the traditional sense, but rather a profound demonstration of moral strength and spiritual maturity.
