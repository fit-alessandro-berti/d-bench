This is an excellent and penetrating question, as Genesis 4:6–7 is one of the most psychologically and theologically dense passages about moral agency in the Hebrew Bible. The text presents a picture of evil that is neither a simple internal impulse nor a purely external demon, but something more dynamic and relational.

Let's break down the picture being drawn and the responsibility it places on the person.

### The Picture of Evil: The Crouching Beast at the Door

The most dominant image comes from the Hebrew of verse 7: “sin lieth at the door.” The Hebrew word for "sin" here is *chatta’t*, but the key verb is **“crouches”** (רֹבֵץ, *rovetz*). This is the same verb used for a predatory animal, specifically a wild beast (like a lion or a demonic creature in Mesopotamian mythology), that is crouching, coiled, and ready to spring.

So, evil is primarily presented as an **external, predatory force** that waits for a moment of vulnerability. It is not a disembodied impulse or a mere habit—it has an active, almost personal, will. It is a presence that dwells at the very threshold of one's consciousness and action (“at the door”).

However, the text immediately complicates this external image with a second, internal dynamic: **“And unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him.”**

This phrase is a direct echo of God’s words to Eve in Genesis 3:16 (“your desire shall be for your husband, and he shall rule over you”). The language is not about romantic or marital desire, but about a **struggle for mastery and control**. The word “desire” (*teshuqah*) implies an urge to dominate, overwhelm, or consume.

Therefore, the full picture is this: Evil is a **crouching, predatory force that desires to master you** (it desires you, to control you). But the moral imperative is that **you must master it**.

### Differentiating the Models

Based on this, we can see how the text relates to your proposed models:

- **Impulse:** Not quite. An impulse is an internal push. The text separates the person from the sin. The sin is "at the door," not "in the heart" (yet). Cain’s anger is the *mood* that makes him vulnerable, but the sin is a separate entity that uses that vulnerability.
- **Beast:** This is the most precise and literal metaphor. The crouching, predatory beast perfectly captures the external, aggressive, and opportunistic nature of the evil waiting to claim Cain.
- **Habit:** Not yet. A habit is a pattern of behavior. Cain has not yet committed the act (murder), so this is the moment *before* the habit is formed. He is given the chance to not form it.
- **Temptation:** Yes, but with a crucial caveat. The temptation is not a mere suggestion or a "voice." It is an active, mounting pressure—a beast that *desires* to consume him. The temptation is to *give in* to the desire to master his brother (the earlier murderous thought), which aligns with the beast’s desire.

So, the text offers a synthesis: **Evil is a predatory, external temptation that seeks to master a person’s will.** It is a beast at the door of action, waiting for the door of the heart to be opened by raw emotion (wrath, fallen countenance).

### The Responsibility Placed on the Person

The text places a profound, classical, and absolute responsibility on Cain. God’s rhetorical questions and commands are not a description of fate, but a call to agency.

1.  **Self-awareness precedes choice:** “Why art thou wroth? Why is thy countenance fallen?” God demands that Cain examine his own emotional state. The responsibility begins with recognizing the dangerous mood that has opened the door.

2.  **The principle of proportionate consequences:** “If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?” God makes a simple, direct connection. Right action leads to acceptance (literally, "lifting up," *se'eth*). This places the power of the outcome entirely in Cain's hands. He is not a victim of his circumstances.

3.  **The dialectic of mastery:** The core command is “thou shalt rule over him [the sin].” This is the hinge of the entire passage. The responsibility is not to *discuss* the temptation, to *pray about it*, or to *wait for it to pass*. It is to **actively and willfully master it**. The verb “rule” (*mashal*) implies governance, sovereignty, and control. Cain is commanded to be the king of his own inner world, not a slave to the beast at the door.

4.  **The possibility of failure is inherent in the command:** The very fact that God says "you must rule over him" implies that the beast is powerful and that failure is a real possibility. The responsibility is to *fight*. The text does not promise God will remove the temptation; it commands Cain to overcome it.

### Conclusion: The Picture of a Moral Warrior

The picture of moral agency in Genesis 4:6–7 is that of a **moral warrior at a threshold**. The person stands at the door of a choice. Outside (and pressing in) is a dangerous, predatory force (evil, sin, temptation) that desires to devour the person's will and actions. Inside (and available) is the power of the human will, commanded by God to be the sovereign master.

**Responsibility is total and active.** It is the duty to:
- **Recognize** the dangerous mood (anger, disappointment).
- **Understand** the direct consequence of one's own actions.
- **Actively exert mastery** over the external, personal force of evil that seeks to overpower you.

Evil is not an excuse. It is an opponent. The person is not a passive vessel for either good or evil, but an active agent who must choose to **rule over the beast** or be **ruled by it**. The text’s tragedy is that Cain, knowing this, chose to open the door.
