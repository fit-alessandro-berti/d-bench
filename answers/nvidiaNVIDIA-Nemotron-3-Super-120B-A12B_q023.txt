

**Why the verse mentions both “way” and “thoughts”**

Isaiah 55:6‑7 reads  

> *“Seek ye the LORD while he may be found, call ye upon him while he is near:  
>  Let the wicked **forsake his way**, and the unrighteous **forsake his thoughts**:  
>  and let him return unto the LORD, and he will have mercy upon him; and to our God, for he will abundantly pardon.”*  

The two objects of the verb *forsake* are:

1. **“his way”** – the *external* pattern of life, the concrete actions, habits, and conduct that the wicked person follows.  
2. **“his thoughts”** – the *internal* realm, the motives, imaginations, plans, and inner reasoning that drive those actions.

By pairing them, the prophet makes it clear that genuine repentance is not a half‑measure. One cannot merely change outward behavior while keeping the same corrupt heart, nor can one merely remodel inner thoughts while continuing to act in the old sinful patterns. Both dimensions must be abandoned together.

---

### What this implies about outward conduct, inward imagination, and mercy

| Aspect | What the verse highlights | What it implies for repentance |
|--------|---------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Outward conduct (“way”)** | The wicked person's *way* is the visible, habitual conduct that can be observed by others. | Repentance must include a concrete turning‑away from sinful actions – stopping the harmful practices, adopting righteous deeds, and living in a manner that aligns with God’s commandments. |
| **Inward imagination / motive (“thoughts”)** | The unrighteous person's *thoughts* are the inner scheming, desires, rationalizations, and fantasies that fuel the wicked way. | Repentance must also involve a change of heart: renouncing evil imaginations, rejecting selfish plans, and allowing God to renew the mind (cf. Romans 12:2; Ezekiel 36:26). |
| **Possibility of mercy** | After both way and thoughts are forsaken, the promise follows: *“let him return unto the LORD, and he will have mercy upon him; … abundantly pardon.”* | Mercy is not granted for a superficial change; it is assured when the sinner genuinely abandons both the external sinful conduct *and* the internal sinful imagination. The pardon is “abundant” because God’s grace meets the totality of the person’s turning—both outward and inward. |

In short, the verse teaches that **true repentance is holistic**:

* **External:** a visible break from sinful *ways* (actions, lifestyle).  
* **Internal:** a genuine renunciation of sinful *thoughts* (motives, imaginations, desires).  

Only when both are surrendered does the invitation to “return unto the LORD” become effective, and the divine response is the abundant mercy and pardon promised in verse 8.

---

### Practical take‑away for the reader

1. **Examine your “way.”** Identify specific habits, behaviors, or patterns that contradict God’s will (e.g., dishonesty, violence, neglect of the needy). Commit to laying them aside.  
2. **Examine your “thoughts.”** Look at the underlying attitudes, justifications, fantasies, or secret desires that sustain those habits (e.g., pride, greed, lust, self‑justification). Ask God to renew your mind and heart.  
3. **Trust the promise.** When you have honestly forsaken both the outward *way* and the inward *thoughts*, you can rely on God’s faithful word: He will receive you back, show you mercy, and pardon you abundantly.  

Thus, the pairing of “way” and “thoughts” in Isaiah 55:6‑7 is a theological shorthand for the full‑scope repentance that makes divine mercy possible.
