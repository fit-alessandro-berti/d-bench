
This is a perceptive and important question, and the passage of Ephesians 4:31–32 invites a nuanced answer. Let’s break it down step by step, using both the text and some theological and psychological insight.

### 1. **Structure of the Passage**

Ephesians 4:31 begins with a **command** for the Christian community to "put away" certain negative attitudes and behaviors:  
- bitterness  
- wrath  
- anger  
- clamour (noisiness, quarreling)  
- evil speaking  
- all malice

These are all described as things to be "put away" (ἀπολλύστεσαι)—the Greek verb suggests removal, elimination, or cleansing. It’s not just about feeling momentarily angry or having a bad moment; it’s about a **lifestyle, a habitual posture**, a way of relating that is unhealthy for the Christian life.

Then, in verse 32, Paul pivots to the **positive counterbalance**:  
"Be ye kind one to another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you."

Here, the command shifts from **removal of negatives** to the **practice of positives**: kindness, tenderness, forgiveness.

### 2. **Why Are They Placed Together?**

Paul is **contrasting**. He’s not just telling believers to stop feeling certain emotions, but to transform their whole character by replacing those unhealthy dispositions with godly ones. The "put away" list is not just about suppressing emotions in the moment but about eliminating the root causes of division and ungodliness in their relationships.

#### **Bitterness as More Than a Feeling**

Bitterness is not just a fleeting emotion. Scripture often warns that bitterness is a **deep-seated attitude** (see Colossians 3:9, "Let all bitterness and wrath and anger be put away from you, with malice..."). It can be:
- A **mental habit**, cultivated by repeated hurts or unhealed wounds (see James 1:22).
- A **posture of the heart**, shaping how one relates to others (see Romans 12:19).
- **Contagious**, as it often spreads through communities (see Proverbs 24:12-13).

In other words, bitterness is not only an emotion but a **pattern of thinking and responding**, making it more than just a feeling.

#### **Forgiveness as the Counter-Posture**

To "put away" bitterness is to open the door to forgiveness. Forgiveness is not just an act when someone apologizes; it’s a **choice made in the heart** (see Matthew 6:14), a response to how God forgave us first (Ephesians 1:7). When we forgive, we reject the holding on to resentment or anger (which is the "bitterness" that festers), and instead choose kindness and compassion.

### 3. **The Theology of the Contrasts**

Paul frames this as a **moral transformation** within the Christian community. Just as Christ has forgiven the believers’ sins (Ephesians 1:7), so they are called to forgive one another, modeling God’s own character. This is not a legalistic "stop feeling this or that," but a **radical reorientation of heart and behavior**.

The command to "put away" bitterness is thus paired with the imperative to **practice forgiveness**, which is the antithesis of holding on to negative feelings. Bitterness is the seed of division and hatred; forgiveness is the fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22).

### 4. **Practical Implications**

- **Bitterness is contagious and destructive:** It poisons relationships, breeds division, and can harden the heart (see Luke 6:37).
- **Forgiveness is healing and unifying:** It restores relationships, brings peace, and reflects God’s nature (see 1 Peter 4:8).
- **Both are choices:** We choose to let go of bitterness (not by suppressing emotion, but by changing our mindset and heart), and to respond with forgiveness (which is itself a form of love).

### 5. **Summary Answer to Your Question**

**Why are bitterness and forgiveness placed directly against each other?**  
They are placed in opposition because they represent two fundamentally different ways of relating: one is destructive and divisive (bitterness), the other is redemptive and unifying (forgiveness). The passage is not just about not feeling bitter, but about **replacing a harmful heart posture with a godly one**—mirroring how God has forgiven us. Bitterness is to be removed because it is a persistent, harmful attitude that breeds conflict and estrangement; forgiveness is to be practiced because it reflects God’s own forgiveness and fosters love and healing in the community.

**Is bitterness treated as a feeling, habit, or contagious?**  
Yes—it is more than a fleeting feeling; it is a deep-seated attitude or habit that can infect the whole community and is resistant to change unless addressed at the heart level. The call to put it away is thus a call to change one’s foundational responses and dispositions, not just to control temporary emotions.

In essence, Ephesians 4:31–32 is a call to **transformation**: from a life marked by hostility and resentment to one characterized by love, mercy, and the radical forgiveness that flows from being made like Christ.
