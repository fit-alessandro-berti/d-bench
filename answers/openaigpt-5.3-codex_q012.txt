Great question. **Jeremiah 17:9–10 is not just cynical; it’s diagnostic.**

### 1) Pessimistic or realistic?
In context, Jeremiah is confronting Judah’s persistent covenant unfaithfulness. So this is not a detached statement about psychology; it’s a prophetic exposure of sin.

- **“Deceitful”** (v.9) means the heart is tricky, not transparent even to itself.
- **“Desperately wicked”** can also carry the sense of being *incurable/sick beyond easy remedy*.
- Then v.10 balances it: **God searches what humans cannot fully read** and judges rightly.

So: it’s **realistic about self-deception under sin**, and therefore sobering, but not nihilistic. The point is not “humans are unknowable forever,” but “you are not a reliable final judge of your own motives—God is.”

### 2) How to talk about motives if the heart is unreliable?
With **humility, caution, and accountability**.

A good biblical posture is:

1. **Speak tentatively about your own motives**  
   “As far as I can tell, I wanted to help—but I may be mixed.”

2. **Avoid overconfident motive-reading of others**  
   We can evaluate actions and fruit more reliably than inner intent (Matt. 7:16).

3. **Invite God’s examination**  
   “Search me, O God…” (Ps. 139:23–24) is the right response to Jer. 17:9–10.

4. **Use external checks**  
   Scripture, wise community, correction, and time often reveal what we couldn’t see.

5. **Judge by fruit, not self-story**  
   v.10 speaks of “ways” and “fruit of doings.” Real patterns matter.

In short: **Jeremiah 17 says don’t trust your heart as supreme authority; submit your heart to God’s searching light.** That gives both realism and hope.
