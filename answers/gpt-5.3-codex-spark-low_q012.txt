• Short answer: mostly realistic in a theological sense, and yes—it's both a warning about self-knowledge limits and a
  call to rely on God’s judgment.

  Jeremiah 17:9 is describing human tendency to deceive ourselves (deceitful, desperately wicked) rather than saying
  people are irredeemably evil in every instant.
  Jeremiah 17:10 balances it: God searches the heart and evaluates by actions (“fruit of his doings”). So it’s not
  hopeless nihilism; it’s diagnosis plus accountability plus hope for transformed life.

  How to talk about motives if the heart is unreliable:

  1. Distinguish confession from self-interpretation.
      - Don’t assume you have full insight into your motives by introspection alone.
      - Speak in terms of uncertainty: “I think I meant X, but I may be missing blind spots.”
  2. Judge by observable fruit.
      - Follow Jesus’ line: repeated patterns, outcomes, and character matter more than claimed intentions.
  3. Use community and humility.
      - Invite trusted others to reflect back what they see.
      - Motive talk becomes safer when it includes correction, apology, and willingness to revise.
  4. Keep a theological frame.
      - If you hold this passage as Scripture, ultimate truth about motives belongs to God (who “searches the heart”),
        so the practical posture is repentance and dependence, not self-invention.

  A practical phrasing can be:
  “My stated intention may be good, but I don’t always see my own motives; I’m willing to be shown the pattern in my
  actions and change what’s wrong.”
