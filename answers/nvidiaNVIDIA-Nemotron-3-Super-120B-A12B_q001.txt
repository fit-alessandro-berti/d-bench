

**Moral agency in Genesis 4:6‑7**

The dialogue God has with Cain after his offering is rejected can be read as a compact anthropology of choice:

| Element in the verse | How it is portrayed | What it implies for agency |
|----------------------|---------------------|----------------------------|
| **God’s question** – “Why art thou wroth? and why is thy countenance fallen?” | Calls attention to Cain’s internal state (anger, dejection). | Cain is already aware of his feeling; he is capable of self‑reflection. |
| **“If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?”** | Presents a clear alternative: righteous action leads to divine acceptance. | The moral good is *available* to Cain; he can choose it. |
| **“If thou doest not well, sin lieth at the door.”** | Sin is depicted as something that *lies waiting* — a presence at the threshold, ready to enter. | Evil is not an inevitable fate; it is a potential that can be acted upon or resisted. |
| **“And unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him.”** (most modern translations render this as “its desire is for you, but you must rule over it.”) | Sin is given a *desire* that seeks to dominate Cain, yet Cain is commanded to *rule* over it. | The text assigns Cain the power to master the impulse; responsibility lies in exercising that mastery. |

### What kind of “evil” is being described?

1. **Impulse / temptation** – The image of something crouching at the door, eager to enter, resembles a sudden urge or temptation that appears when one’s guard is down.  
2. **Not a settled habit or intrinsic nature** – The text does not say Cain *is* sinful; it says sin *lies* waiting, suggesting a conditional, external influence rather than an ingrained disposition.  
3. **Beast‑like metaphor** – The “crouching” language evokes a predatory animal, underscoring the danger of letting the impulse gain control, but it does not reduce evil to a mere beast; the beast is subordinate to the human’s capacity to rule it.  

Thus, evil is presented primarily as **a lurking temptation or impulse** that seeks to master the person, not as an unavoidable fate or an inherent nature.

### Responsibility placed on the person

- **Awareness and self‑examination** – Cain is asked to discern why he is angry; moral agency begins with recognizing one’s internal state.  
- **Choice to act well** – The conditional “if thou doest well…” makes the good action a viable option that Cain can actually take.  
- **Active mastery** – The command to “rule over him” (sin) requires deliberate effort: Cain must exert control over the impulse rather than passively succumb to it.  
- **Accountability** – Failure to rule over sin results in its influence (“sin lieth at the door”), implying that the outcome — acceptance or rejection — rests on Cain’s decision.

### Summary

Genesis 4:6‑7 sketches a picture of **moral agency as the ability to choose between a readily available good and a tempting evil that lies in wait**. Evil is framed as an **impulse/temptation** (described with beast‑like imagery) that seeks to dominate the human, but the human is entrusted with the responsibility to **recognize the impulse, choose the good, and exercise rule over it**. Responsibility, therefore, rests squarely on the individual: to act well and to master the sin that crouches at the door.
