Amos isn’t attacking worship *as such*; he’s attacking worship that has become the religious face of corruption. In that situation, God doesn’t ask for a “better balance” because the worship itself has turned into a lie.

### 1. The setting: intense piety, deep injustice

In Amos 5 the people are:

- Keeping **feast days**, **sacrifices**, and **music** (vv. 21–23).
- At the same time:
  - “You trample on the poor” (5:11)
  - “You afflict the righteous, you take a bribe, and turn aside the needy in the gate” (5:12)
  - “You who turn justice to wormwood” (5:7)

So they are very religious and very unjust.

In the covenant law, sacrifices, feasts, and songs were *good* and commanded. But they were never meant to stand on their own; they were signs of a relationship marked by **justice (mishpat)** and **righteousness (tsedaqah)**. When those disappear, the signs become counterfeits.

### 2. Why outright rejection, not “more balance”?

Two key reasons:

#### a) Injustice doesn’t just “unbalance” worship; it *poisons* it.

Think of a marriage: if someone is openly unfaithful, brings gifts, speaks sweetly, and says, “Let’s just have a better balance between love and loyalty,” the gifts aren’t neutral—they’re offensive. They try to *cover* betrayal instead of ending it.

That is Amos’s point. Their offerings and songs are not innocent; they are:

- An attempt to **buy God off** while keeping their oppression.
- A way to **feel righteous** while refusing to be righteous.

So God says, in effect: “I reject your worship because your very way of worshipping me is to use religion as cover for evil.” You can’t fix that with “better balance”; you must abandon the fraud.

#### b) Their worship has become an alternative to obedience, not an expression of it.

Sacrifices and feasts were supposed to be the overflow of lives lived under God’s rule. Here they have become a substitute for obedience:

> “I hate, I despise your feast days…”  
> “…Take away from me the noise of your songs…”

Notice the pronouns: **your** feasts, **your** songs—God no longer owns them. The system still uses his name, but it no longer represents his character.

So God isn’t saying, “Add some social concern to your worship.” He’s saying, “What you are doing in my name is so opposed to who I am that I want none of it.”

### 3. The specific fraud Amos exposes

Several layers of spiritual and moral fraud are being called out at once:

#### 1) **Ethical hypocrisy**

They perform all the right rituals while:

- Perverting courts (“in the gate”)
- Exploiting the poor
- Living in luxury built on injustice (5:11)

This is not imperfect people stumbling; it is a stable pattern where injustice is normal and worship is the respectable veneer.

#### 2) **Religious laundering of injustice**

Their religion is functioning as a laundering mechanism:

- It **cleans their reputation** while leaving their practices untouched.
- It allows them to feel “close to God” while they crush the weak.

So worship itself is now working *for* injustice, not against it. That is why God calls the songs “noise.”

#### 3) **Presuming on God’s favor**

In the wider chapter, they long for “the day of the LORD” (5:18), assuming God is on their side. Their rituals make them confident that God will punish others, not them.

Amos exposes this as delusion: you cannot invoke the God of justice to bless a society built on injustice.

#### 4) **Misrepresenting God’s character**

When God’s name is used to uphold an unjust order, the problem is not only ethical but also theological: it **lies about who God is**. It suggests:

- God is impressed by aesthetics—beautiful music, solemn worship—while indifferent to the oppressed.
- God can be “handled” by proper rites instead of honored by obedience.

To keep participating in that is to bear God’s name falsely (a deep form of spiritual fraud).

### 4. What God wants instead: justice as worship

The climax explains the alternative:

> “But let judgment [justice] run down as waters,
> and righteousness as a mighty stream.”

This is not “add a little social ethics to your worship set.” It is:

- Justice and righteousness **as the central, flowing reality** of life with God.
- Personal, legal, economic, and communal right-ordering that reflects God’s own character.

In biblical terms, this *is* worship. Ritual without this is idolatry-in-God’s-name.

---

So the passage rejects worship so sharply because the worship itself has become an instrument of deceit:

- A pious cover for systemic injustice  
- A way to feel close to God while resisting his will  
- A misrepresentation of the God of justice to the world  

In that scenario, God doesn’t ask them to “rebalance” worship and ethics. He calls their whole religious performance a fraud and demands that the riverbed be cleared so that justice and righteousness can finally flow.
