To grapple with Matthew 5:44–45 is to run headfirst into one of the most radical demands in human literature. It is precisely because Jesus’s command feels so utterly disconnected from the mechanics of survival that it is rarely taken seriously. 

To answer your question, we have to look at why it feels impossible, and then unpack whether it is sentimentality, strategy, or something far more formidable.

---

### Part 1: Why is enemy-love so difficult to take seriously?

Enemy-love is difficult because it runs contrary to the foundational grammar of human nature and social order, which is built on **reciprocity**. 

1. **The Biological Imperative of Self-Preservation:** Evolution has wired us for in-group loyalty and out-group suspicion. From an evolutionary standpoint, loving your enemy looks like a genetic dead end. It feels biologically unsafe.
2. **The Demand for Justice:** Human beings have an innate, fierce sense of fairness. When someone harms us, our moral intuition demands retribution, compensation, or at least accountability. Jesus’s command feels like a suspension of justice—an insult to the victim.
3. **The Stigma of Weakness:** In a competitive world, turning the other cheek or praying for an abuser looks like cowardice. Modern culture, much like the ancient Roman world, respects power, self-assertion, and the ability to dominate or cancel one's opponents. 
4. **Cognitive Dissonance:** Most people assume Jesus is using hyperbole. We reduce it to a poetic metaphor because taking it literally requires a transformation of the ego that most of us are simply unwilling to undergo.

---

### Part 2: Is it sentimental, strategic, or a deeper form of moral strength?

It is certainly **not sentimental**. Sentimentality implies warm feelings, emotional mushiness, and the absence of boundaries. You cannot feel "warm affection" for someone who is actively trying to destroy you; forcing yourself to feel it would be psychologically dishonest and emotionally toxic. 

So, is it strategy or moral strength? It is actually a fusion of **the highest form of moral strength** and a **paradoxical, world-shattering strategy**.

#### 1. It is the Ultimate Form of Moral Strength (Agency)
To hate your enemies is actually to be controlled by them. If someone treats you cruelly and you respond with hatred, your emotional state, your peace of mind, and your actions are now being dictated by that person. They are pulling your strings.

By commanding enemy-love, Jesus is offering a path of absolute sovereignty. To love an enemy—to refuse to hate them—means **refusing to let another person dictate the condition of your soul.** As Martin Luther King Jr. noted, *“Returning hate for hate multiplies hate, adding deeper darkness to a night already devoid of stars.”* Refusing to hate requires an ironclad, diamond-hard moral discipline. It is the strength to break the cycle of retaliation.

#### 2. It is a Deconstructive Strategy (Imitating God)
Notice the reason Jesus gives: *“That ye may be the children of your Father... for he maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good.”* 

This points to a cosmic strategy. God does not govern the world by dividing humanity instantly into winners and losers, saints and sinners, using the sword. Instead, God maintains a posture of relentless, patient grace, sustaining the very people who rebel against Him. 

When humans adopt this posture, it acts as a subversive social strategy. It completely disarms the adversary. As the theologian Walter Wink pointed out in *The Powers That Be*, Jesus’s methods (like turning the other cheek) were not passive submission; they were non-violent tactics that exposed the injustice of the oppressor by refusing to play by the oppressor's rules. Hatred validates the enemy's narrative of "us versus them." Unwarranted love shatters that narrative and forces the enemy to confront their own humanity.

---

### Conclusion

Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it is entirely **un-natural**. But Jesus was not asking for what is natural; He was introducing what theologians call *grace*—a supernatural posture. 

It is not sentimentality. It is the hardest, most muscular form of love possible. It is the refusal to hate, the refusal to dehumanize, and the stubborn insistence that the cycle of violence stops *right here, with me*.
