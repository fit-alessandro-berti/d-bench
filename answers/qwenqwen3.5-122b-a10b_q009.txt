This passage from Romans 12 presents one of the most counter-intuitive ethical commands in the New Testament. To answer your questions, we must look at the definition of "victory" Paul proposes and the nature of the power required to enact it.

### 1. How does this passage define victory over evil?

In the conventional human understanding, victory over an enemy is defined by **dominance**: the enemy is defeated, silenced, punished, or removed. In Romans 12:21, Paul radically redefines victory as **moral transformation.**

*   **Breaking the Cycle:** Victory is not achieved by inflicting harm, but by refusing to propagate it. If an enemy acts with evil and you respond with evil, evil has won because it has successfully infected a second person. By responding with good, you stop the infection.
*   **Preservation of Integrity:** Victory is defined as the believer refusing to be "overcome." If hatred makes you hateful, or violence makes you violent, the enemy has conquered your character. Victory is maintaining one's own moral integrity despite provocation.
*   **Redemption over Retribution:** The goal of victory is not the destruction of the opponent, but the neutralization of the evil itself. The "victory" is the potential restoration of the relationship or the conviction of the enemy's conscience.

### 2. Is the command weak, manipulative, or genuinely strong?

This command is **genuinely strong**, though it is often mistaken for weakness or manipulation by those viewing it through a lens of power dynamics.

**Why it is not Weak:**
Passivity is not the same as this command. "Feeding an enemy" is an **active** pursuit. It requires discipline, courage, and the expenditure of resources. It takes far less energy to ignore an enemy or retaliate against them than it does to actively seek their well-being while they are hostile. It is the difference between a coward who hides and a soldier who disarms himself to show peace.

**Why it is not Manipulative:**
Manipulation seeks to use an action as a means to a selfish end (e.g., "I will be nice to you so you feel guilty and do what I want").
*   **The Intent:** Paul commands feeding the enemy "if he hunger." The motive is the *enemy's need*, not the actor's gain.
*   **The Outcome:** If the enemy does not repent, the believer is still commanded to do good. The "good" is not a transaction; it is a reflection of the believer's nature. Therefore, it is not a trap, but a gift.

**Why it is Genuinely Strong:**
*   **Active Love:** It requires "aggressive goodness." It is not merely "not hitting back"; it is "reaching out."
*   **Disarming Power:** As Martin Luther King Jr. famously noted, this method disarms the opponent. It creates a cognitive dissonance in the aggressor who expects hate but receives love. This shatters their psychological framework for conflict.
*   **The Cross:** Theologically, this is the strongest possible command because it mirrors the ultimate sacrifice. It is the power of the Cross, where love absorbs violence without returning it.

### The Meaning of "Coals of Fire"

To fully grasp the strength of this passage, one must understand the metaphor in verse 20: *"thou shalt heap coals of fire on his head."*

There are two common interpretations:
1.  **Punitive:** That the good deeds will make the enemy suffer more (a form of "burning them up"). This contradicts the command to "overcome evil with good."
2.  **Redemptive:** Most scholars agree this alludes to Proverbs 25:21–22, where the "coals" represent **shame or conviction.** Just as coals are used to kindle a fire, the unexpected kindness of the victim burns away the hardness of the aggressor's heart. It is the heat of conviction that leads to repentance.

### Conclusion

Romans 12:20–21 defines victory not as the **annihilation of the enemy**, but as the **annihilation of evil's power** to dictate human behavior.

The command is not weak because it requires immense self-control; it is not manipulative because it seeks the enemy's welfare regardless of their response; and it is genuinely strong because it uses the weapon of love to break the cycle of violence. It demands that the believer become a fortress of goodness that evil cannot penetrate.
