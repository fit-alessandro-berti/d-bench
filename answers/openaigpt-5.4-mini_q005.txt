Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it runs against several deep human instincts at once: self-protection, fairness, tribal loyalty, and the desire to see harm answered in kind. It sounds impossible because it asks for a response that is not governed by retaliation.

A few ways to think about it:

### 1. It is not sentimental
Enemy-love is often mistaken for a soft feeling or emotional warmth toward those who harm us. But in Matthew 5, Jesus is not describing affection; he is describing action:

- **love**
- **bless**
- **do good**
- **pray**

These are concrete, disciplined forms of goodwill. They do not require pretending evil is harmless or that injustice does not matter. Enemy-love is not “I feel nice about you.” It is “I refuse to let your evil determine my moral response.”

### 2. It is not merely strategic
A strategic reading would say: love enemies because it may improve society, reduce conflict, or eventually win them over. Those effects can happen, but they are not the primary point. Jesus grounds the command in the character of God:

> “that ye may be the children of your Father… for he maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good”

That means enemy-love is not mainly a technique for better outcomes. It is a way of participating in God’s own generosity. God gives life, sun, and rain even to the undeserving. The disciple imitates that pattern.

### 3. It is a deeper form of moral strength
Enemy-love is hard because it requires a kind of inner freedom that vengeance does not allow. To hate back is easy; it keeps us locked in the enemy’s moral orbit. To love an enemy is to refuse to become what harmed you.

That is why it is morally strong:
- It resists dehumanization.
- It separates the person from the evil they do.
- It keeps justice from becoming revenge.
- It preserves the possibility of reconciliation without demanding it.

This kind of love does not deny justice. It places justice inside a larger commitment to the good of the other and the good of the world.

### Why it feels unbelievable
We tend to measure moral seriousness by proportional response: punish the guilty, reward the good. Enemy-love seems to break that grammar. But Jesus is pointing to a higher measure: not “What do they deserve from me?” but “What does faithful love require of me?”

So enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it asks for a transformed imagination. It is neither naïve sentiment nor mere pragmatic diplomacy. It is a costly, deliberate participation in divine mercy—a form of strength that can absorb wrong without reproducing it.
