This passage is the conclusion of the Parable of the Prodigal Son (Luke 15:11-32), where the elder brother reacts to the father’s joyful welcome of the repentant younger brother.

### Analyzing the Elder Brother’s Deepest Problem

Let’s briefly touch on **each** of the options before synthesizing:

---

#### 1. **Anger**
He *is* angry, but this anger seems to be a symptom rather than the root. His anger is triggered by the perceived unfairness, but anger itself is not the most fundamental problem.

#### 2. **Envy**
Envy is present. He feels slighted that his brother, who squandered the inheritance, gets a feast, when he feels he’s received nothing comparable for his loyalty. However, envy too seems tied to something deeper.

#### 3. **Wounded Fairness**
He believes he deserves more because of his obedience. His complaint, “neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment,” and “thou never gavest me a kid,” reveals a spirit of *merit-based relationship*—it’s unfair to him that his brother, who was wayward, is celebrated.

#### 4. **Pride in Obedience**
He’s proud of his record, and it separates him from both his brother (“this thy son”) and his father (“lo, these many years do I serve thee”). His performance has become grounds for exclusion and resentment.

#### 5. **Discomfort with Mercy Itself**
At core, the elder brother cannot rejoice with his father because he cannot stomach this *extravagant mercy*. He’s uncomfortable with grace that is given *freely* and not *earned*.

---

### **Synthesis: The Deepest Issue**

The *deepest* problem is **a discomfort with mercy itself**, rooted in **self-righteousness** and a transactional view of the father’s love.

- **He thinks belonging and celebration must be earned**—by obedience and good behavior.
- **He cannot accept that the Father’s mercy “breaks the rules” of fairness** to rescue and restore the lost.
- **His pride in obedience blinds him to his own need for grace**; he is “outside” the celebration, not because the father rejects him, but because he rejects the way the father loves.

Thus, **his deepest problem is his inability to embrace the radical, unearned mercy of the father**—he is “lost” in a different way from his younger brother.

---

**Conclusion:**  
The elder brother’s deepest problem is his discomfort with mercy itself—he cannot accept a love that is not based on merit. This discomfort, rooted in self-righteousness, leaves him alienated from both his brother and his father’s heart.
