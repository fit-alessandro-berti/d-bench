In Galatians 6:1, Paul is not just giving a procedure for correction. He is describing a *moral posture* without which correction becomes destructive.

The verse holds together in one sentence because it is an antidote to Galatians 5:26, the verse just before it:

> "Let us not be desirous of vain glory, provoking one another, envying one another."

Vain correction loves to be right, to win, to expose. Spiritual correction loves to heal.

### 1. The premise: "if a man be overtaken in a fault"

The word is *prolambano* - to be surprised, caught, tripped up. This is not the picture of a defiant, calculating predator, but of someone who has been caught by sin faster than his intention could keep up with him. 

That immediately requires a different posture. You don't hunt a man who has been overtaken; you rescue him.

And Paul begins with *"Brethren"* - you are family, not judge and defendant.

### 2. The agent: "ye which are spiritual"

*Hoi pneumatikoi* - not "ye which are perfect" or "ye which are officers." The qualification is not rank, but walk. In context, that means those who are bearing the fruit of Galatians 5:22-23, walking by the Spirit.

The only person safe to correct is the person who is already under correction himself by the Holy Spirit.

### 3. The aim: "restore such an one"

The word is *katartizete*. It is a surgeon's verb and a fisherman's verb.

It is used for setting a dislocated bone back into joint, and for mending torn nets in Mark 1:19. The image is not penal but medicinal. The person is not a problem to be disposed of, but a limb out of joint or a net made useless that is to be made whole and useful again.

If restoration is not your aim, you are disqualified from the work. A lot of correction wants to punish, shame, or win an argument. Paul says the question is always: "Will this make this brother whole again?"

### 4. The manner: "in the spirit of meekness"

*En pneumati praotes*. *Praotes* is not weakness. It is strength under control - power that could crush but chooses to be gentle enough to heal.

Why is it essential? Because a bone cannot be set with a hammer. If you come in harshness, even if you are right, you will break what you are trying to restore. The person overtaken is already bruised. Meekness is the gentleness that the wounded can tolerate.

### 5. The guardrail: "considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted"

*Skopon seauton* - keep a watchful eye on yourself. This is the self-suspicion that holds it all together.

Without it, two temptations are certain:

**First, the temptation to the same sin.** The person who confronts another with secret pride - "I would never do that" - is already on the edge of the same pit. As Augustine said, there is no sin one man commits that another man could not commit.

**Second, and more common, the temptation of a worse sin.** You may not fall into his sin, but you will fall into pride, harshness, vainglory, and a censorious spirit - which are more spiritually deadly. You set his dislocated bone, but in the process, you contract gangrene.

This is why the three must be kept together:

* **Without restoration as the goal**, meekness becomes mere niceness that never tells the truth, and self-suspicion becomes an excuse to never speak.

* **Without meekness as the manner**, restoration becomes a brutal surgery where the patient dies of your bedside manner.

* **Without self-suspicion as the guardrail**, both restoration and meekness become theater. You will perform humility while secretly enjoying your superiority.

Paul's logic is this: The only man safe enough to confront a brother is the man who knows deeply that he himself needs the same grace. He does not come as a superior to an inferior, but as one beggar showing another beggar where he found bread - and remembering that tomorrow, the roles may be reversed.

That is the moral posture: not "I am correcting you," but "We are both under mercy, and because I know my own vulnerability, I want to help you back to wholeness, gently."
