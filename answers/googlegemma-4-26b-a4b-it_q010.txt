Based on the text provided, here is an analysis of the power being described and the psychological tension inherent in its expression.

### 1. What kind of power is being praised here?

The power being praised is not **coercive power** (the ability to force others to obey), but **moral and ontological power** (the strength of character and the capacity to self-determine). 

In classical theology and philosophy, this is often referred to as *kenosis* (self-emptying). The passage describes a paradox of strength:

*   **The Power of Sovereignty:** The text begins by establishing the subject's supreme status: "being in the form of God" and being "equal with God." This establishes that the subject possesses absolute, unassailable authority.
*   **The Power of Volition:** The true "power" displayed is the ability to *refrain* from using that authority. It is the power of self-restraint. True strength is demonstrated not by how much one can grasp or demand, but by what one is capable of giving up.
*   **The Power of Descent:** Instead of upward mobility (climbing to achieve more), this power is defined by downward mobility (descending to serve). It is the power to choose vulnerability over invulnerability.

### 2. Why can humility joined to greatness feel more unsettling than open domination?

Open domination is predictable; humility joined to greatness is a paradox that disrupts our standard social and psychological maps. It feels unsettling for several reasons:

**A. The Subversion of Expectations**
Human social structures are built on the expectation that those with power will use it to protect their status, accumulate resources, and exert influence. When a "great" being (one with the "form of God") chooses the "form of a servant," it violates the logic of hierarchy. This creates **cognitive dissonance**: our brains struggle to reconcile "Supreme Being" with "Lowly Servant."

**B. The Loss of an Enemy to Fight**
Open domination provides a clear target. A tyrant is easy to identify; you know where the threat lies, and you can organize resistance against it. However, a great being who practices radical humility offers no friction. You cannot fight someone who refuses to assert their dominance. This leaves the observer without a clear stance, creating a sense of psychological instability or "unsettledness."

**C. The Mirror of Moral Accountability**
Domination is an external threat, but humility is an internal provocation. If a being of infinite greatness can humble themselves to the point of death, it exposes the vanity, pride, and small-mindedness of the observer. Domination makes us feel like victims (which allows us to feel righteous in our resentment), but the humility of the great makes us feel *unworthy*. It shifts the conflict from a struggle against an external oppressor to a struggle against one’s own ego.

**D. The Unpredictability of Love**
Domination is driven by self-interest, which is easy to calculate. Humility driven by greatness is often driven by sacrificial love, which is mathematically irrational. We find it unsettling because we cannot "game" the system; you cannot bribe, threaten, or manipulate someone who has already surrendered their reputation and their life for the sake of others.
