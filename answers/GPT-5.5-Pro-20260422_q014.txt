The text says **both**, but the force of the passage leans toward **treasure trains the heart**, and therefore also **reveals** it.

Jesus does **not** say:

> “Where your heart is, there your treasure will be.”

He says:

> “Where your treasure is, there will your heart be also.”

That order matters. The heart follows the treasure. What one stores, protects, counts on, and imagines as one’s good becomes the place where the heart takes up residence.

So treasure is not merely a symptom of the heart; it is also a **school for the heart**.

### Possession becomes more than possession

The passage is not condemning the mere fact of having things. The key phrase is “lay up for yourselves.” This is possession turned into security, identity, future, and self-protection.

A thing becomes “treasure” when it is no longer just something one uses, but something one **rests in**.

Food, clothing, money, reputation, influence, career, comfort, even religious respectability can become treasure when they become the stored-up good around which the self organizes itself.

### Attention follows treasure

Earthly treasure is vulnerable: moth, rust, thieves. Because it can be lost, it demands vigilance. One must watch it, maintain it, insure it, defend it, compare it, increase it.

So treasure captures attention.

What you treasure keeps calling your mind back to itself. You check on it. You worry over it. You fantasize about it. You measure yourself by it. The treasure becomes a center of gravity for thought.

That is why the next image in Matthew 6 is about the eye: the way one looks determines the condition of the whole person. Treasure directs attention; attention shapes perception.

### Love follows attention

The “heart” in biblical language is not merely emotion. It is the center of desire, trust, choice, and allegiance.

So Jesus is describing a progression:

**What you store, you watch.
What you watch, you desire.
What you desire, you come to love.
What you love, you serve.**

That is why the passage shortly leads to, “Ye cannot serve God and mammon.” Treasure does not remain external. It becomes master.

### So the sentence is diagnostic and formative

It is diagnostic because your treasure shows where your heart already is. Look at what you protect, fear losing, sacrifice for, and return to in thought, and you will learn something about your heart.

But it is formative because Jesus gives a command: “Lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven.” He is not only saying, “Your treasure exposes you.” He is saying, “Choose your treasure carefully, because your heart will be drawn after it.”

In that sense, the passage is deeply practical. The heart is not trained by direct command alone: “Love God more.” It is trained by relocating treasure. Put your goods, hopes, labor, generosity, and imagination toward what belongs to God and endures with God, and the heart begins to live there too.

A compact way to put it:

**Treasure reveals the heart because treasure has already trained the heart.
And treasure trains the heart because the heart follows what it has learned to count as wealth.**
