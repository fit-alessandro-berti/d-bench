To say that Luke 6:37 implies "moral judgments never matter" is to fundamentally misunderstand the nature of the text, as well as the historical and linguistic context of Jesus’s teachings. 

The verse is not a prohibition against recognizing right and wrong. Rather, it is a severe warning against the *disposition of the heart*—specifically, the transition from **discernment** (a necessary act of moral clarity) to **condemnation** (a usurpation of divine authority).

Here is an exploration of how Luke 6:37 differs from moral relativism, and the crucial distinction between discernment and the appetite to condemn.

---

### 1. How this is different from "moral judgments never matter"

To claim that moral judgments never matter is to claim that nothing is objectively wrong, or that we cannot evaluate actions. This is not what Jesus teaches. In fact, to say "moral judgments never matter" is itself a massive moral judgment.

Jesus explicitly commands his followers to engage in moral evaluation, but he strictly defines *how* and *why* it should be done:

*   **By the fruit, you will recognize them:** In the very same speech (Luke 6:43-45), Jesus tells his followers to judge people by their actions. Moral discernment is required to tell a "good tree" from a "bad tree." 
*   **Hypocrisy requires judgment:** In verses 41-42, just before our passage, Jesus uses the vivid imagery of a speck in a brother's eye and a log in one's own. You cannot remove the speck (fix the moral failure) without first *judging* that it is there and that it needs to be removed. You cannot recognize a "log" without moral discernment.
*   **God’s authority vs. Human authority:** The difference lies in the verb. In English, "judge" can mean both "to discern" (diagnose) and "to condemn" (sentence). Jesus is saying: *Stop playing God by handing down final, eternal sentences on people.* However, he absolutely expects us to use our intellect and conscience to discern between destructive and constructive behaviors. To say "morality doesn't matter" is to abandon the speck and the log entirely, leaving people blind to their own faults and the faults of others.

---

### 2. Discernment: The Eye of Clarity

Discernment is the capacity to see reality accurately. It is diagnostic, humble, and restorative. It asks: *What is this? Is this healthy or destructive? How can this be healed?*

**Characteristics of Discernment:**
*   **Inward before Outward:** In the logic of Luke 6, discernment begins with the self. Before you address the "speck" in someone else's life, you must deal with the "log" in your own. Discernment looks inward first to ensure your motives are pure.
*   **Objective and Factual:** Discernment separates observable truth from subjective bias. It says, "Stealing is wrong," or "Lying damages trust," based on an objective standard (in the Christian context, the character of God and the well-being of humanity).
*   **Aimed at Restoration:** You discern a wound because you want to heal it. You discern a destructive pattern in a friend's life because you want to warn them before they get hurt. 

Discernment is the tool of a doctor. A doctor discerns that a patient has cancer. The doctor is not being hateful; they are applying objective reality to save a life.

---

### 3. The Appetite to Condemn: The Heart of Judgment

If discernment is about *seeing* reality, condemnation is about *punishing* the person. Condemnation is driven by an "appetite"— a hunger to feel superior, to exact vengeance, or to write someone off as irredeemable. It asks: *How can I punish this? How do I prove I am better than them? Who deserves to be cast out?*

**Characteristics of the Appetite to Condemn:**
*   **The Theft of Grace:** To condemn someone is to declare that they are beyond redemption. Jesus warns us to back off, because vengeance and final judgment belong to God. When we condemn, we are acting as usurpers of God’s throne. 
*   **Fueled by Pride:** The appetite to condemn rarely looks at the self. It delights in finding specks in others' eyes while ignoring the logs in its own. It uses moral failures as leverage to feel superior.
*   **Dehumanizing:** Condemnation turns people into categories or monsters. It strips them of their humanity and complexity. It forgets that everyone is fighting battles we cannot see.

---

### 4. The Bridge: "Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven"

Jesus bridges the gap between discernment and condemnation with the command to forgive. This acts as the litmus test for our hearts.

How can you tell if you are discerning or condemning? Look at your willingness to forgive. 

*   **The Disciple (Discerning):** "What you did was wrong (discernment). It caused harm. However, I do not hold your past against your future. I release you from the debt you owe me, and I hope you find healing."
*   **The Condemner:** "What you did was wrong. Therefore, you are trash. You owe me, and I want you to suffer. You are defined by this moment forever."

Jesus tells us to forgive because he knows that the human appetite naturally wants to hold grudges to maintain moral superiority. By commanding us to forgive, Jesus forces us to separate the *sin* (which we are allowed—and sometimes commanded—to morally reject) from the *sinner* (whom we are commanded to love and leave room to grow).

### Summary
Moral judgments *do* matter—because truth matters, and human flourishing depends on knowing the difference between good and evil. But Jesus warns us that we are terrible judges of the *human heart*. Only God knows the motives, the backstory, and the capacity for future change in a person's soul. 

Therefore, we are commanded to **discern** objective reality with clear eyes, acknowledge the sin that destroys us, but utterly **resist the appetite to condemn**, leaving final judgment to God, and offering the grace of forgiveness to others as we hope to receive it ourselves.
