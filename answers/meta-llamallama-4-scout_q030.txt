What a beautiful and profound passage!

The portrait of love in Corinthians 13:4-7 is characterized by a striking balance of positive and negative descriptions. The negations of pride, envy, irritability, and suspicion serve several purposes:

1. **Contrast with cultural ideals**: In ancient Greek culture, as in many cultures today, pride, envy, and self-assertion were often seen as marks of strength, nobility, or excellence. By negating these traits, the apostle Paul presents a countercultural vision of love that challenges conventional notions of strength and virtue.
2. **Highlighting selflessness**: By denying that love "vaunteth not itself" and "seeketh not her own," Paul emphasizes that love is not self-centered or self-exalting. Instead, love is focused on others and their well-being.
3. **Emphasizing humility and gentleness**: The negations of pride, envy, and irritability suggest that love is humble, gentle, and patient. This humility is not weakness, but rather a strength that allows love to be open, receptive, and unassuming.

The proposed vision of strength is one of **mature, enduring, and selfless love**. This love:

* **Endures all things** (vs. 7), persevering through challenges and difficulties.
* **Believes all things** (vs. 7), trusting in the goodness and worth of others.
* **Hopes all things** (vs. 7), maintaining a positive and optimistic outlook, even in adversity.
* **Beareth all things** (vs. 7), carrying the burdens and supporting the needs of others.

In this sense, the strength proposed here is not a brute force or a self-assertive power, but rather a robust, resilient, and gentle power that is capable of withstanding trials and difficulties while remaining committed to others.

This vision of love offers a compelling alternative to the conventional ideals of strength, highlighting the transformative power of humility, selflessness, and enduring commitment.
