In Amos, the rejection is absolute—“I **hate**, I despise”—because the worship being offered is not merely *neglected* justice with some religious garnish added on the side. It has become the **mechanism of the injustice itself**. God does not say, “Keep the feasts, but do them more sincerely,” because these particular feasts, sacrifices, and songs are functioning as **moral camouflage**. To ask for “better balance” would miss the point: you cannot balance righteousness and oppression; they are mutually exclusive in covenantal terms.

Here is the specific fraud being exposed:

### 1. Ritual as a substitute for ethics (the “bribe” mentality)
Israel is treating worship like a transaction. They are offering the best burnt offerings, the fattest peace offerings, and polished liturgical music—exactly what the law prescribed—while simultaneously “selling the righteous for silver, and the poor for a pair of shoes” (Amos 2:6), trampling the needy, and perverting the courts (5:11–12). The implicit logic is: *If we maintain the cult, we maintain our standing with God, regardless of what we do to one another.* Amos exposes this as a lie. In covenant theology, love for God and justice toward neighbor are not two separate accounts; they are one account. To perform the vertical rituals while destroying the horizontal relationships is not imperfect worship—it is **counterfeit worship**.

### 2. False assurance and compartmentalization
The people are gathering at Bethel and Gilgal for solemn assemblies, presumably feeling secure in their election (“We are Israel; the day of the Lord will be light for us,” 5:18–20). The fraud is **religious compartmentalization**: they have carved out a “sacred” sphere (temple, feast, music) where God is honored, and a “secular” sphere (market, gate, court) where the poor are crushed. They believe the sacred sphere can sanitize the secular sphere. God’s sharp rejection says: *Your sacred sphere is polluted because it is funded by and silent about the secular sin.* The noise of their songs is not acceptable worship drowned out by bad behavior; it is part of the noise of oppression.

### 3. Worship has become part of the oppression
Notice that God refuses to *smell* the offerings or *hear* the music. In the ancient world, sacrifice was often conceived as feeding or appeasing the deity—giving God his due so he will leave you alone. Amos demolishes this idea. God is not a tribal deity who can be bought off with fat beasts and choir performances while his covenant people break covenant with the vulnerable. The rituals have become a **performance of piety that enables injustice**—giving the elite a clear conscience and a religious identity to hide behind.

### 4. Why not moderation?
If God asked only for “better balance,” he would implicitly validate the system: keep your feasts, just add a justice committee. But Amos demands that **justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like a mighty stream**—not a seasonal festival, but a perpetual, unstoppable flood. The image is deliberate: worship in Israel was often tied to seasonal festivals (the “feast days” of v. 21). Justice, by contrast, must be constant, like an ever-flowing wadi that never dries up. You cannot “schedule” righteousness for holy days and ignore it on market days.

In short, the fraud is **hypocrisy disguised as orthodoxy**: using correct religious form to deny correct covenantal substance. The worship is rejected so sharply because, in this context, it is not an alternative to injustice—it is its **religious justification**. True worship, Amos insists, is not found in the sanctuary separated from the street, but in the streets themselves, where “judgment” and “righteousness” flow without interruption.
