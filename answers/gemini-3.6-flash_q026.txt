In Matthew 12:34–35, Jesus establishes an inescapable, organic link between the unseen inner life and visible outward behavior. By using the metaphor of a "treasure" (Greek: *thēsauros*, meaning a storehouse, vault, or repository), he presents the human heart not as a neutral organ, but as a moral dynamic where thoughts, desires, and posture accumulate over time.

To answer your second question directly: **The passage suggests that words are all three—symptoms, choices, and revelations—operating in a unified moral framework.**

Here is a breakdown of how the text connects speech to this hidden stockpile, and how it encompasses all three dimensions.

---

### 1. Words as **Symptoms** (Diagnostic)
A symptom is an outward, physical indicator of an underlying internal condition. In this text, speech acts as a diagnostic tool for the soul. 

* **The Mechanism of Overflow:** Jesus states that "out of the abundance [*perisseuma*—overflow, surplus] of the heart the mouth speaketh." Speech is presented as what happens when the heart’s container becomes full and spills over. 
* **Organic Necessity:** Just as a venomous snake ("generation of vipers") naturally produces venom, an "evil man" naturally produces evil words. A person cannot consistently express genuine goodness if their internal stockpile is toxic. Therefore, slip-ups, outbursts, or deceitful speech are not random glitches; they are symptoms showing that the heart's interior atmosphere is diseased.

### 2. Words as **Revelations** (Unveiling)
A revelation brings to light that which was previously hidden. The heart is invisible to human observers, making it easy for individuals to construct false public personas or engage in religious hypocrisy (which Jesus was confronting in the Pharisees).

* **Unmasking Hypocrisy:** Jesus asks, "How can ye, being evil, speak good things?" He is pointing out the impossibility of sustained moral mimicry. A person can attempt to curate their language, but under pressure, exhaustion, or spontaneity, the "hidden treasure" will inevitably be revealed.
* **Exposing the Vault:** Whether the stockpile contains "good treasure" (faith, love, humility) or "evil treasure" (malice, pride, envy), speech acts as the door opening to the vault. Words strip away pretense and reveal the actual moral capital a person has accumulated.

### 3. Words as **Choices** (Volitional)
At first glance, the text might sound deterministic—if the heart is evil, speech *must* be evil. However, in the broader context of Jesus’ teaching (including verses 36–37, where he warns that people will give account for "every idle word" on the day of judgment), words are clearly tied to human agency and responsibility.

* **Building the Stockpile:** The "treasure" of the heart does not appear overnight; it is built through a series of daily volitional choices—what a person dwells on, values, and fosters internally. 
* **Accountability:** Because speech is the fruit of choices that shape the heart, human beings are morally accountable for their words. Words are choices in themselves, but more importantly, they represent the cumulative choices of a lifetime.

---

### Conclusion: All Three Working Together

The brilliance of Jesus’ metaphor is that these three aspects do not contradict one another; they form a single continuum:

1. **Choice:** Through daily desires, thoughts, and actions, a person chooses what to **stockpile** in the hidden vault of the heart.
2. **Symptom:** As that stockpile fills to "abundance," it affects the internal health of the person, making certain types of speech the natural, involuntary **symptom** of their state.
3. **Revelation:** When the mouth finally speaks, it serves as a public **revelation** of what was chosen and cultivated in secret. 

Ultimately, Jesus rejects the idea that words are "just talk" or harmless slips of the tongue. Instead, words are the definitive diagnostic evidence of the soul's true alignment.
