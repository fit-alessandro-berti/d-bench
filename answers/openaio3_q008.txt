1. The thought-line in 1 John 4  

John is confronting people who were loudly claiming a lofty, “spiritual” knowledge of God while at the same time separating themselves from, and even despising, ordinary Christians (cf. 1 Jn 2:9, 19; 3:14).  
His answer through the whole letter is:  

• God is love (4:8,16).  
• Whoever is truly begotten of that God will necessarily share His quality of love (4:7).  
• Therefore love of the brethren is the public, observable evidence that the hidden relationship with God is real (3:10,14,18-19; 4:12).  

Verse 20 is the concise, climactic statement of that logic.

2. Why the neighbour is the “test”

a. Easier → harder (argument a minore ad maius)  
Loving the brother whom I can see, speak to, and help is the easier task; loving an invisible God whom I cannot see, hear, or tangibly serve is the harder one.  If a person fails the easier test, it is absurd to claim success in the harder: “how can he love God whom he has not seen?”

b. Concrete → intangible  
Real love always takes concrete form (4:9-10; 3:17-18).  The brother supplies the concrete occasion for love; God, until the eschaton, does not.  The only available way to show love for the unseen God is by showing love to His visible image-bearers (cf. Mt 25:40; Jas 3:9).

c. Same object, different mode  
The brother is not a distraction from God; he carries God’s likeness and is part of God’s family.  The two loves are therefore one movement.  Hatred of the brother signals rejection of the Father whose child he is (5:1).

3. The self-deception being exposed

The verse unmasks a particular religious illusion:

• Mistaking feelings, words, or cultic devotion for love.  
A person may feel pious, pray eloquently, have mystical experiences, or defend orthodox doctrine and say, “I love God.”  But if the same person harbours resentment, indifference, or contempt toward fellow believers, the apostle’s verdict is: “he is a liar.”  

• Creating an imaginary “God” one finds easy to love.  
By detaching God from the inconvenient neighbour, one can project onto God any qualities one likes and then congratulate oneself for loving Him.  John insists that the real God is met precisely in self-giving toward flesh-and-blood people; refusal of them is refusal of Him.

4. Summary

Love of the visible neighbour functions as the decisive test because:

1. It is the simpler, more verifiable exercise of love.  
2. It is the only tangible avenue available for loving the presently invisible God.  
3. The neighbour embodies God’s own life, so the two loves are inseparable.

Claiming to love God while hating a brother therefore reveals a self-deception that substitutes sentimental or merely verbal “love” for the authentic, self-sacrificing love that flows from new birth in God.
