• It’s difficult because it cuts against several hardwired human instincts at once:

  1. Psychologically costly: Our brains prioritize self-protection and retaliation. Harm triggers threat responses, so
     forgiving or caring for an enemy feels unsafe, not “natural.”
  2. Socially risky: In many contexts, hostility and group conflict are identity-based. Loving enemies can look like
     betrayal of one’s own group or weakness, so there’s pressure to conform to anger.
  3. Moral counterintuitive: We usually treat people by what they do to us. This teaching asks judgment to be re-
     centered on what God is like, not on reciprocity.

  That’s why it can become sentimental if reduced to a vague “be nice” slogan—no cost, no boundaries, no justice, just
  feel good. It can also be strategic when it’s only used as conflict management: be tactically kind to reduce backlash.

  In the Matt 5:44–45 frame, it’s primarily a deeper moral discipline:

  - it breaks the cycle of revenge,
  - it preserves the giver’s character under injury,
  - it reflects God’s impartial mercy (“sun” and “rain” on all),
  - it can coexist with truth-telling, accountability, and justice.

  So the hard part is not just emotion, it’s identity: it asks you to be “God-like” in a way that is costly,
  disciplined, and non-retaliatory even when justice still remains needed.
