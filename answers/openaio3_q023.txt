“Let the wicked forsake his way  
and the unrighteous man his thoughts”

1. Two Hebrew nouns  
   • way (דֶּרֶךְ déreḵ) – one’s road, course of life, observable behaviour.  
   • thoughts (מַחְשְׁבוֹת maḥšəḇōṯ) – plans, designs, inner reasoning.

Isaiah therefore names the full human person:  
• The public side – what I actually do.  
• The private side – what I continually think about and scheme.

2. Sin works in both spheres  
Biblical writers never separate act from intention (cf. Gen 6:5; Jer 17:9; Mt 5:27-28).  
• A man may be “wicked” because his deeds are violent.  
• Another may look respectable yet be “unrighteous” inside, cultivating covetous, lust, revenge, unbelief.  
God sees both (Ps 139:1–4). If only the “way” changed, hypocrisy would remain; if only the “thoughts” changed, obedience would be empty talk. Real repentance must reach roots and branches alike.

3. Literary preparation for vv. 8-9  
Immediately after the call Isaiah quotes God: “My thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways.”  The parallel shows the prophet’s logic:  
• Abandon your ways/thoughts.  
• Receive God’s ways/thoughts.  
Repentance is an exchange, not mere regret.

4. Mercy is as wide as the demand is deep  
Because guilt touches both deed and intention, pardon must be “abundant” (Heb. yarbeh – “multiply, overflow”).  Isaiah’s promise is that the same God who requires total turning supplies total forgiveness: every outward act and every hidden imagination can be erased – but “while he may be found,” i.e., while the word of invitation is still sounding.

Summary  
Repentance involves “way” and “thoughts” because God claims the whole person. Outward conduct must be forsaken; inward imagination must be surrendered. Only a repentance that is both external and internal fits the comprehensive mercy that God stands ready to give.
