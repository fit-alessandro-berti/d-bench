In this parable (Luke 18:9–14), the Pharisee’s condemnation stems primarily from **pride** and **self-righteous comparison**, though these are closely tied to the broader issues of **false doctrine** (a distorted view of righteousness) and **the use of virtue as self-display**. Here’s a breakdown of the key elements:

### **1. Pride (Primary Condemnation)**
The Pharisee’s prayer is a **boastful declaration of his own righteousness**, not a humble appeal to God. He doesn’t pray for mercy or repentance; instead, he **praises himself** for his moral and religious achievements (fasting, tithing) while **looking down on others** (v. 11). This reflects the **sin of pride**, which Jesus repeatedly condemns (e.g., Luke 18:14: *"For every one that exalteth himself shall be abased; and he that humbleth himself shall be exalted"*).

### **2. Comparison (Contempt for Others)**
The Pharisee’s prayer is **explicitly comparative**:
- *"I thank You that I am not like other men"* (v. 11).
- *"Not even like this tax collector"* (implied in his contrast).
This **contemptuous attitude** toward others violates Jesus’ teaching on humility and love (e.g., Matt. 7:3–5; Luke 6:37). The tax collector, by contrast, **does not compare himself to anyone**—he only acknowledges his own sinfulness before God.

### **3. False Doctrine (Distorted View of Righteousness)**
The Pharisee’s righteousness is **self-generated and self-reliant**, not dependent on God’s grace. He **mistakes outward religious performance (fasting, tithing) for true righteousness** (cf. Matt. 23:25–28). Jesus exposes this as **hypocrisy**—his actions (e.g., tithing) are **empty without a humble heart** (Luke 11:42). The tax collector, though a "sinner," **trusts in God’s mercy**, not his own works.

### **4. Virtue as Self-Display (Not True Worship)**
The Pharisee’s prayer is **not worship but performance**. He **uses his piety to elevate himself** rather than to glorify God. The tax collector’s posture—**beating his breast (a sign of repentance), not lifting his eyes to heaven (a sign of pride), and crying out for mercy**—shows **genuine contrition and dependence on God**, not self-promotion.

### **Why the Tax Collector’s Posture is Spiritually Truer**
The tax collector’s attitude reflects **true repentance and faith**:
1. **Humility** – He doesn’t claim righteousness; he **acknowledges his sin** (v. 13).
2. **Dependence on Grace** – He doesn’t trust in his own works but **cries out for mercy** ("God, be merciful to me a sinner").
3. **No Comparison** – He doesn’t judge others; he only **judges himself**.
4. **True Worship** – His posture (beating his chest, not lifting eyes) shows **brokenness before God**, not self-righteousness.

Jesus’ point is clear: **God exalts the humble and abases the proud** (v. 14). The Pharisee’s **self-justification** makes him **unjustified in God’s sight**, while the tax collector’s **humble repentance** leads to **justification by faith** (Rom. 3:28; Gal. 2:16).

### **Conclusion**
The Pharisee is condemned most directly by his **pride and comparison**, but these flow from a **false doctrine of self-righteousness** and the **misuse of virtue for self-display**. The tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer because it reflects **genuine repentance, humility, and trust in God’s mercy**—the very heart of the Gospel.
