Paul’s catalogue of “what love is not” reads almost like a surgical removal of every self-regarding impulse that would corrode genuine affection.  By defining love in the negative—“not proud, not jealous, not irritable, not suspicious”—he shows us both

 1.  How easily our relationships are poisoned by pride, envy, quick tempers and mistrust  
 2.  How radically countercultural true love must be if it is to endure.

In other words, love’s strength isn’t a brash assertion of rights or a triumph of ego.  It is the power to

 •  Forbear when indignation rises (patience rather than provoked anger)  
 •  Rejoice in another’s success instead of brooding with envy  
 •  Speak and act humbly instead of vaunting oneself  
 •  Trust and hope rather than harbor suspicion or cynicism  

What Paul proposes is a vision of strength as self-mastery and self-giving:  
–  A strength born not of coercion or self-promotion but of inner freedom from the restless demands of pride and resentment.  
–  A strength that can bear wrongs without retaliation, believe the best, and endure hardship all for the sake of the beloved.  

In this way, love becomes a kind of “soft power,” rooted in humility and sustained by hope, rather than in the force of arms or the insistence on one’s own way.  That is the radical, god-shaped strength at the heart of 1 Corinthians 13.
