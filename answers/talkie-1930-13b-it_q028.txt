It forbids judgment, exposes hypocrisy, interrupts mob-righteousness, and goes deeper, since it reminds us all that the task of judging our fellow-men is a task for which we are unfit. The fitness of judging is proportioned to moral similarity; and, in proportion as we resemble the criminal, we are qualified to sit in judgment upon him. Now, the resemblance between an innocent man and a great offender must be slight indeed. The former has probably never been tempted to commit the offence; and the latter has certainly been tempted to abstain from it. The innocent man has no knowledge of the criminal’s struggle, and the criminal has no knowledge of the innocent man’s security; and thus, between them, there can exist but a scanty matter of comparison. He, therefore, who has never been tempted to evil, is not qualified to estimate the measure of temptation which overcame his neighbour; and he, who has been strongly tempted to good, can have little sympathy with a weaker brother who fell in the conflict.
