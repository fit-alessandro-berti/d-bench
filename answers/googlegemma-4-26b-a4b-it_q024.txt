The passage in Ezekiel 36:26–27 represents one of the most profound shifts in biblical theology: the transition from the **Mosaic Covenant** (law written on stone) to the **New Covenant** (law written on the heart).

To answer your question, we must look at the distinction between behavioral modification and ontological transformation.

### 1. External Compliance vs. A New Heart

The difference between these two states is the difference between **obedience through effort** and **obedience through nature.**

*   **External Compliance (The Old Way):** This is characterized by "effortful morality." In this state, a person follows rules because they are external commands. The motivation is often fear of punishment, social pressure, or a desire for merit. However, because the internal desires (the "heart") remain unchanged, the compliance is often performative, inconsistent, or driven by hypocrisy. The person is "obeying" the law, but the law is fighting against their true nature.
*   **Receiving a New Heart (The New Way):** This is "instinctual morality." When God promises a new heart, He is proposing a change in the *source* of the person’s desires. Instead of a person struggling to force their sinful nature to obey an external law, the new heart creates a new set of appetites. The person begins to *want* what God wants. Compliance is no longer a heavy burden imposed from the outside; it becomes the natural outflow of a new internal identity.

In short: **External compliance is the hand following the law; a new heart is the will loving the law.**

### 2. Why Hardness is Described as a Material Condition

The use of the terms "stony heart" and "heart of flesh" is not merely poetic; it describes a fundamental change in the "composition" of the human person. There are three theological reasons why hardness is described as a material condition that must be *replaced* rather than simply repaired:

#### A. The Impossibility of Reform
A stone cannot be "reformed" into flesh. You can polish a stone, carve it, or smooth its edges, but no matter how much you work on a stone, it will never breathe, feel, or grow. By using material metaphors, Ezekiel is saying that human nature is not just "broken" or "damaged"—it is fundamentally the wrong substance for the life God intends. You cannot "fix" a heart of stone; you must undergo a "transplant."

#### B. The Problem of Sensitivity (The "Pulse" of the Soul)
*   **A Stony Heart** is characterized by impermeability. It is unresponsive to the touch of God, indifferent to the suffering of others, and incapable of being shaped by instruction. It is rigid and fixed.
*   **A Heart of Flesh** is characterized by sensitivity and responsiveness. Flesh is soft, it bleeds, it feels, and it reacts to stimuli. A heart of flesh can be "moved" by the Holy Spirit. This suggests that the goal of spiritual life is not just "being good," but being *perceptive* and *responsive* to God’s presence.

#### C. The Necessity of Divine Intervention (The "Surgical" Aspect)
By describing the heart as a material object that must be "taken away" and "given," the text emphasizes that salvation is a work of **monergism** (God acting alone). 

If the problem were merely a lack of knowledge or a lapse in willpower, a human could solve it through education or discipline. But if the problem is a *material condition*—a heart made of the wrong substance—then the human is powerless. Only the Creator can perform the "surgery" required to replace the stone with flesh. It moves the focus from human striving to divine grace.

### Summary
The transition from stone to flesh represents the move from **duty to delight.** The stone heart obeys because it *must*; the heart of flesh obeys because it *loves*.
