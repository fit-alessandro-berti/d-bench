Ephesians 4:31–32 puts bitterness and forgiveness right next to each other because Paul is drawing a contrast between the old self you "put away" and the new self you "put on". The list in v31 and the command in v32 are mirror images.

### Why they’re placed directly against each other

Paul just spent Ephesians 4:17-30 describing the “old man” that corrupts and the “new man” created in God’s likeness. Bitterness is the root; forgiveness is the fruit. 

**Bitterness** starts the list in v31: `bitterness, and wrath, and anger, and clamour, and evil speaking, with all malice`.  
**Forgiveness** ends the parallel in v32: `kind... tenderhearted, forgiving one another`. 

Structurally, he’s showing a progression: bitterness ferments into wrath → anger → public outbursts → slander → broad malice. Forgiveness is the replacement that cuts the progression off at the source. You can’t carry both at once. One crowds the other out.

The “even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you” gives the reason: forgiveness isn’t just a nicer mood. It’s an imitation of what you’ve already received. Bitterness says “I’m still owed.” Forgiveness says “I’ve been released, so I release.”

### Is bitterness a feeling, habit, posture, or contagious?

The Greek word *pikria* literally means something sharp or pointed, like an acrid taste. In context here, Paul treats it as all four, but with emphasis:

**1. It starts as a feeling**  
Hebrews 12:15 also calls it a “root of bitterness” that springs up and defiles. It’s the initial taste when you rehearse a wound. You can’t always stop the first flare of hurt.

**2. It becomes a habit**  
The verb “let... be put away” *arthētō* is a decisive act. The fact that you have to “put it away” implies you’ve been carrying it. Bitterness is what happens when anger gets old and settles in. Aristotle called it “long-lasting anger.” It’s resentment you nurse until it’s your default lens.

**3. It’s a chosen posture**  
Paul lists it among things you’re responsible to remove. He doesn’t say “pray that God takes your bitterness.” He says “you put it away.” That means you can keep it, too. It’s the stance of the creditor standing at the door with a ledger. Forgiveness is the stance of someone who lays the ledger down because Christ already paid theirs.

**4. It’s contagious**  
Notice the plural: “from *you*” – the whole church at Ephesus. And the progression in v31 moves from inward *bitterness* to outward *evil speaking* and *clamour*. One bitter person creates wrath, which creates arguments, which creates slander, which becomes community-wide *malice*. Hebrews 12:15 again: a root of bitterness “springs up and thereby many be defiled.” It spreads because unresolved hurt recruits allies. Forgiveness stops the infection.

**So the text’s logic:** Bitterness is a feeling you can feed until it becomes a habit. That habit hardens into a posture toward people and God. If left there, that posture goes airborne and poisons others. Forgiveness is the only thing sharp enough to cut the root before it flowers into wrath and malice.

Paul isn’t asking them to manage their feelings better. He’s telling them to evict a tenant so a new one – kindness shaped by the cross – can move in.
