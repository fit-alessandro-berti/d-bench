This question cuts to the heart of the Christian ethic’s scandal. Why *is* enemy-love so difficult? It is rarely merely impractical; it often feels existentially threatening. We can understand its difficulty through three lenses: the sentimental, the strategic, and the moral—but the deepest reason is that it dismantles the architecture of our identity.

### 1. The Sentimental Lens: It Feels Like a Betrayal of Justice
On the surface, enemy-love appears sentimental because it asks us to suspend our moral instincts for fairness and reciprocity. Our sense of justice is wired for balance: harm demands response, insult demands retort. To bless a curse or do good to hatred feels like a betrayal of the self—like we are denying our own dignity. Sentimentality here is not soft emotion, but a *misplaced emotional loyalty* to our own victimhood. We cling to the narrative that we *deserve* to be wronged, and loving the enemy would invalidate that story. Thus, the difficulty is not that love is weak, but that it forces us to relinquish the comfort of being right.

### 2. The Strategic Lens: It Threatens Our Power
A strategic view asks, "What’s the advantage?" Here, enemy-love can seem naive or even manipulative. If love is a tool, then its value is in its return. But Jesus’s command is not transactional. It is strategic in a deeper sense: it is a disruption of the logic of domination. By refusing to demonize the enemy, you expose the machinery of hatred that relies on dehumanization. Yet this is precisely why we resist it—it strips us of the justification for our own violence, whether physical, verbal, or systemic. Enemy-love is strategic because it collapses the battlefield, but we reject it because we depend on the battlefield to maintain our power, our security, and our sense of control.

### 3. The Deeper Moral Strength: The Death of the "Self" as Sovereign
The core difficulty is not sentimental weakness or strategic miscalculation; it is **ontological**. Enemy-love is a direct assault on the modern conception of the autonomous self. Our identity is built on boundaries: friend/enemy, just/unjust, us/them. To love the enemy is to dissolve these boundaries. It is a form of moral resurrection—a dying to the self that demands to be recognized, protected, and honored.

This is where it becomes a "deeper form of moral strength" not because it is heroic in a conventional sense (conquering by force), but because it is *kenotic* (self-emptying). It requires a strength that does not need to prove itself, a security that does not require a threat. The sun rises on the evil and the good; the rain falls on the just and the unjust. This is not a call to be passive, but to participate in a different economy of value—one that is not measured by return, but by faithfulness to a reality that precedes and sustains all.

### Conclusion: The Scandal of Unearned Love
Enemy-love is difficult because it is **scandalous**. It bypasses the calculus of fairness and enters the realm of grace. To take it seriously is to accept that our moral worth is not contingent on our enemies' behavior. It is not about changing them, but about transforming us—from children who demand a just parent into children who reflect a Father who gives without condition. The difficulty is not in the method, but in the surrender of the self that seeks to justify itself.
