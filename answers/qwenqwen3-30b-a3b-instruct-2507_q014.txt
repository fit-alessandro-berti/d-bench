Matthew 6:19–21 presents a profound spiritual teaching that connects **treasure**, **heart**, and **attention** in a way that reveals deep truths about the nature of the human soul and where our true priorities lie.

Let’s unpack the passage and answer your question in two parts:

---

### **1. Does this text say treasure *reveals* the heart, or that treasure *trains* the heart?**

The passage most strongly suggests that **treasure reveals the heart**, though it implicitly acknowledges that treasure also *influences* or *shapes* the heart.

- **"For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also."**  
  This is a **revealing** rather than a **formative** statement. The Greek construction emphasizes *correspondence*: your heart goes where your treasure is *already*. It reflects your inner priorities, affections, and ultimate allegiance.

  Think of it like a magnet: your treasure doesn't *create* your heart’s orientation; it *attracts* or *shows* where your heart already is.

  So, **treasure reveals the heart** — but this revelation is not passive. The act of hoarding earthly treasures *indicates* a heart devoted to this world, while investing in heavenly treasures indicates a heart oriented toward God and eternity.

  However, the *practice* of laying up treasures in heaven — through generosity, self-denial, and devotion to God — *does* shape the heart over time. So, while the verse primarily states that treasure *reveals* where the heart is, the *ongoing pursuit* of heavenly treasure has a formative effect: it *trains* the heart to love what is eternal.

  Thus, the dynamic is both **revealing and forming** — but the **primary emphasis** in this passage is *revealing*.

---

### **2. How do possession, attention, and love become tied together here?**

These three interrelated concepts are woven together in a powerful spiritual triad in this passage:

#### **a. Possession → Where you place your treasure**
- "Lay up for yourselves treasures upon earth" (or in heaven).
- The verb *lay up* (Greek: *thēsaurizō*) implies deliberate hoarding, storing, or accumulating.
- This is not just about material wealth — it's about *what we hold dear and invest in*.
- Possession is not the end; it’s a sign of where we *invest ourselves*.

#### **b. Attention → Where your heart is drawn**
- "For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also."
- The heart (Greek: *kardia*) is not just a physical organ, but the center of emotion, will, and moral decision-making — the *inner self*.
- Your **attention** — what you focus on, what you care about — is governed by your treasure.
- If you hoard earthly things, your attention is pulled toward security, reputation, comfort.
- If you lay up treasures in heaven, your attention is drawn toward God, righteousness, and eternal values.

#### **c. Love → The motive behind the treasure**
- While love isn’t explicitly stated here, it's *implied* in the concept of treasure and heart.
- The heart is the seat of love (cf. Deut 6:5; Mark 12:30).
- You don’t invest in something unless you love it — you treasure what you love.
- So, **love motivates possession**, which directs **attention**, which forms the shape of the **heart**.

---

### **The Interlocking Cycle:**

1. **Love** → drives you to **possess** certain things (treasures).
2. Your act of possession (hoarding earthly possessions or pouring out resources for others) directs your **attention**.
3. Where your attention goes, your **heart** follows.
4. And your heart (where your love truly resides) determines your ultimate allegiance.

Thus, this passage reveals a spiritual law:  
> **What you treasure, you love.  
> What you love, you attend to.  
> What you attend to, shapes your heart.  
> And your heart reveals where you truly belong.**

---

### **Conclusion:**

- **Treasure reveals the heart** — it shows where your deepest love and allegiance lie.
- But the **practice of treasure-keeping** — especially spiritual treasure like generosity, worship, service — also *trains* the heart to be less attached to the temporal and more aligned with God.
- **Possession** reflects what we value.  
- **Attention** follows what we possess.  
- **Love** is the root that fuels both.

So this passage is not just about money or possessions — it’s about **spiritual orientation**. It calls us to examine not just what we have, but what we *love*, and what our **heart’s true home** is.

> *“Where your treasure is, there your heart will be also.”*  
> That’s not just a warning — it’s an invitation: **Reconfigure your treasures, and your heart will follow.**
