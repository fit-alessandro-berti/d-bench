Based on the text and the broader context of the Parable of the Prodigal Son, the elder brother’s deepest problem is not merely anger or envy, but a **foundational misunderstanding of his relationship with his father—specifically, a heart of legalistic pride that views love as a transaction rather than a gift.**

While all the options listed play a role, they form a hierarchy of symptoms leading to a root cause. Here is an analysis of why **Pride in Obedience** (manifesting as a transactional view of God) is the deepest issue.

### 1. The Surface Symptoms: Anger and Envy
Anger (v. 28) and envy are the visible manifestations of his condition, but they are not the root.
*   **Anger:** He is angry because the "economy" of his life has been upended. He thought he was accruing credit, and the father’s grace bankrupted his moral ledger.
*   **Envy:** He envies the celebration, but deep down, he envies the father’s freedom to love. He wants the father’s goods (the fatted calf), but he does not share the father’s heart.

### 2. The Intellectual Struggle: Wounded Fairness
The elder brother operates on a system of **meritocracy**.
*   In verse 29, he presents his case like a lawyer: *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment."*
*   His argument is built on "fairness" defined as *quid pro quo* (this for that). He believes that goodness earns benefits, and sinfulness earns punishment.
*   His "wounded fairness" is a real struggle, but it is a struggle born of a wrong premise: that he is an employee owed wages, rather than a son owed love.

### 3. The Root Cause: Pride in Obedience (Self-Righteousness)
This is the deepest problem because it is the lens through which he views reality. Several details in the text reveal this:

*   **He views himself as a servant, not a son.**
    He says, *"these many years do I serve thee."* The father corrects him gently: *"Son, thou art ever with me."* The elder brother has lived in the father's house physically, but relationally, he has been standing in the yard, working as a hired hand. He has missed the intimacy of sonship because he was too busy building a resume of service.

*   **He views obedience as a bargaining chip.**
    He reminds the father of his impeccable record: *"neither transgressed I at any time."* This is the height of religious pride. He believes his moral performance has purchased him leverage over the father. He is effectively saying, "I have earned the right to be heard, and I have earned the right to a feast."

*   **His obedience was loveless.**
    The tragedy is highlighted in the father's response: *"all that I have is thine."* The elder brother had access to the father’s wealth the entire time, but he never asked for a goat to celebrate with his friends. He was so focused on *earning* his inheritance that he never enjoyed his father's presence. His obedience was not an act of love; it was a duty performed to secure a reward.

### 4. The Result: Discomfort with Mercy
Because the elder brother bases his identity on his moral performance ("I never transgressed"), **mercy is an offense to him.**
*   If the prodigal son— who devoured the living with harlots—can be welcomed back with a ring and a robe, then the elder brother’s years of "slavery" were unnecessary for earning love.
*   Grace destroys the value of his "currency" (good works). If the father loves the sinner just as much as the saint, then the elder brother’s moral superiority evaporates.
*   He hates the mercy shown to his brother because it reveals that the father’s love is not a wage to be earned, but a gift to be received.

### Conclusion
The elder brother's deepest problem is that he is **lost at home**. He is just as alienated from the father as the younger brother was in the pigpen, but his alienation is hidden behind a wall of self-righteousness. He resents the father’s grace because he trusts in his own goodness. He represents the religious person who obeys God out of a sense of duty to control God, rather than out of a heart of gratitude.
