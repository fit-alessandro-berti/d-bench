1. Why so many negatives?  
• Paul is writing to a church that is full of the very things he negates.  In the preceding chapters the Corinthians are jockeying for honor, competing over spiritual gifts, suing one another, dividing around favorite teachers, and even getting drunk at the Lord’s-supper.  By piling up the “is not … does not … is never …” Paul is turning a mirror toward their current behaviour and erasing, one vice at a time, the false versions of “love” they have been displaying.  
• Greek rhetoric often defines a virtue both positively and negatively (“courage fears no danger…”) because the negative borders mark the territory of the virtue.  Paul sketches the silhouette of agapē by carving away everything that distorts it—boasting, envy, touchiness, score-keeping, delight in another’s fall.  
• The gospel itself is stated in a series of divine “negations”: Christ “did not consider equality with God something to be grasped…but emptied himself” (Phil 2).  Paul therefore describes love in the same kenotic (self-emptying) way.

2. What vision of strength is being proposed?  
The passage substitutes a very different idea of power for the Corinthian (and contemporary) one.

Worldly strength  
• displays itself (“vaunteth”)  
• guards its own interests (“seeketh its own”)  
• retaliates quickly (“is easily provoked”)  
• establishes superiority by comparison (“envieth not” implies envy is a weapon of rivalry)

Gospel strength  
• “suffers long” – it has such interior solidity that it can absorb injury without disintegrating.  
• “is kind” – its energy flows outward in creative good rather than in self-advertisement.  
• “bears, believes, hopes, endures all things” – it is durable, forward-looking, and resilient because it is rooted in God’s unchanging character, not in human applause.

Thus the strength Paul commends is cruciform: it looks weak because it refuses pride, envy and retaliation, yet it alone can “endure all things.”  It is the strength we see in Christ, who could be mocked, stripped and nailed to wood and still pray, “Father, forgive them.”  The community that lives by that sort of love no longer needs the props of status, giftedness, or being “right”; it is free to serve, free to rejoice in another’s good, and free to trust God for the future.  That, Paul insists, is the only power that will finally remain when “prophecies cease” and “knowledge passes away.”
