

**What makes the image “sharp”?**  

| Element | Why it cuts deep |
|---------|-----------------|
| **Contrast of size** – a *mote* (a tiny speck of dust or wood‑shaving) versus a *beam* (a large timber‑joist). The disparity is absurd: you could never see the speck if a plank were lodged in your eye. The exaggeration forces the listener to picture an impossible, almost comical situation. |
| **Bodily, visceral setting** – the eye is one of the most sensitive, immediate parts of the body. To speak of something *in* the eye makes the fault felt, not just imagined. The audience can almost feel the irritation of a speck and the blinding obstruction of a beam. |
| **Every‑day carpentry allusion** – Jesus’ audience (many of whom were artisans, laborers, or at least familiar with a workshop) would instantly recognize “mote” and “beam” as the shavings and the heavy timber that a carpenter constantly deals with. The metaphor is therefore *culturally resonant*; no explanation is needed. |
| **Hyperbolic irony** – the command “first cast out the beam… then you will see clearly to cast out the mote” is logically impossible if taken literally (you cannot remove a beam from your own eye without help). The irony highlights the *self‑defeating* nature of hypocritical judgment: the very act you are trying to perform is blocked by the fault you ignore. |
| **Rhythmic, chiastic structure** – the Greek (τί δὲ βλέπεις τὸ κάρφος … τὴν δὲ δοκὸν …) mirrors the thought: *see‑mote* → *ignore‑beam* → *hypocrite* → *first‑beam* → *then‑mote*. The chiasmus tightens the logic and makes the passage memorable. |

Together these features turn a simple moral warning into a vivid, almost visual, slap‑in‑the‑face that sticks in the mind long after the words are spoken.

---

**How the passage criticizes hypocrisy **without** forbidding moral correction altogether**

1. **The target is the *attitude* of the judge, not the act of judging.**  
   - Jesus calls the person a “*hypocrite*” (ὑποκριτής) because they *notice* the mote in the brother’s eye *while* ignoring the beam in their own. The sin condemned is the *selective* perception — seeing others’ faults clearly while being blind to one’s own greater fault.

2. **The prescription is *sequential*, not prohibitive.**  
   - The imperative is: “*First* cast out the beam out of thine own eye; *then* shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote out of thy brother’s eye.”  
   - The *first* step is self‑examination and repentance. Only after that does the *second* step — helping the brother — become permissible. The text therefore **conditions** moral correction on personal integrity, rather than banning it outright.

3. **The logic assumes that correction is possible and desirable once the obstacle is removed.**  
   - If Jesus intended to forbid any correction, he would have said something like, “Do not judge at all.” Instead, he assumes that after the beam is removed, the eye will be capable of *clear* vision and thus able to assist the brother. The correction is framed as an act of loving assistance, not a condemnatory verdict.

4. **Contextual clues in the Sermon on the Mount.**  
   - Verses 1‑2 warn against *“judging that you be not judged”* (μὴ κρίνητε, ἵνα μὴ κρithēte). The following verses (3‑5) explain *how* to avoid the pitfall of unjust judgment: by first dealing with one’s own larger sin. The broader passage (Matt 7:1‑12) goes on to teach the “Golden Rule” (v.12) and the pursuit of righteousness (vv.13‑14), indicating that ethical engagement with others is expected, provided it stems from a humble, self‑aware heart.

5. **Scholarly consensus.**  
   - Most commentators (e.g., N.T. Wright, *Matthew for Everyone*; Dale C. Allison Jr., *A Critical and Exegetical Commentary on the Gospel of Matthew*; John Nolland, *The Gospel of Matthew*) read 7:3‑5 as a *warning against hypocritical, self‑righteous judgment*, not a blanket prohibition on fraternal correction. They note that the Greek verb *κρίνω* (to judge) in v.1 carries a condemnatory sense, while the act of “casting out the mote” is presented as a *helping* action after self‑purification.

**In short:** The image is sharp because Jesus piles together a startling size contrast, a bodily‑sensitive scenario, a familiar carpentry metaphor, and ironic hyperbole — all wrapped in a memorable chiastic form. The passage condemns the *hypocritical* attitude of noticing others’ tiny faults while ignoring one’s own massive fault, but it does **not** outlaw moral correction. Instead, it makes correction contingent on the corrector’s own moral clarity: first deal with the beam, then you are equipped to help with the mote. This preserves the possibility of loving, constructive confrontation while eliminating the condemnatory, self‑righteous kind that Jesus warns against.
