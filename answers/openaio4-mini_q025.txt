In Matthew 6:1 Jesus isn’t issuing a blanket ban on doing good in public; He is warning against the *motive* that so often accompanies it.  A brief sketch of what’s at stake:

1. The Pharisaic pattern  
   – In the first century synagogue and marketplace, almsgiving, prayer, fasting, etc., were done in full view of others.  
   – That visibility became a temptation to turn acts of piety into acts of self-advertisement.  
   – Jesus calls that “hypocrisy”: you play the part of the righteous, you get your reward (human applause), and you lose the only reward that truly matters—your Father’s.

2. Visibility vs. craving for recognition  
   – Visibility in itself is morally neutral.  There is nothing intrinsically wrong with giving alms, praying, or doing acts of mercy where others can see.  
   – What is corrupting is the *desire* to be seen, admired, envied.  When the applause of men becomes the goal, the whole enterprise ceases to be worship and becomes instead a form of self-promotion.

3. “No reward…in heaven”  
   – Jesus isn’t saying that if you give publicly, God refuses to notice.  He’s saying that if your inner aim is to win human esteem, you have already cashed your reward on earth.  
   – You’ve exchanged the “treasure in heaven” for a momentary pat on the back.

4. The positive flip-side (cf. Matthew 5:16)  
   – Later in the Sermon on the Mount Jesus says, “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works and glorify your Father.”  
   – That verse presumes a pure motive: you do good publicly not to bank human praise, but so that others might see *God’s* compassion and be drawn into His kingdom.

5. Practical application  
   – Before you give alms, pray, or serve publicly, examine your heart: Am I doing this to impress certain people? Or am I simply wanting to obey God and show His love?  
   – If you catch yourself craving compliments or noticing how many “likes” you get, you’ve already slipped into the very danger Jesus warned about.

In short: it’s not the public setting that is condemned but the *craving to be seen* in that setting.  The visible act of generosity becomes morally dangerous only when it is turned into a vehicle for self-glory rather than God-glory.
