Kind of moral posture required  

Paul does not merely tell the Galatians to “rebuke” or “expose” a brother who has sinned.  
He asks for three simultaneous attitudes that together make up the only Christian way to correct another believer:

1. A restorative intention – “restore such a one” (katartízete).  
   • The verb means “to mend, to set a bone, to put back in joint.”  
   • The aim is therefore remedial, not punitive; to bring the fallen person back to usefulness and fellowship, not to disgrace him.

2. A gentle manner – “in a spirit of meekness” (en pneumati prautētos).  
   • Meekness (prautēs) is strength under control, the opposite of irritability or harshness.  
   • It is listed two verses earlier (5:23) as a fruit of the Spirit; hence only the “spiritual” can display it.

3. A humble self-watchfulness – “considering yourself lest you also be tempted.”  
   • The participle skopōn (“keeping an eye on”) pictures a lookout who continually watches his own heart.  
   • The corrector remembers that the same temptation, or another, may conquer him tomorrow (1 Cor 10:12; Matt 7:1-5).

Why the three are kept together  

These three elements are mutually corrective; drop any one of them and the whole enterprise becomes un-Christian.

• Restoration without meekness degenerates into cold professionalism (“Here is the rule you broke; here are the steps to fix it”).  
• Restoration without self-suspicion breeds condescension (“I will lift you up from my superior height”).  
• Meekness without a restorative aim turns into sentimental tolerance that leaves the sinner unreconciled.  
• Self-suspicion without a restorative intent can slide into mere self-protective silence (“Who am I to say anything?”).

By linking them, Paul ensures that…

1. The GOAL is the brother’s healing (restoration),  
2. The MANNER is Christ-like gentleness (meekness), and  
3. The MOTIVATION is a shared vulnerability to sin (self-suspicion).

In practical terms, the corrector approaches the sinner more like a surgeon setting a fracture than a judge pronouncing sentence: competent (restorative), steady-handed (meek), and keenly aware that he himself could one day be on the operating table (self-watchful).

Thus the “moral posture” required is loving, gentle, and humble—a posture that mirrors the gospel Paul has just described in Galatians 5 as life “by the Spirit.”
