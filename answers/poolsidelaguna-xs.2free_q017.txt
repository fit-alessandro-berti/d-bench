
This passage probes one of the most unsettling paradoxes of the human condition: the desire to remain hidden from light. Let's explore why light—truth, clarity, divine revelation—is so often hated.

## The Psychology of Shame and Concealment

When light enters a space, it reveals what was previously hidden. For those with "deeds [that are] evil," this revelation is threatening because:

- **Exposure reveals complicity**: Darkness isn't just absence of light—it's active concealment. People don't merely avoid light; they actively prefer darkness as a protective cloak for their actions.
- **Shame precedes condemnation**: Before God judges, He illuminates. The fear of being "reproved" (reproved means exposed to blame or shame) creates resistance to truth itself.

There's a crucial distinction here between *hating light* and *being hateful*. The haters aren't necessarily malicious—they're defensive. They've invested in darkness because darkness doesn't interrogate. Darkness doesn't ask uncomfortable questions about integrity, motives, or consequences.

## The Paradox of Truth and Freedom

Interestingly, the passage presents a paradox: those who "do truth" *come to light*, not because they're perfect, but because they have "deeds [that are] wrought in God." Their identity is aligned with truth, so exposure becomes liberation rather than condemnation.

This suggests that our hatred of light often stems from:
1. **Alienation from our true self**: We've invested in personas that can't withstand scrutiny
2. **Fear of accountability**: What we've hidden might require change, cost, or responsibility
3. **Comfort with the familiar**: Even painful darkness feels safer than uncertain light

## The Spiritual Dimension

Verse 21 hints at a deeper spiritual reality: "he that doeth truth cometh to the light, that his deeds may be made manifest, that they are wrought in God." This isn't merely about behavioral correction—it's about *identity transformation*. 

Those who hate light often do so because:
- They sense something is wrong but cannot or will not name it
- They've made darkness their ally, finding in it a false sense of security or belonging
- They fear that true light will demand everything—including repentance

## The Tragic Irony

Perhaps most tragically, those who hate light often do so out of love—not for evil, but for their own constructed version of safety. They prefer the known pain of darkness to the unknown possibility of healing light. This is why Jesus came: not to condemn those in darkness, but to draw them into the light where their deeds might be revealed as they truly are, and thus become what they were meant to be.

The question this passage implicitly asks is: *What are you willing to have seen?*
