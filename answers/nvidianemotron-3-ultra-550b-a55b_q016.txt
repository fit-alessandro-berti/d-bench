The elder brother’s deepest problem is **discomfort with mercy itself**—specifically, a **transactional view of relationship** that makes mercy not just unwelcome, but logically offensive.

The other options (anger, envy, wounded fairness, pride in obedience) are real, but they are **symptoms** of this root disorder. Here is why the discomfort with mercy is the taproot:

---

### 1. The Diagnostic Clue: The Father’s Final Word
Jesus puts the diagnosis in the father’s mouth (v. 31–32):
> *“Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine. It was meet that we should make merry and be glad…”*

The father does not correct the elder son’s *fact-checking* (the inheritance *is* his; the calf *was* killed for the younger). He corrects the **category error**. The elder son thinks the feast is a **reward for performance**; the father says it is a **response to resurrection** (“was dead, and is alive again”).

If the elder brother’s problem were merely *envy* or *wounded fairness*, the father would say, “You’re wrong about the assets,” or “Life isn’t fair.” Instead, he says: **“You are missing the genre of this moment. This is not a payroll department; this is a household. And in a household, death reversed demands a party, not a ledger.”**

---

### 2. The “Contractor” Mindset vs. The “Son” Reality
The elder brother’s opening plea reveals his self-understanding:
> *“Lo, these many years do I serve thee… neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment.”* (v. 29)

Note the vocabulary: **“serve” (douleuō — to slave/serve as a bondservant), “commandment,” “transgressed.”** He does not speak as a son (*huios*); he speaks as a **contractor who has fulfilled the SLA (Service Level Agreement)**. 

*   **Pride in obedience** (Option 4) is the *fruit* of this mindset: he parades his perfect compliance as leverage.
*   **Wounded fairness** (Option 3) is the *grievance mechanism*: “I kept my end; you broke the contract by rewarding a breach.”

But the **deepest problem** is that **he does not know he is an heir.** He thinks he is an employee. And employees *resent* bonuses given to slackers—because in a wage economy, mercy is injustice. Only in a gift economy (sonship) is mercy the very currency of the realm.

---

### 3. The Refusal to Enter: The Anatomy of Anti-Mercy
> *“He was angry, and would not go in.”* (v. 28)

This is the decisive act. The father *comes out* to him (grace pursues the resistor). The elder brother *stays out*.

Why? Because **to enter the feast is to ratify the mercy.**  
If he goes in, he eats the fatted calf *with* the prodigal. He participates in the celebration of unearned restoration. His refusal is not a tantrum; it is a **theological strike**. He prefers the darkness outside—where the ledger balances, where “fairness” reigns, where he is “right”—to the light inside—where the dead live, the lost are found, and the books are burned.

**Discomfort with mercy** is the only sin that **excludes you from the party while you are still at home.** The younger son was lost *in* the far country; the elder son is lost *in* the father’s house.

---

### 4. How the Other Options Function as Defenses Against Mercy
| Symptom | How it protects him from Mercy |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Anger** | A shield: “I have a right to be furious” feels stronger than “I have a heart that cannot rejoice.” |
| **Envy** | A distraction: Fixating on the *calf* (the gift) keeps him from seeing the *father* (the Giver). |
| **Wounded Fairness** | A counterfeit justice: “Fairness” is a law he can master; mercy is a gift he cannot control. |
| **Pride in Obedience** | A false identity: “I am the *good* one” is a fragile shield against “I am the *loved* one.” |

All four allow him to **stand on his record** rather than **fall on his father’s neck**.

---

### 5. The Parable’s Context: The Pharisees’ Mirror
Luke 15:1–3 frames the trilogy (Lost Sheep, Lost Coin, Lost Sons):
> *“The Pharisees and scribes murmured, saying, ‘This man receiveth sinners, and eateth with them.’”*

The elder brother **is** the Pharisaical murmur incarnate. His problem is not that he *obeys*; it is that his obedience has **curdled into a claim on God**. He wants a God who **pays wages**, not a Father who **throws feasts**. He is comfortable with *justice* (the younger son getting what he deserves); he is **allergic to mercy** (the younger son getting what he *doesn’t* deserve).

---

## Conclusion
The elder brother’s deepest problem is **discomfort with mercy itself**, rooted in a **slave/contractor identity** that cannot survive the economy of grace.

*   **Anger** is the smoke.  
*   **Envy** is the heat.  
*   **Wounded fairness** is the fuel.  
*   **Pride in obedience** is the engine.  

But **the fire** is a heart that has **never learned to receive**—only to earn—and therefore **cannot celebrate** when someone else receives what they did not earn. He is **lost in the house**, starving at the feast, clutching a paycheck the Father never asked for.
