The image is so sharp because it is deliberately absurd.

A **mote** is something tiny, like a speck of dust. A **beam** is something huge, like a plank of wood. Jesus creates a comic, almost ridiculous picture: a man fussing over a tiny speck in someone else’s eye while a massive piece of timber is sticking out of his own. The exaggeration makes the point unforgettable.

Why it works so powerfully:

- **It exposes lack of self-awareness.**  
  The man can inspect another person’s fault in detail, yet is blind to his own much greater fault.

- **It reveals distorted moral vision.**  
  The problem is not only that he has a beam, but that with a beam in his eye he is in no position to see clearly at all.

- **It unmasks pride.**  
  He assumes the role of helper or judge while being in worse condition himself.

- **It is both humorous and cutting.**  
  The near-cartoonish image makes the rebuke memorable, but the target is serious: religious or moral hypocrisy.

The passage does **not** forbid moral correction altogether. That is clear from the last line:  
**“first cast out the beam out of thine own eye; and then shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote out of thy brother’s eye.”**

So Jesus does not say, “Never remove the mote from your brother’s eye.” He says:

1. **Begin with self-examination.**
2. **Repent of your own sins first.**
3. **Then you may truly help another person.**

The criticism, then, is not of correction itself, but of **correction without humility**, **judgment without self-judgment**, and **reproof from a hypocritical spirit**.

In short: the image is sharp because of its grotesque contrast—speck versus plank—and because it dramatizes how hypocrisy makes a person morally ridiculous. But the passage still leaves room for correction: once one’s own vision has been cleared, one may help a brother rightly, not as a hypocrite, but as someone chastened, humble, and able to see clearly.
