**The moral posture demanded by Gal 6:1**

> “Brethren, if a man be overtaken in a fault, ye which are spiritual, restore such an one in the spirit of meekness; considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted.” (KJV)

The verse is a miniature job‑description for every Christian who steps into the delicate work of *fraternal correction*. It tells us **who** may correct (the “spiritual”), **how** to correct (in “meekness”), and **why** they must keep a watch on themselves (“lest thou also be tempted”).  

Below is a step‑by‑step unpacking of the three elements—*restoration*, *meekness*, and *self‑suspicion*—and why they are held together as a single, inseparable posture.

---

### 1. The “spiritual” are called to a humble, relational posture

- **“Ye which are spiritual”** (πνευματικοί, *pneumatikoi*).  
  Paul uses this term in the letter to describe believers who walk “by the Spirit” (Gal 5:16, 25) and who display the fruit of the Spirit (5:22‑23). They are not sin‑free, but they are *guided* by the Spirit and therefore equipped to handle a brother’s failure with spiritual discernment, not with fleshly judgment.

- **Implication:** The one who corrects must first *recognize* his own reliance on the Spirit. The posture is not that of a superior judge but of a fellow‑pilgrim who has been given a “gift of service” (Eph 4:7) to build up the body.

---

### 2. Restoration – the *goal* of correction

**Greek term:** *ἀποκαθιστάναι* (apokathistanai) – “to restore, to bring back to its original condition.”  

- In the New Testament the word is used for **healing** (e.g., the restoration of a paralytic in Mark 2:9) and for **re‑establishing** a broken relationship (e.g., “restore such a one” in Gal 6:1).  
- The purpose is *redemptive*, not punitive. The aim is to bring the brother back into:
  1. **Fellowship with God** (re‑established righteousness),  
  2. **Fellowship with the community** (re‑integration into the church), and  
  3. **Spiritual health** (growth in holiness).

- **Why “restoration” matters:** If correction ends in condemnation, the “fault” remains and the brother may be driven further away. Paul’s language intentionally evokes a *medical* image: the spiritual leader acts like a physician who sets a broken bone, not a judge who hands down a sentence.

---

### 3. Meekness – the *manner* of correction

**Greek term:** *πραΰτης* (prautēs) – “gentle strength,” “meekness,” “humility under God’s authority.”  

- **Meekness is a fruit of the Spirit** (Gal 5:23) and is repeatedly linked with the way Christ Himself dealt with sinners (e.g., Matt 11:29: “I am gentle and humble in heart”).  
- In the context of correction it means:
  - **No harsh condemnation** – the brother is not “outed” publicly for the sake of the corrector’s reputation.
  - **Controlled power** – the “spiritual” person has the authority to act, but chooses to “empty himself” (Phil 2:7) and serve rather than dominate.
  - **Patience** – willingness to give the offender time to repent and heal, trusting the Spirit’s work.

- **Why meekness guards the goal:** Without meekness, restoration becomes a tool for control or self‑righteousness. Meekness ensures that the correction *preserves the dignity* of the one being restored and points always to the love of Christ.

---

### 4. Self‑suspicion (self‑examination) – the *interior check*

**Greek phrase:** “considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted” (σεαυτὸν πειράζων μὴ καὶ σὺ πειρασθῇ).  

- **The word “tempted” (πειράζω)** in the New Testament often carries the sense of *testing* or *trying* by external pressures or internal desire (cf. James 1:13‑14). The warning is: *You, who stand ready to correct, are not immune to the same kind of fall*.  
- **Self‑suspicion** is not a paralyzing doubt; it is a *humble awareness* that:
  1. **I am still being sanctified** – I have my own “faults” and hidden sins.  
  2. **My motive must be examined** – Am I correcting to *help* the brother, or to *elevate* myself?  
  3. **Mercy must be reciprocal** – The grace I desire for myself when I fall is the same grace I must extend to him (Matt 7:12).

- **Why it is indispensable:** Pride is the chief danger in any corrective act. The moment a “spiritual” person thinks he is *above* the temptation, the correction becomes a weapon for spiritual arrogance. Self‑suspicion keeps the heart tender and the correction genuine.

---

### 5. Why Paul links the three tightly

| Element | What it guards against | What it protects |
|---------|-----------------------|-------------------|
| **Restoration** | Mere punishment or exclusion | The offender’s return to fellowship |
| **Meekness** | Harshness, self‑righteousness, public shaming | The offender’s dignity and openness to change |
| **Self‑suspicion** | Pride, spiritual superiority, hidden agenda | The corrector’s humility and genuine love |

All three are required **simultaneously** because:

1. **The goal (restoration) needs a proper method (meekness) and a right heart (self‑suspicion).** If any element is missing, the corrective act becomes ineffective or harmful.  
2. **They reflect the very character of Christ**, who is *the* Restorer (Luke 19:10), the Meek One (Isa 53:7), and the One who “was tempted in every way, just as we are” (Heb 4:15).  
3. **Together they fulfill “the law of Christ”** – “Bear one another’s burdens” (Gal 6:2). Bearing a burden means *carrying* the brother back to health, *in* the Spirit’s gentleness, while *acknowledging* our own frailty.

---

### 6. Practical outworking for today’s church

1. **Before you speak:**  
   - Ask, “Am I acting in the Spirit (i.e., bearing the fruit of meekness)?”  
   - Check your motive: “Do I want this brother restored, or do I want to prove I’m right?”

2. **When you speak:**  
   - Use *restorative* language: “How can we help you get back on track?” rather than “You’re wrong.”  
   - Adopt a *gentle* tone: lower voice, private setting, open‑ended questions; avoid public condemnation.

3. **After you speak:**  
   - **Self‑examine:** “What temptation might I be vulnerable to that resembles his fault?”  
   - **Follow up:** Continue to walk alongside the brother, offering prayer and support (Gal 6:2‑5).

---

### Summary

The moral posture required by **Gal 6:1** is one of **humble, Spirit‑led service** that seeks **restoration** (the redemptive aim), is exercised in **meekness** (the gentle, non‑judgmental method), and is guarded by **self‑suspicion** (the ongoing awareness of one’s own susceptibility to temptation).  

Paul ties these three together because each protects the other: *without restoration the act is empty; without meekness the act becomes harsh; without self‑suspicion the act degenerates into pride.* When all three are present, the church lives out the “law of Christ”—loving one another enough to bring each other back into the full fellowship of God’s kingdom.
