Proverbs 3:5-6 invites a nuanced form of trust that goes beyond either mere reliance on divine guidance as blind faith or a dismissal of human intellect. Let's break down the elements to clarify the type of trust being advocated:

1. **Trust in the Lord with all thine heart**: This phrase suggests a deep, sincere, and holistic trust in God. It implies confidence not just in selective aspects of life but across all situations and decisions. This is not blind faith; instead, it's a recognition of God's sovereignty and a reliance on His wisdom.

2. **And lean not unto thine own understanding**: Here, the emphasis is on not elevating one's own understanding above God’s wisdom. Humble self-distrust acknowledges the limitations and fallibility of human reasoning. It is an awareness that human understanding alone cannot grasp the fullness of existence or divine will. This does not require abandoning intellect, but rather integrating it with faith. It involves being open to re-evaluating one's perspectives in light of divine guidance.

3. **In all thy ways acknowledge him**: Acknowledging God in all aspects of life means aligning actions, decisions, and intentions with the recognition of God’s presence and oversight. This suggests a lifestyle that continuously seeks to honor and understand God’s will.

4. **And he shall direct thy paths**: The promise here is that if one truly acknowledges God and remains humble, then God will provide guidance. This is not a passive reliance but an engaged trust that seeks and follows divine direction.

### Distinguishing Humble Self-Distrust from Anti-Intellectualism

- **Humble self-distrust** involves recognizing both the value and the limits of one's own understanding. It appreciates human intellect but keeps it in perspective, always open to correction or expansion by divine insight. It acknowledges that while reason is important, it should be seen as a tool that works best in concert with spiritual wisdom.

- **Anti-intellectualism**, on the other hand, dismisses or devalues human reason and intellect as inferior or even dangerous when considering spiritual matters. It often leads to ignoring critical thinking and empirical evidence, relying instead on notions that may not be as rigorously examined.

In essence, Proverbs 3:5-6 advocates for a balanced approach where faith and reason coexist, rather than compete. It calls for using one's understanding thoughtfully within the context of a trusting relationship with God, allowing divine wisdom to guide and inform one's understanding rather than replace it. This approach emphasizes humility, open-mindedness, and a willingness to see beyond one's own capabilities and insights.
