Paul’s portrait is largely negative because love is tested less by elevated feelings than by what it refuses to let the ego do. In Corinth—a community marked by rivalry, status-seeking, boasting, and competition over spiritual gifts—love is the opposite of using other people to establish one’s own importance.

The negations identify love’s chief enemies:

- **Pride** turns gifts into grounds for superiority.
- **Envy** treats another’s good as one’s own loss.
- **Self-seeking** makes relationships transactional.
- **Irritability** lets wounded ego govern conduct.
- **“Thinketh no evil”** means, more literally, that love does not *keep an account of wrongs*—it does not maintain a ledger for resentment or revenge.
- **Rejoicing in iniquity** would mean taking satisfaction in another person’s failure; love instead sides with truth and restoration.

The strength proposed here is therefore not dominance, emotional hardness, or the ability to defeat others. It is **self-governed, enduring strength**: the capacity to remain kind without demanding recognition, to absorb injury without becoming vindictive, to hope without becoming cynical, and to persist without surrendering truth.

“Believeth all things” does not commend gullibility, nor does “beareth all things” require tolerating abuse or concealing wrongdoing. The line “rejoiceth in the truth” rules that out. Rather, love resists reflexive suspicion, seeks the good, and does not allow another’s wrong to turn it into hatred.

Paul’s paradox is that love appears vulnerable because it does not boast, retaliate, or grasp for power. Yet it is stronger than pride precisely because it does not need to protect an inflated self. Its power is the power to remain truthful, patient, and committed under pressure.
