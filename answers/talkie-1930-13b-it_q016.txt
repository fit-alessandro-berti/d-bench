The elder brother’s problem is not anger, but discontent with the father’s merciful gladness. He is angry, but angry because he cannot share the father’s joy. He is hurt, but hurt because he has no part in the welcome home of the prodigal. He resents the feast, not because a kid is given to the wanderer, and nothing to him, but because a wanderer is feasted, and he himself passes unfed. He has no quarrel with kindness, but he has a quarrel with gladness which he may not partake. He will not go in to supper, because he must sup alone. The father’s mercy moves him to no thankfulness, but to jealousy and sullen displeasure.
