Based on Philippians 2:5–8, here are the answers regarding the power being praised and why that specific mode of humility is unsettling.

***

### 1. What kind of power is being praised?

The passage praises **Sovereign Servant Power** (often referred to in theology as *Kingship* or *God’s Kingdom through Subordination*). It highlights a form of sovereignty that:
*   **Reverses the expected hierarchy:** Instead of using status to dominate, Christ chooses submission. It affirms that true authority does not require violence or fear; it requires love and self-sacrifice.
*   **Operates via Trust rather than Fear:** This power is rooted in voluntary obedience ("thought it not robbery") rather than coercion.
*   **Is a gift for others:** Since this power is given voluntarily, it becomes the source of liberation for all humans who are "made in the likeness of men."

***

### 2. Why does humility joined to greatness feel unsettling?

The feeling of instability or unease regarding Jesus' humility comes from several psychological and theological layers that contrast with open domination:

*   **The Challenging Substitution of Power:** Open domination is inherently about **agency**—someone takes command, controls the outcome, and defines status through fear or force. It feels "safe" because it guarantees a specific order (who rules, who obeys). However, humility joined to great divine authority unsettles us because it challenges the very assumption that *power comes with protection*. To be powerful without the threat of loss is to lack security.
*   **The Paradox of Self-Denial:** In our culture, there is a natural instinct to equate power with strength and safety. When we see someone who has "greatest" (divinity) but takes upon him the form of a servant or even death, it creates tension because humanity naturally associates greatness with dominance. The unsettling feeling arises when a "God-like" being actively chooses to be *below* human expectation to save us, suggesting a vulnerability that we do not usually associate with God's nature.
*   **The Risk of Loss:** Domination is typically associated with stability (you have control). But the text suggests greatness without safety is not true greatness; it is just power masked as humility. The unsettling nature lies in realizing that "greatness" (divine status) is often *used* by someone who feels compelled to surrender it for the sake of the relationship. Humans feel unsettled because their desire for control and self-worth clashes with the radical choice given through this submission.

**Conclusion:**
Ultimately, Philippians 2:5–8 teaches that **true authority is found in those who refuse to hold power for themselves.** It is unsettling because it calls us to trust a relationship defined by service rather than fear, challenging our natural desire for certainty and control in the face of divine grace.
