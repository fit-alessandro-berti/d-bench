**Pride/self-righteous self-trust, expressed as contemptuous comparison and self-display of virtue; the tax collector’s humility and plea for mercy is the true posture of justification.**

Jesus frames the parable explicitly in Luke 18:9: it is aimed at those “which trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others.” That is the core indictment. The Pharisee’s problem is not primarily that his listed practices (fasting, tithing) are false or that he holds an elaborate wrong doctrinal system elsewhere. Within the story he may well perform those things. What condemns him is that he approaches God confiding in his own righteousness, measures himself favorably against others (especially the publican), and turns even thanksgiving into a display of comparative superiority: “God, I thank thee, that I am not as other men… or even as this publican… I fast… I give tithes….” The prayer is self-referential (“I… I… I”), contemptuous of the man next to him, and lacking any request for mercy or recognition of personal need. Self-trust + despising others = the fatal combination; comparison and virtue-as-self-display are how that attitude appears in speech and posture.

By contrast, the publican “standing afar off, would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven, but smote upon his breast, saying, God be merciful to me a sinner.” He does not claim merit, does not compare himself upward, and makes no list of achievements. He acknowledges his unworthiness and casts himself on God’s mercy. Jesus’ verdict is categorical: “this man went down to his house justified rather than the other.” The posture is spiritually truer because it matches reality before a holy God: every person is a sinner who needs mercy rather than coffers of self-attained credit. Self-righteous confidence closes the door; humble dependence is the condition of being set right (justified). The parable therefore subordinates even genuine religious discipline to the decisive issue of how one stands before God—self-justifying or mercy-seeking.

In short: the Pharisee is condemned for trusting his own righteousness and despising the other man (pride + contempt, enacted by comparison and self-display). The tax collector is vindicated because he knows he is a sinner and asks for mercy.
