Romans 12:20–21 defines **victory over evil as refusing to let evil determine your response**.

Evil “wins” when it makes you become like it: bitter, retaliatory, cruel, contemptuous, or obsessed with payback. Paul’s command is not merely, “Do not do evil.” It is stronger: **answer evil with concrete good**.

> “If thine enemy hunger, feed him; if he thirst, give him drink.”

That is not sentimentality. It is costly, embodied mercy. The enemy has a real need, and the Christian is commanded to meet it rather than exploit it.

The key line is:

> “Be not overcome of evil, but overcome evil with good.”

So victory is not defined as destroying the enemy, humiliating him, or getting even. Victory is **remaining free from evil’s pattern** and acting from God’s goodness instead of from injury, pride, or revenge.

The phrase “heap coals of fire on his head” can sound manipulative, as though Paul is saying, “Be kind so your enemy suffers more.” But in context, that reading misses the point. Paul has just said, “avenge not yourselves” and “give place unto wrath,” because vengeance belongs to God. So the kindness commanded here is not a disguised form of revenge. It is not “hurt him by being nice.”

The “coals of fire” most likely picture the burning shame, conviction, or moral awakening that undeserved kindness may produce. Your goodness exposes the evil for what it is. It may lead the enemy to repentance. And even if it does not, it leaves judgment with God rather than turning you into an agent of vengeance.

So the command is **not weak**. Weakness would be being controlled by the enemy’s evil. Weakness would be reacting predictably, striking back because one cannot do otherwise.

It is also **not manipulative**, at least not when obeyed rightly. Manipulation would mean doing outward good while inwardly seeking the enemy’s pain. Paul commands genuine good: feed him, give him drink, do not be overcome.

The command is **genuinely strong** because it requires mastery over the self, trust in God’s justice, and courage to do good without guarantee of appreciation. It does not mean pretending evil is harmless, staying in danger, or enabling abuse. Boundaries, justice, and protection of the vulnerable can still be necessary. But the heart is forbidden from becoming vengeful.

In Paul’s vision, evil is overcome not when the enemy is crushed, but when evil fails to reproduce itself in you — and when good remains active, concrete, and unconquered.
