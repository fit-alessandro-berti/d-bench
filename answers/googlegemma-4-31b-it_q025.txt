Based on the linguistic structure and the broader context of the Sermon on the Mount, the moral danger in Matthew 6:1 is not the **visibility** of the act, but the **motivation** behind it—specifically, the craving to be seen.

Here is a detailed breakdown of why the craving, rather than the visibility, is the core issue:

### 1. The Grammar of Intent: "To be seen"
The Greek construction used in this verse indicates purpose. The warning is not against doing good deeds that happen to be public, but against doing them *for the purpose* of being seen (*ina blepōsin*). 

The danger is not that other people are watching, but that the performer is using the act of charity as a tool for social climbing or ego gratification. The "goodness" ceases to be an act of love for the poor and becomes an act of self-promotion.

### 2. The Displacement of Reward
Jesus explains the consequence: "...otherwise ye have no reward of your Father." This suggests a "transactional" spiritual reality. 

*   **The Secret Motive:** When a person seeks the applause of men, they are essentially choosing their "payment" upfront. The social prestige, the praise, and the reputation are the rewards.
*   **The Moral Hazard:** By seeking human validation, the actor shifts their allegiance from God to the crowd. The moral danger is a form of idolatry; the "glory" that belongs to God (who enables the goodness) is stolen by the ego of the giver.

### 3. Visibility vs. Publicity
If visibility itself were the sin, then any act of leadership, teaching, or public service would be morally dangerous. However, the Bible frequently encourages public testimony and communal support. 

The distinction lies in the **internal orientation**:
*   **Public Visibility:** I am helping this person; others see it, but my heart is focused on the needs of the recipient.
*   **Publicity Seeking:** I am helping this person so that others will see me as a "generous person."

In the latter case, the recipient of the alms is no longer a human being to be loved, but a **prop** used to enhance the giver's image. This is where the act becomes morally dangerous: it commodifies the suffering of others for the sake of the giver's reputation.

### Summary
The problem is not the **light** (visibility), but the **mirror** (the craving to see oneself reflected in the admiration of others). Public goodness becomes dangerous when the "performance" of virtue replaces the "practice" of virtue.
