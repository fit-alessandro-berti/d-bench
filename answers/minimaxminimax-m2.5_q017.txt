Your question gets to the very heart of human psychology and spiritual dynamics as presented in this passage. The hatred of light is not about the light itself being harmful, but about the **reaction it triggers in those exposed by it**.

Here’s a breakdown of the relation between truth, exposure, shame, and the desire not to be seen clearly:

### 1. Light as Truth and Exposure
In John’s Gospel, "light" is a central metaphor for **truth, holiness, and the revealing nature of God**. Light doesn't just illuminate; it **makes manifest**. It exposes what is hidden—the true nature of our actions, motives, and character.

*   **The Core Issue:** When light (truth) comes, it doesn't change the deed itself; it changes its **visibility**. A deed done in darkness is private, deniable, and feels "safe." When brought into the light, it becomes undeniable, public, and open to judgment.

### 2. Why "Hate"? The Link to Evil Deeds
The text says, *"men loved darkness rather than light, because their deeds were evil."* The word "hate" here implies more than dislike; it suggests an **active avoidance and hostility**. Why?

*   **The Fear of Condemnation:** The Greek word for "condemnation" (*krisis*) can also mean "judgment." Light doesn't just expose; it **judges**. For those whose deeds are not aligned with truth, the exposure carries the inevitable consequence of negative judgment—either by others, by themselves, or by God.
*   **Self-Condemnation:** There is a powerful internal judge. When we do wrong, we know it. We carry a secret knowledge that conflicts with the image we want to project. The light threatens to **break down this protective wall of self-deception**.

### 3. The Chain Reaction: Exposure → Shame → Avoidance
This dynamic creates a powerful emotional chain:
*   **Truth (Light)** comes.
*   It **Exposes** the dark deed.
*   The exposure brings **Shame**—the painful feeling of being seen as defective, wrong, or unworthy.
*   The mind then rationalizes avoidance: *"Lest his deeds should be reproved."* The person fears the reproof (rebuke, exposure, correction).

This is why the text says they **"hate the light."** They do not hate it for what it is, but for what it **does to them**. It threatens their carefully constructed inner and outer world.

### 4. The Desire Not to Be Seen Clearly
This desire is universal. It manifests in many ways:
*   **Hiding our flaws** from loved ones.
*   **Rationalizing** our bad choices.
*   **Avoiding situations** where we might be truly known (like deep counseling, honest community, or prayer).
*   **Creating personas** on social media or in public life.

The desire is for a **controlled narrative**—to be seen only as we wish to be seen. True light robs us of that control.

### 5. The Paradox of the Light
The beautiful twist in John's passage is in the **purpose** of coming to the light (v.21). It's not merely for condemnation.
*   For the **evildoer**: The light *exposes* to condemn.
*   For the **one who does truth**: The light *exposes* to **manifest** and **justify**.
*   *"That his deeds may be made manifest, that they are wrought in God."* The one who acts in truth **wants** the light, because they know it will reveal the good, not just condemn the bad. Their identity is not built on hiding.

### Conclusion: A Choice About Identity
Ultimately, this is a choice between two identities:
*   **The identity of darkness:** Built on hiddenness,image-management, and the fear of being found out. It is a **fragile, anxious** existence.
*   **The identity of light:** Built on exposure, honesty, and being known in one's weaknesses and strengths. It is a **free, transformative** existence.

The hatred of light is the hatred of **being truly known**—of dropping the pretense and admitting, "This is who I am, with all my flaws." The passage challenges us to ask: Do we fear the light because it will only condemn us, or do we trust that, in the light, we can finally be made manifest as something **"wrought in God"**—crafted, healed, and made whole?
