In Isaiah 55:6–7, the call to repentance involves both "way" and "thoughts" to address the entirety of a person's life: their actions and their internal mindset. This dual focus implies several key aspects about repentance and its relation to mercy:

1. **Outward Conduct ("Way"):** The "way" refers to a person's actions, behavior, and overall lifestyle. When God calls for the wicked to forsake their way, it means abandoning sinful actions and behaviors that are contrary to God's will. Repentance involves making tangible changes in how one lives and interacts with others.

2. **Inward Imagination ("Thoughts"):** The "thoughts" refer to a person's internal mindset, attitudes, and intentions. This suggests that repentance is not just about changing actions but also involves a transformation of the heart and mind. It means reevaluating one's motivations, desires, and beliefs to align with God's values.

3. **Comprehensive Repentance:** By addressing both "way" and "thoughts," the text emphasizes that true repentance is comprehensive. It requires a holistic change that includes both outward conduct and inward reflection. This comprehensive approach ensures that a person is not merely altering behavior superficially but is genuinely seeking to turn away from sin.

4. **Possibility of Mercy:** The promise of God's mercy and abundant pardon is contingent on this complete repentance. God is willing to forgive and show mercy to those who sincerely repent, indicating that He desires a genuine transformation rather than mere outward conformity. The call to return to the Lord with both a changed way and thoughts highlights God's readiness to forgive when a person is truly contrite and committed to living according to His will.

In summary, the inclusion of both "way" and "thoughts" in the call to repentance underscores the need for a sincere and holistic transformation of both external actions and internal attitudes. This comprehensive repentance makes possible the reception of God's mercy and abundant pardon.
