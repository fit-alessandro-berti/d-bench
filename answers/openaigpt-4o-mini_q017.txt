The passage from John 3:19-21 highlights a profound and complex relationship between light and darkness, truth and deceit, exposure and shame. To understand why light may be hated, we must consider several interconnected themes: the nature of truth, the implications of exposure, the experience of shame, and the instinctual desire to hide from scrutiny.

### 1. **The Nature of Light and Truth**
In many philosophical and theological traditions, light symbolizes truth, purity, and knowledge. When light "comes into the world," it represents a revelation of reality and an unveiling of the moral and spiritual state of humanity. Truth can be illuminating, but it is also confronting. It forces individuals to face their reality and the reality of their actions. 

### 2. **Exposure and the Fear of Judgment**
The concepts of exposure and judgment are central to understanding the hatred of light. When one's actions are exposed to light, there is a risk of scrutiny and condemnation. As the text notes, "For every one that doeth evil hateth the light," suggesting that individuals who engage in wrongdoing often fear the repercussions of being seen for who they truly are. The light exposes not only actions but also intentions, and the fear of misjudgment can lead to a defensive reaction against the truth.

### 3. **Shame and Vulnerability**
The hatred of light is closely tied to feelings of shame and vulnerability. When individuals engage in actions that they know are wrong or harmful, coming into contact with truth can evoke a strong emotional response—shame. Shame arises from the realization that one's behavior does not align with moral or ethical standards, leading to an instinct to withdraw from light, which symbolizes exposure and judgment.

### 4. **Desire for Acceptance and Comfort in Darkness**
Humans often seek comfort and acceptance in their environments, and darkness can symbolize a state of ignorance or denial that allows for a more comfortable existence, free from the burden of accountability. In this sense, those who love darkness may choose it because it allows them to continue in their behaviors without confrontation, avoiding the discomfort that comes with self-examination.

### 5. **Choice and Agency in Coming to the Light**
The passage contrasts those who engage in evil with those who "doeth truth" and come to the light. This reflects a crucial choice: the decision to accept truth and be vulnerable to exposure, or to retreat into darkness to avoid confrontation with one’s shortcomings. Those who seek truth and come to the light demonstrate a willingness to be transformed and to have their deeds manifest as “wrought in God,” implying an alignment with divine principles and a pursuit of righteousness.

### Conclusion
In summary, light is hated because it represents the exposure of truth that can lead to judgment and shame. The instinct to hide from light arises from a desire to avoid discomfort and maintain a semblance of control over one's narrative. However, in coming to the light, individuals can find not only accountability but also the potential for growth and redemption, aligning with a deeper moral truth that transcends the fleeting security found in darkness. The invitation to the light invites personal transformation and the embrace of a more authentic existence.
