The Pharisee’s condemnation arises not from a single flaw but from a cluster of heart-attitudes that Luke signals in the opening verse: those who “trusted in themselves that they were righteous and despised others.” The parable then exposes the inner workings of that self-trust and contempt.

**1. Pride and self‑righteousness (“trusted in themselves”)**  
The Pharisee’s prayer is not really a prayer. He “prayed thus with himself” (v. 11). God is addressed, but the Pharisee is his own audience. He asks for nothing, confesses nothing, and acknowledges no dependence. His entire speech is an announcement of his own moral achievements. The underlying sin is pride—a refusal to see himself as a debtor before God. This makes his “righteousness” a product of self‑effort rather than a gift received.

**2. Comparison and contempt (“despised others”)**  
The Pharisee’s self‑assessment requires a foil. He defines his goodness by contrasting himself with “extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even this publican.” The prayer is poisoned by contempt: he judges the tax collector rather than loving him. The comparison serves to elevate himself, which is the opposite of the humility that God requires (cf. Isa. 66:2).

**3. Virtue as self‑display (“use of virtue as self‑display”)**  
His fasting and tithing are good things, but they have become badges of superiority, listed before God as though they establish a claim on divine approval. By boasting, “I fast twice in the week, I give tithes of all that I possess,” he turns acts of devotion into a résumé. This instrumental use of virtue is a form of “practicing your righteousness before men to be seen by them” (Matt. 6:1), except here the audience is himself and God.

**4. Implicit false doctrine**  
While the parable doesn’t present the Pharisee teaching false doctrine, his posture *embodies* one: that a person can be justified by one’s own works and moral record, without mercy. Jesus’s shocking conclusion—“this man [the publican] went down to his house justified rather than the other”—directly reverses the merit-based logic the Pharisee assumes.

**Why the tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer**  
- **He knows who he is before God.** Standing “afar off” and unwilling to lift his eyes, he signals profound unworthiness.  
- **He brings no list of merits.** He doesn’t compare himself with others or offer excuses.  
- **He asks solely for mercy.** The cry, “God be merciful to me, a sinner,” is a plea for atonement (the Greek verb hints at “be propitiated”). This acknowledges that his only hope is God’s gracious initiative.  
- **He goes down justified.** The word “justified” is the same one Paul uses for God’s forensic declaration of righteousness. The tax collector receives a right standing with God not because of anything he has done, but because he throws himself on divine mercy—which is the very posture that Jesus says characterizes those who enter the kingdom (Luke 18:17).

In short, the Pharisee’s condemnation is a seamless blend of pride, comparison, contempt, and the use of virtue as self‑display, all flowing from a spiritual system that trusts in self rather than in God. The tax collector’s posture is truer because it reflects the one thing God cannot grant to the proud: a heart that knows its need and looks to mercy alone.
