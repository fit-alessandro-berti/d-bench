This is an excellent question that gets to the heart of Jesus’ teaching in the Sermon on the Mount. The verse you’ve cited (Matthew 6:1) is the thesis statement for the three examples that follow: giving to the needy (alms), prayer, and fasting.

The distinction you’re asking about—visibility itself versus the craving to be seen—is crucial. The text provides a clear answer, but it’s a nuanced one.

**The core problem is the craving to be seen, not visibility itself.** However, visibility is the *temptation* that makes the craving morally dangerous.

Here’s a breakdown of why:

### 1. The Greek Word "Theathenai" (to be seen)

The key phrase is “to be seen of them” (Greek: *theathenai*, from which we get “theatre”). This implies more than just being visible. It means to be *made a spectacle of*, to perform for an audience with the specific intention of being looked at and admired.

- **It’s not the fact that someone *sees* you.**
- **It’s the *purpose* of your action: to create a show for human applause.**

If you give money to a panhandler on a busy street, people will see you. That is a fact of the situation. The question is: *Why are you doing it?* Are you doing it for the person in need, or are you doing it so that the people walking by will think, “What a generous, holy person”?

### 2. The Result: Two Different "Rewards"

Jesus explicitly contrasts two types of reward:

> **“otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven.”**

The logic is simple. If you perform an act of goodness with the craving to be seen, you succeed. People see you, they admire you, and you get your reward: **their praise**. The transaction is complete. You have your payment in full.

But because you traded the heavenly reward for an earthly one, you have **no reward left from the Father**. The moral danger is that you have exchanged an eternal, divine relationship and blessing for a fleeting, human opinion.

### 3. The Deeper Danger: Idolatry of the Self

The craving to be seen is, at its root, a form of **idolatry**. It means you care more about your public reputation than about your private relationship with God. You are using God’s gifts (your money, time, or piety) as currency to buy the admiration of others.

- **It turns goodness into a commodity.** Charity becomes a tool for self-promotion.
- **It hollows out the act.** The gift is no longer about the recipient's need; it's about the giver's ego.
- **It poisons the heart.** The craving for approval becomes the primary motivation, making you a performer rather than a disciple.

### The Nuance: What About "Let Your Light Shine"?

This might seem contradictory to Jesus’ earlier command in Matthew 5:16: **“Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven.”**

There is no contradiction. The difference is the **motive** and the **object of glory**.

- **Matthew 6:1 (The Danger):** Do your good works *so that* [men] will see them and *praise you*. The glory stops at you.
- **Matthew 5:16 (The Call):** Let your light shine so that [men] will see your good works and *glorify your Father in heaven*. The glory passes through you to God.

**Visibility is not the sin. Self-glorification is.** In fact, visibility is often necessary for love and service (e.g., a publicly-funded hospital, a pastor preaching, a mother caring for her child in public). The question is always: **What is the final aim of the visibility?**

### Conclusion: The Real Problem

**The moral danger in Matthew 6:1 is not visibility itself, but the craving to be seen—the desire for an audience of admirers to validate your own goodness.**

Visibility is neutral. It is a context. The craving is the corruption. It transforms a holy act of worship into a self-serving performance. It cheats you out of the only reward that lasts: the quiet, affirming, unseen smile of a Father who “seeth in secret” (Matthew 6:4, 6, 18).
