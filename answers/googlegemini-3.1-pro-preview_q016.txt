To answer what the elder brother’s *deepest* problem is, we have to recognize that the text presents a cascading series of psychological and spiritual symptoms. Anger, envy, wounded fairness, pride in obedience, and discomfort with mercy are all explicitly present. 

However, if we trace these symptoms down to their bedrock, his deepest problem is **a fundamental misunderstanding of his relationship with the Father.** He suffers from an orphan mentality despite living in his Father’s house; he views their relationship as **transactional rather than relational.** 

Here is a breakdown of how the emotions you listed function as symptoms, leading to the core diagnosis:

### 1. The Symptoms
*   **Pride in obedience and Wounded Fairness:** In verse 29, he says, *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment."* He views his life through a rigid ledger of merit. By his accounting, he has earned a reward, yet the rule-breaker receives the prize. Fairness has been violated.
*   **Envy and Anger:** He points out that he never even received a "kid" (a young goat) to celebrate with his friends, while the brother gets the "fatted calf" (the highest form of celebration). 
*   **Discomfort with Mercy:** The elder brother literally refuses to enter the house (v. 28) because grace is being freely distributed. In his transactional worldview, mercy is an offense because it invalidates his years of hard work. If the father can just *give* the fatted calf to a sinner, the elder brother's rigorous rule-keeping feels meaningless.

### 2. The Deepest Problem: A Transactional View of Love
Beneath all of these feelings is a profound theological and relational tragic flaw. The elder brother does not see himself as a son; he sees himself as an employee, and he views the father as a boss. 

We can see this deeply embedded in his own words in verse 29: *"Lo, these many years do I **serve thee** [literally: slave for you], neither transgressed I at any time thy **commandment**: and yet thou never **gavest** me a kid..."*

His vocabulary is that of a master and slave: *serve, commandment, wage (give).* Because he views the relationship as an employment contract, he expects to be paid based on performance. 

### 3. The Father’s Diagnosis
The father’s incredibly gentle response in verse 31 exposes the elder brother's deepest problem: *"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine."*

The father dismantles the transactional worldview by establishing two realities of true sonship:
1.  **"Thou art ever with me":** The reward of being in the family isn't a goat or a party; *it is being with the father*. The brother has missed the intimacy of the relationship entirely. He wanted a goat to go party *away* from the father ("with my friends").
2.  **"All that I have is thine":** The elder brother's envy is tragic because it is misplaced. He is angry that the father didn't *give* him a goat, but the father replies that the brother already *owns* the entire flock. The elder brother has been starving himself in a mansion of abundance because he was waiting to be paid a wage instead of taking his rightful place as an heir.

### Summary
The elder brother’s deepest problem is that **he is alienated from the Father’s heart.** 

He is just as "lost" as the younger brother, but his lostness is much more dangerous because it is masked by perfect behavior. The younger brother was lost in the far country; the elder brother is lost in the living room. His anger, envy, and disgust at mercy all stem from the fact that he thought he was working for a Master, entirely missing the reality that he was loved by a Father. 

*(Note: It is highly significant that Jesus ends the parable without telling us if the elder brother ever goes into the party. Because Jesus was directing this parable at the Pharisees—the "elder brothers" of His day—He leaves the story open-ended, essentially laying the choice to accept the Father's mercy at the feet of the listener.)*
