While the elder brother displays all the symptoms mentioned—anger, envy, and pride—his deepest problem is **a fundamental misunderstanding of his relationship with his father.** He views his relationship as a **contract**, whereas the father views it as a **covenant.**

Here is a breakdown of why the other symptoms are secondary to this core issue:

### 1. The Illusion of "Wounded Fairness"
The elder brother argues from a standpoint of meritocracy: *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee... and yet thou never gavest me a kid."* He believes that obedience is a currency used to purchase rewards. To him, mercy shown to the prodigal is an "unfair" redistribution of wealth. However, his sense of fairness is flawed because he forgets that he was already the beneficiary of everything the father owned.

### 2. Pride in Obedience (The Pharisee Spirit)
His pride is not just in being "good," but in his record of performance (*"neither transgressed I at any time"*). He identifies himself not as a "son," but as a "servant." By focusing on his lack of transgression, he reveals that his obedience was not born out of love for his father, but out of a desire for standing and security. He is "obedient," but his heart is just as distant as the younger brother’s was when he was in the far country.

### 3. Discomfort with Mercy
The elder brother is horrified that the father's grace is not proportional to the son's desert. He views the fatted calf as a "payment" for the younger brother's return, rather than a celebration of a resurrection. He cannot conceive of a love that is given freely; he only understands a love that is earned.

### The Root Problem: The Servant’s Heart
The climax of the passage is the father’s response: **"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine."**

The elder brother’s deepest tragedy is that **he lived in his father’s house as a slave.** He spent years "serving" a father he didn't actually know. He felt neglected (*"thou never gavest me a kid"*) because he was looking for external rewards to validate his worth, failing to realize that he already possessed the ultimate reward: the father’s presence and total inheritance.

**Conclusion:**
His deepest problem is **spiritual blindness.** He believed he was the "good son" while he was actually a "hired hand" in his own mind. His anger and envy were merely the surface symptoms of a heart that valued **performance over relationship** and **justice over grace.**
