The passage presents speech as an **overflow** from an inner reservoir rather than an independent or superficial act. Jesus uses two related images: the heart as a “treasure” (Greek *thesauros* — a storehouse or vault) and speech as what is drawn or poured out from that storehouse. The key phrase “out of the abundance [or overflow] of the heart the mouth speaks” indicates that words are not manufactured on the spot in isolation; they are the visible discharge of what has been accumulating within.

### The Connection Between Speech and the Heart’s “Moral Stockpile”

The “treasure” is not neutral raw material. It consists of moral and spiritual goods or evils that have been deposited over time—thoughts, desires, attitudes, habitual responses, and settled character. Because the heart is the source, the quality of what comes out in speech is constrained by what has been stored. A corrupt storehouse cannot reliably produce sound goods, just as a polluted well cannot produce clean water. This is why Jesus says it is impossible for those whose hearts are evil to “speak good things” in any sustained or authentic way. The words eventually betray the inventory.

This is not merely poetic. It is diagnostic. Jesus is using the principle to expose the Pharisees: their inability to speak truthfully about him (or their tendency to twist words) is not a surface-level mistake but evidence of the evil they have accumulated.

### Symptoms, Choices, or Revelations?

The text supports **all three**, though not with equal emphasis:

- **Revelations**: This is the dominant function in context. Jesus is not giving abstract moral philosophy; he is unmasking hypocrisy. The mouth functions as a witness that testifies to what the heart has been hoarding. In this sense, words are revelatory—they make the hidden visible. People may manage their behavior for a time, but speech has a way of eventually disclosing the real contents of the inner vault.

- **Symptoms**: The imagery strongly supports this. “Out of the abundance” suggests an overflow or natural consequence rather than a calculated performance. Just as fever is a symptom of infection rather than the infection itself, corrupt or gracious speech is a symptom of the heart’s existing condition. The words are downstream from the deeper reality.

- **Choices**: This element is present but secondary and more complex. Speaking is an act of the will—we do choose our words. However, the passage stresses that those choices are not free-floating; they are fed by and expressive of prior deposits. What we repeatedly choose to dwell on, entertain, or justify becomes the “treasure” that later shapes what we are able or likely to say. In other words, choices both *reveal* what is already stored and *add to* the stockpile for the future. The passage does not treat speech as morally neutral self-expression; it treats it as morally loaded because it participates in the ongoing formation (or deformation) of the heart.

### Practical Implication

The logic of the passage moves in both directions. Words are dangerous because they expose what we have become. At the same time, because speech draws from and reinforces the treasure, guarding one’s speech is one way of guarding the heart (though not the only or deepest way). Jesus’ point is that any attempt to produce good speech while maintaining an evil or careless heart is ultimately futile. The storehouse must be addressed.

In short, the text presents speech as the point where the hidden moral inventory becomes public—simultaneously a symptom of what is there, a revelation of character, and a choice that either draws from or adds to that inventory.
