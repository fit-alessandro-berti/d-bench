Enemy-love is difficult because it confronts some of our strongest moral and psychological instincts: self-protection, loyalty to those harmed, the desire for justice, and the wish that wrongdoing be answered by proportionate suffering. An enemy does not merely inconvenience us; an enemy threatens, humiliates, injures, or opposes what we love. To “love” such a person can sound like betrayal of the victim or denial that evil is evil.

But in Matthew 5, Jesus is not asking for sentimentality.

## Not sentimental

Sentimental love says, “It is all right,” or “They probably did not mean it,” or “Just feel warmly toward them.” Jesus says none of those things.

To love an enemy does **not** mean:

- approving their actions;
- pretending abuse or oppression is harmless;
- abandoning justice;
- refusing to name evil truthfully;
- remaining in danger;
- restoring trust without repentance and change;
- allowing another person to keep harming oneself or others.

One may love an enemy while seeking legal protection, setting firm boundaries, exposing wrongdoing, resisting oppression, or refusing reconciliation until there is repentance. Love is not gullibility. Forgiveness is not the same thing as immediate access, trust, or the removal of consequences.

Indeed, enemy-love takes evil more seriously than revenge sometimes does. Revenge can be satisfied by making the enemy suffer. Love seeks something harder: that the wrongdoer cease to be evil, be brought to truth, and—if possible—be restored.

## More than strategy

Enemy-love can have practical effects. It can interrupt cycles of retaliation, reduce violence, disarm hatred, and create the possibility of reconciliation. Figures such as Martin Luther King Jr. drew explicitly on this teaching in arguing that hatred multiplies hatred.

But Jesus’ reason is deeper than strategy: “that ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven.” The pattern is God’s own generosity. God gives sun and rain—the ordinary goods of life—not only to the good but also to the evil and unjust.

That does not mean God is morally indifferent. Matthew’s Gospel speaks strongly of judgment, repentance, and accountability. Rather, it means that God’s goodness is not merely reciprocal. He does not love only those who first deserve it. His benevolence exceeds the logic of “good for good, harm for harm.”

So enemy-love is not primarily a technique for getting better results. It is participation in the character of God.

## A deeper moral strength

The command calls for a strength greater than instinctive retaliation. Anyone can return affection for affection and hostility for hostility. Jesus’ question in the surrounding verses is essentially: what is distinctive about that? Even ordinary social life operates by exchange—help friends, hurt enemies, reward loyalty, punish betrayal.

Enemy-love breaks that economy.

It means willing the true good of the enemy: not their comfort in evil, but their conversion from it. It can therefore include prayer, truthful confrontation, refusal to retaliate, and active concern for their humanity—even while resisting their actions.

This is why “pray for them which despitefully use you” is so demanding. Prayer prevents the enemy from becoming merely a monster in one’s imagination. It does not erase responsibility; it refuses to surrender one’s own soul to hatred.

In that sense, enemy-love is moral strength because it says:

- “You may have harmed me, but you will not determine the kind of person I become.”
- “I will oppose your wrongdoing without becoming your mirror image.”
- “I will seek justice without feeding on vengeance.”
- “I will recognize your humanity without excusing your cruelty.”

That is not weakness. It is disciplined freedom from being ruled by resentment.

## Why it remains hard

It is hard because justice and mercy can seem opposed. We fear that mercy will trivialize suffering. Sometimes people invoke this passage carelessly, especially to pressure victims of abuse into silence, continued exposure, or premature reconciliation. That is a misuse of Jesus’ words.

Christian enemy-love must never be used to protect abusers or evade justice. The victim may need distance, safety, advocacy, and the full force of lawful accountability. Loving an enemy may initially mean nothing more emotionally dramatic than refusing to seek personal revenge and entrusting judgment to God while taking necessary steps to prevent further harm.

So the teaching is neither soft sentiment nor mere strategy. It is a demanding vision of love as a form of holy strength: a love that resists evil, seeks justice, guards the vulnerable, and yet refuses to let hatred have the final word.
