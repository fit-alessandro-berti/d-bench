**The craving to be seen (the motive), not visibility itself.**

Matthew 6:1 explicitly flags the purpose clause: “to be seen of them” (Greek: *pros to theathēnai autois*, “in order to be seen by them”). The warning is against performing acts of righteousness (here “alms,” more broadly *dikaiosynē* or righteous deeds in the following verses) *with the intention* of gaining human applause, admiration, or social status. When that motive dominates, the deed is no longer offered to God; it has already received its reward in the fleeting approval of the crowd. There is “no reward of your Father who is in heaven” because the heart has chosen the wrong audience and the wrong treasure.

### Why motive, not mere visibility?
- **Contrast with Matthew 5:16**: Just verses earlier, Jesus commands, “Let your light shine before others, so that they may see your good works and give glory to your *Father* who is in heaven.” Public visibility is *required* when the goal is God’s glory. The same outward act can be commanded or condemned depending on the direction of the heart.
- **The rest of Matthew 6** reinforces this. Jesus does not forbid all public prayer, fasting, or generosity; he condemns doing them *theatrically*—sounding trumpets, standing on street corners, disfiguring faces—so that people will notice and praise the performer. The sin is ostentation and self-glorification (vainglory), a subtle idolatry that replaces God with the self as the center of the act.
- **Theological logic**: A genuinely good act is corrupted at the root when it is performed primarily for the praise of men. The deed may still benefit the recipient, but before God it becomes hypocritical (*hypokritai*—the word Jesus uses repeatedly in this section). The performer has exchanged eternal reward for temporal ego gratification. This is why the chapter repeatedly urges *secret* practice: not because secrecy is a virtue in itself, but because it purifies motive and realigns the heart toward the Father “who sees in secret.”

In short, the moral danger is not that good deeds are sometimes visible. It is that the *craving* to be seen turns virtue into performance, worship into self-promotion, and heavenly reward into earthly applause. Jesus is diagnosing a disease of the heart—pride disguised as piety—rather than issuing a blanket rule against public goodness. The remedy is single-minded devotion to the Father’s approval above all.
