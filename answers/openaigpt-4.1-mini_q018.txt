Ephesians 4:31–32 presents a striking contrast between negative attitudes—bitterness, wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, malice—and positive responses—kindness, tenderheartedness, and forgiveness. Your question about why bitterness and forgiveness are placed so directly against each other touches on important aspects of the text’s theology and practical ethics.

### Bitterness and Forgiveness: The Contrast

1. **Bitterness as More Than a Feeling:**  
   The Greek word translated “bitterness” (πικρία, *pikria*) often carries the sense of a deep-seated, festering resentment or a corrosive attitude that hardens the heart. It’s not just a fleeting feeling of annoyance but a lasting disposition that can poison relationships and one’s own inner life.

2. **Bitterness as a Chosen Posture and Habit:**  
   The context of Ephesians 4:31—“Let all bitterness... be put away from you”—implies intentionality. The verb used suggests an active removal or casting out of these attitudes. This language indicates bitterness is understood as something more than involuntary feeling; it is a posture or habit that believers are called to reject.

3. **Bitterness as Contagious and Destructive:**  
   Bitterness, along with wrath and evil speaking, is grouped in a way that suggests these attitudes produce ongoing harm. Like a poison or infection, bitterness can lead to quarrels and division (cf. James 3:14–16, where bitterness is linked to disorder and evil practice). This further supports the idea that bitterness is a posture or ongoing condition that spreads relational damage.

4. **Forgiveness as the Antidote:**  
   Forgiveness in verse 32, “forgiving one another, even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you,” is presented as the positive countermeasure to bitterness. Forgiveness entails a release from resentment and ill will, offering mercy rather than holding onto grudges. It is a deliberate, gracious choice modeled on God’s forgiveness in Christ.

### Summary

- **Bitterness and forgiveness are set directly against each other because they represent two fundamentally opposed ways of engaging with offense and relational brokenness:** bitterness clings to resentment and malice, while forgiveness releases and restores.
- **Bitterness is not portrayed as a mere involuntary feeling but as an entrenched posture or habit of heart that believers must actively “put away.”**
- **This posture is harmful and contagious, capable of damaging one’s own spiritual life and communal harmony.**
- **Forgiveness is the divine imperative and means by which believers overcome bitterness, modeled on God’s own gracious forgiveness.**

Thus, Ephesians 4:31–32 calls the Christian community to ongoing ethical and spiritual transformation, rejecting the corrosive and self-perpetuating nature of bitterness and welcoming the healing, restorative power of forgiveness.
