### Why Is Enemy-Love So Difficult to Take Seriously?
Enemy-love feels profoundly unnatural and impractical to many people, which is why it's often dismissed or watered down. Here are some key reasons, rooted in human psychology, culture, and history:

1. **Instinctual Human Responses**: Evolutionarily and psychologically, we're wired for self-preservation and reciprocity. When someone harms us, our brains trigger fight-or-flight responses, releasing stress hormones like cortisol and adrenaline that fuel anger, fear, or vengeance. Loving an enemy—blessing those who curse you or praying for persecutors—runs counter to these instincts. Psychologists like Jonathan Haidt (in *The Righteous Mind*) describe this as a conflict between our "elephant" (emotional impulses) and "rider" (rational self), where emotions often win. It's easier to hate back because it feels justified and provides immediate emotional relief.

2. **Cultural and Social Norms**: In most societies, justice is framed as retribution or deterrence—think "an eye for an eye" or modern legal systems. Enemy-love can seem like weakness or naivety in a world that valorizes strength, power, and "winning." For example, in politics or warfare, loving enemies might be mocked as "soft" or unrealistic (e.g., critics of pacifism during World War II). Media and pop culture reinforce this by glorifying revenge narratives (e.g., movies like *John Wick* or *The Revenant*), making forgiveness look like a plot device rather than a serious ethic.

3. **Personal and Collective Trauma**: If you've been deeply wronged—abused, betrayed, or oppressed—loving your enemy can feel like invalidating your pain or enabling further harm. On a larger scale, groups with histories of persecution (e.g., during genocides or colonialism) may view enemy-love as a luxury afforded only to the powerful, not the vulnerable. Theologian Dietrich Bonhoeffer, who resisted the Nazis, grappled with this in his writings, noting that while Jesus' command is absolute, applying it in extreme evil (like fascism) requires discernment to avoid complicity.

4. **Skepticism About Its Feasibility**: Critics often point out that the teaching seems idealistic or hypocritical when not practiced consistently by its proponents. For instance, Christian history is rife with wars and inquisitions, leading secular thinkers like Friedrich Nietzsche to call Christianity's love ethic a "slave morality" that masks resentment. In a pragmatic world, people question: Does it actually work, or is it just pious talk?

In short, enemy-love is hard to take seriously because it demands overriding deeply ingrained survival mechanisms, cultural scripts, and emotional realities. It's not just difficult—it's subversive, which is why Jesus frames it as a path to becoming "children of your Father in heaven," emulating God's impartial grace (sun and rain for all).

### Is It Sentimental, Strategic, or a Deeper Form of Moral Strength?
The verse can be interpreted in multiple ways, and it's not mutually exclusive—different lenses reveal different facets. Here's a breakdown:

1. **Sentimental (Naive Idealism)**: Some view enemy-love as overly emotional or romanticized, a feel-good platitude disconnected from reality. Critics like philosopher Ayn Rand might argue it's altruistic mush that sacrifices the self for unworthy others, ignoring human flaws. In this reading, it's "sentimental" because it assumes love can magically dissolve hatred without addressing root causes like injustice. For example, telling a victim of abuse to "just love their abuser" without safeguards can come across as tone-deaf or enabling. However, this critique overlooks the verse's context: Jesus isn't advocating passivity but a transformative love that challenges evil non-violently (e.g., turning the other cheek in Matthew 5:39 as a defiant act of dignity, not submission).

2. **Strategic (Practical Wisdom)**: Others see it as a clever tactic for long-term gain. In game theory terms, responding to hostility with kindness can disrupt cycles of retaliation, potentially converting enemies or de-escalating conflicts. Historical examples abound:
   - Martin Luther King Jr. drew on this ethic in the Civil Rights Movement, using non-violent resistance to expose injustice and win public sympathy, leading to legal reforms.
   - Mahatma Gandhi's satyagraha (truth-force) applied similar principles against British colonialism, arguing that loving the oppressor humanizes them and undermines their power.
   - Even in modern psychology, practices like cognitive behavioral therapy encourage reframing enemies as fellow humans with their own pains, which can reduce personal stress and open dialogue.

   The verse's reference to God's impartial provision (sun and rain) suggests a strategic universality: Just as nature sustains everyone, loving enemies models a world where conflict isn't zero-sum. It's not about being "nice" but about breaking vicious cycles—strategic in the sense that it aims for reconciliation and societal healing.

3. **A Deeper Form of Moral Strength**: This is perhaps the most profound interpretation, rooted in theology and ethics. Enemy-love isn't weakness but supreme strength, requiring inner fortitude to transcend ego and resentment. Theologians like C.S. Lewis (in *Mere Christianity*) describe it as aligning with divine love (agape), which is unconditional and not based on merit. It's "deeper" because it fosters moral maturity: By loving enemies, you become like God, who blesses the "evil and the good" alike. Philosophers like Søren Kierkegaard emphasize its existential demand—it's not about outcomes but authenticity, testing whether your morality is genuine or conditional.

   In this view, it's a form of radical freedom: You're not controlled by your enemy's actions but by your own principles. Figures like Nelson Mandela embodied this by forgiving his jailers post-apartheid, turning personal strength into national reconciliation. It's moral strength because it confronts the human capacity for evil within ourselves—loving enemies forces us to acknowledge our shared humanity, reducing the "othering" that fuels hatred.

Ultimately, I lean toward it being a deeper moral strength with strategic elements, rather than mere sentimentality. Jesus' teaching isn't a Hallmark card; it's a call to embody God's kingdom in a broken world, which requires courage and wisdom. If it's sentimental, it's only in the shallowest readings—deeper engagement shows it's profoundly challenging and transformative.
