Based on Matthew 6:1, the moral danger of public goodness lies entirely in **the craving to be seen**, not in visibility itself. 

The text makes a careful distinction between doing a good deed that *happens* to be seen, and doing a good deed *in order* to be seen. 

Here is a breakdown of why this nuance matters and what makes this craving so morally dangerous according to the text:

---

### 1. The Greek Linguistic Clue: Performance vs. Purpose
In the original Greek text, the phrase translated as "to be seen" is *pros to theathēnai*. The root word here is the source for the English word **"theater."** 

Jesus is using language associated with acting on a stage. The moral danger is turning a sacred act of compassion (almsgiving) into a performance for an audience. When the motive shifts from helping the recipient to performing for the spectator, the moral integrity of the act is destroyed.

### 2. The Contextual Paradox: Matthew 6:1 vs. Matthew 5:16
To understand why visibility itself is not the problem, one only has to look back one chapter in the Gospel of Matthew:

*   **Matthew 5:16:** *"Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."*
*   **Matthew 6:1:** *"Take heed that ye do not your alms before men, to be seen of them..."*

At first glance, these two verses seem to contradict each other. However, the difference lies in **the direction of the glory**:
*   In **Matthew 5**, the deed is visible so that observers praise **God**.
*   In **Matthew 6**, the deed is visible so that observers praise **the doer**.

Visibility is often unavoidable and sometimes necessary for encouragement and public good. Therefore, visibility is neutral; the *intent behind the visibility* is what carries moral weight.

---

### 3. Why the Craving to be Seen is Morally Dangerous

Jesus identifies two primary spiritual and moral dangers associated with seeking human applause through public virtue:

#### A. The Transmutation of Goodness into Egoism
When a good deed is motivated by the desire for public approval, it ceases to be an act of love for the recipient. Instead, the recipient becomes an object—a tool used by the giver to build their own reputation. This subtly corrupts love into self-aggrandizement.

#### B. The Exhaustion of the Reward
Jesus warns that those who perform righteousness for human audience "have their reward" (Matthew 6:2). In the ancient Greek financial vocabulary used later in the chapter (*apechousi ton misthon*), this meant "paid in full." 

The moral danger is a bad trade: exchanging the eternal, deep affirmation of God ("your Father which is in heaven") for the fleeting, superficial applause of human onlookers. Once the applause fades, the value of the act has been completely cashed out.

---

### Summary
The problem is not that good deeds are seen by men, but that they are **done to be seen by men**. Public goodness becomes morally dangerous when the doer's primary audience shifts from God to the crowd, transforming a selfless act of mercy into a selfish act of self-promotion.
