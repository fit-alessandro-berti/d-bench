# Discernment vs. the Appetite to Condemn

This is a genuinely important distinction, because Luke 6:37 is frequently flattened into "never make moral judgments"—a reading that doesn't survive contact with the rest of the same passage or the broader teaching.

## What the verse is *not* saying

Just a few verses later (Luke 6:43-44), Jesus tells his listeners to evaluate trees by their fruit: "every tree is known by his own fruit." That requires judgment in the sense of *discernment*. Elsewhere he tells people to "judge righteous judgment" (John 7:24). So the prohibition can't be against assessing right from wrong, or the teaching would be incoherent within its own context.

Moral judgments clearly *do* matter here. The same teacher is making vigorous ones constantly.

## The distinction the Greek and context suggest

The word *krino* carries a range from "discern/evaluate" to "condemn/pass sentence." The verse pairs "judge not" with "condemn not," and "forgive" appears as its positive counterpart. This pattern points the meaning toward:

- **Discernment** — evaluating actions, ideas, and consequences so you can act wisely, love well, and protect others.
- **Condemnation** — assigning a person to a verdict, writing off their worth, appointing yourself their final judge.

The difference isn't *whether* you assess, but *what posture and authority* you assume in doing so.

## The "appetite" framing is the key

You used a sharp word: **appetite**. The text seems aimed less at the act of judgment than at the *hunger* for it. There's a particular pleasure in condemnation—it elevates the condemner, simplifies a messy person into a settled case, and lets us spend moral energy on others rather than ourselves.

A useful diagnostic set of questions:

| Discernment | Condemnation-appetite |
|---|---|
| "Is this action harmful?" | "Now I get to feel superior." |
| Aims at protection or repair | Aims at dismissal or punishment |
| Holds the person open to change | Files the person as a closed case |
| Costs you something (love is harder) | Pays you something (status, relief) |
| Applies the same standard to yourself | Exempts yourself |

That last row is the load-bearing one. The reciprocal structure—"and ye shall not be judged"—suggests the warning is about *the measure you use*. The appetite to condemn nearly always involves a double standard, which is why "first cast out the beam out of thine own eye" follows immediately.

## A way to hold both

You can say "that was cruel" without saying "you are beyond redemption." You can refuse to platform someone's behavior while still refusing to relish their downfall. Discernment can be exacting; condemnation pretends to a finality—a final verdict on a soul—that the verse reserves for someone other than us.

So the difference isn't *strength* of judgment. A condemner can be lax; a discerner can be severe. The difference is **whether you remain accountable to the same standard, and whether you keep the other person in the category of "human being capable of change" rather than "case closed."**

The teaching doesn't ask you to stop seeing. It asks you to notice when seeing has curdled into *feeding* on someone.
