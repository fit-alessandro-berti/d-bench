Luke 6:37 (and its parallel in Matthew 7:1-2) is frequently misread as a blanket prohibition on moral evaluation. It is not. The verse is a warning against a particular *disposition* in judgment, not against judgment itself.

### The Verse in Context

The immediate follow-up in Matthew 7:3-5 makes the target clear: the person who is eager to correct the small fault in his brother while ignoring the massive fault in himself. Jesus is describing a hypocritical, self-exalting form of judgment — the kind that uses moral language primarily to establish superiority rather than to pursue truth or restoration. The warning that “you will be judged with the measure you use” is a principle of reciprocity, not a claim that all moral distinctions are illegitimate.

Jesus elsewhere explicitly calls for “righteous judgment” (John 7:24) and instructs his followers to confront sin in others (Matthew 18:15-17, Luke 17:3). The New Testament is full of moral evaluations of specific behaviors and even of individuals. So the text itself rejects the idea that moral judgment is inherently wrong.

### Discernment vs. the Appetite to Condemn

The crucial distinction is between two different operations of the mind and will:

**Discernment** is the faculty of distinguishing reality — what is true, good, harmful, or disordered. It operates on actions, patterns, and character. It asks questions like:
- Is this behavior consistent with reality and human flourishing?
- Does this person show a pattern that suggests they are safe or unsafe to trust?
- What is the actual nature of what is happening here?

Discernment can be exercised with sorrow, caution, or even mercy. It is compatible with recognizing that the person being evaluated is still a moral agent who might change. In practice, it is necessary for any functional ethics, relationships, or society. Without it, one cannot protect the vulnerable, maintain standards, or even give honest counsel.

**The appetite to condemn** is different in kind. It is not primarily interested in accurate description but in *pronouncing sentence*. Its energy comes from the desire to place someone beneath oneself morally. Key markers include:
- A quickness to move from “this was wrong” to “you are contemptible.”
- Enjoyment or relief in the act of condemnation itself.
- Selective blindness to one’s own comparable or greater failures.
- Little interest in whether the person can repent or whether the judgment is proportionate.

This appetite often masquerades as moral seriousness. It uses the vocabulary of justice while serving pride, resentment, or the need to feel righteous by comparison. The biblical warning is that this posture invites the same treatment in return — not because all judgment is forbidden, but because this *kind* of judgment is corrosive and usually dishonest.

### Why the Difference Matters

Treating the verse as “never judge” collapses into a form of moral paralysis that the rest of Scripture does not support. It would make accountability, correction, and the protection of the innocent impossible. At the same time, the verse rightly identifies a real human tendency: the desire to turn moral perception into a weapon of status. Many people who claim to be “non-judgmental” are actually quite skilled at condemnation when the target is outside their chosen group; they have simply redirected the appetite rather than dismantled it.

The practical test is often internal:
- Does my evaluation make me more humble or more inflated?
- Am I primarily describing what is the case, or am I savoring the feeling of moral superiority?
- Would I apply the same standard and tone to someone I like?

Discernment survives this test. The appetite to condemn usually fails it. The verse is not telling you to stop seeing clearly. It is telling you to stop using moral sight as a way to exalt yourself.
