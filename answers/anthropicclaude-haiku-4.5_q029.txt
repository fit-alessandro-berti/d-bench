# The Moral Posture of Correction

## The Three-Part Stance

Paul binds these elements together because correction **without this posture** becomes its own sin:

**Restoration** — The aim is *recovery*, not punishment or superiority display. The spiritual person sees the fallen brother as salvageable, not condemned.

**Meekness** — Not weakness, but *humility about one's own standing*. The corrector approaches as someone who understands human frailty, not from a platform of moral achievement.

**Self-suspicion** — "Considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted" is the crucial check. It means:
- Recognizing you're capable of the same fault
- Acknowledging the very act of correcting creates vulnerability to pride
- Staying aware that judgment itself is a form of temptation

## Why These Belong Together

They're inseparable because **correction easily becomes the corrector's moral trap**:

- Restoration without meekness → becomes condescension
- Meekness without self-suspicion → becomes false humility (performative)
- Self-suspicion without restoration → becomes paralysis ("who am I to judge?")

Only together do they form what we might call **humble accountability** — the willingness to say: "I see a problem, I care about your restoration, I'm aware of my own capacity for this same failure, and I approach you from that awareness."

The posture is almost forensic: *assume your own guilt even as you address another's.*
