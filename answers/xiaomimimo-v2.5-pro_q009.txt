# A Meditation on Romans 12:20–21

## The Passage in Context

This passage sits at the climax of Paul's ethical teaching in Romans 12, where he moves from spiritual gifts to the radical demands of Christian love. The "coals of fire" imagery draws from Proverbs 25:21–22, and scholars debate its precise meaning — but the overall thrust is clear.

## How It Defines Victory

The passage redefines victory in a striking way:

- **Victory is not destroying the enemy** — it is *feeding* them
- **Victory is not overcoming evil by superior force** — but by superior character
- **Evil is overcome when it fails to reproduce itself** — when hatred meets kindness and cannot find purchase

The command is explicitly framed as *overcoming* (Greek: *nikaō* — the same word used for Christ's conquest in Revelation). Paul is not counseling surrender. He is counseling a different kind of warfare.

## Is It Weak?

No. Consider what this actually demands:

> It is far easier to retaliate. The person who strikes back acts on impulse. The person who feeds an enemy must exercise extraordinary self-mastery, courage, and moral clarity.

Retaliation is the path of least resistance. Kindness toward an enemy requires a kind of strength that most of us simply do not have without deep conviction.

## Is It Manipulative?

This is the harder question. The phrase "heap coals of fire on his head" *does* reference a consequence — the enemy's shame or potential repentance. Could this mean the kindness is strategic rather than sincere?

I think not, for two reasons:

1. **The passage commands genuine action** — feeding the hungry, giving drink to the thirsty — not a performance designed to extract guilt. The acts are real, costly, and aimed at actual human need.

2. **The concluding verse clarifies the motivation**: "Be not overcome of evil, but overcome evil with good." The focus is on *one's own character and victory*, not on manipulating the other person. The coals of fire may be a *result*, but they are not the *purpose* in any calculating sense.

There is a subtle but important difference between:
- *"Be kind so that your enemy feels ashamed"* (manipulation)
- *"Be kind because this is how evil is overcome — and incidentally, your enemy may be transformed"* (conviction)

## Genuinely Strong

What strikes me most about this passage is its radical confidence. It assumes that:

- **Good is more powerful than evil** — not just morally, but *practically*
- **A person secure enough in their identity can afford mercy** — they don't need the enemy's destruction to feel safe
- **The cycle of evil can actually be broken** — not by escalation, but by a deliberate, courageous refusal to participate in it

This is not the ethic of a person who is afraid. It is the ethic of a person who believes that goodness, honestly and persistently enacted, is the most formidable force in the world.

## A Final Thought

The passage ultimately asks: *What kind of person do you want to be?* Not, "How do you want your enemy to suffer?" but "Will you allow evil to turn you into something you're not?"

Victory here is measured not by what happens to your enemy, but by what happens to *you*.
