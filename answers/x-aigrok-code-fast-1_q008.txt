### Theological Context and Logic

The verse from 1 John 4:20 is part of a broader epistle emphasizing authentic Christian faith through love. In Christian theology, God is depicted as immaterial and invisible (e.g., John 1:18; Colossians 1:15), making direct "proof" of love for Him intangible. Instead, the Bible often tests spiritual claims through observable actions. Loving God manifests in loving one's "neighbor" (fellow humans), as per Jesus' teachings (e.g., Matthew 22:37-40, the Great Commandment). The verse frames love for a visible person (the "brother") as a tangible benchmark for love of an abstract deity because:
- **Practical Demonstration**: Loving a visible person requires real-world effort, like kindness, forgiveness, or service, which reveals whether one's "love" for God is just words or genuine conviction. If hatred or indifference defines reactions to people, it exposes a disconnect—true divine love should flow into relational actions.
- **Emotional and Ethical Consistency**: Loving God implies aligning with His "image" (Genesis 1:27) and character, which is defined by love (1 John 4:8). Hating those made in God's likeness contradicts this, suggesting the claim of divine love is performative or self-flattering rather than heartfelt.

This setup treats neighbor-love as a "test" because it's verifiable in daily life, unlike esoteric devotion to an unseen God, which can be easily faked or confused with emotion alone.

### Self-Deception Exposed

The verse exposes a form of **hypocritical self-delusion**—where someone convinces themselves of a noble spiritual identity while ignoring contradictions in behavior. This isn't mere lying to others but a deeper internal blindness:
- **Irrational Rationalization**: The speaker might rationalize hatred (e.g., as "righteous indignation" or "necessary judgment") without recognizing it violates biblical directives, deluding themselves that selective love equates to loving God.
- **Emotional Substitution**: Claiming to "love God" often stems from comforting feelings (e.g., from worship or doctrine) rather than transformative action. The verse pinpoint this gap: if love doesn't extend to the visible and imperfect neighbor, the invisible God-love is likely an illusion, perhaps rooted in pride or avoidance of vulnerability.
- **Psychological Insight**: This aligns with cognitive biases like confirmation bias, where people seek evidence confirming their self-image. Here, lack of neighbor-love forces confrontation with insincerity, preventing harm from misleading oneself about one's faith.

In essence, the verse warns against a faith that's head-only, urging a "heart and hands" consistency. Christians often interpret it as a reminder that spiritual growth requires relational evidence, preventing the self-deception that benefits the ego more than God.
