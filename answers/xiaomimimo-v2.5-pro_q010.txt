This passage presents a striking vision of **kenotic power**—power expressed through voluntary self-emptying and limitation. Here’s a breakdown of the questions you raised:

---

### **1. What kind of power is being praised?**
The power described is **not the overt, dominating power** we often associate with authority. Instead, it is:

- **Power through humility** – Christ, though divine, chose not to assert His divine rights but emptied Himself (“made himself of no reputation”).
- **Power through servanthood** – He took on “the form of a servant,” showing His authority was expressed in serving rather than ruling over.
- **Power through obedience** – He submitted even to death—on a cross, a shameful, agonizing death—yet this was an act of supreme love and redemptive victory.
- **Power through self-limitation** – By voluntarily becoming vulnerable, He revealed that true strength is not in grasping but in giving.

This is the power of **redeeming love**—a power that wins not by force, but by sacrifice. It’s the paradox of the Gospel: victory through apparent defeat, life through death.

---

### **2. Why can humility joined to greatness feel more unsettling than open domination?**
This is a profound psychological and spiritual insight. Several reasons come to mind:

#### **a) It disrupts our expectations**
We instinctively associate **greatness with assertive control**. When someone powerful chooses humility, it shakes our mental categories—we struggle to reconcile authority with vulnerability. It challenges us to rethink what power really is.

#### **b) It exposes our own thirst for dominance**
Seeing someone with every right to claim superiority *choosing* servanthood can make us uneasy. It raises uncomfortable questions:  
- *Why do we crave recognition and status?*  
- *Is our own drive for power rooted in insecurity rather than love?*  
The humility of greatness can feel like a silent judgment on our self-centered ambitions.

#### **c) It demands a response**
Open domination can be resisted, resented, or evaded. But humble greatness invites us into a relationship of **gratitude, trust, and imitation**—which is harder to dismiss. If Christ with all divine power chose servanthood, what does that call us to do?

#### **d) It disarms defensiveness**
We know how to fight or flee from overt domination. But humble love has no armor to attack, no ego to wound. It meets us not in confrontation, but in invitation—leaving us with the discomforting choice to accept its grace or walk away.

---

### **In short:**
The power praised here is **authority expressed as love, might wrapped in gentleness, victory achieved through surrender**.  
It feels unsettling because it turns our instinctive ideas of power upside down—it shows that the highest form of greatness is not ruling over others, but laying down oneself for others.

This passage calls us not just to admire Christ’s humility, but to **let the same mind be in us**—to find true greatness not in domination, but in self-giving love.
