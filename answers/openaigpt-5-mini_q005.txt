Short answer: It’s not mere sentimentality and it can have strategic benefits, but at root Jesus is calling for a radical reorientation of the will and imagination — a deeper moral strength that refuses simple reciprocity, remakes identity, and models God’s impartiality.

Why it’s so hard
- Natural instincts. Evolutionary and psychological wiring favors reciprocity, in‑group loyalty, and avoidance of threat. These instincts make “returning hate with love” feel like betrayal of self‑preservation.
- Emotion and justice. Anger, fear, humiliation, and a genuine desire for justice push back against any command that looks like “put up and shut up.”
- Moral confusion. People conflate love with liking, approval, or weakness. Loving an enemy is often read as condoning wrongdoing or abandoning boundaries.
- Social cost. Loving an enemy can look foolish or dangerous in a group that expects retaliation; it risks social status and safety.
- Theological and existential demand. The Gospel call to love enemies asks one to share the character of God (his impartial “sun and rain”), which requires humility, forgiveness, and an inner conversion.

Is it sentimental, strategic, or moral strength?
- Not merely sentimental. Sentimentality treats love as soft feeling; Jesus’ command is intentional, costly, and often opposed to feeling.
- Sometimes strategic. Nonviolent resistance and reconciliation can produce powerful practical results (Gandhi, MLK, restorative justice). Loving enemies can undermine cycles of violence, win allies, and open paths to repair.
- Fundamentally a deeper moral strength. It is a discipline of will and character (agape): choosing the good of the other, refusing to repay evil with evil, and reflecting God’s impartiality. That requires courage, self‑control, imagination (seeing the enemy as created and wounded), and often sacrificial risk.

How to take it seriously without being naïve
- Distinguish love from approval: love can coexist with seeking accountability and justice.
- Keep wise boundaries: refusing retaliation doesn't force exposure to continued harm.
- Practice concrete disciplines: pray for and about enemies, perform small acts of kindness where safe, learn narratives that humanize the other.
- Seek community and counsel: love in isolation is vulnerable; community helps hold moral aims and practical prudence together.
- Aim for both justice and reconciliation: love should not short‑circuit structural repair but can be the motor for transformative justice.

Conclusion
Loving your enemy is demanding because it cuts against deep instincts and social logic. It’s not mere softness nor only clever strategy; it’s a cultivated, courageous ethic that redefines identity and responsibility. When taken seriously it reshapes how you see others, neighbors, and God — and it often produces both moral growth and powerful social consequences.
