The text from Matthew 7:3–5, often known as the parable of the mote and the beam (though in Jesus' words, a "mote" is literally a speck in a plainer, while a "beam" refers to a large piece of timber), presents one of Christ’s most powerful critiques of judgment.

### 1. The Sharpness of the Image
The image is sharp because of its **radical disproportion** and the **mechanics of perception**.

*   **Scale:** A mote (a tiny speck of dust or wood) causes localized, immediate irritation. A beam represents a massive structural weight in human experience; it can obscure sight or collapse a house. The contrast highlights the absurdity of complaining about a minor flaw in someone else while ignoring a major failing within oneself.
*   **Visibility:** A beam is so large that it physically blocks vision to its owner. When Jesus asks, "Or how wilt thou say to thy brother," he suggests that if you are blinded by your own sin, the judgment you project onto others will be distorted—like trying to read a map through a telescope blocked by your own nose.
*   **Clarity vs. Hypocrisy:** The image cuts deep because it argues that true moral authority depends on clarity of vision. You cannot be an effective teacher or judge if your own eyesight is compromised. It exposes the hypocrisy not as "pretense," but as literal blindness caused by one's own failure to see clearly.

### 2. Criticizing Hypocrisy Without Forbidding Correction
The passage does not ban moral correction; it rather **reorders the prerequisites** for valid correction. It makes "self-honesty" the condition for "other-correction."

*   **Defining Hypocrisy:** In this text, hypocrisy is defined as **judgmental arrogance**. A hypocrite is someone who claims to know how to fix *others' problems* but lacks self-awareness regarding how they affect their own moral lens. It criticizes the *mechanism* of judgment: "You claim the right to intervene in me because you are better than me, yet you are blinded by your own flaws."
*   **Conditionality over Prohibition:** Jesus says, *"First cast out... and then shalt thou see clearly to cast out."* Note the conditional structure. If someone removes their beam, they have the *capacity* to help. The instruction does not forbid removing the mote; it forbids doing so as a way of self-righteousness.
    *   **The Correction Allowed:** Moral correction remains valid because the goal is restoration, not humiliation or control. The "mote" in a brother represents genuine sin or harm that needs addressing for the other person’s own sake.
    *   **The Requirement:** However, this correction becomes possible only when the judge is self-attuned. If you have your light dimmed by your own beam, you cannot see what you are correcting, meaning the correction would be based on false premises or ego rather than objective truth.

In essence, Jesus teaches that **clarity precedes charity.** You can and will correct others, but if you do not correct yourself, your correction is less about justice and more about performing righteousness to cover up your own blindness.
