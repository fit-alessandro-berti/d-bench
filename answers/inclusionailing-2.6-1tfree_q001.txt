The text sketches moral agency as an **interior politics of attention and response**, rather than a battle against an alien “thing.”  
Evil is presented primarily as **a crouching presence at the threshold**—not yet fully interiorized, but poised to enter through the door of choice.  
The decisive responsibility is **to “do well”** (initially, to act rightly in the offering and in the response to God), and secondarily, **the mastery of desire** (“rule over him”).

Below are the key features of this picture and how they bear on your specific terms.

---

### 1. The nature of “sin” (חַטָּאת) in the image

- **Location and posture:** Sin is “lying (רֹבֵץ) at the door.”  
  - רֹבֵץ is used of beasts lying in wait (Gen 49:9; of Judah; cf. a lion crouching), but here it is sin itself, not a beast separate from sin.  
  - The door marks a **boundary between outside and inside**, between opportunity and act. Sin is not yet *in* the house; it is at the entrance, pressing to come in.

- **Not mere impulse, not yet habit:**  
  - If it were impulse, it would be inside and driving behavior automatically.  
  - If it were habit, it would have settled into the structure of the person’s life.  
  - Here sin is **prospective and contingent**: it awaits the outcome of Cain’s choice (“If thou doest not well…”).  

- **Temptation is implied, but indirect:**  
  - The text does not depict a tempter whispering in the ear.  
  - Temptation is represented as the *attractive force of sin’s desire* (“his desire is toward you”), which will seek to master Cain if Cain does not master it.

---

### 2. The relation of desire (תְּשׁוּקָּתוֹ) and rule (וְאַתָּה תִּמְשָׁל־בּוֹ)

- **Two possible readings of “his desire”:**  
  1. **Sin’s desire for Cain** (personified): Sin wants to possess Cain, to enter and dominate him.  
  2. **Cain’s own sinful desire** (possessive understood): The desire that belongs to sin (the desire to do wrong) is at the door, and you must rule it.

- **Rule (מָשַׁל) is a political, not merely a physical, term:**  
  - It suggests governance, rightful authority, and responsibility.  
  - The same verb is used in Gen 1:28 (humans ruling over creatures) and later in Gen 49:10 (the ruler’s staff).  
  - Thus, moral agency here is **governmental**: the person is to exercise sovereign self-direction over the forces that would usurp that role.

---

### 3. Responsibility placed on the person

- **Primary condition: “If thou doest well…”**  
  - Responsibility is anchored in concrete *doing* (ethical action, worship, treatment of others) rather than merely feeling or intention.  
  - Acceptance (“accepted” in many translations) is conditional on this doing, not on mood or victimhood.

- **The question of countenance:**  
  - God asks why Cain’s face has fallen—pointing to the **interpretation of one’s own affect**.  
  - Anger and dejection are not merely “how he feels”; they are signs to be examined, not excuses. The agency lies in whether one allows them to dictate action.

- **Threshold responsibility:**  
  - The warning about sin at the door means the decisive moment is **before the act**, not after compulsion.  
  - To say “sin made me do it” is already to have lost the battle; the text presumes the door is still shut and can be kept shut by “doing well.”

---

### 4. How this fits your options

- **Impulse?**  
  Not quite. Impulse suggests a sudden inner push that is hard to resist. Here, sin *can be* resisted; it is pictured as outside, not yet governing.

- **Beast?**  
  Close in imagery (crouching), but the text does not call sin a beast; it uses an animal-like verb to describe its posture. The emphasis is on its *threat* and *intent to dominate*, not its species.

- **Habit?**  
  No—habit implies repetition and settled character. This is the moment *before* habit is formed; the warning is to avoid letting it settle in.

- **Temptation?**  
  Partially, but temptation is usually an external lure; here the lure is already at the boundary, and the danger is *subjection* to it. The focus is less on the lure and more on the response and the governance of desire.

- **Something else?**  
  Yes: **a rival regime seeking annexation**. Sin is like a pretender to the throne of the self, waiting for the ruler (Cain) to abdicate through wrongful action. The ruler’s duty is to govern justly (“do well”) so that the rival cannot seize power.

---

### 5. Summary picture of moral agency

- Human beings are portrayed as **rulers of a domain that includes their own desire and potential sin**.  
- Evil is **not an internal tyrant yet**, but a **present danger at the gate**, requiring vigilance and right action.  
- Responsibility is **future-directed and political**: act rightly now, and you maintain sovereignty; fail, and you will be mastered by what now lies at your door.  
- The text thus avoids both **determinism** (“you can’t help it”) and **dualism** (“an alien devil inside you”); instead it offers an **ethic of attentive, governing action** in the face of recognizable danger.
