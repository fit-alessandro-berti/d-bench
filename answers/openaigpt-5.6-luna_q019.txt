James exposes the contradiction of **honoring God in worship while dishonoring those who bear God’s image**. The same mouth praises “God, even the Father,” and then speaks of human beings as though they were unworthy of dignity or goodwill. The problem is not merely inconsistency in vocabulary; it is inconsistency in moral vision.

To bless God rightly is to acknowledge his worth and authority. But God’s worth cannot be separated from the worth he has given his creatures. Since people are made “after the similitude of God” (echoing Genesis 1:26–27), contempt for them is indirectly an insult to their Maker. James therefore treats love of God and respect for neighbor as inseparable. A person cannot coherently say, “God is glorious,” while using speech to deny the dignity God has stamped upon another person.

The phrase “out of the same mouth” emphasizes that the contradiction comes from the same inner source—the same person, heart, and will. Religious speech may be correct while the character expressed toward others remains proud, resentful, or cruel. James is exposing a divided spirituality: devotion in one direction and hostility in another.

Speech reveals moral disorder quickly because it is a near-immediate overflow of what a person values and desires. It often escapes before careful self-presentation can conceal pride, anger, envy, or contempt. James develops this image with the ideas of a spring and a fruit tree: what comes out reveals what is within. The tongue also makes inward attitudes public and relational; it does not merely expose the speaker’s disorder but can wound, diminish, and set communities against one another.

Thus “these things ought not so to be” is a call to more than avoiding bad words. It calls for an undivided heart—one whose praise of God is matched by reverence for God’s image in other people.
