Galatians 6:1 requires a posture of **humble, Spirit-shaped responsibility**. Correction is necessary, but it must not become domination, humiliation, or self-congratulation.

- **Restoration gives correction its goal.** The verb translated “restore” (*katartizō*) can mean to mend, repair, or put back into proper condition. The fallen person is not merely to be exposed or punished, but helped back toward faithful life and fellowship.

- **Meekness governs its manner.** Meekness is not passivity or moral softness. It is strength without harshness—the refusal to use another person’s failure as an occasion for superiority. In the context of Galatians 5, gentleness is fruit produced by the Spirit.

- **Self-suspicion guards the corrector.** “Considering thyself” calls for watchfulness over one’s own vulnerability. The corrector may be tempted by the same sin, but also by pride, cruelty, gossip, or the illusion of being beyond failure. Paul shifts from the plural—“you who are spiritual”—to the singular—“yourself”—making the warning personal.

These belong together because each prevents correction from becoming distorted:

- restoration without meekness becomes coercive;
- meekness without restoration becomes mere avoidance;
- correction without self-examination becomes hypocrisy and pride.

Thus the “spiritual” person is not the one who stands safely above the offender, but the one who bears the offender’s burden while remembering that both stand in need of grace. The moral posture is neither condemnation nor indulgence, but **truthful, gentle, and vigilant solidarity**.
