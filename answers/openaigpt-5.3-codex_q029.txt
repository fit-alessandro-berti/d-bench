Great question. In **Galatians 6:1**, Paul describes a very specific moral posture for correction:

1. **Aim to heal, not to win**  
   “Restore” (Greek *katartizō*) means to mend, set right (like setting a broken bone).  
   So correction is **therapeutic**, not punitive.

2. **Act from spiritual maturity, not superiority**  
   “You who are spiritual” means those walking by the Spirit (context: Gal 5), not self-appointed moral elites.  
   Real spirituality looks like the fruit of the Spirit—especially gentleness.

3. **Use meekness as the manner**  
   “Spirit of meekness” means strength under control: truth spoken without aggression, contempt, or shaming.

4. **Keep watch on yourself**  
   “Considering yourself, lest you also be tempted” adds moral self-suspicion:  
   - you could fall into the same sin, or  
   - into different sins while correcting (pride, harshness, hypocrisy).

---

Paul keeps **restoration + meekness + self-suspicion** together because if one is removed, correction is corrupted:

- Restoration without meekness becomes control.
- Meekness without restoration becomes moral passivity.
- Both without self-watch become hypocrisy.

So the posture is: **humble, gentle, self-aware, and healing-oriented**—a fellow sinner helping another sinner back to wholeness under the Spirit.
