Luke 6:37 is one of the most cited—and most frequently misunderstood—verses in the New Testament. When read in isolation, it can sound like a command for moral relativism: a suggestion that because no one is perfect, no one has the right to identify wrong or distinguish between right and wrong.

However, a careful reading of the text and its broader biblical context reveals that Jesus is not abolishing moral judgment; He is reorienting the **posture** of the judge. The verse distinguishes between the necessary work of **discernment** and the destructive impulse of **condemnation**.

Here is an exploration of how this verse differs from the idea that moral judgments never matter, and the critical distinction between discernment and condemnation.

---

### 1. The Misconception: "Judge Not" as Moral Relativism
The common secular or superficial interpretation of "Judge not" is that moral categories are subjective. Under this view, to say something is "wrong" is to impose one's own bias, and therefore, all moral criticism is forbidden.

If this were the meaning of Luke 6:37, it would contradict the rest of Jesus' teaching. Throughout the Gospels, Jesus makes sharp moral distinctions:
*   He calls the Pharisees "whitewashed tombs" (Matthew 23:27).
*   He warns, "Beware of false prophets" (Matthew 7:15).
*   He commands, "Do not give dogs what is holy" (Matthew 7:6).

If moral judgments never mattered, Jesus would not have needed to warn His followers to be discerning. Therefore, Luke 6:37 cannot mean "stop evaluating behavior." It must mean something more specific about the *nature* of that evaluation.

### 2. The Distinction: Discernment vs. Condemnation
To understand the difference, we must look at the intent, the posture, and the outcome of the act.

#### Discernment (The Necessary Judgment)
Discernment is the ability to perceive truth, distinguish between good and evil, and identify danger or error. It is an act of protection and truth-seeking.
*   **Goal:** Restoration, safety, and truth.
*   **Posture:** Humility. The discerner acknowledges, "I am also capable of error; I am looking at this to help or protect."
*   **Example:** A doctor diagnosing a disease. The doctor identifies the sickness (moral judgment of the condition), not to shame the patient, but to prescribe a cure.
*   **Biblical Parallel:** In 1 Corinthians 5, Paul instructs the church to judge those *within* the community who are living in unrepentant sin, not to cast them out forever, but to bring them to repentance.

#### Condemnation (The Forbidden Judgment)
Condemnation (Greek: *katakrinō*) is a judicial sentence of guilt that results in rejection. It is an act of finality and superiority.
*   **Goal:** Vindication of the self, exclusion of the other, or retribution.
*   **Posture:** Arrogance. The condemner assumes a position of moral superiority, acting as the final arbiter of a soul's worth.
*   **Example:** A judge sentencing a criminal without considering context, mercy, or the possibility of rehabilitation, simply to assert power.
*   **Biblical Parallel:** The Pharisees bringing the woman caught in adultery (John 8). They sought to condemn her to death to prove their own righteousness. Jesus intervened not to say "sin doesn't matter," but to say, "Let him who is without sin cast the first stone."

### 3. The Theological Mechanism: The "Measure"
The second half of Luke 6:37 provides the key to the distinction: *"For with the measure you use, it will be measured to you."*

This suggests that the problem is not the *act* of judging, but the **standard** applied.
*   **Hypocritical Judgment:** When I judge you by a standard of perfection that I do not apply to myself, I am usurping the role of God. I am demanding strict justice from you while claiming grace for myself. This is the "appetite to condemn."
*   **Discernment within Grace:** When I judge a situation, I do so with the awareness that I am also in need of grace. I acknowledge that while the action is wrong, the person is redeemable.

If moral judgments never mattered, the "measure" would not apply. But because moral reality is objective (sin exists, truth exists), the *way* we engage with that reality determines how we are treated by God and others.

### 4. Why Moral Judgments Still Matter
Luke 6:37 forbids us from becoming the **executioners** of morality, but it does not forbid us from being the **guardians** of it.

*   **Protection:** We must judge a wolf as a wolf to protect the sheep. If we refuse to make that moral judgment, we endanger the vulnerable.
*   **Integrity:** A community cannot function without shared moral standards. If we cannot say "this is unjust," we cannot pursue justice.
*   **Love:** True love requires truth. To allow someone to continue in destructive behavior without naming it is not love; it is enabling. Discernment says, "This is wrong, and I love you too much to let you destroy yourself."

### Summary
Luke 6:37 is not a command to abandon moral reasoning; it is a command to abandon **moral superiority.**

*   **To say moral judgments never matter** is to deny the existence of truth and the reality of harm.
*   **To obey "Judge not"** is to recognize that while we must distinguish between right and wrong (discernment), we must not assume the role of the final Judge who determines eternal worth (condemnation).

We are called to hold moral standards high (discernment) while holding our own judgment of others low (humility), leaving the final verdict to God and the opportunity for forgiveness open to all.
