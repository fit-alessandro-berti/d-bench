The imagery in Matthew 7:3–5 is sharp because it employs **vivid, relatable metaphors** that highlight the absurdity of self-righteous judgment. The contrast between a "mote" (a tiny speck) and a "beam" (a massive wooden plank) is hyperbolic, meant to expose the illogicality of focusing on minor faults in others while ignoring one’s own glaring flaws. This metaphor resonates across cultures and time, as it taps into a universal human tendency: the ease with which we notice others’ imperfections but blind ourselves to our own. The physicality of the image—someone literally unable to see clearly due to a massive beam in their own eye—forces the listener to confront the practical and moral blindness of hypocrisy.

### How the Passage Criticizes Hypocrisy Without Forbidding Moral Correction:
1. **Exposing Hypocrisy Through Hyperbole**:  
   Jesus uses the "beam" and "mote" to ridicule hypocrisy, which is rooted in **self-deception and double standards**. The term "hypocrite" (Greek: *hypokrites*, literally "actor") underscores the performative nature of such judgment—it is insincere, prioritizing self-justification over genuine moral concern. The critique is not of correcting others *per se*, but of doing so while neglecting one’s own sin.

2. **Preserving Moral Responsibility with a Condition**:  
   The passage does not abolish moral correction; it redefines its proper context. Jesus commands, "First cast out the beam," implying that **self-examination and repentance must precede admonishing others**. This aligns with the broader theme of the Sermon on the Mount (e.g., 6:1–6, 12), where true spirituality begins with inner transformation. Moral correction is permitted, but only when it arises from humility, not self-righteousness.

3. **Logical Order of Moral Action**:  
   The structure of the argument moves from the absurdity of judgment (*"Why beholdest thou the mote..."*) to the necessity of self-purification (*"First cast out the beam"*). This "beam-first" principle reflects a **hierarchical approach to ethics**: one must address personal sin before engaging in communal repair. It guards against the arrogance of judging others while acknowledging the legitimacy of righteous intervention when done with integrity.

4. **Theological and Cultural Context**:  
   In Jewish tradition, admonishing a neighbor for sin was a moral duty (Leviticus 19:17), but it required wisdom and self-awareness. Jesus sharpens this by demanding that the admonisher first remove their own "beam"—a metaphor for systemic sin, pride, or unrepentant behavior. This echoes the prophetic tradition, where God’s prophets often confronted hypocrisy (e.g., Isaiah 1:11–17), emphasizing that ritual or moral acts are hollow without justice and humility.

### Modern Relevance:
The image remains sharp because human nature persists in this pattern: we often critique others’ minor flaws while ignoring our own systemic issues (e.g., prejudice, hypocrisy, or unaddressed trauma). The passage invites a reflexive approach to morality, where **self-awareness and accountability** become prerequisites for meaningful engagement with others’ struggles. It does not forbid correction but insists it be rooted in love and self-awareness, avoiding the destructive cycle of judgment and defense.

In essence, Jesus’ critique is both a warning *against* hypocrisy and a call *to* humble, grace-filled action in the world.
