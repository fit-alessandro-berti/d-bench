The moral danger Jesus identifies in Matthew 6:1 is **the craving to be seen**, not visibility itself.

The verse warns against doing good deeds **for the purpose of being seen by others**. The problem isn’t that others might witness your actions—the text isn’t calling for secrecy per se. Rather, it’s about **motivation and reward-seeking**.

When goodness is performed **to be seen**, the reward sought is human approval or admiration. That becomes the *only* reward received—because God does not honor actions done to glorify self rather than to honor Him or sincerely help others.

Later in the same discourse (Matthew 5:16), Jesus tells believers,  
“Let your light shine before others, that they may see your good deeds and glorify your Father in heaven.”  
Here, visibility is encouraged—but the goal is God’s glory, not personal acclaim.

So the key distinction is:
- **Visibility with righteous motivation** → draws attention to God.
- **Goodness performed to be seen** → draws attention to self, empties the act of spiritual reward.

In essence, it’s the inward craving—the desire for human recognition—that corrupts the outward act.
