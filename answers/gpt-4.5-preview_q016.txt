The elder brother’s deepest problem is a complex mixture, but fundamentally, it's rooted in his pride in obedience and discomfort with mercy itself.

He frames his complaint as wounded fairness—he feels his faithful obedience has been overlooked. Yet beneath this anger and envy is a deeper spiritual problem: he sees his relationship with the father in transactional terms. Obedience, for him, is a currency, something owed reward or acknowledgment, rather than an expression of genuine love or familial bond.

Thus, when the father generously shows mercy to his lost son, it threatens the elder brother’s moral calculus. He is uncomfortable with mercy precisely because mercy defies his transactional expectations and reveals that the father's love isn’t earned by obedience alone. It exposes the elder brother’s pride in his own virtue, challenging him to confront his own lostness—a lostness masked by external conformity.

Ultimately, his problem is not simply anger or envy; it's a deeper unwillingness to accept grace, both for his brother and for himself.
