The Pharisee is condemned not because of his *actions* (he was pious), but specifically because of the **motive and attitude behind his actions.** While "false doctrine" plays a background role, the immediate condemnation in the text is **the use of virtue as self-display**, which manifests as **pride, comparison, and contempt.**

To understand the failure of the Pharisee and the spiritual success of the publican (tax collector), we must look at the text as a confrontation between two methodologies for approaching God: **Justification by Performance** versus **Justification by Mercy.**

### 1. What Exactly Condemns the Pharisee?

The KJV introduction to the parable is critical: *"he spake this parable unto certain which trusted in themselves that they were righteous."* The word "trusted" here is rooted in *ekklinxein*—to lean upon oneself.

The Pharisee is condemned not by one single factor, but by a **cluster of sins working in unison:**

*   **Self-Justification & Pride:** The text states he "trusted in themselves." His prayer is not about his relationship with God; it is about his relationship to *himself* as righteous. He is praying *with himself* ("prayed thus with himself"). His prayer is a monologue of self-applause rather than a supplication to God. He does not look to God for forgiveness; he tells God he does not need it because he has already earned credit through tithes ("I give tithes of all").
*   **Comparison & Hierarchy:** He compares himself to "other men" (a crowd) to elevate his own status. He views salvation as a competition where he is the winner.
*   **Contempt:** He explicitly despises the publican. Yet, strangely, in his prayer he uses the publican as a prop: *"even as this publican."* By pointing out the publican's sin, the Pharisee essentially puts himself on a pedestal, thinking, "Because *you* are dirty, I am clean." This is a reversal of truth: God does not forgive *unchaste* people in exchange for *virtuous* behavior. The Pharisee proves himself right only by making himself right relative to another sinner.

**Verdict:** If forced to choose from your options, the root is **the use of virtue as self-display.** He used religious works (fasting, tithing, piety) not to draw near to God's Holy Presence, but to draw near to self-importance. He treated virtue as a currency for value, rather than a means of grace.

### 2. Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?

The tax collector (publican) was despised for often collaborating with Rome and being corrupt. However, this parable establishes that spiritual standing is determined by one's orientation to God's grace, not by historical reality.

The tax collector’s posture is truer because it is **honest before the reality of the Divine Judgment.**

**A. The Physical Posture ("Standing Afar Off")**
*   **Theology of Shame:** He distances himself because he acknowledges he is "unclean." He understands that he does not belong in the temple. By "looking down" rather than looking up, he acknowledges the gap between his sin and God's holiness.
*   **Contrast:** The Pharisee stood *in* the corner (closer to the crowd and the temple court), asserting his presence. The Tax Collector stood *afar off*, acknowledging his unworthiness. Spiritually, the distance from the altar is often the closest approach to truth.

**B. The Bedroom "Gestures" ("Smote upon his breast")**
*   **Physical Grief:** In antiquity, striking one's chest was a sign of intense remorse and repentance. It was a physical admission of guilt. This is not a pose for an audience; it is an internal sorrow made external. The Pharisee's data point was his calendar (I fast twice); the tax collector's data point was his own stomach/chest (My heart is broken).

**C. The Verbal Posture ("Be merciful to me a sinner")**
*   **Confession of Need:** This is the core difference. The Pharisee thanked God for grants he had already received (or for what he did not lack). The tax collector prayed for a mercy he did not yet deserve. He did not claim innocence. He claimed **God's standard (mercy) as a necessary resource.**
*   **The Request:** He did not ask God to look down on him; he asked God to *look up* to him as a friend to sinners. He did not measure God's standard of holiness against the Pharisee's standard of *good behavior*.

### Conclusion: The Difference Between Servitude and Receiving

The Pharisee treats God like a **Judge** in which he is seeking to show up for the verdict and win the case with his merchandise. He is trading merit for merit-neutral goods.

The tax collector treats God like a **Healer** to whom he is seeking to be cured. He has no claim on the diagnosis of the disease.

**We are condemned as Pharisees when:**
1.  Our prayer is a resume of our good works.
2.  We "trust in ourselves" to do well, rather than trusting God to be good.
3.  We compare our faithfulness with one another to elevate ourselves.

**We approach God as the Publican when:**
1.  We admit we are needy ("I am a sinner").
2.  We leave our self-advocacy at the door.
3.  We accept that "mercy" (the God-given love that saves) is the only thing we are owed; we receive, rather than demand, it.

Therefore, the tax collector is "justified" (declared righteous) because he was the only one who actually "justified" God by accepting His grace as grace. He did not try to earn a pardon; he simply asked for one—thereby showing true trust.
