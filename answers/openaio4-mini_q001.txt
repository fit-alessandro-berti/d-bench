In these two verses Cain is addressed not as a helpless victim of some inscrutable “force,” but as a free moral agent who has before him two clear options.  The figure of speech is striking:

  •  “Sin lieth at the door” (v. 7) – literally “crouches” or “creeps,” like a wild animal waiting to spring.  
  •  “Unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him” – sin is personified (even gendered, in Hebrew) as an adversary whose “desire” is to master you, but whom you are commanded to master in turn.

What kind of “evil” is this?  It is not

  – An impersonal flaw in creation (thereby unavoidable),  
  – Mere habit (something you’ve already done so often you can’t stop),  
  – Some excuse for “I couldn’t help myself.”  

Rather, it is a deliberate, lurking temptation—an externalized “evil inclination” at your door—yet one which you are fully capable of resisting.  The text insists on Cain’s personal responsibility:

  1. “If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?” (v. 7)  Your standing with God depends on your choice.  
  2. “If thou doest not well, sin lies at the door.”  Sin waits to pounce, but only if you give way.  
  3. “Thou shalt rule over him.”  You are commanded both to recognize sin’s power and to exercise dominion over it.

In short, Genesis 4:6–7 paints evil as a predatory temptation—a stalking beast at the threshold—but one that does not enslave you unless you decide to let it in.  Moral agency here is robust: Cain (and by extension every human) is held fully responsible for choosing to do right and for “ruling” over the sin that would otherwise rule him.
