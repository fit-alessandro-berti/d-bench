This passage from the Gospel of Matthew draws a direct connection between a person's speech and the moral character of their heart. It suggests that our words are an overflow or outpouring of what is stored up inside us morally and spiritually.

There are a few key insights we can glean:

1. Words as symptoms - Jesus implies that evil speech is a symptom or manifestation of an underlying evil nature, just as good words reflect inner goodness. In this sense, our words are like vital signs that provide a window into the health of our soul. 

2. Words as revelations - The passage indicates that our speech reveals and exposes the "abundance" or contents of our heart. What we say uncovers and displays what is hidden within - whether good or evil. We cannot conceal our true moral stockpiles, because they will eventually be disclosed by our words.

3. Words as choices - At the same time, the passage suggests we have agency in what we speak. The rhetorical question "how can ye, being evil, speak good things?" implies that aligning our speech with goodness is difficult when evil resides in the heart. Yet the possibility of speaking rightly is still assumed, even for the "evil man." This hints that we can to some degree choose our words, even if our natural inclination is toward evil.

So in summary, the passage presents our words as spiritually symptomatic, revealing of our inner morality, and to some degree chosen - though heavily influenced by the state of our heart. It calls us to attend not just to our speech itself, but to the seat of our speech, letting the transforming work of God renovate us from the inside out. Only then will the "treasure" we produce with our lips consistently reflect the goodness of Christ.
