**Step‑by‑step analysis of Isaiah 55:6‑7**

| Element | Biblical wording | Typical interpretation |
|---------|------------------|-----------------------|
| **“Seek ye the Lord while he may be found”** | *Seek* (Hebrew **רָדַע**, *radah*) | An active, purposeful effort to encounter God while the opportunity is still open. |
| **“Call ye upon him while he is near”** | *Call* (Hebrew **קָרָא**, *qara*) | Invoking God’s presence, asking for help, acknowledging dependence. |
| **“Let the wicked forsake his **way**”** | **way** (*derech*), figurative of habitual conduct | The outer, observable pattern of life that expresses sin (e.g., unrighteous deeds, relationships, lifestyle). |
| **“…and the unrighteous man his **thoughts**”** | **thoughts** (*miṣṣaw*), inner mental attitudes | The subconscious, reflective, or imaginative orientation that shapes motives, desires, and intentions. |
| **“…and let him return unto the Lord”** | *return* (Hebrew **שׁוּב**, *shuv*) | A turn‑around: both from external actions and from the inner perspective that sustains those actions. |
| **“…and he will have mercy upon him; and to our God, for he will abundantly pardon.”** | Mercy and abundant pardon | The promised divine response to such sincere repentance. |

---

### Why both **way** and **thoughts** are called for

1. **Repentance is holistic**  
   - The Hebrew term *way* points to *behavior* that is visible to others and to God.  
   - *Thoughts* refer to *mind‑sets* that are hidden but decisive: they determine why a person acts as he does.  
   - God, however, sees both the act and the inner motive (Psalm 139:23‑24). Thus, true repentance must address both spheres.

2. **Correlation of inner and outer**  
   - If a person only ceases sinful deeds while still harboring the prideful or rebellious mindset, the change is superficial.  
   - Conversely, merely “thinking” righteously without changing one’s lifestyle leaves the heart still in rebellion (James 2:14‑26).  

3. **Scriptural parallelism**  
   - Isaiah 1:16‑20 likewise commands: *“Wash you, make you clean; put away the evil of your doings from before Mine eyes; cease to do evil … learn to do well.”*  
   - The pattern “stop the outward act **and** renew the inward thought” underscores that restoration of relationship with God requires *both* the cessation of the corrupt *way* and the conversion of the corrupt *thoughts*.

---

### Implications for **outward conduct**, **inward imagination**, and **mercy**

| Dimension | What the verse demands | Why it matters for mercy |
|-----------|-----------------------|--------------------------|
| **Outward conduct (way)** | **Forsake the wicked path** → cease specific sinful actions, reorient daily life toward righteousness. | God’s mercy is expressed through the removal of judgment; a changed outward life demonstrates that the sinner is sincere and is no longer a threat to divine holiness. |
| **Inward imagination (thoughts)** | **Renew the inner mindset** → let pride, resentments, idols, and self‑centered fantasies be replaced with humility, love, and trust in God. | Mercy reaches the heart; when the inner imagination aligns with God’s will, the sinner no longer merely *pretends* to be faithful but *believes* it, which is required for the abundant pardon promised. |
| **Possibility of mercy** | **Both must be renewed** → a partial repentance (only deeds or only thoughts) is insufficient; the *combined* turning results in divine mercy. | The promise “he will have mercy upon him; … he will abundantly pardon” hinges on **complete repentance**. The abundant nature of the pardon implies that God’s grace exceeds any measure of past transgression because the sinner has fully turned away—both outwardly and inwardly. |

**Key theological points**

1. **Divine omniscience** – God knows motives; thus, outward reforms without inner purification are incomplete (cf. Jeremiah 17:9).  
2. **Transformation of purpose** – The Hebrew verb *shuv* (“return”) literally means “to turn back,” implying a *reorientation* of life’s purpose toward God, not merely a behavioral checklist.  
3. **Mercy as restoration** – “Mercy” (חֶסֶד, *hesed*) in the OT is God’s faithful, covenant‑keeping love. When a sinner returns both in deed and thought, God’s covenant love is re‑established, leading to forgiveness that exceeds the offense.  
4. **Practical application** – For a believer today, repentance means:  
   - **Identifying concrete sins** (the “way”) and ceasing them.  
   - **Examining hidden attitudes** (the “thoughts”) such as bitterness, lustful fantasies, or prideful self‑justification, and asking the Holy Spirit to renew them (cf. 2 Cor 10:5).  

---

### Conclusion

Isaiah 55:6‑7 teaches that genuine repentance must encompass **both** the external “way” of a person’s life **and** the internal “thoughts” that shape that life. By forsaking both, the sinner demonstrates sincere turning to God, which in turn makes God’s mercy accessible and abundant. The implication is that mercy is not granted on the basis of superficial behavior alone; it is granted to those who present a **holistically renewed heart and life**, allowing God’s covenant love to flow freely and fully forgive.
