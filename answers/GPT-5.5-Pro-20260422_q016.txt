The elder brother’s deepest problem is **not simply anger, envy, or a wounded sense of fairness**. Those are real, but they are symptoms. His deepest problem is that **he is alienated from the father’s heart while remaining physically near the father’s house**.

He has the posture of a servant, not a son.

When he says, “Lo, these many years do I serve thee,” his obedience sounds like labor for wages. He has stayed home, but inwardly he has been keeping accounts. His complaint is not, “I missed my brother,” or even, “You were dishonored.” It is, “I was not rewarded as I deserved.” That reveals a relationship built around **merit, comparison, and entitlement**.

So the options fit like layers:

| Possible problem          | How it appears                                       | Why it is not the deepest layer                                                                           |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Anger**                 | “He was angry, and would not go in”                  | Anger is the surface reaction.                                                                            |
| **Envy**                  | “Thou never gavest me a kid”                         | He resents another’s joy as a loss to himself.                                                            |
| **Wounded fairness**      | “This thy son… devoured thy living…”                 | He is not entirely wrong about the younger son’s guilt, but he turns justice into a weapon against mercy. |
| **Pride in obedience**    | “Neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment” | His obedience has become self-justifying rather than loving.                                              |
| **Discomfort with mercy** | He refuses to celebrate restoration                  | This is close to the heart of it, but even this comes from something deeper.                              |

The deepest issue is that **he cannot rejoice in grace because his identity depends on deserving more than the one who receives grace**.

That is why his language is so revealing. He says, “this thy son,” not “my brother.” The father gently corrects him: “this thy brother was dead, and is alive again; and was lost, and is found.” The elder brother has not only distanced himself from the younger son; he has distanced himself from the father’s joy.

The father’s answer also exposes the false fairness claim: “Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine.” The elder brother has not actually been robbed. His inheritance is secure. The celebration of mercy toward the younger son does not diminish the father’s love for him. But he experiences another’s restoration as an insult because he thinks in terms of **rank, reward, and desert**, not communion.

So, in one sentence:

**The elder brother’s deepest problem is self-righteous sonship without shared love: he obeys the father’s commands but does not share the father’s mercy, and therefore he cannot enter the father’s joy.**

He is “lost” too, but in a different way. The younger brother was lost in rebellion far away; the elder brother is lost in resentment at home.
