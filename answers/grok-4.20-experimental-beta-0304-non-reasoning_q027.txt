**The Pharisee is condemned primarily by prideful self-righteousness, which manifests as contempt for others and the use of genuine virtues as a tool for self-display and self-justification before God.**

### What Exactly Condemns the Pharisee?

The text itself diagnoses the audience and the problem at the outset: Jesus tells the parable to “certain which **trusted in themselves that they were righteous**, and **despised others**” (v. 9). That is the root. Everything else flows from it.

- **Pride / self-trust** is the core sin. The Pharisee does not approach God as a debtor; he approaches as a creditor presenting his invoice. His prayer is not really addressed to God (“prayed thus **with himself**” — the Greek *pros heauton* can carry the sense of “to himself” or “regarding himself”). He uses the language of thanksgiving (“God, I thank thee…”) but the content is self-congratulation. He is performing righteousness for his own approval and for the approval of any bystanders.

- **Contempt** is the immediate fruit of that pride. He does not simply note moral differences; he defines himself over against “other men” and specifically “this publican.” The tax collector becomes a useful negative foil that makes the Pharisee’s own résumé look better. This is the “despising others” Jesus named in the introduction. It is not mere comparison; it is relational superiority that flows from a heart that has already declared itself righteous.

- **Use of virtue as self-display**. The Pharisee’s listed works are not imaginary. Fasting twice a week (beyond the required once) and tithing everything (even herbs) were real expressions of Jewish piety. The problem is not that he did them, but *why* and *how* he presents them. He weaponizes them to justify himself and to elevate himself above the sinner standing nearby. Virtue has been turned into spiritual capital with which he buys the right to look down on his neighbor and to presume upon God. This is the opposite of the biblical pattern in which good works are the fruit of a already-grateful, already-humble heart (cf. Eph 2:8–10; Tit 3:5–7).

False doctrine is not the main issue here. The Pharisee’s theology of merit and reward was certainly defective, but the parable does not focus on doctrinal error. Jesus does not correct the content of the Pharisee’s beliefs about fasting or tithing; he exposes the heart that uses those beliefs to avoid the need for mercy. Even correct doctrine, when filtered through pride, becomes fuel for self-righteousness.

Comparison itself is not automatically sinful; the tax collector is implicitly aware of his own sinfulness in contrast to a holy God. The sin lies in the *direction* and *purpose* of the comparison: the Pharisee compares himself to a worse sinner in order to feel secure, rather than comparing himself to God’s holiness and feeling his own bankruptcy.

### Why Is the Tax Collector’s Posture Treated as Spiritually Truer?

The tax collector’s actions and prayer embody the opposite spirit:

- **Distance and bodily humility**: He stands “afar off,” will not even lift his eyes to heaven, beats his breast. These are not theatrical tricks; they are the instinctive gestures of a man who feels the weight of his guilt before a holy God. He knows he has no right to approach.

- **Simple, honest plea**: “God be merciful to me a sinner” (literally, “the sinner” — he sees himself as *the* representative sinner). He offers no résumé, no comparative excuses, no promises of future improvement in the moment. He appeals only to God’s mercy (*hilasthēti* — the same root as the mercy seat in the tabernacle). This is the language of the Psalms and of the sacrificial system: the only hope for a guilty man is propitiation.

Jesus’ verdict is stark: the tax collector “went down to his house **justified** rather than the other.” The passive verb implies God is the one who justifies. The man who claimed no righteousness received it as a gift; the man who claimed much righteousness left with none. This is the classic Pauline and Protestant point (see Rom 3:20; 4:5; 10:3; Phil 3:9) already present in Jesus’ own teaching: God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble (Prov 3:34; Jas 4:6; 1 Pet 5:5).

The tax collector’s posture is truer because it corresponds to reality. Before an infinitely holy God, all human virtue is insufficient for justification. The only fitting response is contrition and dependence. The Pharisee’s posture denies that reality and therefore receives no mercy. The parable is not anti-works or anti-piety; it is anti-*self-righteous* works and piety. Genuine virtue offered in humility and gratitude is beautiful; the same virtue offered as a bribe or a boast is repulsive.

In short: the Pharisee is condemned because he used the good gifts of the law to insulate himself from the need for the gospel. The tax collector is justified because he refused that insulation and threw himself on mercy alone. The story is therefore both a warning to the religiously serious and an encouragement to the morally failing: the door into justification is not a résumé but a humble plea for mercy.
