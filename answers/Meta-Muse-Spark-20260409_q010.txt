**Philippians 2:5–8** is praising a kind of power that looks like the opposite of power: *self-emptying power*. Paul calls it *kenosis* — literally "emptying." 

### What kind of power is being praised here?

It’s not power-as-control, but **power-over-self**. 

- **Power to lay down status**: Christ “being in the form of God” had the right to every privilege of deity. The passage praises the power to *not grasp* what was rightfully His. Grasping is what most of us do with power. He had the strength to release it.
- **Power to choose descent**: “Made himself of no reputation” — the Greek *ekenōsen* means He emptied Himself. That takes more strength than climbing. Anyone can be driven by ambition. It takes a deeper agency to step down on purpose.
- **Power to serve without losing identity**: He “took the form of a servant” but never stopped being who He was. That’s command of self, not erasure of self. He could enter weakness without being defined by it.
- **Power to obey through cost**: “Obedient unto death, even the death of the cross.” Roman crucifixion was designed for maximum shame. The passage praises power that can absorb humiliation and still remain true to its mission.

So the text exalts **restrained, directed power** — power so secure it doesn’t need to advertise itself, so sovereign it can choose a cross.

### Why humility joined to greatness feels more unsettling than open domination

Open domination is legible. We know the rules: the strong take, the weak submit. It fits our instincts about hierarchy. But when greatness kneels, it scrambles the categories we use to feel safe.

1. **It removes our excuses**: With a tyrant, I can say “I’d be good if I had power, but I don’t.” When Christ, who *did* have ultimate power, uses it to wash feet and die for enemies, that argument dies. It suggests real strength looks like sacrifice. That implicates me.
2. **It can’t be managed or flattered**: You can negotiate with a bully. You can rebel against a tyrant or appeal to his ego. But how do you coerce someone who’s already given up His rights? How do you shame someone who chose the cross? Humility joined to greatness is *invulnerable* in a way force isn’t. And invulnerability without aggression is uncanny.
3. **It demands a response from us**: Domination demands submission. Kenosis invites imitation: “Let this mind be in you.” It’s not just “Christ was humble.” It’s “so you be too.” That’s unsettling because it calls our own grasping — for status, reputation, being right — into question. A dictator wants your compliance. Christ’s example wants your character.
4. **It reveals our definition of power as small**: We’re used to power that takes, controls, and protects itself. Philippians 2 shows power that gives, yields, and dies to give life. If that’s real power, then most of what we call power is just fear with better PR. That’s a disruptive thought.

**C.S. Lewis** put it like this: “The real problem of Christianity is not that it is too weak, but that it is too strong.” A God who dominates is just a bigger Caesar. A God who kneels and bleeds breaks the game entirely. 

That’s why the early world called the cross “foolishness” and a “stumbling block.” Domination we understand. A crucified Lord overturns the board.
