Paul is not here laying down a legalistic “if–then” rule, but sketching the *attitude* with which a truly spiritual person (one walking in the Spirit) may help a brother or sister who has stumbled.  Three elements are held in delicate balance:

  1. Restore (καταρτίζετε).  
     – The goal is healing and reintegration into the body, not shame or expulsion.  
     – You come alongside, pick up the fallen, set the joint “back in its socket,” so to speak.

  2. In the spirit of meekness (ἐν πνεύματι πραΰτητος).  
     – Meekness is not weakness but gentleness under control.  
     – You do not thunder condemnation from above; you approach with quiet strength and kindness, owning that you too are frail.

  3. Considering thyself (προσεχῶς ἑαυτῷ), lest thou also be tempted.  
     – A constant self–examination: “I too could fall.”  
     – This keeps pride and judgmentalism at bay, and stirs compassion rather than self–righteousness.

Why bind these three together?  Because any one of them alone can run off the rails:

  • If you simply *restore* without *meekness*, you become a bulldozer—putting people “back in line” by force.  
  • If you are meek without aiming to *restore*, you end up a sentimental bystander who never intervenes.  
  • If you restore meekly but never *consider yourself*, you slip into hypocrisy—calling out sins in others that you secretly harbor yourself.

In brief, the healthy posture for correction in Christ’s body is a three–fold grace:  
  – A *restorative* aim,  
  – a *meek* carriage, and  
  – a *humble* self–watchfulness.  
Together they ensure that church discipline is an act of loving grace, not of moral superiority.
