1.  The place of 4:31-32 in the paragraph  

   In 4:25-32 Paul is giving a series of “put off / put on” instructions that flow out of the larger command to “put off the old man … and put on the new” (4:22-24).  
   • v.25  stop lying → speak truth  
   • v.26-27  handle anger rightly  
   • v.28  stop stealing → work and share  
   • v.29-30  stop rotten talk → speak what builds up  
   • v.31-32  sweep away destructive attitudes → clothe yourselves with kindness and forgiveness  

   Verses 31-32 are therefore a summary pair: the old way of relating is gathered up in six nouns headed by “bitterness”; the new way of relating is gathered up in three nouns headed by “kindness,” crowned by the command to forgive “as God in Christ forgave you.”

2.  What “bitterness” means  

   Greek πικρία (pikria) originally meant “a sharp, biting taste.” Figuratively it came to mean an inner sourness— rancor, resentful harshness. The same word is used:  
   • Acts 8:23  “gall of bitterness” (a poison)  
   • Heb 12:15  “a root of bitterness” that can “defile many”  
   • Col 3:19  husbands are told not to “be bitter” toward their wives  

   It is therefore more than a momentary feeling; it is a settled, continuing state of resentment that colours one’s outlook and inevitably seeps out in words and actions.

3.  The logic of the list in v.31  

   Paul moves from the most interior to the most exterior:  
   • bitterness – the deep-seated attitude  
   • wrath (θυμός) – explosive flare-ups  
   • anger (ὀργή) – simmering indignation  
   • clamour (κραυγή) – shouting, verbal outbursts  
   • evil speaking (βλασφημία) – slander, abusive talk  
   • with all malice (κακία) – ill-will that lies behind the whole sequence  

   Bitterness is placed first because it is the root from which the others grow; malice is placed last as the catch-all term.

4.  Why forgiveness is its opposite  

   When a wrong has been done, two basic responses are open:  
   a. Keep the score, rehearse the hurt, allow resent­ment to set like concrete (bitterness).  
   b. Release the debt, cancel the score, treat the offender with the grace God has shown you (forgiveness).  

   The verb Paul uses for “forgive” (χαρίζομαι, charizomai) is built on the noun χάρις, “grace, an undeserved gift.” Forgiveness, then, is an act of grace that consciously chooses to give up the claim of retribution; bitterness is the refusal to do so. Because they are mutually exclusive responses to the same provocation, Paul places them directly over against each other.

5.  Feeling, habit, or contagion?  

   • Feeling: bitterness certainly involves emotion, but the aorist passive imperative ἀρθήτω (“let it be taken up/removed”) treats it as something the believer is responsible to discard, not merely to feel or not feel.  
   • Habit/posture: the use of abstract nouns (rather than verbs like “be bitter”) points to continuing dispositions, habitual ways of reacting.  
   • Contagion: Hebrews 12:15 echoes the Old-Testament picture of “a root of bitterness” whose “shoots” defile many; resentful attitudes seep into a community and poison it.  

   Hence bitterness is best understood as a chosen, persistent posture that starts in the heart, expresses itself in behaviour, and infects relationships unless it is decisively “lifted away.” The only antidote Paul offers is the habit of forgiveness grounded in the gospel: “even as God in Christ forgave you.”

6.  Summary answer  

   Bitterness in Ephesians 4:31 is the deep-seated resentful stance that springs from withheld forgiveness and produces a cascade of destructive emotions and words. Paul counters it with the deliberate, grace-based choice to forgive, because forgiveness uproots bitterness at its source. The text therefore treats bitterness chiefly as a habitual posture—one that is personally chosen yet socially contagious—and commands believers to replace it with the Christ-like habit of forgiving grace.
