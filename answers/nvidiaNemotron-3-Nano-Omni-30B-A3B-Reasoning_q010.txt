**Answer:**

The passage praises **the power of self‑emptying (kenotic) humility** — a strength that does not rely on outward authority, status, or coercive force, but on voluntarily relinquishing privilege and taking on the role of a servant. This kind of power is “great” because it is rooted in the divine nature (the “form of God”) yet chooses to be lowly, obedient, and even sacrificial.

### Why humility‑joined‑greatness feels more unsettling than open domination

| Aspect | Open Domination | Humble‑Greatness (Kenosis) |
|--------|----------------|----------------------------|
| **Visibility of power** | Clearly displayed through authority, control, or force; the source of power is obvious. | Hidden; the individual relinquishes the “form of God” and appears as an ordinary servant. The source of greatness is not obvious. |
| **Moral expectation** | Often expected or even demanded; people anticipate the dominant to exercise power. | Subverts expectations; the dominant *chooses* to be lowly, which can feel paradoxical and thus disconcerting. |
| **Psychological impact** | Can create resentment, fear, or rebellion because the imbalance is evident. | Generates awe, discomfort, or even suspicion because it challenges the usual calculus of power (the more you have, the more you can dominate). |
| **Theological implication** | Suggests that power is a right to be exercised; may imply inequality. | Points to a paradoxical strength: true greatness is measured by self‑sacrifice, not by control. This re‑defines what “power” means. |
| **Emotional response** | May inspire obedience, compliance, or resistance. | Can evoke a deeper, more unsettling mix of admiration, humility, and existential unease — because it asks the observer to reconsider the nature of greatness itself. |

In short, **the power praised in Philippians 2 is not the power to dominate, but the power to *be* — to hold divine status while willingly becoming nothing.** This reversal of the usual power dynamic makes humility joined to greatness feel more unsettling: it forces us to confront the idea that true greatness may be expressed precisely by *letting go* rather than by *holding on*.
