While the elder brother’s speech is a tangled web of anger, envy, and a sense of wounded fairness, his **deepest problem** is not merely an emotional reaction, but a fundamental misunderstanding of his relationship with his father. 

Ultimately, his deepest problem is **a transactional worldview rooted in prideful obedience, which results in a profound discomfort with mercy and relational alienation.** 

To understand why, we must look at how the symptoms (anger, envy, wounded fairness) grow out of this deeper root.

### 1. The Symptoms: Wounded Fairness and Envy
The elder brother’s immediate reaction is a sense of mathematical injustice: *“These many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment: and yet thou never gavest me a kid.”* 
He is operating on a strict system of merit. In his mind, good behavior should be rewarded proportionally, and bad behavior should be punished. His envy of the "fatted calf" is not just about the meat; it is about the perceived violation of the cosmic rulebook he has spent his life following. His anger is the natural emotional byproduct of a system that he believes has failed him.

### 2. The Root: Pride in Obedience and a Transactional Mindset
The most revealing phrase in the elder brother's dialogue is: *"These many years do I **serve** thee."* The Greek word used here (*douleuō*) literally means to work as a slave or bond-servant. 

This exposes his deepest spiritual blindness: **He does not view himself as a son; he views himself as an employee.** He has reduced his relationship with his father to a cold, transactional contract. His obedience is not born out of love, joy, or a desire to know his father, but out of a desire to secure his own standing and accrue moral capital. His pride in his flawless record (“neither transgressed I at any time”) is a shield he uses to demand payment. Because he believes he has *earned* his place, he feels entitled to dictate the father's actions.

### 3. The Ultimate Fruit: Discomfort with Mercy and Relational Alienation
The tragedy of the elder brother’s condition is revealed in how he speaks about his sibling. He does not say, "As soon as *my brother* came." He says, "As soon as *this thy son* was come." 

In a moment of deep alienation, he severs the family tie. He cannot tolerate mercy because mercy invalidates the entire transactional economy he has built his life upon. If the father can give unmerited favor to a rule-breaker, then the elder brother’s lifetime of rule-keeping is stripped of its superior status. Mercy is offensive to the moralist because it cannot be earned, controlled, or leveraged.

### The Father’s Response: The Diagnosis
The father’s gentle rebuttal diagnoses the elder brother’s condition perfectly: *"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine."*

The father reminds him of two truths the elder brother has forgotten:
1. **Relational Permanence:** "Thou art ever with me." You are not a servant who can be fired or a contractor who must constantly prove his worth. You are a son.
2. **Total Inheritance:** "All that I have is thine." The elder brother was mourning the loss of a single goat, completely blind to the fact that he already legally owned the entire estate, including the fatted calf. He was acting like a pauper while sitting on a throne.

### Conclusion
The younger brother was lost in the "far country" through irreligion and rebellion; the elder brother is lost in the "father’s house" through religion and self-righteousness. 

While his anger and wounded fairness are real, they are merely the smoke. The fire is his **prideful, transactional view of relationship**, which makes him incapable of enjoying the father’s love and utterly hostile to the extending of mercy to others. He is standing outside the party, just as his brother once sat in the pigsty, waiting for the father to come out and plead with him to simply come home and be a son.
