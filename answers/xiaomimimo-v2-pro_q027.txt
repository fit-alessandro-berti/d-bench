This is an excellent question that gets to the heart of Jesus' teaching. The parable condemns the Pharisee not for his **doctrines or practices**, but for a **fatal corruption of the heart** that turns virtue into vice. Here’s a breakdown of what exactly condemns him and why the tax collector’s posture is "truer":

### What Condemns the Pharisee:

1.  **Pride as the Root Sin:** This is the core. His prayer is a monologue of self-congratulation. He "trusted in themselves that they were righteous" (v.9). His thankfulness is not directed toward God's grace enabling his obedience, but toward his own innate superiority. He sees his righteousness as a personal achievement, not a gift.

2.  **Contemptuous Comparison:** This flows directly from pride. He doesn't just list his virtues; he defines himself *against others*—specifically "other men" and "this publican." His spiritual identity is built on being "not like" those he deems inferior. This comparison is the engine of his self-justification.

3.  **The Use of Virtue as Self-Display:** His virtues (fasting, tithing, avoiding gross sins) are real and commendable under the Law. However, in his heart, they have become tools for **self-display** and **social ranking**. He performs them to be seen (by God and others) as righteous, not out of love for God or neighbor. His virtue is transactional, a means to establish his own status.

4.  **A Fundamental Misunderstanding of Righteousness:** At its deepest level, he is condemned for a **false doctrine of justification**. He believes righteousness comes from:
    *   **External Performance:** Fasting, tithing.
    *   **Negative Avoidance:** "I am not like..."
    *   **Social Distinction:** Separating himself from "sinners."
    He approaches God on the basis of his own moral ledger, not on the basis of need or mercy.

**In summary:** The Pharisee is condemned because his heart has inverted the purpose of religion. Instead of drawing him closer to God in humility and dependence, his practice of the Law has driven him into a spiritual isolation chamber of self-worship and disdain for others. His prayer reveals a soul that believes it has **no need**.

### Why the Tax Collector’s Posture is Spiritually Truer:

1.  **Honest Self-Awareness:** He "would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven." This posture physically embodies his spiritual reality: he knows he has no right to approach God on his own merits. He is not performing; he is confronting the raw truth of his condition.

2.  **The Cry for Mercy:** "God be merciful to me a sinner." This is the essence of saving faith.
    *   **"God..."** – He directs his plea to the right source.
    *   **"Be merciful..."** – He appeals to God's character (grace, *hesed*), not his own record. He seeks propitiation, not praise.
    *   **"to me, a sinner."** – He names his identity truthfully. He does not say "a sinner *like that Pharisee over there*." He owns his guilt personally and absolutely.

3.  **Dependence, Not Achievement:** His justification comes from what he *receives* (mercy), not what he *achieves* (virtue). He approaches God as a beggar, empty-handed. This aligns with the foundational biblical principle that salvation is "by grace... not by works" (Ephesians 2:8-9). He possesses the one thing that opens the door to God: a broken and contrite spirit (Psalm 51:17).

### The Core Contrast:

*   **The Pharisee** says, **"I thank you that I am not..."** His righteousness is comparative and exclusionary.
*   **The Tax Collector** says, **"Be merciful to me..."** His hope is restorative and inclusive of all who share his need.

**Conclusion:** The Pharisee is condemned for a **pride that masquerades as piety**, turning good works into a monument to self. The tax collector is justified because his **humble, repentant faith** accurately perceives the human condition before a holy God: we are all dependent on mercy. His posture is "truer" because it is based on reality—the reality of human sin and the greater reality of divine grace. He goes home "justified" because he aligned himself with the truth, and "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble" (James 4:6).
