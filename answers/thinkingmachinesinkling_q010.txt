In this passage—the ancient Christ-hymn—Paul is not praising power as **domination, possession, or coercive display**. He praises a *kenotic* power: the authority of the One who is “in the form of God” and yet refuses to exploit that equality. Jesus “emptied himself” (the Greek *ekenōsen*). The power on display is therefore **self-giving love that takes the shape of service and obedience**, reaching its apex not on a throne but on a cross. It is power that operates not by grasping status (*harpagmos*) but by relinquishing privilege for the sake of others. In other words, it is the power of God revealed precisely in weakness, vulnerability, and voluntary humility.

### Why this can feel more unsettling than open domination

Open domination is at least honest about its intentions. It establishes a clear hierarchy: one commands, the other obeys or resists. Our position in relation to it is obvious—we can fear it, fight it, or flatter it, but we know what game is being played.

Humility joined to true greatness shatters that framework in several disquieting ways:

**1. It subverts the logic of power itself.**  
We instinctively believe that greatness *requires* assertion—that to be “equal with God” means to dominate the field. When the highest being refuses to clutch at that status and instead becomes a servant, the very definition of power is inverted. We are forced to ask whether our own ambitions are built on a lie.

**2. It removes our excuses for pride.**  
If Christ, being in the form of God, did not consider equality something to be seized or displayed, then our own reluctance to serve, to forgive, or to lower ourselves is exposed not as “necessary self-respect” but as vanity. Open domination lets us justify our grasping for status (“everyone is competing”); this humility indicts it.

**3. It demands imitation, not just submission.**  
Domination asks for compliance; humility asks for transformation. Paul introduces the hymn with “Let this mind be in you.” The text is not merely describing Jesus for our admiration but prescribing a pattern for our own lives. That is far more threatening than being told to bow to a tyrant, because it strikes at our ego’s need for recognition.

**4. It collapses the distance between “great” and “lowly.”**  
A dominating ruler keeps subjects at arm’s length. But here, God enters “the likeness of men,” takes on flesh, and dies a criminal’s death. This proximity is unsettling because it refuses to let us categorize the divine as safely remote and ourselves as comfortably inferior. It means the sacred is found in the servant, the slave, the crucified—and by extension, in the people we are tempted to overlook or dominate.

**5. The paradox is cognitively and emotionally destabilizing.**  
We expect omnipotence to look overwhelming. Instead, we find it washing feet and hanging on wood. The scandal of the cross is that power looks like failure. That contradiction can feel more disturbing than straightforward tyranny because it refuses to fit our categories; it forces a re-evaluation of everything we associate with strength, honor, and success.

In short, open domination threatens the body or the will, but **humility united with greatness threatens the ego’s architecture**. It reveals that true authority needs no defense, that love is stronger than coercion, and that the path “up” is actually a path “down.” That revelation is liberating—but also deeply unsettling, because it asks us to lay down the very status we have spent our lives trying to secure.
