The trust called for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is neither blind faith nor a rejection of reason, but a *humble, radical reliance on God’s wisdom and guidance*—a trust that acknowledges human limitations while affirming the supremacy of divine truth. This is best understood as **faith rooted in humility, not in intellectual dismissal**.

### 1. **Trust as Humble Self-Distrust (Not Anti-Intellectualism)**
- **Humble self-distrust** means recognizing that our understanding is finite, flawed, and often biased. We may have wisdom, insight, and experience—but we are not omniscient. As Proverbs 3:5 says, “lean not unto thine own understanding,” not because human reason is evil, but because our reasoning can be skewed by pride, sin, or incomplete information.
- This is not a call to stop using our minds but to *submit* them under God’s sovereignty. It’s like a student trusting a professor’s expertise not because the student is incapable of thought, but because the professor has trained for decades in the subject.

### 2. **Not Anti-Intellectualism**
- The passage does **not** reject human intelligence, reason, or effort. In fact, the entire book of Proverbs celebrates wisdom, discernment, and sound thinking. Proverbs is *the* wisdom literature of Scripture—precisely because it values thought, reflection, and moral judgment.
- What it rejects is **self-reliance**—placing ultimate confidence in our own judgment apart from God. If we trust *only* in our intellect, experience, or logic, we risk becoming proud and detached from divine truth.
- The contrast is between *trusting God with all your heart* while still using your mind—“in all thy ways acknowledge him”—which implies a *continual, intentional alignment* of thought and action with God’s will, not a surrender of thought.

### 3. **The Balance: Wisdom as a Gift from God**
- In Proverbs 2:6, we read, "For the Lord gives wisdom; from his mouth come knowledge and understanding." True wisdom is not merely human cleverness but divine insight. So, to “acknowledge him” in all ways means seeking His wisdom through prayer, Scripture, and the guidance of the Spirit.
- The result—“he shall direct thy paths”—is not magical or passive. It assumes faithful engagement: you use your mind, make decisions, take action—but you do so with the posture of submission, asking, “Is this in line with God’s character and purposes?”

### Summary:
The trust in Proverbs 3:5–6 is **a humble, trusting dependence on God**—not a rejection of intellect, but a recognition of its limits. It calls for:
- **Self-distrust** regarding our own wisdom (not pride in our understanding),
- **Faith in God’s supreme wisdom** (not abandonment of thought),
- And **active discernment**—using our minds to “acknowledge him” in every decision.

In short: *Trust God with all your heart*—not because your heart is better than your mind, but because your mind is best when aligned with the One who perfectly knows and loves you. This is wisdom, not anti-intellectualism.
