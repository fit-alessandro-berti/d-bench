# Treasure as Magnet, Not Mirror

The most common reading of the closing line is **diagnostic**: your treasure *reveals* where your heart already is. On this view, Jesus is handing you a mirror — audit your spending and you'll discover your true loves. It's a useful reading, but it's the weaker one, and the grammar of the passage pushes past it.

## The Case for *Training*

Three details pull toward the stronger claim — that treasure *trains* the heart:

1. **The imperative mood.** The passage opens with commands: "Lay not up... but lay up." If treasure merely diagnosed the heart, the logical response would be introspection, not action. Jesus commands a *behavioral change* in where you invest, which only makes sense if that investment has causal power over the heart. He's prescribing a discipline, not just describing a symptom.

2. **The future tense.** "There *will* your heart be also." This isn't "there your heart *is*" (a present-tense diagnosis). The Greek ἔσται (*estai*) is future. Your heart isn't merely located where your treasure already is — your heart **will arrive** where your treasure has gone ahead of it. Treasure is the scout; the heart is the army that follows.

3. **The structural context.** The verses immediately following (6:22–24) move from treasure to the *eye* (attention) to the *master* (love/allegiance). The sermon itself traces the chain: what you invest in captures your gaze, and what captures your gaze eventually commands your loyalty. You cannot serve two masters.

The passage, then, describes a **feedback loop with a directional bias**: you place treasure → your attention follows → your heart settles there → you place more treasure. The command to invest in heaven is not a demand to love heaven first and then invest accordingly. It's the instruction to *invest* in heaven so that your heart will learn to love it.

## How Possession, Attention, and Love Bind Together

The binding mechanism is **anxiety and maintenance**.

Earthly treasure requires *vigilance* — moth, rust, thieves. You must watch it, manage it, protect it, check on it. This anxious attention is the bridge between possession and love. You don't just *own* earthly treasure; you *tend* it, and tending is a form of devotion. Every hour spent guarding your hoard is an hour your heart spends in the company of that hoard, and the heart grows fond of the company it keeps.

Heavenly treasure, by contrast, is described precisely by its **freedom from maintenance**: incorruptible, unstealable. It demands no anxious vigilance. The love it produces is therefore *uncoerced* — it doesn't bind your heart through the fear of loss.

So the chain runs:

| | Earthly | Heavenly |
|---|---|---|
| **Possession** | Fragile, degradable | Incorruptible |
| **Attention** | Anxious, defensive | Free, unburdened |
| **Love** | Bound by fear of loss | Uncoerced |

The deepest insight here may be that we tend to think of possession as the *servant* of love — we acquire things because we already desire them. Jesus reverses this: possession is the *architect* of love. What you acquire and maintain will, over time, restructure what you desire. The heart doesn't lead the treasure. The treasure *leads the heart*.

And that is exactly why the passage is a command rather than an observation.
