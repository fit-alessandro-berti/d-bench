Great question.  
Enemy-love is hard to take seriously because it sounds, at first hearing, either **naive** or **morally offensive** (“Am I supposed to be nice to evil?”).

A few reasons it feels impossible:

1. **It clashes with our justice instinct.**  
   We want wrong to be answered proportionally. “Love your enemy” can sound like “ignore evil.”

2. **We confuse love with liking or trust.**  
   Jesus is not commanding warm feelings or removal of boundaries. Biblical love is willing the other’s good, even while resisting their wrongdoing.

3. **Hate feels like strength.**  
   Anger gives energy and identity. Letting go of vengeance can feel like surrender, when in fact it often requires more discipline.

4. **We fear enabling abuse.**  
   A valid concern. Enemy-love is not passivity; it can include confrontation, law, protection of victims, and truth-telling.

---

So is it sentimental, strategic, or deeper moral strength?

- **Not sentimental** (at least in Jesus’ sense).  
  It is not emotional softness or denial of harm.

- **Sometimes strategic** (it can de-escalate conflict, break cycles of retaliation).  
  But if done only for advantage, it becomes manipulation.

- **Primarily a deeper moral strength.**  
  In Matthew 5:45, the model is God’s own character: sun and rain on just and unjust. Enemy-love means refusing to let another person’s evil determine your moral posture. It is strength under moral control.

In short: Jesus’ command is radical not because it is unrealistic, but because it demands a higher kind of power—**the power to seek justice without becoming hatred’s mirror image**.
