The command to love one’s enemies is widely considered the most radical and difficult ethical instruction in the Christian tradition. It challenges the deepest instincts of human survival and social cohesion. To answer your question, we must look at why this command triggers resistance, and then evaluate whether it is sentimental, strategic, or a form of strength.

### 1. Why is enemy-love so difficult to take seriously?

There are three primary barriers that make this teaching feel unrealistic or impossible:

*   **The Instinct of Reciprocity:** Human psychology is wired for fairness. We operate on a "tit-for-tat" basis: kindness for kindness, harm for harm. To love an enemy feels like a violation of justice. It feels like letting the offender "get away with it" without paying the moral debt they owe.
*   **Confusion of Love with Affection:** Most people define love as an emotion (*eros* or *philia*). If love is a feeling, then loving an enemy is impossible because you cannot force yourself to *like* someone who hurts you. If the command requires a feeling, it is dismissed as sentimental fantasy.
*   **Fear of Vulnerability:** Loving an enemy requires lowering one's defenses. In a world where power is often equated with the ability to retaliate, loving an enemy feels like disarming oneself in the face of a threat. It invites the fear of being exploited or destroyed.

### 2. Is it Sentimental?

**No.** To view enemy-love as sentimental is to misunderstand the nature of the love (*agape*) Jesus describes.

*   **Sentimentality is passive; this command is active.** Jesus does not say, "Feel warm toward your enemies." He lists four active verbs: *love, bless, do good, pray.* These are volitional acts of the will, not involuntary responses of the heart.
*   **Sentimentality ignores reality.** Sentimentality often ignores the evil of the enemy to maintain a "nice" feeling. Biblical enemy-love acknowledges the enemy's hostility but refuses to return it. It does not pretend the wound didn't happen; it chooses to heal the wound rather than widen it.
*   **It requires hardness, not softness.** It takes a "hard" will to override the instinct for revenge. As the theologian Dietrich Bonhoeffer noted, this love is not a soft indulgence; it is a discipline.

### 3. Is it Strategic?

**Partially, but not primarily.** There is a pragmatic value to enemy-love, but making it purely strategic undermines its moral weight.

*   **The Strategic Benefit:** History shows that non-violent resistance and enemy-love (as seen in Gandhi or Martin Luther King Jr.) can disarm aggressors and break cycles of violence. If the goal is social peace, enemy-love is a highly effective strategy.
*   **The Spiritual Trap:** If the *only* reason to love an enemy is to convert them, shame them, or de-escalate a conflict, the love becomes manipulative. It becomes a means to an end rather than an end in itself.
*   **The Textual Evidence:** Look at verse 45. The reason given is not "so that they will stop hurting you" or "so that society will be peaceful." The reason is **"That ye may be the children of your Father."** The motivation is *identity*, not *outcome*. You do it to reflect God, not to manage the enemy.

### 4. Is it a Deeper Form of Moral Strength?

**Yes.** This is the most accurate description. Enemy-love is the apex of moral strength because it requires the conquest of the ego.

*   **It requires Trust in Justice:** To not take revenge requires trusting that God is the ultimate judge. Verse 45 illustrates this with the imagery of the sun and rain. God does not need to punish the "unjust" immediately to maintain order; He sustains them. To love an enemy, you must trust that God’s justice is sufficient, freeing you from the burden of being the executioner.
*   **It breaks the Cycle of Violence:** It takes immense courage to refuse to pass on the violence you received. Hate begets hate; only love can break the chain. This requires a strength that is not reactive, but creative.
*   **It Imitates Divine Nature:** The text argues that loving the "just" is easy (even tax collectors do that). Loving the "unjust" is what makes you distinctively "children of your Father." It is a strength that allows a human to participate in the nature of God, who sustains life even in the face of rebellion.

### Conclusion

Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it asks us to surrender our right to self-defense and retribution. It is **not sentimental**, because it demands action over emotion. It is **not merely strategic**, because its primary goal is spiritual formation, not conflict resolution.

It is, ultimately, a **deeper form of moral strength**. It is the strength to absorb evil without becoming evil, to trust God's justice more than one's own, and to reflect the "common grace" of the Creator who sends rain on the just and the unjust alike. It is the hardest thing a human can do, precisely because it is the most divine thing a human can do.
