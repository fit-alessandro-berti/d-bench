Short answer: the test is that true love for God cannot exist in isolation from love for real people. If you say you love God but hate or refuse to love your visible neighbor (the brother you can see), your claim is hollow and self-deceived.

Here’s a fuller way to see it:

- Why use the visible neighbor as the test?
  - God is invisible, but he has made himself known most clearly in and through his people. John’s point is that genuine love for the invisible God will inevitably show up as tangible love for the people around you—especially fellow Christians who are “brothers.”
  - Love, in John’s language, is not mere sentiment or private feeling. It’s action—a self-giving concern that treats others as valuable because they bear God’s image and are members of the same family of faith.
  - The immediate benchmark is the visible brother: you can see him, you can act toward him. If you won’t, you are not truly loving the God you say you know.

- What kind of self-deception is exposed?
  - Moral hypocrisy: claiming to love God while withholding love from people God has placed in your life.
  - A hidden, inconsistent anthropology: you insist on a vertical, “spiritual” devotion that never translates into horizontal, practical love for others.
  - A denial of the test of faith: for John, knowing God is inseparable from keeping God’s commands and loving others (the chain of thought runs from God is love, to loving others, to knowing God).
  - In short, cognitive dissonance: you parcel out love as if it were optional for the neighbor but essential for God, which is incompatible with the way John understands God’s nature and what it means to know him.

- How does this fit the wider context of 1 John?
  - John repeatedly ties knowing God to living like Jesus, which includes loving one another (see 1 John 3–4; 4:7–21).
  - He is arguing against real-world counterfeits (false teachers, antichrists) who claim spiritual status without the ethical fruit of love.
  - The language is deliberately strong (“liar”) to joltingly expose the inconsistency between profession and practice.

- Practical takeaway
  - Examine your relationships: is there ongoing hatred, resentment, or neglect of others in the church? Are you quick to forgive, to serve, to bless, to help?
  - If you find a gap between your stated love for God and your treatment of others, bring that to God in confession and seek reconciliation with those you’ve harmed or who have wronged you.
  - Let the test push you toward a more integrated faith: belief about God and love for neighbor belong together, and both are the ongoing evidence of a heart truly touched by God.
