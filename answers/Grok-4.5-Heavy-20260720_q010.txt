**The power being praised is kenotic power** — the power of voluntary self-emptying.

It is not the power that seizes, clings, or dominates. The text says Christ, already “in the form of God,” did not regard equality with God as something to be grasped or exploited (ἁρπαγμός). Instead He emptied Himself, took the form of a slave, and obeyed all the way to a shameful death. The power held up for imitation is the power that is so secure in its own identity that it is free to descend, serve, and give itself away.

This is power expressed as radical humility rather than as force. It is authority that takes the shape of a servant. The passage presents this inverted hierarchy — divinity choosing the lowest place — as the true “mind” that should be in believers.

### Why humility joined to greatness feels more unsettling than open domination

Open domination is legible. It operates by rules we already understand: strength over weakness, higher over lower, force against force. You can hate it, fear it, envy it, resist it, or submit to it. The categories remain intact.

Humility joined to genuine greatness short-circuits those categories. When the highest possible being freely chooses the lowest path, several things happen at once:

- The ordinary scramble for status is exposed as unnecessary and even slightly ridiculous. If the One who is equal with God does not need to grasp, then our grasping is revealed as a symptom of insecurity rather than of strength.
- Domination itself starts to look like a lesser, more anxious form of power. True strength, the text implies, can afford to lower itself; only a threatened power must constantly assert and defend itself.
- There is no clean way to compete with or dismiss this kind of power. You cannot fight it without looking absurd, and you cannot write it off as mere weakness because the subject is explicitly “in the form of God.” It leaves the observer with moral and existential vertigo rather than a simple contest of force.
- It places a claim on the will. Open domination asks only for submission or resistance. This kind of power asks for a change of mind — the very φρονεῖτε the passage begins with. That is more intimate, and therefore more threatening.

In short, domination confirms the world’s logic of power. Kenotic humility judges that logic from above and below at the same time. That double pressure is what makes it feel more unsettling.
