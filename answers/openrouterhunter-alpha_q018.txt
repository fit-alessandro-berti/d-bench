This is a really rich question, and I think the passage is doing something more layered than simple moral instruction. Let me walk through it.

## The Structure of the Juxtaposition

Notice the two verses aren't just paired — they're **mutually exhaustive**. Verse 31 says "put away *all* bitterness" with that broad list cascading outward, and verse 32 says "be kind *to one another*" with its own cascading qualities. The passage isn't giving you a menu of options; it's presenting two total orientations of the self that cannot coexist. You cannot inhabit both postures simultaneously. That's why the contrast is so direct — not because forgiveness is merely the antidote to bitterness, but because they are **opposing modes of being**.

## What Kind of Thing Is Bitterness Here?

I'd argue the text is treating it as all three of your categories — feeling, habit, and chosen posture — but with one crucial observation: **it's presented as the root, not the fruit.**

Look at the list in verse 31. Bitterness stands first. The other items — wrath, anger, clamor, slander, malice — are progressively more outward, more public, more explicitly harmful. Bitterness is the seedbed. It's the internal corrosion that, left unaddressed, generates everything that follows. The wrath doesn't appear from nowhere. The clamor doesn't erupt in isolation. Bitterness is the settled disposition of a soul that has been wronged and has chosen to metabolize that wound in a particular way.

This maps onto the Greek word *pikria* (πικρία), which carries the sense of something that has curdled — like milk turning sour. It's not just anger; it's anger that has **aged into a permanent state**. The metaphor in the word itself suggests transformation: something that was once fresh and potentially nourishing has become poisonous through neglect and time.

So is it a feeling? Initially, yes. But the text seems to be describing what happens when a feeling is **allowed to harden into an identity** — when someone begins to organize their inner life around an offense rather than around grace.

## Why Forgiveness Requires a Ground

Here's what strikes me most about the passage: forgiveness is not presented as a brute moral achievement. It's not "just forgive because you should." Verse 32 gives you a **ground** — "even as God in Christ has forgiven you." This is doing serious theological work.

The passage is implicitly saying that bitterness is what the human heart produces *left to itself*. Forgiveness is what becomes possible only when the heart is standing on something outside itself. Without that ground, the instruction to "be tenderhearted" sounds naive, even impossible given what real offenses do to people. But the text isn't offering forgiveness as a human technique. It's offering it as participation in a pattern — "forgiving one another *as*..." The *as* matters enormously. It means forgiveness here is **mimetic**. It's the soul learning to do toward others what it has already received from God.

## The Contagion Question

You asked whether bitterness is something contagious, and I think the text actually implies it is — though the verb is more like **eruption** than infection. Notice "clamor" (*krauge*) and "evil speaking" (*blasphemia*) in verse 31. These are public, verbal, social acts. Bitterness that starts as one person's inner wound becomes a shouting match, a campaign of slander, a shared malice. The passage seems to recognize that unreconciled people don't suffer alone — they **generate communities of grievance**.

Which is precisely why the positive list is equally communal: "be kind *to one another*." The remedy is also contagious, but it requires someone to go first — to absorb a wound without returning it. And the text says that's only possible if you've already received that pattern from somewhere.

## What I Find Most Compelling

The deepest move in this passage, to my reading, is that **bitterness and forgiveness are not presented as symmetrical opposites**. Bitterness is what the fallen heart produces by default. Forgiveness is what the redeemed heart produces by grace. One is self-generating; the other requires a source outside the self. The juxtaposition isn't 50/50. It's asking you to notice which of the two requires supernatural help — and to draw the obvious conclusion about what you're actually capable of on your own.

That's a humbling reading, but I think it's faithful to what the text is doing structurally. The placement of bitterness and forgiveness against each other isn't just moral instruction. It's anthropology — a claim about what human beings are like without intervention, and what becomes possible when intervention arrives.
