# Luke 6:37 — Discernment vs. the Appetite to Condemn

## What the verse is *not* saying

A common misreading flattens this verse into: "Never evaluate anyone's behavior." But that reading collapses under its own weight almost immediately:

- Just a few verses later (Luke 6:43–45), Jesus tells his hearers to recognize trees by their fruit — an explicitly evaluative act.
- In Luke 17:3, he says, "If thy brother trespass against thee, *rebuke him*." Rebuke requires moral judgment.
- Paul tells the Corinthians to "judge them that are within" the community (1 Cor. 5:12).

So Jesus is not abolishing moral discernment. He is targeting something more specific — and more spiritually dangerous.

## The Greek behind the English

The verb κρίνω (*krinō*) in "judge not" carries the sense of *passing sentence*, not merely *noticing*. κατακρίνω (*katakrinō*), "condemn," is stronger still — to hand down a verdict against someone. The structure is escalating:

1. **Judge** — render a verdict on a person
2. **Condemn** — pronounce them worthy of rejection
3. **Forgive** — release them from the debt

Each pair is reciprocal: the measure you use is measured back to you (v. 38). Jesus isn't targeting the *perception* of wrong; he's targeting the *posture* of the one perceiving.

## Discernment vs. the appetite to condemn

Here is the real distinction the verse presses on:

| Discernment | Appetite to Condemn |
|---|---|
| Aimed at the act or pattern | Aimed at the person's worth |
| Seeks truth to respond rightly | Seeks a verdict to feel righteous |
| Holds its conclusions loosely | Grips them tightly; enjoys them |
| Leaves room for repentance | Forecloses the person's future |
| Costs the one judging (grief, labor) | Rewards the one judging (pleasure, status) |
| Knows itself also under judgment | Exempts itself from the measure it uses |

Discernment is a *tool* — picked up when needed, set down when done. The appetite to condemn is a *taste* — something a person begins to *enjoy*, to seek out, even to cultivate.

You can tell the difference by asking: *How do I feel when I turn out to be right about someone's fault?* Sober grief is discernment. Private satisfaction is the appetite Jesus is warning against.

## Why the appetite is spiritually lethal

The warning "and ye shall be judged" isn't just karmic tit-for-tat. It reflects a deeper mechanism:

- To condemn freely, you must quietly exempt yourself. The log-and-mote image (Luke 6:41–42) isn't about hypocrisy in general — it's about the specific blindness that the condemning posture *requires* in order to operate.
- The more you pronounce sentence, the less access you have to mercy — because mercy is a category you've trained yourself not to inhabit.
- The forgiving posture, by contrast, keeps you porous to your own need for forgiveness.

## The practical upshot

The verse calls for a particular kind of inner watchfulness — not over what you notice, but over what you *do with* what you notice. Ask:

- Am I seeing this in order to *respond* or in order to *pronounce*?
- Would I be disappointed if this person turned out better than I assumed?
- Am I applying the same measure to myself right now?

Discernment without the appetite to condemn is hard, rare, and largely silent. The appetite to condemn is easy, common, and loud. The verse is not telling you to stop seeing. It is telling you to stop *feeding* on what you see.
