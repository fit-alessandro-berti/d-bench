Great question. Hebrews uses **“root of bitterness”** very deliberately.

### Why a **root** and not just an act?

Because a root is:

1. **Hidden** – It grows underground before anyone sees it.  
   Bitterness often starts inwardly: wounded pride, unresolved anger, jealousy, sense of injustice.

2. **Living and growing** – It is not static.  
   An isolated offense can become a settled disposition if not dealt with.

3. **Source-producing** – A root feeds visible fruit.  
   Bitter speech, division, slander, coldness, retaliation—these are often the *fruit*; bitterness is the *root*.

4. **Hard to remove once established** – Pulling up a deep root takes effort.  
   So the warning is early intervention: “looking diligently.”

---

### What does this image suggest about delayed damage?

It suggests moral damage is often **slow, cumulative, and initially invisible**.  
You may not “see” the problem at first, but over time it surfaces in attitudes, habits, and relationships. By then, it has spread internally and become harder to uproot.

So Hebrews warns believers to watch carefully before hidden resentment matures into visible corruption.

---

### How can one person’s resentment spread to others?

Roots spread through soil; bitterness spreads through community.

- One person’s unresolved offense becomes negative talk.
- Negative talk creates suspicion and camps.
- Suspicion weakens trust and love.
- Soon “many are defiled” (morally stained, relationally damaged).

This is why the text is communal: not just “guard your own heart,” but **watch over one another**.  
Bitterness is contagious when normalized.

---

In short: calling bitterness a **root** emphasizes that sin often begins unseen, grows over time, and eventually bears destructive fruit—not only in one life, but in the whole body.
