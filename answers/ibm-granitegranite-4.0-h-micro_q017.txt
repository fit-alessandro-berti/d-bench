The passage from John 3:19-21 in the Bible presents a profound theological concept that can be interpreted in various ways. Here, "light" symbolizes truth, righteousness, and divine revelation. Conversely, "darkness" represents ignorance, deception, and moral corruption. 

The text suggests that people inherently prefer darkness over light. This preference is not merely a matter of personal taste or cultural preference, but rather a reflection of a deeper, psychological disposition. The reason for this preference is rooted in the human condition. The Bible implies that people are inherently sinful and prone to evil deeds. Consequently, they fear the light because it exposes their true nature, their sins, and their moral shortcomings. 

The fear of exposure is a powerful motivator. When exposed to the light, one's deeds can be judged and potentially condemned. This fear of judgment is a form of shame. The desire not to be seen clearly, therefore, is a manifestation of this shame. It is a fear of being found out, of being judged, and of facing the consequences of one's actions. 

The passage also suggests that those who do truth, or who live according to divine principles, are drawn to the light. This is because the light of truth reveals the path of righteousness and the path to God. It is a path that leads to redemption and salvation. 

In summary, the text suggests that people hate light because it exposes their sinful nature and the potential for shame and judgment. Conversely, those who seek truth and live according to divine principles are drawn to the light because it offers a path of righteousness and salvation. This passage underscores the importance of truth and the fear of exposure in human nature, and it highlights the transformative power of divine truth.
