This passage is often misread as unnuanced pessimism about human nature, but its core function is to name a universal, observable reality of self-deception—one that transcends religious contexts, even as it carries specific theological weight in Jeremiah’s prophetic message. To unpack this, and address the question of how to talk about motives, we can break the answer into two parts:

### First: This passage names realistic self-deception, not arbitrary pessimism
Jeremiah delivers this line to a Judean community that has repeatedly rationalized its unfaithfulness to its covenant commitments: they had exploited marginalized people, worshipped other powers, and convinced themselves their choices were just, even righteous, before God. The claim that “the heart is deceitful above all things” is not a blanket statement that all humans are irredeemably wicked, but a warning that our internal self-assessments are inherently untrustworthy. We are wired to rationalize our choices, soften our own flaws, and frame our motives as purer than they often are. The question “who can know it?” is not a cry of despair, but a recognition that independent, self-generated self-knowledge is impossible—we cannot fully see our own blind spots.
Even within Christian theological traditions that frame this passage as part of a teaching on human depravity, its purpose is not to leave people in pessimism: it is balanced by the second verse, which states that God alone can discern true motives, and holds people accountable to the tangible fruit of their actions (not just their flawed self-reports). Its goal is to push people toward humility, not despair.

### How to talk about motives, rooted in this passage’s warning
If the heart is an unreliable witness to its own intentions, this shapes how we discuss motives for ourselves and others in three key ways:
1. **Never claim definitive knowledge of someone else’s hidden motives.** If we cannot even trust our own assessment of our hearts, we have no basis to claim we can unpack another person’s core intentions. When addressing harm, shift away from speculative accusations of secret malice (“You only bailed on the project because you’re selfish”) and center the observable impact of their actions (“When you didn’t follow through on your commitments, the rest of us carried extra load and missed our deadline, which was incredibly stressful”). This honors the passage’s claim that only a power greater than us can fully search the heart, so we leave ultimate judgment of motives to that higher standard.
2. **Assess your own motives through tangible fruit, not just internal feelings.** The passage ties accountability to “the fruit of his doings”—the real-world outcomes of choices, not our internal sense of our own goodness. When examining your own motives, test your self-justifications against the impact of your actions, and invite trusted, honest community to name your blind spots. For example: if you convince yourself you skipped a friend’s gathering to “rest,” ask if that choice consistently leaves you isolating yourself from people who need you, or if you would accept that same justification from someone else who bailed on you.
3. **Frame conversations about motives with both grace and accountability.** Self-deception is a universal struggle, not a flaw unique to “bad people.” This does not mean you avoid holding others (or yourself) accountable for harmful actions, but it means you do not write people off as irredeemably wicked. You can acknowledge that we all are prone to misjudging our own intentions, and invite reflection rather than issuing condemnation—for others, and for yourself.
