

**Short answer:**  
Enemy‑love is hard to take seriously because it asks us to act against our most instinctive self‑protective impulses, and it can look either like naïve sentimentality or like a calculated maneuver. When we look more closely, however, the command in Matthew 5:44‑45 points to a *deeper moral strength* — a disciplined, vision‑shaping habit that re‑orients the whole of our character toward the goodness of God rather than the fleeting calculations of self‑interest.

---

## 1. Why it feels difficult

| Dimension | What makes it feel hard | Why it can be read as … |
|-----------|------------------------|------------------------|
| **Psychological** | Our brains are wired for threat detection and retaliation (amygdala‑driven fight/flight). Extending goodwill to a perceived danger feels counter‑intuitive and even dangerous. | **Sentimental** – “just being nice” ignores the real risk. |
| **Social / Cultural** | Most societies celebrate justice, retribution, or at least “standing up for yourself.” Loving an enemy can be seen as letting the offender off the hook or as a sign of weakness. | **Strategic** – “I’ll pretend to love them to gain advantage or avoid conflict.” |
| **Theological** | The command appears to contradict the Old‑Testament emphasis on divine justice (“eye for an eye”). It forces a reinterpretation of God’s character that many find unsettling. | Both sentimental and strategic readings can emerge as ways to sidestep the radical claim. |

---

## 2. Sentimental reading: “Just be nice”

* **What it looks like:**  
  - A vague, feel‑good injunction that asks us to smile at people who hurt us.  
  - Often reduced to “love everyone” without concrete guidance on boundaries, justice, or accountability.  

* **Why it fails as a full explanation:**  
  - Sentimentality ignores the *cost* of loving an enemy (emotional labor, risk of continued harm, need for discernment).  
  - It can become a way to avoid the hard work of confronting evil, turning the command into a passive optimism that lets injustice persist.  

* **When it might be useful:**  
  - As a starting point for cultivating empathy, especially when the hostility is mild or rooted in misunderstanding.  
  - In pastoral care, where reminding people of God’s indiscriminate benevolence can soften hardened hearts before deeper work begins.

---

## 3. Strategic reading: “Love as a tool”

* **What it looks like:**  
  - Treating enemy‑love as a means to an end: defusing tension, gaining moral high ground, or inducing reciprocity (“kill them with kindness”).  
  - Seen in political or conflict‑resolution strategies (e.g., non‑violent civil disobedience, restorative justice circles).  

* **Why it falls short:**  
  - Reduces love to a *instrument*—if the strategy fails, the love is abandoned.  
  - Risks manipulation: the enemy may sense the ulterior motive and distrust any genuine goodwill.  
  - Ignores the *intrinsic* value of loving the other for their own sake, not merely as a lever.  

* **When it can be illuminating:**  
  - Highlights that enemy‑love often produces tangible social benefits (reduced violence, opens dialogue).  
  - Shows that the command is not naïve; it expects real-world consequences that align with God’s common‑grace provision (sun and rain on both just and unjust).

---

## 4. Deeper moral strength: A virtue‑ethical reading

### 4.1. What “deeper moral strength” means
- **Habituated disposition:** Love of enemies is not a one‑off feeling but a cultivated *virtue* (agape) that shapes perception, desire, and action over time.  
- **Orientation to the Good:** It aligns the agent’s ultimate end with God’s character — *the Father who makes his sun rise on the evil and on the good.*  
- **Courageous restraint:** It requires the strength to forego immediate retaliation, trusting that justice will be upheld by a higher order (divine providence or the long‑term moral arc of community).  

### 4.2. Why this view resolves the difficulty
| Objection | How the virtue‑ethical answer meets it |
|-----------|----------------------------------------|
| “It’s unrealistic – I’ll get hurt.” | The virtue includes *prudence*: loving an enemy does not mean abandoning wise boundaries; it means refusing to let hatred dictate one’s response while still protecting oneself and others. |
| “It lets evil go unpunished.” | Agape is compatible with *justice*: one can love the person while opposing the wrongdoing (e.g., praying for their conversion, advocating for restitution, or supporting restorative processes). |
| “It feels fake or manipulative.” | When love is a *settled disposition*, it is not performed for effect; it flows from a heart that has been reformed by grace, making the action authentic rather than tactical. |
| “It contradicts Scripture’s call for justice.” | The Sermon on the Mount re‑defines justice: true justice mirrors God’s generosity (common grace) and seeks the *transformation* of the offender, not merely retributive balance. |

### 4.3. Biblical and philosophical support
- **Biblical:**  
  - *Romans 12:20‑21* – “If your enemy is hungry, feed him… Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good.”  
  - *Luke 6:27‑31* – The “golden rule” framed within the context of loving enemies.  
  - *John 13:34‑35* – Love as the distinguishing mark of discipleship, rooted in Jesus’ own self‑giving.  
- **Philosophical:**  
  - **Aristotelian virtue ethics** – Agape as the *excellence* of the rational soul directed toward the good of another, even when that other is hostile.  
  - **Immanuel Kant** (though he stresses duty) – Acting from respect for the humanity in the other, even an enemy, aligns with the categorical imperative to treat persons as ends.  
  - **Contemporary care ethics** – Emphasizes responsiveness to vulnerability; loving an enemy is an extreme case of attending to the other's need for recognition and transformation.  

### 4.4. Practical markers of this deeper strength
1. **Consistent prayer for the enemy’s well‑being** (not just for one’s own relief).  
2. **Concrete acts of goodness** that address genuine needs (food, shelter, fair treatment) *without* expecting reciprocation.  
3. **Willingness to confront wrongdoing** while maintaining a posture of hope for the enemy’s change (e.g., restorative justice dialogues, truth‑telling councils).  
4. **Emotional regulation**: acknowledging anger or fear but not letting them dictate the response; instead, channeling them into compassionate action.  
5. **Community reinforcement**: belonging to a group that models and nurtures this virtue (church, peace‑building circles, non‑violent movements).  

---

## 5. Summing up: Sentiment, strategy, or strength?

- **Sentimental capture** reduces the command to a fleeting feeling and ignores its demanding nature.  
- **Strategic capture** reduces it to a tactic and risks instrumentalism.  
- **Deeper moral strength** captures the full vision: a *habitual, courageous, grace‑filled orientation* that seeks the good of the enemy *as an expression of the character of God*, even while upholding justice and protecting the vulnerable.

When we see enemy‑love through the lens of virtue — shaped by prayer, disciplined action, and a hope rooted in God’s indiscriminate benevolence — we recognize it not as a naïve sentiment nor a manipulative ploy, but as the **hard‑won moral strength** that enables believers to reflect the Father who “maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust.”  

In practice, cultivating that strength is a lifelong journey: it asks us to confront our fear, to reshape our desires, and to let our love be *both* brave and wise — precisely the kind of love that can change hearts, communities, and ultimately, the world.
