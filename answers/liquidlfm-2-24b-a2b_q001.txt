Let’s break down **Genesis 4:6–7** in light of your question about **moral agency** and the nature of evil:

---

### The Passage (NIV):

> "The Lord said to Cain, 'Why are you angry? Why is your face downcast? If you do what is right, will you not be accepted? But if you do not do what is right, sin is at the door. It desires to have you, but you must rule over it.'"

---

## 1. **Picture of Moral Agency**

**Moral agency** refers to the capacity of a person to make choices and be held accountable for those choices. In this passage, God addresses Cain directly, engaging with him personally.

- **Cain is portrayed as a moral agent** who has the ability to choose between right and wrong.
- God does not say Cain *must* sin; rather, He presents a choice: **"If you do what is right, will you not be accepted?"** This implies agency—Cain can choose obedience or rebellion.
- The passage emphasizes **self-control** ("If you do not do what is right, sin is at the door") and **responsibility** ("you must rule over it").

---

## 2. **Nature of Evil Presented**

Evil here is depicted primarily as **sin**, which is an internal force or inclination that disrupts relationship with God and others.

- **Sin is personified** as something that "desires" (v. 7), suggesting it is not merely a passive impulse but an active, almost sentient presence.
- The passage frames evil as something that **presses upon** the individual, but ultimately **depends on the person's response**: Cain can "rule over it" by choosing good.

---

## 3. **Responsibility Placed on the Person**

- **Personal accountability:** God asks Cain, "Why art thou wroth?" implying Cain is responsible for his emotional state and actions.
- **Choice and discipline:** The text instructs Cain to "do what is right," showing that moral agency involves deliberate action.
- **Self-mastery:** The command to "rule over it" (sin) places responsibility on Cain to control his impulses and desires.

---

## **Summary Table**

| Aspect                  | Explanation                                                                                 |
|-------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Picture of Agency       | Cain is shown as a moral agent capable of making ethical choices                            |
| Nature of Evil         | Evil is presented as sin—a powerful, internal force that threatens but does not overwhelm    |
| Responsibility          | Cain is held accountable for controlling his emotions and actions; he must choose good     |

---

**In conclusion:**  
This passage draws a picture of moral agency where evil is presented as **sin**—a powerful, internal force—but one that Cain can resist through righteous action and self-discipline. The text places full responsibility on Cain to choose good and to master his own impulses.
