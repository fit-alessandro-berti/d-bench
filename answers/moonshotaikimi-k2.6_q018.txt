 In Ephesians 4:31–32, bitterness and forgiveness are set against each other because they are **mutually exclusive ways of inhabiting the same community**. The passage is not offering a private therapy session but a public charter for the life of the church. To see why, we need to look at the grammar, the imagery, and the logic of grace that holds the two verses together.

### 1. The Structural Antithesis
Verse 31 commands a decisive stripping off: *“Let all bitterness… be put away from you.”* The verb (Greek *apotithēmi*) is the same one Paul uses earlier for “putting off the old self” (4:22). It pictures a garment being removed and discarded. Then verse 32 commands a new set of clothes to be worn: kindness, tenderheartedness, forgiveness. The structure is deliberate replacement, not mere suppression. You cannot wear bitterness and forgiveness at the same time because **bitterness is the spiritual refusal to release a debt, while forgiveness is the deliberate release of it**. Where bitterness nurses a wrong, forgiveness absorbs its cost.

### 2. Bitterness as More Than a Feeling
The Greek word for bitterness here, *pikria*, evokes something sharp and acrid. It certainly involves painful emotion, but the text does not treat it as a passive sensation that washes over a person. It is listed in a catalog of volitional sins—*wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, malice*—all of which are **chosen postures that have become habits**. You cannot directly command someone to stop feeling hurt, but you can command them to stop nurturing a resentful stance. 

So in this context, bitterness is:
*   **More than a feeling.** Emotion is involved, but *pikria* names the heart’s insistence on rehearsing the injury and sharpening it.
*   **A habit and a posture.** The aorist imperative (“put it away”) calls for a decisive break, while the present imperatives in verse 32 (“be kind… forgiving”) imply an ongoing, repeated practice. Bitterness is the habitual refusal to practice grace.
*   **A chosen stance.** Because the command is addressed to the will, the text assumes moral agency. Bitterness is clung to; forgiveness is chosen.

### 3. The Contagious Dimension
While Paul does not use the word “contagious” here, the placement of this command inside Ephesians 4 makes the social dimension unavoidable. The chapter opens with a call to maintain the unity of the Spirit in the bond of peace (4:3), and the vices listed in verse 31 are all **community-destroying noises**: clamor, evil speaking, malice. Bitterness is the silent killer in that list—it does not need loud spectacle to tear a body apart. 

This is illuminated by Hebrews 12:15 (likely from the same Pauline circle), which warns against a “root of bitterness” that “springing up… defiles many.” A root is hidden, but its effects reach outward. In the corporate life of the church, one person’s settled resentment sends out toxins that others begin to drink. **Bitterness is contagious because it teaches the community that debts must be hoarded rather than cancelled.**

### 4. The Theological Logic
The reason bitterness must be put off is given in the final clause: *“even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you.”* Grace is the unnegotiable environment in which Christians live. To insist on your own bitterness is to deny the atmosphere you yourself breathe. If God absorbed the debt of your sin in Christ, then for you to hold another’s debt against them is to live as though the Gospel were not true for you. Forgiveness is therefore not simply a nice moral achievement; it is the **natural exhalation of a life that has inhaled grace**.

### Summary
The text treats bitterness primarily as a **chosen, habitual posture**—one that begins as emotional pain but hardens into a refusal to extend the forgiveness one has received. Because it festers in secret and poisons the Body, it also functions as something **contagious**. Paul places it opposite forgiveness because grace and grievance cannot coexist in the same heart; the church can only be built up when the garment of bitterness is stripped off and replaced with the kindness of God.
