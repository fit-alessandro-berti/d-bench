**The passage ruthlessly rejects worship because the people’s religion is fraudulent theater that mocks God while they practice injustice; the ceremonies themselves have become abominations.**

Amos prophesies to a prosperous, religiously active northern kingdom whose worship is not merely “out of balance” but is a lie. Feasts, burnt offerings, peace offerings, songs, and instruments are the very things God rejects with strong language (“I hate, I despise,” “I will not smell,” “I will not accept,” “take away… the noise,” “I will not hear”). This is not a call for reform of liturgy or a plea for “both/and.” It is a verdict: the worship is offensive *because* it coexists with, and covers over, systemic oppression of the poor, corrupt courts, and exploitation (context of Amos 2:6–8; 5:7, 10–12, 15; 8:4–6). Ritual without righteousness is not incomplete piety; it is spiritual fraud.

The fraud exposed is **hypocritical religiosity**—using sacred forms to maintain a divine relationship while living in direct contradiction to God’s character and demands. The people treat cultic observance as a substitute for (or a license for) justice. By continuing the forms, they claim fidelity and blessing; God answers that the claim is false. Their “solemn assemblies” and “melody of viols” are noise that drowns out the cries of the crushed. God will not be bribed by sacrifices or serenaded into overlooking injustice. Hence the demand is absolute: stop the performances and let justice “run down as waters” and righteousness “as a mighty stream”—an unceasing, overwhelming flood that replaces the stagnant puddle of empty ritual.

This is why mere “better balance” is insufficient. Once religion becomes a mask or a transaction that allows the rest of life to remain unjust, the mask itself is reviled. The passage (and the prophetic tradition it joins—Isa 1; Hos 6:6; Mic 6:6–8) insists that genuine knowledge of God produces justice and righteousness; without them the worship is not salvageable but provocatively false.

Final note: the text does not abolish worship forever; it abolishes *this* worship—the kind that impressions God while grinding the vulnerable—and restores the priority of covenantal fidelity expressed in justice.
