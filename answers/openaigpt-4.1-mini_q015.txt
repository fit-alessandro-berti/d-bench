The passage from Matthew 7:3–5 uses a vivid metaphor involving a "mote" (a small speck) and a "beam" (a large plank) in the eyes to convey its message sharply and clearly.

**What makes this image so sharp?**  
The sharpness comes from the exaggerated contrast between the tiny mote in someone else's eye and the huge beam in one's own eye, highlighting the absurdity and hypocrisy of critiquing others when one has a much larger fault themselves. This stark and relatable image vividly illustrates how people often overlook their own significant shortcomings while fixating on the minor faults of others, making the lesson immediate and impactful.

**How does the passage criticize hypocrisy without forbidding moral correction altogether?**  
The passage does not forbid correcting others; instead, it insists that one must first address and remove their own larger faults ("cast out the beam out of thine own eye") before trying to help someone else with their smaller faults ("cast out the mote out of thy brother’s eye"). This means moral correction is appropriate, but only when it is done from a place of self-awareness, humility, and without hypocrisy. The focus is on ensuring that the person giving correction is morally sound and sincere, rather than judgmental or self-righteous.

In summary, the imagery is sharp because of its exaggerated contrast, and the passage calls for thoughtful self-examination before offering moral correction, thereby condemning hypocritical judgment while allowing for sincere, humble guidance.
