Bitterness is called a **root** because the danger begins beneath the surface before it appears in visible conduct.

The image suggests several things:

- **Hidden moral growth.** A root may be alive and developing while nothing is yet visible above ground. In the same way, resentment, unbelief, envy, or resistance to God’s grace can be inwardly cultivated before it produces words or actions.

- **Delayed but inevitable fruit.** An isolated act may end when it is done, but a root continues to feed future behavior. Unaddressed bitterness can eventually produce hostility, division, revenge, cynicism, or apostasy.

- **Increasing difficulty of removal.** The longer a root grows, the deeper and more established it becomes. What begins as a wounded thought can become a settled disposition and then part of a person’s identity.

- **Communal spread.** Hebrews says that by it “many be defiled.” One person’s resentment rarely remains private: it is expressed through complaints, accusations, suspicion, factions, and imitation. Others begin to share the grievance, and the whole community can be poisoned.

The wording also echoes **Deuteronomy 29:18**, which warns of “a root that beareth gall and wormwood.” There the root is not merely an unpleasant emotion but a person or disposition that turns away from God and produces poisonous fruit among the covenant people. Thus, Hebrews’ “root of bitterness” can include resentment, but it is broader: it is any hidden, grace-resistant condition that grows into spiritually destructive fruit.

That is why the command is communal: “looking diligently.” Believers are to watch over one another, addressing the root early—not merely condemning the visible acts after the damage has spread.
