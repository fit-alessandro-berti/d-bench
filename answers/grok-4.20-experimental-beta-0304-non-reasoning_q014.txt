**The text says both, but primarily that *treasure reveals the heart* while also implying that *treasure trains the heart*.**

### 1. What the verse explicitly says
The key sentence is the conclusion in v. 21:

> “For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also.”

Grammatically and logically, Jesus is stating a diagnostic reality: **your heart follows your treasure**. The location of your treasure is the *revealer* of where your heart actually is.  
- It is not “where your heart is, there will your treasure be.”  
- It is “where your *treasure* is, there your *heart* will be.”

This is the same diagnostic logic Jesus uses elsewhere: “out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks” (Matt 12:34). The visible or concrete thing (treasure, words, actions) discloses the invisible reality (the heart).

So the primary direction in this specific text is:  
**Treasure reveals the heart.**

### 2. How it also trains the heart
Although the verse is phrased as revelation, the entire unit (vv. 19–21) is *hortatory*—it is commanding us to *choose* where to put our treasure precisely *because* the heart will follow it. Jesus is giving us a strategy for discipleship:

- Do *not* store treasure on earth (v. 19)  
- *Do* store treasure in heaven (v. 20)  
- *Why?* Because wherever you put your treasure, your heart will inevitably go there.

In other words, Jesus is teaching that **the heart is not static**; it is malleable. What you invest in, protect, and value will progressively capture your deepest loyalties, affections, and attention. This is why the passage functions as both diagnosis *and* prescription.  
- Revelation: “Show me your bank statement, your calendar, and your worries, and I’ll show you your functional god.”  
- Training: “If you want your heart to be in heaven, start putting your treasure there—because the heart will follow the treasure.”

This is the same principle Paul uses in Colossians 3:1–3 (“Set your hearts on things above… for you died, and your life is now hidden with Christ”) and in 1 Timothy 6:17–19 (instruct the rich “to be rich in good deeds… so that they may take hold of the life that is truly life”).

### 3. How possession, attention, and love become tied together

The passage weaves three realities into one bundle:

- **Possession** (“treasure” – *thēsauros*)  
  This is the concrete thing you own, invest in, or hoard—money, property, reputation, achievements, comfort, security, pleasure. Jesus is not against owning things, but against *treasuring* earthly things as your ultimate security.

- **Attention** (implied by the corruptibility and vulnerability of earthly treasure)  
  Earthly treasures require constant mental energy: worry about moths, rust, inflation, thieves, market crashes, status loss. You cannot stop thinking about what you treasure. Jesus is saying your mind will orbit whatever you have declared valuable.

- **Love / Heart** (*kardia*)  
  In biblical language “heart” is not just emotion; it is the center of affection, allegiance, value, and direction. It is where love and loyalty live. Jesus treats the heart as the *organ of valuation*. Whatever you treasure is what you love most.

**The tight knot**:  
Where you *put* your possessions (treasure) determines where your *attention* will be drawn, and that in turn shapes and reveals what you truly *love*.  
The three cannot be separated. Jesus is exposing a spiritual law of gravity: the heart is pulled toward whatever it invests in. Therefore the wise disciple *uses* possessions strategically to direct attention and affection toward heaven.

### Pastoral paraphrase of the passage
“Don’t make earth your retirement portfolio, because everything here decays or gets stolen. Instead, invest in heaven—where nothing can touch it. Why? Because your heart is a follower, not a leader. It will always migrate to wherever you have put your money, time, and energy. So put them in the one place that will keep both your treasure *and* your heart safe forever.”

This is why Jesus can say in the very next verse (v. 24), “You cannot serve God and money.” The competition is not between two *masters* in the abstract; it is between two *treasures* that will inevitably capture the heart.
