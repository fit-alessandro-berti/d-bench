The difficulty in taking enemy-love seriously—from Matthew 5:44–45—is not because it is sentimental or merely strategic, but because it demands a **deeper form of moral strength** that upends our natural instincts, social norms, and even our sense of justice.

### Why It’s Hard to Take Seriously:

#### 1. **It Contradicts Human Nature**
Human beings are wired for reciprocity: *You hurt me, I hurt you.* Revenge, resentment, and self-defense are evolutionarily entrenched. To love those who harm us feels like a betrayal of our own dignity. It’s not just hard—it feels *unnatural*, even dangerous. To love an enemy sounds like surrender, not strength.

#### 2. **It Challenges Justice**
Modern society prizes accountability, punishment, and boundaries. Enemy-love seems to blur moral line-drawing. If we bless those who curse us, does that mean we condone evil? Isn’t justice being compromised? But Jesus is not calling us to ignore evil—he’s calling us to *respond* to it with a higher logic: that love breaks the cycle of violence, not by weakness, but by radical moral agency.

#### 3. **It’s Not Sentimental**
Sentimentality implies emotion without cost. But Jesus’ command is not about feeling warm fuzzies toward your persecutor. It’s about *action*: *do good*, *bless*, *pray*. It’s a disciplined, intentional choice rooted in the character of God—not our mood. Moses, Paul, and Holocaust survivors like Corrie ten Boom all demonstrated this: love in the face of evil was not a feeling; it was a vow.

#### 4. **It’s Not Strategic**
Some try to reframe enemy-love as a clever tactic: “If you treat them kindly, they’ll stop hating you.” But that’s manipulation, not love. Jesus doesn’t promise that enemy-love will change the other person. He says: **“That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven.”** The goal is not outcomes—it’s identity. You are called to reflect God’s character: impartial, generous, life-giving—an enemy-love that showers sun and rain on the unjust as much as the just.

#### 5. **It’s Moral Strength—Radically Redefined**
True moral strength isn’t found in dominating your enemy, but in refusing to become like them. It’s the courage to remain humane when dehumanized. It’s the power to choose compassion when every instinct screams for tit-for-tat. Think of Gandhi, Martin Luther King Jr., or Desmond Tutu: they didn’t love their oppressors because it was easy—they did it because they saw love as the only force capable of transforming systems of sin.

### The Divine Paradox:
God’s love is *unearned* and *unconditional*. He blesses the wicked with the sun and rain—not because they deserve it, but because He *is* that kind of love. To love our enemies is to participate in His economy: a kingdom where grace outlasts grievance, where mercy wins over vengeance, and where peace isn’t negotiated—it’s *embodied*.

### Conclusion:
Enemy-love is difficult because it calls us beyond fairness into holiness. It is not sentimental (it costs everything), not strategic (it doesn’t guarantee results), but profoundly **moral**—a disciplined, divine response that forms us into the image of God. It is the hardest commandment because it is the most revolutionary: to love as God loves—even when it threatens everything we hold sacred.

In the end, enemy-love isn’t about the other person.  
It’s about who *we* become when we choose it.  
And that is the deepest kind of strength.
