The pairing is deliberate and comprehensive: *derek* (“way,” the traveled road, habitual course) and *maḥšəḇōṯ* (“thoughts,” the internal calculus, the interior theater where decisions are rehearsed) together map the full theater of moral agency.  The prophet is not merely adding a second, poetic synonym; he is distinguishing two interlocking levels of sin so that repentance will not be shallow or self-deceived.

1.  **“Way” as the grammar of habit**  
    In Isaiah, a person’s “way” is the embodied history of his choices (Isa. 59:6–8; cf. Jer. 6:16).  It includes social practices, economic habits, the routine use of power, the treatment of the vulnerable.  It is sin objectified: a path that can be watched, one that wears a rut.  To “forsake” it is to step off a trajectory that has momentum; it is a public, visible re-routing.  Without this, “repentance” is mere mood.

2.  **“Thoughts” as the workshop of justification**  
    The Hebrew noun points to the interior workshop where the self narrates, edits, and licenses deeds (“thoughts” in the collective sense of mental acts and settled convictions).  Sin here is not yet action, but it is the draft on which action is sketched (cf.  Isa. 47:10;  59:4).  The wicked imagination *renarrates* violence as prudence, greed as shrewdness, pride as natural right.  To forsake “thoughts” is to dismantle that self-flattering script; it is to withdraw consent from the interior argument that made the “way” thinkable.

3.  **Tension and simultaneity**  
    Isaiah puts them in parallel not because they are identical but because they are mutually conditioning:  
    - The “way” shapes the “thoughts” through repetition (habit normalizes itself).  
    - The “thoughts” redesign the “way” in advance (ideology refashions practice).  
    Therefore repentance must be both behavioral interruption (*forsake his way*) and cognitive unlearning (*forsake his thoughts*).  If one only changes the outward gait while keeping the inner script, the old path will eventually be retrofitted with new religious décor (Isa. 1:10–17; 29:13).  If one only reshuffles the inner script without altering concrete trajectory, the mind becomes a theater of piety that never opens onto the street.

4.  **Implications for mercy and pardon**  
    The double forsaking clarifies the *kind* of mercy being offered.  The Lord does not merely commute a sentence; he reclaims a life.  “Abundantly pardon” (*yārōb yislaḥ*) is covenantal language: a surplus of forgiveness that outruns the arithmetic of debt, but it is given to the one who has actually turned around (*sub*, “return”)—that is, who has reversed course in deed and reoriented desire.  Because both conduct and imagination have been recalibrated, mercy is not a legal fiction covering residual rebellion; it is the atmosphere in which a genuinely new history becomes possible.  

In short, Isaiah 55:6–7 insists that repentance must reach both the sidewalk and the drafting table.  The outward “way” makes sin communal and historical; the inward “thoughts” make it inventive and ideological.  Only when both are surrendered does “return” become a true U-turn rather than a detour, making room for the “abundant pardon” that remakes not only the record but the actor.
