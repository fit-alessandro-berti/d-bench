The kind of self being denied in Mark 8:34–35 is the **ego-driven self**, a self defined by personal ambitions, worldly achievements, desires for comfort, security, and societal approval. It is the identity built around preserving one's life on one's own terms, driven by self-interest, fear, pride, and the pursuit of pleasure and power.

### Why liberating?

For some, this call to self-denial is deeply liberating because it offers freedom from the oppressive burden of constantly having to define, defend, and promote oneself. When individuals relinquish the ego, they're released from anxiety over reputation, competition, and material success. They find a deeper sense of meaning, purpose, and joy precisely because they no longer measure their worth by shifting external standards or self-centered desires. Instead, their identity becomes grounded in something greater, transcendent, and lasting—devotion to God and service to others.

### Why offensive?

To others, this call feels deeply offensive or threatening. It directly challenges modern assumptions about autonomy, individual rights, and self-expression. To deny the self in contemporary culture can sound like a call to suppress personal identity, individuality, desires, or authentic experiences. It runs counter to values emphasizing self-realization, independence, and the pursuit of personal happiness. Furthermore, it implicitly critiques lifestyles centered on comfort, pleasure, status, and control, causing discomfort or outright resistance to the idea that real life is found through letting go rather than through accumulation and self-assertion.

Thus, the liberating and offensive dimensions hinge on fundamentally different understandings of what it means to be human, what constitutes freedom, and what makes life meaningful.
