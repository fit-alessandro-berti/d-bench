Great question! Let’s break down **Philippians 2:5-8** and address both parts of your inquiry.

---

## 1. **What kind of power is being praised here?**

The passage praises **the power of humility**—specifically, the *self-emptying* (kenosis) of Jesus Christ. Paul highlights that Jesus, though He was **in the form of God** (possessing all divine power and glory), did **not cling to His equality with God**. Instead, He **"made Himself nothing"** (Greek: *exōmenon tēn tēsēn*), meaning He voluntarily relinquished His privileges and status.

But this wasn’t weakness or loss—it was an act of **voluntary submission**. Jesus then **took the form of a servant** and became truly human, ultimately submitting even to **death on a cross**. The culmination is His **obedience unto death**.

So, the "power" being praised is:
- **Divine humility:** The willingness to surrender power for love.
- **Redemptive obedience:** True greatness shown through self-sacrifice.
- **Love in action:** Power used not for domination but for service and salvation.

---

## 2. **Why does humility joined to greatness feel more unsettling than open domination?**

### **A. Subversion of Expectations**
- **Open domination** is what we often expect from those in power: to command, to rule, to enforce authority.
- **Humility**, especially when paired with supreme power, **upsets our expectations**. It’s counterintuitive—how can the most powerful person be the most humble?
- This inversion challenges our assumptions about leadership and worth.

### **B. Threat to Ego**
- When someone in power chooses humility, it **exposes our own pride** and dependence on status.
- Open domination affirms our belief that strength equals control; humility suggests that true strength lies in service and vulnerability.

### **C. Unsettling Example**
- **Open domination** feels safe because it’s predictable: the strong rule, the weak obey.
- **Humility in greatness** unsettles us because it asks us to reconsider what real greatness looks like. It’s uncomfortable to see power wielded gently, not cruelly.

### **D. Transformative Impact**
- Humility in greatness doesn’t just change situations—it changes hearts. It invites others to see value in service rather than dominance.
- It’s unsettling because it calls us to **re-evaluate our own attitudes toward power and respect**.

---

**In essence:**  
Paul celebrates the paradoxical power of Christ—He who could have remained divine chose to become human, submit to death, and serve. This is unsettling because it overturns our usual ideas about power, showing that true greatness is found not in domination, but in humble service.
