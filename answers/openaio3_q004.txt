Proverbs 3:5-6 is not a charter for laziness of mind; it is a call to place every faculty, including the mind, under God’s rule.

1. The words themselves  
• “Trust” (Heb. bāṭaḥ) means to place one’s weight on, to rest one’s security in another.  
• “Lean” (šaʿan) is the picture of propping yourself up on a staff.  
• “Understanding” (bînâ) is a prized word in Proverbs (2:2; 4:5); it denotes clear, discerning thought.  
Thus the verse does not disparage understanding; it warns against treating one’s own understanding as the final staff that holds you up.

2. What the text is asking for: humble, whole-hearted reliance  
a. Relational: The “Lord” is the covenant God (YHWH). Trust is not an abstract leap into the dark but confidence in a known, faithful Person.  
b. Whole-person: “with all your heart” (lēb) includes intellect, emotions and will. It is an integrated act, not the silencing of thought.  
c. Submissive: “In all your ways acknowledge (yādaʿ, ‘know’) him.” We continually submit plans, options, fears and reasoning to God, inviting his correction.  
d. Expectant: “He will make your paths straight” – the promise that God will clear the way or show the right way as we move forward.

3. How humble self-distrust differs from anti-intellectualism  

Humble self-distrust  
• Recognises creaturely limits: “I see only in part” (1 Cor 13:12).  
• Knows the moral bent of the heart: pride, self-deception, blind spots (Jer 17:9).  
• Therefore submits reason to God’s Word, prayer, the counsel of the godly and the illumination of the Spirit.  
• Still prizes learning: “Incline your ear to wisdom … seek it like silver” (Prov 2:2-4).  
• Produces teachability, willingness to revise conclusions when Scripture or better evidence requires it.

Mere anti-intellectualism  
• Distrusts the very activity of thinking or education as though mind were the enemy of faith.  
• Treats feelings, slogans, or “what I already believe” as sufficient.  
• Can be a covert form of pride: “I don’t need to wrestle with arguments or facts.”  
• Ignores the biblical mandate to love God “with … all your mind” (Matt 22:37) and to “test everything” (1 Thess 5:21).  
• Often ends in gullibility or fanaticism rather than godly confidence.

4. The place of the mind in Proverbs as a whole  
• Proverbs repeatedly urges the reader to “get wisdom, get insight” (4:5-7).  
• The “fear of the Lord” is called “the beginning of knowledge” (1:7) – not the replacement of knowledge.  
• Therefore 3:5-6 is the balancing principle: intellectual pursuit is good, but autonomy is deadly. We think hard, yet we do not crown our thoughts king.

5. Practically lived out  
• Study, investigate, plan – but hold every conclusion before God with the prayer, “Search me … and lead me” (Ps 139:23-24).  
• Let Scripture have veto power over the cleverest argument.  
• Invite wise believers to question your assumptions (Prov 15:22).  
• When clarity is lacking, move forward in obedience to what God has already revealed, trusting him to “straighten the road” as you go.

In short, Proverbs 3:5-6 calls for a trust that is intellectually active yet spiritually dependent: reason is embraced as God’s gift, but God himself is the final ground of confidence. This is humble self-distrust, not anti-intellectualism.
