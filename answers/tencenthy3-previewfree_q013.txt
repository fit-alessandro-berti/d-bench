The sharpness of Amos’s rejection is not a renunciation of worship itself, but a renunciation of **worship offered as a substitute for obedience.**

To understand why God does not simply ask for "better balance" (i.e., "keep worshiping, but also be moral"), we have to look at the specific spiritual fraud being committed. The prophets often condemned the dangerous idea that religiosity could sanitize or cover up systemic injustice.

Here is a breakdown of the moral and spiritual fraud exposed in Amos 5:21–24.

### 1. The Fraud: "Cover-up" Worship
The core issue is that the Israelites believed their religious rituals were **canceling out** their social sins. They operated under a "transactional" view of God:
*   **The Logic:** "We may be oppressing the poor and perverting justice in the courts (as mentioned earlier in Amos 5:7, 12), but look at how many bulls we are sacrificing! Surely God is pleased with us."
*   **The Exposure:** God exposes this as a delusion. He reveals that He is not fooled by the arithmetic of religion. One act of crushing the poor (Amos 5:11) renders a thousand burnt offerings null and void.

### 2. The Rejection of "Noise" vs. "Justice"
The passage contrasts two things: **ritual noise** and **righteousness**.
*   **"The noise of thy songs" (v. 23):** The Hebrew word for "noise" (*sheon*) often implies a tumultuous, chaotic sound, or simply a disturbance. God is saying that the music intended to soothe Him or gain His favor is actually disturbing to Him because it is the soundtrack of a nation that is comfortable while oppressing the weak.
*   **"Let judgment run down as waters" (v. 24):** The word "judgment" (*mishpat*) here refers to **justice** and **legal fairness**.

The reason God rejects the worship "sharply" is that the worship is being used as a **distraction**. The people are singing loudly to drown out the cries of the oppressed. God stops the music so He can hear the cries of the victims.

### 3. Why Not "Balance"?
If God had asked for "balance," He would have tacitly accepted the premise that ritual and morality are two separate columns on a ledger that need to be equalized. Amos teaches that they are not separate columns; **ritual is the evidence of morality.**

*   **Worship without Justice is Fraud:** To worship a Holy God while participating in systemic injustice is to lie about the character of God. It says, "God loves the songs of the oppressor."
*   **Abolishing the Mask:** By rejecting the feasts and offerings entirely, God strips away the mask. Without the religious ceremony, the nation stands exposed for what it truly is: a society built on exploitation.

### 4. The Theological Irony
There is a deep irony in the text that explains the sharpness of the rejection:
*   **The Fat Beasts:** In verse 22, God mentions "the peace offerings of your fat beasts." Peace offerings (*shelamim*) were eaten by the worshipers. They were essentially a banquet with God.
*   **The Reality:** The people were getting fat and rich off the backs of the poor (Amos 4:1 calls them "cows of Bashan"). They were using stolen money to buy the fat cattle to throw a party for God, and then eating the meat themselves.

God is saying: **"I will not attend your banquet because the meat is seasoned with the blood of the poor."**

### Summary
The passage rejects worship sharply because the worship was **hypocritical**. It was a spiritual fraud where the people used religious fervor to absolve themselves of their ethical responsibilities.

God does not want "balance"; He wants **integrity**. He wants a life where the melody of praise flows naturally from a life of justice, rather than trying to drown out the noise of injustice. As the prophet Micah later summarizes: *"He has shown you, O man, what is good... to do justly, to love mercy, and to walk humbly with your God"* (Micah 6:8).
