Based on the text of Matthew 12:34–35, the passage connects speech with the heart by establishing a causal relationship between internal moral storage and external verbal output.

To answer your specific question: The passage suggests that words are **all three** (symptoms, choices, and revelations), though it emphasizes the diagnostic nature of speech (symptoms and revelations) most strongly.

Here is a breakdown of how the text supports each concept:

### 1. Words as Symptoms
The primary thrust of the passage is diagnostic. Jesus asks, *"How can ye, being evil, speak good things?"* This rhetorical question implies that speech is an outward indicator of an internal condition.
*   **The Connection:** Just as a fever is a symptom of an infection, speech is a symptom of the heart's moral state.
*   **The Text:** The phrase *"out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh"* suggests that speech is an overflow. You cannot have a healthy overflow from a corrupted source. Therefore, the words are a symptom of the spiritual health (or sickness) of the speaker.

### 2. Words as Revelations
The passage uses the metaphor of a *"treasure"* (Greek: *thesauros*). A treasure is hidden; it is stored away and not immediately visible to the outside observer.
*   **The Connection:** Speech acts as the mechanism that exposes what is concealed. When a person speaks, they reveal the contents of their hidden "stockpile."
*   **The Text:** *"A good man... bringeth forth good things: and an evil man... bringeth forth evil things."* The act of speaking "brings forth" the hidden reality. It strips away the mask, revealing whether the internal storehouse is filled with good or evil.

### 3. Words as Choices
While the text emphasizes the *inevitability* of the overflow (suggesting one cannot speak good if they are evil), it also implies moral agency in the formation of that overflow.
*   **The Connection:** A "treasure" is not accidental; it is accumulated over time through listening, thinking, and valuing. The "abundance" of the heart is the result of past moral choices. Furthermore, the speaker is held morally accountable for the output ("being evil").
*   **The Text:** By distinguishing between a *"good man"* and an *"evil man,"* Jesus implies that the heart's condition is the result of moral alignment. While the specific utterance may flow automatically from the heart, the *content* of the heart was formed by choices, making the speech the culmination of a moral history.

### Conclusion
Matthew 12:34–35 presents a holistic view of human communication:
1.  **Symptoms:** They diagnose the current spiritual condition.
2.  **Revelations:** They expose the hidden contents of the soul.
3.  **Choices:** They reflect the accumulated decisions of the past that filled the "treasure."

Ultimately, the passage argues that **you cannot separate the word from the worker.** Speech is not merely a tool to be used for good or ill at will; it is the natural, revealing, and symptomatic fruit of what has been stored in the heart.
