**Analysis of Matthew 12:34–35**

The passage from Matthew 12:34–35 examines the relationship between words and the moral state of a person, framed within the broader theological concept of divine responsibility and human accountability. The text is particularly relevant to understanding the connection between speech and the inner moral stockpiles of the heart.

### Key Connections:

1. **Speech as a Reflection of Heart**: 
   - The text suggests that **speech is a direct reflection of one's inner moral stockpiles**. When a "good man" speaks out of a "good treasure in the heart," his words bring forth positive attributes and actions. This implies that words carry the moral essence of an individual, highlighting the core of their character.

2. **Speech as a Choice**:
   - Conversely, when an "evil man" speaks out of an "evil treasure in the heart," his words propagate negative qualities and actions. The phrase "out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh" directly connects speech to one's emotional and spiritual resources. A reduced or empty heart ("an evil treasure") produces evil speech, indicating that **words are not just reflections but also choices** based on one’s inner state.

3. **Speech as Revealing Symptom**:
   - The passage hints that **speech acts as a revealing symptom of the moral condition**. Words, when driven by good or evil, serve as a mirror to show the condition of the heart. A "good speech" reflects a person’s moral worth, while an "evil speech" reveals their ethical decay. This lens suggests that **speech is not simply coded messages but acts as a diagnostic tool** for internal moral health.

4. **Speech as Both Choice and Revelation**:
   - The dual nature of speech—choice and revelation—emerges clearly. The "good man" who speaks good things is not just narrating truths but actively shaping them from his internal reserves. In contrast, the "evil man" who speaks evil things is not just narrating truths; his speech is an act of choice that reveals the absence of moral stockpiles in his heart. This duality underscores that **speech is both a reactive symptom (revealing moral states) and a proactive choice ( shaping one’s inner moral judgment)**.

### Philosophical Integration:

This passage aligns with broader theological themes, such as the responsibility of humans to align their actions with divine fiat (governed by God) and the intrinsic connection between speech, character, and morality. Within this framework:

- **Symptom**: Speech reveals hidden moral resources or deficiencies. Words act as a diagnostic, guiding individuals to either cultivate good habits or confront their internal flaws.
  
- **Choice**: The act of speaking is a conscious decision rooted in one’s moral compass. A "good speech" emerges from a heart rich in virtues, whereas an "evil speech" flows from a barren moral well. This suggests a moral imperative, where choices in speech mirror one’s ethical journey.

- **Revelation**: Speech is not just a mirror but also an instrument for revelation. It reveals one’s alignment with divine will (good speech) or opposition to it (evil speech). This dual aspect positions speech as a critical path for spiritual and ethical discernment.

### Summary: 
The passage connects speech to **three interconnected dimensions**: 
1. **Choice**: Speech reflects one’s moral intentions and actions.
2. **Symptom**: Words act as a diagnostic for hidden moral resources or deficiencies.
3. **Revelation**: Speech is both a mirror to one’s heart and a tool for moral growth or moral decay. It is not merely words but a multifaceted expression of one’s moral character.

This view suggests that speech does not merely amplify external truths but is deeply intertwined with a person’s inner moral stockpiles, serving as a vital indicator of their spiritual state.
