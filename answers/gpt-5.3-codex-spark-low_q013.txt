• Amos rejects the worship because the problem is not too much religion but religion that is being used to cover
  injustice.

  The issue is that the people were offering “correct” sacrifices while continuing practices like exploitation, legal
  corruption, and oppression (themes throughout Amos). In that setting, worship was becoming a moral alibi: rituals were
  performed so they could keep their status and avoid accountability, while their neighbors were harmed. The prophet is
  saying God is not impressed with form when life is opposite to the form.

  So it’s not a request for “better balance” between ritual and ethics; it is a denunciation of spiritual fraud:

  - Ritualism without righteousness (doing holy things for display while living unjustly).
  - Lips and drums without justice (public piety, private corruption).
  - Treating sacrifice as transaction (“we paid the religious bill, so we are in good standing”).
  - Collective self-deception (“we are faithful because our calendar is full of festivals”).

  In other words, Amos exposes a religion that has become a mask.
  “Judgment as waters, righteousness as a mighty stream” then points to what true worship requires: not louder worship,
  but a social and moral order aligned with justice, fidelity, and mercy.
