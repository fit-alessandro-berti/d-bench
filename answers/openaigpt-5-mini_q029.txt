Short answer: Paul calls for correction that is restorative (seeking repair and return), delivered in meekness (gentle, humble, not domineering), and tempered by self-scrutiny (awareness of your own weakness and susceptibility). The three belong together because each guards against a different abuse and together shape a corrective posture that heals rather than humiliates.

Why each element matters
- Restore (καταρτίζετε, “katartizete”): the goal is to mend and reinstate the fallen person into fellowship, not to punish or to score moral points. The verb points to repair, completion, making whole.
- Meekness (ἐν πνεύματι πραΰτητος): correction must be given gently and patiently. Meekness here is strength under control — it opens the heart of the one sinned against and avoids provoking defensiveness or resentment.
- Self-suspicion (“σκοπῶν σεαυτόν,” literally “watching/considering yourself”): the one who corrects must remember his own vulnerability to sin and temptation. That prevents pride, harsh judgment, hypocrisy, and guarantees empathy.

Why Paul pairs them
- Restoration without meekness easily becomes coercive or shaming and so defeats its purpose.
- Meekness without self-awareness can slide into false humility or sentimentality, or allow the corrector to ignore his own faults.
- Self-examination without an intention to restore can become paralyzing or indulgent of sin (never confronting), or else lead to cowardice in speaking the truth.

Together they create a balanced pastoral ethic: act to heal, speak and act gently, and maintain humble vigilance about your own standing so correction is sincere, cautious, and effectively redemptive.

Practical corollaries
- Confront privately and kindly (cf. Matt. 18:15); assume the best, listen, and offer help.
- Check your motives and tone before speaking; pray and prepare.
- Remember you can be the next to fall; that empathy will shape patience and persistence rather than contempt.

Bottom line: the required moral posture is humble, compassionate, and self-aware — a restorative love that protects the dignity of the fallen and the integrity of the community.
