The progression from "cease to do evil" to "learn to do well" is a foundational structural move in Hebrew prophetic literature. It creates a sequence that answers your second question definitively: **goodness is not mere restraint; it is a practiced competency of love and justice.**

Here is an analysis of why the text moves from the negative to the positive command and what it implies about the nature of righteousness.

### 1. The Insufficiency of the "Negative"
The first command, "cease to do evil," is a negative prohibition. It defines the moral minimum. If a person stops stealing, they are no longer a thief; however, the text implies that they are not yet "good" in the fullest sense.

In the prophetic worldview, sin is not just a stain to be removed; it is a distortion of the way things ought to be. If a man stops oppressing his neighbor but refuses to help him when he is in need, he has left a vacuum. In the biblical imagination, a vacuum of inaction is not neutral—it is dangerous. 

The text moves to "learn to do well" because moral neutrality is impossible. You are either actively bending the world toward justice, or you are complicit in its brokenness. "Ceasing to do evil" clears the ground, but it does not build the house.

### 2. Goodness as a Skill: The Imperative to "Learn"
The choice of the verb **"learn"** (*lamad*) is the most revealing part of the passage. We do not typically speak of "learning" to be good; we usually speak of "deciding" to be good or "trying" to be good.

The use of *lamad* suggests that doing good is a skill, a craft, or a discipline. It implies that:
*   **We are naturally inept at it:** Just as a student does not instinctively know algebra, humans do not instinctively know how to seek justice or relieve the oppressed. Our natural inclination is often toward self-preservation, which leads to the "evil" mentioned in verse 16.
*   **It requires practice:** One learns through repetition, failure, correction, and instruction. You cannot simply flip a switch and "do well." It is a process of re-habituation.
*   **It requires a teacher:** The implication is that the community must be instructed in the ways of * mishpat* (justice).

### 3. The Definition of "Doing Well": Active Intervention
The text does not leave "doing well" as an abstract concept. It defines it immediately with four specific actions:
1.  **Seek judgment:** Justice must be hunted down; it does not happen by default.
2.  **Relieve the oppressed:** This requires physical action to lift a burden.
3.  **Judge the fatherless:** This involves entering into the legal defense of those who have no protector.
4.  **Plead for the widow:** This requires advocacy for the vulnerable.

These are not passive virtues like "don't steal" or "don't murder." These are high-energy, high-risk activities. They require leaving one's comfort zone to intervene in the lives of others.

This answers the suggestion about "mere restraint." If goodness were mere restraint, the text would be satisfied with "ceasing to do evil." But the text demands a transformation of agency. The hands that were formerly used to oppress (evil) must now be retrained to relieve and defend (good).

### 4. The Metaphor of Washing
Verse 16 begins with "Wash you, make you clean." This creates a theological backdrop for the transition.
*   **Washing** is the removal of the past (repentance).
*   **Learning** is the investment in the future (righteousness).

You cannot "learn to do well" while your hands are dirty with the "evil of your doings." The transition suggests that **repentance is not complete without action.** The proof that one has truly "ceased to do evil" is that they have begun to "do well." If the learning does not follow the washing, the cleaning was superficial.

### Summary
The movement from "ceasing" to "learning" suggests that the moral life is a two-step motion: **turning away** from destruction and **turning toward** restoration.

Goodness, therefore, is not the absence of a negative, but the presence of a positive. It is a practiced form of love and justice that must be studied, rehearsed, and actively lived out. The text refuses to let the reader be a moral bystander; you are either a student of injustice, or a student of righteousness.
