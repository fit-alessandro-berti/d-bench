In Isaiah 55:6–7 the prophet pairs **“way”** (Heb. *derek*, path/road) with **“thoughts”** (Heb. *machăshāḇôṯ*, plans, reasonings, intentions) to show that biblical repentance is never partial. It is a turning of the *whole person*—body and mind, action and imagination—toward God. The text implies three interconnected truths:

---

### 1. Outward Conduct: The “Way” Must Be Forsaken
“Way” in Hebrew idiom means one’s habitual manner of life, daily choices, and visible behavior. To “forsake his way” is to abandon concrete sins: unjust dealings, cruelty, idolatry, oppression, or whatever pattern of disobedience has marked the wicked. Repentance is not merely a private mood; it must interrupt actual behavior. If the hands continue in evil while the mouth confesses sorrow, the repentance is hollow. The demand to leave the “way” guards against a merely sentimental or internalized religion that never reaches the street, the marketplace, or the home.

---

### 2. Inward Imagination: The “Thoughts” Must Be Forsaken
If “way” is the fruit, “thoughts” are the root. The Hebrew term embraces schemes, rationalizations, worldviews, and the silent arguments by which we justify ourselves. The “unrighteous man” must forsake not only his crimes but the *cognitive framework* that made them seem sensible, even desirable. This targets self-deception: the human tendency to re-label greed as “prudence,” contempt as “honesty,” or exploitation as “justice.” True repentance reaches the imagination, the daydreams, and the idle speculations where sin is often conceived before it is committed. Without this inward turn, outward change becomes performance—temporary conformity that leaves the heart still oriented away from God.

---

### 3. The Possibility of Mercy: Wholeness Met by Abundance
The verse does not say, “Clean yourself up completely, and then perhaps God will notice.” Rather, it sets the imperative (*forsake… return*) within a promise (*he will have mercy… he will abundantly pardon*). The structure implies:

- **Mercy is certain for whole-person repentance.** God does not bargain for perfectionism, but He does require integrity. The pairing of external “way” and internal “thoughts” defines the sincerity He looks for: a conversion that is neither hypocritical (right outside, rotten inside) nor vaporous (emotional inside, unchanged outside).
- **Divine pardon outstrips human repentance.** We return with the limited resources of a contrite but flawed creature; God responds by multiplying forgiveness (*yarbeh liślōaḥ*—literally, “he will multiply to pardon”). The abundance of grace corresponds to the totality of the turn. Because God sees that the wicked man has abandoned both his road and his rationalizations, He meets that movement with mercy that overflows the debt.

---

### Summary
Isaiah insists on both “way” and “thoughts” because **halfway repentance is no repentance at all.** To seek the Lord “while he may be found” means seizing the present moment to reverse direction—not only where we walk, but how we think. The promise that follows is that such unreserved turning never finds a stingy God. He who demands the whole person in repentance gives back far more in pardon: mercy, not as a calculating reward, but as a generous, abundant welcome.
