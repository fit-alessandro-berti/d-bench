The distinction between Luke 6:37 and the idea that "moral judgments never matter" is profound. It lies in the difference between **objective discernment** (recognizing truth and error) and **subjective condemnation** (assuming a posture of superiority, finality, and punitive intent).

Here is an exploration of that difference, focusing on the tension between discernment and the appetite to condemn.

### 1. Discernment vs. Condemnation: The Core Difference

**Discernment** is the ability to judge or distinguish between right and wrong, true and false, healthy and harmful. It is a cognitive and spiritual necessity for navigating life.
**Condemnation** is a moral verdict passed from one person onto another, often accompanied by contempt, exclusion, or a desire for punishment. It assumes the role of God as the final Judge.

| Feature | Discernment | Condemnation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Purpose** | To protect, guide, correct, or understand. | To punish, shame, exclude, or elevate oneself. |
| **Posture** | Humble, cautious, aware of one’s own flaws. | Arrogant, final, self-righteous. |
| **Outcome** | Restoration, clarity, safety. | Alienation, bitterness, hypocrisy. |
| **Biblical Example** | Jesus warning against false prophets (Matt 7:15). | The Pharisees condemning the woman caught in adultery (John 8:1-11). |

Jesus’ command “Judge not” does not mean “ignore evil.” Elsewhere, He commands His followers to “judge with righteous judgment” (John 7:24) and to identify “wolves in sheep’s clothing” (Matthew 7:15). This requires discernment. What He forbids is the *spirit* of condemnation—a harsh, hypocritical, unforgiving attitude toward others.

### 2. Why Moral Judgments Still Matter

If moral judgments didn’t matter, there would be no concept of sin, justice, or righteousness. The Bible consistently affirms that:
- **Truth exists:** There are objective standards of good and evil.
- **Actions have consequences:** Sin harms individuals and communities.
- **Correction is loving:** Proverbs 27:6 says, “Faithful are the wounds of a friend,” implying that pointing out fault can be an act of love if done rightly.

Therefore, Christians are called to make moral evaluations every day: Is this action loving? Is this teaching biblical? Is this behavior destructive? These are moral judgments. But they must be made without the *appetite to condemn*.

### 3. The Appetite to Condemn

The “appetite to condemn” refers to the human tendency to enjoy feeling morally superior to others. It is characterized by:

- **Hypocrisy:** Focusing on the speck in another’s eye while ignoring the log in one’s own (Luke 6:41-42).
- **Finality:** Acting as if one has the authority to declare someone beyond redemption.
- **Lack of Mercy:** Refusing to extend grace because one believes the other person “deserves” punishment.
- **Self-Righteousness:** Using another’s failure to bolster one’s own sense of virtue.

This appetite is dangerous because it corrupts the heart of the judge. It replaces compassion with cruelty and truth with pride.

### 4. How to Practice Discernment Without Condemnation

Luke 6:37 offers a three-part remedy that transforms how we engage with moral failures:

#### A. “Judge not, and ye shall not be judged”
- **Practice:** Evaluate actions, not ultimate worth. Recognize that you too are under judgment. Approach others with humility, knowing you are also a sinner saved by grace.
- **Example:** Instead of saying, “You’re a terrible person for doing that,” say, “That action was harmful and goes against what we believe is right.”

#### B. “Condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned”
- **Practice:** Leave final judgment to God. Your role is not to execute justice but to reflect mercy. Avoid shaming, excluding, or writing people off.
- **Example:** When someone fails, offer support and accountability rather than ostracization. Remember that Christ died for them too.

#### C. “Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven”
- **Practice:** Actively release resentment and the desire for retribution. Forgiveness does not mean ignoring harm or avoiding consequences, but it means refusing to hold the offense against the person in your heart.
- **Example:** If someone wrongs you, seek reconciliation and restoration rather than revenge.

### Conclusion

Luke 6:37 does not abolish moral responsibility. Instead, it abolishes **moral arrogance**. 

- **Moral judgments matter** because truth matters. We must discern good from evil.
- **But condemnation is forbidden** because only God has the right to finally judge the heart. 

The Christian call is to be **clear-eyed about sin** but **soft-hearted toward sinners**. We are to hate the sin but love the sinner—not as a cliché, but as a disciplined practice of humility, mercy, and forgiveness. In doing so, we reflect the character of God, who is both just and merciful.
