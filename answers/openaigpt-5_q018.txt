Short answer: In Ephesians 4, “bitterness” and “forgiveness” are set against each other as the root and the remedy. Bitterness is more than a passing feeling; it is a settled, corrosive posture that breeds other relational sins. Forgiveness, modeled on God’s grace in Christ, is the practiced posture that uproots it and preserves the unity of the community.

Details:
- Context: Paul is urging the church to “put off” the old self and “put on” the new (4:22–24), especially in speech and relationships (4:25–5:2). The aim is communal unity by the Spirit (4:3, 30).
- Terms: “Bitterness” (Greek pikria) evokes acridness—an entrenched resentful spirit. It heads a list that moves from inner disposition to outward expression: bitterness → wrath (thymos) → anger (orgē) → clamor/shouting (kraugē) → slander (blasphēmia), capped by “all malice” (kakia). This suggests bitterness is a root that feeds the rest.
- Grammar: “Let it be removed from you” (aorist passive imperative) signals decisive renunciation by the whole community. “Become kind…forgiving” (present imperative) calls for an ongoing, cultivated practice. So bitterness is to be cut off; forgiveness is to be grown.
- Nature of bitterness: Not merely a feeling but a chosen, habituated posture that seeps into words and actions. It is socially contagious—spreading through clamor and slander—and is treated as something the body must remove “from among you.” Compare Hebrews 12:15’s “root of bitterness” that “defiles many.”
- Nature of forgiveness: The word for “forgiving” (charizomenoi) carries the sense of “granting grace.” It is not primarily an emotion but an active, continual practice grounded in God’s prior forgiveness “in Christ.” It breaks the cycle of injury → resentment → retaliation and makes kindness and tenderheartedness possible.
- Why the direct contrast: Unresolved anger hardens into bitterness (cf. 4:26–27), which grieves the Spirit and fractures fellowship. Forgiveness, patterned on God’s grace, is the only adequate antidote. The text opposes not just two feelings but two rival postures shaping a community’s life.

Bottom line: Bitterness here is a deep, habitual posture with communal fallout; forgiveness is the deliberate, grace-shaped practice that uproots it and heals the body.
