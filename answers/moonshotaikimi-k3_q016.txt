The five candidates are not really rivals. They are concentric rings around a single center, and the text lets us watch each one give way to the next.

**Anger** is what the narrative names first — *ōrgisthē*, he flew into a rage — but anger is smoke, not fire. **Envy** sits just beneath it: a fatted calf for him, not even a kid for me. But envy itself needs an explanation, and the elder brother supplies it: **wounded fairness.** "These many years do I serve thee... and yet thou never gavest." He keeps books. Rewards should track deserts, and the ledger has been violated. Beneath the ledger is **pride in obedience** — his righteousness is not gratitude but an investment, and the feast for the prodigal bankrupts the investment.

But listen closely to how he describes that obedience, because this is where the deepest ring appears:

*"Lo, these many years do I **serve** thee."* The word is *douleuō* — **I have been slaving for you.** Not serving as a son. Slaving. He has lived in his father's house as a hired hand lives, accumulating wages in his imagination, waiting for a payoff that was never promised because it was never needed. The father's reply dismantles the whole economy in one sentence: *"Son — teknon, child — thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine."* You cannot envy a calf when you own the herd. You can only envy a calf when you think the herd belongs to your employer and the calf is your withheld wage.

So the deepest problem is **discomfort with mercy itself** — but more precisely, the thing that *makes* mercy intolerable to him: he has never actually related to his father as a father. Mercy extended to his brother reads to him as injustice toward himself, because his entire identity is collateralized in the wage-system that mercy abolishes. Grace to the undeserving is not merely offensive to the elder brother; it is existentially threatening. If the father celebrates the wastrel, then the elder brother's years of slaving were not the currency he thought they were — and the ledger is all he has.

Several details sharpen this:

- **"That I might make merry with my friends."** His dream celebration excludes the father — which is precisely what the younger son wanted too. Both sons crave the father's goods apart from the father's presence. One goes to the far country with his body; the other goes there in his heart while staying home. The far country, it turns out, is any distance from the father's joy.

- **"This thy son"** — he severs the fraternal bond. The father's last word gently restores it: *"this thy brother."* The whole exchange is a tug-of-war over the family grammar.

- **"Devoured thy living with harlots"** — the text never says this. Verse 13 reports only "riotous living." The harlots are the elder brother's own embellishment. Resentment always writes the worst possible screenplay for the other.

- **"Neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment"** — spoken while refusing to enter the feast, publicly shaming his father before the guests. His one visible act of disobedience in the parable is the one he is committing *as he claims perfection*.

And here is the turn that converts diagnosis into tragedy: the father does to the elder son exactly what he did to the younger — **he goes out to him.** He ran to meet the prodigal; he comes out to plead with the slave-hearted son, and the imperfect tense (*parekalei*) suggests he kept on pleading. A patriarch begging in his own doorway is as undignified as a patriarch running down the road. The mercy the elder brother despises is, at that very moment, being extended to him in the only form he can receive it: the father's humiliation on the doorstep. The parable's final question is whether grace can save the respectable.

Which is why the story refuses to end. The younger son was lost far away and had a famine to wake him; the elder son is lost at the threshold, with no swine-trough to bring him to himself — only the sound of music and dancing he cannot interpret, and a father standing in the dark, pleading. Jesus leaves him there because the parable was spoken to the Pharisees and scribes (15:1–2), and they are the ones standing on that doorstep. The ending is theirs to write.

So: anger is the symptom, envy the immediate cause, wounded fairness the creed, pride in obedience the identity — and beneath them all, a sonship never appropriated, which makes mercy feel like robbery. He resents the feast because he never discovered that the whole house was already a feast. He served a paymaster who never existed, and resents the father who does.
