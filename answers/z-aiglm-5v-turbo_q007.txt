To understand the difference between Luke 6:37 and the idea that "moral judgments never matter" (moral relativism), we have to look at the context of the passage and the specific definitions of the words used.

The verse does not demand that we turn off our brains or our moral compasses; rather, it demands that we change the **posture** of our hearts when evaluating others. The Bible consistently commands believers to discern good from evil (e.g., Hebrews 5:14, Philippians 1:9-10), so it cannot be arguing that all evaluation is forbidden.

Here is an exploration of the difference between **discernment** (necessary judgment) and the **appetite to condemn** (forbidden judgment).

### 1. The Logical Contradiction
If "moral judgments never matter," then the second half of the verse—*"and ye shall not be condemned"*—becomes meaningless. You cannot fear being condemned if you believe judgment itself is irrelevant. Furthermore, you cannot "forgive" someone (the third command in the verse) unless you first acknowledge that they did something that requires forgiveness (a moral judgment).

Therefore, Jesus is not saying **"Do not distinguish between right and wrong."** He is saying **"Do not set yourself up as the executioner of the human soul."**

### 2. Discernment vs. Condemnation

We can break down the difference by looking at the **object**, the **goal**, and the **self-reflection** involved in each attitude.

#### A. The Object: Behavior vs. Identity
*   **Discernment** evaluates **fruit and action**. It looks at a behavior and labels it accurately (e.g., "This action is dishonest," or "This habit is destructive"). In the very next chapter of Luke (Luke 7:43), Jesus asks the Pharisees, *"Do you see this woman?"* implying they should have been able to discern her character (repentance and love) versus Simon’s character (arrogance).
*   **Condemnation** attacks **identity and worth**. When we move from "This action was wrong" to "This person is garbage/beyond hope," we are condemning. Condemnation writes someone off as irredeemable. It usurps the role of God, who alone knows the heart and the future of a person.

#### B. The Motivation: Restoration vs. Destruction
*   **Discernment** aims at **restoration**. In Galatians 6:1, Paul says, *"Brothers, if anyone is caught in any transgression, you who are spiritual should restore him..."* You cannot restore someone if you haven't first judged (discerned) that they have fallen. Discernment acts like a surgeon cutting out cancer—it hurts, but the intent is healing.
*   **Condemnation** feeds the **appetite to destroy**. This is what psychologists might call "moral grandstanding" or spiritual arrogance. The "appetite to condemn" is satisfied not when the sinner repents, but when the sinner is crushed or shamed. It finds pleasure in saying, *"I am better than you."*

#### C. The Prerequisite: The "Plank" and the "Speck"
The most critical difference lies in the famous illustration immediately following this verse (Luke 6:41–42), regarding the speck in a brother's eye versus the plank in one's own eye.

*   **The Appetite to Condemn** is **blind hypocrisy**. It ignores one’s own massive faults (pride, anger, lack of mercy) to obsess over another’s small faults. This type of judgment is prohibited because it is delusional. It uses others as a distraction from one own's sin.
*   **Discernment** requires **self-judgment first**. Jesus says, *"You hypocrite, first take the plank out of your eye..."* He acknowledges that there *is* a speck in the brother's eye (meaning: yes, the brother *is* doing something wrong, and yes, you *should* see it). However, you are unqualified to help until you have dealt with your own stuff.

### Summary of Differences

| Feature | Discernment (Commanded) | Condemnation (Forbidden) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Premise** | There is objective truth/right & wrong. | I am the standard of truth/right & wrong. |
| **Focus** | The issue/behavior. | The person's character/worth. |
| **Attitude** | Sorrow, caution, humility. | Contempt, superiority, glee. |
| **Goal** | To help, protect, or heal. | To punish, exclude, or elevate self. |
| **Self-Awareness** | "I am fallible and could do the same." | "I would never do such a thing." |

### Conclusion
When Luke 6:37 says "Judge not," it is prohibiting the **totalizing** judgment that decides a person has no value or no hope. It forbids the **hypocritical** judgment where we measure others by a strict ruler while measuring ourselves by a loose string.

It creates a culture where we can say, *"What you are doing is wrong and will hurt you"* (Discernment), without ever saying, *"You are dead to me"* (Condemnation).
