### Interpretation of "Way"
In the context of Isaiah 55:6-7, "way" refers to outward conduct or external behavior—the actions, habits, and lifestyles that define a person's path in life. This aligns with biblical themes where "way" often symbolizes a person's mode of living (e.g., Proverbs 10:9 describes the upright walking securely, implying righteous actions). Abandoning the "wicked" way means turning away from sinful deeds or patterns, such as violence, idolatry, or injustice prevalent in Isaiah's time. Without this transformation in outward conduct, repentance remains superficial, as actions reveal true character (Matthew 7:16–20 echoes this, noting that fruit—i.e., deeds—identifies the tree).

### Interpretation of "Thoughts"
Similarly, "thoughts" point to inward imagination and mindset—the internal realm of ideas, desires, intentions, and motivations. This encompasses cognitive and emotional states, like harboring malice, coveting, or rebellious ideologies against God. Repentance requires abandoning unrighteous thoughts, implying a mental renewal. The Hebrew term used here (machashabah) often denotes schemes or plans (e.g., Isaiah 59:7 links evil thoughts to sinful deeds), indicating that inner imaginings drive behavior. Biblical parallels, such as Philippians 4:8 urging fixing minds on noble things or Jeremiah 4:14 urging cleansing of evil from the heart, reinforce that true change involves purging malign internal frameworks.

### Implications for Outward Conduct, Inward Imagination, and Mercy
The dual emphasis on "way" and "thoughts" implies that repentance must be holistic, addressing both the visible (outward conduct) and the invisible (inward imagination) aspects of human nature. Outward conduct alone might mask untransformed inner motives (e.g., hypocritical piety, as in Matthew 23:25–28), while inward imagination must align to prevent relapse into sin. This reflects God's call for a comprehensive heart change (e.g., Deuteronomy 6:5–6: love God with heart, soul, and strength), ensuring actions flow from renewed thoughts. For mercy, the verse emphasizes God's abundant pardon—"he will have mercy... abundantly pardon"—highlighting boundless forgiveness upon genuine repentance. This mercy is not earned but granted freely, underscoring God's willingness to restore relationships (e.g., Luke 15:11–32's prodigal son parable illustrates merciful reception after turning away from a destructive path and mindset). Rather than conditional requirements, it presents mercy as God's initiating grace, available "while he may be found," urging prompt response over delay. Critics might argue this passage reflects Old Testament legalism, but it aligns with New Testament teachings on atonement through Christ (Romans 5:8–9), where repentance leads to transformative forgiveness.
