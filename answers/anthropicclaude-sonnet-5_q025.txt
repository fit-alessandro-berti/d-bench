The verse is careful to locate the danger not in visibility as such, but in the *motive* that visibility feeds—the craving to be seen.

**The text's own distinction**

Notice the precision of the wording: "that ye do not your alms *before men, to be seen of them*." That second clause isn't decorative—it's the hinge of the whole warning. Jesus doesn't say "do not your alms before men," full stop. He says don't do them before men *for the purpose of* being seen. The phrase "to be seen of them" (Greek *pros to theathēnai autois*—literally "toward the being-watched by them") names an intention, a directedness of the will. Almsgiving performed publicly but without that inward aim wouldn't fall under the condemnation; almsgiving performed privately but still secretly performed *for* an imagined audience—one eye on how it would look if others knew—would.

So the moral danger isn't the act's exposure to sight. It's that the alms-giving has been *repurposed*. The act was meant to address a neighbor's need and to express gratitude toward God; when the craving to be seen enters, the act now serves a different end—the acquisition of a reputation, an image, a status as "generous person." The neighbor's need becomes incidental, almost a prop. The visible act stays outwardly identical, but its inner *telos* has been swapped out.

**Why the swap ruins the reward**

This is why Jesus says "ye have no reward of your Father." The theological logic depends on there being exactly one reward available per act, and it goes to whoever the act was actually performed *for*. If you perform charity to be seen by men, you have directed the act toward men, and men are precisely who reward you—with attention, esteem, social credit. That payment is real and immediate, which is what makes it seductive; it isn't punished by disappearing, but by being exactly what it advertised itself as: a finite, human, exhaustible payment. It simply forecloses the other reward, because the act was never addressed to the Father in the first place. There isn't a stacking of two rewards for one deed; the deed's *address*—who it was performed toward—determines who owes payment.

**Why this reframing matters**

Read this way, the verse isn't anti-social or anti-visibility. It's not urging secrecy as a technique, as though hiddenness itself purifies motive (the following verses about the left hand not knowing what the right hand does are hyperbolic training-wheels for a will still prone to self-congratulation, not a literal prescription that public goodness is impossible). The deeper claim is that the *self* watching itself be good, deriving satisfaction from being watched being good, is a subtle corruption available to a private donor scrolling through his own memory of the act just as much as to one who did it in the town square. Visibility is just the occasion that makes the temptation easy to indulge—it supplies an actual audience to gratify the craving. But the craving could in principle survive even without an audience, feeding on imagined spectators, and that's the real target of the warning: the heart's orientation toward being *seen* to be good, rather than simply *being* good.
