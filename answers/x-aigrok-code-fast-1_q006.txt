### Interpretation of "Self" in Mark 8:34–35
In this passage, Jesus calls for denying oneself as a core aspect of discipleship. The "self" being denied here refers to the ego-driven, self-centered aspects of human nature—such as personal desires, ambitions, pride, and the instinct for self-preservation—that often prioritize material comfort, social status, or individualistic goals over spiritual or communal commitments. This aligns with biblical themes where the "old self" (associated with sin and worldly influences) must be rejected to embrace a "new self" aligned with God's will. For example, in Romans 12:2, Paul expands on this by urging transformation through a renewed mindset, evidencing that self-denial isn't about self-hatred but surrendering control to a higher purpose, exemplified by Jesus' own sacrificial path (e.g., his crucifixion as the "cross" metaphor).

This denial is not about rejecting identity or worth but about subordinating fleeting, self-serving impulses to eternal values, such as love, service, and faith. Taking up one's "cross" symbolizes voluntary suffering or sacrifice, as crosses in Jesus' era were instruments of execution, implying embrace of hardship for a greater good.

### Why This Sounds Liberating to Some
To individuals burdened by internal conflicts, unfulfilled pursuits, or the relentless grind of self-reliance, Jesus' words can feel liberating. It offers release from the exhausting cycle of constantly chasing personal gain or identity validation, promising true "life-saving" fulfillment (as in the paradox: a life lost to God is ultimately gained). Followers in marginalized or struggling communities (historically, like early Christians facing persecution) might find it empowering, as it shifts focus from societal pressures to transcendent peace. Psychologically, this resonates with concepts like flow or self-actualization in higher purpose, providing meaning and camaraderie in faith communities—echoing testimonies in Christian writings (e.g., Augustine's *Confessions*, where he describes conversion as a liberating surrender from self-inflicted turmoil).

### Why This Sounds Offensive to Others
For those entrenched in secular or individualistic cultures that prize autonomy, achievement, and self-expression (e.g., modern Western ideals), the call to deny self can seem repressive or insulting. It challenges the notion of self-actualization as the pinnacle of human endeavor, potentially dismissing personal rights, dreams, or well-being as secondary. Critics might view it as promoting masochism, self-loathing, or groupthink, offensive to those valuing agency (e.g., philosophers like Nietzsche criticized similar ideologies as weakening the will). In contemporary debates, skeptics argue it undermines mental health or equality, associating it with historical abuses where self-denial justified oppression (e.g., uncritical obedience in religious institutions). This polarity stems from differing worldviews: where faith sees surrender as victory, secularism may see it as defeat.
