The passage from Proverbs 3:5–6 describes a specific kind of faithfulness characterized by intellectual humility and spiritual surrender rather than self-reliance.

### What Kind of Trust Is Being Asked For?
Proverbs 3:5–6 asks for **Trust in the Lord with all thy heart**, characterized specifically as **Humble Self-Distrust**. This is distinct from blind, unthinking faith; it requires an acknowledgment that one's own human capacity (specifically intellectual understanding) is limited and potentially flawed. It calls for a dependence on divine guidance *more* than on one's own perception or reasoning abilities.

### Distinguishing Humble Self-Distrust from Anti-Intellectualism

To understand the correct biblical direction, it is necessary to distinguish between this form of trust and true "Anti-Intellectualism."

| Feature | **Humble Self-Distrust (Proverbs 3:6)** | **Mere Anti-Intellectualism** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Core Attitude** | Acknowledges that human understanding has limits but relies on God for direction. | Refuses to engage with the mind, intellect, or rational truth-seeking for self-protection or pride. |
| **Relationship to Knowledge** | Knowingly uses knowledge but directs it to God for correction (e.g., "My brain sees what is wrong, but you must be right"). | Rejects logical engagement because it feels unnecessary, leads to no thought of knowing, or creates an avoidance of the mind's role. |
| **Risk** | Leads to spiritual blindness if one stops *thinking*, leading to unguided faith. | Can lead to arrogance (if thinking too much) or isolation (if refusing to think about anything). |
| **Biblical Goal** | Trust in God's wisdom is better than trusting one's own thoughts alone; this requires active trust, but not "intellectual arrogance." | Is the absence of the mind's input entirely; or it rejects the need for reasoning and faith (Proverbs 3:5 asks to trust with "all thy heart"). |
| **Example** | A scientist who admits their data isn't perfect and submits analysis to a higher authority but still conducts careful research. | A person who avoids thought out of fear, refuses to question the text because they don't think they can handle criticism, or rejects all intellectual engagement for blind compliance. |

### The Key Distinction
**Proverbs 3:5–6 does not call for Anti-Intellectualism.** Instead, it calls for **Intellectual Humility** paired with Total Trust.

*   **"Lean not unto thine own understanding"** means you are not going to trust your own mind *as the sole arbiter of truth*. It is a protection against thinking that "you know what God wants better than the Holy Spirit."
*   However, it permits using your intellect for study, reflection, and gathering data.
*   **Anti-Intellectualism**, on the other hand, would be a refusal to engage reason itself. The verse says *trust in the Lord with all thy heart*—including the heart that thinks, feels, and believes—but warns you not to lean too hard on "thine own intellect" (which implies trusting your own thoughts as the final authority).

**Conclusion:**
The trust being asked for is **humble self-distrust**—the honest confidence that human judgment and reason are insufficient to guide spiritual living alone. This must be distinguished from anti-intellectualism, which is a rejection of thought and reasoning altogether. The verse asks us to use our minds but not let them rule our lives; we must trust God *over* our own understanding.
