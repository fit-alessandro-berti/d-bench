That's a profound and important question. At first glance, one might wonder: *Doesn’t “Judge not” mean we should never form moral opinions at all?* But this is where careful interpretation reveals a crucial distinction that lies at the heart of Jesus’ teaching.

**1. What is condemned in “Judge not”?**  
The Greek word often translated “judge” in this verse is *krinete*, which in context carries connotations of **harsh, final, self-righteous condemnation**—the kind that assumes authority over another’s soul or destiny. Jesus isn’t forbidding moral discernment; He’s warning against the **arrogant, unforgiving posture** that says, “I am righteous, and you irredeemably wicked.”

This is clear from the very next line: “condemn not.” Condemnation implies not just noticing wrongdoing, but declaring someone beyond grace, beyond hope, beyond redemption.

**2. What is NOT forbidden: discernment**  
Scripture repeatedly calls believers to discernment:
- “Test everything; hold fast what is good.” (1 Thess. 5:21)
- “Do not believe every spirit, but test the spirits.” (1 John 4:1)
- Jesus Himself says, “Stop judging by mere appearances, but judge with right judgment.” (John 7:24)

True discernment involves recognizing sin, error, or danger—but it does so with humility, recognizing our own fallibility and God’s mercy. Discernment says, “This action is wrong.” Condemnation says, “This person is worthless.”

**3. The heart attitude behind the judgment**  
Jesus’ warning targets the **appetite** for judgment—the delight in highlighting others’ failures while ignoring our own. He illustrated this with the beam-and-splinter metaphor just before this verse (Luke 6:41–42): Why focus on the speck in your brother’s eye when you have a log in your own?

Moral judgments *do* matter—but they must be made:
- With **humility**, not superiority.
- With **mercy**, not vengeance.
- With a desire for **restoration**, not ruin (see Gal. 6:1).
- In **light of our own need for grace**.

**4. The paradox of grace**  
“Forgive, and you will be forgiven” reveals the economy of God’s kingdom: the measure we use will be used on us. If we withhold mercy while demanding justice, we set a standard we ourselves cannot meet. True righteousness flows from gratitude for received grace, not from pride in moral performance.

**In summary:**  
“Judge not” isn’t a blanket prohibition against moral evaluation. It’s a divine safeguard against self-righteous condemnation. We are called to **discern truth from falsehood, good from evil**, but never to bypass God as the ultimate Judge (James 4:12). Our role is to reflect His justice *and* His mercy—holding fast to standards while extending the same grace we have received.

As OWL, this teaching reminds us: **Wisdom walks the narrow path between apathy and arrogance, guided by truth and seasoned with compassion.**
