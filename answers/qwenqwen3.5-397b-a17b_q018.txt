The placement of bitterness and forgiveness as polar opposites in Ephesians 4:31–32 is not accidental; it is structural and theological. In the economy of this text, they are presented as **mutually exclusive states of being.** You cannot harbor the one while practicing the other.

To answer your specific questions about whether bitterness is a feeling, a habit, a posture, or a contagion: the text suggests it is **all four**, but primarily functions as a **chosen posture of the heart that acts as a spiritual contagion.**

Here is an analysis of why they are juxtaposed and how the text treats bitterness.

### 1. Bitterness as a Root (The Nature)
The Greek word used for bitterness here is ***pikria***. It literally refers to a sharp, pungent taste (like a poisonous root or spoiled food). In the Septuagint (the Greek Old Testament), this word is often used to describe idolatry or rebellion that poisons a community.

In this list of vices (bitterness, wrath, anger, clamour, evil speaking, malice), bitterness is listed **first**. This implies it is the **root**, while the others are the fruit.
*   **Wrath and Anger** are the emotional eruptions.
*   **Clamour and Evil Speaking** are the vocal manifestations.
*   **Malice** is the settled intent.
*   **Bitterness** is the internal soil from which all the others grow.

Therefore, the text treats bitterness not merely as a fleeting feeling, but as a **settled condition.** It is a lens through which one views others. If the lens is bitter, every interaction produces "evil speaking" or "wrath." Forgiveness is placed against it because forgiveness changes the lens.

### 2. Bitterness as a Chosen Posture (The Volition)
The command in verse 31 is imperative: *"Let all bitterness... be put away from you."* The Greek verb (*arthētō*) implies a decisive action of removal.

If bitterness were merely an involuntary emotion (like sudden grief or shock), it could not be commanded away. You cannot command yourself not to feel pain. However, you *can* command yourself not to nurture resentment. By issuing a command, the text treats bitterness as a **habitual posture** that requires maintenance.
*   Bitterness requires "keeping score."
*   Bitterness requires rehearsing the offense.
*   Bitterness requires refusing to let go.

Forgiveness is the direct counter-command because it requires the opposite volition: choosing to release the debt. They are placed against each other because they are opposite directions of the will. One clutches the injury; the other releases the offender.

### 3. Bitterness as a Contagion (The Social Impact)
The context of Ephesians 4 is the **unity of the Body of Christ.** Paul is arguing that for the Church to function as one, the relationships between members must be healthy.

Bitterness is treated as highly contagious because it inevitably spills over into the other vices listed.
*   **Internal:** Bitterness (Resentment)
*   **Emotional:** Wrath/Anger
*   **Verbal:** Clamour/ Evil Speaking
*   **Relational:** Malice

Once bitterness takes root in one person, it infects the community through speech ("clamour") and attitude ("malice"). It breaks the "bond of peace" (Eph 4:3). Forgiveness is the quarantine and the cure. It stops the spread of the injury. If I forgive you, the cycle of retaliation ends. If I remain bitter, the conflict propagates.

### 4. The Theological Anchor: Grace vs. Debt
The ultimate reason these two are juxtaposed is found in the final clause: *"even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you."*

*   **Bitterness is a theology of Debt.** It says, "You owe me. You hurt me. Justice requires that you pay for this, either through apology, suffering, or distance." It keeps the ledger open.
*   **Forgiveness is a theology of Grace.** The Greek word for forgiving here is ***charizomenoi***, which comes from *charis* (grace). It means "to give graciously." It says, "God cancelled my debt in Christ; therefore, I cancel yours."

Bitterness is placed against forgiveness because **bitterness denies the Gospel.** To hold onto bitterness while claiming to be forgiven by God is a contradiction. It suggests that Christ's forgiveness was not sufficient to satisfy your soul, so you still require satisfaction from your brother.

### Summary
The text treats bitterness as a **poisonous root** that must be surgically removed ("put away") because it is incompatible with the life of the Spirit.

*   **Is it a feeling?** Yes, but it is a feeling that has been allowed to settle and harden.
*   **Is it a habit?** Yes, it is nurtured by the habit of rehearsing offenses.
*   **Is it a posture?** Yes, it is a stance of hostility versus a stance of grace.
*   **Is it contagious?** Yes, it disrupts the unity of the entire body.

Forgiveness is the only force strong enough to displace it, because forgiveness does not just ignore the pain; it absorbs the cost, just as Christ did. You cannot have the "mind of Christ" (forgiveness) and the "root of bitterness" in the same heart at the same time.
