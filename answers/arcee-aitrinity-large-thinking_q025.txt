
Excellent question. This verse gets to the very heart of Jesus' teaching on authentic righteousness versus performative religion.

The answer, based on the text and its context, is that **the problem is not visibility itself, but the craving to be seen—the *intention* behind the action.**

Here’s a breakdown of why:

1.  **The Direct Object of the Warning:** The verse doesn't say, "Take heed that ye do not your alms." It says, "Take heed that ye do not your alms **before men, *to be seen of them*."** The critical qualifying phrase is *"to be seen of them."* The danger is the *purpose*.

2.  **The Consequence:** The result of this craving is stated clearly: "otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven." The loss is not of social capital or reputation, but of **divine reward**. The action is emptied of its eternal value because its true aim was misdirected.

3.  **Contrast with Later Teaching:** Just a few verses later (Matthew 6:2-4), Jesus gives instructions on *how* to give alms *privately* ("let not thy left hand know what thy right hand doeth"). He is not abolishing public charity; He is regulating the *heart* behind it. The prescription for secrecy is a cure for the disease of craving visibility.

4.  **The Parallel with Prayer (Matthew 6:5-6):** The exact same structure is used for prayer. The problem is not praying in public (Jesus prayed publicly), but praying **"to be seen of men."** The issue is hypocrisy—staging piety for an audience.

5.  **The "Reward" Dichotomy:** The core of the passage (Matthew 6:1-18) is a contrast between two systems of reward:
    *   **System 1:** Do good to be honored by people. **Reward:** Human praise (which is temporary and ultimately unsatisfying).
    *   **System 2:** Do good for the Father's eyes only. **Reward:** The Father's eternal reward.

**Why the Craving is "Morally Dangerous":**

*   **It Corrupts the Act:** The moral value of an act of goodness is intrinsically linked to the love and devotion to God from which it should flow. When the primary motive becomes human admiration, the act becomes a transaction for self-exaltation, not an expression of worship or love.
*   **It Fosters Hypocrisy:** It turns the person into a performer. The inner self—the values, desires, and relationship with God—becomes disconnected from the outer show. This is the essence of hypocrisy (play-acting).
*   **It Misplaces Treasure:** Jesus teaches that "where your treasure is, there will your heart be also" (Matthew 6:21). Craving human sight treasures human approval, thereby displacing God as the ultimate audience and treasure of one's life.
*   **It Undermines True Community:** If goodness is performed for status, it breeds competition, judgment, and pride within a community, rather than genuine encouragement and humble service.

**Conclusion:**

Visibility is not the enemy; **the self-exalting heart is.** An act done in public can be pure if the doer's heart is fixed on pleasing the Father, even if people see. Conversely, an act done in secret can be corrupt if the secret motive is to manipulate God or earn a reputation for "humility."

The danger Jesus warns against is the **craving to be seen**, because that craving fundamentally reorients the entire moral and spiritual economy of the act—from a vertical relationship with God to a horizontal pursuit of human honor. It makes the self, rather than God, the center of the moral universe.
