While all the options you listed describe aspects of the elder brother’s condition, the **deepest problem is his discomfort with mercy itself.**

While anger, envy, and wounded fairness are his emotional symptoms, and pride in obedience is the mechanism he uses to justify them, they all stem from a fundamental theological distortion: **he views the Father’s love as a limited resource to be earned, rather than an endless grace to be received.**

Here is the breakdown of why "discomfort with mercy" is the root, and how it subsumes the other issues:

### 1. The Symptoms: Anger and Envy
The text opens with "And he was angry." This anger is a symptom, not the disease.
*   **Why it’s not the deepest:** If the brother could accept his brother’s restoration, he would be relieved, not angry. His anger arises because he perceives the Father’s action as an injustice done against *him*.
*   **The Envy:** His envy ("thou hast killed for him the fatted calf") is born of scarcity thinking. He believes there is not enough celebration to go around. If he truly understood his standing ("all that I have is thine," v. 31), his brother’s celebration would not threaten his inheritance.

### 2. The Argument: Wounded Fairness
The elder brother’s primary argument is one of transactional justice: *"I serve thee... never gavest me a kid."*
*   **Why it’s not the deepest:** This "wounded fairness" is a cognitive distortion caused by his pride. He measures the Father's goodness by his own performance. However, fairness is a legal category; mercy is a relational one. The brother wants the Father to be a Judge, not a Parent. When the Father acts as a Father, the Brother feels "unfairly" treated.

### 3. The Mechanism: Pride in Obedience
The elder brother says, "I serve thee" (*douleuō*, to be a slave/servant), whereas the Father calls him "Son."
*   **Why it is foundational:** This is the psychological engine of his rebellion. He is so proud of his obedience that he has forgotten his identity as a son. He treats the Father like an employer and himself like an employee. This pride makes him incapable of receiving grace, because to receive grace, one must admit the need for it—and the elder brother believes he has none.

### 4. The Deepest Problem: Discomfort with Mercy
Why do Pride and Wounded Fairness matter? They matter because they reveal a **hostility toward the Father’s character.**
*   **The Conflict:** The Father’s heart is defined in verse 32: *"It was meet that we should make merry... for this thy brother was dead, and is alive again."* The Father defines joy by **restoration.** The Elder Brother defines joy by **retribution.**
*   **The Pharisaic Parallel:** In the context of Luke 15, Jesus is answering the complaint of the Pharisees who complained that He "welcomes sinners." The elder brother represents the religious elite who believe God’s favor is a reward for moral behavior. Therefore, they cannot stand that God gives that favor to the immoral.
*   **The Spiritual Crisis:** The deepest spiritual failure is not that he stayed home to work, but that **he could not rejoice in the Father’s joy.** He was "with" the Father physically, but his heart was "outside" because he was alienated from the Father’s heart of mercy.

### Conclusion
The elder brother’s tragedy is that **he is lost in the presence of salvation.**
He believes he is faithful, but his obedience has become a cage. He is angry because he does not understand the economy of the Kingdom: that grace is free to the unworthy, and therefore, the righteous must rejoice in the mercy given to the sinner rather than resent it.

**"Discomfort with mercy itself"** captures the totality of this issue. It explains why he feels wounded fairness (mercy violates his rules), why he is angry (mercy insults his merit), and why he stays outside (he prefers the comfort of his own righteousness over the scandal of grace). The story ends with him still outside, implying that **the rejection of mercy is the one thing that keeps a person out of the Father's house, even if they have never left the home physically.**
