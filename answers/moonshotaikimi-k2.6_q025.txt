The moral danger in this verse is **the craving to be seen**, not visibility itself. The verse does not condemn the public nature of the act; it condemns the *purpose* for which it is done.

### The Evidence of Intentionality
The King James Version captures a crucial detail: “**to be seen** of them.” The underlying Greek uses the construction *pros to theathēnai*—an infinitive of purpose. Jesus is warning against doing righteousness **“with a view to being observed.”** The danger is not that the act happens to be visible, but that visibility is the engine driving the act.

This distinction is confirmed by the immediate contrast with **Matthew 5:16**, where Jesus commands:

> “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven.”

In Matthew 5:16, good works are deliberately public, yet morally commendable. In Matthew 6:1, they are also public, yet morally bankrupt. The difference is the **motive**:
- **5:16** → They see your works and glorify **God**.
- **6:1** → You do works so that they glorify **you**.

### Why the Craving Is Destructive
Jesus calls those who do this “hypocrites” (from *hypokritēs*, meaning “actor”). The moral problem is theatrical: the person is treating righteousness as a performance to win human applause rather than an act of love toward God and neighbor.

This craving creates a kind of spiritual transaction. Jesus says such people “**have their reward**” (6:2, 5, 16)—they have already been paid in full with human admiration. By choosing the lower, earthly currency of praise, they forfeit the higher, heavenly reward from the Father.

### A Deeper Point About the Heart
The verse implies that even secret goodness can be corrupted by the *desire* for recognition (God, after all, “seeth in secret” [6:4, 6, 18]). But public settings intensify the temptation. Jesus targets public almsgiving, prayer, and fasting precisely because these are the easiest arenas in which to simulate virtue while actually feeding the ego.

**In short:** Visibility is the occasion of temptation, but the craving for human glory is the sin. Public goodness becomes morally dangerous when the eyes of others become the reason for the act.
