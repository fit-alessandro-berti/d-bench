# A Dual Reading — and Both Meanings Matter

This is one of those places where the text is richer than either reading alone, and I think the honest answer is that Jesus means *both*, but in an asymmetric relationship.

## The Revealing Reading

At one level, Jesus is giving a diagnostic. The parable of the rich young ruler in Matthew 19 does this explicitly — "Go, sell what you possess" is a diagnostic test precisely because possession *reveals* attachment. Where your treasure sits is a reliable index of where your affection already lives. We don't hoard what we don't love.

This reading is real. You can often tell what a person truly values by watching where they spend money without being asked to.

## The Formative Reading

But look at the grammar of the passage more carefully. Jesus gives two **imperatives**:

> *Do not lay up*... but *lay up*...

These are commands to *do something*. And the verse beginning with **"For"** (γάρ) gives the reason for those commands — it's explanatory. The logic runs:

> *Do this* → **for** → *where your treasure is, there your heart will be also*

The future tense — "there **will be** your heart" — suggests a consequence, not merely an observation. Jesus seems to be saying: *redirect your treasure, and your heart will follow*. He is prescribing a practice that *forms* the affections.

## How Possession, Attention, and Love Are Tied Together

Here is where the passage becomes psychologically profound. Jesus identifies a **triangle** that operates in human life:

- **Possession** (what you store, accumulate, invest in) creates **attention** — you watch over what is yours, you protect it, you check on it
- **Attention** cultivates **love** — prolonged focus on something naturally attaches the affections to it
- **Love** motivates further **possession** — what you love, you pursue storing up

This is a self-reinforcing loop. And Jesus is intervening at the point of *possession* because that is where the loop can be redirected. You cannot simply command love. But you can redirect what you accumulate, and the heart will follow the trail of your investments.

## Why Both Readings, Asymmetrically

The deepest insight is this: treasure *reveals* the heart **because** treasure *forms* the heart. Over time, the practices that shape us become so deeply embedded that they serve as diagnostic indicators. What began as a choice becomes a settled disposition.

Jesus is not merely describing a static fact about human psychology. He is offering an **ethical technology** — a practical, accessible entry point for reorienting the deepest affections of the self. If you want your heart in heaven, start by laying up treasure there. The heart will not resist where the treasure leads.

This is why the passage is so subversive: it bypasses the illusion that we can love God with our hearts while investing everything in the material world. The heart does not float free of its investments. It *follows* them.
