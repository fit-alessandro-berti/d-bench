## The Moral Posture of Galatians 6:1

The verse demands something rare: **authority exercised from a position of acknowledged vulnerability**. The "spiritual" person is called to act, but the verse immediately strips the act of any superiority. Correction here is not a verdict delivered from above; it is aid rendered by one fellow-traveler to another who has stumbled on the same road.

### Why the three elements cannot be separated

**1. Restoration sets the goal — and excludes punishment as the aim.**
The Greek verb *katartizō* was used for setting a broken bone or mending torn nets. The image is medical and practical, not judicial. The fallen person is not a defendant but a fracture. This reframes the entire encounter: success is measured not by whether the fault was named, but by whether the person was made whole. Many "corrections" succeed as accusations and fail as repairs.

**2. Meekness sets the manner — because the goal determines the method.**
A bone cannot be set roughly. If the aim is restoration, harshness is not merely unkind; it is *incompetent* — it defeats the purpose. Meekness here is not weakness or vagueness about the fault. It is strength under restraint: the corrector knows the truth must be spoken, but speaks it in a way the wounded person can actually receive. Severity often serves the corrector's ego; gentleness serves the fallen person's recovery.

**3. Self-suspicion sets the guard — because correction is itself a temptation.**
This is the most striking move. The verse warns the corrector: *"considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted."* The shift to the singular ("thyself") is pointed — Paul turns from the group to look each corrector in the eye. The danger named is double:

- You may fall into the *same sin* you are addressing (you share the same nature).
- You may fall into a *new sin* through the act of correcting — pride, contempt, the pleasure of standing over someone.

Moral superiority is the occupational hazard of moral correction. The verse defuses it by making humility a precondition, not an ornament.

### The logic of keeping them together

Remove any one element and the act becomes corrupt:

- **Restoration without meekness** becomes coercion — the right goal pursued by destructive means.
- **Meekness without restoration** becomes tolerance or evasion — gentleness that abandons the person to their fault.
- **Both without self-suspicion** becomes self-righteousness — the subtlest failure, because it can look exactly like virtue.

The deep insight is that the verse defines "spiritual" by this posture rather than presupposing it. Anyone can rebuke. Only the person who corrects *as one who could fall tomorrow* — who sees in the other's fault a mirror rather than a contrast — is actually qualified to correct at all. The fault is described as something the person was "overtaken in," ambushed by, which already extends to the fallen the same generous realism the corrector must extend to himself.

In short: the required posture is **solidarity in weakness combined with responsibility for one another** — judgment without judgmentalism, truth without triumph.
