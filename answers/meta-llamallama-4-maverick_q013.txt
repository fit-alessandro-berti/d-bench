The passage from Amos 5:21-24 is a scathing critique of the Israelites' worship practices, and it rejects their worship so sharply because it has become empty, hypocritical, and disconnected from the moral and ethical demands of God. Here's why:

1. **Worship without obedience is worthless**: The Israelites were going through the motions of worship, offering sacrifices and singing songs, but their hearts and actions were not aligned with God's will. They were not living out the moral implications of their faith. Amos is condemning a form of worship that is not accompanied by a commitment to justice and righteousness.
2. **Rituals without righteousness are an affront to God**: The Israelites were using their worship as a way to appease God, without considering the impact of their actions on others. They were ignoring the vulnerable, the poor, and the marginalized, and instead, were exploiting and oppressing them (Amos 5:11-12). Their worship was not a reflection of their true relationship with God, who demands justice and compassion (Micah 6:6-8).
3. **Formalism and complacency**: The Israelites had become complacent in their worship, thinking that the mere act of offering sacrifices and singing hymns was sufficient to earn God's favor. They had lost sight of the true purpose of worship: to honor God and reflect His character. Amos is confronting their formalism and challenging them to a more authentic, heart-felt faith.
4. **Moral and spiritual duplicity**: The passage is exposing a deep-seated moral and spiritual duplicity. The Israelites were pretending to be devoted to God, but their actions revealed a different reality. They were living in a state of moral schizophrenia, where their worship and daily life were disconnected. Amos is highlighting the contradiction between their profession of faith and their actual behavior.

In essence, Amos is exposing a profound moral and spiritual fraud. The Israelites were using worship as a way to mask their true nature, which was characterized by injustice, exploitation, and a lack of compassion. By rejecting their worship, Amos is calling them to repentance, to a radical reorientation of their lives around the values of justice and righteousness.

The passage is not asking for a better balance between worship and social justice; it is demanding that worship be inextricably linked to a life of righteousness. It is saying that true worship is not just about ritual or emotional experience, but about a deep commitment to living out God's justice and compassion in the world.
