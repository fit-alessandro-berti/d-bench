Short answer
- External compliance is outward obedience to rules or commandments.
- A “new heart” and a Spirit-filled inner life produce genuine, voluntary obedience from the inside, not just outward conformity.
- The hardness described as a “stone” heart is a vivid way of saying the will can become unresponsive to God; only God can replace that with a pliable “heart of flesh.”

More developed explanation

1) What the two kinds of obedience look like
- External compliance: Doing what God’s law requires in outward behavior, but without the inner motivation or renewal of the person. This can happen under an old‑covenant framework where the emphasis is on rule-keeping, even if the heart remains unreceptive to God (think of Jesus’ critique of Phariseeism in the Gospels).
- New heart and Spirit-enabled obedience: God promises to change the heart itself and to put His Spirit inside the person. Obedience then flows from inner transformation and relationship with God, not merely from willpower or fear of punishment. Ezekiel 36:26–27 says God will “give you a new heart” and “put my Spirit within you,” so that people will walk in God’s statutes as a result of that inner change.

2) The “hardness” imagery
- In Ezekiel, the stubborn, unreceptive human will is pictured as a stone heart. Stone is inert, unresponsive, and unchangeable by human effort. By contrast, a “heart of flesh” is soft, pliable, and responsive.
- This isn’t merely poetic; it communicates a real moral transformation. Dead, hard, self-willed hearts can’t please or obey God. Only God’s action can replace that hard inward condition with a heart that can respond to Him.

3) Why describe hardness as a material condition
- The prophets often use concrete, sensory imagery to express spiritual realities. Describing the heart as “stone” or “flesh” makes the inner state visible and imaginable: you can’t bend stone, but you can shape flesh.
- It also emphasizes dependence on God. The old condition (stone) is something humans cannot fix by effort; the new condition (flesh) comes from God’s gracious act—“I will give you” a new heart and “I will put my Spirit within you.”
- The language points forward to the broader biblicalTheme: the new covenant writes God’s law on the heart (Jeremiah 31:33; Hebrews 8:10) and is accomplished by the Spirit (2 Corinthians 3; Romans 8).

4) How this fits the larger biblical picture
- In the Old Testament, obedience was closely tied to the covenant community and external law-keeping. But prophets repeatedly stressed that true righteousness requires inward renewal (for example, Deuteronomy’s call to circumcise the heart; Jeremiah 31:33; Ezekiel’s promise here).
- In the New Testament, this is fulfilled in the Spirit: regeneration (being “made new” in Christ), God’s law written on the heart, and living by the Spirit (John 3; Romans 7–8; 2 Corinthians 5; Hebrews 8–10).
- Jesus and the apostles critique mere external conformity (hypocrisy) and point believers to transformation from within. This is the same core idea Ezekiel 36 presents in seed form.

5) Practical takeaway
- If you find yourself mainly doing what God requires outwardly but sensing resistance or hardness inside, the passage invites prayer for renewal: ask God to give you a new heart and His Spirit to inhabit you (Psalm 51:10; Ezekiel 36:26–27).
- True obedience is the fruit of inward renewal—loving God, trusting Him, and living in reliance on His Spirit—so that your actions naturally align with His statutes and judgments.

Key cross-references to consider
- Jeremiah 31:33; Hebrews 8:10 (law written on the heart, new covenant)
- Ezekiel 11:19; 36:25–27 (inner renewal, Spirit within)
- Psalm 51:10 (create in me a clean heart)
- Romans 8 (life in the Spirit; putting to death the misdirected desires)
- Matthew 23 (warning about outward show without inward obedience)

In sum, Ezekiel 36:26–27 contrasts two kinds of obedience: external rule-keeping without inner renewal, and interior transformation effected by God’s gracious act of giving a new heart and His Spirit, which makes genuine, wholehearted obedience possible. The stone-to-flesh imagery vividly communicates how radical and necessary that inner change is.
