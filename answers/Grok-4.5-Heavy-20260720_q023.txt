**Repentance in Isaiah 55:6–7 involves both “way” and “thoughts” because true turning to God must be comprehensive: it addresses the whole person, outward and inward.**  

The verse pairs them deliberately:  
“Let the wicked forsake his way, and the unrighteous man his thoughts: and let him return unto the Lord…”  

In Hebrew, “way” is *derek* (דֶּרֶךְ)—path, manner of life, course of conduct, lifestyle. “Thoughts” is *machashavot* (מַחְשְׁבוֹת)—plans, purposes, devices, imaginations, the inner workings of the mind and heart.  

The text does not allow a partial repentance that reforms only behavior while leaving the inner life untouched, or that cleans up the mind while the pattern of life continues unchanged. Sin is not merely external acts; it originates in the heart and expresses itself in the life. Therefore both must be forsaken.  

### Implications

**Outward conduct**  
The “way” is the visible path a person walks—the habits, choices, practices, and direction of life. The wicked are called to abandon that path entirely, not merely to slow down or decorate it. There is no room here for external respectability that still follows a self-chosen course.

**Inward imagination**  
The “thoughts” reach deeper: the intentions, desires, schemes, rationalizations, and fantasies that fuel the outward way. God requires the renunciation of corrupt inner purposes, not just better behavior. A change of conduct without a change of mind is incomplete; the battleground of righteousness includes the mind itself. Classic commentators note this clearly: there must be “not only a change of the way, but a change of the mind” (Matthew Henry). One must alter judgments, dislodge corrupt imaginations, and quit vain pretenses.  

This pairing guards against hypocrisy. Outward reform alone can be Pharisaical whitewashing. Inward reform alone can remain theoretical. Genuine repentance involves both.

**Possibility of mercy**  
The structure of the verse is vital: forsake the way and the thoughts, *and* return to the Lord—*then* “he will have mercy upon him… for he will abundantly pardon.”  

Mercy is not earned by the quality or completeness of the forsaking. It is freely and lavishly given by God to the one who turns. The Hebrew for “abundantly pardon” intensifies the idea: God multiplies forgiveness. The invitation is urgent (“while he may be found… while he is near”) yet the promise is overflowing.  

Note the immediate context. Verses 8–9 use the same two words: “For my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways… as the heavens are higher than the earth, so are my ways higher than your ways, and my thoughts than your thoughts.” The call is to abandon our lower ways and thoughts in order to be aligned with God’s higher ones. The same God who demands the turning is the One whose thoughts and ways of mercy far exceed human calculation.  

In short: repentance here is holistic because sin is holistic. Outward conduct and inward imagination both belong to the person who must return. And when that return is made, the possibility of mercy is not limited, reluctant, or partial—it is abundant.
