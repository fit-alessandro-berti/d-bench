The elder brother's speech gives away where the wound actually is: "these many years do I serve thee... and yet thou never gavest me..." His whole self-understanding is transactional. He has treated obedience as a form of *wage-earning*, not as the natural shape of sonship. So when grace is extended to someone who didn't earn it, his accounting collapses — because if the younger son can be received without merit, then the elder's years of careful merit-keeping never actually purchased anything either. That's intolerable to him, because merit is his identity.

So the surface emotions — anger, envy, a sense of unfairness — are all real, but they're symptoms. The deeper problem is that he doesn't understand grace at all, and specifically doesn't understand his own relationship to the father in gracious terms. He thinks he has been *serving* a master who owes him wages, when in fact he has been *living* as a son who already possesses everything ("all that I have is thine" — the father has to remind him of this). His obedience, rather than being the overflow of love, has become a claim, a ledger entry, a means of establishing superiority. That's what makes it *pride in obedience* rather than mere rule-keeping: he doesn't merely obey, he obeys *as leverage*.

This is also why "discomfort with mercy itself" is close but not quite the root — the discomfort is downstream of the pride. Mercy is offensive to him not in the abstract but because it exposes that his own righteousness was never the currency he imagined it to be. If grace is real, his years of dutiful service can't function as superiority over his brother. The father's welcome to the prodigal implicitly says: sonship was never about earning. That is precisely what the elder brother cannot accept, because his whole self-worth is built on the earning.

So: anger is the emotion, envy is the comparison, wounded fairness is the accusation — but underneath all three is a self-righteousness that has never actually believed in grace, and therefore cannot rejoice when grace triumphs. He stands outside the feast not because he's excluded, but because rejoicing over unearned mercy for someone else would require him to relinquish the very framework by which he has secured his own worth. That refusal — clinging to a ledger instead of receiving sonship as gift — is the deepest problem.
