Enemy-love is hard to “take seriously” precisely because it flies in the face of every instinct we share with the rest of the animal kingdom—and with every conventional theory of justice.  We’re wired to protect ourselves, to retaliate when we’re hurt, and to reward our friends while punishing our foes.  Jesus’ call to “love your enemies” overturns all of that.  

1. It isn’t mere sentimentality.  
  •  Feeling affection for someone who’s hurting or persecuting you is almost impossible.  Jesus isn’t telling us to cultivate mushy warm-fuzzies; he’s calling us to will another’s good, to pray for them and act kindly toward them even when they deserve reproach.  That takes discipline, self-denial and—at its root—dependence on God’s grace.  

2. It isn’t simply a shrewd strategy.  
  •  We do discover, in practice, that non-retaliation and unexpected generosity can disarm hostility, break cycles of revenge, and open doors to reconciliation.  Gandhi, Martin Luther King Jr., and others proved that.  But Jesus doesn’t hold out pragmatic payoff as the chief reason.  He holds up his Father in heaven, “who causes his sun to rise on the evil and on the good,” as the model we’re meant to imitate—not because it always “works,” but because it reveals who God is.  

3. It is a deeper kind of moral strength.  
  •  Jesus’ command summons us into a new form of powerlessness that is actually true power: the power to bless rather than inflict pain; to absorb injury without returning it; to forgive rather than hold grudges.  It refuses the world’s definition of strength.  In so doing, it reflects the character of God himself, whose love embraces enemies as well as friends.  

In short, “enemy-love” is no naïve sentimentalism, nor merely a ploy.  It is a call to a higher ethic—a supernatural way of living that, grounded in union with Christ and empowered by the Spirit, breaks the iron laws of retaliation and transforms both us and those we once called foes.
