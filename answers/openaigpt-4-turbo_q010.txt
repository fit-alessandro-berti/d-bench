The passage from Philippians 2:5–8 is praising the power of humility and self-sacrifice, as exemplified by Christ Jesus. According to the text, although Jesus possessed the nature and status of God, He did not consider His equality with God something to cling to for His own advantage. Instead, He willingly relinquished His divine privileges, took on the form of a servant, and embodied humanity. Not stopping there, Jesus further humbled Himself by obeying God’s plan to the point of undergoing the humiliating and painful death on the cross.

This portrayal of power is fundamentally different from typical worldly conceptions of power, which are often associated with strength, control, dominance, and the accumulation of status or wealth. The power being praised here is a "power through weakness," which is counterintuitive to human logic. It suggests a strength that is made perfect in vulnerability and a leadership that serves rather than dominates.

The reason humility, particularly when coupled with greatness, can feel more unsettling than outright domination is manifold:

1. **Contradiction to Expectations:** Humility in the face of power contradicts common expectations about how powerful individuals should behave. Leaders are typically expected to exert dominance and authority to manifest their power, while humility might falsely appear as a weakness or relinquishment of authority.

2. **Challenge to Self-interest:** Great humility in powerful individuals challenges others to consider motives beyond self-interest and personal gain. In a world where self-promotion is often seen as the way to success, witnessing true humility can be disconcerting or challenging as it suggests a different set of values and priorities.

3. **Conviction and Reflection:** When a person of great power embraces humility, it forces observers to reflect on their own practices and beliefs about power, success, and ethics. It sets a standard that can be uncomfortable because it compels individuals to evaluate the authenticity and motivations behind their own actions.

4. **Perceived Vulnerability:** By choosing to lower themselves, powerful figures embracing humility can appear vulnerable. This vulnerability is unsettling because it goes against the typical goal of power, which is to protect oneself and expand one's influence, often through asserting strength rather than exposing weakness.

5. **Moral Authority:** Humility, particularly in conjunction with self-sacrifice, can confer a type of moral authority that is more compelling and persuasive than that conferred by traditional power. This moral authority can be unsettling because it demands a higher ethical standard.

In conclusion, the passage in Philippians highlights a paradigm of power that operates through selflessness, servitude, and humility. This form of power, as demonstrated by Jesus, challenges conventional attitudes and behaviors related to leadership and influence, making it both revolutionary and unnerving in a world that often values different expressions of power.
