Luke itself gives the key:  

> “He spoke this parable **to some who trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others**” (v.9).

So the “condemning” element is not fasting, tithing, or even doctrinal statements, but the *way* his religious life functions before God: self‑trust leading to contempt.

### 1. What is wrong with the Pharisee?

Look at his prayer line by line:

> “God, I thank thee, that I am not as other men are, extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even as this publican.  
> I fast twice in the week, I give tithes of all that I possess.”

- He **addresses God** and speaks of socially recognized sins. Nothing there is obviously false.
- He cites genuine practices (fasting, tithing) that Scripture itself elsewhere commends.

The problem is the *inner logic* of his prayer:

1. **Self‑trust as the ground of righteousness**

   He is “trusting in himself that he is righteous” (v.9). His moral and religious record is what gives him confidence before God. He is, in effect, presenting a résumé rather than seeking mercy.

   That is why the story ends with the contrast:

   > “This man (the tax collector) went down to his house **justified**, rather than the other” (v.14).

   The Pharisee came seeking confirmation that he *already* stood justified because of what he *is* and what he *does*.

2. **Comparison as a way of securing identity**

   > “I thank thee that I am **not as other men are** … or even as this publican.”

   His sense of righteousness is comparative: he is righteous “because I am not like them.” This is a way of grounding identity in differentiation from other sinners rather than in relation to God.

3. **Contempt (“despised others”)**

   Luke frames the story with this: he spoke it against those who “despised others” (v.9). The prayer embodies this: he is looking down on “this publican.”

   That contempt violates the command to love one’s neighbor and reveals that his piety does not produce mercy. Spiritually, others’ sin functions for him as raw material for self-exaltation.

4. **Pride and self-display**

   The text says he:

   > “stood and prayed **thus with himself**…”

   The phrase can mean he prayed *to himself* or *by himself*, but Luke immediately shows that his “thanksgiving” is mainly self-directed: he describes his moral superiority and religious performance without any confession, petition, or real dependence.

   The virtues he names—fasting, tithing—have become a way of *displaying* a self he believes already acceptable to God.

**So in terms of your options**:

- **False doctrine?** Not in the narrow sense (he’s not denying God or Torah), but he lives a *practical* heresy of justification by his own virtue.
- **Pride?** Yes. That is the root: self-exaltation before God.
- **Comparison?** Yes, as the mechanism by which he secures his pride (“not like other men…”).
- **Contempt?** Yes. Luke explicitly targets this. His righteousness *requires* a class of people he can look down on.
- **Virtue as self-display?** Yes. His good works are not surrendered to God; they are leveraged before God.

All of those are aspects of one thing: **self-righteousness**—trusting his own moral and religious performance, and using others’ sin to validate it.

### 2. Why is the tax collector’s posture “spiritually truer”?

Contrast the tax collector:

> “standing afar off, would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven, but smote upon his breast, saying,  
> ‘God, be merciful to me, a sinner.’”

Key elements:

1. **He tells the truth about himself**

   - “a sinner” (literally, “*the* sinner,” as if he is focusing on *his own* condition, not anyone else’s).
   - No excuses, no comparison (“at least I’m not as bad as…”). His posture matches reality as Scripture portrays all humans: morally bankrupt before God and in need of grace.

2. **He depends solely on mercy**

   The verb translated “be merciful” (hilastheti) is related to atonement/propitiation terms. He is, in effect, asking: “God, provide atonement for me, the sinner.”

   There is:
   - no appeal to his record,
   - no mention of compensating good deeds,
   - only a plea: “Your mercy or nothing.”

   That is precisely the stance the New Testament later calls *faith*: throwing oneself on God’s mercy rather than one’s merits.

3. **He stands before God, not over against other people**

   - He “stands afar off” and won’t lift his eyes—signs of unworthiness.
   - He does *not* say, “Have mercy on me; I’m not as bad as *those* people.”
   - His focus is vertical (God and himself), not horizontal (himself versus others).

4. **He is the one declared ‘justified’**

   Jesus’ verdict:

   > “I tell you, this man went down to his house **justified rather than the other**;  
   > for everyone that exalteth himself shall be abased; and he that humbleth himself shall be exalted.” (v.14)

   - The **principle** Jesus gives is not “the more sinful you are, the better,” but:
     - Self‑exaltation before God → being humbled.
     - Self‑humbling before God → being exalted (declared righteous).

   The tax collector’s posture is “spiritually truer” because:
   - It matches the *actual* human condition.
   - It recognizes God as the only source of justification.
   - It abandons comparison and contempt, and stands naked before mercy.

### 3. Summary

The Pharisee is not condemned for fasting and tithing, nor merely for having some prideful thoughts. He is exposed as:

- **trusting in himself that he is righteous,**
- **grounding that sense of righteousness in comparison with and contempt for others,**
- **using real virtues as evidence for his own acceptability before God.**

The tax collector is commended because he does the opposite:

- confesses himself a sinner,
- appeals to mercy alone,
- stands before God without using others to make himself look better.

In short: self-righteousness closes a person to grace, while truthful humility opens a person to it—and that, in Jesus’ words, is the difference between going home “justified” and not.
