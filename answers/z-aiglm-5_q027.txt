The parable of the Pharisee and the Publican is a masterclass in the theology of grace versus the psychology of merit. To answer your question, we must look at how the text frames the two men, beginning with the Pharisee.

### 1. What Condemns the Pharisee?

It is tempting to pick a single vice from your list, but the text suggests that the Pharisee is condemned by a **confluence of pride, comparison, and contempt**, all stemming from a fundamental error in how he views righteousness.

**The Core Issue: Self-Justification (Trusting in Self)**
The text begins by telling us exactly who Jesus is speaking to: those who "trusted in themselves that they were righteous." This is the root of the Pharisee’s problem. His condemnation is not that he has false doctrine (he believes in the right God) or that he fails to perform religious duties (he fasts and tithes). His condemnation is that he views righteousness as something he *possesses* by his own effort, rather than something he receives from God. He believes he has no need for mercy; he only needs an auditor to agree with his resume.

**Comparison as the Mechanism of Pride**
You asked if it is comparison. Yes, comparison is the mechanism by which his pride operates, but it is a corrupted form of judgment. True righteousness is measured against the holiness of God. The Pharisee, however, measures himself against "extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even as this publican."
By shifting the standard from God’s perfection to human sinfulness, he creates a rigged system where he always wins. This comparison allows him to bypass repentance. If you are only comparing yourself to the worst in society, you never have to deal with the darkness in your own heart.

**Contempt and the Weaponization of Virtue**
This leads to the most damning aspect of his prayer: contempt. The Pharisee cannot even finish his prayer of thanksgiving without spitting venom at the man praying behind him. This reveals that his religion has not made him loving; it has made him arrogant.
His "virtue as self-display" is hollow because it is inseparable from his scorn for others. As the 19th-century theologian Søren Kierkegaard noted, the Pharisee essentially thanks God that he is not human like the rest of them. He has turned his obedience (tithing, fasting) into a weapon to bludgeon others, rather than a gift to offer God.

**Conclusion on the Pharisee:** He is condemned because he is **self-deceived**. He confuses his social status and religious performance with spiritual purity. He brings his "scorecard" to God, unaware that in the Kingdom of God, the only currency accepted is a broken spirit.

### 2. Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture "Spiritually Truer"?

The tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer because it aligns with the **objective reality of the human condition before a Holy God.**

**Alignment with Reality**
The Pharisee lived in a delusion of his own making ("I am not as other men"). The tax collector, however, faces the truth. He does not compare himself to the Pharisee or to other sinners; he stands before God alone. "Standing afar off" and refusing to lift his eyes signifies that he understands the vast chasm between God’s holiness and his own sinfulness. His posture is not one of groveling self-hatred, but of **honest assessment**. He is a sinner. To pretend otherwise would be a lie. Therefore, his posture is "truer" because it is the only honest way to stand before God without Christ.

**The Posture of Receptivity**
The Pharisee stands tall (literally and metaphorically) and fills the space with his own voice, listing his achievements. There is no room for God to speak because the Pharisee is too busy declaring his own goodness.
In contrast, the tax collector’s posture—beating his breast, eyes downcast—is one of **emptying**. He makes space. By acknowledging he has nothing to offer ("God be merciful to me a sinner"), he positions himself to receive the only thing God can give: grace. You cannot fill a cup that is already full; the Pharisee was full of himself, while the tax collector was empty.

**The Nature of Justification**
The term "justified" (Greek: *dedikaiōmenos*) is a passive participle here. It means "having been declared righteous." The tax collector did not justify himself; he was justified *by God* because he asked for mercy.
The spiritual truth here is that **justification is a gift received by the empty, not a wage earned by the full.** The tax collector’s posture is truer because it reflects the gospel reality: we come to God as beggars, not as bosses. He went home justified because he came to God as he truly was, whereas the Pharisee went home empty because he came to God as he wished he was.

### Summary
The Pharisee is condemned because he **trusted in himself** and used his virtue to fuel his **contempt** for others. The Tax Collector is justified because his posture reflected **spiritual poverty**—an accurate understanding of his need for mercy, which is the only prerequisite for receiving grace.
