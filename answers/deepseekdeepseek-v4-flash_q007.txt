The verse from Luke 6:37 is often misunderstood as a blanket dismissal of moral judgment, but the context and broader biblical teaching reveal a crucial distinction between condemning others and exercising godly discernment. Let’s unpack the difference.

## 1. “Judge not” is not a command to never evaluate

In the parallel passage in Matthew 7:1–5, Jesus immediately follows “Judge not” with a warning about hypocrisy: “Why do you see the speck in your brother’s eye, but do not notice the log that is in your own eye?” He doesn’t say “never notice the speck,” but rather, “first take the log out of your own eye, **and then you will see clearly to take the speck out of your brother’s eye**.” So the goal is not to abandon all discernment, but to remove self-righteousness and prepare to help others.

Similarly, in John 7:24, Jesus explicitly commands, “Do not judge by appearances, but judge with righteous judgment.” This shows that there *is* a kind of judgment that is both necessary and right—one based on truth, fairness, and love. The prohibition in Luke is against a particular *attitude*: the appetite to condemn.

## 2. Discernment vs. the appetite to condemn

**Discernment** is the ability to distinguish good from evil, truth from error, wisdom from folly. It is a gift (1 Kings 3:9, Hebrews 5:14) and is essential for loving others well (Philippians 1:9–10). Discernment:

- Looks at actions and motives with humility, aware of one’s own fallibility.
- Seeks to understand, correct, or protect—never to merely punish or exalt oneself.
- Is done in relationship, often with the goal of restoration (Galatians 6:1).
- Acknowledges that final, eternal judgment belongs to God alone (James 4:12).

**The appetite to condemn**, on the other hand, is a self-exalting, harsh, and unforgiving spirit. It:

- Focuses on others’ faults while ignoring or minimizing one’s own.
- Speaks with finality—“You are beyond hope”—rather than offering a path back.
- Seeks to tear down rather than build up.
- Often involves gossip, slander, or a sense of moral superiority.

Luke 6:37 directly targets this condemning appetite: “Condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned.” To condemn is to hand down a final, punitive verdict, which is not our role. Forgiveness, by contrast, is the antidote: “Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven.”

## 3. Why moral judgments do matter

If moral judgments never mattered, we would have no basis for:

- Calling evil evil (e.g., injustice, abuse, false teaching).
- Rebuking sinners in love (Matthew 18:15–17).
- Protecting the vulnerable from harm.
- Discerning true from false prophets (Matthew 7:15–20).
- Pursuing justice and righteousness in society.

Jesus Himself made sharp moral judgments—against the Pharisees (Matthew 23), against hypocrisy, against those who cause little ones to stumble. But His judgment was not driven by a desire to condemn; it was driven by love for truth and for the people being hurt.

## 4. The heart of the matter: posture, not prohibition

The verse’s warning is about **our inner posture**. When we judge in the sense of condemning, we put ourselves in God’s place and close the door to mercy. When we discern, we remain open to grace—for others and for ourselves. The two differ in motive, in method, and in outcome.

So Luke 6:37 does not say “moral judgments never matter.” It says: *Do not assume the role of final judge. Do not be quick to condemn. Do not withhold forgiveness.* That leaves plenty of room for wise, humble, loving discernment—precisely because moral judgments *do* matter, and they must be made with the gravity and grace they deserve.
