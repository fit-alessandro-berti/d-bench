**The verse rejects hypocritical condemnation and a spirit of unforgiveness, not the necessity of moral judgment or discernment.**

Luke 6:37 (KJV, as quoted) sits in the Sermon on the Plain and parallels Matthew 7:1–5. Jesus is addressing the attitude of condemning others from a place of self-righteous superiority while ignoring one’s own faults, and he links judgment, condemnation, and forgiveness. The promised reciprocity (“ye shall not be judged… forgiven”) underscores that merciless people invite the same measure they apply. It is not a blanket ban on assessing right and wrong.

### Moral Judgments Matter Profoundly
Saying “moral judgments never matter” would collapse ethics into pure subjectivism or indifference. That is foreign to the teaching of Jesus and the rest of Scripture:

- Jesus repeatedly makes sharp moral judgments: calling the Pharisees hypocrites and whitewashed tombs (Matthew 23), cleansing the Temple, distinguishing good and bad fruit/trees (Matthew 7:15–20; Luke 6:43–45), defining adultery of the heart, and declaring that people will be held accountable for their deeds.
- He commands discernment: “Do not judge by appearances, but judge with right judgment” (John 7:24). Followers are told to recognize false prophets by their fruit, practice church discipline (Matthew 18:15–17), refuse to cast pearls before swine (Matthew 7:6), and examine themselves and their teachers.
- The broader biblical witness affirms objective moral reality—commands, rebukes of injustice, lists of virtues and vices, and the ultimate judgment of God. Without moral judgment, categories such as love of neighbor, justice, confession of sin, or the need for forgiveness lose meaning. A society or person that pretends no judgments are needed quickly becomes exploitable and incoherent.

The verse does not erase these. It forbids the posture of the self-appointed final judge who delights in fault-finding while exempting himself.

### Discernment vs. the Appetite to Condemn
- **Discernment** is sober, truth-seeking assessment guided by God’s standards, self-knowledge, and charity. It asks: What is true? What is good or harmful? How should I respond wisely? It includes evaluating conduct by revealed moral norms, protecting the vulnerable, correcting error, and choosing associations carefully. It is humble (aware of one’s own need for mercy), patient, and oriented toward restoration where possible. Jesus himself models it; the apostles practice it.
- **The appetite to condemn** is an eager, prideful, often hypocritical hunger to pronounce others guilty, elevate oneself, and withhold forgiveness. It magnifies others’ faults, ignores its own plank (Matthew 7:3–5), treats people as disposable, and pretends private vengeance is justice. This is what Luke 6:37 targets alongside the call to forgive. Condemnation here is not the same as saying “this act is evil” or “this teaching is false”; it is the spirit that says “I stand in God’s place over you, permanently.”

Key practical differences:
- Motives and measure: Discernment aims at truth and (where appropriate) the other’s good; condemnation aims at superiority or revenge. Jesus warns that the measure we use will be used on us.
- Scope and finality: We must make provisional, remedial judgments in this life (family, church, civil, personal decisions). Only God issues the ultimate, eschatological sentence. The verse rebukes aspiring to that role.
- Character: Discernment includes self-examination and readiness to receive correction. The condemning appetite resists it.
- Fruit regarding forgiveness: The same passage immediately stresses forgiveness. Refusing to forgive while gleefully cataloguing others’ sins is the opposite of the grace received.

In short, Luke 6:37 does not ground moral relativism. It restrains the prideful, unforgiving condemner so that judgment can be righteous rather than toxic. Moral distinctions remain indispensable; Jesus presupposes them throughout his teaching. Right judgment (discernment) is required; the appetite to play God is forbidden. The same Christ who says “Judge not” also says “judge with right judgment” and holds people accountable for their lives.
