The difficulty of taking Christ’s command to "love your enemies" seriously stems from a fundamental conflict between human nature (the default setting of self-preservation and reciprocity) and the divine nature being described.

The reason it is often dismissed, and whether it is sentimental, strategic, or moral strength, can be broken down as follows:

### 1. Why is it so difficult to take seriously?

**The Law of Retaliation:**
For most of human history and in our natural psychology, the operating system is *lex talionis* (an eye for an eye). We believe that resentment is a form of justice and that anger is a proper response to being wronged. To forgive or love an enemy feels like a betrayal of justice—as if we are letting them "get away with it."

**The Confusion of Definitions:**
We often equate "love" with "like" or "romantic affection." If you hate someone, being told to "like" them feels not only impossible but fake (sentimental). If we understand love as *agape* (a deliberate will to seek the best for the other), it becomes a disciplined action rather than a feeling, but that distinction is hard to maintain when emotions are high.

**The Perception of Weakness:**
In a survival-of-the-fittest world, loving an enemy looks like suicide. It looks like rolling over and allowing abuse. We fear that if we stop fighting back, we will be destroyed.

### 2. Is it Sentimental? (No)

**Sentimentality** is emotion without substance; it is a feeling that makes *us* feel good but changes nothing. Enemy-love is the opposite of sentimental.

*   **It is Gritty:** The text specifies "pray for them which despitefully use you, and persecute you." This implies the enemies are actively harming you. Sentimentality evaporates when you are actually being persecuted.
*   **It is Active:** It commands "do good." This requires energy, planning, and sacrifice. Sentimentality is passive; true enemy-love is costly.

### 3. Is it Strategic? (Partially)

There is a strategic element to it, often called "moral jiu-jitsu." When someone hits you and you do not hit back, you disrupt their expectations.

*   **Breaking the Cycle:** Resentment creates a loop: I hurt you, you hurt me, I hurt you more. Loving an enemy breaks the loop.
*   **The Prodigal Son Dynamic:** In the parable of the Prodigal Son, the father’s unconditional love shatters the son’s expectations and leads to repentance.
*   **However:** If the *only* reason you love an enemy is to manipulate them into changing, it becomes a form of pride. True enemy-love continues even if the enemy *never* changes.

### 4. Is it a Deeper Form of Moral Strength?

**Yes. This is the core of the teaching.** Matthew 5:45 provides the theological reason: *"That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven."*

Here is why it represents the highest form of strength:

**A. The Ability to Self-Govern:**
Anger is reactive; it means the enemy has control over your emotions. When you love an enemy, you reclaim your agency. You are saying, "You cannot dictate my internal state. I will act according to my values, not your hostility." That is mastery over self.

**B. Imitating the "Unfair" Creator:**
Jesus uses the imagery of the sun and rain. God sends sunshine (a blessing) and rain (a necessity for growth) to *everyone*, regardless of their moral standing.
*   If you only love those who love you, you are acting like a pagan tax collector (as Jesus says earlier in the chapter). That is standard human behavior.
*   To love the unlovable requires a strength that transcends human nature. It requires participating in the character of God.

**C. The Courage to Be Vulnerable:**
It takes more courage to offer a blessing than to throw a punch. A punch is a reflex; a blessing in the face of persecution is a conscious choice to value the *soul* of the persecutor over your own comfort or safety.

### Summary
Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it feels like losing. However, it is not sentimental (it isn't soft), and it is only partially strategic (it isn't just a trick).

It is **Moral Strength** because it requires the strength to:
1.  Resist the biological urge for revenge.
2.  Bear the cost of the other person's sin without passing it on.
3.  Reflect the character of a God who loves impartially.

It is the definitive mark of a "child of the Father"—someone who has the spiritual maturity to love not based on the *worthiness* of the recipient, but based on the *character* of the giver.
