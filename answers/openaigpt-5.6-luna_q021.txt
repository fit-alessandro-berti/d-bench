In Proverbs, pride is more than an excessive feeling of self-importance. It is a false interpretation of reality. The proud person treats a limited, dependent creature as though he were self-sufficient, superior, and entitled to control outcomes. That distortion produces both guilt and blindness.

- **It produces guilt** because self-exaltation is a refusal of creaturely limits and dependence on God. It also tends toward contempt, exploitation, and injustice: if one regards oneself as superior, others become instruments or obstacles.
- **It produces blindness** because pride makes correction seem like insult. The proud person overestimates his wisdom, discounts warnings, explains away failure, and interprets success as proof that his judgment is sound. Pride therefore prevents the repentance that might avert disaster.
- **It produces collapse** because decisions made from an inflated self-estimate are fragile. Pride encourages overreach, isolation from counsel, rivalry, and the neglect of real dangers. Proverbs also understands this collapse as divine judgment: God’s moral order eventually exposes the falsehood on which pride rests.

The repeated word *before* indicates an ordered relationship: the “haughty spirit” is not merely present before the fall in time; it is the inward condition that prepares and helps cause the fall. The external disaster reveals an internal disorder already at work.

“The lowly” provides a significant psychological contrast. The lowly are not necessarily worthless or passive, nor does the proverb praise misery for its own sake. They are those who occupy a humble posture—often socially vulnerable, but also willing to recognize their limits and dependence. Humility gives a person:

- a more truthful view of self,
- openness to instruction and correction,
- solidarity with others rather than contempt for them,
- greater resilience because identity is not built on appearing invulnerable.

Thus the proverb’s comparison is deliberately sharp: it is better to share life with the humble—even without the apparent triumph of “dividing the spoil”—than to enjoy success in the company and spirit of the proud. “Spoil” represents visible gain and victory; “the lowly” represent moral fellowship and a rightly ordered self. The saying does not deny that the proud may prosper for a time. It denies that prosperity is worth the spiritual blindness and eventual instability that pride carries within it.
