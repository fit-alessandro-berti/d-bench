# On Victory and Strength in Romans 12:20-21

## The Definition of Victory

This passage redefines victory fundamentally:
- **Not** as domination or the enemy's destruction
- **But as** preventing evil from reshaping your own character

"Be not overcome of evil" is the primary command. The threat isn't that your enemy will win militarily—it's that *responding in kind* makes you evil too. You've then lost yourself.

## The "Coals of Fire" Question

This phrase creates real interpretive tension:

**If manipulative:** You're performing kindness hoping to make them feel shame/regret (weaponized guilt). The enemy is still the target.

**If genuine:** You're doing good because it's right, and *if* they have a conscience, that confrontation with undeserved kindness might actually change them. But that's their choice, not your leverage.

The ambiguity matters. Paul doesn't say "so that they'll feel terrible"—he says you'll heap coals on their head, leaving the outcome open.

## On "Weakness"

This strategy is **not weak**—it's strategically difficult:
- Requires discipline your enemy may not expect
- Doesn't depend on superior force
- Breaks cycles rather than perpetuating them

But it **is** vulnerable. It offers no guarantee of winning them over. That's not weakness; that's accepting you can't control their response.

**The genuine strength here**: doing right without needing your enemy's transformation to validate it.
