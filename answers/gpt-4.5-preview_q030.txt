This portrait of love from 1 Corinthians emphasizes negations—pride, envy, irritability, suspicion—because genuine love is envisioned as fundamentally opposed to self-centered impulses that undermine relationships. The passage systematically dismantles common distortions of affection, exposing pride ("vaunteth not itself, is not puffed up"), envy ("envieth not"), irritability ("is not easily provoked"), and suspicion ("thinketh no evil") as destructive precisely because they prioritize self-assertion over genuine communion.

By including these negations, Paul presents love as a form of strength rooted not in dominance, but in humility and vulnerability. True strength here is patience ("suffereth long"), generosity of spirit ("is kind"), and steadfastness ("endureth all things"). The vision is one of moral and relational power that quietly endures hardship and misunderstanding without retaliating or retreating into bitterness.

Thus, strength in this passage is paradoxical: it is shown not by the aggressive assertion of one's own rights or reputation, but by a resilient gentleness and trustfulness—enduring all things, believing and hoping all things. In other words, true love is strong precisely because it refuses to surrender to the self-defensive impulses that fragment human relationships, instead persistently pursuing truth, kindness, and unity.
