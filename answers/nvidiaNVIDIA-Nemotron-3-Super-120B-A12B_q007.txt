

**Short answer**

Luke 6:37 is not a blanket denial that moral judgments ever have any value.  
It warns against **the personal, self‑serving impulse to condemn other people** (the “appetite to condemn”) while leaving room for **discernment**—the honest, loving effort to tell what is right from what is wrong, especially when it protects the community or restores the sinner. In other words, the verse distinguishes **judgment‑as‑punishment** (which Jesus forbids) from **judgment‑as‑discernment** (which the Scripture elsewhere affirms and even commands).

Below is a step‑by‑step unpacking of why the saying is different from the claim “moral judgments never matter,” followed with a clearer picture of the two concepts—*discernment* and *the appetite to condemn*—and how they relate to the broader biblical ethic.

---

## 1. What Luke 6:37 Actually Says

> **“Judge not, and ye shall not be judged: condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned: forgive, and ye shall be forgiven.”** (KJV)

| Phrase | Literal force | Typical NT usage |
|--------|---------------|------------------|
| **Judge not** (μὴ κρίνετε) | *Do not sit in the seat of a judge who passes a binding, punitive verdict on another.* | Correlates with Matt 7:1‑2; Romans 14:10‑13; James 4:11‑12. |
| **Condemn not** (μὴ καταδικάζετε) | *Do not actively seek to declare someone guilty and worthy of punishment.* | echoes the same passages; also 1 Cor 4:5 (“Therefore judge nothing before the appointed time…”) |
| **Forgive** (ἀφίετε) | *Extend the same mercy you hope to receive.* | Mirrors the “forgive as you have been forgiven” motif (Matt 6:12, 14‑15; Eph 4:32). |

The three imperatives form a **trio**: *avoid passing punitive judgment, avoid the desire to condemn, and practice forgiveness.* The verse is therefore **prohibitive**—it tells us what *not* to do—not a declarative statement that *all* judgment is meaningless.

---

## 2. Why This Is Not the Same as “Moral Judgments Never Matter”

| Claim | What it asserts | How Luke 6:37 relates |
|-------|----------------|-----------------------|
| **“Moral judgments never matter.”** | *Any act of telling right from wrong, announcing guilt, or declaring innocence is pointless or even sinful.* | Luke 6:37 **does not** say that. It only forbids a *certain kind* of judgment—judgment that seeks to **punish** or **exclude**—while leaving intact the possibility of judgment that seeks to **heal**, **guide**, or **protect**. |
| **Luke 6:37** | *Do not take on the role of a condemning judge; do not nurture a desire to see others punished; instead, forgive.* | It acknowledges that **judgment can be proper** when it is *restorative* (e.g., church discipline, parental correction, prophetic rebuke) but **improper** when it is *retributive* or *self‑aggrandizing*. |

Thus, the verse distinguishes **two moral‑judgment modalities**:

1. **Condemnatory/judgmental** (the “appetite to condemn”) – prohibited.
2. **Discerning/judicial** (the act of distinguishing right from wrong for the sake of love, truth, or community health) – permitted, even commanded elsewhere.

---

## 3. Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn

| Aspect | Discernment (healthy judgment) | Appetite to Condemn (unhealthy judgment) |
|--------|--------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|
| **Motive** | Love for the other, desire for their good, protection of the community, obedience to God’s truth. | Pride, self‑justification, desire to see the other suffer, satisfaction in feeling morally superior. |
| **Goal** | Restoration, correction, edification, clarification of truth. | Punishment, exclusion, vindication, affirmation of one’s own status. |
| **Biblical examples** | • Nathan confronting David (2 Sam 12). <br>• Paul’s instruction to “judge nothing before the appointed time” (1 Cor 4:5) *in the sense of not prematurely condemning*, while still urging the church to “examine yourselves” (2 Cor 13:5). <br>• Jesus telling the adulterous woman, “Go and sin no more” (John 8:11) after refusing to condemn her to death. | • The Pharisees seeking to stone the adulterous woman (John 8:3‑5). <br>• Korah’s rebellion against Moses, claiming moral superiority (Numbers 16). <br>• The early church’s conflict over “judging” others’ food practices (Romans 14) when the motive was to elevate one’s own observance over another’s liberty. |
| **Outcome** | Growth, repentance, deeper unity. | Alienation, hardened hearts, self‑righteousness. |
| **Scriptural warrant** | • “Judge not according to appearance, but judge with righteous judgment” (John 7:24). <br>• “Test everything; hold fast what is good” (1 Thess 5:21). <br>• “Let the word of Christ dwell in you richly… teaching and admonishing one another” (Col 3:16). | • “Do not judge, or you too will be judged” (Matt 7:1‑2). <br>• “Why do you look at the speck in your brother’s eye…?” (Matt 7:3‑5). <br>• “Let no corrupting talk come out of your mouths… but only what is good for building up” (Eph 4:29). |

**Key theological point:**  
The New Testament repeatedly permits—and even commands—the *evaluation* of doctrine, behavior, and character **when the purpose is edification**. What it forbids is the *evaluation* that seeks to **declare guilt and impose punishment** without love, mercy, or a view toward restoration.

---

## 4. How the Two Concepts Play Out in the Larger Biblical Narrative

| Biblical Theme | Discernment (allowed) | Appetite to Condemn (forbidden) |
|----------------|----------------------|---------------------------------|
| **Law & Prophets** | Prophets *declare* sin to call Israel back to covenant (e.g., Isaiah 1:2‑4). They do so with tears, hoping for repentance. | Prophets *pronounce* doom on nations **without** offering a chance for repentance (e.g., the “woe” oracles that are purely declarative of impending judgment). |
| **Jesus’ Ministry** | Jesus *exposes* hypocrisy (Matt 23) to bring the Pharisees to self‑examination. He also *forgives* the woman caught in adultery (John 8) after refusing to condemn her to death. | Jesus *rebukes* the desire to call down fire on a Samaritan village (Luke 9:54‑56) because the motive was vindictive, not redemptive. |
| **Apostolic Teaching** | Paul tells the Corinthians to “judge nothing before the appointed time” (1 Cor 4:5) **concerning** motives, while still urging them to “examine yourselves” (2 Cor 13:5) to see if they are in the faith. | Paul warns against “judging your brother” (Rom 14:10) when the judgment is about *disputable matters* (food, days) motivated by personal superiority rather than love for the weak. |
| **Church Discipline** | Matthew 18:15‑17 outlines a process: private confrontation, then witnesses, then church involvement—aimed at **restoration** (“if he listens, you have gained your brother”). | If the process is used merely to **expel** someone without hope of return, it becomes condemnation rather than discipline. |

Thus, the biblical ethic consistently draws a line: **judgment that seeks the other's good is permissible; judgment that seeks the other's ruin is not.**

---

## 5. Practical Take‑aways for Today

1. **Check your motive before you speak.**  
   - Are you saying something because you want the other to grow, repent, or be protected? → *Discernment* (allowed).  
   - Are you saying something because you feel superior, want to see them suffer, or enjoy the feeling of being “right”? → *Appetite to condemn* (forbidden).

2. **Distinguish the *content* of your judgment from its *purpose*.**  
   - The *content* (“this behavior is sinful”) may be true.  
   - The *purpose* (to restore vs. to punish) decides whether it aligns with Luke 6:37.

3. **Employ the “forgive as you have been forgiven” rhythm.**  
   - After a corrective word, follow it with an offer of grace, prayer, and tangible support—mirroring the divine pattern seen in Jesus’ dealings with the adulterous woman.

4. **Remember the corporate dimension.**  
   - Discernment often functions for the *body* (e.g., protecting the flock from false teaching). Condemnation tends to fracture the body, creating “us vs. them” mentalities.

5. **Leave ultimate judgment to God.**  
   - While we may exercise *discerning* judgment in the present age (church discipline, parental correction, ethical discernment), the *final* vindicatory or condemnatory verdict belongs to the Lord alone (Romans 12:19; James 4:12).

## 7. Bottom Line

- **Luke 6:37** does **not** claim that *all* moral judgments are irrelevant.  
- It **rejects** the *appetite to condemn*—the self‑serving, punitive desire to declare someone guilty and see them suffer.  
- It **affirms** (by implication) the legitimacy of *discernment*—the loving, truthful effort to distinguish right from wrong for the sake of the other's good, the community's health, and fidelity to God.  

Thus, the verse invites us to exercise **judgment that heals**, while cautioning us against **judgment that harms**—a distinction that is central to the whole New Testament ethic of truth spoken in love.
