Galatians 6:1 requires a posture of **humble, restorative correction** rather than punitive, superior, or contemptuous correction.

Paul does not say, “Ignore the fault.” A real “fault” has occurred. But neither does he say, “Expose, crush, or shame the person.” He says, **restore** him. The goal of correction is not to win a moral victory over someone, but to help a brother or sister be made whole again.

The three ideas belong together because each protects the others from corruption.

**Restoration** gives correction its aim.
The person is not treated as an enemy, a spectacle, or a case study in failure. He is someone “overtaken” — caught, trapped, overcome — and therefore someone to be recovered. The verb translated “restore” can carry the sense of mending, setting right, putting back into proper condition. Correction, then, is medicinal before it is judicial. It seeks healing.

**Meekness** gives correction its manner.
The “spiritual” person is not the harsh person, the triumphant person, or the person eager to rebuke. In Galatians, spirituality is tied to the fruit of the Spirit: love, patience, gentleness, self-control. So the one who corrects must embody the very Spirit by whom restoration is possible. Meekness does not mean moral softness; it means strength without self-exaltation. It is truth spoken without cruelty.

**Self-suspicion** gives correction its humility.
“Considering thyself” means the corrector must remember his own vulnerability. Paul does not allow the restorer to stand above the fallen person as though he belongs to a different moral species. The one correcting is also temptable. He may be tempted by the same sin, or by a different sin that often accompanies correction: pride, harshness, impatience, contempt, delight in another’s exposure.

So restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion are kept together because correction is spiritually dangerous. The act of correcting another person can itself become a temptation. One may begin by addressing sin and end by committing another form of sin in the process.

Paul’s moral posture is therefore something like this:

> Correct as one sinner helping another sinner back toward God, not as a superior inspecting an inferior.

The “spiritual” person is not someone who has outgrown weakness, but someone governed by the Spirit enough to deal with another’s weakness gently. True correction requires moral clarity, but also brotherly solidarity: “This person has fallen; I too could fall. Therefore I must seek his restoration, not my own vindication.”
