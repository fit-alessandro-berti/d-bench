This sentence, drawn from the biblical narrative of Jesus and the woman caught in adultery, is indeed morally destabilizing because it fundamentally attacks the human ego’s claim to moral superiority. 

While most answers fall within the categories you listed (hypocrisy, mob-justice, judgment), the text functions at a psychological and theological level that makes those categories impossible to fulfill without self-deception.

Here is an analysis of why this specific sentence creates such deep moral turbulence:

### 1. It turns the moral mirror inward (Hypocrisy)
While "exposing hypocrisy" is certainly part of it, Jesus’ statement goes deeper than just shame; **it exposes ontological impossibility.** 
A common defense for judgmenters is that they are acting from a place of righteousness—a need to uphold God's law. By stating, *"He that is without sin among you,"* Jesus places the burden of judgment on perfectibility. Since *no one* can be defined as "without sin" in the eyes of divine justice (if we look at the broader New Testament context regarding the Law given through Moses), the accusers realize they cannot fulfill the condition to even begin casting stones. Their own humanity is revealed as flawed before they even speak a single word. This isn't just about hypocrisy; it's about the realization that **you cannot separate yourself from the sin you condemn in others.**

### 2. It interrupts Mob-Rightseousness
This sentence physically stops violence because of the sheer impossibility of executing judgment *upon oneself* as a prerequisite. 
By demanding this first stone be cast, Jesus doesn't necessarily say "Don't judge," but rather "**Judge only if you can stand above me.**" For anyone who has sinned—meaning anyone in the room—they cannot satisfy that requirement. It dissolves the mob not because of legal authority (like a prosecutor's warrant), but because it removes the collective standing necessary to justify their own aggression. The "mob-righteousness" is destabilized when they realize they are, at this very moment, the sinners being judged.

### 3. It Forbids Self-Accusation
The sentence creates a paradox: You can't throw the first stone because you yourself have sin. 
This doesn't mean judgment is forbidden entirely—it refers to the *mob* casting their own law. Jesus establishes a crucial distinction here: **God's judgment is different from man's.** Jesus does not give a commandment to refrain from judging in principle, but He creates a standard that is too high for humanity to meet. It forbids **human** self-righteous judgment, which relies on an identity of moral purity.

### 4. Something Deeper: Grace vs. Law
The most destabilizing aspect of the sentence is that it points toward the core of Jesus' mission in the Gospel—not as a reformer of laws, but as a liberator from the pressure to be "good." 
This forces a profound recognition: **Sin separates us from God's judgment.** The "destabilization" occurs because the accusers (and the reader) are forced to confront the truth that they have no standing before Him. They leave the scene without killing the woman, not because Jesus gave her a life permit, but because they lost their **moral license** to act. 

### Summary
Jesus' statement destabilizes morality by stripping it of the foundation that allows for human superiority over others. It reveals that if we are capable of judging *others* (as in this scene), we must be perfect ourselves—a state humanity is incapable of maintaining without divine grace. The "something deeper" is that **we have no right to cast our stones, and God forgives us with a measure greater than the one He demands from them.** 

The accusers' departure one by one—from the eldest first—signals a moral awakening where pride was too great and humility required, or perhaps simply, they were terrified of being judged for their own sins.
