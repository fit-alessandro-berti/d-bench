### Analysis of the Elder Brother's Deepest Problem in Luke 15:28–32

The passage you quoted is from the Parable of the Prodigal Son (Luke 15:11–32), a story Jesus tells to illustrate God's boundless mercy and the human struggles with grace. In this climactic section, the focus shifts to the elder brother, who reacts negatively to the father's joyful welcome of the repentant younger son (the "prodigal"). The elder brother's response reveals layers of emotional and spiritual tension, and your question probes what lies at the heart of his issue: anger, envy, wounded fairness, pride in obedience, or discomfort with mercy itself.

To unpack this, let's first briefly contextualize the parable and then examine the elder brother's words and attitude. I'll draw directly from the text and common biblical interpretations (e.g., from scholars like N.T. Wright or Kenneth Bailey, who emphasize cultural and theological nuances in the story). Finally, I'll address which of these options seems to be his "deepest" problem.

#### Quick Context of the Parable
- The younger son demands his inheritance, squanders it in reckless living, hits rock bottom, and returns home repentant.
- The father (representing God) runs to embrace him, restores him fully, and throws a lavish feast—symbolizing grace and celebration over a "lost" soul found.
- The elder son, who's been dutifully working in the fields, hears the party and refuses to join, highlighting a contrast in responses to mercy.

This parable is part of a trio (Lost Sheep, Lost Coin, Lost Son) aimed at the Pharisees and scribes, who grumbled about Jesus associating with sinners (Luke 15:1–2). The elder brother embodies their self-righteous attitude.

#### The Elder Brother's Attitude: Breaking Down the Options
The elder brother's complaint is raw and revealing: He emphasizes his long-term obedience ("these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment") and contrasts it with the younger son's wastefulness ("devoured thy living with harlots"). He feels overlooked—no "kid" (young goat) for his own celebration—while the prodigal gets the "fatted calf." The father's gentle response underscores relationship and joy over the lost being found.

Let's evaluate the options based on the text:

- **Anger**: This is the most immediate and surface-level issue. The text explicitly says, "he was angry, and would not go in." His refusal to enter the feast and his sharp words to the father show resentment boiling over. However, anger is more of a symptom than the root cause—it's how his deeper frustrations manifest.

- **Envy**: There's a clear jealous streak here. He envies the attention and celebration given to his brother, despite the prodigal's failures. He fixates on what the younger son receives (the feast) versus what he lacks (even a small party with friends). Envy drives his complaint, but it's tied to a sense of comparison and competition, not necessarily the core problem.

- **Wounded Fairness**: This is prominent in his logic. He appeals to justice and equity: "I've been loyal and obedient—why does he get rewarded for messing up?" In the cultural context of first-century Judaism, inheritance and family honor were big deals, and the elder son might feel the father's mercy disrupts a fair system of merit (e.g., the older brother traditionally gets a double portion). This "wounded fairness" feels like entitlement, but it's still somewhat superficial—it's his rationale for being upset, not the underlying worldview.

- **Pride in Obedience**: Here's where it gets deeper. The elder brother boasts about his track record: unbroken service, no transgressions. He views himself as the "good" son, earning his place through duty. This pride turns obedience into a transaction ("I've done X, so you owe me Y"). It's a legalistic mindset, common in religious contexts, where faithfulness becomes a point of superiority rather than humble devotion. This pride blinds him to the father's ongoing generosity ("Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine")—he's always had access to the father's blessings but doesn't appreciate them.

- **Discomfort with Mercy Itself**: This strikes at the deepest level. The elder brother's real issue isn't just personal slights; it's a fundamental resistance to the father's grace. He can't stomach the idea that someone undeserving (the "lost" brother) receives unearned forgiveness and joy. By calling him "this thy son" (distancing language, not "my brother"), he rejects the relational restoration the father celebrates. The parable's punchline—"this thy brother was dead, and is alive again; and was lost, and is found"—highlights mercy as the heart of the story, but the elder son wants no part of it. This mirrors the Pharisees' discomfort with Jesus' ministry to tax collectors and sinners: mercy offends their sense of moral order.

#### What Is the Deepest Problem?
Based on the text and the parable's theological purpose, I believe the elder brother's **deepest problem is discomfort with mercy itself**. Here's why:

- All the other issues (anger, envy, wounded fairness, pride) flow from this core resistance. His pride in obedience makes mercy seem unjust; his sense of fairness is wounded because mercy doesn't operate on merit; envy and anger erupt because mercy celebrates the "unworthy." But at the root, he fails to grasp the father's heart—a love that rejoices more over the repentant lost than the steadfast "found" (as Jesus explains in the earlier parables of the chapter: "There is joy in the presence of the angels of God over one sinner that repenteth," Luke 15:10).

- The father's response invites the elder son into this mercy: "It was meet that we should make merry, and be glad." It's an appeal to join the celebration, not a rebuke of his obedience. Yet the brother remains outside, symbolizing how discomfort with grace can isolate us from God's joy and community.

- Theologically, this aligns with Jesus' message: Grace isn't earned; it's freely given. The elder brother's mindset represents a "works-based" religion that resents God's extravagance toward the broken. As scholar Kenneth Bailey notes in his cultural analysis, the elder son's refusal to enter the feast is a public insult to the father, underscoring how his anti-mercy stance damages relationships.

That said, interpretations can vary. Some might argue pride in obedience is equally deep, as it fuels the anti-mercy attitude (e.g., it's a form of self-righteousness). Others see it as a mix. If we view the parable as a mirror for self-reflection, the "deepest" problem might depend on the reader—what resonates as the heart issue?
