The elder brother’s **deepest problem** is **discomfort with mercy itself**—manifested primarily through **wounded fairness** and **pride in obedience**. Here's the breakdown:

1. **Wounded Fairness**:  
   The elder brother feels entitled to recognition and reward for his lifelong loyalty and service. He believes his obedience has been unquestionable ("I served thee for many years... never transgressed thy commandments"), yet his father prioritizes the prodigal son’s return over his own sacrifices. This perceived injustice wounds his sense of fairness, making him resentful that his efforts are dismissed.

2. **Pride in Obedience**:  
   His identity is tied to being the "good son" who adheres strictly to rules. The father’s celebration of the prodigal son—a figure he views as rebellious—threatens this self-image. He cannot reconcile his rigid adherence to duty with the father’s radical mercy, which seems to invalidate his life’s work.

3. **Anger and Envy (Secondary)**:  
   While anger and envy surface (he resents the son’s reward), these emotions stem from deeper wounds. His anger is not just about being excluded but about feeling unappreciated and misunderstood. Envy arises from comparing himself to his brother’s perceived "freedom," but this is secondary to his core struggle with the father’s values.

4. **Discomfort with Mercy**:  
   The elder brother cannot grasp a world where grace supersedes strict justice. His worldview equates worthiness with merit, making it hard to accept unconditional forgiveness. The father’s actions force him to confront a reality where love is not transactional—a concept he resists.

**Conclusion**: The elder brother’s resistance is rooted in **wounded fairness** (feeling his loyalty is unrecognized) and **pride in obedience** (needing validation through rule-following). These intertwine to create his inability to rejoice in his brother’s redemption, revealing a heart more concerned with justice than grace. The parable critiques legalism and highlights the tension between human notions of fairness and divine mercy.
