To understand the elder brother's deepest problem, let's analyze his response and the context provided by the parable.

1. **Initial Reaction**: The elder brother is "angry, and would not go in" to the celebration (Luke 15:28). This indicates a strong negative emotional response to the festivities for the younger son.

2. **Complaint to the Father**: He lists his grievances to his father:
   - He has served his father faithfully for many years without ever transgressing his commandment.
   - Despite his faithful service, he was never given a kid to celebrate with his friends.
   - The return of his brother, who squandered his inheritance on harlots, is met with a grand celebration, including the killing of the fatted calf.

3. **Analysis of the Complaint**:
   - **Anger and Envy**: The elder brother is clearly angry, and there's an element of envy in his resentment towards the attention and celebration given to his younger brother.
   - **Wounded Fairness**: He feels that it's unfair that he was never given a similar celebration. His perception is that his faithful service was overlooked, while his brother's return is lavishly celebrated despite his past actions.
   - **Pride in Obedience**: The elder brother takes pride in his obedience and service to his father, which he contrasts with his brother's disobedience. This pride seems to be a significant part of his identity.
   - **Discomfort with Mercy**: The father's merciful response to the younger son's return is not appreciated by the elder brother. He seems uncomfortable with, or perhaps unable to understand, why his father would so readily forgive and celebrate his brother's return without demanding further penance or restitution.

4. **The Deepest Problem**:
The father's response to the elder brother ("Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine") addresses the issue of fairness and possession directly. It suggests that the elder brother already has a share in everything his father has, implying that his grievance is based on a misunderstanding or misperception of his status and the nature of his relationship with his father.

The elder brother's deepest problem seems to be a **discomfort with mercy itself** and a pride in his own obedience that blinds him to the grace extended to his brother. His issue isn't merely with the celebration but with the principle behind it—that his brother, despite his wrongdoing, is welcomed back without condition. This discomfort reveals a heart issue, suggesting that the elder brother struggles with understanding or embracing the father's merciful character.

Furthermore, the elder brother's self-righteousness ("Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment") indicates a legalistic or transactional view of his relationship with his father, rather than a relational one based on love and grace. This perspective makes it difficult for him to appreciate the father's joy in the restoration of his lost son.

In summary, while the elder brother exhibits anger, envy, and a sense of wounded fairness, his deepest problem lies in his discomfort with the merciful and gracious nature of his father, as well as his pride in his own obedience, which together prevent him from rejoicing in his brother's restoration.
