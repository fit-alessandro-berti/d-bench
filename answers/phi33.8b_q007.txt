The Bible verse Luke 6:37 encourages us to practice non-judgmental kindness and forgiveness, with the understanding that such behavior reciprocates with personal absolution, as opposed to advocating that moral judgments and moral condemnation have no relevance. The verse does not suggest that moral judgments or condemnations are irrelevant or inapplicable - in fact, it promotes a higher level of understanding where forgiveness and non-judgment play a critical role.


It essentially advises against hastily passing judgments or condemnations on others without fully understanding their circumstances. Instead, it implores us to exercise empathy, discretion, and judicious thinking before passing moral judgments or making condemnatory remarks. It does not say morals or actions are irrelevant; rather, it encourages us to consider the situation, context, and intent behind people'thy actions before meting out judgement.


By choosing not to judge prematurely, it doesn't mean we ignore morality. It highlights our moral imperative to forgive while emphasizing the importance of not condemning others without thorough consideration. This approach helps maintain harmony and fosters an understanding environment because it allows for growth, change, and redemption. 


In the context of this verse, judgment should ideally come from a place of empathy and understanding, not from a desire to condemn. Therefore, it's crucial to differentiate discernment (thorough evaluation of moral scenarios) which helps form a fair judgment, from the impulse to condemn, which could be driven by anger or ego.


So, while moral judgments have their place, Luke 6:37 reminds us of the need for humility, kindness, understanding, and the healing power of forgiveness in our moral and societal interactions. 


To put it into perspective, the verse is offering a guiding principle on how to make moral decisions. It suggests that in our daily interchange, we should refrain from hastily rendering judgments (negative conclusions about someone's character or actions based on limited observation) and choosing to condemn (make negative personal resolution against someone) without understanding their situation or context. It isn't about ignoring the presence of wrongdoing but responding to it with empathy and wisdom, emphasizing forgiveness. The verse acknowledges the importance of judgement and condemnation when done rightly but strongly discourages rash, unfair condemnation and judgment. It encourages us always to examine others' actions critically while promoting compassion, understanding, and forgiveness. We should strive for an approach that balances fairness and love when navigating the moral gray areas, urging us to seek wisdom, as per Proverbs 1:5, which encourages us to seek understanding before passing moral judgments.


Thus, Luke 6:37 isn't proposing moral judgments don't matter. It's advocating for a careful, understanding, and empathetic response when faced with moral gray areas, which may demand condemnation or condemnation and seeking forgiveness and mercy when the time calls for it. This can be interpreted as a call to find the golden mean in moral issues that requires a balance of righteousness and compassion. It doesn't negate the need for self-reflection, uprightness, and accountability. It's more of a guideline to help in navigating morality's gray area, pushing us towards humane and understanding responses while advocating accountability or condemnation when necessary.


Let's take another angle into this: 

While you can't ignore wrongdoings, the verse reminds us not to ignore the individual's human condition or their context before condemning someone outright. Wrongdoings can often stem from ignorance, desperation, fear, or a flawed upbringing – elements that call for understanding, guidance, rebuke, and reformation rather than outright condemnation. This doesn't mean turning a blind eye to moral evils. However, it does mean approaching condemnation from a perspective of mercy and compassion rather than just punitive justice. The call for forgiveness it advises comes with the understanding that wrongdoing often breeds further transgressions if not addressed appropriately. 


Furthermore, the quote does not mandate absolute innocence of all, meaning there could be moral indiscretions that aren't forgivable. The verse can be even misused to shield the perpetrators of wrongdoings, which makes it undeserving of condemnation, reinforcing harmful actions. However, genuine repentance and changes, coupled with forgiveness and mercy show an individual's potential for reformation - offering them a second chance.


In summary, Luke 6:37 isn't an utter indifference towards morality or dismissive of wrong. But it does suggest a more empathetic, understanding, and forgiving approach when dealing with moral issues rather than condemnation and judgment, which only serves to perpetuate anguish and further transgressions.

Avoid condemning and seeking justice isn't to deny or ignore moral breaches. It's about a comprehensive approach where the objective is rehabilitation and transformation, considering the human element's complexities while upholding justice and righteousness. It's about balancing the scales of judgment and compassion; holding individuals accountable where they break societal or ethical codes without losing sight of their humanity and the potential for betterment. So, instead of blanket condemnations and unfair judgments, seeking reconciliation, corrective actions, and pardon foster an environment of trust and growth.
