This passage defines victory not as defeating the enemy, but as **refusing to become like them**.

Romansas 12:19 sets the table: “Do not avenge yourselves… leave room for God’s wrath.” The victory in verses 20–21 is therefore **active, not passive**: you counter evil with its opposite—food for hunger, drink for thirst, kindness for hostility. The command “Be not overcome of evil” means evil wins if it pulls you into retaliation, bitterness, or mirroring violence. You “overcome evil with good” by breaking that reproductive cycle. You maintain moral agency; the enemy no longer dictates your actions.

### Is it weak?
Only if strength is measured by the capacity to hurt. By that standard, feeding an enemy looks like surrender. But the text demands far more discipline than retaliation does. Retaliation is reflexive; kindness to an enemy requires self-mastery, emotional control, and a refusal to let hatred govern you. In this framework, weakness is being *overcome*—allowing evil to colonize your own heart and behavior. The command is therefore **radically strong**, though its strength is internal and moral rather than coercive.

### Is it manipulative?
That concern usually centers on “heaping coals of fire on his head” (quoting Proverbs 25:21–22).

- If you feed someone **solely** to make them feel guilty or to trigger divine punishment on them, then yes, the kindness becomes a disguised weapon—psychological manipulation using charity as bait.
- But the text commands genuine care: *feed him, give him drink*. The “coals” are best understood as a proverbial result, not a hidden agenda. In context, Paul has just forbidden personal vengeance; the image likely means either (1) your kindness provokes shame/remorse that can lead to repentance, or (2) you are leaving justice to God (the “coals” being His, not yours to administer).

The point is not strategic humiliation but **freedom from the need to win by destroying the other**. If you do good genuinely, the “coals” happen in the enemy’s conscience or in God’s justice—not in your calculated scheme.

### Conclusion
The passage is **genuinely strong**. Victory is defined as the integrity of good refusing to be corrupted by evil. It is not weakness because it takes greater power to love an enemy than to strike him. It is not manipulation provided the kindness is real; the “coals” describe a consequence (shame, repentance, or divine judgment left to God), not the motive for the meal. The command asserts that good is more formidable than evil because it alone can stop evil’s replication.
