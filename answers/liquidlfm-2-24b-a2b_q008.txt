This verse from **1 John 4:20** is one of the most profound statements about Christian ethics and theology. Let's break down its meaning and answer your question step by step.

---

### **1. The Core Statement:**

> “If a man say, I love God, and hateth his brother, he is a liar: for he that loveth not his brother whom he hath seen, how can he love God whom he hath not seen?”

At first glance, this may seem harsh or confusing. But it's rooted in deep theological and ethical insight.

---

### **2. Why Does It Treat Love of the Neighbor as a Test of Love of God?**

The verse connects **love for God** with **love for one’s neighbor** (specifically, a fellow believer). Here's why:

- In the Bible, especially in the New Testament, **love for God** is inseparable from **love for others**. Jesus says:
  > “Love the Lord your God with all your heart… and love your neighbor as yourself.” (Matthew 22:37–39)

- In 1 John, the apostle is addressing believers who claim to love God but may be failing to live out that love in practical ways—especially toward other believers.

- The phrase “his brother whom he hath seen” refers to a **visible, tangible person**—a fellow Christian. If someone claims to love God but shows hatred or indifference toward another believer, they are contradicting themselves.

- Therefore, **love of God is not just a private, internal feeling**—it must be expressed outwardly through **actions toward others**, especially those who are part of the same faith community.

So, the verse says:  
> If you truly love God, you will love those you see—your brothers and sisters in Christ. If you don’t, then your claim to love God is insincere.

---

### **3. What Kind of Self-Deception Is It Exposing?**

This verse exposes a form of **self-deception** known as **hypocrisy** or **spiritual pride**. Specifically:

- **Spiritual pride**: A person may believe they are spiritually mature because they have a strong relationship with God, yet they fail to show love and kindness to others—especially within the church.

- **Denial of reality**: The person may convince themselves that their love for God is real, even while treating others poorly. They might say, “I’m spiritual,” or “I have a personal relationship with God,” while ignoring the needs of those around them.

- **False belief in separation**: Some people think that if they have a personal experience with God, they are exempt from needing to love others. But the Bible teaches that **true love for God produces love for others**.

- **Moral blindness**: The person may not even realize they are being hypocritical. They may genuinely believe they are serving God, when in fact they are failing to reflect His love.

---

### **4. Summary Answer:**

The verse treats love of the visible neighbor as a test of love of the invisible God because **genuine love for God must necessarily express itself in love for others**, especially fellow believers. It exposes **self-deception** in the form of **hypocrisy**, where individuals claim a deep spiritual relationship with God while failing to demonstrate that love in their actions toward others. This contradiction reveals a lack of true spiritual transformation and a failure to embody the teachings of Christ.

---

### **Bonus Insight:**

This idea is echoed in other parts of Scripture, such as:
- **James 2:14–26** – Faith without works is dead.
- **1 Corinthians 13** – Love is the greatest virtue and the foundation of all Christian life.
- **John 13:34–35** – Jesus commands us to love one another as He has loved us.

In short, **love of God is proven and defined by love of neighbor**.
