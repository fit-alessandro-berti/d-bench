In **Genesis 4:6–7**, evil is depicted vividly as something external yet intimately personal—like a predator or a beast crouching at the threshold ("sin lieth at the door"). This portrayal strongly suggests evil as an ever-present temptation or impulse, almost animalistic in nature, waiting for an opportunity to overpower Cain. However, the crucial point of moral agency here is that Cain is not helpless before this evil impulse; instead, the responsibility clearly rests upon his shoulders.

The text emphasizes Cain's accountability by highlighting his freedom to choose: "If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?" implies moral choice and a reward for righteous behavior. Conversely, if he chooses poorly, the beastly sin is ready to dominate him. But importantly, the scripture underscores Cain’s ability and moral obligation to master or rule over this evil impulse: "thou shalt rule over him."

Thus, the picture painted by this text is one where:

* **Evil**: Portrayed as an external yet personal force—an impulse or temptation that one must actively resist.
* **Moral Agency**: Clearly lies with the individual; Cain is presented with full awareness and the capacity to choose good over evil.
* **Responsibility**: The individual is accountable for actively mastering and controlling the impulse toward sin.

In essence, Genesis 4:6–7 depicts moral life as a continuous struggle with temptation, in which humans are fully responsible for their actions, empowered with the agency and obligation to overcome evil impulses through conscious choice.
