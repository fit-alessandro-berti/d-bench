This parable is one of the most psychologically acute and theologically dense passages in the Gospels. To answer your question precisely, we must distinguish between the *root* sin and its *manifestations*.

### 1. What Condemns the Pharisee?

While all the options you listed are present in the text, they are not of equal weight. They form a hierarchy where **Pride (Self-Righteousness)** is the root, and the others are the fruit.

**The Verdict:** The Pharisee is condemned primarily by **Pride**, specifically manifested as **Self-Righteousness**.

Here is how the specific options break down:

*   **False Doctrine?** *No, not strictly.* The Pharisee’s theology was not heretical. He acknowledged God. He acknowledged the law (tithing, fasting). He did not deny sin (he acknowledged others were extortioners). His error was not in *what* he believed about God, but in *how* he understood his own standing before God. He believed he could merit righteousness through performance rather than receive it through grace.
*   **Comparison?** *Yes, but secondary.* Comparison is the mechanism he used to feed his pride. By saying, "I am not as other men," he established a hierarchy where he was superior. Comparison is the tool, but pride is the hand that holds it.
*   **Contempt?** *Yes, but secondary.* Verse 9 explicitly states the audience "despised others." The Pharisee’s contempt for the publican is the social consequence of his spiritual pride. You cannot despise others without first elevating yourself.
*   **Virtue as Self-Display?** *Yes, but incomplete.* It is not merely that he displayed virtue; it is that he treated virtue as a **currency**. He brought his good deeds to the temple not as a response to grace, but as a transaction to secure justification. He thanked God *for* his own goodness, rather than thanking God *for* grace.

**The Core Condemnation:**
The Pharisee is condemned because he sought **justification by works** rather than **justification by mercy**.
*   **The Prayer:** He prayed "with himself." The content of his prayer was a monologue of self-congratulation disguised as worship.
*   **The Theology:** He assumed the role of the Judge. By listing his own merits, he implicitly told God, "I have earned this."
*   **The Result:** He left the temple *unjustified* because he refused to admit he needed justification. As long as you believe you are righteous, you cannot receive righteousness.

### 2. Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?

The tax collector (publican) is not justified because he is "nicer" or because he has a "better attitude." He is justified because his posture reflects **ontological reality** and **soteriological truth**.

**A. Physical Posture Reflects Spiritual Reality**
*   **"Standing afar off":** In Jewish ritual, distance indicated unworthiness to approach the Holy. The tax collector physically enacted his spiritual reality: he recognized a gap between himself and God. The Pharisee stood close, assuming no gap existed.
*   **"Would not lift up so much as his eyes":** This signifies shame. To look God in the eye while standing in sin is an act of presumption. The tax collector acknowledged his unworthiness to gaze upon the Divine.
*   **"Smote upon his breast":** This was a gesture of deep remorse and self-condemnation. It was a physical manifestation of conviction.

**B. The Content of the Prayer**
The tax collector offers no defense, no list of works, and no comparison to others.
*   **"God be merciful":** He appeals to God's nature (mercy), not his own merit.
*   **"To me a sinner":** He agrees with the verdict of the Law. He does not argue; he confesses.

**Why is this "truer"?**
It is truer because it aligns with the truth of the human condition. Every person stands before God as a debtor.
*   The Pharisee’s prayer was a **lie** (even if his deeds were factually true, his claim of *righteousness* was false).
*   The Tax Collector’s prayer was the **truth** (he was a sinner; he needed mercy).

### Summary

The Pharisee is condemned not because he was a bad man in the eyes of society (he was likely a pillar of the community), but because he was **spiritually blind to his own need**. He trusted in himself (v.9).

The tax collector is justified not because he was a "good sinner," but because he was **honest about his need**.

The parable concludes with the principle of the Kingdom: **"For everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, but he who humbles himself will be exalted."**

*   The Pharisee tried to exalt himself through works; God humbled him by leaving him unjustified.
*   The tax collector humbled himself through confession; God exalted him by declaring him justified.

**In short:** The Pharisee is condemned by **Self-Trust**; the Tax Collector is saved by **Self-Abandonment**.
