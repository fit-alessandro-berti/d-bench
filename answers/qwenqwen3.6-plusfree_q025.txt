Based on the text and its immediate context, **the moral danger lies not in visibility itself, but in the motive: the craving to be seen and praised by others.** Here's why:

### 1. The Text Explicitly Targets Motive, Not Location
The key phrase in Matthew 6:1 is `"to be seen of them"` (Greek: `πρὸς τὸ θεαθῆναι αὐτοῖς`), which literally means `"in order to be seen by them."` The Greek construction `πρὸς τὸ` + infinitive indicates **purpose or intent**. Jesus isn't condemning doing good where others can see; he's condemning doing good *for the purpose* of human admiration.

### 2. The Immediate Context Confirms This
Verses 2–4 illustrate the point:
- `"When you give to the needy, do not announce it with trumpets, as the hypocrites do... to be honored by others."` (v. 2)
- `"But when you give... let your left hand not know what your right hand is doing, so that your giving may be in secret."` (v. 3–4)

The contrast isn't public vs. private spaces, but **human applause vs. divine approval**. The "hypocrites" (literally "stage actors") turn charity into performance. Their reward is the momentary praise of people; it ends there.

### 3. Jesus Explicitly Commands Public Goodness Elsewhere
Just one chapter earlier, Jesus says:
> `"Let your light shine before others, that they may see your good deeds and glorify your Father in heaven."` (Matthew 5:16)

Here, visibility is **commanded**, but the purpose is flipped: not to draw attention to the giver, but to point observers to God. This proves that public goodness isn't inherently dangerous; it becomes dangerous when the heart's aim shifts from God's glory to human approval.

### 4. The Theological Danger
The verse identifies two intertwined risks:
- **Displaced Reward**: Seeking human praise trades an eternal, divine reward for a temporary, earthly one. `"You have received your reward in full"` (v. 2) means the transaction is complete; there's nothing left for God to honor.
- **Moral Corruption**: When being seen becomes the goal, goodness becomes self-serving. It breeds hypocrisy, pride, and performative righteousness, which erodes authentic love and trust in God.

### Conclusion
Visibility is morally neutral; **motive is decisive**. Public goodness becomes dangerous in Matthew 6:1 only when it's driven by the craving for human recognition. Jesus calls his followers to examine why they do good, ensuring that even public acts are rooted in devotion to God rather than appetite for applause.
