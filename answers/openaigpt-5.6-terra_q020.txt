Bitterness is called a **root** because the writer is not describing merely a single angry act, harsh word, or moment of offense. He is describing an inward disposition that can take hold beneath the surface and eventually produce visible fruit.

The image suggests several things:

1. **It begins hidden.**  
   Roots are ordinarily unseen. Resentment may remain concealed behind outward politeness, silence, or even religious activity. A person may appear peaceful while privately rehearsing injuries, refusing forgiveness, or nurturing suspicion.

2. **It is a source, not merely an outcome.**  
   Bitter words, withdrawal, hostility, gossip, and division are the visible growth. The “root” is the deeper settled resentment, unbelief, envy, grievance, or resistance to God’s grace from which those acts arise.

3. **It develops over time.**  
   A root grows gradually. Bitterness is often fed by repeated remembering of a wrong, self-justification, isolation, or refusal to seek reconciliation. What begins as understandable hurt can become a fixed moral posture if it is not brought into the light.

4. **Its damage may be delayed.**  
   Roots can spread underground long before a plant or tree becomes visibly destructive. Likewise, bitterness may not immediately erupt, but eventually it can “spring up”—in a conflict, a crisis, a church disagreement, a family tension, or an opportunity to wound someone.

5. **It spreads beyond the original person.**  
   Hebrews says that “thereby many be defiled.” Bitterness is socially contagious. A resentful person can shape the atmosphere around them through complaints, insinuations, partisan stories, cynicism, and pressure on others to share the grievance. Families, friendships, and congregations can be divided by an offense that was first harbored in one heart.

The language likely echoes **Deuteronomy 29:18**, which warns against “a root bearing poisonous and bitter fruit” in the covenant community. In that setting, the danger is not only private sin but a person’s inward turning away from God that becomes a source of corruption for others. Hebrews applies the same communal concern: believers are to watch diligently, not merely for obvious public scandals, but for underlying attitudes that can poison the whole body.

So the remedy is not simply, “Do not act bitterly.” It is to deal with the root: receive rather than “fall short of” God’s grace, confess resentment honestly, forgive as one has been forgiven, pursue reconciliation where possible, and allow the community to practice loving vigilance. Peace and holiness in verse 14 are not superficial calm; they require the removal of the hidden roots that threaten both.
