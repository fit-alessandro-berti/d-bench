Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it goes against our natural instincts for self-preservation and defense. It requires us to set aside our emotions of anger and resentment and instead respond with love and kindness. 

Some may view enemy-love as sentimental, believing that it is simply an idealistic notion that is not practical in the real world. Others may see it as strategic, understanding that showing love and kindness to enemies can often lead to reconciliation and peace. 

However, for many, enemy-love is a deeper form of moral strength. It requires us to overcome our own pain and anger, to extend grace and forgiveness to those who have wronged us. It allows us to break the cycle of hatred and violence, and instead choose a path of compassion and mercy. In this way, enemy-love is not only a moral imperative, but a transformative practice that can bring healing and reconciliation in a broken world.
