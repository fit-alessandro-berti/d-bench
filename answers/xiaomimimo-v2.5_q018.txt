This is a perceptive question that gets to the heart of the passage’s pastoral and psychological insight.

The direct opposition between “bitterness” (and its associated vices) and “forgiveness” is deliberate and structural. The text places them as two fundamentally incompatible ways of living in community. Here’s a breakdown of why they are set against each other:

### 1. **The Nature of Bitterness: More Than a Feeling**
The Greek word for bitterness here is **πικρία (*pikria*)**. It’s not just a temporary emotion like annoyance. In the New Testament, it often describes a **settled, entrenched disposition**—a sour, poisoned state of the heart that has taken root. It’s a *chosen posture* that has calcified into a *habit*.

*   **A Feeling?** Yes, but it has moved beyond feeling into the will.
*   **A Habit?** Yes, as it’s fed by repeated rehearsing of grievances.
*   **A Chosen Posture?** Absolutely. It is the ongoing choice to hold onto a wrong, to cherish the resentment.
*   **Contagious?** Very much so. This is why it’s linked to “clamour” (public outburst) and “evil speaking” (corrupt talk). Bitterness inevitably spills out, poisoning conversations and relationships. It’s a spiritual and social toxin.

The list that follows—*wrath, anger, clamour, evil speaking, malice*—shows that bitterness is not a private, internal affair. It’s the root system that produces a whole tree of destructive behaviors.

### 2. **The Nature of Forgiveness: An Active, Imitative Choice**
Contrast this with the command to “be ye kind… forgiving one another.” The structure of the forgiveness side is crucial: it’s **grounded in a divine model** (“*even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you*”).

*   Forgiveness is not presented as a feeling that magically appears. It’s a **command**—an act of the will.
*   It is **imitative**. We are to forgive *as* God forgave. God’s forgiveness was costly, initiated in love, and based on Christ’s sacrifice. It is an act of **grace**, not a transaction.
*   Therefore, our forgiveness is also a deliberate, costly, grace-based act of releasing the debt someone owes us.

### 3. **Why the Direct Opposition?**
The text contrasts them so sharply because they represent two diametrically opposed **kingdoms or systems of living**:

*   **The Kingdom of Bitterness:** A self-centered system of justice where I keep accounts, hold power through grievance, and define myself by my wounds. It *feels* like protection but actually becomes a prison (Hebrews 12:15 warns against a “root of bitterness” that defiles many).
*   **The Kingdom of Forgiveness:** A God-centered system of grace where I release the debt, define myself by my identity as a forgiven person, and participate in God’s work of reconciliation. It is the path to freedom and true community.

The direct opposition shows that **you cannot inhabit both kingdoms at once.** One mindset actively uproots the other. You cannot nurture a root of bitterness while walking in the footsteps of the God who, in Christ, cancelled a debt we could never pay.

### In Summary:
The text treats bitterness as a **chosen, settled posture of the heart that becomes a destructive habit and is inherently contagious.** It is placed in direct opposition to forgiveness because **forgiveness is the chosen, active, grace-fueled posture that uproots bitterness and restores communion.** One is a prison built of remembered wrongs; the other is the key that unlocks it, modeled for us in the heart of God.
