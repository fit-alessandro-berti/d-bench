To understand how Luke 6:37 differs from a blanket dismissal of moral judgment, one must look closely at both the linguistic context of the verse and the broader witness of biblical ethics. Far from abolishing the concept of right and wrong, this passage targets a specific corruption of the human heart: the usurpation of the divine role as the ultimate arbiter of human worth.

Here is an exploration of how Jesus’ instruction allows—and even requires—moral discernment, while sharply condemning the appetite to condemn.

---

### 1. Discernment vs. Condemnation: Definitions

To parse Luke 6:37, one must first separate two distinct mental and spiritual operations:

*   **Discernment (*Diakrisis*):** The ability to recognize reality, evaluate character, assess actions, and distinguish truth from falsehood, or good from evil. It is observational and functional. 
*   **Condemnation (*Katadikazo*):** A judicial act of declaring someone guilty, writing them off, and assigning them a permanent moral status or eternal doom. It is final and punitive.

Saying "that action is harmful" is an act of **discernment**. Saying "you are a worthless person because of that action" is an act of **condemnation**. 

### 2. The Biblical Context: Other Texts on Judgment

If Luke 6:37 meant "never judge anything," it would directly contradict other explicit instructions within the New Testament and the teachings of Jesus Himself. 

*   **Matthew 7:15:** Jesus warns, *"Beware of false prophets..."* To beware of false prophets, one must first judge their doctrine and behavior to be false.
*   **John 7:24:** Jesus explicitly commands, *"Judge not according to the appearance, but judge righteous judgment."*
*   **1 Corinthians 6:2–3:** The Apostle Paul asks the Corinthians if they are incompetent to settle trivial disputes among themselves, noting, *"Do you not know that we will judge angels? How much more matters of this life?"*

Clearly, scripture assumes that humans must make moral evaluations constantly. The question is *how* and *from what posture* those evaluations are made.

### 3. Why Moral Judgments Still Matter

If moral judgments never mattered, justice would become impossible. If right and wrong, truth and falsehood are interchangeable:

*   **Harm goes unchecked:** Oppression, cruelty, and deceit could no longer be called out or resisted.
*   **Wisdom becomes obsolete:** Biblical wisdom literature relies entirely on distinguishing the "wise person" from the "fool," the "righteous" from the "wicked."
*   **Forgiveness becomes meaningless:** Forgiveness requires an initial recognition that a real wrong has been committed. You cannot forgive a debt if you pretend the debt never existed. 

Therefore, moral judgments matter immensely because they protect the vulnerable, uphold truth, and define the reality of brokenness that makes redemption necessary.

### 4. The Pathology of the "Appetite to Condemn"

What Jesus forbids in Luke 6:37 is not the *mind* of discernment, but the *malice* of the condemning spirit. The appetite to condemn is a deeply ingrained human pathology driven by several dark motivations:

*   **Self-Righteousness:** By vigorously condemning others, the critic attempts to elevate their own standing. As psychologists and theologians alike observe, hyper-criticalness is often an unconscious defense mechanism to mask one’s own deep-seated insecurities or secret guilt.
*   **Schadenfreude and Superiority:** Condemnation offers a cheap thrill of moral superiority. It allows the individual to play God—sitting on a judgment seat, looking down on others, and pronouncing sentence without bearing any of the burden of redemption.
*   **Finality (Playing God):** True judgment belongs to God because only God knows the full context, genetic makeup, trauma history, and secret intentions of a human heart. When humans condemn, they act as if their knowledge is absolute and as if the person standing before them is beyond grace. They write people off permanently.

### 5. The Synthesis: Discernment Coupled with Mercy

Luke 6:37 presents a paradox that resolves only through love: **One must see clearly in order to forgive genuinely.**

Discernment looks at a broken situation, names the wrong honestly, but refuses to lock the offender into a permanent identity of "condemned." It says: *“This action was destructive (discernment), but I refuse to act as your ultimate judge, nor will I withhold my willingness to be reconciled to you (forgiveness).”*

In short, Jesus is not saying, *"Evil does not exist, so never mind it."* He is saying, *"Leave the gavel in God’s hands, drop your stones of self-righteous condemnation, and instead pick up the tools of mercy and truth."*
