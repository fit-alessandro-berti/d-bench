Great question. Let's break down Ephesians 4:31–32 and the relationship between bitterness and forgiveness in this passage.

**Text Recap:**
> “Let all bitterness, and wrath, and anger, and clamour, and evil speaking, be put away from you, with all malice:
> And be ye kind one to another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you.”

### 1. Bitterness and Forgiveness as Opposites

In this passage, **bitterness** and **forgiveness** are set in direct contrast. The first line tells believers to "put away" bitterness and related emotions, while the next line commands them to "be... forgiving one another." The "even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you" part shows that forgiveness is modeled on God's forgiveness of us.

#### What is Bitterness Here?
Bitterness in this context is **not just a passing emotion**, but a **persistent attitude or disposition**. In New Testament usage, bitterness often refers to a deep-seated resentment or holding onto hurt and offense, rather than a fleeting feeling of anger. It's something that can fester and shape your character if not dealt with.

- **Not just a feeling:** While you can feel bitter momentarily, in Ephesians it's more about a *habitual state*—a way of relating to others and to God that's characterized by holding onto hurt and refusing to let go.
- **A chosen posture:** Bitterness can become a choice. It's not just an emotion that happens to you; it's something you nurture by refusing to forgive and move on.
- **More than a feeling:** It's a **spiritual condition** that affects relationships and your walk with God. It's linked to "malice" (ill will) and "evil speaking," which are ongoing actions or attitudes.

#### What is Forgiveness Here?
Forgiveness here is **an active, ongoing practice**. It's not just a one-time event of excusing someone's wrongdoing, but a continual attitude of letting go of the right to retaliate or harbor resentment. The passage ties it to God's forgiveness of us, emphasizing that our forgiveness of others mirrors God's forgiveness of us.

### 2. Why Are They Placed Against Each Other?

The structure is deliberate:

- **Put away bitterness and all its kin** (negative, inward-focused, destructive)
- **Be kind, tenderhearted, forgiving** (positive, outward-focused, life-giving)

This is not just about suppressing negative emotion, but about **transforming your inner life and relationships**. Bitterness keeps you stuck in the past and poisons your relationships. Forgiveness frees you and enables loving, healthy community.

#### Is Bitterness Contagious?
Yes, in a sense. Bitterness is contagious in human relationships. One person's bitterness can sour the whole community (think of how "a little leaven leavens the whole lump" in 1 Corinthians 5:6). That's why Paul lists "clamour" and "evil speaking" alongside bitterness—they're all ways bitterness spreads.

But more fundamentally, bitterness is contagious in the spiritual sense: it undermines the unity and love that the church is supposed to embody.

### 3. Summary

In Ephesians 4:31–32, **bitterness is presented as a habitual, relational, and spiritual condition to be rejected**, while **forgiveness is the active, grace-filled alternative that reflects God's own forgiveness**. They are set against each other because one keeps you bound in resentment and the other sets you free to love and be loved. Bitterness is more than a feeling—it's a posture, a habit, and a source of spiritual and relational contamination.

**In short:**  
Bitterness here is a chosen, ongoing attitude of resentment and ill will, not just a fleeting emotion. Forgiveness is the active, repeated decision to let go of that bitterness and reflect God's grace. The text opposes them because they are mutually exclusive in a life that seeks to be Christ-like and community-oriented.
