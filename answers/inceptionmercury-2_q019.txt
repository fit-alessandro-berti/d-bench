**The contradiction James is pointing out**

1. **Two claims that are held together in the same person**  
   - *“Therewith bless we God, even the Father”* – the speaker acknowledges that God is holy, worthy of praise, and that the speaker’s own life is rooted in that reverence.  
   - *“…and therewith curse we men, which are made after the similitude of God.”* – the same speaker treats other people, who bear God’s image, as objects of contempt, insult, or even “cursing.”

2. **Why the two claims cannot logically coexist**  
   - If *all* human beings are “made after the similitude of God,” then each person carries a fragment of the divine likeness. To **bless** God is to affirm that likeness; to **curse** a fellow human is to deny it.  
   - The *same mouth* is the instrument of both actions. A mouth that truly honors God should, by definition, honor the image of God in every other person. When the same organ produces both praise of God and condemnation of God‑image‑bearers, the heart is split: it is simultaneously oriented toward the divine and toward the profane.  
   - This split is the “inner contradiction” James calls “moral disorder.” The believer’s *belief* (that humans share God’s image) is at odds with his *behavior* (cursing those very humans). The contradiction is not merely a logical inconsistency; it is a **division of the heart**—a person who loves God but does not love his neighbor.

**Why speech lays bare that disorder so quickly**

| Reason | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Speech is the most immediate output of the heart** | The tongue is the “public face” of inner attitudes. While deeds can be delayed, hidden, or rationalized, a word is uttered in the moment and cannot be postponed or edited as easily. |
| **The mouth is a single organ, so mixed messages are conspicuous** | A single mouth cannot truly “be two mouths.” When it says “God is holy” in one breath and “you are worthless” in the next, the listener instantly perceives a split in the speaker’s allegiance. |
| **Biblical witness that “by your words you shall be known”** (Matt 12:36‑37; Prov 10:20) | The New Testament repeatedly teaches that speech is a diagnostic of the soul. If the heart is divided, the division shows up in the contradictory utterances. |
| **Cognitive‑dissonance is first verbalized** | Psychological research shows that when people hold contradictory beliefs, the tension is most readily expressed in language (“I love God, but I hate my neighbor”). The verbal clash is the first symptom of the deeper internal conflict. |
| **Speech is socially observable** | Others hear the words instantly; they can point out the inconsistency before any private “good” deeds are performed. The public nature of speech forces the speaker to confront the contradiction. |
| **The tongue is a “small” organ with “great power”** (James 3:5‑6) | Because the tongue can both build and destroy, any misuse (cursing) is magnified. The same small organ that can lift God’s name also drags down a fellow human, making the disorder stark and memorable. |

**Putting it together**

James is not merely criticizing a rude word; he is exposing a *spiritual incoherence*: a person who truly perceives God’s image in every human would not be able to utter a curse against another human without also cursing God. The “same mouth” is a vivid illustration of a divided heart, and because speech is the quickest, most observable expression of that heart, the moral disorder becomes evident at once. The remedy, as James later says (vv. 11‑12), is to “tame the tongue” and align one’s speech with the unity of love for God and for those made in His likeness.
