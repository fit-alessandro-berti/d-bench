 The power praised in Philippians 2:5–8 is **kenotic** power—power expressed through self-emptying, humility, and sacrificial love.

Christ is “in the form of God” and possesses equality with God, yet he does not cling to that status. Instead he “makes himself of no reputation,” becomes a servant, and submits even to death on a cross. The passage therefore values a power that:

- **Renounces coercion** — he refuses to dominate or demand recognition.
- **Chooses obedience and vulnerability** — real authority is shown in descending to serve and suffer.
- **Wins through self-gift rather than force** — greatness is measured by love given up for others.

---

### Why humility joined to greatness can feel more unsettling than open domination

Open domination is at least *legible*: we know where we stand with a tyrant. We can resent, resist, or rationalize our subservience as fear. But greatness that humbles itself is **uncanny**—it overturns our assumptions about strength.

1. **It indicts the ego.** A dominating power flatters our self-interest: we obey because we must. Humble greatness exposes our own pride and self-promotion, asking not merely for compliance but for imitation.

2. **It cannot be defeated by ordinary means.** Coercion can be resisted with force or counter-power. But a king who lays aside his rights, who refuses to retaliate, disarms those who oppose him not by crushing them but by converting them.

3. **It blurs the categories we rely on.** We expect the great to be served and the lowly to serve. When the great one kneels, the world we thought we understood is rearranged.

4. **It demands surrender of the heart, not just the body.** Domination controls behavior; humble love asks for an inward change. That request often feels far more threatening than external force, because it reaches the part of us we most want to keep for ourselves.

In short, open domination asserts, “I am greater than you”; humble greatness asserts, “I am here for you.” The first provokes fear; the second provokes both wonder and uncomfortable self-examination.
