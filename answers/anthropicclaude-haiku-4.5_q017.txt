# Light, Exposure, and the Comfort of Darkness

## The Mechanics of Shame

Light doesn't harm—it *reveals*. The hatred isn't of light itself, but of what light does: **make visible what was hidden**. This passage identifies a peculiar human psychology:

- Evil deeds depend on obscurity to flourish
- Exposure threatens the narrative we've constructed about ourselves
- We can tolerate our actions in darkness; seeing them clearly is intolerable

The passage suggests shame operates as a kind of self-knowledge we resist. We don't want to see ourselves as we are.

## Why Darkness Feels Safer

In darkness, several comfortable fictions remain possible:

1. **Ambiguity persists** — we can interpret our actions charitably to ourselves
2. **Accountability is deferred** — what isn't seen clearly can be rationalized
3. **Identity stays fluid** — we're not yet forced to integrate contradictions between who we claim to be and what we do
4. **Judgment seems avoidable** — out of sight, potentially out of judgment's reach

Light forces *integration*—the collision of self-image with reality.

## The Paradox of the Truth-Seeker

Notably, the passage distinguishes two responses to light:

- Those who do evil avoid it (shame precedes)
- Those who "do truth" *seek* it, wanting deeds revealed "that they are wrought in God"

This suggests: **what we're truly ashamed of determines what we avoid seeing.** 

The person living in alignment needs no darkness; exposure simply confirms what they already know.
