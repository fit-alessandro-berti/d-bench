In Isaiah 55:7, the parallelism between “way” (*derek*) and “thoughts” (*machashabah*) reveals a comprehensive, holistic vision of repentance. By naming both, the prophet insists that true return to God is neither merely behavioral nor merely intellectual; it requires a total transformation of the human person. 

Here is why repentance must involve both, and what it implies about our conduct, our imagination, and the mercy of God.

### 1. The "Way" (Outward Conduct)
The Hebrew word *derek* literally means a path, a road, or a journey. By metaphor, it refers to a person’s habitual course of life, their visible behavior, and their outward actions. 

*   **Implication:** Sin leaves a footprint. The "way of the wicked" is the tangible damage, the broken relationships, the injustice, and the selfish habits that mark a person's life. Repentance must begin here because outward harm must cease. 
*   **The Danger of Stopping Here:** If repentance only involves the "way," it degenerates into moralism or legalism. A person can change their behavior out of fear of punishment, social pressure, or a desire to look respectable, while their fundamental desires remain entirely unchanged. This is superficial repentance—covering up the crime without dealing with the criminal.

### 2. The "Thoughts" (Inward Imagination)
The Hebrew word *machashabah* goes deeper than just passing ideas; it encompasses intentions, plans, purposes, and the deep imagination of the heart. It is the internal schema by which a person decides what is good, true, and desirable.

*   **Implication:** Jesus taught this exact principle in the Sermon on the Mount: murder begins with inward anger, and adultery begins with an inward lustful look (Matt. 5:21-28). Before the foot ever walks the wicked "way," the mind has already plotted the map. The "thoughts" are the root; the "way" is the fruit. 
*   **The Danger of Ignoring This:** If a man forsakes his wicked *way* but retains his wicked *thoughts*, he is merely a restrained wicked man. He is like a pressurized bottle; the outward flow has been stopped, but the internal corruption is building, ensuring that he will eventually return to his old path. True repentance requires abandoning the very logic, justifications, and fantasies that make sin appealing.

### 3. The Synergy of Way and Thoughts
By insisting that the wicked must forsake *both* his way and his thoughts, Isaiah shows that humans are unified beings. You cannot permanently change your direction without changing your destination in your mind. 

This dual-forsaking turns the sinner away from self-rule and toward God. It is the difference between *remorse* (regretting the consequences of an action) and *repentance* (grieving the rebellion of the heart against God). 

### 4. The Possibility of Mercy
Why does this dual-forsaking open the door to God's "abundant pardon"? 

*   **A Vacuum Must Be Filled:** If you only forsake your outward way, you become an empty house, "swept and put in order" (Matt. 12:44), leaving room for the sin to return with worse vigor. But when you forsake your inward thoughts, you create a vacuum in your deepest imagination—a void that God alone can fill. 
*   **Alignment with God's Reality:** Immediately following this passage, God says, *"For my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways"* (Isa. 55:8). The command to forsake our ways and thoughts is the prerequisite to receiving God's ways and thoughts. We cannot receive His kingdom while clutching our own blueprints for life. 
*   **The Nature of Abundant Pardon:** God does not offer a grudging, minimal forgiveness; He offers an *abundant* pardon (the Hebrew *rabah* implies multiplying, increasing, making much of). But abundant pardon restores the whole person. If God merely pardoned our outward "way" but left our inward "thoughts" intact, His pardon would be shallow, and we would remain internally enslaved to sin. Because God's mercy is abundant, it demands and enables a total reset—judging and forgiving both the fruit of our hands and the womb of our minds.

In summary, Isaiah 55:7 teaches that sin is a total-person problem, and therefore, repentance must be a total-person solution. Outward conduct must change because sin destroys the world around us; inward imagination must change because sin originates in our rebellion against God. Only when both are surrendered can we truly "return to the Lord" and receive the overwhelming, life-altering mercy He is eager to give.
