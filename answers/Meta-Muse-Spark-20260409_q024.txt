**External compliance vs. “new heart”**  

The difference Ezekiel 36:26–27 is pointing to is the difference between *behavior modification* and *desire transformation*.

**External compliance**
- **Mechanism**: You obey rules because of pressure: law, guilt, fear of punishment, social approval.
- **Relationship to the law**: The law stays outside of you. It’s a list you consult and measure yourself against.
- **Failure mode**: Fragile. When no one’s watching, or when the cost of disobedience drops, the behavior stops. You can “keep the judgments” while resenting them. Think Pharisees in the Gospels: technically compliant, internally hostile.

**Receiving a “new heart”**
- **Mechanism**: The motive itself changes. Obedience flows from altered affections, not just willpower. 
- **Relationship to the law**: The law is written *inside* you. God says “I will cause you to walk in my statutes.” The impulse comes from within.
- **Durability**: Internal. The same Spirit who gives the commands also supplies the desire and ability to do them. It’s not you trying harder with the same heart; it’s you acting from a different heart.

**Why hardness is described like a material condition**  

“I will take away the stony heart… and give you a heart of flesh.”

1. **It’s not metaphorical fluff to Ezekiel’s audience**  
   In Hebrew thought, *lev* “heart” meant the core of will, intellect, and emotion. A “stony heart” isn’t just “stubborn.” Stone is non-living tissue. It can’t respond, can’t be wounded, can’t flex. You can yell at a rock all day and it won’t change shape. Ezekiel is saying Israel’s problem wasn’t ignorance of the law. They had the Torah. The problem was *ontological*: their core was non-responsive to God.

2. **You can’t reform stone**  
   Laws, threats, and exile addressed behavior but couldn’t change the material. You don’t teach a stone to be soft. You replace it. That’s why the passage uses surgical language: “take away,” “give,” “put within.” It’s transplant, not coaching. 

3. **“Heart of flesh” = living, responsive tissue**  
   Flesh can be cut and feel it. It has blood flow. It heals and grows. The contrast is between dead, inert compliance and living, responsive relationship. Paul picks this up in 2 Cor 3:3: “not on tablets of stone but on tablets of human hearts.”

**The core claim of the text**  
External compliance asks, “How do I get you to do the right thing?”  
The “new heart” answers a deeper problem: “Why don’t you *want* to do the right thing?”  

Ezekiel’s point is that real obedience can’t be imposed from outside because the defect isn’t in the rulebook. It’s in the reader. So God doesn’t just hand down new rules. He installs a new reader.
