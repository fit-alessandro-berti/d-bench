The elder brother’s **deepest problem** is not merely anger or envy, though both are present. It is **a heart that relates to the father through merit rather than love, and therefore resents grace**.

In other words: his core issue is **self-righteousness exposed by mercy**.

### Why this seems deepest

His words reveal how he sees the relationship:

- **“These many years do I serve thee”** — he thinks in terms of service rendered.
- **“Neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment”** — he defines himself by obedience.
- **“Thou never gavest me a kid”** — he believes blessing should be distributed as earned compensation.
- **“This thy son”** — he refuses brotherhood because grace offends his moral accounting.

So the problem is bigger than:
- **anger** — that is the immediate emotional expression;
- **envy** — he does resent the celebration;
- **wounded fairness** — he does feel the situation is unjust;
- **pride in obedience** — this is very significant;
- **discomfort with mercy** — also true.

But underneath all of these is this:  
he does not truly share the father’s heart. He wants the father’s goods more than the father’s joy.

### What makes this spiritually serious

The younger brother was alienated through **open sin**.  
The elder brother is alienated through **virtuous self-regard**.

He is physically near the father but inwardly distant. He obeys, but not as a son who delights in the father; rather, almost as a servant who expects wages. That is why the father says, **“Son, thou art ever with me”** — the father offers relationship, while the elder brother is thinking in terms of reward.

### So which option best fits?

If one must choose the deepest issue, it is:

**pride in obedience that produces resentment toward mercy.**

Or more sharply:

**self-righteousness that cannot rejoice in grace given to the undeserving.**

That is why he stands outside the feast. The prodigal’s sin kept him far away for a time; the elder brother’s righteousness keeps him far away while standing at the door.
