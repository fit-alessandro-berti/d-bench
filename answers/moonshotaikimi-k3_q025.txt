The verse's own grammar settles the either/or. "To be seen of them" is a purpose clause — Greek *pros to theathēnai*, "in order to be seen." Jesus does not say "do no righteousness before men"; he says do not do it *in order that* they see. The location of the act is morally neutral. The aim of the act is everything.

## The sermon's own cross-check

The strongest proof is a few verses earlier in the same sermon: "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven" (5:16). Same visibility — commanded. The difference is the destination of the audience's response: to the Father rather than to the doer. Read together, the two verses form a single ethic: goodness should be visible, but the light must pass through you to its source. The sin is not having an audience; it is intercepting what the audience's gaze was meant to carry toward God.

## What the craving does to the act

Several features of the passage show why the craving, specifically, is corrosive:

- **It turns virtue into theater.** *Theaomai* (to be seen) is the root of "theater," and the very next verse calls such people *hypokritai* — in classical Greek, stage actors. The craving to be seen doesn't just add vanity to a good act; it reconstitutes the act as a performance, shaped by what will be applauded rather than by what is needed.
- **It converts a gift into a purchase.** "They have their reward" (*apechousin ton misthon*) is commercial language — paid in full, receipt closed, nothing outstanding. The applause-seeker has bought honor with his alms, and once the transaction completes, no debt remains on God's side, because nothing was ever directed Godward. The danger isn't losing a future reward; it's discovering you already collected the only reward you were actually working for.
- **It installs the wrong god.** The passage sets two audiences against each other — men, and "thy Father which seeth in secret." Whichever audience you perform for functions as your judge and rewarder. Craving human visibility is therefore not mere vanity; it is a relocation of the sacred gaze, an idolatry of opinion.
- **It makes virtue contingent.** If observation is the fuel, goodness evaporates unobserved. Character is what remains when no one is watching, and applause-dependent goodness never becomes character.

## What the remedy reveals

Notice the prescribed cure: "let not thy left hand know what thy right hand doeth." This hyperbole is not aimed at other witnesses — it is aimed at the *internal* spectator. The craving survives privacy. You can perform for yourself, keep your own ledger, admire your own generosity in the mirror of your mind. Secrecy is prescribed less to hide from others than to starve the audience within.

## Where visibility is still dangerous

And yet — if visibility were innocent, why not simply say "do good publicly with pure motives"? Instead, secrecy is made the default for giving, praying, and fasting. The reason is in the opening word: "Take heed" — be on guard. The appetite for recognition is so subtle and self-deceiving that publicness is hazardous terrain: it awakens the craving and then lets you mistake the craving for devotion. Visibility is the *occasion* of the sin, not the sin itself — but occasions matter to a heart that is practiced at deceiving itself. (It is also worth noting that the earliest manuscripts read "righteousness," not "alms": verse 1 is a heading for the whole of religious practice, so the principle governs everything, not just charity.)

So the compact answer: the problem is the craving, not the visibility — but visibility is dangerous precisely because it feeds the craving and conceals it. Goodness may be seen; it may not be staged. The moment it is staged, the deed changes hands — no longer offered to the Father who sees in secret, but sold to the crowd, whose coin is spent the moment it is paid.
