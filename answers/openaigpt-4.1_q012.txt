Great question! Let’s break your question into its parts and explore both the biblical context of Jeremiah 17:9–10 and its practical implications.

### 1. **Pessimism vs. Realism in Jeremiah 17:9–10**

#### **Pessimistic View**
- The language of the passage is stark and serious: “deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked.” This has often been read as *pessimistic*—a statement that the fundamental condition of the human heart is corrupt, unreliable, and self-deceiving.
- In the Hebrew Bible context, this is written to a people repeatedly turning away from God, suggesting a pattern of chronic self-deception and waywardness.

#### **Realistic View of Self-Deception**
- The text can also be seen as *realistic* rather than cynical or hopeless. It observes a universal human truth: **we frequently fail to grasp our own mixed motives or capacity for self-justification**.
- “Who can know it?” expresses humility about self-knowledge; it’s a caution against assuming we fully understand our own motivations.
- The answer in verse 10 shifts the focus: God alone truly searches and knows the heart.

**Summary:**  
While the language is strong, the intent is less about universal condemnation and more about **honest realism regarding human self-deception and the need for divine evaluation**. It doesn’t say all humans are utterly evil, but that our self-understanding is limited and flawed.

---

### 2. **How Should Someone Talk About Motives, Given an Unreliable Heart?**

Jeremiah’s message, echoed elsewhere in the Bible (Psalm 139:23–24; Proverbs 21:2), prompts a posture of **humility and caution**:

#### **Cultivate Humility**
- Recognize our motives are often mixed or unclear, even to ourselves.
- Be slow to claim total clarity or moral purity in our intentions.
- Avoid self-justification or defensiveness: “The heart is deceitful…” suggests we might be blind to aspects of our own behavior.

#### **Invite Examination**
- Instead of assuming pure motives, invite self-examination and even welcome the scrutiny of God or others.
- Prayer: “Search me, O God, and know my heart…” (Psalm 139:23).
- Community: Be open to wise counsel or correction from others who can help us see ourselves more clearly.

#### **Acknowledge God as Ultimate Judge**
- The passage pivots to God (“I the Lord search the heart…”), indicating that ultimate knowledge of motives belongs to God.
- This doesn’t mean we abandon trying to understand ourselves or others, but we recognize our limitations.

---

### **Practical Application**

**When discussing motivations:**
- Use tentative language: “I think my motives are…, but I recognize I might be self-deceived.”
- Avoid judging others’ inner motives definitively—since, biblically, only God fully knows the heart.
- Focus on observable “fruit” (actions and consequences), not just inner intentions.

---

**In summary:**  
**Jeremiah 17:9–10** is more *realistic* than pessimistic. It’s a call to humility about our inner life, warning us not to trust in our self-perception alone. It encourages us to let God “search the heart,” to be honest about our limitations, and to be cautious and humble when talking about motives—both our own and others’.
