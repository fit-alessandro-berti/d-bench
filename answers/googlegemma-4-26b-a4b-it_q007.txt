To understand why Luke 6:37 is not a mandate for moral nihilism (the belief that nothing is right or wrong), one must distinguish between the **function of judgment** and the **spirit of condemnation.**

The verse is not an instruction to abandon the ability to perceive truth; rather, it is an instruction on the posture of the heart when facing the failures of others. The difference lies in the distinction between *discernment* and *condemnation*.

### 1. Discernment: The Function of Wisdom
Discernment is a cognitive and spiritual faculty used to distinguish between good and evil, truth and falsehood, or constructive and destructive behavior. In the biblical context, discernment is actually commanded elsewhere (e.g., Hebrews 5:14, Romans 16:17).

*   **The Purpose of Discernment:** It is protective and navigational. It allows a person to say, "This action is harmful," or "This teaching is untrue." It is about identifying the nature of a thing so that one can respond with wisdom.
*   **The Object of Discernment:** It focuses on the **act** or the **truth**. Discernment asks: *Is this behavior consistent with righteousness?*
*   **The Goal of Discernment:** To preserve truth, protect the community, and guide oneself toward better paths.

### 2. Condemnation: The Appetite of Pride
Condemnation, which Jesus warns against, is a judicial and emotional posture. It goes beyond identifying a wrong; it seeks to pass a final sentence on a person's soul or worth.

*   **The Purpose of Condemnation:** It is punitive and exclusionary. It is used to place oneself above another, establishing a hierarchy of "purity."
*   **The Object of Condemnation:** It focuses on the **person**. Condemnation says, "Because you did this, you are fundamentally bad/worthless/beyond hope."
*   **The Goal of Condemnation:** To diminish the other to elevate the self. It is fueled by a "spirit of judgment"—an appetite to play the role of God.

### 3. The Key Differences

| Feature | Discernment | Condemnation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Focus** | The behavior/The truth | The person/The identity |
| **Motivation** | Wisdom and protection | Superiority and punishment |
| **Result** | Clarity and correction | Isolation and resentment |
| **Core Question** | "What is happening here?" | "Who is better than whom?" |

### 4. Why the distinction matters in Luke 6:37
When Jesus says, *"Judge not, and ye shall not be judged,"* He is addressing the **reciprocity of mercy.** 

If we approach others with a "condemning" heart—looking for reasons to cast them out or declare them irredeemable—we are essentially asking God to use that same rigid, unforgiving standard upon us. 

However, if we use "discernment" to recognize that an action is wrong, we can still fulfill the third part of the verse: *"Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven."* You cannot forgive something unless you have first discerned that it was a wrong. To forgive, one must first recognize the offense; therefore, discernment is the prerequisite for the very forgiveness Jesus commands.

### Summary
Moral judgments matter deeply because they define our relationship with truth. However, Luke 6:37 warns us that while we must discern the truth, we must never hijack the authority of God to condemn the human soul. **Discernment sees the sin; condemnation hates the sinner.** One seeks to fix the world, while the other seeks to destroy the person.
