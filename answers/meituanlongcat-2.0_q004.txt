The trust called for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is a wholehearted, radical reliance on God that deliberately relocates the center of confidence from one’s own limited perspective to God’s infinite wisdom. It is not a vague sentiment but a settled orientation of the heart and mind: trusting the Lord **with all your heart**—the inner core of will, affection, and commitment—and refusing to **lean** (take ultimate support) **on your own understanding**. This trust invites active **acknowledgment** of God in every area of life, which then opens the way for divine direction. It is trust as a governing posture, not merely a momentary feeling.

### What humble self-distrust looks like
Humble self-distrust is the sober recognition that human understanding is partial, prone to bias, and incapable of grasping the full scope of reality or God’s purposes (1 Cor. 13:12). It is not a despising of reason but a distrust of reason’s *claim to ultimacy*. The person who practices this:
- Uses their mind fully while acknowledging its limits.
- Seeks God’s wisdom through Scripture, prayer, the Holy Spirit, and godly counsel before making major decisions.
- Holds their own plans and insights with an open hand, ready to revise them in light of God’s character and revelation.
- Avoids the arrogance of assuming their finite perspective is sufficient to navigate life’s complexities.

This self-distrust flows from humility before a holy God, not from self-loathing or mental laziness. It says, *“I think, I study, I plan—but I do not make my understanding the final arbiter. I need God to make my paths straight.”*

### What mere anti-intellectualism looks like
Mere anti-intellectualism is a rejection of intellectual rigor altogether—a disdain for learning, critical thinking, or education, often parading as “faith” but actually rooted in pride about ignorance or fear of hard questions. The anti-intellectual:
- Disparages study, reason, or specialized knowledge as spiritually dangerous or spiritually inferior.
- Relies on simplistic slogans or emotional impressions without testing them, sometimes even boasting in its own lack of learning.
- Denigrates the mind as a gift from God and treats intellectual effort as if it were inherently unspiritual.
- Can easily slip into presumption, mistaking uninformed convictions for the light of the Lord.

Anti-intellectualism is not humility; it is often a different kind of arrogance—an arrogance of the narrow heart that refuses the discipline of thinking under God’s lordship. It fails to “acknowledge him in all your ways” because it won’t bring every thought captive (2 Cor. 10:5) or love God with the mind (Matt. 22:37–38).

### The key distinction
| **Humble Self-Distrust** | **Mere Anti-Intellectualism** |
|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| Recognizes that human reason is *fallible and finite*, and therefore refuses to make it the ultimate authority. | Rejects human reason *altogether*, often treating any serious intellectual effort as incompatible with faith. |
| Seeks divine wisdom in moral and eternal direction, while still employing the mind vigorously. | Neglects the mind’s faculties and often substitutes credulity for trust. |
| Is open to correction because it knows its own understanding is limited. | Can be stubbornly closed to correction, mistaking its own uninformed certainty for God’s voice. |
| Is compatible with a love for truth wherever it is found, since all truth is God’s truth. | Tends toward a fortress mentality, suspicious of any knowledge that seems new or complex. |
| Leads to the pursuit of wisdom as commanded throughout Proverbs (e.g., Prov. 4:7). | Violates the Proverbs’ constant call to “get wisdom” and “get understanding.” |

In short, Proverbs 3:5–6 calls us to **trust the Lord with all our heart**, not to trash our heads. “Lean not on your own understanding” is an admonition against idolizing our own calculations, not an injunction against thinking. Humble self-distrust says, *“I will think deeply, but I will think on my knees.”* Anti-intellectualism says, *“I don’t need to think at all—except to feel good about not thinking.”* The former is the posture of a disciple who loves God with heart, soul, mind, and strength; the latter is a parody of faith that dishonors the God who gave us minds to know Him.
