The condemnation falls not on **false doctrine**—the Pharisee’s practices were actually stricter than the Law required (voluntary twice-weekly fasting, tithing even on acquired goods)—but on **the corruption of the heart that turns virtue into self-justification**. Jesus frames the parable precisely at this point: it is spoken to those who “*trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others*” (v. 9).

Here is exactly what condemns the Pharisee, layered together:

**1. Self-trust / Prayer “with himself”**
The text says he “*prayed thus with himself*” (v. 11). He is not truly addressing God; he is talking to himself about himself. He needs nothing—no mercy, no forgiveness, no grace—because he believes his own record is sufficient.

**2. Comparison and contempt**
His prayer is a negative self-definition: “*I am not as other men are*… or even as this publican” (v. 11). He locates his righteousness in being superior to named sinners and, viciously, to the actual man praying nearby. Religion becomes a measuring stick for despising others.

**3. Virtue used as self-display**
He lists his credentials—fasting, tithing—not as thankfulness for grace that empowers obedience, but as merits he brings before God. His “thank you” is not humble gratitude but self-congratulation disguised as prayer: “*God, I thank thee, that I am…*”

**4. Pride that refuses humility**
Jesus summarizes the verdict in v. 14: “*every one that exalteth himself shall be abased.*” The Pharisee exalts himself; therefore he leaves unjustified—declared righteous in his own eyes, but not in God’s.

---

**Why the tax collector’s posture is “spiritually truer”**

The tax collector embodies the opposite of every failing above:

- **Position:** He stands “*afar off*” (v. 13)—aware that his sin creates distance from holy God, not claiming privileged proximity.
- **Body language:** He “*would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven*” and “*smote upon his breast*” (v. 13)—external signs of internal contrition, shame, and grief over sin.
- **Speech:** His prayer is one brief plea: “*God be merciful to me a sinner*” (Greek: *hilasthēti moi, tō hamartōlō*—“make propitiation/atonement for me, **the** sinner”). He offers no comparison, no defense, no résumé. He identifies himself entirely with his need and with God’s mercy alone.

The tax collector receives justification (v. 14) not because humility is a “work” that earns salvation, but because he asks for what God actually gives—**mercy to the undeserving**—while the Pharisee asks for nothing because he trusts in his own supply. He “humbles himself” and therefore is “exalted” (justified); the Pharisee exalts himself and is therefore “abased.”

**In short:** The Pharisee is condemned for using religious virtue to establish his own righteousness and despise others, leaving no room for grace. The tax collector is justified because he abandons every claim except his sin and God’s mercy—recognizing that before God, all human comparison collapses, and only received grace stands.
