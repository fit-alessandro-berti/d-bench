The posture is Spirit-led, restorative, gentle, and self-distrusting.

- You who are spiritual: Not a rank but a condition—those currently walking by the Spirit (cf. 5:16–26), not ruled by conceit or rivalry (5:26). Correction belongs in the hands of the mature, not the reactionary.
- Restore: The verb suggests mending a net or setting a broken bone. The aim is healing and reintegration, not exposure, shaming, or winning an argument (cf. 6:2 “bear one another’s burdens”).
- In a spirit of meekness: Gentleness (prautēs) is a fruit of the Spirit. It is strength under control, the tone that fits a healing goal. Harshness may secure compliance but rarely produces repentance or trust.
- Considering yourself: A vigilant humility—aware of your own vulnerability—so you are not “tempted,” whether into the same sin or into sins that often attach to correcting others: pride, severity, gossip, voyeurism, impatience, or power-play (cf. 1 Cor 10:12; Matt 7:1–5).

Why these belong together:
- The goal dictates the manner. If the telos is restoration, the fitting means is meekness; force and scorn break what you’re trying to set.
- Humility protects both parties. Self-suspicion guards the corrector from moral contagion and from the subtler temptations of superiority. It also keeps the approach non-threatening, which opens space for the other’s repentance.
- It embodies the gospel and the “law of Christ” (6:2). As forgiven people, we bear another’s burden rather than add to it; we correct as fellow patients, not as judges.
- It serves the community’s health. Spirit-led people mend what is torn without tearing more.

In short: correction should be done by those walking by the Spirit, with a surgeon’s hand (restore), a quiet voice (meekness), and a penitent heart (self-watchfulness).
