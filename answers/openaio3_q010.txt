1. What kind of power is being praised?  
Paul is holding up what later Christian writers call kenotic power (from the Greek kenōsen, “he emptied himself”).  

• It is real power: Christ is “in the form of God,” already possessing every divine prerogative.  
• Yet it is power exercised, not by grasping or exploiting that status, but by voluntarily laying it aside, stepping downward, serving, suffering and dying.  
• Its driving motive is self-giving love, not self-assertion; persuasion, not coercion; redemption, not display.  

In other words, the text celebrates the paradoxical strength that is free enough to renounce its rights for the good of others. The highest place is revealed through the lowest act.

2. Why can humility joined to greatness feel more unsettling than open domination?  

a. It removes familiar categories. When sheer might dominates, the roles are clear: powerful/weak, ruler/ruled. But when limitless power kneels to wash feet or hangs on a cross, the hierarchy is turned inside out and we have no ready script.  

b. It exposes our motives. Confronted with raw tyranny we can feel justified in resistance or resentment. Confronted with voluntary self-sacrifice we are forced to ask why we cling to our own prerogatives. The contrast puts our pride in stark relief.  

c. It is morally compelling. Domination can be met with fear or hatred, but it rarely wins the heart. Humble greatness, precisely because it does not coerce, lays a claim on our conscience; we sense we ought to respond in kind, and that obligation is unsettling.  

d. It is unpredictable. A tyrant’s next move is to grasp more power; a servant-king’s next move cannot be calculated by normal power equations, and that unpredictability keeps us off balance.  

e. It hints at a larger reckoning. If the one who could command angels chooses crucifixion, what hidden order of values must govern the universe? The implication of a moral order beyond brute force can be more disturbing than brute force itself.

Thus Philippians 2 praises a power that is strongest when it seems weakest, and it unsettles us because it quietly overturns every ordinary expectation about status, safety, and self-interest.
