Hebrews calls bitterness a **“root”** because it is not merely a visible misdeed but a **hidden, sustaining source** out of which many misdeeds can grow.

### 1) A root is underground: bitterness can be inward and concealed  
An *act* is public and definite; it can be confronted and stopped. A *root* is typically **unseen**, embedded in the inner life—memory, grievance, suspicion, wounded pride, unresolved envy. A person may look outwardly “fine” while the root is **feeding on the soil of the heart**.

That fits the passage’s warning: “looking diligently” (watch carefully). Roots require vigilance because they often aren’t obvious until they surface.

### 2) A root suggests slow moral growth and delayed damage  
Roots work gradually. Bitterness often begins as something “reasonable” (a real hurt, a perceived injustice), but when it is **nursed rather than healed**, it spreads beneath the surface and strengthens over time. Only later does it “spring up” visibly—perhaps in:

- cynicism and harshness  
- unforgiveness and refusal of reconciliation  
- sarcasm, withdrawal, or contempt  
- retaliatory behavior, divisiveness, or despair  

So the image highlights **delayed consequences**: the damage appears later, but it began earlier.

### 3) A root produces fruit: bitterness becomes actions and patterns  
The Bible’s “root” language often implies that what is hidden will eventually become visible as “fruit” (conduct, words, choices). Bitterness is treated as a **source-sin**: not simply one wrong reaction, but a continuing posture that generates many wrong reactions.

Hebrews is echoing Old Testament imagery (especially **Deuteronomy 29:18**) about “a root bearing poisonous and bitter fruit.” The point is that bitterness is not self-contained; it tends to yield **toxic outcomes**.

### 4) A root spreads: one person’s resentment can “defile many”  
Roots can extend, intertwine, and send up new shoots. Likewise bitterness rarely stays private. It spreads through:

- **speech**: complaint, gossip, insinuation, recruiting others to “take my side”  
- **emotional contagion**: distrust and suspicion become communal assumptions  
- **relational fracture**: factions form; peace becomes harder; holiness is compromised  
- **moral permission**: one person’s settled resentment normalizes unforgiveness in others  

That explains “thereby many be defiled.” “Defiled” is community language: the bitterness of one can make the group’s life unclean—disrupting peace, weakening shared holiness, and pulling others into the same posture.

### Summary  
Bitterness is called a **root** because it is **hidden**, **nourishing**, **slow-growing**, and **reproductive**. It may begin internally and quietly, but it tends to surface later in destructive “fruit,” and it has the power to spread through a community until “many” are affected.
