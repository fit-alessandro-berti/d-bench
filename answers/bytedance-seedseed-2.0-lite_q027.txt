To answer this, we can ground our analysis in the parable’s explicit framing and first-century theological context, unpacking the interplay of the flaws you named to identify their root, then explain the tax collector’s superior spiritual posture:
### What condemns the Pharisee?
The parable opens by targeting the foundational sin that drives all the Pharisee’s other missteps: *pride that leads him to trust entirely in his own ability to earn righteousness before God*. This root flaw spawns the other observable sins you list: he compares himself to the publican to prove his own superiority, harbors contempt for that publican, and twists his otherwise legitimate religious practices (fasting, tithing, which were faithful expectations for first-century Jews) into a self-display to justify himself before God, rather than bringing his life before God with humility.
False doctrine is not the core issue here: the Pharisee’s practices themselves were not doctrinally wrong, but he misused them to claim he deserved God’s favor, rather than recognizing he, like all people, needed unmerited mercy.
### Why the tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer
The publican’s posture aligns with the core spiritual truth the parable teaches: no one can earn righteousness through their own good works. He does not compare himself to others to inflate his own worth, does not pretend he has a record clean enough to stand before God on his own merit, and humbly begs for God’s mercy instead of demanding favor for his deeds.
The full, uncut closing line of the parable (omitted from your excerpt) reinforces this dynamic: “For everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, and he who humbles himself will be exalted” (Luke 18:14b). The tax collector’s humility opens him to receive the free gift of justification God offers, while the Pharisee’s self-exalting pride cuts him off from that grace.
