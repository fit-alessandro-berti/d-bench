# Bitterness as a Structural Problem

The pairing suggests bitterness isn't primarily about *feeling* bitter momentarily—it's more like **a settled stance that blocks the very mechanism forgiveness requires**.

Notice what bitterness does in this passage:

- It clusters with **wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, malice**—all outward expressions, not just inward states
- It gets "put away" (removed, discarded) like a practice you stop doing
- It directly contradicts being "tenderhearted"

This points to bitterness as **a chosen (or habituated) posture**—a way of organizing your responses. You *maintain* bitterness through rehearsal, complaint, and withholding. It's performative.

## Why the Direct Opposition?

Forgiveness requires:
- Releasing a debt owed
- Softness toward the other person
- Accepting that the past won't be repaid in kind

Bitterness *prevents* all three. It:
- Insists the debt remain open
- Hardens the heart deliberately
- Banks on eventual repayment (from them, from circumstances, from God)

## The Contagion Element

Your question about contagiousness is perceptive. The passage moves from *individual* bitterness ("put away from you") to *relational* kindness ("one to another"). 

Unaddressed bitterness spreads because it poisons how you treat others—it becomes the lens through which you see offenses, real or imagined.

What makes this passage particularly sharp: **the standard given isn't "forgive as much as you feel able," but "even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you"**—absolute, unmerited, already-accomplished.
