This passage establishes a causal and inseparable link between the internal moral condition (the heart) and external expression (speech). By using the metaphors of **overflow** ("abundance") and **storage** ("treasure"), Jesus argues that speech is never neutral or isolated; it is always funded by the soul's accumulated moral capital.

Here is an analysis of how the text connects speech to these hidden stockpiles, and why the answer to your question is **all three**.

### 1. The Connection: Overflow and Treasury
The passage relies on two primary images to connect the heart to the mouth:
*   **The Hydraulic Image ("Abundance"):** The Greek word translated as "abundance" (*perisseuma*) implies a surplus or an overflow. Just as a cup that is filled to the brim must spill over when shaken, the heart, when filled with a specific moral quality, inevitably spills out through the mouth.
*   **The Economic Image ("Treasure"):** A treasure implies accumulation over time. It is not a fleeting emotion but a stored reserve. One cannot spend what they do not have. Therefore, speech is a withdrawal from the moral bank account of the heart.

### 2. Are Words Symptoms, Choices, or Revelations?

The text supports all three categories, as they function together to describe the mechanics of human morality.

#### As Symptoms (The Diagnostic View)
A symptom is an involuntary or inevitable indication of an underlying condition.
*   **Textual Evidence:** Jesus asks, *"How can ye, being evil, speak good things?"* This rhetorical question implies a spiritual impossibility. Just as a diseased tree cannot produce healthy fruit, a corrupted heart cannot sustainably produce good speech.
*   **Implication:** In this sense, speech is a **symptom** of the heart's health. You cannot treat the speech (the symptom) without treating the heart (the disease). The "generation of vipers" label suggests their evil speech is a clinical sign of their spiritual nature.

#### As Choices (The Volitional View)
A choice implies agency and responsibility.
*   **Textual Evidence:** The text says a man *"bringeth forth"* good or evil things. This is active language. The heart provides the *capacity*, but the person performs the *action* of bringing it out.
*   **Implication:** Speech is a **choice**, but it is a choice constrained by the available "treasure." A person chooses to speak, but the *quality* of what they choose is limited by what they have stored inside. They are morally responsible for the output because they are responsible for maintaining the treasury.

#### As Revelations (The Epistemological View)
A revelation is the making known of something previously hidden.
*   **Textual Evidence:** The heart is described as a "treasure," which is typically hidden from view. The mouth, however, is public. When a person speaks, they are unknowingly (or knowingly) unpacking their private inventory for public inspection.
*   **Implication:** Speech is a **revelation**. It unmasks the true self. The Pharisees may have appeared righteous outwardly, but their words revealed the "evil treasure" hidden within. Speech acts as a light that exposes the contents of the dark storehouse.

### Conclusion: All Three
To limit the passage to only one of these categories is to flatten its meaning.
*   If words are only **symptoms**, human responsibility is removed (it's just a disease).
*   If words are only **choices**, the depth of the heart's influence is minimized (as if one could choose good easily without a good heart).
*   If words are only **revelations**, the causal power is lost (it explains knowledge, but not origin).

**Verdict:** The passage suggests words are **all three**.
They are **symptoms** of the heart's condition, **choices** made by the will based on that condition, and **revelations** that expose the hidden moral stockpile to the world. The mouth is the gateway through which the hidden treasure of the heart becomes public history.
