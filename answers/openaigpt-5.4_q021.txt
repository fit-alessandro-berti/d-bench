Pride is linked to **blindness** because it distorts perception before it produces outward ruin.

In Proverbs 16:18–19, pride is not treated merely as a wrong attitude for which one is later punished; it is shown as a **self-deceiving condition**. The proud person overestimates himself, underestimates danger, resists correction, and assumes stability where there is none. That is why pride “goeth before destruction”: it creates the mental and moral conditions that make collapse likely. Arrogance is not just blameworthy; it is **disorienting**.

A proud person tends to think:

- I am secure.
- I am above warning.
- I do not need counsel.
- I deserve elevation.
- My success proves my superiority.

That inner posture produces blindness. One can no longer see limits, dependence, vulnerability, or the claims of others. So the fall is often not accidental; it is the fruit of a false vision of reality.

The contrast with **“the lowly”** is important. The lowly are not simply the socially poor; they are those who accept creaturely limits, live without pretension, and do not build their identity on domination or display. Humility here is a form of **clear-sightedness**. The humble person is more able to receive correction, live within bounds, and remain in right relation to God and neighbor.

So morally and psychologically, arrogance suggests:

1. **Inflated self-regard** — the self becomes too large in its own eyes.
2. **Resistance to truth** — correction feels like insult.
3. **Misreading of success** — prosperity is taken as invulnerability.
4. **Social distortion** — others become tools, rivals, or inferiors.
5. **Instability** — because the proud self is built on illusion, it is fragile.

That helps explain verse 19: “Better… to be of an humble spirit with the lowly, than to divide the spoil with the proud.” The issue is not only outcome but **company and character**. Even if the proud seem to prosper and “divide the spoil,” their triumph is spiritually diseased. Their success is already corrupted by the posture that will undo them. To be with the lowly is better because humility is aligned with reality, teachability, solidarity, and reverence.

In short: pride leads to collapse because it first leads to **misperception**. Humility is better not merely because it is morally nicer, but because it is **truer**—truer to who we are, to our limits, and to our dependence on God.
