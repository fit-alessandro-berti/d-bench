Great question. In Isaiah 55:6–7, repentance is described in **two dimensions** on purpose:

- **“Let the wicked forsake his way”** = turn from **outward behavior** (path, habits, lifestyle, actions).
- **“and the unrighteous man his thoughts”** = turn from **inward life** (plans, motives, fantasies, values, self-justifying ideas).

So repentance is not just “stop doing bad things,” nor merely “feel sorry inside.” It is a **whole-person turning**: conduct and consciousness, deeds and desires, life-direction and heart-direction.

### Why both?
Because sin itself is both:

1. **External** (what we do),
2. **Internal** (what we love, imagine, and intend).

Biblically, “thoughts” are often not just passing ideas but the deep counsels of the heart. Isaiah is saying: don’t only change your road; change your mind-set that keeps choosing that road.

### What does this imply about mercy?
The verse immediately says, “**let him return unto the Lord, and he will have mercy… abundantly pardon.**”  
That means:

- God’s mercy is offered to people whose lives are truly disordered, both outside and inside.
- The call to radical repentance is matched by **radical pardon**.
- No one is too far gone if they “return”—because God does not pardon reluctantly; he “abundantly” pardons.

In short: Isaiah 55 teaches that true repentance is **comprehensive**, and divine mercy is **superabundant**. The depth of the turning matches the depth of the forgiveness.
