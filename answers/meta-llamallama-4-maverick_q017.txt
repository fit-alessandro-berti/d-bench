The passage from John 3:19-21 suggests that the arrival of "light" into the world, symbolizing truth and understanding, is met with resistance by those whose actions are malevolent or "evil." It implies that individuals who engage in such actions have an inherent aversion to being exposed or having their deeds scrutinized. This aversion is rooted in the fear that their wrongdoings will be revealed and reproved. To understand why "light" would be hated, it's essential to explore the dynamics between truth, exposure, shame, and the desire to remain unnoticed or obscure.

1. **Truth and Exposure**: The concept of "light" in this context represents truth and clarity. When actions or deeds are exposed to the light, they become visible and subject to judgment. For those whose actions are morally reprehensible or contrary to accepted norms, this exposure is unwelcome because it brings their wrongdoing into the open.

2. **Shame and Accountability**: Exposure to truth often leads to feelings of shame or guilt, especially if the actions in question are deemed wrong or immoral by societal or personal standards. The fear of being held accountable for one's actions is a powerful motivator for avoiding the light. By staying in the "darkness," individuals may feel they can avoid the consequences or judgment associated with their deeds.

3. **Desire Not to Be Seen Clearly**: The desire to remain in "darkness" signifies a preference for obscurity or a lack of transparency regarding one's actions. This can be driven by a wish to avoid criticism, judgment, or repercussions. When one's deeds are evil or contrary to the values represented by the "light," there is a natural inclination to keep them hidden.

4. **Psychological and Social Factors**: Psychologically, the aversion to light or truth can be linked to the discomfort or pain associated with confronting one's own wrongdoing. Socially, the fear of ostracism, loss of status, or punishment can further reinforce this aversion. The passage suggests that the hatred for light is not just a personal preference but is deeply rooted in the recognition that one's actions are not in alignment with what is considered right or good.

5. **Contrast with Doing Truth**: The passage contrasts those who do evil and hate the light with those who "do truth" and come to the light. Doing truth implies living in accordance with moral and ethical principles that are illuminated by the light. Coming to the light signifies an acceptance of scrutiny and a willingness to be transparent, suggesting that one's actions are not shameful but rather are grounded in a deeper, possibly divine, reality ("wrought in God").

In summary, the hatred for light stems from its power to expose and reprove evil deeds, leading to feelings of shame, guilt, and a desire to avoid accountability. The aversion to being seen clearly is driven by the fear of judgment and the consequences that follow the revelation of wrongdoing. In contrast, those who act with integrity and truth are drawn to the light, as it validates their actions and reveals their authenticity.
