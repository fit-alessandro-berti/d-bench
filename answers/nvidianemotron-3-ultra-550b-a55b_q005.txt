The difficulty of Jesus’ command in Matthew 5:44–45 lies in the fact that it **violates the deepest instincts of human survival, justice, and identity.** It is not difficult because it is vague; it is difficult because it is *exactly* clear—and exactly costly.

To answer your tripartite question directly: **It is primarily a deeper form of moral strength (ontological alignment with God), which is frequently mistaken for sentimentality, and which *can* function strategically but ceases to be the thing Jesus commanded the moment it is adopted *merely* as strategy.**

Here is a breakdown of why it is so difficult to take seriously, and how the three categories you propose map onto the reality of the text.

---

### 1. Why It Is So Difficult to Take Seriously

**A. It attacks the "Logic of Reciprocity" (The Social Contract)**
Human society runs on *do ut des* ("I give so that you may give"). We love those who love us (Matt 5:46) because it stabilizes the tribe. Enemy-love breaks the circuit of reciprocity. It refuses the "eye for an eye" logic that feels like *justice* but functions as *containment*. To take it seriously is to voluntarily step outside the protection of the social contract.

**B. It threatens the Ego’s Need for Moral Superiority**
We define ourselves *over against* our enemies. "I am good *because* they are bad." Loving the enemy dissolves the boundary that props up our self-righteousness. If I bless the one cursing me, I lose the satisfaction of my own victimhood. It is a form of **spiritual death**—the death of the "false self" that needs an adversary to exist.

**C. It Confuses "Feeling" with "Will" (The Category Error)**
We moderns equate "love" with *affection* (sentiment). Jesus (using *agape*) means *deliberate action toward the other’s good*. The difficulty arises when we try to *manufacture warm feelings* for someone traumatizing us. That is impossible. The command is not "feel fondness," but "act for their flourishing." We fail to take it seriously because we misdiagnose the faculty required: it is not the heart (emotions) but the will (choice).

**D. The "Sun and Rain" Theodicy Problem**
Jesus grounds the command in the Father’s character: *He makes the sun rise on the evil and the good.* This is the scandal of **common grace**. It offends our sense of *merit*. We want the rain to fall *only* on the just. To love the enemy is to imitate a God who "wastes" blessing on the undeserving. Taking it seriously means becoming a person who "wastes" dignity, kindness, and resources on those who will likely squander or attack them.

---

### 2. Evaluating Your Three Categories

#### ❌ Is it Sentimental?
**No. Sentimentality is cheap; this is ruinously expensive.**
*   **Sentimentality** avoids conflict, prefers comfort, and confuses "niceness" with love. It says, "Let’s just get along."
*   **Jesus’ command** assumes conflict ("persecute you," "hate you," "despitefully use you"). It operates *inside* hostility, not in its absence.
*   Dietrich Bonhoeffer called "cheap grace" the preaching of forgiveness without repentance, baptism without discipline. *Sentimental enemy-love is cheap grace.* Real enemy-love is **costly grace**—it looks like the Cross. It absorbs the blow without retaliation, which requires a hardness of will far exceeding "being nice."

#### ⚠️ Is it Strategic?
**It *works* strategically, but it fails if *motivated* by strategy.**
*   **The Strategic Case:** History shows non-violent resistance (King, Gandhi, Mandela’s later years) exposes the brutality of the oppressor, breaks cycles of violence, and wins the "third party" (public opinion). Psychologically, it disarms the aggressor (Romans 12:20 – "heaping coals of fire").
*   **The Trap:** If I love my enemy *so that* they change, *so that* I win, *so that* I feel morally superior, I am not loving *them*; I am using *them* as a prop for my outcome.
*   **The Text’s Logic:** Jesus says, *"That ye may be the children of your Father."* The telos (goal) is **identity/ontology**, not efficacy. You love the enemy *because that is what God does*, and you are His child. The strategy (transformation of the enemy) is a byproduct of the obedience, not the fuel for it. If the enemy *doesn't* change, the strategic motive collapses; the filial motive remains.

#### ✅ Is it a Deeper Form of Moral Strength?
**Yes. It is "Moral Aikido"—the strength to absorb force without mirroring it.**

*   **Reactive Strength (Weakness):** "You hit me, I hit back." This is mimicry. The enemy sets the terms of the engagement. You are their puppet.
*   **Proactive Strength (Power):** "You hit me, I choose the response." This is **sovereignty**. The enemy loses the power to dictate your character.
*   **The "Children of the Father" Clause:** This is the key. In the ancient Near East, "son of X" meant "one who bears the character/image of X." Jesus is saying: *The defining family trait of God is indiscriminate benevolence.* To love the enemy is to exercise the **royal prerogative of the Kingdom**. It proves you are not a slave to your instincts, your trauma, or your opponent. You are free.

---

### 3. What "Taking It Seriously" Actually Looks Like (Praxis)

If it is moral strength, how do we distinguish it from the other two?

| **Sentimental** | **Strategic** | **Moral Strength (Filial)** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| "I don't feel angry." (Denial) | "I won't retaliate *so they see I'm the bigger person*." (Performance) | "I feel rage/fear, but I choose *not* to let that rage dictate my action." (Integration) |
| "Let's pretend it didn't happen." (Conflict avoidance) | "I will serve them *until* they apologize." (Transactional) | "I will pray for their flourishing *even if* they destroy me." (Cruciform) |
| Focus: **My comfort.** | Focus: **My outcome.** | Focus: **God’s character / Their humanity.** |

### Summary
Enemy-love is difficult because it is **ontological surgery**. It cuts out the tumor of *mimetic rivalry* (I become what I hate) and transplants the DNA of the Father (*He sends rain on the just and unjust*).

It is **not sentimental** (it requires steel in the spine).
It is **not merely strategic** (it works even when it "fails").
It is **moral strength**—specifically, the strength to remain *human* (and divine) when the enemy is doing everything in their power to turn you into a beast.
