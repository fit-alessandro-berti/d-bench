# The Moral Posture of Correction in Galatians 6:1

## The Required Posture

Paul prescribes a remarkably demanding moral posture — one that holds together three elements that, on the surface, seem to pull in different directions:

1. **The authority to correct** — "ye which are spiritual" implies a recognized capacity for discernment and moral seriousness.
2. **The gentleness of restoration** — the goal is not punishment or exposure but *reconstitution* of the fallen person.
3. **The humility of self-suspicion** — "considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted."

## Why These Three Are Kept Together

Paul binds them because **each one checks the corruption of the others**:

- **Without restoration as the aim**, correction becomes mere condemnation. The corrector drifts into self-righteousness.
- **Without meekness**, even well-intended correction becomes an act of domination — the corrector imposes rather than heals.
- **Without self-suspicion**, the entire enterprise collapses into hypocrisy. The one who stands over the fallen has forgotten that standing is itself precarious.

The phrase "considering thyself" (σεαυτὸν σκοπῶν) is not merely a warning against future sin. It is an epistemological claim: **you cannot truly see another's fault without simultaneously recognizing the conditions of your own vulnerability.** The one who corrects while feeling immune has already begun to fall.

## The Deeper Logic

Paul's grammar is telling. The spiritual person is to restore "in the spirit of meekness" (ἐν πνεύματι πραΰτητος) — meekness is not a technique but a *spirit*, a disposition that must already inhabit the corrector. And this spirit is inseparable from the awareness of shared fragility.

The three elements form a single moral posture because **genuine correction is only possible for those who know they themselves are in need of correction.** The moment the corrector feels secure in his own standing, the act of restoration has already been corrupted — even if the words spoken are perfectly true.

This is why Paul does not say "judge" or "rebuke" but "restore" (καταρτίζω) — a word used for mending nets, for setting bones. The image is surgical, not judicial. And surgery requires steady hands, which only humility can provide.
