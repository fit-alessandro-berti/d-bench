Based on the text of Luke 15:28–32 and the theological framework of the Parable of the Prodigal Son, the elder brother's deepest problem is **discomfort with mercy itself**.

While his anger, envy, wounded fairness, and pride all coexist, they are reactions or defenses of the core issue. Here is the analysis of why this is the root problem:

### 1. The Root: Discomfort with Mercy
The narrative arc of Luke 15 is explicitly about the Father’s relentless pursuit of the lost ("the sheep-was found," "the coin-was found," "the son-was found"). The climax is not about the legitimacy of the elder son's service, but the nature of the Father's love.
*   **The Father's Reaction:** The Father’s final speech (v. 32) stops the discussion of fairness or service and pivots entirely to **restoration**. He defines the occasion not as a celebration of a return to service, but as a celebration of *mercy*: "It was meet that we should make merry... for this thy brother was dead, and is alive again."
*   **The Son's Objection:** In verse 30, the elder brother views the situation through the lens of **justice**, not grace. He complains that the brother who "devoured [the father's] living with harlots" is met with celebration, while the faithful one is ignored. He cannot conceive that a father could love a son better than he loves himself without a transactional reason. He is clashing with the idea of *unmerited favor*.

### 2. Why the Other Options are Symptoms
The other options provided in the question are the mechanisms he uses to express his discomfort with mercy:

*   **Anger:** This is the **emotional symptom**. It is the outward expression, but it serves the internal logic (that mercy is unjust).
*   **Privilege/Fairness (vv. 29–31):** This is the **rhetorical argument**. He lists his years of service and lack of sin ("neither transgressed I at any time") to prove that the Father is treating him unfairly. This implies a belief that obedience *should* be rewarded with celebration, whereas grace treats everyone reckonedly saved as one.
*   **Pride in Obedience:** This is the **motive**. By emphasizing his own righteousness ("never transgressed"), he implicitly values his own behavior over his brother's return. His reliance on his "record" is what makes him unable to accept the Father's definition of "sonship" which is based on status and bond, not performance.
*   **Envy:** This is the **secondary feeling**. He resents the calf. But he wouldn't be so bitter if the younger brother had died. His envy is directed at the *Father's reaction*, not just the brother.

### Conclusion
The elder brother represents those who believe they have a "contract" with God or the Father. He believes his rewards come from his obedience, while the Father believes they come from His nature to forgive.

If the elder brother were truly comfortable with **mercy**, he would be able to see the younger brother as "a brother again" rather than a thief. His refusal to enter the celebration (v. 28) signifies a refusal to share in the Father's joy. Therefore, the deepest problem is his **discomfort with the Father's nature to forgive without account.**
