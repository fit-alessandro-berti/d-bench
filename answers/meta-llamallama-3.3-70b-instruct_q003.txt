This short moral summary in Micah 6:8 is demanding because it presents a trinity of virtues that are interconnected and mutually reinforcing. Each virtue - doing justly, loving mercy, and walking humbly with God - corrects and balances the others, making the overall standard extremely high. Here's how they interact:

1. **Doing justly**: This virtue emphasizes the importance of upholding what is right and fair. It involves treating others with equity and respecting their rights. However, if justice is the only consideration, it can lead to a rigid, legalistic approach that forgets the circumstances and needs of individuals. Justice without mercy can be harsh and unyielding.

2. **Loving mercy**: Mercy tempers justice by introducing compassion and understanding. It recognizes that individuals have limitations, make mistakes, and face hardships. Mercy encourages forgiveness, kindness, and help towards those in need. Yet, if mercy is exercised without a foundation in justice, it can lead to favoritism, inequality, and even enable harmful behaviors by not holding people accountable for their actions.

3. **Walking humbly with God**: Humility is crucial as it grounds both justice and mercy in a deeper, spiritual perspective. It acknowledges that true wisdom, justice, and compassion come from a higher authority. Humility prevents the practice of justice and mercy from becoming self-righteous or arrogant. It reminds us of our limitations and the need for guidance from a divine source. Without humility, the practice of justice and mercy can become self-serving, with individuals imposing their own versions of what is just and merciful without considering a higher moral framework.

In summary, each of these virtues corrects the potential excesses of the others:
- **Justice** prevents **mercy** from becoming an excuse for lack of accountability or favoritism.
- **Mercy** softens **justice**, ensuring it is applied with compassion and understanding.
- **Humility** ensures that both **justice** and **mercy** are practiced with a recognition of one's own limitations and the need for divine guidance, preventing self-righteousness and arrogance.

This balance is demanding because it requires a nuanced and thoughtful approach to interacting with others and with God. It demands a constant reflective practice, considering when to uphold strict standards of justice, when to show mercy, and how to remain humble in all actions. Achieving this balance is a lifelong endeavor that requires wisdom, empathy, and a deep spiritual commitment.
