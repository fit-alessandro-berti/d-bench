This is a sharp theological and pastoral question. To answer it well, we need to look at the text in its original context, clarify what the biblical authors meant by “heart,” and then draw out practical wisdom for navigating the reality of self-deception.

### 1. Pessimistic or Realistic?
**It is primarily diagnostic realism, not philosophical pessimism.** Here’s why:

- **“Heart” in Hebrew thought** (`leb/lebab`) is not the seat of emotions as in modern Western usage. It’s the integrated center of intellect, will, moral reasoning, and decision-making. When Jeremiah says the heart is “deceitful,” he’s describing how human judgment, desire, and rationale are prone to self-justification, rationalization, and hidden idolatry.
  
- **“Desperately wicked” vs. “incurably sick”**: The KJV’s “desperately wicked” has colored popular reading, but the Hebrew `אָנֻשׁ` (`anush`) means “sick,” “incurable,” or “beyond human remedy.” Most modern translations (NRSV, ESV, NIV, CEB) render it as “desperately sick” or “beyond cure.” This shifts the tone from moral condemnation to **clinical diagnosis**: human moral perception is chronically impaired and cannot self-correct.

- **Context in Jeremiah 17**: Verses 5–8 contrast trusting in human strength vs. trusting in the Lord. Verse 9 explains *why* human-centered confidence fails: we cannot reliably assess our own motives. Verse 10 immediately answers the rhetorical question (“who can know it?”): **God can**. The passage isn’t ending in despair; it’s redirecting epistemic and moral authority to God, who examines and judges justly.

- **Canonical balance**: Jeremiah’s diagnosis is never left without prognosis. The same prophet later promises a new covenant where God will “put my law within them, and I will write it on their hearts” (Jer 31:33). Elsewhere, Psalm 139:23–24 invites God to search the heart precisely because we can’t fully know it ourselves. The biblical trajectory is **realism about brokenness + hope in divine transformation**, not pessimism.

**Verdict**: The passage is profoundly realistic about the mechanics of self-deception and moral blindness. It’s pessimistic only if read in isolation from the broader biblical witness to grace, renewal, and divine illumination.

### 2. How to Talk About Motives When the Heart Is Unreliable
If we accept that self-knowledge is limited and motives are easily obscured, we can still speak responsibly about them. Here’s a framework grounded in the text and pastoral wisdom:

#### 🔹 A. Practice Epistemic Humility
- Avoid absolute claims about inner states: `“You only did that because…”` or `“I know exactly why I chose this.”`
- Use probabilistic, pattern-based language: `“It seems like X might be at play,”` or `“I’m noticing a recurring impulse toward Y.”`
- Acknowledge that even well-intentioned actions can be mixed-motive, and that doesn’t invalidate their goodness or impact.

#### 🔹 B. Evaluate by “Fruit,” Not Just Self-Report
Verse 10 explicitly ties divine judgment to `“ways”` and `“fruit of doings.”` Scripture consistently measures inner reality by outer patterns:
- Repeated choices, not isolated incidents
- How one responds to correction, suffering, or anonymity
- Whether actions produce life, justice, humility, and love over time (cf. Matt 7:16–20; Gal 5:22–23)
In conversation, focus on **observable trajectories** rather than psychoanalyzing hidden drives.

#### 🔹 C. Invite External Mirrors
Since the heart deceives itself, biblical wisdom relies on:
- **Community discernment**: Proverbs stresses the value of counselors. Trusted others often see patterns we rationalize away.
- **Spiritual disciplines**: Confession, examen, fasting, and meditation expose hidden attachments.
- **Scripture as a mirror**: James 1:23–25 and Hebrews 4:12–13 describe God’s word as penetrating where self-awareness fails.
When discussing motives, frame it as a shared seeking, not a court of inquisition.

#### 🔹 D. Separate Motive from Impact
A person’s stated motive may be partially self-deceived, but the real-world effect still matters. Conversely, good fruit doesn’t automatically prove pure motives, nor does mixed motive negate genuine good. Hold both in tension:
- `“Your help mattered, regardless of how mixed your reasons were.”`
- `“The outcome hurts, even if you didn’t intend it.”`
This avoids both cynicism (“nothing is ever pure”) and naivety (“good fruit = pure heart”).

#### 🔹 E. Point to Grace, Not Suspicion
Recognizing the heart’s deceitfulness should produce:
- **Humility**, not superiority
- **Openness to correction**, not defensiveness
- **Dependence on God’s searching**, not paranoid self-scrutiny or cynical mind-reading of others
The proper response to Jeremiah 17:9 is not despair or suspicion, but the prayer of Psalm 139: `“Search me, O God, and know my heart!”`

---

### In Summary
Jeremiah 17:9–10 is not a verdict of human hopelessness; it’s a sober diagnosis that clears the ground for divine grace and honest community. It dismantles the myth of transparent self-knowledge so we don’t mistake rationalization for righteousness. When talking about motives, we speak with humility, evaluate by fruit, welcome external discernment, distinguish motive from impact, and ultimately leave final judgment to the One who searches and renews the heart. This approach honors the text’s realism without collapsing into pessimism, and it creates space for both truth and grace in human relationships.
