Matthew 12:34–35 presents a profound theological and psychological insight into the relationship between speech and the heart (or inner moral character). The passage suggests that **words are not merely incidental but are deeply revelatory of the hidden moral "stockpiles" of the heart**. Here’s how the connection works and what it implies about words as symptoms, choices, and revelations:

---

### **1. Words as Revelations of the Heart**
The core claim is that **speech exposes the true nature of the heart**. Jesus uses the metaphor of a "treasure" (Greek: *thēsauros*)—a storehouse of accumulated moral and spiritual content. What comes out of the mouth is not random but a **direct overflow** of what has been stored inside:
- **"Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks"** (v. 34): The word *abundance* (*perisseuma*) implies a surplus or excess—what fills the heart will inevitably spill out in speech. This suggests that words are **unfiltered glimpses** into the heart’s true state.
- **"A good man out of the good treasure... an evil man out of the evil treasure"** (v. 35): The "treasure" is not just fleeting thoughts but the **accumulated moral character** of a person. Words thus **reveal** the hidden quality of that treasure.

**Implication:** Words are not neutral; they are **diagnostic tools** that expose the heart’s condition. This aligns with Jesus’ broader teaching that **actions (including speech) reveal inner reality** (e.g., Matthew 7:16–20, where fruit reveals the tree).

---

### **2. Words as Symptoms**
The passage also frames speech as a **symptom** of deeper moral health or corruption:
- Just as a fever is a symptom of an underlying illness, **hateful, deceitful, or blasphemous words** are symptoms of an "evil treasure" (a heart corrupted by sin).
- Conversely, **gracious, truthful, or edifying words** are symptoms of a "good treasure" (a heart shaped by God’s grace).

**Implication:** Speech is not the root problem but a **manifestation** of it. This is why Jesus elsewhere warns that **what defiles a person comes from within** (Mark 7:20–23), not from external sources.

---

### **3. Words as Choices (and Moral Responsibility)**
While speech reveals the heart, it is also a **deliberate act** for which humans are accountable:
- The context of Matthew 12 is Jesus’ rebuke of the Pharisees for their **blasphemous words** against the Holy Spirit (v. 31–32). Their speech was not just a symptom but a **culpable choice** reflecting their hardened hearts.
- The phrase **"how can ye, being evil, speak good things?"** (v. 34) implies that **evil people cannot consistently produce good speech** because their words flow from their nature. Yet this does not absolve them of responsibility—it underscores that their **words are evidence of their moral condition**.

**Implication:** Words are both **revelatory** (showing what’s inside) and **responsible** (a choice for which we are judged). This duality is why Jesus warns that **"every idle word men shall speak, they shall give account thereof in the day of judgment"** (Matthew 12:36).

---

### **4. Synthesis: Words as All Three**
The passage suggests that words function as:
1. **Revelations** – They expose the hidden moral "treasure" of the heart.
2. **Symptoms** – They are outward signs of inner health or corruption.
3. **Choices** – They are deliberate acts for which we are morally accountable.

This threefold role reflects a **holistic anthropology**:
- **The heart is the source** (revelation).
- **Speech is the overflow** (symptom).
- **Words are actions** (choice), binding us to moral consequences.

---

### **Theological and Practical Implications**
1. **Self-Examination:** If words reveal the heart, then **our speech is a mirror**—we should listen to ourselves to diagnose our spiritual condition (James 1:26).
2. **Transformation:** To change our words, we must **change our hearts** (Ezekiel 36:26; Psalm 51:10). This requires repentance and the work of the Holy Spirit.
3. **Judgment:** Words matter because they **reveal our standing before God** (Matthew 12:37). This is why Jesus ties speech to eternal destiny.
4. **Grace:** For those in Christ, the "good treasure" is not self-generated but **God’s work in us** (Philippians 2:13). Our words can increasingly reflect His grace as we are renewed.

---

### **Conclusion**
Matthew 12:34–35 teaches that **words are not trivial but profoundly connected to the heart’s moral stockpile**. They are **symptoms** of what lies within, **revelations** of our true nature, and **choices** that carry eternal weight. This challenges us to guard our hearts (Proverbs 4:23) and to recognize that **our speech is either a testimony to God’s work in us or a condemnation of our unrepentant state**.
