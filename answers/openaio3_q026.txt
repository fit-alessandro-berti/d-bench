The imagery Jesus uses is that of a storehouse (the Greek word for “treasure,” thēsauros, is literally a storage room).  
Put simply:

1. The heart is the storeroom where moral capital—good or bad—is amassed.  
2. The mouth is the door of that storeroom.  
3. Every sentence we utter is something taken off the shelf and carried outside.

Because of that picture the passage treats our words in three overlapping ways.

1. Symptom (overflow).  
   “Out of the abundance (perisseuma, ‘overflow, surplus’) of the heart the mouth speaks.”  
   Speech is the spill-over of whatever is most plentiful inside; it therefore acts as a diagnostic sign of the heart’s true condition.

2. Revelation (unveiling).  
   The act of “bringing forth” (ekballei) makes the hidden stash visible. Others learn what is in us by what comes out of us.

3. Choice (moral action).  
   The verb “brings forth” is active: the person selects from the storehouse and puts it on display. We are therefore accountable for our words (v. 36 goes on to say we will answer for every idle word).

So the text links speech to the “hidden moral stockpile” by declaring that:

• nothing can be spoken that is not already stored within;  
• what is spoken inevitably shows what is stored;  
• and the speaker is responsible for deciding to bring that stored material into public view.

In other words, words are simultaneously symptoms, revelations, and moral choices—and that three-fold linkage is exactly what Jesus presses on His hearers.
