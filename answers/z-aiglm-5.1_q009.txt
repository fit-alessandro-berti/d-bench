Romans 12:20–21 is one of the most counterintuitive and radical passages in the New Testament. It upends human instincts about power, justice, and retribution. To understand it, we must unpack its definition of victory and examine the nature of the strength it commands.

Here is a breakdown of how this passage defines victory over evil, and whether its command is weak, manipulative, or genuinely strong.

---

### 1. How the Passage Defines Victory Over Evil

Paul defines victory over evil in three distinct ways, flipping the conventional understanding of "winning" on its head.

**Victory is breaking the cycle of contagion.**
Paul warns, *"Be not overcome of evil."* Evil is portrayed here as a contagion or a conquering army. If someone harms you, and you harm them in return, evil has won—because you have now become an agent of evil. Retaliation is how evil replicates itself. Therefore, victory is defined as **refusal to participate in evil’s reproductive cycle**. You defeat evil by refusing to let it dictate your actions and character.

**Victory is subversion, not domination.**
The world defines victory as destroying the opponent. Paul defines it as disarming the opponent through the exact opposite of what they expect. Feeding a hungry enemy throws them off balance. It strips them of the narrative that you are the villain, robbing them of the moral justification for their hatred. 

**Victory is transformation.**
The ultimate goal of this passage is not the destruction of the enemy, but their transformation. Paul is quoting Proverbs 25:21-22. The imagery of "heaping coals of fire on his head" (explained more below) points toward bringing the enemy to a point of conviction, shame, and ultimately, repentance. True victory over evil is turning an enemy into a brother or sister. 

---

### 2. Is the Command Weak, Manipulative, or Genuinely Strong?

At first glance, this command can appear weak (yielding to abuse) or manipulative (being nice just to inflict psychological pain). A deeper look reveals it is neither—it is a profound demonstration of strength.

#### The Illusion of Weakness
To the natural mind, refusing to retaliate looks like surrender. However, it takes far more strength to absorb a blow without passing it on than to strike back. Retaliation is actually the easiest and most instinctual reaction; it is a loss of control. When Paul says, *"Be not overcome of evil,"* he implies that the person who gives in to rage or vengeance is the one who has lost the battle. The one who maintains their composure and chooses to love is the one exercising true power. It is the strength of a shock absorber: it takes the crushing force of a blow, but instead of shattering, it dissipates the energy.

#### The Danger of Manipulation
Is the command manipulative? Many read *"thou shalt heap coals of fire on his head"* and assume Paul is advocating for a kind of sanctified passive-aggressiveness: *"Kill them with kindness, because it will make them suffer more."* If this were the meaning, the passage would indeed be manipulative and cruel, masking revenge in the guise of charity.

However, this misunderstands the ancient metaphor of "coals of fire." In the ancient Near East, there are two primary interpretations of this phrase, neither of which are about torture:
1. **The Ritual of Repentance:** In ancient Egypt, a person who was deeply remorseful would carry a pan of burning coals on their head as a public sign of their grief and repentance. Thus, your kindness acts as a catalyst for their conviction, leading them to repentance.
2. **The Gift of Warmth:** If a neighbor’s fire went out in the cold of night, the charitable thing to do was to carry hot coals from your hearth to theirs in a clay pot carried on the head. In this reading, "heaping coals" is an act of profound, life-sustaining provision. 

Even if the "burning shame" interpretation is partly in view, the intent matters. The kindness must be **genuine**. Romans 12:9 explicitly states just a few verses earlier, *"Let love be without dissimulation [hypocrisy]."* If you feed your enemy just to torture them with guilt, you are still acting out of malice, and you *have* been overcome by evil. The command requires a supernatural, genuine desire for the enemy's well-being, leaving the conviction of their heart up to God.

#### The Reality of Genuine Strength
Ultimately, this passage commands a genuinely strong, subversive power. It operates on a deep theological truth: **good is ontologically stronger than evil.** Evil is essentially parasitic; it can only destroy or twist what already exists. Good is creative and life-giving. 

Darkness cannot drive out darkness; it can only multiply it. Only light drives out darkness. Paul is telling the Romans that you cannot defeat the darkness of hatred with more hatred. You can only defeat it by introducing a foreign element—goodness—that the darkness cannot consume. 

### Conclusion
In Romans 12:20–21, victory over evil is defined as **breaking the chain of retaliation and winning the person through radical love.** It is not a command to be a doormat; it is a command to be a shock absorber. It is not a call for passive-aggressive manipulation, but a call to sincere, enemy-loving benevolence. 

It is the ultimate display of strength: possessing the power to strike back, but choosing instead to heal. It is the exact moral framework that Martin Luther King Jr. later articulated when he said, *"Darkness cannot drive out darkness; only light can do that. Hate cannot drive out hate; only love can do that."*
