Isaiah 55:6–7 presents repentance as a turning of the *whole person* to God—not merely a change in visible behavior, nor merely an inward feeling.

- **“Way”** refers to one’s lived path: conduct, habits, choices, patterns of injustice, and practical manner of life. The wicked must “forsake his way.” Repentance therefore has outward fruit. A person cannot sincerely return to God while deliberately continuing in the path he knows to be evil.

- **“Thoughts”** refers not simply to passing mental impressions, but to plans, purposes, calculations, desires, and inward moral reasoning. The “unrighteous man” must abandon the thoughts by which he imagines, justifies, or intends evil. Sin begins not only in acts but in the heart’s orientation and designs.

Thus Isaiah rejects two inadequate versions of repentance:

1. **Outward reform without inward change**: changing one’s public behavior while retaining pride, hatred, greed, deceitful intentions, or self-justifying plans.
2. **Inward regret without outward turning**: feeling sorry, speaking religiously, or claiming faith while persisting in the same sinful “way.”

The command is then positive as well as negative: “let him return unto the Lord.” Repentance is not merely abandoning bad actions and thoughts; it is a return to a personal, covenant God. The Hebrew idea of “return” (*shuv*) conveys turning back—leaving a false path in order to come home to the Lord.

And the ground of that return is not human worthiness but divine mercy: “he will have mercy … and … abundantly pardon.” God’s pardon is not reluctant or scarce. The phrase carries the sense that he will multiply forgiveness. The sinner is called to abandon both his external path and internal designs precisely because mercy is genuinely available.

The following verses deepen this point: “For my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways” (Isa. 55:8). Human “thoughts” and “ways” are corrupt and self-centered; God’s ways are higher, including his astonishing willingness to forgive those who truly return. Repentance is comprehensive, but mercy is greater still.
