In this parable, several elements contribute to the condemnation of the Pharisee rather than the tax collector:

1. **Pride**: The Pharisee's prayer is filled with self-righteousness and pride. He uses his religious practices to elevate himself above others, thanking God for his perceived superiority. This pride distances him from God because it assumes his worthiness is based on his own actions rather than God's grace.

2. **Comparison and Contempt**: The Pharisee explicitly compares himself to others, including the tax collector, whom he describes in derogatory terms (extortioners, unjust, adulterers). This comparison not only shows his lack of humility but also an attitude of contempt for others, which is contrary to the teachings of love and compassion.

3. **Use of Virtue as Self-Display**: The Pharisee focuses on his own religious observances—fasting twice a week and giving tithes—as evidence of his righteousness. This emphasis on external acts as a display of virtue, rather than as a genuine form of worship or humility, suggests a transactional relationship with God, seeking merit rather than grace.

4. **Lack of Repentance and Awareness of Sin**: The Pharisee does not acknowledge his own need for mercy; instead, he views himself as already justified. In contrast, the tax collector recognizes his sinfulness and humbly asks for God's mercy, demonstrating an awareness of his need for forgiveness.

The tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer because:

- **Humility and Recognition of Sin**: The tax collector's standing afar off, his refusal to lift his eyes to heaven, and his act of smiting his breast are all signs of humility and repentance. He acknowledges his unworthiness and dependence on God's mercy.

- **Dependence on Grace**: By asking for mercy, the tax collector demonstrates an understanding that his salvation comes not from his own deeds but from God's grace. This attitude aligns with the biblical teaching that true righteousness is a gift from God, not something humans can earn through good works.

In summary, the parable highlights the importance of humility, genuine repentance, and reliance on God's grace over self-righteousness and self-reliance. The tax collector's approach is seen as spiritually truer because it reflects a heart posture that is open to and seeking of God's mercy.
