Jesus’ command to love one’s enemies in Matthew 5:44–45 is one of the most countercultural, challenging teachings of the Christian tradition, which is precisely why it is so often dismissed as naive, irrelevant, or even harmful. To answer the question, we first have to grapple with why it feels so difficult to take seriously, before untangling whether it is sentimental, strategic, or a form of deeper moral strength.

### Why enemy-love feels impossible to take seriously
The teaching cuts against almost every core moral, social, and even evolutionary instinct humans hold:
1. It violates the default norm of reciprocal justice that structures nearly all human societies. From the ancient "eye for an eye" code Jesus references earlier in the Sermon on the Mount to modern ideas of "just deserts," we are taught that kindness is owed to those who are kind to us, and neutrality or punishment to those who harm us. A command to love those who curse, hate, persecute, and abuse us feels like a betrayal of basic fairness, and even of self-preservation: after all, enemies are, by definition, people who threaten our well-being.
2. It can feel like it erases the reality of harm. For people who have survived abuse, violence, or systemic persecution, the command often reads as a demand to excuse the harm done to them, or to prioritize the well-being of their oppressor over their own dignity. This leads many to dismiss it as a privileged, out-of-touch sentiment that ignores real-world injustice.
3. It defies standard moral frameworks that tie obligation to behavior. Most moral systems, secular and religious, condition moral duty on the other person’s actions: you owe care to people who treat you well, and you do not owe care to people who treat you badly. Jesus’ command makes obligation completely unconditional, tied not to the enemy’s behavior but to the character of the God you follow—a claim that feels unmoored from practical, everyday justice.

### Is it sentimental?
No, though this is the most common knee-jerk dismissal of the teaching. Sentimentality prioritizes warm, comfortable feelings over hard moral truth, and often avoids confronting evil or injustice. But the "love" Jesus commands here is not affectionate, romantic, or even friendly love: it is the Greek term agape, a deliberate, volitional commitment to the other’s flourishing, regardless of how you feel about them.
The command presupposes that the enemy has done real, serious harm (Jesus is explicitly addressing people who curse, hate, persecute, and abuse you). It does not ask you to feel fondness for your abuser, or to ignore the evil of their actions. It asks you to act for their ultimate good (e.g., their repentance, their cessation of harm, their healing) even when you are justifiably furious, hurt, or resentful. Dismissing it as "just sentiment" reduces a radical moral demand to a feel-good platitude, misreading its core as a call to feeling rather than a call to action.

### Is it strategic?
It has strategic benefits, but it is not primarily a strategy. A strategic approach to enemy love would be conditional: you only practice it if it achieves a desired outcome (e.g., de-escalating conflict, winning over opponents, avoiding retaliation). There are clear real-world strategic dividends to this approach: nonviolent resistance movements from the U.S. Civil Rights Movement to anti-colonial struggles have used enemy love to break cycles of violence, expose the injustice of oppressors, and win public sympathy. But these are side effects, not the point of the teaching.
Jesus grounds the command explicitly in imitating God’s character, not in achieving practical results: "That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven: for he maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust." The goal is not to get your enemy to stop harming you, or to win a conflict, but to reflect the impartial, unearned love of God. If it were only strategic, we would abandon it when it fails to produce results (e.g., if a persecutor continues their abuse even after you bless them), but the command is unconditional, calling us to love even when it brings us no benefit, or even greater suffering. Strategy is by definition self-interested and outcome-oriented; enemy love, as Jesus teaches it, is neither.

### Is it a deeper form of moral strength?
Yes—this is the core of the teaching, and the reason it is so demanding. Far from being a soft, sentimental rule, it requires tremendous moral courage, discipline, and humility:
1. It requires the strength to break the cycle of retribution. The default human response to harm is to retaliate, which almost always perpetuates cycles of violence. Loving your enemy means refusing to let their evil action dictate your response, taking full responsibility for your own moral character rather than reacting to the harm done to you. This is a radical act of self-possession: you refuse to let your enemy turn you into the kind of person who hates, seeks vengeance, and dehumanizes others in return.
2. It requires the strength to see the humanity of your enemy, even when they are dehumanizing you. It is easy to reduce an enemy to a monster, an "other" unworthy of care. Agape love insists that even the person persecuting you is still a person made in God’s image, capable of change, worthy of good. This does not mean excusing their harm, or tolerating abuse—loving your enemy can include holding them accountable, setting boundaries, seeking justice, and protecting yourself and others from further harm. It means refusing to let their evil erase their humanity, even as you resist their evil actions.
3. It requires the strength to trust in God’s justice rather than your own desire for vengeance. Loving your enemy means releasing your claim to "pay back" the person who hurt you, trusting that God will ultimately right wrongs, even if you do not get to be the one to punish them. This is a profound act of moral humility, denying your own impulse to be the sole arbiter of justice. It is also liberating for the victim: carrying hatred and desire for revenge is corrosive, harming your own soul far more than it harms your enemy. Letting go of that burden is an act of strength, not weakness.

Ultimately, Jesus’ command to love enemies is not a rule for the faint of heart, nor a vague sentiment for the privileged. It is a call to participate in the very character of God, whose love extends even to those who reject him. It is difficult to take seriously precisely because it asks us to rise above our natural instincts, to choose moral goodness even when it costs us, and to trust that love is a stronger force than hatred, even when the world tells us otherwise.
