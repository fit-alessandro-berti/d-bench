Excellent and perceptive question. The beauty and power of this parable lie in its layered critique. The condemned elements you list are all present, but they are not并列 (side by side); they are interconnected, stemming from a single root: **a false understanding of one's relationship with God and others, built on self-justification rather than grace.**

Let's break down what specifically condemns thePharisee and why the tax collector is justified:

### What Condemns thePharisee? (The Core Issue)

Your list covers the symptoms; the disease is **self-righteous pride that replaces a need for God's mercy with a performance-based resume.**

1.  **Comparison & Contempt:** ThePharisee's prayer is not addressed *to* God so much as it is *about* himself. He compares himself to "other men" (the worst of them) and specifically to the tax collector. This comparison is the engine of his self-praise. He doesn't simply say, "I am righteous"; he says, "I am not like *them*." This establishes his righteousness not on an absolute standard from God, but on a relative, self-defined scale where he is at the top. This is the essence of **contempt**—looking down on others as less worthy.

2.  **Pride & False Doctrine:** His fundamental error is theological. He believes his **status** and **works** have earned him a right standing with God. He operates under a **false doctrine of justification**: that one can be declared righteous by God through checklist obedience. This is a distortion of the Torah, which was meant to reveal human inability to keep the law, not a means of earning salvation.

3.  **Virtue as Self-Display:** His列举 (listing) of good deeds (fasting, tithing) is not offered in humble service but as **evidence for his defense**. The very acts that should be acts of worship and obedience become trophies. He is "thanking" God, but the tone is more like a lawyer presenting a case. Theuda (the good deeds) are displayed, not surrendered.

4.  **The Posture of the Heart:** More than any single action, it is his **self-exaltation** and **presumption**. He stands boldly, speaking to himself (KJV "with himself"), confident in his own righteousness. He sees no need for mercy.

**In summary:** ThePharisee is condemned because his entire religious system is built on **self-trust** (as the parable's opening phrase states: "which trusted in themselves that they were righteous"). His prayer is a monument to himself, not a surrender to God.

### Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?

The tax collector's actions are the precise antidote to thePharisee's disease, making his prayer the essence of **biblical humility and true worship.**

1.  **Genuine Humility (Not Just Behavior):** He "stood afar off"—a physical posture of unworthiness and distance from a holy God. He "would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven," unable to face the purity of God. This is not a ritual; it's the involuntary response of a soul that knows its true state.

2.  **Contrast & Dependence:** His prayer is the **antithesis** of thePharisee's. There is no comparison to others (only to his own sin), no listing of deeds, no presumption. It is a simple, desperate cry for **mercy** ("be merciful to me a sinner"). He places his entire hope not in his own goodness but in the character of God—specifically, His mercy.

3.  **The "God Be Merciful" (Hilastēri) Prayer:** This Greek word (*hilasterion*) is profound. It can mean "propitiate" or "atone." The tax collector is implicitly asking God to deal with his sin through some means of expiation—to make amends. It is a prayer that acknowledges a *need* for a solution outside of himself. This is the very heart of saving faith: dependence on God's provision, not one's own performance.

4.  **Repentance (Metanoia):** His actions—standing afar off, beating his breast—are outward signs of **internal repentance** ("a broken and a contrite heart, O God, thou wilt not despise" - Psalm 51:17). He doesn't just ask for forgiveness; his whole being signals a turning from self to God.

### The Theological Verdict (Why He Is Justified)

Jesus' concluding statement—"I tell you, this man went down to his house justified rather than the other"—is the crux.

*   **Justification** here is a legal declaration of righteousness, not a measure of moral perfection.
*   The tax collector is **justified by grace through faith** (though the word "faith" isn't explicitly used in his prayer, it is implicit in his desperate reliance on mercy).
*   ThePharisee is **self-justified**, trusting in his own righteousness. But according to Romans 3:20, "by the deeds of the law no flesh shall be justified in his sight."
*   The tax collector represents the **sinner who knows they are a sinner** and cries out for mercy. ThePharisee represents the **self-deceived "righteous" who thinks they are not sinners** (cf. 1 John 1:8-10). Jesus came to call sinners to repentance (Luke 5:32), and only those who see their need can receive it.

**Conclusion:** ThePharisee is condemned not merely for being proud or comparing himself to others, but for the ** Idolatry of the Self**—placing his trust in his own righteousness rather than in God's mercy. The tax collector is justified because his **honest brokenness and dependence on God's grace** align with the true nature of prayer and the fundamental requirement for fellowship with a holy God. His posture is "spiritually truer" because it accurately reflects the reality of the human condition before a holy God: that we are sinners in need of mercy, not performers in need of a commendation.
