**Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously primarily because it violates deep-seated human instincts, intuitions about justice, and practical survival logic.**

### Why It's So Hard
Human beings are wired for reciprocity—"an eye for an eye" feels like basic fairness. Evolution favors in-group loyalty, quick retaliation against threats, and self-preservation; loving those who harm you runs directly against the amygdala-driven response of anger, fear, and revenge. Psychologically, trauma and betrayal make warm feelings toward an enemy almost impossible without massive internal work. Culturally and socially, it is easily read as weakness, disloyalty to your own side, or naive betrayal of victims. In polarized times—whether ancient Roman-occupied Judea or today’s conflicts, politics, and cancel culture—it can appear irresponsible, even suicidal. How do you "love" someone actively seeking your destruction or the destruction of innocents? The teaching feels like an impossible ideal rather than livable ethics.

The text itself anticipates this difficulty by grounding the command in something beyond human instinct: “That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven.” It is not presented as natural or easy. It is supernatural.

### Sentimental, Strategic, or Moral Strength?
- **Not merely sentimental.** Reducing enemy-love to warm fuzzy feelings or passive niceness distorts it. The verse commands concrete, costly actions: *bless* those who curse you, *do good* to those who hate you, *pray* for those who persecute you. Jesus is not advocating denial of evil or emotional suppression. He lived this at the cross while experiencing real betrayal and torture. Superficial sentimentality collapses under pressure; this teaching was meant to survive it. Misreadings that turn it into naive pacifism that enables evil miss the point.

- **Partially strategic—but not ultimately.** There *are* strategic dimensions. Nonviolent resistance inspired by this ethic (Gandhi, Martin Luther King Jr., and others) has repeatedly shown it can disrupt cycles of violence, claim the moral high ground, expose injustice, and sometimes transform enemies. In game-theoretic terms, forgiveness in repeated interactions can outperform endless retaliation. Yet the passage does not motivate the command by promised earthly success or pragmatic results. It points to the character of God, who “maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust.” The strategy flows from theology, not the other way around. Treating it *purely* as a tactic cheapens the radical demand.

- **Fundamentally a deeper form of moral strength.** This is the heart of it. Enemy-love is presented as the path to true sonship—reflecting the impartial, generous, non-retaliatory love of God Himself (common grace extended to all). It requires profound self-transcendence: dying to ego, overriding natural justice instincts, seeing the image of God in the enemy, and trusting ultimate justice to God rather than seizing it yourself. Far from weakness, it is one of the most demanding expressions of moral and spiritual maturity. It takes courage to break the spiral of hate rather than ride it. Theologians like Dietrich Bonhoeffer described this as “costly grace.” Nietzsche saw it as slave morality born of resentment and weakness; the Christian tradition sees it as the opposite—divine strength made possible by grace, not mere willpower.

In practice, this strength often looks like praying for persecutors while still pursuing justice and protecting the innocent where possible. It is not approval of evil, nor the absence of boundaries. It is willing the true good of the other despite feelings, history, or likely reciprocity. Very few achieve it perfectly; most experience it as an ongoing, grace-dependent struggle.

The teaching remains difficult to take seriously precisely because it is so high. It exposes how natural our tribalism and vengeance feel, and how supernatural the alternative is. Whether one accepts the theological grounding or not, the question it poses is sharp: Do we want a world that merely mirrors existing hatreds at ever-higher intensity, or one that interrupts them at the level of the human heart? The latter demands more strength, not less.
