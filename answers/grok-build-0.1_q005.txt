Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously primarily because it violates some of the deepest and most functional features of human psychology and social life. Humans are not neutral processors of moral commands; we are creatures shaped by reciprocity, threat-detection, status hierarchies, and group loyalty. The command to actively will the good of those who have harmed us, or who intend to, collides with these structures.

### Why it feels implausible

- **Reciprocity is wired in.** Most moral systems, including secular ones, operate on some version of proportionate response. "Do not do to others what you would not have them do to you" is already demanding; "do good to those who do evil to you" appears to break the symmetry that keeps cooperation stable. Without reciprocity, exploitation becomes rational for the ruthless.
- **Moral psychology resists it.** Anger and the desire to see wrongs righted serve protective functions. They signal boundaries and deter future aggression. Suppressing or redirecting them toward benevolence requires sustained counter-habituation that most people experience as psychologically costly or even self-betraying.
- **Power and vulnerability.** In real conflicts, the person or group labeled "enemy" often holds or seeks asymmetric power. To respond with deliberate non-retaliation can look indistinguishable from capitulation, especially to those who believe strength is demonstrated through dominance or credible threat.

### Is it sentimental?

Often caricatured as such, but the text itself pushes against pure sentiment. It does not primarily command warm feelings toward enemies (which are hard to manufacture and sometimes impossible). It commands blessing, doing good, and praying—actions of the will and behavior. Sentimentality would be "feel nice thoughts about them." This is closer to a disciplined refusal to be defined by the other's hostility. The sentimental reading usually comes from people who have never been seriously injured by the people they are now told to love.

### Is it strategic?

It can be, and sometimes is. Breaking cycles of retaliation has clear long-term advantages for communities (see certain readings of Gandhi, or the Truth and Reconciliation process in South Africa). Refusing to mirror cruelty can also be a form of asymmetric warfare that exposes the enemy's character while preserving one's own moral resources. However, treating enemy-love as *primarily* strategic instrumentalizes it. The passage gives a different rationale: imitating the indiscriminate generosity of God, who does not calibrate sunlight and rain according to moral desert. That is not a tactic; it is a claim about the nature of ultimate reality and what it means to participate in it.

### Is it a deeper form of moral strength?

This is the most accurate category, though "strength" here needs careful definition. Enemy-love is not the absence of strength but its redirection. It requires:
- The capacity to separate the person from their actions without denying the reality of the harm.
- The ability to act from a center that is not controlled by the emotional economy of the conflict.
- A form of patience that is not passivity but the refusal to let the enemy's timeline dictate one's own character.

This is harder than ordinary courage because ordinary courage often aligns with natural instincts (protecting one's own, fighting back). Enemy-love requires a second-order strength: the power to override first-order impulses in service of a different vision of the good. Historically, the people who have practiced versions of this most rigorously (certain early Christian communities, some monastic traditions, and scattered individuals) have not been the sentimental or the strategically clever, but those who had undergone a fairly rigorous re-formation of desire and reactivity.

The real difficulty is that this kind of strength is not obviously rewarded in the short term by the world as it is currently structured. It looks like weakness until the frame of reference shifts. Most people intuitively sense this mismatch, which is why the command continues to provoke either dismissal or uneasy respect rather than easy adoption.
