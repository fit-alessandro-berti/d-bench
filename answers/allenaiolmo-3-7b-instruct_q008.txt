
This is a profound and classic question about 1 John 4:20, and it touches on the heart of Christian theology regarding faith, love, and the relationship between the material and spiritual worlds.

Let’s break it down step by step:

---

### 1. **The Structure of the Verse**

John says:  
"If a man says, ‘I love God,’ but hates his brother, he is a liar. For he who does not love his brother whom he has seen cannot love God whom he has not seen."

- **“I love God”** — a claim about a spiritual, transcendent being.
- **“Hates his brother”** — refers to a human being, someone visible and tangible (the “brother” is another person).
- The implication is that if someone’s love is conditional or directed only toward the visible (the brother), rather than consistent and unconditional, it cannot truly extend to the invisible God.

---

### 2. **Why is Love of the Neighbor a Test of Love for God?**

#### a) **Unconditional Love as a Mark of True Faith**

In the Christian worldview, God’s love is described as **unconditional, eternal, and not dependent on our actions or appearances** (see 1 John 4:8–10; John 3:16). True love for God is not measured by how we treat people we know or see, but by whether our love reflects the nature of God Himself—i.e., *perfect, self-giving, and without conditionality*.

By contrast, love for a brother (a human being) is **conditional**—it depends on our relationship with him, our feelings, our experiences, our convenience. If one’s love for God is evidenced only by how they treat people they know (rather than being *pure*, *unconditional*, and *disinterested* love), then that is not the love of God at all.

#### b) **The “Invisible” vs. “Visible” Distinction**

- God is described as “invisible” (John 1:18; 1 John 4:12).
- A brother is “visible”—we see him, interact with him, have personal experience with him.
- If someone’s love for God is only active when it is convenient or pleasant (e.g., when they are with their brother), then it is not the self-sacrificing, all-encompassing love of God, but rather love of people or even of themselves (self-love).

Thus, **true love for God transcends the limitations of human relationships and is demonstrated in how we love those who are close and visible, without expectation of reward or favor in return.**

---

### 3. **What Kind of Self-Deception Does This Expose?**

The verse is a warning against **conditional love masquerading as love for God**. Specifically:

- **The illusion of spiritual love based on convenience or preference.**
    - Someone might say, “I love God,” but their love is really defined by their feelings toward people they like or with whom they have positive experiences.
    - They only love God *when it’s convenient* (e.g., when it aligns with their feelings for their brother), but not when it’s hard or costly (like loving an enemy, or a stranger).
- **Confusing the nature of love.**
    - They mistake *selfish love* (love of people for their own sake, or even love of the good feeling) for *divine love*.
    - They believe that if they love certain people, that automatically means they love God, when in fact, God’s love is supposed to be the standard—universal, unconditional, and above all relationships.
- **Self-deception in thinking that relationships with people are a proof of faith or love for God.**
    - Instead of examining whether their love for God is *separate* from and greater than their love for particular people, they mistake the two for being directly correlated.

---

### 4. **Analogous Example**

Imagine someone who claims to love Jesus but only prays for people they know and dislike praying for those they don’t. Or someone who says they are a Christian but is cruel to an enemy of Christ. John is saying: “If your love for God is only shown in how you treat your friends, then it’s not real love for God at all.”

It’s like saying, “I love the sky, but only because I love the clouds that form in it.” That’s not true love for the sky—it’s love for the clouds.

---

### 5. **Theological Point**

This verse is a **test of orthodoxy and heart condition**. Genuine faith produces genuine love for all people, regardless of visibility or convenience. The love of God is demonstrated not in our preferences or relationships, but in our willingness to love even those we find difficult or inconvenient—just as God loves us without reservation.

---

## **Summary Answer**

The verse treats love of the visible neighbor as a test because, in Christian theology, true love for the invisible God must transcend all human relationships and be unconditional. If someone’s supposed love for God is only present when it’s convenient or in connection with their feelings for a brother, it is not the love of God but of themselves or others—a form of self-deception that mistakes conditional, relative love for divine, unconditional love. It exposes the danger of believing that our love for God is proven by how we treat people we like, rather than by a self-giving, universal love that reflects God’s nature.
