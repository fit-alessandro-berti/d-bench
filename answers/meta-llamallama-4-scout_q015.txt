## Step 1
The passage in Matthew 7:3–5 uses a vivid and powerful metaphor to criticize hypocrisy, comparing the act of judging others to trying to remove a small speck (mote) from someone else's eye while having a large plank (beam) in one's own eye. The sharpness of the image comes from the stark contrast between the minor issue in the other person's eye and the significant problem in one's own eye, highlighting the absurdity and hypocrisy of the situation.

## Step 2
The critique of hypocrisy is levied against those who judge or attempt to correct others for minor faults while ignoring their own major flaws. The passage suggests that such behavior is not only unhelpful but also morally reprehensible because it indicates a lack of self-awareness and a double standard.

## Step 3
The passage does not forbid moral correction altogether. Instead, it emphasizes the need for self-reflection and personal correction before attempting to correct others. The instruction to "first cast out the beam out of thine own eye" indicates that one must address their own significant issues before they can effectively or morally help others with their relatively minor issues.

## Step 4
By stating "and then shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote out of thy brother’s eye," the passage implies that once an individual has dealt with their own major flaws, they will be in a better position to help others. This suggests that moral correction is not inherently wrong but must be done with integrity and self-awareness.

## Step 5
The passage, therefore, advocates for a humble and introspective approach to moral correction. It encourages individuals to prioritize self-reflection and to address their own shortcomings before offering criticism or assistance to others.

The final answer is: The passage criticizes hypocrisy by highlighting the inconsistency between judging others for minor flaws while ignoring one's own major flaws, and it does not forbid moral correction but rather emphasizes the need for self-correction first.
