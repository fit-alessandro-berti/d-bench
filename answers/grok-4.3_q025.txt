The danger in Matthew 6:1 is not visibility itself, but the *motive* of seeking to be seen. Jesus is warning against performing acts of righteousness (here, giving to the needy) with the deliberate aim of gaining human admiration or approval.

### The Key Phrase
The critical words are “to be seen of them.” The Greek construction emphasizes purpose or intent: doing the good deed *in order to* be observed and praised by people. This is not a prohibition against public goodness, but against turning goodness into a performance for an audience.

### Why This Is Morally Dangerous
When the motive shifts from pleasing God to impressing people, several things go wrong:

- The act ceases to be an offering to God and becomes a transaction for human applause. Jesus says the person has already received their reward (the attention or reputation they wanted), so there is nothing left for God to reward.
- It breeds hypocrisy. The person may appear generous while actually being self-centered, using the needy as props for their own image.
- It distorts the heart. Over time, the desire for recognition can replace genuine compassion, making the person more concerned with optics than with actual mercy.

### Visibility Is Not the Problem
Jesus does not condemn public acts of righteousness in principle. Elsewhere in the same sermon, he tells his followers, “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven” (Matthew 5:16). The difference is the intended audience and purpose: one is done to glorify God; the other is done to glorify oneself.

This is why the same chapter later instructs believers to pray and fast “in secret.” The issue is not secrecy as an absolute rule, but avoiding the specific temptation to turn spiritual practices into public displays for self-promotion.

### Summary
The moral danger is the *craving to be seen*, not the fact of being seen. When good deeds are performed primarily to secure human recognition, they lose their spiritual value because they are no longer directed toward God. The visibility is incidental; the corruption lies in the heart’s orientation.
