### The underlying “wounded fairness”

1. **A contractual view of the father‑son relationship**  
   The elder son frames his service as a *contract*: “I have served for many years, never transgressed, yet you have never given me a celebration.” He expects a proportional reward for his obedience. When the father gives a feast to the wayigal, the elder perceives a breach of that contract.

2. **Self‑righteous identity**  
   His identity is built on *doing* rather than *receiving*. The father’s love is unconditional, but the elder son’s self‑esteem depends on being the “good” one. The prodigal’s forgiveness threatens that identity because it shows that love is not contingent on merit.

3. **Injustice as the emotional trigger**  
   The elder’s anger and envy are both *responses to a perceived injustice*. He feels that the father’s mercy is “unfair” because it rewards the sinner while ignoring the faithful. This sense of injustice is what makes him “wounded” – his internal moral ledger has been upset.

4. **Why “discomfort with mercy” is a symptom, not the root**  
   Mercy, in the parable, is the father’s response to the prodigal’s repentance. The elder brother’s discomfort with that mercy is because it *violates* his sense of fairness. If his sense of fairness were healthy, he could rejoice in the mercy without feeling slighted.

### A concise formulation

> **The elder brother’s deepest problem is a wounded sense of fairness that is tied to his self‑righteous pride in obedience.**  
> He cannot reconcile the father’s unconditional mercy with his own expectation that loyalty must be rewarded proportionally. Consequently, his anger and envy are merely the outward expressions of this deeper injustice.

### Practical implication for interpretation

- **Pastoral reading:** The passage warns against a “legalistic” spirituality that values *earned* merit over *grace*. The elder’s struggle mirrors anyone who measures God’s love.
