This passage praises the power of **kenotic love** — strength expressed through voluntary self-emptying, servanthood, and ultimate obedience to a redemptive purpose. In Christ’s case, it is the power of God manifested not as overwhelming force, but as humble, self-sacrificial love.

---

**Why can humility joined to greatness feel more unsettling than open domination?**

1. **It exposes the human instinct for power as control**  
Most human societies — and often our own hearts — equate power with dominance, visibility, and self-assertion. When someone who *could* dominate chooses instead to serve, it challenges our assumptions about strength and security. Open domination is familiar, even if oppressive; humble greatness is disorienting because it doesn’t play by the expected rules.

2. **It makes moral demands we can’t easily dismiss**  
If power asserts itself through force, we can resist it as an external threat. But power that approaches us in humility, especially when we recognize its hidden greatness, confronts our conscience. We cannot simply fight it — we must either submit to its transforming love or harden ourselves against it. This creates a more profound crisis of the will.

3. **It reveals a different economy of value**  
In much of human history, worth is tied to status, reputation, and achievement. Christ “made himself of no reputation” — He willingly embraced lowliness. This overturns our normal measures of importance. When greatness chooses obscurity, it judges our obsession with image and control, and invites us into a new way of seeing ourselves and others.

4. **It feels vulnerable — and vulnerability is unsettling when combined with actual authority**  
We are used to authority protecting itself. Here, authority becomes vulnerable, even to the point of death. For those in power, this seems dangerous; for those without power, it can feel too good to be true. The combination of real power and real vulnerability subverts our ideas of safety and trust.

---

In short, humble greatness is unsettling because it does not coerce — it convicts. It does not demand through fear — it invites through love. And in doing so, it exposes our own hearts, leaving us with a choice that open domination never gives us: to surrender not because we are overpowered, but because we are loved.
