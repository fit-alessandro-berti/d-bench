1. Why “root” and not “act”?  
   • The Greek text literally says “root of bitterness” (ῥίζα πικρίας). Ῥίζα names the underground part of a plant—the place where life begins, is fed, and from which everything visible finally grows.  
   • An act is momentary and open; a root is continuous and hidden.  By calling bitterness a root the writer is talking about an underlying attitude lodged in the heart, not a single overt deed.  Acts are the fruit; bitterness is the subterranean system that produces them.  

2. Hidden moral growth  
   • Roots work out of sight.  Resentment can lie undetected while it is being nourished by rehearsed grievances, jealousy, or pride.  
   • What finally appears above the ground—angry words, division, retaliation—has already been alive and growing for some time.  The image warns disciples to “look diligently” (v. 15) before anything is visible and cut the root early.  

3. Delayed but inevitable damage  
   • The writer borrows the phrase from Deuteronomy 29:18, where a “root bearing poisonous and bitter fruit” eventually brings covenant judgment on the whole nation.  In both passages the danger is not immediate but sure: once the root exists it will “spring up” (ἀνω φύουσα) sooner or later and bring trouble.  
   • A root deepens the longer it stays; the longer resentment is indulged the harder it is to pull out.  When it finally breaks the surface it brings “trouble” (ἐνοχλέω = to cause pain) first to the carrier and then beyond.  

4. Contagion—“and by it many be defiled”  
   • Roots send out shoots and runners; a single tap-root may feed several stems.  So one person’s smoldering resentment is rarely private.  
   • Bitterness is verbal and emotional: it vents itself in complaint, criticism, sneering, faction-making.  These are communal sins; they give others permission to adopt the same attitude.  Like a weed it reproduces, spreads, and soon “many” share the poison.  
   • The Old-Testament background ties the metaphor to apostasy: when one Israelite tolerated idolatrous thoughts a whole tribe could be led astray.  Hebrews hears the same danger in a church that is being tempted to fall back from grace.  

Summary  
Calling bitterness a “root” stresses:

• Origin: it is an inner disposition, not merely external behaviour.  
• Invisibility: it can live unnoticed while it feeds on the heart.  
• Persistence: once established it is hard to eradicate.  
• Potential: it inevitably produces visible, bitter “fruit.”  
• Contagion: its underground system can nourish many shoots, so one person’s resentment can defile an entire community.

Therefore the exhortation is to watch carefully, to weed early, and to pursue “peace with everyone and holiness,” because if the root is allowed to stay it will sooner or later break out and damage many.
