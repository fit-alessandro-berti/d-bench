# Judgment vs. Condemnation: A Crucial Distinction

Luke 6:37 is often misread as a blanket prohibition on moral discernment—as if Jesus is saying "never evaluate anything as right or wrong." But this reading collapses under scrutiny of the very passage itself. Just verses earlier and later, Jesus tells his listeners to recognize false prophets by their fruit, to notice specks and beams in eyes, to distinguish good trees from bad. You cannot avoid a mote in your brother's eye if you're forbidden from seeing that it's there. The text assumes discernment; what it targets is something else.

## What's Actually Being Condemned

The Greek word behind "judge" here (*krinō*) carries the sense of a verdict—a final, condemnatory sentence pronounced on a person's worth or standing. This is different from the ordinary cognitive act of *distinguishing* good from bad, wise from foolish, harmful from beneficial. Jesus isn't attacking discernment; he's attacking a particular *posture toward other people*—one that seeks to finalize their guilt, to rank them beneath oneself, to close the book on who they are.

## The Difference in Practice

**Discernment:**
- Asks "what is true here?" 
- Remains oriented toward the person's good, even while naming a problem
- Stays open to change, correction, redemption
- Is often reluctant, careful, self-implicating ("physician, heal thyself")
- Serves love—you discern a friend's self-destructive pattern *because* you care what happens to them

**The appetite to condemn:**
- Asks "how do I rank above this person?"
- Treats the other as a fixed object—defined, finished, unworthy
- Closes the door on change
- Is eager, often gleeful, unaware of its own faults
- Serves the self—it produces the pleasurable sensation of superiority

## Why This Distinction Matters

The appetite to condemn is really a bid for status disguised as moral concern. It's not fundamentally about the other person's actions—it's about establishing where *you* stand relative to them. This is why condemnation so often outruns the facts, why it enjoys itself, why it rarely produces correction and mostly produces enmity.

Discernment, by contrast, can coexist with humility because it doesn't need the other person to be *lesser*—it just needs the truth to be clear enough that love can act rightly. You can discern that someone is lying without hating them. You can see clearly that an act is wrong without needing to annihilate the actor's worth.

## The Warning Embedded in the Text

"Ye shall not be judged... shall not be condemned" isn't a cosmic tit-for-tat so much as a description of what condemnation *does* to the condemner: it locks you into the same rigid, unforgiving framework you've imposed on others. If your standard is "no mercy for failure," that standard will eventually meet you too—and it will not soften.

So the verse isn't asking you to go blind. It's asking whether your seeing is in service of truth and love, or in service of your own elevation.
