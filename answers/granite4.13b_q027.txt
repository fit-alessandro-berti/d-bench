The passage condemns the Pharisee primarily for **pride and the hypocrisy that stems from using his religious observances as a means of self‑display**. Several elements contribute to this judgment:

1. **False Doctrine and Pride:** The Pharisee boasts about how he is “not like other men… extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even as this publican” (Luke 18:11). By contrasting himself negatively with the publican, he subtly signals his own righteousness while implicitly denigrating others. This prideful self‑assessment reveals a false doctrine of superiority—believing that religious deeds alone guarantee spiritual merit.

2. **Comparison & Contempt:** The Pharisee’s prayer is essentially a “self‑praise” and a subtle contempt toward those he perceives as morally inferior (Luke 18:11). His attitude shows a failure to recognize the righteousness of others; instead, it pits him against the publican in an us vs. them theological stance.

3. **Virtue As Self‑Display:** The Pharisee’s focus on his own “virtues”—fasting twice a week and tithing all he possesses—is presented as a façade rather than genuine repentance or humility. He uses these actions not out of sincere devotion but to impress God (and perhaps peers) with his moral superiority.

In contrast, the tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer for several interrelated reasons:

- **Humility:** Standing afar off and “smiting upon his breast” indicates a deep sense of guilt and humility. Unlike the Pharisee, he does not boast about his righteousness but openly acknowledges his sinfulness (Luke 18:13).

- **Repentance Over Ritual:** The tax collector’s prayer emphasizes repentance (“God be merciful to me a sinner”) rather than external religious observances. Genuine conversion and remorse align with the spiritual standards of Scripture, which value inner change more than outward displays.

- **Alignment With Gospel Message:** Jesus repeatedly teaches that humility and contrition, not legalistic righteousness or rituals alone, are keys to righteousness (cf. Matthew 5:3; Luke 18:9–14). Thus, the tax collector’s posture reflects a return to the core teachings of grace and mercy.

Ultimately, the Pharisee is condemned because his prayer is steeped in pride, comparison, and hypocritical moralizing—where virtue serves as an outward façade. Meanwhile, the tax collector exemplifies authentic repentance and humility, which are spiritually truer under Christ’s teaching.
