In Jesus’ imagery, the *heart* is the hidden inner person—the seat of what one loves, trusts, intends, and habitually values (not merely emotions). The “abundance” and “treasure” metaphors treat the heart like a storehouse: over time it accumulates a moral stockpile (good or evil). Speech is then pictured as what naturally *comes out* when that storehouse is “tapped.”

### How speech connects to hidden “stockpiles”
- **“Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks”** (v.34): words are not random leaks; they are overflow. What dominates inside tends to surface in what we say, especially under pressure, conflict, or temptation.
- **“Treasure” (v.35):** what you *store up*—beliefs, loyalties, resentments, reverence, gratitude, pride, contempt—becomes the source from which you “bring forth” speech and deeds. The phrase implies settled dispositions, not momentary impulses.

### Are words symptoms, choices, revelations—or all three?
**All three, with an emphasis on revelation and responsibility.**

1. **Symptoms (expression of inner character):**  
   Words function like an outward symptom of an inward condition. If the heart is habitually evil, speech will tend toward evil (slander, contempt, deception); if habitually good, speech tends toward what is true, fitting, edifying.

2. **Revelations (diagnostic disclosure):**  
   Jesus is using speech as evidence. In context, the Pharisees’ hostile words toward him (“by Beelzebul…”) disclose what they truly are and love. Speech reveals what is otherwise hidden—your “treasure” becomes observable.

3. **Choices (morally accountable acts):**  
   Even though speech flows from the heart, it is still something a person *does*. Jesus’ argument assumes accountability for words (the next verse, v.36, explicitly speaks of judgment for “every idle word”). So words are not excused as mere involuntary leakage; they are willful acts that spring from (and further form) the will.

**Putting it together:** Jesus ties speech to the heart to show that our words are not superficial; they are morally weighty outputs of what we have inwardly “stored.” They *express* the heart (symptom), *expose* the heart (revelation), and *engage* the person’s responsibility (choice).
