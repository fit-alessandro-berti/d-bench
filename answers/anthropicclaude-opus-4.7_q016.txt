The elder brother's deepest problem is not anger, envy, or wounded fairness — those are symptoms. It is not even pride in obedience, though that is closer. His deepest problem is that **he has been living as a servant, not as a son**, and therefore mercy itself feels like injustice to him.

Look at how he describes his own life: *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment."* That is the speech of a hired hand tallying wages, not a son delighting in a father. He has been in the father's house, but his heart has been in the field. His obedience has been transactional — a contract he expects to be paid out on ("thou never gavest me a kid").

That is why the father's reply cuts so precisely: *"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine."* Everything the elder brother has been straining to earn was already his by the fact of sonship. His tragedy is that he never knew it. He stood in the middle of abundance and felt deprived.

From that root, all the other things grow:

- **Anger** — because a contract worker feels cheated when someone gets paid without working.
- **Envy** — because if love is a scarce wage, the brother's portion diminishes his own.
- **Wounded fairness** — because his entire moral universe runs on ledger logic.
- **Pride in obedience** — because obedience is the currency he has been stockpiling.
- **Discomfort with mercy** — because mercy breaks the ledger. Mercy is scandalous to the servant-heart; it is only joyous to the son-heart.

So the deepest problem is the *discomfort with mercy*, yes — but underneath even that lies the reason he is uncomfortable: he does not know he is loved as a son. He thinks he is loved as a worker. Mercy toward the younger brother horrifies him because it exposes the lie he has been living — that love had to be earned. If love is free, then his years of grim dutifulness were never the price of the father's affection; they were only the refusal of it.

In a sense, the elder brother is further from the father at the end of the parable than the younger was at the beginning. The younger went to a far country in body; the elder has been in a far country in the very house. And Jesus leaves the parable open — we never learn whether he goes in. The question hangs over every hearer who has ever tried to earn what was already given.
