To understand Luke 6:37, and the distinction between moral discernment and the appetite to condemn, we first have to reject a common misreading of the verse: it is not a rejection of moral judgment as such, nor does it align with the claim that "moral judgments never matter." To the contrary, the verse presupposes that moral wrongs are real, and it offers a framework for responding to those wrongs that aligns with the broader ethical teaching of Jesus’ Sermon on the Plain.

### Part 1: Why Luke 6:37 is not a rejection of moral judgment’s importance
The claim that "moral judgments never matter" is a form of moral nihilism or relativism: it denies any objective distinction between right and wrong, good and harmful, just and unjust, and holds that no action or character can be meaningfully evaluated. This is entirely alien to the teaching of Luke 6:37 and the Gospel as a whole. Jesus repeatedly and unequivocally engages in moral discernment: he condemns the Pharisees’ hypocrisy, calls out greed, sexual exploitation, violence, and neglect of the poor as sinful, affirms the goodness of compassion, mercy, and justice, and instructs his followers to "test the spirits" to discern false teaching from true.

The structure and context of Luke 6:37 make clear it is not banning moral evaluation, but condemning a specific, destructive form of it:
1. It follows immediately after the teaching of the log and the speck (Luke 6:41-42), where Jesus does not tell his listeners to ignore their neighbor’s fault, but to first address their own far greater moral failures before seeking to correct others. The problem he identifies is not noticing faults at all, but noticing others’ faults while ignoring your own, and doing so with a heart of superiority rather than care.
2. The verse pairs three parallel imperatives: "Judge not [...] condemn not [...] forgive." It does not say "there is no wrong to judge, no harm to condemn, no sin to forgive." Rather, it teaches: If you refrain from harsh, punitive, self-righteous judgment of others, you will not receive that same harsh judgment; if you extend forgiveness to others, you will receive forgiveness. The focus is on the *manner* of our response to moral failure, not the existence of moral failure itself.
3. Forgiveness only makes sense if there is something real to forgive. If moral judgments never mattered, there would be no such thing as wrongdoing, no harm to repair, and no reason to practice forgiveness. The verse presupposes that moral truths are real, and that our actions toward others have moral weight.

The "moral judgments never matter" view would also invalidate the entire ethical framework of Jesus’ teaching, which is rooted in a clear distinction between the way of life (love, mercy, justice) and the way of death (hatred, cruelty, exploitation). Luke 6:37 does not reject that distinction; it calls us to respond to violations of it with humility and grace, not self-righteous punishment.

### Part 2: Discernment vs. the appetite to condemn
The appetite to condemn is a perversion of the good, necessary moral faculty of discernment. Jesus forbids the former, not the latter.

#### Discernment
Discernment is the capacity for moral perception, rooted in love of truth and love of neighbor. It seeks to see reality clearly, including the reality of harm done, in order to respond well to it. Its core features include:
- **Rooted in self-awareness**: As Jesus teaches with the log and the speck, true discernment begins with honest acknowledgement of one’s own moral faults, so that one approaches others with humility, not the arrogance of someone who believes they are morally perfect.
- **Separates the person from their wrongdoing**: Discernment can recognize that a specific action, choice, or pattern of behavior is harmful or wrong, without defining the entire worth or identity of the person by that act. It holds space for the reality that people who do harm are often also wounded, and that redemption is possible.
- **Oriented toward restoration and justice**: The goal of discernment is to respond to harm in ways that repair damage, correct destructive patterns, and promote the flourishing of all people, including the person who has done wrong. It leads to accountability, not punishment for its own sake. For example, discernment might lead a community to hold a person who has committed violence accountable by requiring them to make amends to their victim and receive support to change their behavior, rather than permanently ostracizing or dehumanizing them.
- **Not punitive by nature**: Discernment can recognize that justice requires fair consequences for harm, but it does not delight in the suffering of the person who did wrong. It can call out systemic injustice (e.g., racism, exploitation) without reducing the people who perpetuate those systems to irredeemable villains.

#### The appetite to condemn
The appetite to condemn is a desire rooted in self-righteousness, pride, unresolved anger, or fear, that seeks to denigrate, punish, or exclude others based on their moral failures. It is the exact behavior Jesus warns against in Luke 6:37. Its core features include:
- **Rooted in self-deception**: As Jesus notes, the person who condemns others is almost always willfully ignoring their own far more severe moral faults, in order to feel superior to the person they are condemning. Condemnation is a defense mechanism against facing one’s own sin.
- **Conflates the person with their wrongdoing**: Condemnation does not just say "that action was wrong"; it says "you *are* your sin, you are worthless, irredeemable, less than me, and deserve to suffer." It erases the inherent worth of the person, reducing them to their worst mistake.
- **Oriented toward self-aggrandizement and separation, not restoration**: The goal of condemnation is not to repair harm or help the person change, but to affirm the condemner’s own sense of moral superiority, to vent anger, or to permanently separate the "good" (the condemner and their in-group) from the "bad" (the condemned). It delights in the punishment or suffering of the other, rather than seeking their good.
- **Final and unforgiving**: Condemnation passes a permanent, irreversible sentence on the person, leaving no room for repentance, change, or forgiveness. It is the opposite of the grace Jesus calls his followers to practice.

To return to the example of a person who has stolen from their workplace:
- Discernment would recognize that theft is a breach of trust that harms others, seek to hold the person accountable (e.g., requiring repayment, a performance plan, or support if they are struggling with addiction or financial crisis), and keep the door open for them to rebuild trust if they take responsibility for their actions. The person holding them accountable would also acknowledge their own past ethical lapses (e.g., cutting corners on a team project) to avoid self-righteousness.
- The appetite to condemn would lead someone to publicly shame the employee, call them a "career criminal," spread rumors to blacklist them from the industry, and refuse to ever consider their side of the story—all to feel like a "tough, moral authority" who is better than "criminals," with no regard for repairing harm or helping the person change.

Luke 6:37 does not call us to abandon discernment, which is a core moral good. It calls us to reject the urge to use that discernment as a weapon to condemn others, and instead pair clear-eyed recognition of right and wrong with the humility and forgiveness we hope to receive ourselves.
