Based on the text of Matthew 6:1 and its immediate context within the Sermon on the Mount, the moral danger lies not in **visibility itself**, but in the **craving to be seen**.

Here is an analysis of why the motive is the defining factor:

### 1. The Grammar of Purpose
The key phrase in the verse is "**to be seen of them**" (Greek: *hina theōrēthōsin*). This is a purpose clause. Jesus is not commanding that almsgiving be hidden from existence; He is commanding that it not be performed *for the purpose* of human observation.

If visibility were the sin, the early church’s public sharing of resources (Acts 2:44–45) or Jesus’ own instruction to "let your light shine before others" (Matthew 5:16) would be contradictory. Instead, the text targets the internal orientation of the giver.

### 2. The Economics of Reward
The verse concludes with the consequence: "...otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven."

This introduces a theological economic principle: **You cannot receive payment from two masters.**
*   If the goal of the action is human praise, and you receive human praise, you have received your "reward in full."
*   To then expect a reward from God is to treat God as a backup plan or a second paycheck.
*   The moral danger is that the giver has essentially "cashed out" their spiritual merit for earthly applause.

### 3. The Definition of Hypocrisy
In the very next verse (Matthew 6:2), Jesus identifies the people who do this as "hypocrites." In the Greek context, a *hypokrites* was an actor on a stage.

The moral danger is **performative righteousness**. When goodness is performed for an audience, the actor is no longer serving God or the needy; they are serving their own reputation. The act of charity becomes a tool for self-aggrandizement rather than an expression of love.

### 4. Displacement of the Audience
The verse specifies "before men" vs. "Father which is in heaven."
*   **The Danger:** The moral danger is idolatry of the self. By seeking to be seen, the individual places their own image and reputation above their relationship with God.
*   **The Correction:** Jesus does not say "do not let anyone know." He says "do not do it *to be seen*." It is possible to do good publicly where others happen to see it, provided the intent was to honor God and help the neighbor, not to cultivate a following.

### Summary
The problem is not that the action is public, but that the **intent is theatrical.**

*   **Visibility** is a circumstance (it may happen accidentally).
*   **The Craving** is a sin (it is a deliberate choice of motivation).

Therefore, the moral danger is that the seeker of public praise has shifted their ultimate allegiance from God to the opinion of their peers, thereby nullifying the spiritual value of the good deed.
