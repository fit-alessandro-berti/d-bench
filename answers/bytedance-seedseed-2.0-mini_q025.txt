The verse doesn't explicitly state that public goodness is morally dangerous in and of itself. Instead, it portrays it as a situation where the doer would be left without a divine reward. This is because the potential sin is one of motivation, not of the act's nature. However, depending on the doer's motives, the act of doing good things in public is still fraught with peril.

Matthew 6:1 states that individuals should avoid performing "alms before men" to be "seen of them." While doing so makes the acts visible, the actual moral hazard lies in the motivation behind such public displays of almsgiving. Specifically, the danger arises when one craves the recognition that comes with being seen, leading to actions driven more by a desire for praise or validation from others than by a genuine willingness to do good. This implies that the reward sought for acts of public goodness may be a false honor—earthly fame or praise—rather than the true spiritual reward one should seek. 

The verse suggests that the primary issue is not the visibility of the acts themselves but the underlying craving to be seen: the risk of misplacing one's motivations for doing good. In this view, public displays of generosity should still be driven by a sincere sense of charity rather than a desire to be recognized and praised. Such actions would then lose their value, as the focus of the moral actor would have shifted from a genuine desire to perform good to the pursuit of social approval.
