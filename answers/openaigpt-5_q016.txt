Discomfort with mercy itself—rooted in self-righteousness—is the elder brother’s deepest problem.

- His anger, envy, and wounded sense of fairness are symptoms. The root is a merit-based identity: “These many years I serve/slave for you… you never gave me a goat.” He relates to the father as a servant earning wages, not as a son sharing the father’s heart.
- Because his worth rests on being the obedient, deserving one, the father’s free joy over the repentant brother feels like a threat. Grace levels the field; he cannot rejoice in it.
- He distances himself from both father and brother (“this son of yours”), showing he does not love what the father loves. The father replies, “this your brother,” and invites him into the celebration—into mercy—yet he refuses.

So while pride in obedience is a key component, the deepest issue is a heart out of sync with the father’s joy in saving the lost; he cannot bear mercy when it is given to someone he deems unworthy.
