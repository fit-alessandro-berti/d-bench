**Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it collides head-on with our deepest evolutionary, emotional, and cultural wiring.**

### Why it's hard

1. **Biological and evolutionary mismatch**  
   Human beings are wired for kin-selection and reciprocal altruism. We instinctively protect "us" and distrust or neutralize "them." Loving an enemy requires overriding the amygdala-driven threat response and the dopamine loops that reward tribal loyalty and revenge. From an evolutionary standpoint, consistently blessing those who curse you looks like a maladaptive strategy that could get you killed or exploited. The fact that Jesus immediately ties it to becoming "children of your Father" signals that this is not a natural human virtue; it is a supernatural one.

2. **Moral psychology and justice intuitions**  
   Most humans have a strong innate sense of retributive justice. When someone harms us or those we love, the desire for reciprocity ("they should feel what I felt") feels like moral clarity, not vice. Enemy-love can feel like betrayal of the victims, a softening of the line between good and evil. In an age of social media and identity politics, this instinct is supercharged: the enemy is not just a person but a symbol of an entire oppressive system. To love them feels like moral cowardice or ideological surrender.

3. **Sentimentality objection**  
   A great deal of what passes for "love your enemies" in modern culture *is* sentimental—cheap forgiveness without truth, "all you need is love" bromides that ignore real evil, power differentials, and the necessity of justice. When people see enemy-love reduced to emotional warmth or conflict avoidance, they rightly reject it as naïve or even dangerous. Jesus does *not* say "feel warm fuzzies toward those who are trying to kill you." He commands concrete actions: bless, do good, pray. That is far more rigorous than sentiment.

4. **Strategic objection**  
   Some read it as brilliant long-term pragmatism: kill them with kindness, win them over, or at least rob them of the power to define you. There is *some* truth here—history shows that non-violent movements (Gandhi, King, the early church) often succeed where violence fails. But Jesus does not ground the command in results. He grounds it in the character of God: "that ye may be the children of your Father... for he maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good." The sun and rain are not strategic tools; they are expressions of divine goodness that is not contingent on the recipient's merit. The command is therefore deontological and theological before it is consequentialist.

### So is it sentimental, strategic, or deeper moral strength?

It is primarily the third, but the first two are constant temptations and partial truths.

- **Not primarily sentimental.** True enemy-love is costly, unsentimental, and clear-eyed. It can coexist with sharp moral judgment, boundaries, and even the use of force in restraint of evil (the New Testament never tells the state to turn the other cheek). Jesus himself denounced religious leaders in the strongest terms while still going to the cross for them. The command is not "never oppose evil" but "oppose it in a way that does not make you into the image of the evil you oppose."

- **Not merely strategic.** While it often has powerful strategic fruits, Jesus' rationale is explicitly *imitatio Dei*—the imitation of God's own character. God is not playing a long game to convert the wicked; He is good *by nature*, and therefore gives good gifts indiscriminately. Enemy-love is a participation in divine perfection (see Matthew 5:48), not a technique for social change. Treating it only as strategy cheapens it and leaves you defenseless when the strategy appears to fail (as it did for Jesus on the cross, at least in the short term).

- **A deeper form of moral strength.** This is the heart of it. It is moral strength of the highest order because it requires the greatest possible self-transcendence. It demands that we refuse to let the enemy's hatred define our own identity and response. It is strength because it breaks the cycle of retaliation that has governed human history. Nietzsche thought it was slave morality; in reality, only the strongest souls can do it. Most of us can love our friends and even tolerate strangers. Only someone whose identity is anchored outside this world's economy of honor and shame can bless those who curse them.

The early church understood this. They prayed for the emperors who fed them to lions. That is not weakness. That is a claim that the love of God is more fundamental than the violence of Caesar. When the church forgets this, it becomes just another tribe. When it remembers it, it becomes salt and light—even when it loses.

Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it is supernatural. It is not an advanced human ethic; it is a glimpse of the divine ethic breaking into a world that runs on revenge. That is precisely why Jesus presents it as the distinctive mark of God's children. The question is not whether it "works." The question is whether we are willing to look like our Father.
