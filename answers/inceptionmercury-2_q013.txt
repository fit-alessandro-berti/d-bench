**Short answer:**  
Amos is not merely asking Israel to “improve the balance” between ritual and ethics; he is exposing a **systemic, covenant‑breaking fraud** in which the nation’s religious ceremonies have become a **sham façade** for a society that has abandoned the very justice, mercy, and humility that the covenant demands. The “worship” he rejects is therefore **false worship**—a ritualistic performance that pretends to honor God while the people live in oppression, exploitation, and moral corruption. The passage’s harsh tone reflects the prophetic conviction that **no amount of outward piety can atone for a heart that has turned away from the covenant’s ethical core**.

---

## 1. The historical‑literary setting

| Element | What it means for the passage |
|--------|------------------------------|
| **Amos’s audience** | The northern kingdom of Israel (the “Samaria” kingdom) in the 8th c. BCE, a period of unprecedented wealth, urbanization, and militarization. |
| **Covenant background** | Deuteronomy 10:12‑13, 15‑19 and the prophetic “social‑justice” tradition repeatedly link **faithful worship** with **care for the poor, the widowed, the orphaned, and the foreigner**. |
| **Prophetic genre** | The “court‑oracles” of the Minor Prophets (e.g., “I will not hear your music” — cf. Jer 7:31‑34) are intentionally hyperbolic to shock the audience into repentance. |
| **Immediate trigger** | Israel’s annual “feast days” (Passover, Tabernacles, etc.) and the associated **sacrificial system** were being celebrated with great pomp while the nation’s social fabric was collapsing under exploitation, land‑grab, and corruption. |
