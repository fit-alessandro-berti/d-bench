This is a penetrating question that goes to the heart of Jesus’ parable. The elder brother’s reaction is a complex knot of several issues, but if we look closely, we can identify a **core, root problem** from which the others grow.

Let’s briefly examine each potential issue:

1.  **Anger & Envy:** These are the most *visible* symptoms. He is “angry” and refuses to join the celebration. His speech drips with resentment (“this son of yours,” not “my brother”). He envies the feast and the attention given to his wayward sibling.

2.  **Wounded Fairness:** His complaint has a logical, even moral, structure: “I’ve been perfectly obedient for years and got nothing; he wastes everything and gets a party.” This feels like a violation of a just, reward-based system.

3.  **Pride in Obedience:** He defines his relationship with his father in terms of **slavery and transaction**: “Lo, these many years do I *serve* thee.” He hasn’t broken a commandment, but his obedience has been joyless, duty-bound, and aimed at earning his inheritance (which he already possesses, v. 31).

4.  **Discomfort with Mercy:** This is the deepest layer. He cannot tolerate a system where grace is given, not earned. The father’s mercy to the younger brother isn’t just unfair to him—it *offends* his entire worldview. Mercy disrupts the economy of merit he has built his life upon.

**So, what is the *deepest* problem?**

The elder brother’s **deepest problem is his discomfort with, and rejection of, the father’s unconditional, merciful love.**

All the other issues are fruits of this root:
*   His **anger and envy** flare because he sees mercy extended without merit.
*   His **wounded fairness** is the cry of a meritocratic heart confronting scandalous grace.
*   His **pride in obedience** is the posture of one who tries to *control* the father through service, making love a wage rather than a gift.

**The clinching evidence is in the father’s response (v. 31):** “Son, you are ever with me, and all that I have is yours.” The elder brother has been living in the father’s house, surrounded by his abundance, yet he has **never truly entered into the joy of the father’s heart**. He was physically present but relationally distant. He had access to the inheritance but didn’t enjoy the intimacy.

**In essence, the elder brother is just as lost as the younger one, but in a more respectable way.**
*   The younger brother was lost in *dissolute living*.
*   The elder brother is lost in *self-righteous duty and a heart closed to mercy*.

Jesus told this parable to the Pharisees and teachers of the law who grumbled that he “welcomes sinners” (Luke 15:1-2). The elder brother represents those who believe God’s favor must be earned through obedience and moral purity, and who become scandalized when God lavishes grace on the undeserving.

**Final Answer:** While anger, envy, and pride are all present, the elder brother’s **deepest problem is his inability to celebrate the father’s mercy because it subverts the transactional, merit-based system by which he has chosen to live and be loved.** His heart is closed to the reckless, joyful, unearned grace that is the very heart of the father.
