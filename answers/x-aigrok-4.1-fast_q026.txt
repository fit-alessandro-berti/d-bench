### Connection Between Speech and Hidden Moral Stockpiles

The passage uses vivid metaphors to link **speech** (what the mouth says) with **hidden moral stockpiles** in the heart (described as "treasure" or "abundance"). The heart is portrayed as an internal reservoir or storehouse stocked with either **good** or **evil**—accumulated through one's moral character, beliefs, desires, and choices over time. Speech isn't arbitrary or superficial; it's the **natural overflow** ("out of the abundance") from this hidden treasury. 

- Jesus argues it's **impossible** for an "evil" heart (stocked with vipers' poison, implying deceit and malice) to produce genuine "good things" in speech. 
- Conversely, a "good" heart yields good speech, like treasure brought forth from a vault.

This implies a **causal, involuntary dynamic**: Words aren't manufactured on the spot but betray the heart's dominant content. It's a principle of spiritual diagnostics—speech exposes what one truly values and harbors inwardly.

### Words as Symptoms, Choices, Revelations, or All Three?

The passage suggests words function as **all three**, but with **revelations** as the primary emphasis, **symptoms** as a close secondary, and **choices** as foundational yet indirect:

| Category | How It Fits the Passage | Key Evidence |
|----------|--------------------------|--------------|
| **Revelations** (Primary) | Words **disclose** or unveil the heart's hidden moral stockpiles, making the invisible visible. Jesus uses this to condemn the Pharisees: their hypocritical accusations against Him reveal their true evil nature ("generation of vipers"). Speech is authentic evidence, not deception. | "Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh" + "bringeth forth" (like drawing from a hidden source). |
| **Symptoms** (Strong Secondary) | Words act as **diagnostic indicators** of the heart's moral health, like fruit revealing a tree's root system (echoing Matthew 7:16–20). Evil speech signals an evil-stocked heart; good speech signals good stores. | Rhetorical question: "How can ye, being evil, speak good things?"—evil words are telltale signs of inner corruption. |
| **Choices** (Foundational/Indirect) | Words emerge from **chosen accumulations** in the heart (what one treasures via decisions, habits, and allegiances). Speaking isn't random; it's the selected expression of one's moral inventory. However, the focus is downstream on outflow, not the act of choosing words itself. | "Good treasure" vs. "evil treasure"—implies deliberate stocking over time (e.g., via faith, obedience, or rebellion). |

**Overall**: This isn't just poetic; it's a **theological claim** about human nature in Jesus' teaching. The heart drives speech (Luke 6:45 parallels this), so words serve as a **mirror, not a mask**. To change speech, one must transform the heart (e.g., through repentance). Modern parallels include psychology (e.g., "Freudian slips") or proverb: "The mouth reveals the heart." For application, it warns against self-deception—judge character by consistent words, not claims.
