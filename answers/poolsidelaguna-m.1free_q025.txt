
The moral danger in Matthew 6:1 stems not from the visibility of the act itself, but from the **intent** behind it—the craving to be seen by others and thereby gain their approval or acclaim. Here's a breakdown of why this is the case:

---

### 1. **The Verse’s Focus on Motivation**
   - Jesus explicitly warns against doing alms (acts of charity) "**to be seen of them**" (τὸ φανερὸν ποιῆτε, *to be made visible*). The Greek emphasizes *purpose*: the acts are done *so that* others might observe them. This situates the problem squarely in the **heart’s motive** rather than the act’s visibility.  
   - The consequence—"no reward of your Father which is in heaven"—highlights that God judges the *motive*, not merely the external action. A reward (i.e., divine approval or recognition) requires a pure intention, not performative display.

---

### 2. **Jesus’ Broader Teaching on Hypocrisy**
   - This verse is part of the Sermon on the Mount, where Jesus repeatedly contrasts outward religiosity with inward faithfulness. For example:
     - He tells his audience to let their left hand not know what their right hand does when giving alms (Matt. 6:3), emphasizing *secrecy* to avoid self-glorification.
     - He similarly critiques praying "in the streets" to be seen (Matt. 6:5), contrasting it with private prayer to the Father who "sees in secret" (Matt. 6:4, 6:6).  
   - These teachings critique the hypocrite’s desire for human praise (*doxa*), which prioritizes earthly reputation over divine approval. Visibility becomes dangerous only when paired with this prideful motive.

---

### 3. **The Problem of "Worshiping God in Food and Drink… to Be Seen by Others" (Luke 16:15)**
   - Elsewhere, Jesus emphasizes that God desires "mercy" over "sacrifice" (Hosea 6:6/Matt. 9:13). The danger in public goodness is not the act itself but when it becomes a tool for *self-aggrandizement*.  
   - The Greek concept of *phainein* ("to show") (*phainesthe*, "to be seen") implies performance—an audience-driven act. This aligns with the biblical critique of "works of the flesh" (Gal. 5:19-21), where self-centered showiness is listed among vices.

---

### 4. **The Reward System in Jesus’ Teaching**
   - The "reward" Jesus mentions (Matt. 6:4, 6:6, 6:18) is not about temporal gain but eternal significance. If one’s charity is aimed at human praise, they have already received their "reward" in full (Matt. 6:2). But those who give in secret ("let not thy left hand know...") will be rewarded *by God* who sees and honors concealed virtue.  
   - Thus, visibility itself is neutral. The problem arises when visibility becomes the *goal* rather than a byproduct of sincere service.

---

### Conclusion: Intent Over Visibility  
The moral danger lies in the **craving to be seen**, which corrupts the very act of goodness. When charity becomes a performance for human applause, it turns inward (self-focused) rather than outward (God- and neighbor-focused). Jesus isn’t condemning all public acts of kindness—only those *motivated by pride or vanity*. The hidden motive of seeking earthly approval invites judgment, while discreet, grace-motivated giving secures divine reward.  

In short: *visibility* is circumstantial; *desire for recognition* is the peril.
