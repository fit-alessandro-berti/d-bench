The moral danger in Matthew 6:1 lies primarily in **the craving to be seen**, rather than visibility itself. Here's a breakdown of the reasoning:

### 1. **Textual Analysis**:
   - The verse explicitly states: *"Take heed that ye do not your alms before men, **to be seen of them**..."*  
     The phrase *"to be seen of them"* indicates that the **intention** behind the act is the critical issue. The danger is not merely performing an act publicly, but doing so **with the purpose of seeking human recognition or approval**.

### 2. **Motivation vs. Action**:
   - Jesus later clarifies in verses 6:2–4 that true charity should be done *"in secret"* so that *"your Father, who sees in secret, will reward you."*  
     This suggests that the **visibility of the act** is secondary to the **heart's motive**. If someone gives publicly *without* desiring praise, the act could still be pleasing to God (though secrecy is ideal). However, if the **desire for human approval** drives the action, it becomes morally problematic.

### 3. **Theological Context**:
   - In the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5–7), Jesus emphasizes internal righteousness over external performance (e.g., 5:20: *"Unless your righteousness exceeds that of the scribes and Pharisees, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven"*).  
     The Pharisees were criticized for their ostentatious piety (e.g., tithing spices while neglecting justice and mercy; Luke 11:42). Their wrongdoing lay not in acting publicly, but in **seeking prestige** through public displays.

### 4. **The "Reward" in Question**:
   - The phrase *"no reward of your Father"* (v. 1) implies that **divine approval is voided** when acts are performed for self-glorification. The craving for human praise **replaces** the proper motivation of loving God and neighbor.

### 5. **Broader Biblical Themes**:
   - The Bible consistently links **heart motives** to moral worth (e.g., 1 Samuel 16:7: *"The Lord does not look at the things people look at. People look at the outward appearance, but the Lord looks at the heart"*).  
   - Similarly, Jesus condemns hypocrisy when worship or service becomes a tool for self-aggrandizement (Matthew 23:5–7).

### Conclusion:
The **craving to be seen** is the root issue because it reflects a love for human praise rather than love for God. Visibility itself is not inherently wrong—it is the **desire for recognition** that corrupts the act. Jesus’ teaching redirects the believer’s focus from external validation to internal devotion and humility before God. As He summarizes later: *"Truly, I say to you, you yourselves are the ones who are making the tree fruitful"* (Luke 6:44)—the heart’s condition determines the morality of outward actions.
