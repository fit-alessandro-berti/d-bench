**Kind of Power Being Praised:**

The passage from Philippians 2:5–8 praises a *paradoxical* kind of power—what could be called the power of *humble self-emptying*. Unlike power that asserts itself through domination, coercion, or display, here Paul marvels at **divine greatness revealed in voluntary self-lowering**:

- Christ is described as being "in the form of God" (possessing ultimate status and equality with God).
- Yet, instead of using that status as a privilege, **he "made himself of no reputation"** (literally, "emptied himself"), adopting the position of a servant.
- His humility takes tangible form: not merely acting humble, but truly **becoming human** and further, accepting a humiliating, violent death "even the death of the cross"—the ultimate symbol of shame in the ancient world.

**Why Humility Joined to Greatness Is Unsettling:**

1. **Contradicts Normal Expectations of Power:**
   - Usually, those with the greatest power assert it. When people have status or authority, they reinforce hierarchies and demand respect. Within human societies, domination is the default expression of greatness.
   - For the one who *genuinely* possesses unlimited power to *voluntarily surrender* status, serve others, and embrace suffering **overturns our deepest expectations about how greatness should look and act**.

2. **Demands a Rethinking of Value:**
   - If the highest greatness is best shown not by taking but by giving, not by ruling but by serving, what does that mean for how we live—or what we value in others?
   - Christ’s humility, if truly grasped, invites us to re-evaluate all systems of worth, authority, and ambition.

3. **Strips Us of Excuses:**
   - If God could humble himself to this extent, what excuse remains for our pride, self-assertion, or refusal to take the servant’s role?
   - **Such humility in the greatest** exposes all our attempts to justify selfishness, pride, or ego.

4. **Disarms, Not Subdues:**
   - Domination can be resisted or resented; people know how to react to open power. But humility from the greatest *disarms* us—it exposes our own motives, evokes a response, and often inspires love or awe rather than simple submission.

In summary:  
**The power being praised in Philippians 2:5–8 is the paradoxical and unsettling power of humility—to choose downward mobility and servanthood not from weakness, but from the summit of greatness. It unsettles us because it reverses normal power dynamics, calls our own ambitions into question, and asks us to imagine a new kind of greatness—one rooted in love and self-giving rather than domination.**
