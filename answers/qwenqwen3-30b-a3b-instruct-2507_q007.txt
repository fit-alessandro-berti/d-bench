Luke 6:37 — *"Judge not, and ye shall not be judged: condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned: forgive, and ye shall be forgiven."* — is often misunderstood as a call to abandon all moral evaluation. However, a careful reading reveals a nuanced distinction between **condemning judgment** and **wise discernment**. The passage does not abolish moral responsibility or the need for discernment; rather, it challenges the *attitude* and *motive* behind judgment.

### 1. **Judging vs. Discerning: A Crucial Distinction**

- **Judging (condemning)**: This refers to a self-righteous, critical, or punitive stance toward others. It involves forming harsh opinions about people’s character, motives, or worth based on perceived failure — often from a place of pride or superiority. Jesus warns against this because such judgment reflects our own flaws (cf. Matthew 7:1–5, where He speaks of the "plank" in our own eye). It's not merely disapproval; it's **finality**, **hatred**, and **lack of mercy**.

- **Discernment**: This is the ability to assess situations, behaviors, or beliefs in light of truth and love, without condemnation. Discernment is necessary for spiritual and ethical guidance. For example, Paul urges believers to “test everything; hold fast what is good” (1 Thessalonians 5:21). Discernment does not demand perfection in others but seeks understanding, correction, and restoration (Galatians 6:1).

> **Key Difference**: Judging seeks to *destroy* the person; discernment seeks to *restore* the person.

### 2. **The Appetite to Condemn vs. The Mercy to Forgive**

Jesus connects judgment with consequence: *"Ye shall not be judged... ye shall not be condemned."* This isn't about human courts or justice per se, but about **how we relate to others — and how we open ourselves to God’s mercy**.

- The *appetite to condemn* stems from pride, fear, or a desire for control. It's a heart posture that looks down on others, clinging to self-justification.
- **Forgiveness**, on the other hand, flows from humility, grace, and an awareness of one’s own need for mercy (cf. Matthew 6:14–15). When we forgive, we reflect God’s character — a God who judges with justice but also forgives with abundance.

> As Jesus says elsewhere: *"Be merciful, as your Father is merciful."* (Luke 6:36) — which immediately precedes this verse. Mercy is the hallmark of the believer.

### 3. **Moral Judgments Still Matter — But Possess the Right Heart**

This doesn't mean that moral distinctions are irrelevant. In fact, Scripture is full of moral teaching — about love, justice, purity, truth, etc. But the **manner** in which we uphold truth matters deeply.

- We are called to uphold truth *in love* (Ephesians 4:15).
- We are to confront sin *not to shame*, but to restore (Galatians 6:1).
- We are to point to Christ, not to hold others in emotional exile.

> Example: A church leader noticing doctrinal error in another believer might *discern* and confront the error — but does so with humility, seeking repentance and reconciliation, not public shaming.

### Summary

Luke 6:37 does **not** teach that moral judgments are irrelevant. Instead, it warns against:

- **Self-righteous condemnation** — judging others as though we are faultless.
- **The spirit of judgment** — which fosters bitterness, division, and spiritual pride.

It calls us instead to:

- **Exercise wisdom** (discernment) without arrogance.
- **Affirm truth** without hate.
- **Offer forgiveness** as an act of grace — not because the offense is trivial, but because we know we ourselves have been forgiven.

As C.S. Lewis put it: *"We are not to judge others with anger or pride. But we must still judge—only not with the same heart that judges men."*

> Ultimately, Jesus invites us to live by the principle: *"Do not judge by appearances, but judge with righteous judgment."* (John 7:24) — a judgment shaped not by self, but by the Holy Spirit and the light of Christ.
