Enemy‑love is hard to take seriously because it confronts almost every instinct we have about justice, safety, and identity. In Matthew 5 Jesus isn’t giving a “nice extra” for unusually holy people; he is redefining what it means to be *God’s children*—to resemble the Father who gives sun and rain to both just and unjust.

Let’s take your question in three parts.

---

## 1. Why is enemy-love so difficult to take seriously?

**a. It clashes with our intuitive sense of justice**

We naturally feel:  
- Good should be rewarded.  
- Evil should be punished.  
- Hurt should not be left “unanswered.”

So “love your enemies” can sound like:  
- “Pretend evil isn’t evil.”  
- “Let people walk all over you.”  
- “Abandon justice for sentiment.”

From inside the wound, mercy looks like betrayal—of ourselves, of victims, of justice itself. That’s part of why it feels unrealistic or even offensive.

**b. It conflicts with basic survival instincts**

On an evolutionary/psychological level, we’re wired to:
- Protect ourselves from threats.
- Favor “our group” over “their group.”
- Return force for force to deter aggression.

Enemy-love pushes against all three. It asks you to:
- Stay morally open in the face of threat.
- Recognize the humanity of an enemy.
- Refuse to be defined by fear or retaliation.

That feels like standing unarmed in a world of weapons.

**c. It threatens our identity and narratives**

We get a lot of meaning from:
- Knowing who “we” are.
- Knowing who “they” are.
- Telling a story in which we are the wronged, and “they” are the problem.

Enemy-love doesn’t let you keep clean lines:
- You must see your enemy as capable of more than their worst act.
- You must admit your own capacity for wrong.
- You must surrender the comfort of a simple “us vs. them” story.

That is destabilizing, because hate is often emotionally organizing. Enemy‑love disorganizes our hatred.

---

## 2. Is enemy-love sentimental?

It can be *misunderstood* sentimentally, but in the Sermon on the Mount it is not soft emotion, it is cruciform realism.

**Not what it means:**
- Not liking your enemy.
- Not excusing injustice.
- Not feeling warm or passive.
- Not eliminating consequences, courts, or self‑defense.

**What it does mean (biblically):**
- Willing their good before God (“pray for those who persecute you”).
- Refusing vengeance and hatred as your operating system.
- Seeking their repentance and restoration, not their destruction.
- Imitating God’s indiscriminate generosity (“sun…rain…on the evil and the good”).

In Christian terms, this is *agape*: a chosen, costly goodwill shaped by God’s character, not by the enemy’s behavior.

Sentiment says, “Everyone’s fine, nothing is really wrong.”  
Agape says, “What you did is wrong—and I will not let your wrongness create wrongness in me.”

---

## 3. Is it strategic?

There *is* a strategic dimension, but that’s not its deepest point.

Historically, enemy‑love has proven powerful as a strategy of resistance:
- **Jesus** refuses violent revolt; his non-retaliation exposes the injustice of his execution and becomes the center of Christian faith.
- **Gandhi** (though not Christian) builds a mass movement on nonviolent resistance, aiming to awaken the conscience of the oppressor.
- **Martin Luther King Jr.** explicitly roots civil rights nonviolence in Jesus’ command to love enemies, arguing that:
  - Hate multiplies hate.
  - Only love can break the cycle and transform the opponent.

In all these cases, loving the enemy:
- Unmasks violence.
- Wins public moral clarity.
- Sometimes even changes the oppressor.

So yes, it can be strategically wise—especially where cycles of retaliation only deepen oppression.

But in Matthew 5, the main *reason* is not “because it works,” but:  
> “that ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven.”

The logic is *theological before it is tactical*:  
Do this, because this is what God is like.

---

## 4. Or is it a deeper form of moral strength?

Enemy‑love is best understood as a higher, costlier strength.

**a. Strength over your own heart**

Anyone can:
- Strike back.
- Nurture bitterness.
- Dehumanize an enemy.

It takes greater strength to:
- Absorb the offense without letting it define you.
- Refuse to mirror the wrong done to you.
- Keep your moral clarity without hatred.

Here, victory is first *over yourself*:
- Your rage.
- Your desire for revenge.
- Your delight in the enemy’s downfall.

**b. Strength that breaks cycles**

If you hit me, and I hit you back harder, we both understand it—and we both perpetuate it.  
Enemy-love interrupts the pattern:

- It says, “The chain stops with me.”
- It denies evil the power to endlessly reproduce itself through you.
- It introduces something into history that doesn’t originate in the aggressor: grace.

That is not weakness; it’s a radical exertion of moral agency.

**c. Strength that resembles God, not the enemy**

Jesus’ point in verse 45 is imitatio Dei—imitation of God:
- God gives sun and rain to people who ignore Him and use His gifts against Him.
- God’s generosity is not calibrated to people’s goodness.

To “be children of your Father” is:
- To let your character be shaped more by God’s nature than by your enemy’s actions.
- To refuse to let someone else’s sin become the blueprint of your behavior.

That is a deeper strength than retaliation because it takes its cue from an unchanging standard instead of from the latest injury.

---

## 5. So what is enemy-love, finally?

- It is **not** sentimental softness.
- It is **sometimes** strategically powerful.
- It is, at root, a **form of moral and spiritual strength**:
  - Clear-eyed about evil.
  - Committed to justice.
  - Refusing vengeance.
  - Seeking the enemy’s ultimate good before God.
  - Grounded in the belief that this is how God Himself loves.

Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it demands we give up not only our hatred, but our *right* to hatred. In Christian terms, it is only truly thinkable if one believes what the text assumes: that God sees, judges, and will finally set the world right—so that we are free not to answer every wrong with our own.
