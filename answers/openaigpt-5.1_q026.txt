In this passage Jesus is doing several things at once, and they all turn on the metaphor of the **heart as a treasury** and **speech as its overflow**.

### 1. Heart as “moral stockpile”

The key words are:

- “out of the **abundance** of the heart the mouth speaketh”  
- “good **treasure** of the heart” / “evil **treasure**”

The heart is pictured as a *storehouse* (the Greek word for “treasure” is *thēsauros*—a chest, a treasury). Over time, a person “stocks” this inner treasury with either good or evil—affections, loyalties, beliefs, desires.

Speech is the “overflow” of whatever has been stored up.

So the logic is:

- What fills the heart in abundance  
→ will inevitably spill out in words.

### 2. Words as symptoms

In that sense, words are **symptoms**:

- They *disclose the existing condition* of the heart.
- Jesus is answering the Pharisees’ blasphemous words (calling his works demonic). He diagnoses them: “O generation of vipers… being evil…” Their speech is taken as evidence of an already-formed inner evil.

Thus, speech does not *create* the heart-condition in this text; it *reveals* it. You listen to someone long enough, you learn what is really in their heart.

### 3. Words as revelations

This makes speech also a form of **revelation**:

- It takes what is hidden (the “treasure” in the heart)  
→ and makes it public and knowable.
- The inner moral reality is “brought forth” into the open by words.

In the extended context (vv. 36–37), this revelatory role is so serious that words become usable in judgment: “by thy words thou shalt be justified, and by thy words thou shalt be condemned.” Words are evidence because they uncover the heart.

### 4. Words as choices (moral acts)

At the same time, in Scripture words are not *only* involuntary symptoms. They’re also **morally responsible choices**:

- The command to bridle the tongue (James 3), to put away corrupt speech (Eph 4:29), presupposes that speech is an act of will.
- In this very passage, Jesus warns them about “every idle word” (v. 36). That warning would be pointless if words were sheer reflex with no moral agency.

So while Jesus grounds speech in the heart’s condition (“being evil”), he still holds people accountable for what they say. Speech is:

- Shaped and constrained by the heart’s stockpile;  
- Yet still *chosen* and judged as real moral action.

### 5. Putting it together

In Matthew 12:34–35, speech is:

- **A symptom** – it manifests the underlying moral condition (“out of the abundance of the heart…”).
- **A revelation** – it brings the hidden “treasure” (good or bad) into the open.
- **A choice** – it is a morally accountable act, such that people are judged “by [their] words.”

So the passage does not let us reduce speech to any one of those. Rather:

> Your words are chosen actions that inevitably reveal and diagnose the moral stockpile of your heart.
