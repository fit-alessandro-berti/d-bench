**Repentance in Isaiah 55:6–7 requires forsaking both the wicked “way” (outward conduct/path of life) and the unrighteous “thoughts” (inward imaginations, designs, and mind-sets), because true turning to God addresses the whole person; this shows that surface reformation is insufficient while genuine, comprehensive return meets abundant mercy and pardon.**

### “Way” and “Thoughts”
- **“Way”** (Heb. *derek*) denotes the traveler’s road, manner of life, habitual conduct, and chosen course of action. The call is for the wicked to abandon the lived pattern of sin—the visible, practiced lifestyle that leads away from God.
- **“Thoughts”** (Heb. *maḥšĕbôt*) points to the inner realm: purposes, plans, imaginations, reasoning, and heart-level inclinations. Parallelism pairs the “unrighteous man” with this term so that repentance reaches motives and mental frameworks, not merely external behavior.

The verse deliberately yokes the two. Outward acts spring from (and are sustained by) inward thoughts; changing only one leaves the root or the fruit intact. Scripture consistently treats sin as both deed and disposition (cf. Gen 6:5; Prov 23:7; Matt 15:19; Rom 12:2). Mere behavioral reform without a renewed mind, or pious introspection without a changed life, falls short of the repentance Isaiah summons.

### Implications for Outward Conduct, Inward Imagination, and Mercy
- **Outward conduct** must be forsaken. The “way” is not neutral; it is the wicked path that must be left. Moral seriousness and visible reformation are required—not as self-earned merit, but as the fruit of turning. Superficial religiosity that never alters daily walk is incompatible with the call.
- **Inward imagination** must likewise be forsaken. Thoughts, schemes, self-justifications, and cherished fantasies that sustain sin are to be abandoned. This reaches the hidden life of the mind/heart where desires and intentions dwell. God sees and weighs the heart; partial repentance that never touches the inner world is inadequate.
- **Possibility of mercy and abundant pardon**: The structure of the text is invitation → forsaking of way + thoughts → return → divine response. The same God who commands comprehensive repentance immediately promises “he will have mercy” and “he will abundantly pardon.” Mercy is not withheld until perfection is achieved; it meets the one who genuinely turns. The abundance of pardon matches (and exceeds) the breadth of the repentance demanded—covering both the outer life and the inner world that God alone fully knows. The larger context of Isaiah 55 (free invitation to the thirsty, the sure mercies of David, the efficacious coexistence of God’s word) underscores that this is gospel grace, not works-righteousness: the Lord is near and findable; the barrier is human refusal, not divine reluctance.

In short, Isaiah unites external and internal dimensions so that repentance is neither empty moralism nor mere sentiment. The wicked must leave the road and the thoughts that define life apart from God; those who do so and return find a God who delights to show mercy and to pardon abundantly. This remains the biblical pattern: whole-person turning met by whole-hearted divine grace.
