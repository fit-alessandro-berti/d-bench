**Repentance involves both “way” and “thoughts” because it must be total and holistic.** 

“Way” (*derek* in Hebrew) refers to the outward conduct, lifestyle, habits, and path of life—the visible actions and course one walks in. “Thoughts” (*machashabah*) refers to the inward imagination, intentions, plans, purposes, and desires of the mind and heart. The verse deliberately pairs the *wicked* with his *way* and the *unrighteous man* with his *thoughts*, showing that sin corrupts the whole person. It is never merely external behavior or merely private fantasy; evil thoughts and desires give birth to evil actions, and sinful actions reinforce corrupt thinking (see Genesis 6:5; Proverbs 23:7; Matthew 5:28; James 1:14–15).

This rules out superficial or partial “repentance.” One cannot simply stop certain behaviors while cherishing the same lusts, pride, greed, or rebellion in the heart, nor can one claim inner sincerity while continuing in unrighteous conduct. True repentance is a comprehensive turning (*shub*—return) to the Lord: the outer life must change *and* the inner life must be renewed. The mind must be reoriented, the desires reordered, and the imagination brought under God’s authority. This is why the New Testament speaks of repentance as a change of mind (*metanoia*) that produces fruit, and why Paul urges believers to “be transformed by the renewing of your mind” (Romans 12:2).

**Implications:**

- **Outward conduct** is real and observable; God is not indifferent to injustice, immorality, idolatry, or oppression. These “ways” must be forsaken. But outward change by itself can be hypocritical or self-righteous if the heart remains unchanged.
- **Inward imagination** is the deeper root. God sees and judges the thoughts and intents of the heart (Hebrews 4:12–13). Unrighteous thoughts—bitterness, covetousness, self-exaltation, fantasies of evil—must be actively rejected. The imagination is not neutral; it is a battleground.
- **The possibility of mercy** is therefore wonderfully wide and yet uncompromisingly serious. Even the *wicked* and *unrighteous* are invited to return while the Lord “may be found” and “is near.” When someone truly forsakes both way *and* thoughts in a wholehearted return, God does not offer meager forgiveness—He “will have mercy” and “abundantly pardon” (*yarbeh lisloach*—multiply forgiveness). Grace is not cheap; it requires genuine repentance. But neither is it stingy—God’s pardon is lavish for the truly repentant. 

This passage reveals both the depth of human sin and the height of divine compassion. No one is too far gone if they will seek the Lord with their whole self—actions and heart—while there is still time.
