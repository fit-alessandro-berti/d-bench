The elder brother’s deepest problem is **all of the above — but they all stem from a single root**: a transactional view of righteousness and a distorted understanding of the father’s character.

Let’s break down each layer of his complaint:

1. **Anger** — He is “angry, and would not go in.” This is the immediate emotion, but it’s not the cause; it’s the symptom.
2. **Envy** — He resents the celebration for his brother, revealing a heart that measures love by visible honors.
3. **Wounded fairness** — He says, “thou never gavest me a kid… But as soon as this thy son was come…” He sees love and reward as a system of just deserts, and he feels cheated.
4. **Pride in obedience** — “Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I…” His obedience has become a credential, a claim on the father’s favor rather than a response to love.
5. **Discomfort with mercy itself** — This is perhaps the deepest fracture. He cannot rejoice in unmerited grace because his entire identity is built on merit.

**At the core: The elder brother misunderstands the heart of the father.**  
He thinks the father’s love is **earnable** through obedience and **proportional** to good behavior. He has lived as a servant or employee in his father’s house, not as a son who enjoys the father’s affection freely.

The father’s response — “Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine” — reveals what the elder brother has missed:  
He already had everything, but he never really *received* it. His obedience had become joyless because it was motivated by duty and calculation, not by love. He missed the feast — not because it wasn’t offered, but because he believed he had to earn it, and couldn’t celebrate someone else receiving it for free.

So, his deepest problem is **a heart that cannot believe in unconditional love** — a heart that sees grace as unfair because it makes obedience seem pointless. In Christian theology, this parable exposes the Pharisee’s error: mistaking rule-keeping for relationship, and justice for joy.

The tragedy is that the elder brother stands outside, choosing resentment over the open door — all because he cannot accept that the father’s love is a gift, not a wage.
