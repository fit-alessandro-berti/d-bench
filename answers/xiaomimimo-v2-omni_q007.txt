This verse from Luke is often quoted as "judge not," but its meaning is frequently oversimplified. The distinction you're pointing to is crucial: the difference between *discernment* and *a condemning spirit*.

### The Difference

1. **Discernment** is the God-given ability to distinguish between good and evil, truth and error, wisdom and folly. It's a necessary skill for moral living, leadership, parenting, and self-examination. It involves assessing situations, teachings, or behaviors with wisdom, humility, and a desire for restoration. Jesus himself commanded us to "beware of false prophets" (Matthew 7:15) and to "test everything; hold fast what is good" (1 Thessalonians 5:21), both acts of discernment.

2. **The appetite to condemn** (the "judging" Jesus forbids) is something different. It is a heart posture characterized by:
    *   **Self-righteousness:** Assuming a position of moral superiority.
    *   **Hypocrisy:** Failing to apply the same standard to oneself (the "log in your own eye" of Matthew 7:3-5).
    *   **A desire to punish or belittle** rather than to heal or restore.
    *   **Finality:** Pronouncing a damning verdict on a person's worth or eternal state, a judgment that belongs to God alone.

### Why This Verse Doesn't Make Moral Judgments Irrelevant

If moral judgments never mattered, Jesus would be contradicting the entire Torah and his own teachings. Instead, he is targeting the *heart attitude* behind the judgment.

*   **The context of grace:** The verse is immediately followed by an instruction to give generously (v. 38). The flow is: Don't condemn, and you'll receive grace. Extend that same grace to others. It's a call to mirror the generosity we hope to receive from God. This establishes a moral framework *based on mercy*, not on condemnation.
*   **The standard is raised, not lowered:** The Sermon on the Mount (which includes "judge not") actually intensifies moral standards (e.g., equating lust with adultery, anger with murder). The call is not to abandon moral sense, but to apply it to *ourselves* with ruthless honesty and to others with profound mercy.
*   **The goal is restoration:** Biblical discernment aims to restore the sinner (Galatians 6:1). Condemnation often aims to exalt the accuser. The difference is between a surgeon removing a cancer (painful but life-giving) and an executioner pronouncing a death sentence.

### A Metaphor

Think of a doctor diagnosing an illness.
*   **Discernment** is the accurate, compassionate diagnosis: "You have a serious condition. This is what's wrong, and here is the treatment." It's necessary for health.
*   **The appetite to condemn** is pointing at the sick person and saying, "You are disgusting and deserve to be sick. Your illness defines you, and you are beyond help."

Jesus isn't telling the doctor not to diagnose. He's telling the doctor (who himself has a chronic condition) to treat the patient with the same compassion and hope he would want for himself.

**In essence, Luke 6:37 doesn't say that moral judgments never matter. It says that *our judgment of others must be filtered through the self-same mercy we desperately need from God.* We are to be *discerning* as servants, but never *condemning* as gods.**
