In these verses Jesus pictures the **heart as a storehouse** (*thesauros*, “treasure”) and the **mouth as its overflow valve**. The connective phrase is “out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh”: the heart is not empty, but stocked with accumulated good or evil, and speech is what spills out when the storehouse is tapped.

### 1. Speech as symptom
The passage treats words as **moral fruit**. Just as a fig tree does not produce olives, an evil heart does not produce genuinely good speech. “How can ye, being evil, speak good things?” implies that words are the visible indicator of an invisible condition. When the Pharisees slander Jesus, their words are symptoms of the poison already stored inside them.

### 2. Speech as choice
At the same time, Jesus holds them responsible. In the surrounding context he warns that people will give account for **“every idle word”** (Matt. 12:36). Speaking is an act, not an involuntary reflex. The verbs “bringeth forth” suggest a kind of moral production: a person draws from his treasury and offers something. So words are not merely passive emissions; they are chosen utterances for which the speaker is answerable.

### 3. Speech as revelation
Most pointedly here, words **reveal what was hidden**. A treasury is usually closed and dark; its contents are unseen until it is opened. The mouth opens the heart’s vault and displays its treasure. Words therefore make the secret inner life public: good treasure discloses a good person; evil treasure exposes an evil person.

### Conclusion
The passage does not force us to choose among these. **Words are all three**, but they function in a definite order:

- **Symptomatically**, they reveal the stored condition of the heart.  
- **Revelatory**, they make the hidden heart visible to others.  
- **Volitionally**, they are acts for which the speaker is judged.

Jesus’ main emphasis is diagnostic: your speech is the tell-tale sign of what you have been stocking in your heart.
