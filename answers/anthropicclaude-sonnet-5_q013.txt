This passage isn't calling for better proportion between worship and ethics—it's exposing a fundamental fraud where worship itself has become the mechanism of evasion.

## The Nature of the Fraud

**Worship as substitute, not supplement.** The people weren't neglecting religious duties while pursuing injustice on the side. They were *using* elaborate worship precisely to avoid the harder demands of justice. The feasts, offerings, and music had become a system for purchasing divine favor while maintaining exploitative social practices. This is why "balance" language would miss the point—you cannot balance a bribe against the crime it's meant to cover.

**The theological lie.** The underlying assumption being exposed is that God can be managed through ritual technique—that sincere liturgical performance creates a kind of covering, regardless of how one treats the vulnerable. Amos reveals this as a category error: God is not a deity who can be flattered or fed into complicity. The sensory language ("I will not smell," "I will not hear") insists that God is *actually present* to what's happening in the worship, not merely receiving symbolic tribute abstracted from reality.

**Why not "improve the ratio"?** Because the corruption isn't a deficiency of righteousness alongside adequate worship—it's that the worship has been built *on top of* and *in service of* the injustice. The offerings were likely funded by exploitative gain; the feast days provided social cover; the "solemn assemblies" reinforced communal identity that excluded the poor being crushed elsewhere in the economy (cf. Amos 2:6-8, 5:11-12). Adding a pinch more justice wouldn't fix a system where worship functions as moral laundering.

## The Logic of "Instead"

The demand isn't "worship less, act more"—it's that *until* justice flows, the worship is not merely insufficient but actively offensive. "I hate, I despise" signals that God experiences the ritual as something worse than neutral; it's an affront, because it pretends intimacy with God while perpetuating harm to those God claims as his own concern (the poor, the oppressed—Amos's constant refrain).

## The Positive Vision

"Let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream" isn't offered as an alternative *religious* practice to replace the old one. It's a demand for the actual content that worship was supposed to express and enact: right relationship, restored community, structural fairness. Ritual was meant to be the *symbol and cultivation* of this reality, not a replacement for it. When the symbol detaches from the substance and starts functioning as camouflage, the prophetic word must reject the symbol entirely—not to abolish worship as such, but to strip away the fraud so that true worship (justice enacted) might exist again.

The sharpness of the rejection, then, matches the depth of the deception: this is a warning against religion that has become sophisticated enough to feel devout while remaining, in its ethical substance, a betrayal.
