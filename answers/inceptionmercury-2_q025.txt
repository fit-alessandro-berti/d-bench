**Short answer:**  
In Matthew 6:1 the danger is not the fact that good deeds can be seen; it is the *motivation* behind making them visible—specifically, the craving to be praised by people. When the desire for human approval becomes the primary aim, the act is no longer a true offering to God but a self‑aggrandizing performance, and the “reward” is reduced to fleeting human applause rather than the eternal reward that God promises.

---

## 1. What the verse actually says

| Greek (NA28) | English (NIV) | Key term |
|--------------|---------------|----------|
| **Προσέχετε** (prosechete) – “take heed, be careful” | “Take heed” | A warning to be vigilant |
| **μή** (mē) – “not” | “that you do not” | Negation |
| **τὰ ἀλφά** (ta alms) – “your alms” (charitable giving) | “your alms” | The act of giving |
| **πρὸς** (pros) – “before, in front of” | “before men” | Public setting |
| **ἵνα** (hina) – “that, in order that” | “to be seen” | Purpose |
| **ὁρᾶται** (horatai) – “be seen” | “to be seen” | Visibility |
| **ἀνθρώποις** (anthropois) – “by men” | “by them” | Human audience |
| **ἢ** (ē) – “otherwise” | “otherwise” | Contrast |
| **ἔχων** (echōn) – “having” | “you have” | Possession |
| **μὴ** (mē) – “no” | “no” | Negation |
| **ἁγίαν** (hagian) – “holy” | “holy” | Sacred |
| **ἀνταμοιβήν** (antamoibēn) – “reward” | “reward” | Compensation |

| **οὐρανῷ** (ouranō) – “heaven” | “heaven” | Eternal realm |

The structure is a *negative conditional*: **If you do X (public almsgiving) *to be seen* by people, then you will have no reward from the Father**. The *“to be seen”* clause (ἵνα ὁρᾶται) indicates the *purpose* of the action, not merely its *visibility*.

---

## 2. Why visibility alone is not the problem

| Biblical examples where public good is *praise* |
|---------------------------------------------------|
| **Acts 2:44‑45** – The early church shared possessions openly; the community’s generosity was visible and praised. |
| **Matthew 25:35‑40** – “Whatever you did for one of the least of these… you did for me.” The “least” could be seen by all, yet the reward is from God. |
| **James 2:14‑17** – Faith without works is dead; the works can be public, but they are valuable when motivated by love. |

These passages show that **public generosity can be good** when the *intent* is to serve God and neighbor, not to gain human applause. The *danger* arises when the *goal* shifts from God‑oriented to people‑oriented.

---

## 3. The core moral problem: *craving for human approval*

### 3.1. Psychological insight
- **Self‑presentation theory** (Leary & Kowalski, 1990) posits that people have a fundamental need to maintain a favorable social image. When this need dominates, actions become *instrumental*—they are performed *for* the image, not *because* of the virtue.
- **Moral licensing** (Merritt, Effron, & Monin, 2010) shows that people who seek public recognition for good deeds may feel “licensed” to act selfishly elsewhere, because the outward act satisfies their self‑image.

### 3.2. Theological nuance
- **Greek term “ὁρᾶται” (horatai)** conveys *being observed* rather than merely *being visible*. The verse warns against *performing* the act *so that* it *will be observed*.
- **Patristic commentary** (e.g., Chrysostom, *Homilies on Matthew*): “The sin is not in giving, but in the *pride* that makes the giver look for applause.”
- **Augustine** (Tractatus on John 3:21) distinguishes *charity* (caritas) from *show* (ostentatio): “Charity seeks the good of the neighbor; ostentatio seeks the good of the self.”

### 3.3. The “reward” contrast
- **Human reward** = fleeting praise, status, self‑satisfaction.
- **Divine reward** = eternal, incorruptible, grounded in the *intentional* love for God (cf. 1 Cor 9:9‑10; 2 Cor 9:6‑8).

When the *primary* motive is human applause, the *divine* reward is forfeited because the act is no longer a *gift* to God but a *self‑gift*.

---

## 4. How to distinguish “good public deeds” from “dangerous public goodness”

| Criterion | Safe (God‑oriented) | Dangerous (human‑oriented) |
|----------|--------------------|----------------------------|
| **Primary motive** | Love of God & neighbor | Desire for reputation, admiration |
| **Thought pattern** | “May God be glorified?” | “Will people notice me?” |
| **Attitude toward anonymity** | Willingness to give even if unseen | Reluctance to give if unobserved |
| **Emotional response** | Peace, humility | Pride, excitement at attention |
| **Outcome focus** | Eternal impact, kingdom | Immediate social capital |

A practical test (from the *Catechism of the Catholic Church*, 2470) is: *If the act were done in secret, would you still be delighted?* If the answer is “yes,” the motive is likely pure; if “no,” the craving for visibility is the driver.

---

## 5. The role of visibility: a double‑edged sword

1. **Positive side** – Public acts can *inspire* others, *normalize* generosity, and *demonstrate* the Kingdom’s values (cf. Matthew 5:16 “Let your light shine before men”).  
2. **Negative side** – Visibility *exposes* the heart; it can *tempt* the giver to seek applause, and it can *lead* observers to imitate the *performance* rather than the *principle*.

Thus, visibility is **neutral**; it becomes *dangerous* when it **activates** the craving for human approval.

---

## 6. Summative answer to the question

> **What makes public goodness morally dangerous in this verse? Is the problem visibility itself, or the craving to be seen?**

The verse identifies **the craving to be seen**—the *motivation* to perform charitable acts **for human recognition**—as the moral danger. Visibility per se is not condemned; it is the *purpose* behind making the deed visible that nullifies the divine reward. When the *goal* of the act shifts from honoring God to impressing people, the act loses its spiritual value and becomes a self‑serving performance. The warning, therefore, is against *pride* and *self‑glorification* disguised as generosity, not against the mere fact that good deeds may be observed.

---

### Practical takeaway

- **Self‑examination:** Before giving publicly, ask, “Am I doing this because I love God/neighbor, or because I want applause?”
- **Cultivate humility:** Practice *secret* generosity (Matt 6:3‑4) as a habit; it trains the heart to love the act itself, not the audience.
- **Use visibility wisely:** When public good can *lead others* to faith or charity, do it *without* seeking personal accolades—let the focus remain on the *beneficiary* and the *Giver* (God), not on the *giver’s* reputation.
