In Isaiah 55:6–7, the pairing of “way” (Hebrew: *derek*) and “thoughts” (Hebrew: *maḥăšāḇôṯ*) reveals a profound understanding of repentance as a **whole-person transformation** — not merely behavioral correction, but a reorientation of the inner life that then reshapes outward action.

---

### **1. “Way” – The Outward Path**
- **Meaning**: Your “way” (*derek*) is your conduct, habits, and daily path — the tangible choices you make, how you treat others, the rituals you practice, the systems you participate in.
- **Implication**: Repentance must include *doing things differently*. It’s not enough to feel sorry while continuing in the same patterns. The “wicked” must abandon their actual practices — their oppression, deceit, or self-seeking — and walk a different road.

---

### **2. “Thoughts” – The Inward Imagination**
- **Meaning**: “Thoughts” (*maḥăšāḇôṯ*) refers not just to passing ideas, but to one’s deep imaginings, plans, purposes, and worldview — the inner narratives that shape how you see God, yourself, and the world.
- **Implication**: Repentance requires turning away from inner narratives of self-sufficiency, blame, cynicism, or false gods. It’s a surrender of the mind’s autonomy — the “unrighteous man” must abandon the thoughts that justify his “way.”

---

### **3. The Connection: Why Both?**
- **Root and Fruit**: “Thoughts” are often the root, “way” the fruit. But Scripture treats them as a continuous cycle — wrong thinking leads to wrong acting, and wrong acting reinforces wrong thinking.
- **Whole Life Turned**: God cares about the heart *and* the hands. To “return unto the Lord” is to let Him reshape both your hidden mind and your public life.
- **Hypocrisy Avoided**: If you change your “way” but keep the old “thoughts” (e.g., outward compliance without inward love, or fear without trust), it’s still far from God. If you keep the old “way” but adopt new “thoughts” (e.g., new beliefs without changed actions), it’s incomplete repentance.

---

### **4. Mercy for Both**
- **Outward conduct** is forgiven and cleansed — the “way” is washed and redirected.
- **Inward imagination** is healed and renewed — the “thoughts” are forgiven, then transformed by God’s truth.
- **The promise**: “He will have mercy… He will abundantly pardon.” This pardon covers both the visible and the invisible — the action *and* the intention, the deed *and* the desire.

---

In essence, Isaiah portrays repentance not as a transactional adjustment but as a **re-creation** — God’s mercy meets us in our *entirety*, healing the mind and guiding the feet. The invitation is not just to behave better, but to become a person whose thinking and living flow from trust in God rather than self. That’s why both are named: until our thoughts change, our ways will keep looping back; until our ways change, our thoughts remain theory. Mercy embraces the journey of both turning and transformation.
