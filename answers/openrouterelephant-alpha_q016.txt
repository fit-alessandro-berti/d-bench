The elder brother’s deepest problem is not any single emotion or posture in isolation—**it is the hardness of his heart, revealed most tellingly as pride in obedience that refuses mercy.**

Let’s walk through the text to see why:

1. **Anger** is the surface reaction (“he was angry, and would not go in”), but Scripture does not stop there. The father’s response shows the anger is a symptom, not the root.  
2. **Envy** and **wounded fairness** are present in his complaint: he contrasts his years of service with the sudden celebration for the prodigal. But notice—he never questions the *justice* of the father’s generosity to the other; he resents the *mercy* shown.  
3. **Pride in obedience** (“I served thee… neither transgressed”) is significant, but it becomes dangerous when it hardens into a sense of *deservedness*—as if his obedience earns a right to control the father’s affections.  
4. **Discomfort with mercy itself** is the core, because his logic reveals he sees the fatted calf not as a father’s joy in the lost being found, but as an injustice to the “deserving.” He cannot celebrate restoration; he resists it.

Jesus presents this to expose the self-righteousness that refuses God’s grace when it overflows to others. The father’s final words cut to the heart:  
> “Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine.”  
The elder’s identity and security should rest in being *the son*, not in being the *obedient servant* who keeps score. His problem is a heart that loves its own perceived virtue more than the lost.

So while anger, envy, and wounded fairness are expressions, **the deepest problem is a prideful, unmerciful disposition that resists the Father’s heart.** It is the refusal to enter into the joy of restoration—a hardness that, if unrepented, leads to spiritual death.
