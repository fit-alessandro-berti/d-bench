Proverbs 3:5–6 is calling for **covenantal, whole-person reliance on Yahweh** rather than **autonomous self-reliance**. It is **humble self-distrust** (recognizing the limits and biases of our judgment) and **God-trust** (treating God’s character and instruction as the final authority), not a ban on thinking.

## 1) What “trust” means here
The verb **בָּטַח (bāṭaḥ)** (“trust”) is not mere optimism or a feeling; it means to **rely on** or **rest one’s weight on**. The picture is of putting your security and direction in God, not simply adding God as one factor among others.

“**With all your heart**” in Hebrew idiom means with the whole inner person—will, desires, conscience—not just emotions. The point: *God is not a supplement to self-direction; he is the foundation.*

## 2) “Lean not on your own understanding” ≠ “don’t use your mind”
“Lean” uses **שָׁעַן (shaʿan)**: **to support yourself on**, to brace yourself on something as your prop. And “understanding” is **בִּינָה (bînāh)**: discernment, insight.

So the command is not “have no understanding,” but **do not make your own insight your load-bearing support**—your ultimate court of appeal—especially where your interests, fears, and blind spots distort judgment.

This fits Proverbs broadly: the book repeatedly **commands** acquiring wisdom and understanding (e.g., Prov 4:5–7; 18:15). So Proverbs 3 cannot be anti-intellectual without contradicting the rest of Proverbs.

## 3) The “humble self-distrust” being urged
This is the posture of someone who knows:
- my reasoning can be **limited** (I don’t see all factors),
- my reasoning can be **morally biased** (I rationalize what I want),
- my reasoning can be **temporally short-sighted** (I overweight the immediate),
- and my reasoning is not **sovereign** (I cannot secure outcomes).

That is why Proverbs elsewhere warns against being “wise in your own eyes” (Prov 3:7). The issue is not intelligence; it’s **self-sufficiency**.

## 4) “In all your ways acknowledge him”
“Acknowledge” is **יָדַע (yādaʿ)**—to know in a relational, practical sense. It means: **bring God into the decision as God**, not as an afterthought. Concretely, this includes:
- submitting choices to God’s revealed moral will (you don’t “trust” God while choosing what he forbids),
- seeking wisdom in prayer (cf. James 1:5),
- listening to wise counsel (a major Proverbs theme),
- and acting in reverence (“fear of the Lord”) rather than mere calculation.

## 5) “He will direct (make straight) your paths”
The idea is not “God will give you a private GPS for every choice,” but that God will **make your way straight**—clearer, steadier, morally aligned, and ultimately brought to the right end. The Hebrew behind “direct” has the sense of **making smooth/straight** (cf. “straight paths”).

## 6) How this differs from anti-intellectualism
**Anti-intellectualism** says: “Reason is suspect; stop thinking; ignore evidence; learning is dangerous.”

**Proverbs 3:5–6** says: “Think hard—but don’t enthrone your thinking. Use reason as a tool under God, not as a god over God.”

In other words:
- **Reason is valuable** (Proverbs celebrates it),
- **Reason is fallible** (so it must be corrected and governed),
- **God is ultimate** (so trust shifts from self as final authority to Yahweh as final authority).

A simple test: if your appeal to this passage makes you less teachable, less careful, and less committed to truth, you’ve slid into anti-intellectualism. If it makes you more teachable, more honest about your biases, and more obedient to God’s wisdom, you’re reading it as intended.
