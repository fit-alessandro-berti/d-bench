The passage is **not merely pessimistic** in the sense of saying, “Humans are only evil, so nothing sincere is possible.” It is more like **moral realism about self-deception**, with a severe warning: the inner self is not a neutral, transparent courtroom where we can simply inspect our motives and get the truth.

In context, Jeremiah 17 contrasts the person who “trusts in man” with the person who “trusts in the Lord” (Jeremiah 17:5–8). Then verses 9–10 explain why self-trust is dangerous: the “heart” is not just emotional feeling, but the inner center of thought, desire, will, and loyalty. The “reins” in the KJV means the kidneys, an ancient image for the deepest interior life. So the point is: **God sees what even we do not fully see in ourselves.**

The passage is pessimistic about **unaided self-knowledge**, not about the possibility of repentance, wisdom, or real obedience.

A good paraphrase of the force would be:

> “Do not assume your motives are pure just because they feel pure to you. The human interior is capable of deceiving even itself. God alone can judge the heart truly, and he judges not only hidden motives but also the visible fruit of a person’s ways.”

That last phrase matters: “according to his ways” and “according to the fruit of his doings.” Jeremiah does not leave us trapped in endless psychological guesswork. Motives matter, but they are tested by **patterns, actions, fruits, loyalties, and responses to correction**.

So how should someone talk about motives?

They should talk about them **humbly, provisionally, and concretely**.

Instead of saying:

> “My motives are pure.”

It is wiser to say:

> “As far as I can tell, I am trying to act from love, but I know I can deceive myself.”

Instead of:

> “I know exactly why I did that.”

Say:

> “I think I was motivated by concern, but there may also have been pride, fear, or resentment mixed in.”

Instead of using Jeremiah 17:9 to accuse others:

> “Your heart is deceitful, so I know your motives are bad.”

Use it first on oneself:

> “My own heart is not a final authority on my motives, so I need God’s searching, honest evidence, and correction.”

That is important because Jeremiah 17:9 can be misused. The verse does **not** give people permission to become suspicious mind-readers of everyone else. Verse 10 says **the Lord** searches the heart. Humans can evaluate fruit, patterns, words, and consequences, but they should be cautious about claiming final knowledge of another person’s inner motives.

A healthy way to speak about motives would include three things:

1. **Confession of limited self-knowledge**: “I may not fully understand myself.”
2. **Attention to fruit**: “What did this produce? Did it lead to love, justice, humility, truth, or harm?”
3. **Openness before God and others**: “Search me, correct me, and show me what I am missing,” like Psalm 139:23–24.

This is close to Paul’s attitude in 1 Corinthians 4:3–5. He says he is not aware of anything against himself, but that does not make him innocent; the Lord is the one who will bring hidden things to light. That is not despair. It is humility.

So the passage’s main message is not, “You can never mean well.” It is:

> **Do not make your own heart the judge of your own heart.**

Someone can speak honestly about motives, but not arrogantly. The right posture is: “Here is what I believe I intended; here is the fruit I can see; here is where I may be blind; Lord, search me.”
