This is a profound question that gets at the heart of what Proverbs 3:5–6 is really asking of us.

#### The Text in Context

The passage says:  
> “Trust in the Lord with all thine heart; and lean not unto thine own understanding. In all thy ways acknowledge him, and he shall direct thy paths.”

There are two key components here:
1. **Trust in the Lord with all your heart.**
2. **Do not lean on your own understanding.**

But what does this mean? Is it saying to abandon reason, to be ignorant, or to be humbly open? Let’s unpack the distinction you asked for.

---

### 1. Humble Self-Distrust

**Humble self-distrust** is not about thinking less of yourself or your abilities, but about recognizing the limits of your own perspective and wisdom. It is the posture of someone who admits that their knowledge is incomplete, their wisdom finite, and that there are dimensions of reality beyond their comprehension.

- **Biblical humility:** In the biblical sense, humility is not self-deprecation, but a recognition that God is the source of all wisdom and guidance (see James 1:5; Proverbs 16:9).
- **Trust and surrender:** To “trust in the Lord with all your heart” is to surrender your plans, anxieties, and uncertainties to God, not because you are incapable, but because you recognize that there is something greater and wiser at work.
- **Healthy dependence:** This trust is not weakness, but strength—it is the acknowledgment that we are not alone and that seeking God’s direction is an act of wisdom, not ignorance.

So, **humble self-distrust here is a wise openness to God’s guidance, rooted in the recognition of one’s own limitations—not in a rejection of reason, but in its proper place.**

---

### 2. Mere Anti-Intellectualism

**Anti-intellectualism** is the rejection or suspicion of reason, knowledge, and intellectual inquiry. It’s the attitude that thinking for oneself is bad or dangerous, and that one should simply accept authority or tradition without question.

- **Blindness, not humility:** Anti-intellectualism often leads to uncritical acceptance of dogma or authority, shutting down curiosity, analysis, and discernment.
- **Not what Proverbs teaches:** The passage is not saying “don’t think,” but “don’t trust your thinking as if it were ultimate.” There is a difference between relying solely on your own limited understanding and using your mind while still acknowledging dependence on God.

---

### 3. The Crucial Distinction

- **Humble self-distrust (good):** “I will use my mind and reasoning, but I acknowledge that my understanding is limited. I am open to being guided beyond what I can see or know, trusting that God’s wisdom surpasses mine.”
- **Mere anti-intellectualism (bad):** “I won’t use my mind or reason at all; I’ll just follow without questioning or understanding.”

Proverbs 3:5–6 is calling for the former, not the latter.

#### An Example

Suppose you are facing a difficult decision. Humble self-distrust would say:  
“I’ll pray and seek God’s guidance, recognizing that my own knowledge is incomplete. I’ll listen, reflect, and try to align my will with what I sense is right—but I won’t stubbornly insist that my way is the only way.”

Mere anti-intellectualism would say:  
“I won’t even think about it—I’ll just do whatever I’m told or what seems traditional, without considering the situation at all.”

### Conclusion

Proverbs 3:5–6 calls for **humble self-distrust**: a wise, honest recognition of our limits, leading to trust in God—not a rejection of thought or inquiry. It is an invitation to a deeper, more integrated use of both reason and faith, where we acknowledge our dependence on something greater than ourselves, but not in a way that negates our God-given ability to think and learn.
