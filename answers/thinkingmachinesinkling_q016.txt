The elder brother displays **all** of those symptoms, but they are layers of one deeper sickness: **he is lost in self-righteousness**—a pride in obedience that has turned his relationship with the father into a transaction and made him hate the very grace that saved his brother.

Here is how the layers work in Luke 15:28–32:

| Surface symptom | What he says/does | What it reveals |
|---|---|---|
| **Anger** | “He was angry, and would not go in” (v. 28) | Resentment that grace has disrupted his expected order. |
| **Wounded fairness / envy** | “I never transgressed… yet you never gave me a kid… but for *this* son… you killed the fatted calf” (vv. 29–30) | He measures worth by merit: obedience should earn reward; rebellion should earn punishment. |
| **Pride in obedience** | “These many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment” (v. 29) | Note his word for “serve” (*douleuō*—slave labor). He sees himself not as a beloved son but as a hired hand keeping score. He also distances himself: “this **thy** son,” not “my brother.” |
| **Discomfort with mercy** | He refuses to enter the feast and argue with the father’s joy (vv. 28, 30) | He cannot rejoice in restoration because he believes mercy is unfair. He wants a father who rewards performance, not one who runs to sinners. |

**The root problem**
Jesus told this parable to Pharisees who “murmured, saying, This man receiveth sinners, and eateth with them” (Luke 15:1–2). The elder brother **is** them. His deepest problem is not merely that he is angry or jealous; it is that **he does not understand—or want—grace.**

Because he trusts in his own record (“I never transgressed”), he never realizes he also needs the father’s mercy. And because he thinks he has earned his place, he resents that the father gives the same welcome to the undeserving. His heart is alienated from the father despite living “ever with me” (v. 31). He is physically near but spiritually far—lost in the field of self-righteousness.

So the answer is best summed up as **pride in obedience that hardens into a fundamental rejection of mercy.** His anger and sense of unfairness are just the fruit. The father’s reply—“all that I have is thine… we *should* make merry”—exposes the tragedy: the elder brother already had everything, but he preferred being a slave to being a son who shares the father’s heart.

In short, the elder brother’s deepest problem is that **he is unable to celebrate grace because he is blind to his own need for it.**
