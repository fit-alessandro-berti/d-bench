**The power being praised is the power of self-emptying love—divine authority expressed through voluntary humility and sacrificial obedience.**

### What the passage is actually praising

Philippians 2:5–8 is one of the most profound descriptions of Christ’s “kenosis” (Greek for *emptying*). The text praises a specific kind of power:

- **Ontological power** (the eternal Son is “in the form of God” and fully equal with God).
- **Moral and spiritual power** exercised by *not* grasping, clutching, or exploiting that equality.
- The power to *descend* without losing greatness: He “made himself of no reputation” (literally *emptied himself*), took “the form of a servant,” was “made in the likeness of men,” and went all the way to “the death of the cross.”

This is not the power of coercion, domination, or self-exaltation. It is the power of **self-giving love** that is so secure in its identity and so full in its goodness that it can afford to become small, weak, and obedient for the sake of others. The cross is the ultimate display of this power: the One who had every right to dominate chose instead to die for the dominated.

The early church saw this as the very heart of what makes the Christian God different from pagan gods or human tyrants. Pagan deities and earthly rulers typically displayed power by *taking* (status, pleasure, control). Christ displays power by *giving*. The passage calls this mindset—the “mind of Christ”—the model for all Christian life.

### Why humility joined to greatness feels more unsettling than open domination

Open domination is ugly, but it is *familiar*. We understand the logic of raw power: the strong dominate the weak; the great lord it over the small. That pattern is consistent with fallen human nature and with most of history’s empires. It confirms our cynical expectations.

Humility joined to true greatness is far more disturbing for several reasons:

1. **It exposes us.**  
   When someone who is genuinely superior (in talent, status, intellect, or moral beauty) chooses to serve and lower themselves, our own pride, insecurity, and grasping become obvious by contrast. Their humility is a mirror that shows us we are smaller than we pretend to be—precisely *because* we are so desperate to look big.

2. **It subverts the expected order.**  
   We have an instinctive hierarchy in our hearts: the greater should rule, the lesser should serve. When the Greater reverses that order and serves, it upends our entire moral and social imagination. It feels destabilizing, almost unfair. “Why should the One who is equal with God wash feet or die on a cross?” The question reveals that our discomfort is actually with self-sacrificial love itself.

3. **It demands response.**  
   Coercive power can be resisted, fled from, or submitted to out of fear. But humble, self-giving greatness invites love, not mere compliance. It creates obligation of the heart rather than the body. Many people find that more threatening than a tyrant, because a tyrant lets you keep your inner independence while you outwardly submit. Christ’s humility asks for your heart.

4. **It reveals a different kind of strength.**  
   The greatness that can stoop without losing itself is stronger than the greatness that must constantly assert itself. That kind of strength feels alien and even dangerous to a world that thinks power is zero-sum. It suggests that the highest power might be the capacity to give yourself away without being diminished—an idea that threatens every system built on domination, manipulation, or self-promotion.

C.S. Lewis captured something of this discomfort in *The Problem of Pain* and *Mere Christianity* when he described how the real God is both far more merciful *and* far more demanding than we expect. The combination of majesty and meekness in Christ is unsettling because it is simultaneously too good to be true (He loves us that much) and too costly to accept (He asks us to live the same way).

In short: open domination flatters our cynicism.  
Humility joined to greatness shatters it—and then invites us into a far more beautiful, but far more costly, way of being.

This is why the cross remains offensive. It is not merely suffering; it is *sovereign* suffering. It is power of the most disturbing and attractive kind.
