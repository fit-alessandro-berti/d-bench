This is a beautifully penetrating question, and the passage you’ve cited from John gets to the heart of a profound human struggle. Let’s explore why “light”—representing truth, clarity, divine reality, and exposure—might be met not with joy, but with hatred.

---

### **1. Light as exposure, not just illumination**
In this passage, “light” is not merely a gentle glow—it is a revealing, exposing force. It doesn’t just show the way; it shows *things as they really are*, including motives, hidden actions, and inner states.

When someone “does evil” or lives in self-deception, they often maintain an internal narrative that justifies or hides their actions. Light threatens that narrative. To be seen clearly is to lose the protective veil of self-justification.

---

### **2. The link between exposure and shame**
Shame is the painful feeling of being seen in a way that contradicts how we wish to be perceived.  
If a person’s deeds are “evil”—whether we think of that as morally wrong, selfish, or simply contrary to divine truth—then exposure brings:

- **Loss of control over one’s image**  
- **Fear of judgment or rejection**  
- **The collapse of excuses**

Darkness, in this metaphor, is a comfort zone where one can avoid self-confrontation. It allows a person to live without the dissonance between their actions and their conscience (or divine standard).

---

### **3. Truth as a threat to identity**
Sometimes, our wrongdoings become intertwined with our sense of self. We build identities, relationships, and even livelihoods around things that may not stand in the light.  
To come to the light, then, feels like a kind of death—an unraveling of the constructed self.  
Hatred of the light is, in a way, a defense mechanism of the ego against dissolution.

---

### **4. The social and systemic dimension**
It’s not only individuals who hate the light—institutions, communities, and systems can also prefer darkness.  
Corruption, injustice, and oppression thrive in opacity. Light here means accountability, transparency, and often, the overturning of power structures. Those who benefit from darkness will resist the light fiercely.

---

### **5. Contrast: Those who “do truth”**
The passage offers the opposite response:  
> “But he that doeth truth cometh to the light.”

Here, “doing truth” (*poiōn tēn alētheian*) suggests a life oriented toward authenticity, integrity, and alignment with divine reality. Such a person does not fear exposure because:

- Their deeds are “wrought in God”—done in partnership with or submission to divine will.
- They desire verification, not concealment.
- Their sense of worth is not tied to a hidden or false self.

For them, light is not condemnation but vindication, not exposure but affirmation.

---

### **6. The spiritual paradox**
Ironically, the very light that condemns is also offered as salvation. In John’s theology, Jesus is the light coming into the world. The condemnation is not that the light actively condemns people, but that people’s preference for darkness becomes self-condemnation by refusing the light that could heal and transform.

---

### **Final reflection**
We all have parts of ourselves we keep in the dark—not necessarily because they are “evil” in a dramatic sense, but because they are painful, unresolved, or contrary to our values. The spiritual journey often involves slowly letting light into those hidden places, which is why it feels risky and why resistance is natural.

The passage suggests that love of darkness is ultimately a choice against our own liberation—a choice that can become so habitual it feels like hatred toward the very thing that could free us.
