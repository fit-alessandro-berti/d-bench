### Inner Contradiction Exposed

The passage from James 3:9–10 highlights an **inner contradiction** rooted in the inconsistency between the words that emanate from a believer’s mouth and the nature of that believer's relationship with God. Specifically, it points out the paradox that:

1. **Blessing God**: The same mouth used to **bless God**, acknowledging Him as the source of all goodness and creator, is also employed to **curse** fellow human beings, who are created in God's image (cf. Genesis 1:27). This duality creates a stark moral inconsistency because it suggests that the believer recognizes divine sovereignty and goodness but simultaneously devalues and harms creatures made in God's likeness.

2. **Inherent Moral Dissonance**: The contradiction lies in the fact that the same source (the mouth) is used to express profound reverence for the divine and contempt or harm toward human beings fashioned after the divine image. This dissonance reveals a **moral disorder** within the believer: a lack of coherent integration of theological belief with ethical action.

### Why Speech Reveals Moral Disorder So Quickly

1. **Immediate Expression of Inner State**: Speech is the **most direct and immediate vehicle** for expressing one's inner thoughts, feelings, and attitudes. Unlike behaviors that may be filtered or moderated, spoken words often reveal the **raw, unfiltered** configuration of a person’s mind and heart. Hence, any conflict between belief and practice becomes instantly apparent through speech.

2. **Control and Volition**: The ability to **choose** what to say—and who to bless or curse—is a testament to human volition. When a person’s words betray a split allegiance (to God vs. to fellow humans), it demonstrates an **internal struggle** or **lack of alignment** between belief and behavior. This conflict is swiftly exposed because the choice to speak is an **active, conscious decision**, making the inconsistency glaring.

3. **Universal Accessibility of Language**: Unlike some behaviors that may be situational or culturally mediated, **speech is universally accessible** and **transparent**. Anyone within earshot can witness the contradiction without needing deep cultural or contextual interpretation. This universality ensures that the moral disorder is **readily observable** and cannot be easily concealed or rationalized away.

4. **James’s Emphasis on Discipline**: The apostle James repeatedly emphasizes the **discipline of the tongue** (cf. James 3:1–12), noting that it is **small yet mighty**—capable of great good or great harm. This underscores the idea that because the tongue is such a **powerful and unfettered tool**, any moral inconsistency it reveals is **sharp and unmistakable**. 

5. **Theological Significance**: From a theological perspective, the image of God in humanity (Imago Dei) implies a **shared intrinsic dignity** that demands respect and compassion. To bless the Creator while cursing His image-bearers highlights a **failure to grasp or live out** this theological truth, making the moral disorder evident through the very act of speaking.

### Conclusion

James 3:9–10 exposes an **inner contradiction** where the same speech blesses the divine and curses the human, revealing a **moral disorder** because speech is the **immediate, voluntary, and universally accessible** medium through which one's internal alignment with divine principles is displayed. The rapid and clear manifestation of this inconsistency underscores the apostle’s teaching that true faith must be reflected not only in belief but also in **consistent, righteous actions and words**. This passage serves as a sobering reminder that genuine spirituality requires unity of thought, word, and deed.
