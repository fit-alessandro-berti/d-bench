This is a profound question that gets to the heart of biblical repentance. The pairing of **"way" (דֶּרֶךְ, derek)** and **"thoughts" (מַחְשָׁבָה, machshavah)** reveals that true turning to God requires a change that is both **external** and **internal**, **practical** and **deeply personal**.

Here’s a breakdown of what this implies:

### 1. **Outward Conduct ("His Way")**
   - **"Way"** refers to a person’s habitual actions, lifestyle, behavior patterns, and moral direction—the tangible path of one’s life.
   - Repentance here means **forsaking sinful actions and practices**. It’s not just regret but a decisive turning away from wrong behavior.

### 2. **Inward Imagination ("His Thoughts")**
   - **"Thoughts"** goes deeper, referring to inner motives, intentions, desires, and the seat of imagination and will.
   - This implies that **sinful actions grow from a corrupted inner life**—thoughts, fantasies, rationalizations, and disordered loves. True repentance therefore involves the purification of the heart and mind, not just behavior modification.

### 3. **The Unity of the Two**
   - In the Hebrew worldview, **thoughts and ways are inseparable**. What we think ultimately shapes how we act, and how we act shapes our thinking (Proverbs 23:7, Mark 7:21-23).
   - Thus, Isaiah calls for a **holistic transformation**—a change in both conduct and consciousness. Partial repentance that only changes outward behavior while cherishing sin inwardly is incomplete.

### 4. **The Possibility of Mercy**
   - The structure of the verse itself reveals a beautiful rhythm: **“let him return… and he will have mercy… for he will abundantly pardon.”**
   - The mercy of God is not contingent on our achieving moral perfection first. The promise is that **when we turn—both our ways and our thoughts—God meets us with extravagant mercy and pardon**.
   - This implies that God’s mercy is both **responsive** (to our turning) and **transformative** (it changes us from within). His pardon is not merely legal forgiveness but the renewal of the inner person, making further repentance possible.

### **Conclusion: What This Means for Us**
Isaiah 55:6-7 teaches that God seeks the **whole person**. He does not want just outward ritual or mere behavioral change; He desires the **heart**. The invitation is to bring the entirety of our lives—the visible and the hidden—into alignment with His character.

Because God’s mercy is **“abundant”** or **“plenteous,”** we are not left to clean ourselves up first. We come as we are—**way and thoughts together**—and in returning, we discover that His mercy is large enough to transform both.

In short: **True repentance is a turning of the whole self—deeds and desires—toward God, who meets that turn with pardon that reaches into the deepest corners of our being.**
