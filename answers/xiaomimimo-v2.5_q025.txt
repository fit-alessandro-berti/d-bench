The verse makes clear that the core moral danger lies not in the visibility of a good deed itself, but in the **craving to be seen**—the intention behind the act. The problem is the heart’s orientation: doing good *in order to* gain public praise, social approval, or personal reputation.

Here’s the distinction:

1. **Visibility ≠ Sinful**  
   A good deed done publicly isn’t inherently wrong; in fact, sometimes good works are meant to be seen as an example (Matthew 5:16). The issue arises when the *primary purpose* is human approval rather than obedience to God or genuine love for others.

2. **The Craving for Recognition**  
   Jesus warns against acting “to be seen of them.” This indicates the problem is the **inward motive**—the desire to be praised, validated, or esteemed by people. When that is the driving force, the act becomes a transaction: goodness exchanged for social reward. Jesus says such a person “has received their reward in full” (v. 2)—meaning they get the human approval they sought, but forfeit the eternal reward from the Father.

3. **Spiritual Consequence**  
   The danger is not just hypocrisy, but a subtle **idolatry of self-glory**. When we perform goodness to be seen, we shift the goal from loving God and neighbor to loving our own image. It corrupts the authenticity of the act and severs its connection to heavenly reward.

In essence, the verse invites a purity of heart: good deeds should flow from devotion to God and concern for others, not from a hunger for the spotlight. Visibility isn’t the enemy—but making visibility the *goal* is spiritually dangerous because it reveals where our treasure truly lies (Matthew 6:21).
