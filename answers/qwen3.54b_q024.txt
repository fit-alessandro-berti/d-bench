In the context of Ezekiel’s prophecy—spoken as a warning and promise during the Babylonian exile—the text contrasts **mechanical obedience** (external) with **regenerative transformation** (internal).

### 1. The Difference Between External Compliance and a "New Heart"

The primary difference lies in **motivation, sustainability, and identity**.

*   **External Compliance (Behavioral):**
    If one obeys God’s statutes strictly to avoid punishment or to follow tradition, they are relying on willpower alone. A person with a "stony heart" can learn rules; they can memorize laws; they can offer sacrifices in the proper way. However, their obedience is hollow because it lacks an internal desire for relationship. It is like a machine functioning: it does what it has been programmed to do if pushed, but it feels nothing and wants neither more action nor rest.
*   **The New Heart (Existential):**
    The "new heart" represents an internal restructuring of the person's very desires. In Ezekiel 36, God does not promise they will *stop* doing things wrong on their own initiative alone; He promises to place His Spirit within them so that obedience flows from who they *are*, not just what they *do*.
    *   **Motivation:** External compliance is often fear-based or rule-bound. A new heart is love-based. It is responsive to God's character rather than reactive to human authority.
    *   **Sustainability:** One can maintain a "stony" exterior of righteousness for years but crumble when the pressure is removed. A "heart of flesh" allows for walking in statutes willingly day after day.

### 2. Why "Hardness" is Described as a Material Condition

God describes spiritual resilience not just as a moral failing, but as a physical or material reality that must be surgically replaced. This choice of language highlights two theological realities:

**A. The Immutability of the Human Will**
In the ancient Near Eastern and biblical consciousness, the "heart" was the seat of intellect, will, and emotion combined (Genesis 23). To call it "stony" is to make a point about **unyielding rigidity**.
*   Stone does not soften; it does not respond to touch or pressure. It exists only in its fixed shape.
*   Flesh implies softness, elasticity, blood, and life.
God is not merely saying, “You need to repent more.” He is saying, **the machinery you have used to worship has been broken, calcified, and hardened.** A "stone heart" lacks the capacity for feeling or growth; it can only exist at a static point of stubbornness. To walk in statutes—which requires flexibility and responsiveness—requires the organic pliability that only flesh provides.

**B. The Divine Sovereignty of Restoration**
Describing hardness as material emphasizes that **man cannot repair himself**. A dead organ (stone) cannot be made "alive" by a person’s own will or effort; it needs to be replaced.
*   The text states: *And I WILL put...* (V. 27). The subject is God, the verb is passive agency.
*   This reflects Ezekiel’s theme of replacement and restoration (rebuilding Jerusalem with stone in 36:24-25; replacing a foreign god with Yahweh).

In summary, external compliance is **performative**—one can mimic holiness while internal hardness remains. A "new heart" is **relational and generative**. Hardness is described as material because it suggests that the spiritual life God desires requires organic vulnerability (flesh) rather than rigid inertia (stone), and that this transformation cannot be achieved by the sinner alone, but only through divine intervention.
