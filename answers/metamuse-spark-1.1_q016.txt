All of those are in him, but they are not at the same depth. They are layers.

> "Lo, these many years do I serve thee..."

That one sentence diagnoses him.

**Anger is the surface.** Verse 28: *“And he was angry, and would not go in.”* The Greek is *orgisthe* - he became enraged. But anger is never the deepest problem. Anger is always anger *about* something.

**Wounded fairness and envy is what he feels.** 

> "thou never gavest me a kid... thou hast killed for him the fatted calf."

In his economy, it doesn't add up. A kid vs. the fatted calf. My years of clean living vs. his years with harlots. A small party with *my friends* vs. a huge party for *your son*. He experiences the father's mercy as injustice.

That feeling is real, and it's why the Pharisees to whom Jesus was telling this story - Luke 15:2 - were murmuring. They felt exactly this.

**Pride in obedience is the mechanism that created that sense of injustice.**

Notice his language:

*   **"Do I serve thee"** - *douleuo*. The word is not "serve as a son" but "slave for you as a master." He has lived in the house, but never as a son.
*   **"neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment"** - His obedience is his resume. 
*   **"this thy son"** - verse 30. Not "my brother." He disowns relationship. He is the good son who won't claim his brother, speaking to a father whose goods he wants but whose heart he doesn't share.

This is the deeper lostness. The younger brother was lost *away* from home wanting the father's goods without the father. The elder brother is lost *at* home wanting the father's goods without the father. He obeys to get leverage. His morality is mercenary.

Which is why we get to the deepest layer:

**His deepest problem is discomfort with mercy itself.**

Mercy offends the elder brother because if mercy is true, his whole identity project collapses.

If the father can give away a fatted calf for free to a failure, then what were all those years of joyless, record-keeping obedience for? If his brother is received by grace alone, then he too must be received by grace alone - and he doesn't want grace, he wants wages. He doesn't want a Father, he wants a paymaster who will finally acknowledge he has been the better son.

That is why he cannot come into the feast. A feast is not earned. You can't enter a feast with a timecard. You can only enter it as a gift. 

And notice what the father does: *“therefore came his father out, and intreated him.”* The same father who ran for the younger son now comes out for the older. Both sons are lost. Both need to be sought. He doesn't rebuke the elder's theology; he tenderly corrects it:

> "Son [ *teknon* - my dear child], thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine."

That's the tragedy. He paraphrases it: "You have been angry about a kid, when you have owned the whole farm and never enjoyed it. You slaved when you could have feasted all along. Your brother returned to get what you have had and never wanted."

His obedience never produced joy. That proves it wasn't love.

So in order:

**Anger** is the fruit.
**Envy / wounded fairness** is the emotion.
**Pride in obedience** is the root mindset.
**Discomfort with free mercy** is the root disease.

He cannot rejoice that "this thy brother was dead, and is alive again" because he never understood that he too was dead, even while working in the field.

Jesus ends the parable open-ended. The younger son has come in. Will the elder son come in? That was the question hanging in the air for the Pharisees listening - and for us.
