Great question. The movement in Isaiah 1:16–17 is deliberate and profound:

1. **“Cease to do evil”** is the **negative command**: stop harming, stop injustice, stop sin.
2. **“Learn to do well”** is the **positive command**: goodness is not automatic once evil stops—it must be learned, practiced, and embodied.

Isaiah is teaching that repentance is more than restraint.  
It’s not just “don’t do bad things,” but “become the kind of people who actively do good.”

And notice what “doing well” means in the next phrases:

- **seek justice**
- **correct oppression / relieve the oppressed**
- **defend the fatherless**
- **plead for the widow**

So biblical goodness here is **social, relational, and active**—especially toward the vulnerable. It is a trained habit of covenant love and justice, not mere moral passivity.

In short:  
**Stopping evil is necessary, but not sufficient.**  
**True righteousness is learned love in action.**
