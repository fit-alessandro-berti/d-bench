**Step‑by‑step analysis**

1. **Historical‑literary backdrop**  
   - Amos 5 opens a *judgment oracle* delivered to Israel (the northern kingdom) sometime in the mid‑8th century BCE, just before the Assyrian invasions.  
   - The people are portrayed as enjoying material prosperity, frequent “feast days” and elaborate religious ceremonies, yet they have abandoned justice, mercy, and fidelity to the covenant.

2. **The rhetorical structure of 5:21‑24**  
   - **Verse 21**: *“I hate… your feast days … I will not smell in your solemn assemblies.”* – The language is deliberately extreme (“hate,” “despise”) because God wants Israel to grasp that the rituals themselves are no longer pleasing; they have become a façade.  
   - **Verse 22‑23**: *“Though ye offer… I will not accept them … take away the noise of your songs.”* – The prophet juxtaposes *external offerings* (burnt, grain, peace sacrifices) with *internal attitudes* (song and music). God rejects the outward performance because it masks a heart that is unreconciled to Him.  
   - **Verse 24**: *“But let judgment run down … righteousness as a mighty stream.”* – The corrective image replaces religious ceremony with *social justice*: flowing water symbolizes an unstoppable, transparent flow of fair treatment for the poor and oppressed.

3. **Why the rejection is “sharp” rather than a gentle rebuke**  
   - **Covenantal breach**: Israel’s foundational covenant (Exodus‑Deuteronomy) explicitly links worship to ethical conduct: *“You shall be holy… you shall do justice, love mercy, walk humbly with your God.”* Amos is not merely tweaking the calendar; he is declaring that the people have broken the very contract that makes worship meaningful.  
   - **Prophetic urgency**: The nation stands on the brink of exile. A subtle “balance” warning would be ignored amid parties and sacrifices. Only a stark, almost scandalous proclamation (“I hate your feasts”) can jolt complacent hearts into repentance before destruction arrives.  
   - **Theological emphasis on *heart* over *ritual***: The Deuteronomic reform (and earlier prophets like Isaiah) had already proclaimed that God “desires mercy, not sacrifice.” Amos intensifies this principle by saying the rituals are now repellent because they mask spiritual adultery.

4. **What moral or spiritual fraud is being exposed**  
   - **Religious hypocrisy** – The people present themselves as devout (“feast days,” “solemn assemblies”) while *practicing* oppression, exploitation of the poor, and neglect of widows and orphans (see 2:6‑7; 4:1). Their worship is a performance that hides systemic injustice.  
   - **Covenantal betrayal** – By offering sacrifices they think obligate God’s favor, Israel assumes *transactional* religion (“If I give you burnt offerings, you must bless me”). Amos reveals the fraud of treating divine generosity as a market exchange rather than responding to God’s character.  
   - **Idolatry in practice** – Though not overtly worshiping other deities, Israel has effectively idolized *its own prosperity and ritual* (cf. “the noise of your songs” as pleasing idols). The “viols” become a symbol of self‑gratifying entertainment that replaces true awe of Yahweh.

5. **The corrective vision**  
   - Amos does not abolish worship; he re‑orients it: *“Let judgment run down… righteousness as a mighty stream.”* True worship is inseparable from communal justice, caring for the marginalized, and living according to God’s ethical standards. The “waters” image suggests an unstoppable flow—once begun, it cannot be contained by mere ceremonies.

6. **Implications for later biblical theology**  
   - Jesus (Matthew 23) echoes Amos when He condemns the scribes and Pharisees: *“These people honor me with their lips, while their hearts are far from me.”*  
   - The New Testament (James 1:27; 2 Cor 9:7) likewise stresses pure religion as caring for the widow and orphan—showing that Amos’s thrust became a cornerstone of both Jewish and Christian ethic.

**Conclusion**

Amos 5:21‑24 does not ask for a “better balance” because, in his prophetic context, *balance* would still preserve the underlying fraud: outward religious observance paired with systemic injustice. The passage therefore delivers an unequivocal, shocking rejection (“I hate… I will not smell”) to expose **the spiritual adultery of hypocrisy**—a false worship that pretends devotion while betraying the covenantal call to justice and mercy. By doing so, Amos forces Israel (and us) to recognize that genuine religion is measured by how “judgment” and “righteousness” flow through society, not by the volume or frequency of rites and songs.  

*In short: The sharp rejection reveals a profound moral fraud—religious ritual used as a cover for oppression and covenantal breach—and calls for worship that *embodies* divine justice rather than merely *accompanies* it.*
