Short answer: because the psalmist wants true, transformative knowledge of himself, not just a shield against trouble or a cuddle of comfort. He asks God to search, test, and reveal what is hidden so that God can lead him in the right way.

What kind of honesty is modeled here
- Radical visibility before God: The psalmist knows God fully sees the heart and thoughts (139:1-4). Rather than insisting on his own private privacy, he invites God to expose what he cannot see in himself.
- Honest self-assessment, including hidden sin and faulty motives: “See if there be any wicked way in me” acknowledges that sin and crooked desires may lurk in the inner life, even if not obvious on the outside.
- Openness to correction and refinement: “Try me” implies testing in order to reveal what needs changing. It’s a posture of teachability, not defensiveness.
- Trusting the source of truth: The request presumes a relationship with a God who knows and loves. The psalmist doesn’t fear exposure because to be known by God is to be understood and reoriented for good.
- Alignment with life and direction: The ultimate goal is to be led “in the way everlasting,” i.e., in the path of life and obedience as defined by God, not merely to feel cleaned up or comforted.

Why not merely seek defense or comfort?
- Defense against what? The danger is self-deception. People often hide motives, pride, envy, fear, or bitterness behind defensive postures. “Searching” cuts through excuses and rationalizations.
- Comfort to what end? Comfort is good, but it has to be anchored in truth. Comfort without truth can reinforce bad patterns (comfort in sin, or comfort as a placebo that keeps one from repentance). The psalmist’s longing is for true cleansing that enables a lifelong walk with God.

Biblical context that helps interpret this
- Psalm 51 shows the same spirit of inward honesty: confession, repentance, and a plea for a renewed heart.
- Psalm 139’s broader theme is that God’s omniscience and intimate knowledge of us should humble us and inspire trust, not drive us to stonewall or hide.
- New Testament parallels include James 1:23-25 (the Word as a mirror that reveals what’s inside and leads to obedience) and 1 John 1:9 (confession and cleansing through God's faithfulness).

Practical takeaway for today
- The psalm models a devotional practice of self-examination before God: invite God to reveal hidden areas, motives, and sins; respond with confession and repentance; seek God’s guidance to walk in truth.
- It’s not a license for harsh self-criticism, but a disciplined openness to be transformed by grace.

A simple modern prayer echoing this spirit
- “O Lord, search my heart and try my thoughts. Show me where my motives are prideful, my desires warped, or my behavior hurtful. Lead me in the way of life and truth, for I want to walk in your path today and forever.”
