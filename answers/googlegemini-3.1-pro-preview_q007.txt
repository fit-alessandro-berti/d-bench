Luke 6:37 is one of the most frequently quoted—and most frequently misunderstood—verses in the entire Bible. Culturally, "judge not" is often hijacked to mean, "Do not evaluate my actions," or "No one has the right to say what is right or wrong." 

However, placing this verse in the context of Jesus’ broader teachings reveals that He is not advocating for moral relativism or the abandonment of moral boundaries. Instead, He is targeting the arrogance of the human heart. 

Here is an exploration of why this verse does not invalidate moral judgments, and the vital difference between healthy discernment and the appetite to condemn.

---

### Part 1: Why "Judge Not" Is Not Moral Relativism

To say that Jesus meant "moral judgments never matter" ignores the immediate context of His teachings. Just a few verses later (Luke 6:43-44), Jesus tells His followers that a tree is known by its fruit—a metaphor that explicitly demands evaluating and judging whether "fruit" (behavior, character) is good or rotten. Furthermore, the entire Sermon on the Mount/Plain relies heavily on distinguishing between moral right and wrong.

The distinction lies in **distinguishing between the action and the soul**. 

1. **Judging Actions is Necessary:** A functioning moral compass requires us to look at theft, deceit, or cruelty and judge it as wrong. If moral judgments didn't matter, the concepts of justice, ethics, and human rights would collapse.
2. **Judging Souls is Forbidden:** What Jesus strictly forbids is the act of usurping God's role. We are dealing with human beings whose inner motives, past traumas, and spiritual trajectories are entirely invisible to us. To "judge" in the sense of Luke 6:37 (*krino* in the original Greek) means to pronounce a final, damning verdict on a person’s ultimate worth or standing before God. 

Therefore, Jesus is not saying, "Do not notice that a wrong has been committed." He is saying, "Do not use that wrong to write a final, damning sentence over a person's life."

---

### Part 2: Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn

The core of the prompt requires uncoupling **discernment** (which is a biblical virtue) from the **appetite to condemn** (which is a spiritual sickness). Here are the primary differences:

#### 1. The Posture: Humility vs. Superiority
*   **Discernment:** Operates from a posture of humility and self-awareness. It is the recognition of wrong while remembering that we, too, are capable of wrong. Jesus illustrates this perfectly just a few verses later (Luke 6:41-42) with the analogy of the speck and the log. You must discern the speck in your brother's eye, but only *after* dealing with the log in your own.
*   **The Appetite to Condemn:** Operates from a platform of pride. Condemning someone else is often a psychological tactic to artificially elevate ourselves. When we condemn, we effectively say, "I am good because I am not like *that*."

#### 2. The Goal: Restoration vs. Destruction
*   **Discernment:** Seeks healing. When a doctor diagnoses (judges) a disease, the goal is to eradicate the disease to save the patient. True moral discernment identifies destructive behavior in order to protect boundaries, speak truth in love, and ideally, restore the person.
*   **The Appetite to Condemn:** Seeks punishment and finality. A person with an appetite to condemn takes pleasure in the failure of others. They do not want the wrongdoer to change and be restored; they want the wrongdoer to be discarded. 

#### 3. The Object: The Behavior vs. The Identity
*   **Discernment:** Addresses the specific action. It says, "What you did was destructive and wrong."
*   **The Appetite to Condemn:** Essentializes the person. It says, "Because you did a destructive thing, you are entirely deeply flawed, irredeemable, and worthless."

#### 4. The Appetite Itself: Reluctance vs. Eagerness
*   **Discernment:** Is often reluctant. A healthy person feels a heavy heart when they have to draw a hard moral line, enforce a boundary, or name a sin. 
*   **The Appetite to Condemn:** Is eager and hasty. People who possess this appetite are quick to jump to the worst possible conclusions about others. They thrive on outrage, gossip, and the "canceling" of others, finding a perverse joy in delivering the verdict.

### The Litmus Test: The Presence of Forgiveness

The most brilliant part of Luke 6:37 is its conclusion: *"forgive, and ye shall be forgiven."*

Forgiveness proves that Jesus is not throwing out moral judgments. **You cannot forgive an action unless you have first judged it to be wrong.** If someone steps on your toe and you say, "It didn't hurt, it doesn't matter," that is not forgiveness; that is excusing. 

Forgiveness requires looking squarely at an offense, ethically judging it as wrong, acknowledging the debt it created, and then *choosing to absorb the cost rather than demanding their destruction.* 

In summary, Luke 6:37 does not ask us to turn off our moral brains, but to discipline our moral hearts. We are called to discern sharply, but to replace the human appetite for condemnation with the divine appetite for mercy.
