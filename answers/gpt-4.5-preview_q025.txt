The moral danger described in Matthew 6:1 lies not in visibility alone, but in the underlying craving for recognition and applause. The issue is the motive—the desire "to be seen of men," meaning that one's good deeds become a performance aimed at human approval rather than authentic acts of compassion or obedience toward God.

Public goodness becomes morally dangerous because:

1. **Distorted Motive**: Acts of charity and righteousness are corrupted if the primary intention shifts from genuine care to seeking admiration and social reward. The goodness becomes instrumental rather than intrinsic.

2. **Hypocrisy and Self-Deception**: Performing goodness publicly can lead to self-deception, masking inner selfishness behind external generosity. One might become convinced of their own virtue, neglecting sincere introspection and humility.

3. **Loss of Spiritual Reward**: The text implies that if earthly recognition becomes the primary goal, the deeper, spiritual reward—God’s acknowledgment and relationship—is forfeited. The act loses its eternal significance by settling for fleeting human approval.

Thus, the verse critiques not the simple fact of being visible, but the moral psychology behind visibility: the craving to be admired rather than the genuine desire to do good.
