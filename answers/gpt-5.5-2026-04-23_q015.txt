The image is sharp because it is deliberately **absurd, bodily, and morally exact**.

A “mote” is a tiny speck—something irritating but small. A “beam” is a great plank or roof-beam. Jesus places both in the **eye**, the organ of sight, judgment, and perception. The result is comic and cutting: a man with a massive piece of timber sticking out of his own eye is offering delicate eye surgery to someone with a speck in his.

That makes the image powerful in several ways:

1. **It exposes disproportion**  
   The critic sees a small fault in another person while ignoring a much greater fault in himself.

2. **It exposes blindness**  
   The problem is not only moral failure but distorted vision. The hypocrite cannot “see clearly” because his own sin obstructs his judgment.

3. **It exposes presumption**  
   Removing a speck from someone’s eye requires tenderness, accuracy, and humility. But the hypocrite is in no condition to help; he is dangerous precisely because he thinks he can see.

4. **It uses humor as rebuke**  
   The picture is ridiculous: a plank-eyed man fussing over another’s speck. The absurdity makes the hypocrisy unmistakable.

But the passage does **not** forbid moral correction altogether. Jesus does not say, “Never remove the mote from your brother’s eye.” He says:

> “First cast out the beam out of thine own eye; and then shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote…”

So the order matters.

The criticism is aimed at **hypocritical judgment**, not all judgment. Jesus condemns the person who corrects others while refusing self-examination. But after repentance, humility, and clearer vision, one may help a brother with his fault.

In other words, the passage teaches:

- Do not correct others while excusing yourself.
- Do not magnify another’s small fault while minimizing your own greater one.
- Do not attempt moral “surgery” without humility and self-knowledge.
- But once you have dealt honestly with your own sin, you may be able to help another person more gently and truly.

The goal is not moral indifference. It is **purified judgment**—correction without hypocrisy, truth joined to humility.
