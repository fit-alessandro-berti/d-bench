The immediate opposition between **bitterness** (and anger) and **forgiveness** in Ephesians 4:31–32 is a deliberate rhetorical device to expose the root cause of broken community relationships. It argues that bitterness cannot coexist with true healing unless it is transformed.

Here is an analysis of the specific nature of these concepts in this passage, addressing your questions about whether they are feelings, habits, postures, or more contagious than one might assume.

### 1. Is Bitterness a "Feelings"?
The text refers to them as **"be put away from you"** rather than "leave out of you," which distinguishes between removing a temporary emotion and changing the disposition.
*   **Feeling:** Bitterness is primarily an internal state or "feeling." It is often described in Scripture as making one "threw" something into oneself (like bitterness that causes someone to feel sick).
*   **However,** the text does not treat it merely as a transient emotion like sadness. If bitterness were just a feeling, forgiveness would be easy because an emotion is fleeting. The opposite must be true for verses 32 to hold: "Be ye kind one to another." You cannot truly forgive someone if you hold onto their injury in your heart as a permanent state of mind.

### 2. Is It a "Choice" or a "Posture"?
Yes, the text treats this as a **posture** (or attitude) of the heart.
*   You can act forgivingly even if one feels bitterness for a moment, but to truly be in fellowship with God is not about how you handle an incident, but how the incident affects your relationship with others.
*   By saying "be ye kind" and "tenderhearted," Paul is describing a new identity that cannot be adopted by choosing a feeling of forgiveness (which is rare), but rather by changing one's **posture** in relation to God. This implies that you are not putting away the *bitterness*, but putting away the *attitude* toward others caused by your own bitterness.

### 3. Why "Contagious" Instead of Infectious?
This is a crucial distinction in this chapter. While the text might lead someone to think bitterness is something like an infection that spreads, actually it says **"with all malice,"** suggesting a contagious nature, but not an infectious disease one way or another.
*   **Bitterness is Transmissible:** When you allow bitterness to infect *yourself*, it inevitably leaks into the relationship with others (the community). If you can't let yourself have bitterness, you cannot hold out forgiveness to the other side of your heart.
*   **Community Impact:** The text does not list "contagious disease" but refers to a **"leakage."** Bitterness is like an active force in your heart that pushes against others in ways that make forgiveness impossible. In other words, you don't spread bitterness *by infecting the community*; you spread it by letting your own bitterness *seep* from one side of the relationship.
*   **Grace vs. Disease:** However, "with all malice" is followed by "even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you." This implies that even if we had to forgive others, we were allowed to do so by God first—and that God extends the grace He showed us back to us first, then to the community. Bitterness destroys this capacity because it prevents a heart from being open to receiving and giving forgiveness.

### 4. Theological Purpose: Why Direct Opposition?
The verse places them side-by-side in order to highlight that these are not just personal grievances, but **mutually destructive forces.**
*   **Bitterness is self-reinforcing:** It fuels the cycle of "If I have hurt you, then I should be angry." If you feel justified in being angry at a neighbor (because you feel wronged by them), that anger feeds the bitterness.
*   **Forgiveness is an external response to internal grace:** You must forgive God's kindness toward *you*, not your own grievances. And because that kind of forgiveness comes from God, you are called to give it back to those who love *you*.

### Conclusion: Why Direct Opposition?
The opposition exists for a reason: **To restore a relationship within yourself and the community.**

If you cannot forgive, you cannot truly receive grace (which is essential to "put away" bitterness). Bitterness is not simply an emotion; it is a **hardened state** that stops a person from having God's kind word in their heart. Therefore, bitterness must be removed not just because anger hurts, but because without forgiveness, you cannot be truly one with the other side of the relationship (the community).

In short: We do not fight bitterness because it is hard to get rid of. We fight it because **God has forgiven us.** And we are called to *be* like God by being as tolerant and kind as He is toward us.
