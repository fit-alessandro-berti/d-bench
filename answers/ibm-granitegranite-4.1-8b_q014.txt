**Treasure Reveals the Heart, Not Necessarily Trains It**

In Matthew 6:19–21, Jesus is addressing the nature of true treasure and its relationship to the heart. The passage suggests that **treasure reveals** where one’s heart is focused, rather than that treasure directly **trains** the heart. Here’s a breakdown of how the concepts interconnect:

### **Treasure Reveals the Heart**

1. **Observed Direction:**  
   - The phrase *“where your treasure is, there will your heart be also”* indicates a reflective relationship. The location of one’s treasure—a symbol for what one values most—mirrors the primary inclination of the heart. If someone’s treasure is on earthly possessions, their heart (desires, affections, priorities) is likewise oriented toward those earthly things. Conversely, if the treasure is in heavenly things (e.g., righteousness, spiritual growth, eternal life), the heart follows.

2. **Contrast with “Training” the Heart:**  
   - While some interpret the text as implying that focusing on heavenly treasures *shapes* or *trains* the heart over time, the wording of the verse points more to **instantaneous reflection** rather than a progressive process. Jesus is emphasizing a *present condition*: the heart’s current orientation is evident in what it treasures.

### **Possession, Attention, and Love Intertwined**

1. **Possession as a Reflection of Attention:**  
   - Possessions (whether material wealth or spiritual goods) are not mere objects; they are sites where attention is directed. When we *lay up treasures*—i.e., allocate time, energy, and resources—we are actively *giving attention* to those things. Our accumulation of possessions, therefore, is a visible sign of where we concentrate our mental and emotional effort.

2. **Attention Shaping Love:**  
   - Attention precedes love in the developmental chain:  
     - **Attention → Familiarity → Affection → Love**  
   - When we habitually attend to earthly treasures, we grow familiar with them, develop a love for their security or status, and ultimately prioritize them over higher values. Conversely, constant attention on heavenly treasures cultivates a love for eternal realities, aligning the heart with divine priorities.

3. **Love as the Binding Principle:**  
   - The heart’s *affection* is the emotional glue that binds attention to possession. Love for something makes us desire it, protect it, and seek it. Thus, the path is:  
     - **Love (or lack thereof) → Attention → Possession of (or desire for) that which is loved.**  
   - In Jesus’ teaching, love for God and His kingdom (the “heavenly” treasures) naturally redirects attention away from fleeting earthly goods, revealing a heart oriented toward divine love.

### **Illustrative Summary**

- **Earthly Treasure Example:**  
  - *Possession*: Accumulating wealth, status symbols, comfort.  
  - *Attention*: Constantly checking bank balances, planning acquisitions, worrying about loss.  
  - *Love*: A love for security, status, or material comfort.  
  - *Heart Orientation*: Centered on self-preservation and worldly gain.

- **Heavenly Treasure Example:**  
  - *Possession*: Spiritual growth, acts of kindness, faith in God’s promises.  
  - *Attention*: Praying, studying scripture, serving others.  
  - *Love*: A love for God, righteousness, and eternal truth.  
  - *Heart Orientation*: Centered on divine purpose and eternal life.

### **Conclusion**

Matthew 6:19–21 underscores that **treasure reveals the heart’s true orientation**, rather than serving as a tool to *train* the heart. The interplay of possession, attention, and love is such that what we hold dear (“treasure”) is a mirror of where our hearts love. By recognizing this mirror, we can discern whether our hearts have been aligned with earthly or heavenly values and, if necessary, adjust our affections to reflect the divine priorities Jesus espouses. 

Thus, the passage invites believers to examine their treasured pursuits, ensuring that their hearts—and by extension, their lives—are directed toward the eternal, imperishable riches of heaven.
