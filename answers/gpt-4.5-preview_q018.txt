The text places bitterness and forgiveness directly against each other to sharply contrast two fundamentally opposite orientations toward relationships and personal identity. Bitterness, as described here, isn't merely an isolated feeling but is presented as something deeper and more dangerous—a chosen posture or habit of holding onto grievances. It includes anger, resentment, wrath, malicious speech, and an overall attitude that poisons human relationships.

In contrast, forgiveness is depicted not just as an occasional action but as a consistent posture of kindness, tenderheartedness, and generosity of spirit. Forgiveness here is grounded explicitly in the awareness of having first received forgiveness from God through Christ. It is an intentional, transformative choice that refuses to sustain past hurts.

By setting bitterness directly against forgiveness, the text suggests that bitterness is inherently contagious—an inner attitude that seeps into speech, relationships, and community life. Bitterness is not just private suffering but a communal toxin. Forgiveness, therefore, is the antidote, actively disarming bitterness by interrupting cycles of resentment and hostility.

In short, the passage portrays bitterness primarily as a chosen posture that becomes habit-forming and infectious, directly undermining community life, while forgiveness is the intentional, transformative alternative that restores relationships and reflects divine grace.
