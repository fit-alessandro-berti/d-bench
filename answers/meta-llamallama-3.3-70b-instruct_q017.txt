The passage from John 3:19-21 highlights a profound human dilemma: the preference for darkness over light when one's actions are not in alignment with truth or righteousness. The aversion to light in this context stems from the human tendency to avoid exposure and the resultant feelings of shame or guilt that come with being seen clearly. Let's delve into the relationship between truth, exposure, shame, and the desire not to be seen clearly, and why light, which symbolizes truth and righteousness, would be hated by those who do evil.

1. **Truth and Exposure**: Truth, symbolized by light in the biblical context, has the power to expose. When light shines, it illuminates everything, making the visible, visible. For individuals who engage in evil deeds, the light of truth poses a significant threat because it has the potential to expose their actions, revealing their true nature and the wrongfulness of their behavior.

2. **Shame and Guilt**: The exposure brought about by light leads to feelings of shame and guilt. Shame is the painful feeling of being exposed in one's nakedness or wrongdoing, while guilt is the awareness of having done wrong. For those who do evil, the prospect of facing their own wrongdoing, and facing others who might judge them for it, is daunting. The avoidance of these negative emotions is a powerful motivator for preferring darkness, where such feelings can be evaded or suppressed.

3. **The Desire Not to Be Seen Clearly**: Humans have an inherent desire for privacy and a degree of control over how they are perceived by others. Engaging in evil deeds compromises this desire, as the individual knows they would be judged negatively if their actions were known. The light threatens to remove the cover of secrecy and anonymity, forcing the individual into a position where they must confront their actions and the consequences thereof. This confrontation is uncomfortable and often leads to a preference for remaining in the shadows, away from the revealing power of light.

4. **Fear of Accountability**: Another factor contributing to the hatred of light is the fear of accountability. Light not only exposes one's actions but also demands accountability. In the light, there is no hiding from the consequences of one's deeds. This fear of being held accountable for evil actions further solidifies the preference for darkness, where one can avoid the repercussions of their behavior.

5. **Contrast with Those Who Do Truth**: The passage contrasts those who hate the light with those who do truth and come to the light. For individuals whose deeds are wrought in God, or are in accordance with truth and righteousness, the light does not pose a threat but an opportunity. They welcome the light because it affirms their actions and demonstrates their commitment to living in accordance with higher principles. This welcoming of light signifies a readiness to be transparent, to be seen clearly, and to be judged based on one's righteous deeds.

In summary, the hatred for light among those who do evil stems from the desire to avoid exposure, shame, guilt, and accountability. The preference for darkness allows them to maintain secrecy and avoid the uncomfortable confrontation with their wrongdoing. Conversely, those who do truth embrace the light, as it validates their actions and reflects their integrity. This dichotomy underscores the complex human relationship with truth and how it influences our preferences and behaviors.
