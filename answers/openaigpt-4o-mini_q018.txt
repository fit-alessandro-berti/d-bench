In Ephesians 4:31–32, bitterness and forgiveness are indeed presented in stark contrast to one another. This juxtaposition highlights the fundamental choice individuals face in their emotional and relational lives: to harbor negative feelings and attitudes or to embrace kindness and forgiveness.

1. **Bitterness as a Chosen Posture**: The text implies that bitterness is not merely an involuntary feeling but a chosen attitude or posture. The use of active language—"Let all bitterness... be put away from you"—suggests that individuals have the agency to reject bitterness consciously. This implies a decision to cultivate a different emotional response.

2. **Bitterness as a Habit**: Over time, bitterness can develop into a habit. When negative feelings are not addressed, they can become ingrained in a person's thinking and behavior. The text encourages a breaking away from these patterns in favor of kindness and forgiveness, which may require deliberate effort and practice.

3. **Contagiousness of Bitterness**: The relational implications of bitterness are significant. When one person harbors bitterness, it can affect their interactions with others, spreading negativity and strife. The exhortation to "be ye kind one to another" suggests the importance of fostering a positive relational environment, counteracting the potential contagion of bitterness.

4. **Forgiveness as a Counteraction**: Forgiveness is positioned as the antidote to bitterness. The text points to the divine example of forgiveness offered by God for Christ's sake, indicating that understanding and receiving this grace empowers individuals to forgive others. This notion reinforces that forgiveness is a choice that can heal relationships and counter the corrosive effects of bitterness.

In summary, the text treats bitterness as a chosen posture and a potential habit while recognizing its relational consequences. Forgiveness is presented as an essential counteraction, promoting healing and kindness in interpersonal relationships.
