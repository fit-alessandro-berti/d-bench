Great question. In Luke 15, the elder brother’s **deepest problem is not mainly anger** (though he is angry), nor merely envy or fairness language.  
His core issue is:

## **a righteousness-without-relationship heart**  
—or in one phrase, **self-righteous estrangement from the father’s mercy**.

He says, “these many years **do I serve thee**…” (literally “slave for you”), which reveals a **transactional mindset**:  
- *I obey, therefore I deserve.*  
- *He failed, therefore he should be excluded.*

So:

- **Anger** = symptom  
- **Envy/wounded fairness** = symptom  
- **Pride in obedience** = major mechanism  
- **Discomfort with mercy** = where it finally shows itself

But beneath all of them is this: he does not share his father’s heart. He is physically near the father, yet spiritually distant—“lost at home,” while the younger was “lost away.”

In Luke’s broader context (Jesus with sinners; Pharisees grumbling), the elder brother represents religious people who can keep rules yet resist grace when it is given to the undeserving. The father’s final words (“this **thy brother**…”) invite him to move from merit to mercy, from bookkeeping to family joy.
