**Bitterness and forgiveness are placed in direct contrast because bitterness is fundamentally the fruit and fuel of unforgiveness.** 

In Ephesians 4:31–32, Paul is giving practical instructions for life in the new community created by the gospel (the immediate context is church unity—see Eph 4:1–3, 25–32, and the “put off/put on” language of the new self throughout the chapter). The list in v. 31 begins with *pikria* (bitterness, harsh resentment, a settled sharpness of spirit) and then names its typical expressions: wrath (*thumos*—explosive anger), anger (*orgē*—brooding indignation), clamor (loud quarreling), evil speaking/slander, and malice (ill will). These are not isolated personal emotions; they are relational toxins that destroy the “one body” Christ has made.

Verse 32 supplies the positive replacement: “be kind to one another, tenderhearted, *forgiving one another, even as God in Christ forgave you*.” The structure is deliberate. Bitterness grows most naturally from a refusal to release offenses. When we nurse grievances instead of forgiving as we have been forgiven, resentment hardens into a default posture. Forgiveness, modeled on the gospel, is the active remedy that uproots it. Paul is not saying “stop feeling bitter and start feeling kind.” He is saying: *release the old way of relating* (rooted in self-justifying resentment) and *adopt the new way* (rooted in God’s mercy in Christ). The two are opposites in the same way death and life, the old self and the new, are opposites.

### How the text treats bitterness
The passage treats bitterness as **primarily a chosen posture or cultivable habit that must be actively “put away,” while also recognizing it as something deeper and contagious**.

- **Chosen posture/habit**: The verb “put away” (*apotithēmi*) is the same one used for discarding old, filthy clothes (see Eph 4:22; Col 3:8; James 1:21). It is an imperative—an act of the will, enabled by the Spirit and the new identity in Christ. Bitterness is not portrayed as an uncontrollable feeling that just happens to you. It is something you let grow and then must deliberately reject. It easily becomes habitual, a settled orientation of the heart that colors how you interpret every offense.

- **More than a mere feeling**: While it involves emotion, *pikria* is deeper and more enduring than fleeting anger. The other terms (wrath, anger) cover the more emotional/explosive side. Bitterness is what remains when the initial heat has cooled into cold, corrosive resentment.

- **Contagious (“something more”)**: The New Testament elsewhere makes this explicit. Hebrews 12:15 warns of a “root of bitterness” that “springs up and causes trouble, and by it many become defiled.” One person’s unresolved resentment can poison relationships, spread suspicion, justify gossip or division, and damage the whole community. In the context of Ephesians 4, this is especially serious because Paul is fighting for the unity of Jew and Gentile in one body. Bitterness is not private; it is corporate in its effects. That is why the command is corporate (“*you* [plural]”) and why the antidote—mutual, gospel-shaped forgiveness—is also corporate.

In short, Paul places bitterness and forgiveness in direct opposition because **unforgiveness is the soil in which bitterness grows, and gospel forgiveness is the only thing strong enough to kill it**. The text calls believers to treat bitterness as a dangerous, willful, contagious weed that must be pulled up by the roots and replaced with the tenderhearted kindness that flows from remembering how much we have been forgiven in Christ. This is not mere moral effort; it is sanctification powered by the gospel.
