Yes—this passage connects speech to the **inner moral condition** of the person, and it strongly suggests **all three**: **symptoms, choices, and revelations**.

### 1) Words are **symptoms**
Jesus says, “out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh.”  
That means speech is an outward sign of what already fills the heart. If bitterness, pride, greed, or purity is “stored up” internally, speech reveals it.

So words are **diagnostic**: they show what is already there.

### 2) Words are **choices**
The passage also implies moral responsibility. A person doesn’t merely “leak” words mechanically; they **bring forth** speech from their heart’s treasury. “Bringeth forth” suggests active expression. What someone says is not accidental in a moral sense—it comes from what they have allowed to dwell within them.

So words are also **chosen expressions** of inner character.

### 3) Words are **revelations**
Jesus contrasts “good treasure” and “evil treasure.” That makes speech a kind of unveiling of hidden stockpiles. The mouth exposes what the heart has been storing, valuing, rehearsing, and nourishing.

So words are **revelatory**: they disclose the moral inventory of the heart.

## In short
This passage teaches that speech is:

- a **symptom** of the heart’s condition,
- a **choice** emerging from inner disposition,
- and a **revelation** of the heart’s stored moral wealth or corruption.

### The larger point
Jesus is not saying only that bad people say bad things. He is saying that **speech flows from the heart**, so if the speech is corrupt, the heart must be addressed. The remedy is not merely better vocabulary or self-censorship, but a changed inner life.
