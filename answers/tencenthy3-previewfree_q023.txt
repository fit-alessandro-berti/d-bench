Isaiah 55:6–7 is a climactic call in the prophecy, set within the context of Israel’s exile (and the broader human exile from God). The pairing of **"way"** (*derek*) and **"thoughts"** (*maḥshavot*) is theologically dense. It implies that sin is not merely a behavioral problem, but an ontological and intellectual one.

Here is a breakdown of why repentance involves both, and what this implies about conduct, imagination, and mercy.

### 1. The Dual Nature of Sin: "Way" vs. "Thoughts"

In Hebrew psychology, the "heart" (the inner engine of the person) dictates both the "thoughts" (the internal reasoning) and the "way" (the external path).

*   **The "Way" (Outward Conduct):** This refers to behavior, habits, lifestyle, and the actual trajectory of one's life. It is the visible evidence of where a person is going. In the context of Isaiah, the "way" of the wicked often involved social injustice, idolatry, and covenant breaking (Isaiah 53:6).
*   **The "Thoughts" (Inward Imagination):** The Hebrew word *maḥshavot* comes from a root meaning "to reckon" or "to devise." It refers to the intentions, the rationalizations, the inner logic, and the mindset. It is not just a fleeting idea, but the settled pattern of how one perceives reality.

**Why both?**
If you only change your **way** (behavior) without changing your **thoughts** (mindset), you become a legalist or a hypocrite—externally conforming while internally resentful or idolatrous.
If you only change your **thoughts** (intellectually agree you are wrong) without changing your **way** (behavior), you become a phony or stagnant—having "knowledge without power."

Isaiah emphasizes that true repentance requires a **dual transformation**: the *engine* (thoughts) and the *motion* (way) must both be reversed.

### 2. Implications for Outward Conduct and Inward Imagination

**A. Outward Conduct (The Way)**
The call to forsake the "way" implies that repentance is active. It requires a cessation of destructive patterns. You cannot return to the Lord while continuing to walk away from Him. This implies that morality and ethics are not optional add-ons to faith; they are the fruit of it. The "way" is the proof that the internal change is real.

**B. Inward Imagination (The Thoughts)**
This is the more radical requirement. The "thoughts" represent the deep-seated justifications we use to stay in sin. We often rationalize: *“God wants me to be happy,”* or *“This isn't that bad compared to others,”* or *“I can't change.”*
To forsake the thoughts means to dismantle the **internal logic of sin**. It requires admitting that your assessment of reality (what is good, what is true, what brings life) was wrong. It is a humbling of the intellect before God.

### 3. The Possibility of Mercy: The "For" (Kī) Connection

The verse connects the command to repent with the promise of mercy using the word "for" (Hebrew *kī*):

> "...and let him return unto the Lord, **for** (*kī*) he will have mercy upon him; and to our God, **for** (*kī*) he will abundantly pardon."

The implication here is profound:

1.  **God's Character is the Ground of Repentance:** We are not told to clean ourselves up to *make* God merciful. Rather, we are told to return *because* He is merciful. The mercy precedes the act of turning.
2.  **The "Abundance" of Pardon:** The Hebrew word for "abundantly pardon" is *yasreh*. It literally means "to multiply pardon" or "to enlarge forgiveness." It implies that no matter how entrenched the "way" or how corrupted the "thoughts," the capacity of God’s pardon exceeds the depth of human depravity.
3.  **The Accessibility of Grace:** Verse 6 says, "while he is near." The fact that He is near implies that the barrier is not distance, but the hardness of the human heart (the "way" and "thoughts").

### Summary

The inclusion of both **"way"** and **"thoughts"** implies that **sin is a total corruption** (affecting both action and motive) and therefore **salvation must be a total renovation**.

*   **Conduct:** Must be forsaken (stop doing the bad).
*   **Imagination:** Must be forsaken (stop thinking the bad).
*   **Mercy:** Is available *because* God's nature is to forgive, not because our repentance earns it.

It teaches that God is not interested in mere external compliance; He wants the "thoughts"—the very command center of the soul—to be aligned with His reality, trusting that His mercy is great enough to cover even the darkest inner reasoning.
