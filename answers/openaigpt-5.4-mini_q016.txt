The elder brother’s deepest problem is **not just anger**. Anger is the surface expression.

At the heart of his reaction is a **graceless righteousness**: he has been obeying the father, but he does not know the father’s heart. He thinks in terms of **merit, entitlement, and comparison**, not sonship and mercy.

So among the options you gave, the deepest problem is best described as:

- **pride in obedience**
- **wounded fairness**
- and, underneath both, **discomfort with mercy itself**

### Why this is the deepest issue
The elder brother says, in effect:

- “I’ve earned this.”
- “He doesn’t deserve celebration.”
- “If mercy is shown to him, then my years of service have been ignored.”

That shows he sees his relationship with the father as **transactional** rather than filial. He obeys, but not out of love. He is “inside the house” physically, but still emotionally distant from the father’s joy.

### His real problem
His real problem is that he does not share the father’s priorities. The father rejoices over the lost being found; the elder brother resents that same mercy. So his anger reveals a heart that cannot celebrate grace given to others.

### In one sentence
**The elder brother’s deepest problem is a self-righteous, merit-based heart that resents mercy when it is given freely.**
