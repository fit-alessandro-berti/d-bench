In Matthew 6:1, Jesus warns against doing acts of righteousness—specifically giving alms (charitable deeds)—with the intention of being observed by others. The key moral issue here is not the visibility of the good act itself, but the **motivation behind seeking visibility**.

### What makes public goodness morally dangerous in this verse?

- **It is the craving to be seen by others (pride or desire for human approval)** that corrupts the moral value of the act.
- When people give alms to gain reputation, admiration, or honor from others, their reward is limited to human recognition.
- As a result, **they “have no reward of your Father which is in heaven”**—meaning their spiritual or eternal reward from God is forfeited because the act was not done purely for God's sake but for self-glorification.

### Is the problem visibility itself, or the craving to be seen?

- The problem is **not visibility itself**. Doing good in public is not inherently wrong.
- The problem is **the craving to be seen**—the desire for recognition or praise from others that turns a sincere good deed into an act of hypocrisy or self-promotion.
- This teaching encourages sincerity and humility: doing good should be motivated by genuine love and devotion to God, not by the desire for human approval.

**Summary:**  
Matthew 6:1 teaches that public acts of goodness become morally dangerous when the intent is to attract attention and praise from others rather than to serve God sincerely. It is the *craving to be seen* that undermines the true value of the good deed, not the visibility of the act itself.
