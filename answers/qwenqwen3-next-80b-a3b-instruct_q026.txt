Matthew 12:34–35 connects speech with the hidden moral “stockpiles” of the heart in a profound and unmistakable way: **words are not mere external expressions—they are direct revelations of what is inwardly stored in the heart.** Jesus uses the metaphor of “treasure” to describe the moral and spiritual condition of a person’s inner being. Just as a treasury contains accumulated wealth, the heart contains a “treasure” of values, intentions, affections, and character—either good or evil.

### How Speech Connects to the Heart’s "Treasure":
- **“Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh”** (v.34): This is the central principle. Speech flows *naturally* and *inevitably* from what is most deeply held in the heart. Words are not manufactured on the spot independently of inner state; they are the overflow, the “abundance”—the surplus that can’t be contained.
- **“A good man out of the good treasure... bringeth forth good things”** (v.35): A person whose heart is aligned with goodness produces speech that reflects righteousness—not because they are trying to perform, but because goodness is their default reservoir.
- **“An evil man out of the evil treasure... bringeth forth evil things”**: Likewise, malicious or corrupt inner dispositions naturally express themselves through harmful, cynical, or sinful speech—no matter how hard the person tries to mask it.

### Are Words Symptoms, Choices, or Revelations? — **All Three.**

1. **Symptoms**:  
   Words reveal the *state* of the heart, just as a fever reveals an infection. Jesus is indicting the Pharisees’ blasphemous speech (“He casts out demons by Beelzebul”) as a symptom of their inner spiritual corruption. Their words don’t cause their evil—they *expose* it.

2. **Choices**:  
   While the flow is natural, the passage implies that people are accountable for what they store in their hearts. The “treasure” isn’t passive—it is cultivated through repeated choices. Evil speech is not an accident; it is the consequence of a habitually evil heart, shaped by willful neglect of goodness or embrace of malice. We choose where we deposit our treasures (cf. Matthew 6:19–21).

3. **Revelations**:  
   Most powerfully, speech *unveils* the hidden. Jesus says, “You cannot hide who you really are.” The mouth becomes God’s instrument of exposure. In the courtroom of divine judgment, words are not just evidence—they are *identity*. “By your words you will be justified, and by your words you will be condemned” (Matt. 12:37).

### Conclusion:
Matthew 12:34–35 presents speech as **an irresistible revelation of the heart’s moral inventory**, which itself is formed over time by conscious choices—and therefore, words are also symptoms of moral condition. The triad is inseparable:  
> **Words are the revelation of what the heart treasures, the symptom of what it has become, and the fruit of what it has chosen to harbor.**

This passage calls us to examine not just what we say, but what we *allow to accumulate within*—because eventually, our words will betray the true state of our treasure.
