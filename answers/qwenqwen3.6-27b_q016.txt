The elder brother’s deepest problem is **discomfort with mercy itself**, though it presents itself through layers of anger, envy, wounded fairness, and pride in obedience. To understand why, it helps to trace how these elements function in the passage:

1. **Anger, envy, and wounded fairness** are the *immediate symptoms*. They are visible in his refusal to enter the feast and his sharp complaint about the disparity in rewards. But symptoms point to a deeper cause.

2. **Pride in obedience** is the *psychological and relational mechanism*. His language reveals a works-based identity: “I serve thee… neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment.” Notice he does not say, “I love you” or “I’m glad he’s home.” He speaks like a hired employee keeping a ledger, not a son resting in relationship. His self-worth is tied to his compliance, and his reward expectations are transactional.

3. **Discomfort with mercy itself** is the *theological root*. The elder brother doesn’t just want fairness; he wants a system where mercy is earned. When the father lavishes grace on a repentant rebel, it violates his calculus. In his view, the father’s response to the younger brother isn’t restorative love; it’s unjust favoritism. The real scandal isn’t the younger son’s return; it’s that the father’s grace operates outside a merit-based economy.

The father’s reply exposes this directly: *“Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine.”* The elder brother has been living in the reality of grace but has mistaken inheritance for wages. He claims to be a son while functioning like a servant, blind to the very mercy he already enjoys. His refusal to party is, at its core, a refusal to celebrate a father whose heart breaks mercy over merit.

In Luke’s broader context, this brother represents the Pharisees and scribes who murmur because Jesus dines with tax collectors and sinners (Luke 15:1-2). They are not outraged that sinners get grace; they are outraged that grace disrupts their covenantal performance metrics. 

So while anger, envy, wounded fairness, and pride in obedience are all present, they branch from a deeper inability to accept unearned mercy. The elder brother’s deepest problem is that **he loves his record more than his father’s heart**, and that makes him an outsider in his own house.
