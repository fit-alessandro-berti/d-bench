Based on the text of Matthew 6:1 and its immediate context, the answer is **the craving to be seen.**

While the verse warns against doing alms "before men," the moral danger lies specifically in the qualifying phrase **"to be seen of them."**

Here is a breakdown of why the text identifies the *motive* (the craving) rather than the *visibility* itself as the problem:

### 1. The Grammar of Intent
The Greek phrase used for "to be seen" is *pros to theathēnai autois*. It carries the connotation of theater—to be a spectacle or to be gazed at. The warning is not merely about geography (being in public) but about **orientation** (performing for an audience).

If visibility were the inherent evil, then any act of kindness done in a public workplace or a visible donation would be sinful by default. However, the text targets the *intent* behind the visibility. The danger is turning an act of mercy into a performance of vanity.

### 2. The Economy of Reward
Jesus frames morality here in terms of an economic exchange or a "reward." He posits a binary choice:
*   **Reward A:** Praise from men (social status, clout, reputation).
*   **Reward B:** Approval from the Father (spiritual intimacy, eternal significance).

The verse states that if you perform goodness to secure Reward A, you are disqualified from receiving Reward B. The moral danger is that the **craving for human applause cannibalizes the spiritual value of the act.** Once you have "received" your prize in the form of human admiration, the transaction is closed; there is nothing left for God to repay.

### 3. Contextual Consistency (Matthew 5:16)
A strong argument that visibility itself is not the problem is found just one chapter prior. In Matthew 5:15–16, Jesus says:
> *"Neither do men light a candle, and put it under a bushel, but on a candlestick... Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."*

Here, Jesus commands believers to be visible "before men."
*   **In Matthew 5:** Goodness is public so that people can see it and give glory to **God**. (Focus: God)
*   **In Matthew 6:** Goodness is public so that people can see it and give glory to **You**. (Focus: Self)

### Summary
Public goodness is morally dangerous when it becomes **theatrical**. The verse warns that the ego is hungry for attention; if we feed the ego with public praise, we starve the soul of its connection to the divine. The problem is not that eyes are watching you, but that you are **acting for the eyes** rather than for the Father.
