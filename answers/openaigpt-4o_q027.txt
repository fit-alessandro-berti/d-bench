In this parable, several aspects contribute to the condemnation of the Pharisee, and more than one factor is at play:

1. **Pride**: The Pharisee's prayer is filled with self-congratulation and pride. He expresses gratitude not for God’s mercy and grace, but for his own perceived superiority over others. This self-righteous attitude indicates a lack of humility.

2. **Comparison and Contempt**: The Pharisee compares himself to others, particularly the tax collector, to elevate his own status. He despises those he considers sinners and positions himself as morally superior, demonstrating contempt.

3. **Use of Virtue as Self-Display**: The Pharisee touts his religious practices, such as fasting and tithing, as evidence of his righteousness. He uses these virtues as a means to display his piety rather than as genuine acts of devotion to God.

The tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer because it embodies humility and repentance. Unlike the Pharisee, the tax collector recognizes his sinfulness and approaches God with a contrite heart, pleading for mercy. He does not justify himself or compare himself to others but rather admits his shortcomings and seeks forgiveness. This humility and acknowledgement of one’s need for God's grace is presented as the appropriate and sincere approach to spirituality, leading to his justification.
