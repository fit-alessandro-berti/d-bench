Because in John’s thought, **love for God is never merely “vertical” or internal; it always shows itself in concrete, “horizontal” love.** The “seen” neighbor is where the claimed love of the “unseen” God must become real, or it isn’t real at all.

### 1. Why love of the visible neighbor is the test

Several layers are at work here:

1. **Argument from the easier to the harder**  
   John’s reasoning is basically:  
   - Your brother is *visible*, tangible, close at hand.  
   - God is *invisible*, distant to your senses.  
   If you cannot bring yourself to love the one who is right in front of you—whose needs, weaknesses, and humanity you can’t avoid—then your claim to love the infinitely greater, invisible God is not credible. You are failing at the “easier” task while boasting of doing the “harder” one.

2. **The neighbor bears God’s image**  
   Biblically, humans are made in God’s image. So:
   - How you treat God’s image is how you treat God (cf. Gen 9:6; Jas 3:9).
   - To “hate” the image while claiming to love the Prototype is incoherent.  
   John is exposing a contradiction: you despise what God loves, yet claim to love God Himself.

3. **The incarnational logic of 1 John**  
   1 John strongly emphasizes that:
   - The eternal Word became *flesh* (1 Jn 4:2; cf. Jn 1:14).  
   - True knowledge of God is not abstract “spirituality” detached from body, community, and obedience.
   The “brother whom you have seen” is part of this incarnational reality. Love of the unseen God must pass through the concrete, bodily world of relationships. To bypass the brother is to bypass the very arena where God’s love is meant to appear (1 Jn 4:12).

4. **Love of God and love of neighbor are one command**  
   1 John 4:21 continues:  
   > “And this commandment have we from him, That he who loveth God love his brother also.”  
   In Johannine (and wider NT) theology:
   - Love of God and love of neighbor are inseparable aspects of a single obedience (cf. Matt 22:37–40).  
   So if one is absent, the other is not merely “incomplete”; it is falsified.

### 2. What self-deception is being exposed?

The verse unmasks several kinds of self-deception:

1. **Mistaking religious feeling or language for love**  
   A person may:
   - Enjoy worship, prayer, theology, or “talking about God.”  
   - Feel strong emotions toward God.  
   - Be doctrinally correct and morally conservative.  
   Yet simultaneously harbor contempt, bitterness, prejudice, or indifference toward actual people.  
   John calls this person a “liar” because:
   - In Scripture, love is not primarily a feeling but a commitment to will and seek the good of the other, often at cost to oneself.
   If that concrete willingness is missing toward people, the claimed “love” for God is exposed as sentiment, self-image, or words—not real love.

2. **Separating ‘spirituality’ from ethics and relationships**  
   The self-deception says:
   - “My relationship with God is fine; my issues with people are a separate matter.”  
   John denies this distinction. For him:
   - To “know” God is to be drawn into God’s own love (1 Jn 4:7–8).  
   - If your life is not becoming more like God’s love, you do not in fact “know” Him as you claim.  
   So the person is lying not only to others but to themselves about the state of their soul.

3. **Loving a projection instead of the true God**  
   To “love God” while hating others often means:
   - You love a *version* of God who conveniently affirms your tribe, your grievances, your resentments.  
   - You do not love the true God who “is love,” who sends rain on just and unjust, who loved His enemies.  
   The test of the brother exposes whether you love **God as He is**, or a spiritualized projection of your own ego.

4. **Rationalizing hatred as righteous**  
   Especially in religious contexts, hatred or refusal to love is often baptized as:
   - “Zeal for truth,”  
   - “Defending purity,”  
   - “Protecting the church,”  
   while actually rooted in pride, fear, or rivalry.  
   John refuses to allow that disguise:  
   - If what you call zeal leads you to despise your brother, it is not born of love, and therefore not of God (cf. 1 Jn 3:10–15).

5. **The specific situation behind 1 John**  
   Many scholars think 1 John addresses:
   - A community split, where some left the fellowship (2:19) but claimed superior knowledge of God.  
   Their withdrawal and lack of love for the community exposed their claim as false.  
   The self-deception is: “We are the truly spiritual ones,” while their actual posture toward the brethren is unloving, divisive, and contemptuous.

---

So the verse makes love of the visible neighbor the test of love of the invisible God because:

- Love of God, if genuine, must manifest in the concrete sphere where God has placed us: other people.  
- The brother is both the “easier” object to love (seen, near) and the bearer of God’s image.  
- The God of 1 John is not an abstract idea but the God who is love and who became flesh; to love Him is to participate in His way of loving.

The self-deception exposed is any claim to deep spirituality or love for God that is not accompanied, and proven, by real, sacrificial, patient love toward actual, imperfect people—especially those close enough to see and irritate us.
