The passage from John 3:19–21 presents a profound spiritual metaphor in which **light** symbolizes **truth, goodness, and divine presence**, while **darkness** represents **evil, sin, and ignorance**. The key question—*Why would light be hated?*—invites us to explore the psychological and moral dynamics behind the rejection of truth and exposure.

### Why is light hated?

1. **Light exposes what is hidden:**
   - Light reveals reality clearly. When light enters a dark room, it exposes everything inside—the good and the bad.
   - In a moral or spiritual sense, light exposes a person’s inner thoughts, motives, and actions.
   
2. **People who do evil avoid exposure:**
   - The passage says those who do evil “hate the light” because when their deeds are exposed, they feel shame, guilt, or fear judgment.
   - Evil deeds are often kept hidden in the darkness of ignorance, denial, or secrecy.
   - Exposure threatens their comfortable illusions, social acceptance, or self-justification.

3. **Shame and the desire to avoid shame:**
   - Shame arises when our actions or character are revealed to be contrary to moral or social norms.
   - The “hatred” of light reflects the natural human impulse to avoid shame and the consequences that come with being seen clearly.
   - This may cause people to prefer darkness because it provides a cover, a place where one can live without facing the discomfort of accountability.

4. **Truth as both a convicting and liberating force:**
   - The light not only exposes sin but also invites transformation and healing.
   - Those “that do truth” come to the light willingly, showing a desire for honesty, growth, and alignment with God.
   - They accept exposure because their deeds are “wrought in God” and withstand scrutiny.
   
### Relation between truth, exposure, shame, and the desire not to be seen clearly:

- **Truth demands exposure.** To live truthfully means to align one’s life transparently with what is right. This uncovers what was hidden, sincere or not.
- **Exposure brings accountability.** When exposed to truth (light), actions and motives are judged—not necessarily by others alone, but by one’s own conscience and by God.
- **Shame arises from a clash between exposed reality and desired self-image.** When the reality exposed by light conflicts with how one wants to be seen or how one thinks of oneself, shame and rejection of that light follow.
- **The desire not to be seen clearly stems from fear.** Fear of humiliation, punishment, rejection, or inner guilt can drive people to avoid light, preferring the “darkness” of concealment.
  
#### Summarizing metaphorically:
- **Light = Truth and moral clarity**, inviting transformation and accountability.
- **Darkness = Concealment and avoidance**, allowing continued sin or falsehood without confrontation.
- **Hatred of light = Resistance to truth because it demands change, reveals fault, and confronts human pride and fear.**

Thus, the spiritual condemnation referenced in John 3:19-21 is less about the light itself and more about the human refusal to confront and accept the truth that light brings, choosing instead the comfortable but destructive refuge of darkness.
