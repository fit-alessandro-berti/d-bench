Luke 6:37 is not a command to become morally indifferent. It is a warning against a particular *posture* toward others: the posture that eagerly finds fault, assigns the worst meaning, and delights in rendering a guilty verdict.

Jesus is not saying, “There is no right and wrong.” He is saying, in effect, “Do not take God’s seat as the final, merciless judge of another person.”

## Discernment recognizes; condemnation pronounces

**Discernment** asks:

- What is true here?
- Is this action good, harmful, honest, unjust, wise, or foolish?
- What response is needed to protect people and seek what is right?
- How can I address this truth faithfully and humbly?

**Condemnation** asks something different:

- What is wrong with *that person*?
- How can I establish their guilt and my superiority?
- How can I reduce them to their worst act?
- How can I make sure they receive no mercy?

Discernment can say, “This action is sinful,” or “This person’s conduct is harmful and must be stopped.” Condemnation says, “This is what they are; they are beyond grace; I know their heart; I am entitled to despise them.”

The first seeks truth and restoration. The second seeks a verdict.

## The wider context matters

Immediately before this verse, Jesus says:

> “Be ye therefore merciful, as your Father also is merciful” (Luke 6:36).

And immediately after, he speaks of the “measure” one uses being measured back to oneself (Luke 6:38). The issue is not whether Christians may identify evil; it is whether they do so without remembering that they too stand in need of mercy.

Later in the same chapter Jesus teaches that a tree is known by its fruit (Luke 6:43–45). That requires discernment. One must be able to recognize good and bad fruit. Elsewhere, Jesus calls for judging “with righteous judgment” rather than merely by appearances (John 7:24). The New Testament also calls communities to correct serious wrongdoing, protect the vulnerable, and exercise discipline when necessary.

So “judge not” cannot mean, “Never make a moral assessment.” It means, “Do not engage in self-righteous, harsh, presumptuous judgment.”

## What the appetite to condemn looks like

The appetite to condemn is not simply concern for morality. It is a desire to make another person guilty in a way that gratifies us. It often shows itself through:

- **Eagerness rather than grief:** enjoying exposure instead of mourning sin.
- **Certainty about hidden motives:** assuming one knows why another acted as they did.
- **Selective outrage:** being severe toward others while excusing oneself or one’s own group.
- **Reductionism:** treating a person as identical with their worst failure.
- **No interest in restoration:** wanting punishment, humiliation, or exclusion more than repentance and healing.
- **Forgetfulness of mercy:** acting as though one’s own sins are small and obvious, while others’ are large and unforgivable.

Jesus’ warning is especially sharp because condemnation often masquerades as zeal for righteousness. A person can be correct about the wrongness of an act and still be wrong in spirit—proud, cruel, careless, or hypocritical in the way they judge it.

## Moral judgment still matters

A parent must judge that abuse is wrong and intervene. A court must judge whether a crime was committed. A church leader may need to confront destructive conduct. A friend may need to say, “What you are doing is harming you and others.”

None of that contradicts Luke 6:37 if it is done with truthfulness, proportion, humility, due process, and a desire for genuine good.

The question is not merely, “Is this behavior wrong?” It is also:

- Am I judging by facts or assumptions?
- Do I apply the same standard to myself?
- Do I want repentance and repair, or do I want to see this person diminished?
- Am I leaving room for God’s knowledge, mercy, and final judgment?
- Is my response aimed at protection and restoration, not revenge?

## Forgiveness does not mean denial or lack of boundaries

“Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven” does not require pretending evil did not happen. Forgiveness is the refusal to nourish vengeance or to make another’s debt the center of one’s life. It can coexist with naming wrongdoing, seeking justice, requiring change, and maintaining boundaries.

One may forgive an abuser while still reporting the abuse, protecting victims, and refusing reconciliation without repentance and safety. Forgiveness relinquishes personal vengeance; it does not abolish moral truth or public responsibility.

In short: moral discernment says, **“This is wrong, and it must be faced.”** The appetite to condemn says, **“You are worthless, and I am entitled to destroy you.”** Jesus forbids the latter while calling his followers to the former in the spirit of the merciful Father.
