Amos 5:21–24 is a powerful indictment of religious hypocrisy and moral corruption in Israel’s worship, exposing a deep spiritual fraud where outward devotion masks inward corruption. The passage rejects worship not because it is inherently wrong but because it has been **perverted into a system of empty ritualism, social status, and self-righteousness**—a worship that does not reflect true repentance, justice, or devotion to God. Here’s what is being exposed:

### **1. Ritualism Over Repentance**
- The Israelites were performing religious duties (feast days, solemn assemblies, burnt offerings, meat offerings, and peace offerings) but were **not genuinely turning from their sins** (Amos 5:21–22).
- Their worship was **performative**, not transformative. They were going through the motions of religion while continuing in injustice, greed, and oppression (cf. Amos 5:11–12, where he condemns those who exploit the poor).
- God’s rejection of their offerings is not because He doesn’t want sacrifices (as some might think), but because **true worship requires a broken heart, not just external compliance**.

### **2. Social and Economic Fraud**
- The passage critiques a system where worship was **used to justify oppression** (e.g., taxing the poor, exploiting labor, and ignoring justice).
- The "noise of songs" and "melody of viols" (Amos 5:23) symbolize **empty, self-indulgent praise**—worship that does not accompany righteous action.
- The "fat beasts" (peace offerings) represent **self-serving piety**, where people offer what they can afford to appear pious while maintaining their privilege.

### **3. A Worship That Is Not for God’s Glory**
- God does not want **sacrifices from the hands of the oppressed** (Leviticus 22:29; Deuteronomy 16:16–17). If worship is tied to exploitation, it is **not worship at all—it is a cover for sin**.
- The passage calls for **judgment and righteousness to flow like rivers**, meaning that true worship must be **rooted in justice, mercy, and a commitment to righting wrongs** (cf. Amos 5:24, where "righteousness" is not just moral uprightness but active, life-giving justice).

### **4. The Moral Fraud: Worship as a Shield for Sin**
- The Israelites were likely **using religion to avoid accountability**—claiming they were doing what God commanded while continuing in injustice.
- God’s response is **not to reject worship entirely but to expose the fraud**: If worship is not accompanied by justice, it is **useless and even hypocritical**.
- The contrast between their empty rituals and God’s demand for **righteousness as a mighty stream** shows that true worship must **flow from a heart that has been transformed by God’s grace**.

### **Conclusion: A Call to Authentic Repentance**
Amos is not rejecting worship per se but **demanding that worship be genuine and aligned with justice**. The moral fraud here is that people were **worshipping God while continuing to oppress the poor, ignore the marginalized, and live in self-righteousness**. The passage calls for:
- **A broken heart** (not just a religious posture).
- **Justice for the oppressed** (not just moral lectures).
- **A worship that changes lives** (not just a performance).

In short, the passage exposes **a system where religion was used to justify sin**, and it demands that worship be **rooted in repentance, righteousness, and a commitment to God’s justice**.
