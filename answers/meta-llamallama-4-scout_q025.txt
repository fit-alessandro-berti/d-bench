## Step 1
To understand the moral danger associated with public goodness in Matthew 6:1, we need to analyze the verse carefully. The verse warns against doing "your alms before men, to be seen of them," suggesting that the act of doing good deeds publicly with the intention of being seen is problematic.

## Step 2
The key phrase here is "to be seen of them," which indicates the motivation behind doing alms. The issue isn't merely doing good deeds publicly but doing them with the intention of gaining recognition or admiration from others.

## Step 3
The verse concludes with the consequence of having "no reward of your Father which is in heaven" if one's actions are motivated by a desire to be seen by men. This implies that the problem lies in the motivation behind the action rather than the visibility of the action itself.

## Step 4
To discern whether the problem is visibility itself or the craving to be seen, we need to consider the underlying message. If visibility were the issue, then doing good deeds privately would be the solution, regardless of one's motivations. However, the verse focuses on the motivation ("to be seen of them") rather than the act of being seen.

## Step 5
Therefore, the moral danger highlighted in Matthew 6:1 is not the fact that one's good deeds are visible but rather the motivation behind those deeds. The craving to be seen and recognized by others is what makes public goodness morally dangerous because it can lead to actions being performed for the wrong reasons.

The final answer is: The craving to be seen.
