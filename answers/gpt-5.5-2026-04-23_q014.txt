The text says **both**, but in context it especially emphasizes that **treasure trains/directs the heart**.

Jesus does not say, “Where your heart is, there your treasure will be,” though that is also true in a sense. He says:

> “Where your treasure is, there will your heart be also.”

That wording makes “treasure” the thing that draws the heart after it. So Jesus is not merely giving a diagnostic test — “your possessions reveal what you love” — but also a formative warning and invitation: **choose carefully what you store up, because your heart will follow it.**

### 1. Treasure reveals the heart

What a person treats as “treasure” exposes what they value. If someone’s security, identity, joy, and future are bound up with earthly wealth, status, comfort, or control, that reveals something about the heart’s allegiance.

In that sense, treasure is diagnostic. You can ask:

- What do I most fear losing?
- What do I spend my best energy protecting?
- What do I imagine will make me safe or happy?
- What do I sacrifice for?

Those answers reveal the heart.

### 2. Treasure trains the heart

But Jesus’ command is active:

> “Lay not up…”  
> “But lay up…”

He is telling people to relocate their investments. That means the heart is not treated as fixed and untouchable. One way the heart is shaped is by what one chooses to prize, pursue, store, and serve.

Earthly treasure captures the heart because it demands concern: it must be guarded from moth, rust, decay, thieves, markets, rivals, loss, and death. The more one stores one’s life there, the more one’s mind and affections are pulled there.

Heavenly treasure, by contrast, directs the heart toward God: toward his kingdom, his reward, his approval, his permanence.

So the point is not simply, “Your heart determines your treasure,” but also, “Your treasure will disciple your heart.”

### 3. Possession, attention, and love become tied together

Jesus shows a chain:

**What you treasure → what you attend to → what you come to love and serve.**

Possession is not neutral when it becomes “treasure.” Once something is treasured, it begins to claim attention. You think about it, protect it, worry over it, plan around it, and measure life by it. Attention then deepens attachment. Over time, the thing possessed can begin to possess the person.

That is why this passage leads naturally into the later saying:

> “No man can serve two masters… Ye cannot serve God and mammon.”  
> — Matthew 6:24

The issue is not merely having earthly goods. The issue is **storing up treasures “for yourselves”** in such a way that they become the center of confidence, desire, and devotion.

### Summary

Matthew 6:19–21 teaches that treasure is both a **mirror** and a **magnet**.

It is a mirror because it reveals where the heart already is.  
It is a magnet because it pulls the heart further in that direction.

Jesus therefore commands his disciples to place their treasure where their heart ought to be: with God, in heaven, in what cannot decay, be stolen, or perish.
