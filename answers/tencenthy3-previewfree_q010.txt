This passage from Philippians 2 is often considered one of the most profound theological texts in the New Testament, describing the "Kenosis" (self-emptying) of Christ. To answer your questions, we have to look at the specific Greek words used and the psychological impact of this specific type of power.

### 1. What kind of power is being praised here?

The power praised in this text is **Kenotic Power** (Power through self-giving) juxtaposed against **Ontological Greatness** (Inherent Divine Status).

*   **The Power of *Being* vs. *Doing*:** The text begins by acknowledging Jesus was "in the form of God" (*morphe*). This implies that his divinity wasn't something he possessed as a tool, but something he *was* inherently. The power being praised is not the power to *dominate* reality, but the power to *sustain* one's identity while letting go of privilege.
*   **The Power of Voluntary Limitation:** The text says he "made himself of no reputation" (literally: *he emptied himself*). This praises a power that is secure enough to restrict itself. Most power is praised for its expansion (conquering land, accumulating wealth). Here, the power is praised for its **retraction**. It is the power of a King who has the right to rule, yet chooses to wash feet.
*   **The Power of Obedience:** The passage culminates in "became obedient unto death." In the ancient world (and often today), obedience is seen as the opposite of power; it is submission. Here, obedience is reframed as the ultimate act of strength. It suggests that true power is not the ability to do whatever you want (arbitrary will), but the ability to align your will with what is Good and True, even when it costs you your life.

**Summary:** It is a **relinquishing power**. It is the praise of a strength so massive that it does not need to be loud, threatening, or visible to exist.

### 2. Why can humility joined to greatness feel more unsettling than open domination?

You have identified a deep psychological truth. People generally prefer "open domination" (tyrants, bullies, clear hierarchies) to "humble greatness" because domination is predictable, manageable, and plays by the rules of the ego.

Here is why humble greatness feels more unsettling:

**A. It exposes our own lack of generosity (The Mirror Effect)**
Open domination invites us to be defensive, angry, or resentful. We know how to handle a bully; we fight or flee. However, humility joined to greatness disarms us. When someone with the capacity to crush you instead serves you, it holds up a mirror to our own selfishness. It creates a "moral debt" that we cannot repay. It forces us to ask: *“If he has all that power and is this humble, what does that say about how I use my limited power?”* That is a terrifying question for the ego.

**B. It destroys the pretext for our own pride**
In a hierarchy of domination, we can comfort ourselves with the thought, *"If I had that kind of power, I would be just as corrupt."* We use the oppression of the powerful as an excuse for our own mediocrity. But a leader who is humble and servant-hearted removes that excuse. They prove that greatness and goodness can coexist, which means our failure to be good isn't due to a lack of resources, but a lack of character.

**C. It threatens the logic of the world (The Loss of Control)**
We live in a transactional world: *If I have power, I get comfort.* When Jesus, who has the ultimate power, chooses suffering and service instead of comfort, he breaks the math of the universe.
*   **Domination** is a game we understand; we know the rules (might makes right).
*   **Humble Greatness** is uncharted territory. It suggests that the measure of a person isn't how many people serve them, but how many people they serve. This turns the social order upside down and leaves us feeling untethered.

**D. The vulnerability of the "Strong"**
There is something viscerally unsettling about a being who *could* call down legions of angels but chooses the "death of the cross." Open domination is a wall; it is closed off. Humility joined to greatness is an open hand. It is vulnerable. We find vulnerability unsettling in the powerful because it forces us to drop our own defenses. We cannot attack a person who has already laid down their weapons; we can only join them or walk away in shame.

In short, **open domination confirms our cynicism**, while **humble greatness challenges our conversion**. It is easier to fight a tyrant than it is to let a servant-king change your heart.
