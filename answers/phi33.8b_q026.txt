The passage from Matthew 1seline 12:34-35 draws a clear line between internal character and the external expressions arising from it, suggesting that speech is indeed a symptom of the heart's condition, which is shaped by choices and moral inclinations. According to this perspective, our spoken words are reflections of the riches stored in our hearts, the result of our choices, and the expressions of our inherent goodness or evilness. Here, "the abundance of the heart" refers to one' end of a moral spectrum, whereby only 'good men' will bring forth 'good things', while 'evil men' produce 'evil things.' In essence, the choice each individual makes is the driving force that determines the value and integrity of their speech. Words could be seen as revelations of one's innermost thoughts and motivations, acting simultaneously as a symptom of our moral state, a consequence of our moral decisions or lack thereof. However, the passage doesn't explicitly categorize words as exclusively either one of these; they could potentially serve as all three—symptoms, outcomes, and revelations of our chosen path. As for moral stockpiles, it seems to imply that if one’s heart is full of goodness, all that might manifest, including words, will inevitably echo this goodness. Conversely, if the heart is filled with malevolence, the words, too, will betray this by being harmful or wicked in nature. Ultimately, the passage suggests that our inner character significantly influences our words, and we should be mindful of the content and tone of our speech.
