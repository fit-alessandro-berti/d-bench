This passage from Matthew presents a profound psychological and spiritual principle: **the mouth is the overflow valve of the heart.** Jesus uses vivid metaphors to explain that human speech is not random or isolated, but is inextricably linked to a hidden, accumulated moral reality. 

Here is a detailed analysis of how the passage connects speech to these "moral stockpiles," followed by an examination of whether words function as symptoms, choices, revelations, or all three.

---

### Part 1: The Connection Between Speech and Moral Stockpiles

The passage relies on two primary metaphors to explain this connection: **Abundance** and **Treasure**. 

**1. The "Abundance" (The Hydraulic Metaphor)**
When Jesus says, "out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh," He uses a hydraulic or organic metaphor. The Greek word for "abundance" (*perisseuma*) implies an overflow, a surplus, or that which exceeds a boundary. This suggests that the heart is a container or a wellspring. When the internal reservoir reaches capacity, it naturally spills over into the external world through speech. You cannot reliably draw good water from a poisoned well, just as an evil nature cannot sustainably produce genuinely good speech.

**2. The "Treasure" (The Storage Metaphor)**
In verse 35, Jesus reinforces this by calling the heart’s contents a "treasure" (*thesauros*, from which we get "thesaurus" or "treasury"). A treasure is not accidental; it is a *stockpile* of accumulated value. This word choice implies that a person’s moral character is built over time. Every thought, desire, and action is a coin placed into this internal vault. Eventually, when a person speaks, they are not inventing something new; they are simply "bringing forth" (withdrawing and spending) what has been stored up inside.

Therefore, the connection is one of **inevitable cause and effect**. The hidden moral stockpile is the source, and speech is the guaranteed, physical manifestation of that source.

---

### Part 2: Are Words Symptoms, Choices, Revelations, or All Three?

Based on the mechanics of this passage, words function as **all three**, operating in a deeply interconnected cycle. 

#### 1. Words as Symptoms (The Diagnostic Function)
A symptom is an outward indicator of an underlying, often hidden, condition. Just as a fever is a symptom of an infection, corrupt speech is a symptom of a corrupt heart. Jesus begins by asking, "How can ye, *being* evil, speak good things?" He is diagnosing the Pharisees. Their vicious, accusatory words are not merely bad manners; they are clinical symptoms of a terminal spiritual pathology ("a generation of vipers"). The speech proves the disease.

#### 2. Words as Revelations (The Unveiling Function)
The heart is, by definition, hidden from public view. No one can directly see another person's motives, desires, or "treasure." Speech, however, acts as a revelatory mechanism. It pulls back the curtain on the interior life. When a person speaks, they are involuntarily (or voluntarily) broadcasting the contents of their internal treasury to the outside world. The mouth *reveals* what the heart has been hiding. 

#### 3. Words as Choices (The Active Function)
While the passage emphasizes the *inevitability* of the overflow ("bringeth forth"), it does not absolve the speaker of moral responsibility. The verbs imply active participation. A person "brings forth" good or evil. Furthermore, the metaphor of a "treasure" implies that the heart was stocked through prior choices. We curate our hearts by what we love, dwell on, and practice. Therefore, when we speak, we are making an active choice to express the character we have cultivated. We are held accountable for our words precisely because they are the chosen output of our cultivated nature.

---

### Synthesis: The Feedback Loop of "All Three"

The brilliance of this passage is that it does not separate these three concepts; it weaves them into a single moral feedback loop:

1. **Choices** build the treasure (we choose what to love and dwell upon, stocking the heart).
2. The stocked heart inevitably manifests as **Symptoms** (our true nature bubbles up under pressure).
3. This manifestation serves as a **Revelation** to the world (and to ourselves) of what we truly are.

**Conclusion**
Matthew 12:34-35 suggests that trying to police one's speech without addressing the heart is a futile exercise. You cannot cure the symptom (evil speech) without treating the disease (the evil treasure). Conversely, if one desires to speak good things, the passage dictates that the work must begin inwardly: one must begin stocking the heart with a new, good treasure, from which good speech will naturally, inevitably overflow.
