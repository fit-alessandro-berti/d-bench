Let's break down Luke 18:9-14 to understand what condemns the Pharisee and validates the tax collector.

### What exactly condemns the Pharisee here?

The Pharisee is condemned by a combination of factors, with **pride** and **contempt** being the most prominent and driving forces behind his downfall. While the other options play a role, they are largely manifestations of these core issues.

*   **Pride:** This is the overarching sin. The Pharisee "trusted in themselves that they were righteous." His prayer is a testament to this self-sufficiency and inflated sense of importance before God. He views himself as inherently better than others.
*   **Contempt (Despising Others):** This is explicitly stated in verse 9 ("and despised others"). His prayer directly expresses this contempt for "other men," particularly singling out the publican. This attitude is antithetical to the spirit of God's kingdom.
*   **Comparison:** While not the *root* condemnation, the Pharisee *uses* comparison as a tool to elevate himself. He compares his supposed righteousness to the perceived sins of others, particularly the publican, to highlight his own virtue. This comparison is fueled by his pride and contempt.
*   **Use of Virtue as Self-Display:** The Pharisee lists his virtuous deeds – fasting twice a week and tithing. However, his *motive* for listing them is not genuine gratitude or a desire to honor God in humility, but rather to *display* his righteousness to God (and implicitly, to himself and anyone else listening). It's a performance designed to earn God's favor through his own merits, rather than relying on God's grace.
*   **False Doctrine (Less direct, but implied):** While not a formal theological error in his stated practices (fasting and tithing were legitimate), his understanding of righteousness is flawed. He believes righteousness is achieved through meticulous outward observance and self-effort, rather than through humble repentance and reliance on God's mercy. His doctrine essentially bypasses the need for God's grace by focusing on his own achievement.

**In essence, the Pharisee's condemnation stems from his heart attitude: an arrogant self-righteousness that leads him to disdain his fellow man and believe he can earn God's favor through his own merit.**

### Why is the tax collector’s posture treated as spiritually truer?

The tax collector's posture is treated as spiritually truer because it reflects a **humble, penitent, and grace-dependent heart**.

*   **Humility and Awareness of Sin:** His physical posture – standing "afar off," not daring to lift his eyes, and beating his breast – are outward expressions of deep inner humility and a profound awareness of his sinfulness. He doesn't try to hide or mask his sin; he acknowledges it.
*   **Repentance:** "God be merciful to me a sinner" is a prayer of pure repentance. He isn't asking for recognition of his good deeds (he has none to offer in comparison), but for God's mercy, recognizing his desperate need for it.
*   **Reliance on God's Mercy:** Unlike the Pharisee who relies on his own actions, the tax collector relies entirely on God's unmerited favor. He understands that his only hope is in God's compassion, not in his own worthiness.
*   **Absence of Pride and Contempt:** His prayer is inward-focused, not comparative. He doesn't mention anyone else or any specific good deeds he has done. His sole focus is his own brokenness and need for God.
*   **Authenticity:** His posture and prayer are raw and unpretentious. There is no performance, no self-display. It is the genuine cry of a broken spirit before a holy God.

**The tax collector's posture is spiritually truer because it embodies the essence of true worship and a right relationship with God: recognizing one's own sinfulness and utterly depending on God's boundless mercy and grace.** Jesus contrasts the Pharisee's self-declared righteousness with the tax collector's acknowledged sinfulness, showing that God justifies (declares righteous) those who come to Him with a contrite heart, not those who believe they have already arrived.
