Luke 6:37 is not a blanket claim that moral judgments are irrelevant. It’s a warning against a particular *posture*: the eager, self-exalting impulse to sit in God’s seat and pronounce people “guilty” in a way that hardens the heart and refuses mercy.

A helpful way to see the difference is to separate **discernment** (moral clarity for the sake of truth and love) from **condemnation** (a verdict spirit that aims to disgrace, dismiss, or destroy).

## 1) What Jesus forbids: the appetite to condemn
In Luke 6:37 Jesus pairs terms:

- **“Judge not …”** (κρίνω / *krinō*): can mean to evaluate, but often carries the sense of *passing sentence*—rendering a decisive verdict about someone.
- **“Condemn not …”** (καταδικάζω / *katadikazō*): more explicit—declaring someone worthy of punishment, writing them off.

So the target is not “never think moral thoughts,” but “don’t adopt the role of final arbiter and executioner.”

This fits the immediate context (Luke 6:41–42): the person with a “log” in his own eye focuses on a “speck” in another. The problem isn’t noticing the speck; it’s the **hypocritical, punitive stance**—correction without self-scrutiny, truth without humility, standards without mercy.

## 2) What Jesus does *not* forbid: moral discernment
Elsewhere Jesus explicitly calls for **right judgment**:

- **John 7:24**: “Do not judge by appearances, but judge with right judgment.”
- **Matthew 7:15–20**: discern false prophets by their fruit (that requires moral evaluation).
- **Matthew 18:15–17**: a process for confronting wrongdoing aimed at repentance and restoration.
- **1 Corinthians 5**: church discipline in serious cases (a form of communal moral discernment), but even there the goal is ultimately redemptive, not gleefully punitive.

If “judge not” meant “moral judgments never matter,” these commands would collapse.

## 3) A practical distinction: evaluate actions vs. damn persons
A clean way to phrase it:

- **Discernment says:** “This action/teaching is wrong or harmful; it needs to stop; you’re called to something better.”
- **Condemnation says:** “You are the kind of person who’s beyond hope; you deserve contempt; I’m finished with you.”

Discernment focuses on truth and repair. Condemnation aims at a **final verdict**—often mixed with disgust, superiority, or revenge.

Only God can finally judge the heart and destiny of a person (cf. **James 4:11–12**). Humans can and must assess behaviors, teachings, and patterns—especially to protect others—but we don’t possess the omniscience or moral purity to issue ultimate sentences.

## 4) The “telos” (goal) test: restoration vs. humiliation
One of the best litmus tests is the goal:

- **Discernment (love-governed):** seeks repentance, healing, protection of the vulnerable, and reconciliation where possible (cf. **Galatians 6:1**: “restore… in a spirit of gentleness”).
- **Condemnation (ego-governed):** seeks to shame, exclude, “win,” or feel superior.

This is why Luke 6:37 ends with **“forgive”**. Jesus is not erasing moral categories; he’s training his followers away from the reflex to punish and toward a mercy-shaped response that mirrors God’s.

## 5) The reciprocity principle: the measure you use comes back
The following verses (Luke 6:38) explain the logic: the “measure” you use will be measured back to you. That’s not “anything goes.” It’s: if you live by a harsh, merciless, condemnatory measure—especially while ignoring your own sin—you should not be surprised when you meet the same.

In other words, Jesus is attacking **merciless moralism**, not moral truth.

## Summary
Luke 6:37 differs from “moral judgments never matter” in that it condemns **the spirit and posture of condemnation**—self-righteous, final, unforgiving verdicts—while leaving room (and elsewhere commanding) **discernment**: truthful moral evaluation aimed at repentance, protection, and restoration.
