Isaiah’s sequence is deliberate. The prophet moves from purification and stopping evil to an exhortation to “learn to do well” because God wants more than the absence of sin; he wants the presence of justice and mercy.

A few points that help explain the move:

- The grammar and context. The verbs start with inward or corrective acts (“wash…make clean…put away…cease to do evil”) and then shift to an imperative that presumes formation: “learn” (Heb. lamad). Learning implies skill, habit, instruction — it isn’t automatic once you stop sinning.

- The prophetic critique. Earlier and throughout Isaiah the complaint is ritual without righteousness: sacrifices and cleansings mean nothing if social injustice continues. So stopping evil is a necessary first step, but the text insists on positive, public action as the true sign of repentance.

- The content defines “good.” The command to “learn to do well” is immediately unpacked: “seek justice, relieve the oppressed, judge the fatherless, plead for the widow.” “Good” here isn’t private piety or mere abstention; it’s practiced love expressed in concrete justice for the vulnerable.

- Moral formation. The text assumes humans need training and habituation in goodness. Classical theologians (e.g., Augustine, Aquinas) and biblical wisdom both treat virtue as something formed by practice, not just the elimination of vice.

So Isaiah suggests goodness is both restraint and practice. Ceasing to do evil is necessary but incomplete; genuine goodness is a learned, habitual, outwardly active love and justice toward others.
