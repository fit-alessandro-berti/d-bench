In Matthew 6:1, the moral danger highlighted in the verse pertains more to the intention behind the act rather than the visibility of the act itself. The verse warns against performing good deeds, such as giving alms, with the primary intention of being seen and praised by others. The problem is not necessarily the public nature of the act, but rather the underlying motivation to seek recognition or approval from people, rather than focusing on the sincere and genuine desire to do good as an act of faith and service to God.

The danger lies in the craving to be seen, as this shifts the focus from selflessly helping others and honoring God, to selfishly seeking personal glory and validation. This craving diminishes the spiritual value of the action, rendering it a mere performance aimed at gaining social recognition, and thereby forfeiting any divine reward or spiritual growth. The verse encourages sincerity and humility, emphasizing that true righteousness and fulfillment come from seeking God's approval, rather than human praise.
