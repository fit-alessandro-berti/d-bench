The trust being asked for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is a deep, heartfelt reliance on God that encompasses faith, humility, and recognition of His guidance. This trust is not about abandoning reason or intellectual inquiry; rather, it is an acknowledgment that human understanding is limited and prone to error compared to divine wisdom. 

### Humble Self-Distrust
Humble self-distrust involves recognizing our limitations and the fallibility of our own judgment. It means understanding that despite our best efforts, there are complexities and uncertainties in life that are beyond our comprehension. This kind of trust leads us to seek God’s will and guidance in decision-making, acknowledging that we often do not have the full picture. It encourages a posture of openness and willingness to submit to divine wisdom, even when it conflicts with our own perceptions or desires.

### Anti-Intellectualism
On the other hand, mere anti-intellectualism dismisses the value of reason, intellect, and critical thinking. It promotes a rejection of knowledge and understanding in favor of blind faith or emotional belief. This stance can lead to a failure to engage with the world responsibly and can discourage questioning or exploring tough issues. In contrast to humble self-distrust, it does not embrace the idea that we can still seek wisdom and knowledge while also recognizing our limitations; instead, it outright disvalues intellectual pursuits.

### Summary
The trust called for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is not a call for anti-intellectualism, but rather an invitation to a profound humility that values both faith in God and an acknowledgment of our own limitations. It encourages believers to seek understanding and wisdom through God, encouraging a journey of faith that does not shy away from engaging with the complexities of life and knowledge, all the while trusting that God will lead and direct their paths.
