In Genesis 4:6–7, the picture of moral agency being drawn is one of **robust personal responsibility coupled with a highly vivid, externalized view of evil.** The text presents moral agency not as a passive state of being, but as an active, executive role—specifically, the role of a gatekeeper or a ruler. 

To answer your question directly: evil is presented here as a **predatory beast (something else)** that is fueled by an internal **impulse**, acting as an external **temptation**. 

Here is a breakdown of how the text constructs this picture and the responsibility it places on the individual.

### 1. The Nature of Evil: A Predatory Beast
The most striking feature of this verse is the Hebrew word translated as "lieth" (*robets*). This word is rarely used to describe the posture of humans; it is almost exclusively used to describe animals, particularly wild predators or birds of prey crouching in the tall grass, waiting to pounce. 

Therefore, evil (sin) is not presented merely as an abstract concept, a bad habit, or a theological deficit. It is personified and animalized as a stalking beast. It is a living, hostile entity lurking just outside the threshold of Cain's life ("at the door"). 

### 2. The Relationship between Impulse and Temptation
While sin is depicted as an external beast, it is clearly inextricably linked to Cain’s internal state. God asks, "Why art thou wroth?" Cain's *impulse*—his anger, jealousy, and wounded pride—has drawn the predator to his door. 

In this picture, impulse and temptation are linked in a cause-and-effect relationship. The unchecked internal impulse (anger) acts as bait or blood in the water that attracts the external temptation (the crouching beast). Sin is looking for an entry point, and Cain's emotional state has provided it.

### 3. The Picture of Moral Agency: The Doorkeeper and the Ruler
The text places Cain in a position of distinct authority. Notice the spatial metaphor: Cain is inside; sin is outside at the door. Cain possesses the door. He is the gatekeeper. 

Furthermore, God tells Cain, "And unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him." 
*   The Hebrew word for "desire" (*teshuqah*) is only used three times in the Old Testament. Here, it means a desire to dominate, control, or consume (just as it is used in Genesis 3:16 to describe the dynamic between a husband and wife in a fallen world). 
*   The Hebrew word for "rule" (*mashal*) means to have dominion, to reign, or to govern.

Therefore, the picture of moral agency is one of **sovereignty**. Cain is told that the beast wants to overpower him, drag him out of the house, and take dominion over him—but he has the capacity and the mandate to rule over it instead. 

### 4. The Responsibility Placed on the Person
The text places total, inescapable responsibility on Cain. It dismantles any attempt at determinism or victimhood:
*   **It presumes capability:** God says, "If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?" This implies that Cain knows what "doing well" looks like and possesses the current physical and moral capacity to do it. He is not helpless.
*   **It presumes foreknowledge:** God warns him of the consequence. Cain cannot claim ignorance. He is told exactly what is happening (a beast is at the door) and what will happen if he fails to act (the beast will attempt to rule him).
*   **It rejects passivity:** The onus is entirely on Cain to exercise his agency. God does not say, "I will slay the beast for you," nor does He say, "Let me come inside and protect you." Cain must rule over it.

### Conclusion
In Genesis 4:6–7, moral agency is depicted as the heavy but necessary task of mastering one's own inner world so as not to be devoured by an external evil. Evil is presented as a predatory, opportunistic beast that uses human impulses as its hunting ground. Yet, the text radically affirms human responsibility: the person is not a passive victim of their emotions or of sin. The person is the rightful ruler who, by choosing to "do well," possesses the authority to keep the beast crouching at the door. 

*(Tragically, as the very next verse reveals, Cain fails to exercise this agency. He opens the door, fails to rule over the beast, and becomes the first human to be completely consumed by it.)*
