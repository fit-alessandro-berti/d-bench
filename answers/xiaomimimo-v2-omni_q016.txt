The elder brother’s deepest problem appears to be **a combination of wounded fairness, pride in obedience, and ultimately, a fundamental misunderstanding of grace and mercy**.  

His complaint — *“you never gave me even a goat to celebrate with my friends”* — shows he sees his relationship with his father as transactional. He believes his obedience merits reward, and he resents that the prodigal son receives grace without earning it.  

But the father’s reply — *“all that is mine is yours”* — reveals the elder brother’s real lack: he never truly understood his own sonship or the father’s generosity. He lived in the household but didn’t share the father’s heart.  

Thus, while anger, envy, wounded pride, and discomfort with mercy are all present, they spring from **a deeper failure to comprehend unconditional love and belonging**. He thought service could earn favor, rather than recognizing that sonship itself was a gift. In this, he mirrors the Pharisees’ mindset that Jesus often criticized — one that values rule-keeping over relationship and resents the inclusion of those deemed undeserving.
