Short answer: The danger is not visibility itself but the desire to be seen.

Explanation:
- The verse’s key phrase “to be seen by them” (Greek: pros to theathēnai autois) states purpose. Jesus targets the motive—doing good as a performance for human applause.
- This fits the context (6:2, 6:5, 6:16): “they have received their reward.” Human praise replaces God as the audience and the reward.
- It does not ban public deeds. Just prior, Jesus commands, “Let your light shine before others” (Matt 5:16), provided the result is that people “glorify your Father,” not you.
- “Hypocrites” (actors) signals the moral danger: piety becomes self-display, the needy become props, and the heart is trained to love reputation over God.
- Many manuscripts read “righteousness” in 6:1, widening the principle beyond almsgiving to any act of devotion.

Practical takeaway:
- Prefer secrecy to train the heart away from self-congratulation (“let not your left hand know…”).
- When visibility is unavoidable, aim at God’s honor and others’ good, not your image; receive or deflect praise in a way that keeps God as the audience.
