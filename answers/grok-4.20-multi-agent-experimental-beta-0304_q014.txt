**The text indicates that treasure *trains* (or directs) the heart, though it can also serve as a diagnostic that reveals it.**

The key clause is “For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also” (Greek: *hopou gar estin ho thēsauros sou, ekei estai kai hē kardia sou*). The future indicative “will be” (*estai*) presents a general principle of consequence or direction: your heart follows and settles where you have placed your treasure. Jesus is not primarily saying “your heart is already there, so we can inspect your treasure to diagnose it,” but “put your treasure in the right place *so that* your heart ends up there.”

This fits the imperative structure of the passage: “Lay not up for yourselves treasures upon earth… But lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven…” (vv. 19–20). The command to *act* with your possessions is the means by which you reorient your heart. The logic runs treasure → heart more than heart → treasure, even though the two are tightly coupled in practice. Where you invest, store, or value something becomes the magnet that pulls your inner life.

### How Possession, Attention, and Love Tie Together
- **Possession/treasure** (*thēsauros*) refers to what you actively store up, invest in, protect, and count as valuable—material goods, money, status, security, or instead the kingdom, people, obedience, and God’s approval. It is not merely “things you own” but what you treat as your lasting wealth.
- **Heart** (*kardia*) in biblical language is the center of the person: the seat of affections, desires, loyalties, attention, and will. It is not just emotion but the directional core of life—what you love, fixate on, worry about, and serve (see the immediate context: anxiety in vv. 25–34 and “you cannot serve God and money” in v. 24).
- **The linkage**: What you treasure captures and trains your attention and affections. Earthly treasure is vulnerable (moth, rust, thieves), so investing there ties your heart to what is transient, anxiety-producing, and rival to God. Heavenly treasure is secure, so investing there draws your heart into lasting love for God and his kingdom. The heart does not stay neutral or detached; it migrates to what we treat as our treasure. This is why Jesus commands the action: change the investment and the heart follows.

This principle appears elsewhere (e.g., where your time, words, and money go, your heart is being shaped). It is both diagnostic (you can look at your checkbook, calendar, or worries to see where your heart has been) and formative (intentionally “lay up” treasure in heaven—through generosity, kingdom priorities, worship, service—and your heart will increasingly be there). The verse is therefore motivational: choose heavenly treasure because that is where you want your heart to dwell.

In short, Jesus is not merely observing a static fact but giving a practical strategy for discipleship. Direct your possessions, and your love and attention will follow.
