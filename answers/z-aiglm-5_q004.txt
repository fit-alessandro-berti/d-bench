The trust described in Proverbs 3:5–6 is a **covenantal, whole-person reliance** that engages the mind but rejects intellectual autonomy. It is a call to subordinate one’s finite perspective to God’s infinite wisdom, particularly in times of uncertainty.

To answer your question, we must look at what the text actually prohibits. It does not say "do not use your understanding"; it says, "lean not" on it. The distinction between humble self-distrust and anti-intellectualism lies in how one handles the gift of reason.

Here is the breakdown of that distinction:

### 1. Humble Self-Distrust (The Biblical Model)
This is the posture the text demands. It acknowledges that while human reason is a gift from God, it is finite and fallen. It recognizes that human logic is a tool, not a master.

*   **The Metaphor of "Leaning":** The Hebrew word for "lean" (*shaan*) implies putting one’s full weight upon something for support. The command is not to reject understanding, but to refuse to make it the structural support of one's life. You can have understanding, but you must not rest your soul's weight upon it.
*   **Recognition of Finitude:** Humble self-distrust admits that "I see in part, but God sees the whole." It is the honest admission that human intellect cannot comprehensively grasp the moral complexity of the universe or the future. This is not a refusal to think; it is a refusal to be arrogant about one's conclusions.
*   **Proper Use of the Mind:** In this view, the mind is used to gather information, assess situations, and plan, but the final surrender of the will is reserved for God. A person exercises their intellect to the fullest, then says, "Lord, I have done my best to understand, but Your will be done, not mine." This acknowledges God "in all their ways" (v.6), integrating faith into the process of reasoning rather than treating reason as an enemy.

### 2. Mere Anti-Intellectualism (The Misinterpretation)
This is a distortion where faith is pitted against reason. It treats the mind as an enemy of the Spirit, suggesting that thinking is a hindrance to holiness.

*   **The Rejection of the Tool:** Anti-intellectualism interprets "lean not unto thine own understanding" as "do not use your own understanding." It views education, critical thinking, or inquiry as worldly or unspiritual.
*   **Passivity vs. Trust:** Anti-intellectualism often masquerades as faith but is actually passivity. It refuses to do the hard work of decision-making or study, waiting for a "sign" or feeling while ignoring the wisdom God has already provided. It creates a false dichotomy: either you trust God *or* you use your brain.
*   **Disregard for Agency:** This approach ignores that Proverbs itself is a book of wisdom literature requiring intense intellectual engagement to acquire prudence, discretion, and knowledge. To ignore the mind is to ignore the very faculty God gave humans to exercise dominion and discernment. It fails to "acknowledge Him" through the diligent use of the capacities He gave us.

### Summary
The trust asked for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is **active reliance, not passive ignorance.**

*   **Anti-intellectualism** says: "Turn off your brain and just believe."
*   **Humble self-distrust** says: "Turn on your brain to the fullest, but hold your conclusions with an open hand, knowing God’s wisdom supersedes yours."

The "trust" required is one that uses the mind to navigate the path but looks to God to *direct* the path. It is the difference between a navigator who refuses to look at the map (anti-intellectualism) and a navigator who studies the map intently but trusts the Captain’s authority to steer the ship through the storm (humble self-distrust).
