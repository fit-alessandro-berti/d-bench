The elder brother’s deepest problem in the Parable of the Prodigal Son (Luke 15:25–32) is a **composite of spiritual maladies**, but at its root, it is **pride in his own righteousness and a refusal to embrace the father’s mercy**. His issues can be broken down as follows, with the most fundamental problem being his **legalistic self-righteousness and resistance to grace**:

1. **Pride in Obedience (Self-Righteousness)**
   - The elder brother boasts, *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment"* (v. 29). His obedience is not rooted in love for the father but in a transactional mindset—he expects reward for his works. This reveals a **Pharisaical heart** (cf. Luke 18:9–14), where he sees himself as morally superior to his brother and even subtly accuses the father of injustice.

2. **Wounded Fairness (Entitlement)**
   - His complaint (*"thou never gavest me a kid"*) shows he resents the father’s generosity toward the prodigal because he feels **unappreciated and cheated**. His sense of fairness is tied to **meritocracy**, not grace. He cannot rejoice in mercy because he measures life by what he "deserves," not by the father’s love.

3. **Envy and Anger**
   - His anger (*"he was angry, and would not go in"*) stems from envy—he cannot stand that the prodigal, who "wasted" the father’s wealth, receives a lavish welcome. His envy is a symptom of his **insecurity**: if the father’s love is not earned, then his own standing is not as special as he thought.

4. **Discomfort with Mercy Itself**
   - Most critically, he **rejects the father’s character**. The father’s response (*"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine"*) reveals that the elder brother has always had access to the father’s love and resources—but he preferred **slavery to sonship**. His refusal to celebrate the prodigal’s return shows he **does not share the father’s heart for the lost**. Mercy offends him because it exposes his own **lack of love** and **spiritual poverty**.

### The Core Issue: **Rejection of Grace**
The elder brother’s problem is not just anger or envy—it is that he has **built his identity on his own righteousness** rather than the father’s love. He is the true "lost son" in the parable because, despite his outward obedience, he is **far from the father’s heart**. His pride blinds him to the truth that **everything the father has is already his** (v. 31), and that the father’s joy is not in performance but in **restored relationship**.

Jesus tells this parable to the Pharisees (Luke 15:2), who, like the elder brother, were scandalized by His mercy toward sinners. The elder brother’s attitude mirrors **religious legalism**, which resents grace because it cannot control or earn it. His deepest problem is that he **does not understand—or want—the father’s love on the father’s terms**.

### Key Takeaway:
The elder brother’s tragedy is that he **prefers to be a servant rather than a son**—and in doing so, he misses the feast. His story is a warning against **self-righteousness that rejects grace**, which is the ultimate barrier to entering the kingdom of God (cf. Matthew 21:28–32).
