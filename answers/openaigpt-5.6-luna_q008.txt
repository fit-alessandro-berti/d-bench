The verse is not saying that God and one’s neighbor are the same object of love. It is saying that love for the unseen God necessarily becomes visible in how one treats people whom God has placed before us.

Several ideas are involved:

1. **Love for God is not merely a claim or an inward feeling.**  
   God is invisible, so someone can make a profession of loving him without that claim being immediately verifiable. But love for a brother or neighbor takes concrete forms—patience, mercy, forgiveness, generosity, and a refusal to seek another’s harm. The visible relationship exposes what the invisible claim actually means.

2. **The command to love one’s brother comes from God himself.**  
   John has already said, “This commandment have we from him, That he who loveth God love his brother also” (4:21). Thus hatred of the brother is not just a failure in a secondary area; it is resistance to the God whom the person claims to love. One cannot sincerely embrace God’s will while deliberately rejecting a command so central to it.

3. **The neighbor is the concrete test of an abstract religious claim.**  
   It is easy to love “humanity,” admire the idea of goodness, or speak warmly about God in the abstract. It is harder to love a particular, visible, irritating, needy, or offending person. John exposes the possibility of preferring an invisible God who makes no immediate demands over the visible person whose presence confronts one’s pride and resentment.

4. **God’s love has already become visible.**  
   In John’s theology, the invisible God has made himself known in Jesus Christ, whom the apostles saw and touched (1 John 1:1–3). God’s love is therefore not an unknowable idea; it has appeared in Christ’s self-giving. Those who receive that love are meant to reproduce it toward others.

The self-deception, then, is the attempt to separate **religious devotion from moral and relational reality**: “I love God,” while nursing hatred, contempt, indifference, or a settled refusal to seek a brother’s good. The person may mistake religious language, emotion, orthodoxy, or spiritual experience for love of God. But the visible neighbor reveals whether that profession has actually been transformed by God’s love.

“Hatred” here need not mean constant rage. It can include sustained hostility, contempt, indifference, refusal to forgive, or unwillingness to do good. John is not requiring sentimental affection or denying the need for boundaries in harmful relationships. He is denying that one can genuinely love God while choosing the opposite of love toward those whom God commands one to love.
