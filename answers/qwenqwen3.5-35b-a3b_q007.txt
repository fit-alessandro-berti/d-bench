The common misconception is that Luke 6:37 advocates for **moral relativism**—the idea that right and wrong are subjective and therefore no distinctions can be made. However, the text does not say, "Do not recognize sin." It warns against a specific **posture of the heart**.

To understand the difference between this verse and the idea that "moral judgment never matters," we must distinguish between **Discernment** (the ability to distinguish truth from error) and **Condemnation** (the desire to punish or dismiss based on that distinction).

Here is an exploration of that distinction.

### 1. The Context of "Judge" (Krinō)
In the Greek New Testament, the word translated as "judge" (*krinō*) carries a range of meanings. It can mean to separate, to decide, to evaluate, or to condemn.
*   **Discernment** uses *krinō* to evaluate actions and their effects.
*   **Condemnation** uses *krinō* to pronounce a final, punitive verdict on a person's worthiness.

If Jesus meant that moral judgment never matters, the rest of the verse becomes incoherent. He immediately follows with: *"Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven."* Forgiveness only has meaning if there was a wrong to forgive. If there were no moral distinction, there could be no offense, and therefore no need for forgiveness. The verse assumes a moral order exists; it commands us not to play the role of the ultimate executioner within that order.

### 2. Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn
The distinction lies not in *what* we see, but in *why* we speak and *how* we act.

#### Discernment: "This action is harmful."
Discernment is an objective assessment of reality, often rooted in love and the desire for restoration.
*   **Goal:** Protection of the vulnerable, correction of the errant, preservation of truth.
*   **Tone:** Humble, corrective, restorative.
*   **Scope:** It judges the *deed*, not the eternal *status* of the soul.
*   **Biblical Example:** Paul tells the Corinthians to "judge for themselves what I say" (1 Cor 10:15) and to "test the spirits" (1 John 4:1). These are commands to exercise discernment to protect the community from error.

#### The Appetite to Condemn: "This person is worthless."
Condemnation is a subjective projection of superiority, often rooted in pride, fear, or resentment.
*   **Goal:** To elevate oneself by lowering another, to assert control, to vent anger.
*   **Tone:** Harsh, final, dismissive.
*   **Scope:** It judges the *person*, often ignoring context, struggle, or growth.
*   **Biblical Example:** The Pharisees who condemned the woman caught in adultery. They correctly identified the sin (discernment), but their motivation was to trap Jesus and assert their own moral purity (condemnation). Jesus corrected the behavior but spared the person.

### 3. The "Measure for Measure" Principle
The second half of the verse explains *why* we must not condemn: *"Give, and it shall be given unto you."*

This establishes a cosmic reciprocity. If you hold others to a standard you do not apply to yourself (or if you act as the final arbiter of justice), you invite God to apply that same standard to you.
*   **The Trap of Condemnation:** When we adopt the "appetite to condemn," we implicitly claim authority that belongs solely to God. By taking the throne of the Judge, we set ourselves up for that same scrutiny when God does judge us.
*   **The Freedom of Discernment:** When we practice discernment without condemnation, we acknowledge our limitations. We can say, "This is wrong," without saying, "You are beyond hope."

### 4. The Practical Distinction
To put it into practice, consider how a person applies these concepts in a community setting:

*   **The Condemner:** Sees a friend struggle with addiction. They feel disgust. They tell others about the friend's failure. They conclude, "That person is toxic." The friend is shunned. The Condemner feels righteous and clean.
*   **The Discerner:** Sees a friend struggle with addiction. They feel concern. They speak to the friend in private (Matt 18:15). They recognize the behavior is harmful to the friend's soul. They pray for the friend. They refuse to let the friend continue the destructive behavior, but they hold out the hand of recovery. The Discerner feels a burden of love.

### Conclusion
Luke 6:37 does not negate the existence of good and evil. Rather, it negates **self-righteousness**.

It draws a line between **evaluating actions** (which is necessary for a functioning community) and **passing sentence on souls** (which usurps God's authority). To say moral judgments never matter is to deny the reality of sin; to say "Judge not" is to deny our own right to sit in judgment over that sin with a lack of mercy.

True discipleship requires the clarity of discernment to identify truth, but the humility to withhold the finality of condemnation.
