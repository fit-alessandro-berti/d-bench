Because Paul is trying to show what authentic love (agape/charity) really is by stripping away the common mistakes people make about it. The long list of “not this, not that” does three things at once:

- It corrects mistaken models. In Corinth the church was proud about gifts, status, rhetoric and assertiveness. By saying what love is not (not jealous, not boastful, not puffed up, not self-seeking, not easily provoked, thinks no evil) Paul rejects the idea that strength is domination, competition, suspicion or self-promotion.  
- It defines love’s practical contour by contrast. Negative statements are a careful way to mark boundaries: love will not exploit, seethe, parade itself, or live guarded behind cynicism. That tells you as much about love’s behavior as positive descriptions do.  
- It protects love from being sentimentalized or co-opted. By denying pride and envy, Paul prevents love from becoming a mere badge of virtue or a tool for self-aggrandizement.

What vision of strength is being proposed?

- Strength as self‑mastery and restraint. Real power is the ability to control anger, resist selfish impulses, and refuse retaliation. “Not easily provoked” and “seeketh not her own” point to disciplined will rather than raw force.  
- Strength as courageous vulnerability and trust. “Thinketh no evil,” “believeth all things,” “hopeth all things” portray a confident readiness to trust, hope, and give others the benefit of the doubt—courageous because it risks being hurt.  
- Strength as endurance and sustaining love. “Beareth all things… endureth all things” images a resolute, long‑suffering commitment that carries burdens rather than crushing others with power.  
- Strength rooted in truth and service. The loving person “rejoiceth in the truth,” not in victories over others; strength is used to uphold truth and to serve, not to exalt the self.

In short: Paul proposes a countercultural strength — not domination but steadfast, humble, self‑giving power. Love’s might shows itself in patience, trust, forgiveness, self‑control and an unwavering commitment to the good of others, modeled supremely in Christ’s own sacrificial love.
