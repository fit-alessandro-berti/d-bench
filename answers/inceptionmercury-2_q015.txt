### 1.  Why the picture is so “sharp”

| Element | What it does | Why it sticks in the mind |
|---------|--------------|--------------------------|
| **Concrete visual objects** – *mote* (a speck of dust) and *beam* (a large piece of wood) | Turns an abstract moral claim into a concrete visual scene that anyone can picture. | The brain processes concrete visual details far more vividly than abstract concepts. A tiny mote and a massive beam are opposite extremes; the contrast is instantly grasped. |
| **Scale contrast** – “mote” vs. “beam” | Highlights the absurdity of the speaker’s focus: a trivial flaw in another versus a huge flaw in oneself. | The visual disparity (microscopic vs. macroscopic) creates a mental “pop‑out” effect, making the image jump out of the text. |
| **Second‑person address** – “why beholdest thou…?” | Forces the listener to see himself in the scene, turning the metaphor into a personal mirror. | Direct address eliminates distance; the listener can almost feel the dust on his own eye. |
| **Rhetorical questions** – “And why…? Or how wilt thou say…?” | Invite the audience to answer the question for themselves, turning the passage into a self‑diagnostic test. | The unanswered question leaves a cognitive “gap” that the mind fills by visualising the scene. |
| **Repetition & parallelism** – “mote… beam… mote… beam” | Creates a rhythmic, almost chiasmic structure that reinforces the image. | The parallel structure makes the metaphor easy to recall and recite, anchoring the visual in memory. |
| **Label “hypocrite”** – a sharp, moral‑charge word | Turns the visual metaphor into a moral accusation. | The word carries strong social stigma; combined with the visual, it makes the whole picture emotionally charged. |
| **Imperative climax** – “first cast out the beam… then shalt thou see clearly” | Gives the image a narrative arc: problem → self‑correction → clarity → action. | The progression from “blurred” to “clear” mirrors the physical act of cleaning a lens, a vivid, everyday experience. |

All of these ingredients work together to make the passage a *high‑resolution* mental picture: you can almost see the dust speck, the wooden beam, the act of pulling it out, and the sudden clarity that follows. The metaphor is thus both **visually arresting** and **cognitively efficient**, which is why it has endured for two millennia.

### 2.  How the passage attacks hypocrisy **without** banning moral correction

| Aspect of the text | What it says | How it avoids a blanket prohibition on correction |
|--------------------|--------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| **“Why beholdest thou the mote … but considerest not the beam …?”** | Calls out the *selective* attention of the speaker. | The criticism is *targeted*: it is the *selectivity* (focusing on a tiny flaw while ignoring a huge one) that is condemned, not the act of noticing a flaw at all. |
| **“Or how wilt thou say to thy brother …?”** | Exposes the *hypocritical* tone of offering help while being blind to one’s own defect. | The passage does not say “don’t speak to your brother”; it says “don’t *pretend* to help while you are still blinded by your own fault.” |
| **“Thou hypocrite, first cast out the beam out of thine own eye; and then shalt thou see clearly …”** | Gives a *two‑step* prescription: self‑purification precedes external correction. | The word *first* establishes a *temporal order*. After the beam is removed, the speaker is *allowed*—indeed, *encouraged*—to address the mote in the brother’s eye. |
| **“Thou shalt see clearly to cast out the mote …”** | Links *clarity* (the result of self‑cleaning) to the *ability* to help others. | The passage acknowledges that moral correction is possible and even desirable, but only when the critic’s own vision is unobstructed. |
| **Underlying logic** | **Hypocrisy = “seeing” others’ faults while being blind to one’s own** | The *fault* is not the act of correction but the *state* of the critic. If the critic’s own “eye” is clear, the correction is no longer hypocritical. |

In short, the passage **does not forbid** the act of pointing out a brother’s fault; it merely **conditions** that act on the removal of one’s own, more serious fault. The moral calculus is:

1. **Self‑examination** → remove the *beam* (the dominant, self‑deceptive flaw).  
2. **Resulting clarity** → you can now *see* the *mote* (the minor flaw in another).  
3. **Action** → you may *cast out* the mote, but only *after* you have first cleared your own vision.

Thus the text attacks **hypocrisy** (the inconsistency between self‑deception and moral admonition) while preserving the **right—and duty—of moral correction** once the hypocritical bias has been eliminated. The sharp image of the mote and the beam makes this conditional ethic instantly graspable, memorable, and practically applicable.
