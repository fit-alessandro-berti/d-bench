This is a genuinely rich question, and I think the text rewards looking closely at how it's constructed.

## The Architecture of the Contrast

Notice the movement in these two verses. Verse 31 is almost entirely verbs of *removal* — "put away from you." It's a sweeping list: bitterness, wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, malice. Then verse 32 pivots with a single word: "And be ye..." — now the direction is *toward*, not *away from*. Kindness. Tenderheartedness. Forgiveness.

So structurally, the passage isn't presenting a tidy binary of "bitterness vs. forgiveness." It's presenting a whole *cluster* of destructive postures being swept out, and a whole *cluster* of constructive ones being invited in. Bitterness and forgiveness are placed against each other not because they're exact opposites, but because they represent the general direction of your interior life.

## What Kind of Thing Is Bitterness?

The Greek word here — *pikria* — carries a sense of something sharp, cutting, corrosive. It's the same root behind a word for a bitter taste that lingers. I think that's significant. The text isn't treating bitterness as a feeling that strikes you and passes. It's something that *takes root*. It's a disposition, a lens. It colors how you interpret what people do, what they say, how they look at you. It has a gravitational pull — everything else orbits around it.

This is probably why it appears first in the list. It's not just one emotion among equals. It's a kind of spiritual posture that *generates* the others: wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking. Bitterness is the wellspring; the rest are the overflow. Remove the wellspring, and much of the rest loses its fuel.

## Is It Contagious?

Here's where your question is especially perceptive. The text says "let all bitterness... be put away *from you*." The phrasing suggests bitterness is something that can take hold in a community, not just in a single heart. "Clamor" — public, loud, communal anger — appears in the list. "Evil speaking" — the way people talk about others — is explicitly included.

So yes, I think the text is treating bitterness as something that spreads. One person's unresolved grievance becomes the air everyone breathes. Unaddressed, it becomes the *normative interpretation* of situations within a group. "Of course we're angry — look what was done to us." That kind of posture becomes self-perpetuating. It's contagious in the way that unexamined assumptions are contagious: people absorb the framework before they even realize there's an alternative.

## Why This Matters for the Forgiveness Side

If bitterness is a lens, then "forgiving one another" in verse 32 isn't just an act of the will — it's a kind of *reframing*. And notice the grounds the text gives for this reframing: "even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you." The model is divine mercy received. The posture of forgiveness is then modeled on a prior experience of being forgiven *when you didn't deserve it*.

This reframes forgiveness from a transaction ("I let go of this grudge") into something more like a way of seeing. If you've experienced grace that wasn't earned, you're being invited to extend that same kind of grace to others — not because they merit it, but because that's the lens you now look through.

## So What Is Bitterness, Finally?

I'd say the text treats it as all three things you named — feeling, habit, and chosen posture — but with an emphasis on the last. Feelings come and go. Habits form through repetition. But a *posture* is something you choose to maintain, often without realizing you're choosing. Bitterness becomes the default stance, the lens you put on without thinking. The text is asking you to *notice* that lens, to name it, and then — with deliberate effort, modeled on a grace you yourself received — to set it down.

That's harder than managing an emotion. It's a kind of reorientation. And the passage's movement from "put away" to "be ye kind" suggests it requires both subtraction and replacement. You can't just leave the bitterness behind. You have to walk toward something.
