The movement from **“cease to do evil”** to **“learn to do well”** shows that biblical repentance is not merely stopping bad behavior. It is a turning from evil **and** a formation into active righteousness.

In Isaiah 1, God is rebuking a people who still offer sacrifices and prayers, but whose society is full of injustice, violence, and neglect of the vulnerable. So the command has two sides:

1. **Negative repentance:**  
   “Put away the evil… cease to do evil.”  
   They must stop the practices God hates: corruption, oppression, violence, hypocrisy.

2. **Positive obedience:**  
   “Learn to do well; seek judgment, relieve the oppressed, judge the fatherless, plead for the widow.”  
   They must actively pursue justice, protect the vulnerable, and restore right relationships.

So goodness here is not mere restraint. It is not simply, “Do not harm.” It is **trained, deliberate love expressed through justice**.

The phrase **“learn to do well”** is important. It suggests that doing good is something people must be taught, disciplined in, and habituated to. Sin has malformed them; righteousness must now be practiced. Goodness is not passive innocence but active faithfulness.

Isaiah then defines what “doing well” looks like:

- **Seek judgment/justice** — pursue what is right, not merely avoid what is wrong.  
- **Relieve/correct the oppressed** — intervene on behalf of those being crushed.  
- **Judge the fatherless** — give justice to those without social power.  
- **Plead for the widow** — advocate for those easily ignored or exploited.

In other words, God’s people are called not only to have clean hands, but to use those hands for mercy and justice.

The text teaches that holiness is both separation from evil and devotion to good. True repentance does not end with restraint; it grows into a practiced form of love.
