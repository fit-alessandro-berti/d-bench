**Short answer:**  The text says *treasure reveals the heart* in the indicative sense (where your treasure is, *there* your heart already is), but it implicitly *trains* the heart in the imperative sense because the act of “laying up” treasure is a repeated, voluntary habit that fixes the heart’s orientation.

Below is a breakdown of how the text works, and how possession, attention, and love intertwine.

---

### 1. Reveals vs. Trains: The Logic of “For” (γάρ, *gar*)

The final verse is often read as a proverb: *“Where your treasure is, there your heart will be also.”*  But in Greek, the word γάρ (*gar*, “for”) does not merely introduce a general maxim; it gives the *reason* for the preceding imperative.

- **Imperative (command):** “Do not store up treasures on earth, *but* store up treasures in heaven” (vv. 19–20).
- **Indicative (reason):** “For where your treasure is, there your heart is also” (v. 21).

Read this way, the saying is both **diagnostic** and **directional**:

- **Reveals (diagnostic):** Your heart is *already* wherever your treasure is.  If you are hoarding perishable things, your heart is currently tethered to decay.  The location of your stored value is an X-ray of your affections.
- **Trains (directional):** Because the heart follows treasure, the only way to reorient the heart is to relocate the treasure.  The command to “lay up” heavenly treasure is a spiritual technology: change the deposit, and the heart’s gravitational center shifts.

So the text *primarily* reveals the heart, but it does so by prescribing a new practice that *retrains* the heart.  The indicative serves the imperative.

---

### 2. Possession, Attention, and Love: The Triple Cord

In this short passage, Jesus quietly binds together three things that modern culture likes to separate:

| Theme in the Text | What it Means | How it Ties to the Others |
|-------------------|---------------|----------------------------|
| **Possession** (ἀποθήκη, *apothēkē*; treasure, storage) | What you claim as “mine” and seek to secure. | Possession is not neutral; it is an embodied vote for what you believe is worth guarding. |
| **Attention** (καρδία, *kardia*; heart, the center of thought, desire, focus) | Where your mind habitually goes, what you “keep an eye on.” | The verb “lay up” (θησαυρίζω, *thēsaurizō*) implies repeated action: you *attend* to something by continually storing it.  Attention is the activity of treasuring. |
| **Love** (implicit in “heart”) | The orientation of the will toward a “good” you seek. | In biblical anthropology, the heart is the command center of love.  Where your treasure is, there your *love* is also. |

Notice the logic:

1. **You treasure what you love** (love chooses a “good” and calls it treasure).
2. **You store what you treasure** (love moves into possession: you accumulate, protect, arrange your life around it).
3. **Your attention follows your storage** (possession requires maintenance: you check it, count it, worry about it, admire it—attention is tethered to the location of the treasure).

Thus, **attention is the hinge** between possession and love.  If you want to know what (or whom) a person loves, look at what they possess *and* what they cannot stop looking at.  Jesus compresses this into a single law: *Where your treasure is, there your heart is also.*

---

### 3. The Two Estates: Earth vs. Heaven

The contrast sharpens the relationship:

- **Earthly treasure** is defined by vulnerability: *moth, rust, thieves*.  It is “stored” in a place that is itself corrupting.  The very act of storing it requires anxiety (Matt. 6:25–34) and divided vision.  Your attention is forced to oscillate between the treasure and the threats to it.  Your love is therefore partial, fearful, and riveted on decay.

- **Heavenly treasure** is defined by incorruptibility and freedom from theft.  It is “stored” by acts that cannot be undone by entropy or violence (e.g., generosity, justice, worship—practices that deposit “credit” in God’s economy).  Because it cannot be lost, it does not require a vigilance that breeds anxiety.  Your attention can become single-minded (undivided), and your love can be aimed at the Giver rather than the gift.

In this contrast, **possession is spiritual posture**.  To “lay up” treasure in heaven is to practice a mode of having that is simultaneously a mode of seeing and a mode of loving.

---

### 4. Synthesis: The Heart as a Compass, Not a Cage

Jesus is not saying that the heart is a passive thermometer that merely registers where the treasure is.  He is saying it is a compass that is *magnetized* by the treasure.  Change the pole, and the needle turns.

- **Reveals:**  Check your bank statements, calendar, and photo gallery; they are a map of your heart’s current coordinates.
- **Trains:**  If you want your heart to love God and neighbor without anxiety, practice storing your value in acts that echo God’s economy (mercy, worship, justice).  Over time, your attention will stop scanning for rust and thieves, and your love will be tethered to the eternal.

In the end, **possession, attention, and love are not three separate baskets but one braided rope**.  The text invites us to stop trying to manage our *feelings* (the heart) directly, and instead to change what we *store*—because the heart always, inevitably, follows the treasure.
