This passage from Jeremiah offers a piercing diagnosis of the human condition, but it’s best understood not as pure pessimism—as if we are simply doomed—but as a bracing realism about self-deception that drives us toward the only One who truly knows us. Here’s how to weigh that balance, and what it means for how we talk about our motives.

---

### 1. Pessimism or realism about self-deception?

The verse declares the heart is “deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked” (or “incurably sick,” depending on translation). The rhetorical question “who can know it?” confirms that self-knowledge is not something we can achieve on our own. That sounds dark, but notice: this is a **diagnosis**, not a verdict without remedy.

- **Realism about self-deception**: The core issue isn’t simply that we do bad things, but that we are often blind to our own true motives. The “heart” in Hebrew thought is the inner person—mind, will, emotions. If that inner center is bent, then even our self-perception becomes distorted. We can convince ourselves that selfishness is love, that cowardice is prudence, or that resentment is righteous anger. This isn’t a cynical claim that everyone is outwardly monstrous; it’s a sober admission that **no one is a reliable judge of their own heart**.
- **Why it’s not merely pessimistic**: Verse 10 immediately follows with “I the Lord search the heart, I test the mind.” The point isn’t to leave us in despair, but to turn us away from trusting our own internal press releases. God alone penetrates our fog. In the wider context of Jeremiah, this honest diagnosis is the prelude to the promise of a new heart (Jeremiah 31:33–34). So the passage is *realistic* about our capacity for self-deception, but *hopeful* about God’s capacity to know, judge, and ultimately heal us.

Thus, the emphasis is less “human nature is garbage” (pessimism) and more “human self-assessment is dangerously unreliable” (realism). The shock in verse 9 is meant to unsettle our assumption that we can cleanly understand our own motives.

---

### 2. How to talk about motives when the heart is an unreliable witness

If my own heart can trick me, then any statement that begins with “I know my motives were pure…” needs qualification. This doesn’t mean we stay silent about motives; it means we speak with a different posture.

**a) Start with humility, not certainty.**  
Replace “My motive was X” with “As best I can discern before God, I think my motive was X, but I also see traces of Y, and I invite God to show me what I cannot see.” This echoes Psalm 139:23–24: “Search me, God, and know my heart… See if there is any offensive way in me.”

**b) Invite God’s searching as an ongoing practice, not a one-time event.**  
Jeremiah 10 says the Lord *does* search the heart—present tense, not just on judgment day. We can pray regularly for that searching. When we talk about our motives, we can explicitly frame them as provisional and open to divine correction: “I’m holding this with open hands; God may reveal a deeper fear or pride I’m not seeing yet.”

**c) Speak from the outside in—look at the fruit.**  
The passage says God will give to each “according to his ways, according to the fruit of his doings.” While we can’t fully dissect internal motives, our patterns of behavior and their effects are more observable. It’s often more honest to say, “My actions had this impact, and that may point to a hidden motive,” rather than confidently defending an inner state no one can verify.

**d) Use trusted, wise companions as a check.**  
Because we have blind spots, we need others to lovingly question what we can’t see. Talking about motives in safe community sounds like: “Here’s what I *think* is going on in my heart—what do you see in my life that might confirm or contradict that?” Scripture repeatedly points to the need for counsel (Proverbs 12:15).

**e) Rest in God’s justice and mercy, not in proving our purity.**  
When Paul faced accusations about his motives, he essentially said, “I don’t even judge myself… It is the Lord who judges me” (1 Corinthians 4:3–4). He didn’t claim to perfectly know his own heart, but entrusted it to God. We can do the same: “I honestly don’t fully trust my motives, and I may have gotten it wrong, but I commit this to the God who knows and who is merciful.”

---

In summary, Jeremiah 17:9–10 is realistic about self-deception—our hearts are not neutral witnesses—but it’s not a dead-end pessimism because it points us to the God who searches, judges justly, and later promises a new heart. The practical outcome is a life of humble self-suspicion paired with confident trust in God’s knowing gaze. We talk about motives, but we do so tentatively, prayerfully, and in community, always leaving room for the possibility that we are missing something and that God’s searching is our ultimate safety.
