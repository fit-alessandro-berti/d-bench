The elder brother in Luke 15:28–32 exhibits several issues, but his deepest problem seems to be a combination of wounded fairness and pride in his obedience. This is evident through his reaction and dialogue with his father, which reveals his resentment and feelings of being overlooked or underappreciated.

1. **Anger and Envy:** The elder brother is clearly angry, as indicated by his refusal to join the celebration for his younger brother's return. This anger might be partly driven by envy towards the attention and celebration his prodigal brother receives upon his return.

2. **Wounded Fairness:** The elder brother expresses a strong sense of injustice or wounded fairness when he points out that despite his years of faithful service and obedience, he had never been granted a celebration like the one his father gave for his wayward brother. His phrase, "neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment," underscores his sense of always having done the right thing without receiving due recognition or reward.

3. **Pride in Obedience:** His sense of pride in his continuous obedience is palpable. He contrasts his fidelity to his father's commands with the reckless, indulgent lifestyle of his younger brother. This pride in his moral and dutiful behavior blinds him to the nature of his father's unconditional love and the concept of grace, which is extended irrespective of merit.

4. **Discomfort with Mercy:** Another dimension to his problem could be discomfort with the concept of mercy itself. His sense of justice is rigid and transactional – he believes in earning one's rewards through actions. Therefore, the unconditional mercy and joy expressed by his father upon the return of his repentant brother is alien and unsettling to him.

Overall, while the elder brother's reaction is multifaceted, involving elements of anger, envy, pride, and discomfort with mercy, the roots lie deep in his wounded sense of fairness and his pride in his own obedience and merits. He struggles to understand the grace that goes beyond deeds and actions, which his father epitomizes in his joyous reception of the lost son. The father's reminder, "all that I have is thine," points to an existing promise and provision which the elder brother failed to appreciate due to his focus on merit and reward.
