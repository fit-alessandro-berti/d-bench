This passage from the Gospel of John uses the metaphor of light and darkness to explore profound spiritual and moral truths about human nature. 

The light here represents truth, goodness, and the revelation of God. It exposes reality for what it is. However, the passage states that many people hate the light and prefer darkness. This is because the light reveals their evil deeds and the truth of their sinful condition before a holy God. 

There is a direct link between truth and exposure. Truth by its very nature brings things to light - it shows reality clearly and accurately. This is uncomfortable for those who are living contrary to the truth. They prefer the darkness of secrecy, self-deception and hiding.

The reason the light is hated is because of the shame that comes with being truly seen. When our deeds and our inner depths are exposed, when the light shines on who we really are, it is a painful experience if we know we are not living rightly. It's convicting. We feel unworthy and ashamed in the presence of pure truth and goodness.

So to avoid this shame and the need to change, many prefer to flee the light. They run from truth, both the truth about themselves and the truth about God and his righteous standards. By hiding and obscuring, they can maintain the illusion that things are fine as they are.

But the passage holds out hope for a different way - coming to the light, letting one's deeds be exposed, not to wallow in shame but to find forgiveness and transformation. Walking in truth means owning one's failures and faults but also finding mercy and power to live a new life.

The last verse points to good deeds that flow not from self-effort but from God working within someone. There is no shame in these being brought to light because their source and motive is pure. What is born of God welcomes the light.

In the end, the question is whether we will be people of the light or people of the darkness. Will we embrace truth, even when it's uncomfortable, and let it change us? Or will we hide and resist the light, preferring comfortable self-deception? This passage invites us to come to the light, and let that light transform us from the inside out.
