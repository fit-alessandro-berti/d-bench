### How Luke 6:37 Differs from "Moral Judgments Never Matter"
The verse—"Judge not, and ye shall not be judged: condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned: forgive, and ye shall be forgiven" (KJV)—is part of Jesus' Sermon on the Plain in the Gospel of Luke (paralleling the Sermon on the Mount in Matthew 7). At first glance, it might seem like a blanket prohibition against any form of judgment, leading some to misinterpret it as moral relativism (i.e., "nothing is right or wrong, so don't judge anything"). However, this is a misunderstanding. The verse doesn't advocate for amorality or the irrelevance of moral judgments; instead, it warns against a specific *kind* of judgment—hypocritical, self-righteous, or condemnatory judgment of others—while implicitly affirming the importance of moral discernment.

Here's the key difference:
- **"Moral judgments never matter"** implies a nihilistic or relativistic worldview where there are no objective standards of right and wrong. It suggests apathy toward ethics: actions have no inherent moral weight, so evaluating them is pointless or arbitrary. This could lead to excusing harmful behaviors (e.g., "Who am I to say theft is wrong?"). Philosophers like Friedrich Nietzsche critiqued such relativism as potentially leading to moral chaos, where power or preference replaces truth.
  
- **Luke 6:37, in contrast, assumes moral judgments *do* matter**—but they must be exercised with humility, mercy, and self-awareness. Jesus isn't saying "ignore evil" or "pretend everything is fine." He's addressing how we judge *people*, not the objective morality of actions. The context (e.g., Luke 6:41-42) uses the metaphor of removing the "plank" from your own eye before addressing the "speck" in someone else's, emphasizing self-examination first. This promotes reciprocity: the standard you use on others will be applied to you (a theme echoed in the Golden Rule earlier in the chapter).

In essence, the verse is a call to *responsible moral engagement*, not disengagement. It aligns with broader biblical teachings that encourage distinguishing good from evil (e.g., Hebrews 5:14 speaks of maturing in discernment), but it tempers this with grace. If moral judgments "never mattered," forgiveness wouldn't be necessary—yet the verse explicitly ties non-judgment to receiving forgiveness, implying a moral framework where sin exists but can be overcome through mercy.

Theological thinkers like C.S. Lewis in *Mere Christianity* highlight this: Jesus isn't forbidding moral evaluation (e.g., courts must judge crimes), but rather the pharisaical tendency to condemn others while ignoring one's own flaws. In practical terms, this means you can judge an action as wrong (e.g., "Lying is harmful") without condemning the person ("You're irredeemable for lying").

### Exploring the Difference Between Discernment and the Appetite to Condemn
To delve deeper, let's break down these two concepts. They both involve judgment, but they stem from different motivations, methods, and outcomes. The distinction is crucial in ethics, psychology, and spirituality, as it separates healthy moral reasoning from destructive tendencies.

#### 1. **Discernment: Wise, Humble Evaluation**
   - **Definition and Purpose**: Discernment (from the Greek *diakrisis*, meaning "to distinguish" or "judge between") is the ability to perceive and evaluate situations, actions, or intentions based on wisdom, evidence, and ethical principles. It's about seeking truth and goodness without unnecessary harshness. In biblical terms, it's encouraged as a spiritual gift (e.g., 1 Corinthians 12:10) and a mark of maturity (Philippians 1:9-10).
   
   - **Key Characteristics**:
     - **Humble and Self-Reflective**: It starts with introspection. As in Luke 6:37, you examine your own motives first to avoid hypocrisy.
     - **Objective and Constructive**: Focuses on facts and potential growth. For example, a teacher discerning a student's cheating might address it to help them learn integrity, not to shame them.
     - **Merciful and Restorative**: It leaves room for forgiveness and change. Think of it as "judging the sin, not the sinner" (a phrase often attributed to Augustine).
     - **Motivated by Love or Justice**: It's driven by care for others or upholding moral order, not personal ego.

   - **Examples**:
     - In everyday life: A friend discerns that your habit of gossiping is damaging relationships and gently points it out to help you improve.
     - In ethics: A journalist discerns misinformation in a news story and corrects it factually, without attacking the reporter's character.
     - Philosophically: Aristotle's concept of *phronesis* (practical wisdom) aligns here—discerning the right action in context, balanced by virtues like temperance.

   Discernment fosters personal and communal growth, aligning with Luke 6:37's promise of reciprocal mercy.

#### 2. **The Appetite to Condemn: Harsh, Self-Serving Judgment**
   - **Definition and Purpose**: This is a desire or "appetite" (implying an emotional hunger) to declare others guilty, inferior, or worthy of punishment. It's less about truth-seeking and more about exerting power, venting frustration, or boosting one's self-image. In psychological terms, it can stem from projection (Freud's idea of displacing one's flaws onto others) or moral grandstanding (a modern concept where people condemn to signal virtue).
   
   - **Key Characteristics**:
     - **Hypocritical and Arrogant**: Ignores one's own faults, as Jesus critiques in the "plank vs. speck" analogy. It's often rooted in pride or insecurity.
     - **Destructive and Final**: Aims to label and exclude, not redeem. It escalates to condemnation (from the Greek *katadikazo*, meaning "to sentence against"), implying irreversible judgment.
     - **Motivated by Ego or Vengeance**: Driven by a "taste" for superiority or punishment, rather than justice. It can become addictive, like schadenfreude (pleasure in others' misfortune).
     - **Lacks Mercy**: Forgoes forgiveness, contradicting the verse's call to "forgive, and ye shall be forgiven."

   - **Examples**:
     - In everyday life: Publicly shaming someone on social media for a minor mistake to feel morally superior, without considering context or offering help.
     - In history: The Pharisees in Jesus' time condemned sinners (e.g., the woman caught in adultery in John 8) to enforce rules, but Jesus responded with discernment: "Neither do I condemn you; go and sin no more."
     - Psychologically: This appetite can fuel mob mentalities, cancel culture, or authoritarianism, where condemnation serves group identity over truth.

   The "appetite" aspect highlights its emotional pull—it's not neutral reasoning but a craving that can distort judgment. Philosophers like Immanuel Kant warned against such "heteronomous" motivations (judging based on impulses rather than reason), while Jesus frames it as spiritually risky: condemning others invites divine judgment on yourself.

#### Why the Distinction Matters
- **Practical Implications**: Discernment builds healthier relationships and societies (e.g., restorative justice systems focus on rehabilitation, not just punishment). An appetite to condemn erodes trust and perpetuates cycles of resentment, as seen in polarized debates today.
- **Theological Tie-Back**: Luke 6:37 bridges these by promising that avoiding condemnatory judgment leads to freedom from it. It's an invitation to emulate God's mercy (e.g., Romans 2:1 warns that judging others hypocritically condemns oneself).
- **Potential Pitfalls**: The line can blur—discernment might feel condemning if delivered poorly. Self-awareness is key: Ask, "Am I seeking truth and growth, or just to feel right?"
