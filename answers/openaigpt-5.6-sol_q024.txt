**External compliance** is outward conformity to God’s commands without a corresponding inward love, trust, or willingness. A person may obey because of fear, social pressure, self-interest, or habit while remaining inwardly resistant. Ezekiel’s promise goes deeper: God will change the source from which conduct arises.

In biblical Hebrew, the **“heart”** (*lēb/lēbāb*) is not merely the seat of emotion. It includes the will, understanding, desires, and moral disposition. A **“new heart”** therefore means a renewed inner orientation—one capable of responding faithfully to God. The parallel phrases clarify one another:

- “a new heart” — a renewed will and disposition  
- “a new spirit” — a renewed inner life  
- “my Spirit within you” — God’s own empowering presence  
- “cause you to walk in my statutes” — resulting obedience  

Thus, the passage contrasts obedience imposed from the outside with obedience produced from within by divine renewal. It does not necessarily imply instant sinless perfection or the loss of human agency; rather, God overcomes the settled resistance that has made covenant faithfulness impossible.

The **“heart of stone”** is described almost materially because hardness has become more than an occasional bad choice. Stone is unresponsive, inflexible, and lifeless. Israel’s resistance is portrayed as an entrenched condition that mere additional commands cannot cure. What is needed is not simply better instruction but something like a divine transplant:

- the stone heart must be **removed**, not merely persuaded;
- the heart of flesh must be **given**, not self-manufactured.

Here **“flesh”** is positive, unlike some later uses where “the flesh” signifies sinful human nature. A heart of flesh is living, sensitive, pliable, and responsive—capable of being moved by God.

The material imagery therefore emphasizes both the seriousness of human hardness and the initiative of divine grace. God does not merely demand a better performance; he promises to create the inward responsiveness from which genuine obedience can grow. In context, this is also a corporate promise to restored Israel: cleansing, renewal, the gift of the Spirit, and renewed covenant life all belong together.
