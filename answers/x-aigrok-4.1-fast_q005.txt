### Why Enemy-Love Is Hard to Take Seriously

Loving one's enemies—as commanded in Matthew 5:44–45—feels counterintuitive because it clashes with core human wiring and societal norms. Here's why it's tough:

- **Instinctual Resistance**: Evolution favors self-preservation and reciprocity. Retaliation releases dopamine (the "justice hit"), while forgiveness often feels like weakness. Studies in psychology (e.g., from Paul Bloom's work on empathy) show we extend compassion selectively to our "in-group," not out-group enemies. Loving a betrayer or persecutor triggers visceral disgust or fear.

- **Cultural Reinforcement**: Modern media, politics, and entertainment glorify vengeance (think action movies or social media pile-ons). History's "realists" like Machiavelli or Hobbes argued mercy invites exploitation—why risk it when power dynamics reward aggression?

- **Practical Doubts**: Does it work? Turning the other cheek (Matthew 5:39) seems to enable abusers. Real-world trauma—abuse, war, genocide—makes it feel like denial, not wisdom. Even skeptics like Nietzsche called it "slave morality," a cope for the powerless.

In short, it's hard because it demands overriding survival instincts for something abstract and risky.

### Sentimental, Strategic, or Deeper Moral Strength?

It's **none purely, but primarily a deeper form of moral strength**, with strategic ripples. Let's break it down:

| Category | Description | Fits Enemy-Love? | Why/Not |
|----------|-------------|------------------|---------|
| **Sentimental** | Emotional fluff—warm fuzzies without teeth, like naive idealism ("Kumbaya"). Ignores evil's reality. | No. Jesus wasn't a pushover: he cursed a fig tree (Mark 11:14), drove out moneychangers (John 2:15), and warned of judgment (Matthew 25:41–46). Enemy-love confronts evil by refusing to mirror it, not ignoring it. It's tough love, not Hallmark. |
| **Strategic** | Calculated pragmatism—disarms foes, wins PR, or achieves long-term gains (e.g., Gandhi's satyagraha or MLK's marches exposed injustice without violence). | Partially yes. It breaks revenge cycles (per game theory's "tit-for-tat" with forgiveness), fosters reconciliation (e.g., Mandela's Truth Commission post-apartheid), and reveals enemies' moral bankruptcy. Jesus' approach humiliated Roman oppressors by thriving under persecution. But strategy alone cheapens it—it's not a tactic if motives are self-serving. |
| **Deeper Moral Strength** | Supernatural virtue imitating God's impartiality (sun/rain on all, per the verse). Transforms the lover first (frees from hate's poison), then potentially the enemy. Requires grace beyond human nature. | Yes, core fit. It's "divine rationality" (C.S. Lewis in *Mere Christianity*): God loves without deserving it (Romans 5:8), modeling costly strength. Historical proof: early Christians' enemy-love toppled Rome not by swords, but by exposing empire's cruelty. Modern echoes in Corrie ten Boom forgiving her Nazi captors. It redefines power—not domination, but self-mastery and redemptive suffering. |

**Bottom Line**: Enemy-love isn't easy sentiment or mere strategy; it's moral heroism that echoes God's character. It demands training (practice on small slights first) and faith in ultimate justice (God's, not ours—Romans 12:19). When it "works" (e.g., converting Saul to Paul via Stephen's prayer, Acts 7:60), it's because it unleashes otherworldly power. Try it small-scale: pray genuinely for someone who's wronged you. The difficulty proves its depth.
