Matthew 6:19–21 is a profound passage that speaks to the relationship between treasure, the heart, and the orientation of one’s life. The text suggests a **dynamic interplay** between treasure and the heart—one that involves both **revelation** (treasure *reveals* the heart) and **formation** (treasure *trains* the heart). Here’s how these ideas connect, along with the tie between possession, attention, and love:

---

### **1. Treasure Reveals the Heart ("Where your treasure is, there will your heart be also")**
- **Diagnostic Function**: Jesus states that the location of one’s treasure *discloses* the true allegiance of the heart. Our priorities, investments, and attachments expose what we ultimately value, trust, or worship. For example:
  - If someone hoards wealth, their heart is likely anchored in material security.
  - If someone generously gives to the poor or invests in eternal things (e.g., justice, evangelism, discipleship), their heart is aligned with God’s kingdom.
- **Biblical Parallels**: This aligns with Jesus’ teaching elsewhere that *"out of the abundance of the heart, the mouth speaks"* (Matthew 12:34). Our actions (including how we use possessions) reveal our inner loyalties.

---

### **2. Treasure Trains the Heart (Implied by the Command to "Lay Up Treasures in Heaven")**
- **Formative Function**: While the text explicitly says treasure *follows* the heart, the command to *redirect* treasure (from earth to heaven) implies that **where we place our treasure can reshape our heart’s desires**. This is a **habit-forming** process:
  - **Possession → Attention → Love**: What we own or pursue captures our attention, and sustained attention fosters affection. For instance:
    - If we repeatedly invest in material comforts, our hearts grow more attached to them.
    - If we intentionally give to God’s work or serve others, our affections shift toward heavenly values (e.g., love, generosity, faith).
  - **Neurological/Spiritual Reality**: Modern psychology (e.g., neuroplasticity) and ancient wisdom (e.g., Proverbs 23:7, *"As a man thinks in his heart, so is he"*) confirm that repeated actions shape desires. Jesus’ command is an invitation to *retrain* the heart by reorienting treasure.
- **Theological Basis**: This aligns with the biblical idea that **obedience precedes affection** (e.g., Deuteronomy 6:5–6, *"Love the Lord... these words shall be on your heart"*). We don’t wait to *feel* like storing up heavenly treasure; we act in faith, trusting that our hearts will follow.

---

### **3. The Tie Between Possession, Attention, and Love**
Jesus’ teaching assumes a **causal loop** between these three:
1. **Possession (Treasure)**: What we *have* or *seek* (money, status, relationships, etc.).
2. **Attention (Focus)**: Where we *invest* our time, energy, and resources.
3. **Love (Heart)**: What we *worship* or ultimately trust for security and joy.

- **Earthly Treasure Cycle**:
  Possession (wealth) → Attention (constant care for it) → Love (trust in it for happiness).
  *Result*: Anxiety (moth/rust/thieves), idolatry, and a divided heart (Matthew 6:24).

- **Heavenly Treasure Cycle**:
  Possession (generosity, kingdom investment) → Attention (focus on God’s purposes) → Love (affection for God and others).
  *Result*: Freedom, joy, and an undivided heart (Matthew 6:22–23, *"The eye is the lamp of the body"*).

---
### **Key Takeaways**
1. **Revelation**: Your treasure *shows* where your heart is. (Diagnostic)
2. **Transformation**: Redirecting your treasure *shapes* your heart. (Formative)
3. **Integration**: Possessions, attention, and love are inseparable. To change one is to influence the others.

---
### **Practical Implications**
- **Examine Your Treasure**: Audit your time, money, and energy. What do they reveal about your heart?
- **Reorient Intentionally**: Start small—give, serve, or invest in eternal things, trusting that your affections will follow.
- **Guard Against Idols**: Earthly treasures aren’t inherently evil, but they become dangerous when they *compete* with God for your heart (Colossians 3:5).

Jesus’ call is both a **warning** (earthly treasure corrupts) and a **promise** (heavenly treasure satisfies). The heart follows the treasure—but by God’s grace, we can lead our hearts home.
