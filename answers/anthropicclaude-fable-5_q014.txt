## The Direction of the Logic

The grammar of verse 21 is decisive: "where your treasure is, there will your heart be *also*." The verb is future tense and the movement runs from treasure to heart, not the other way around. Jesus does not say, "Look at your treasure to discover what your heart already loves." He says the heart *follows* the treasure — it migrates toward whatever you have invested in.

This is why the passage opens with a command about *action* rather than affection. Jesus doesn't say "love heavenly things." He says "lay up" (*thēsaurizō* — store, accumulate, invest). The assumption embedded in the command is striking: you cannot directly command your heart, but you *can* command your hands. Choose where your treasure goes, and your heart will eventually relocate to live near it.

So the text is primarily about **training**, not revelation — though the two aren't fully separable. A diagnostic reading isn't wrong (your current treasure does indicate where your heart has already gone), but the *exhortation* only makes sense if the process is reversible and ongoing. If treasure merely revealed a fixed heart, the command to relocate treasure would be pointless. The command presupposes that investment is formative.

## How Possession, Attention, and Love Bind Together

The mechanism Jesus assumes works something like this:

1. **Possession creates stake.** Once you have stored something up, you have something to lose. The vivid imagery — moth, rust, thieves — is the imagery of *anxiety*. Earthly treasure is treasure under threat, and threat compels vigilance.

2. **Stake compels attention.** What can be lost must be watched, maintained, defended, checked on. Your treasure becomes a standing claim on your thoughts. This is the verse's quiet psychological realism: attention is not freely distributed; it is conscripted by investment.

3. **Attention ripens into love.** In biblical anthropology, the *kardia* (heart) is not merely the seat of emotion but the center of will, deliberation, and devotion. What you attend to constantly, you come to care about. The heart settles wherever it spends its time.

This is why the passage sits inside a larger argument in Matthew 6 — it flows directly into the eye as "the lamp of the body" (vv. 22–23), the impossibility of serving two masters (v. 24), and the command not to be anxious (vv. 25–34). The whole sequence is about the economy of attention and allegiance: treasure determines where the eye looks, the eye determines what fills the person, and divided treasure produces a divided, anxious self.

## The Unsettling Implication

Read this way, the text removes a common refuge. People often imagine their loves are interior and authentic regardless of their outward economic behavior — "my heart's in the right place." Jesus reverses this: the heart goes where the deposits go. There is no maintaining heavenly affections while making exclusively earthly investments, because affection is not self-sustaining; it is *funded*.

The corollary is hopeful, though: the same mechanism that lets earthly treasure colonize the heart allows the heart to be deliberately re-formed. You don't wait until you love rightly to invest rightly. You invest, and love follows the investment.

In short: treasure does reveal the heart — but only because it has first trained it.
