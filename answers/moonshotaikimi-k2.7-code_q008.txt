**1 John 4:20 is a “reality check.”** John treats love for the visible brother as the test of love for the invisible God because the two loves are not separate options; they are inseparable expressions of the same allegiance to God.

### Why the visible neighbor is the test

1. **God’s command joins the two loves.**  
   John has already said, “This commandment have we from him, That he who loveth God love his brother also” (1 John 4:21). To claim love for God while refusing the person God commands us to love is, by definition, to disobey God. The “vertical” love for God must show up in “horizontal” love for people.

2. **Love must be concrete, not just a feeling or a slogan.**  
   It is easy to say “I love God” when God is invisible, distant, and unable to annoy or oppose us. A real brother, however, is right in front of us—he can be inconvenient, disagreeable, or different. John argues that if our love cannot survive contact with an actual person, it was never genuine love for God; it was only religious sentiment.

3. **People, especially God’s children, are the visible objects of God’s love.**  
   Human beings bear God’s image, and fellow believers are born of God. To hate or despise them is to despise what God loves and values. That is why John says elsewhere that hatred of a brother shows one is still “in death” (1 John 3:14).

4. **Behavior toward people is the only visible evidence of love for an invisible God.**  
   Since “no man hath seen God at any time” (1 John 4:12), the way we treat those whom God loves becomes the proof of whether we really love Him. Words are cheap; actions toward real people are the courtroom where the claim is tested.

### What self-deception it exposes

John uses the strong word **“liar”** because he is exposing a particular kind of self-deception:

- **Religious self-righteousness.** The person thinks that pious language, correct doctrine, worship attendance, or emotional devotion to God can compensate for contempt, resentment, or cruelty toward others.
- **Splitting love in two.** He imagines he can love God “spiritually” while hating his brother “practically,” as though God accepts one compartment of his life and ignores the other.
- **Justifying hatred as zeal.** He may tell himself his dislike is justified because the brother is wrong, difficult, or unworthy. But John will not allow that excuse: if you love the unseen God, you must love the seen brother.
- **A false assurance of salvation.** This fits John’s larger purpose: he wants readers to examine whether their claim to know God is real. Claiming to love God while habitually hating a brother reveals that the profession is empty.

### In short

The verse says that **love for God is not a private, invisible emotion that can coexist with public contempt for people.** If we do not love the brother we can see, our claim to love the God we cannot see is a lie we tell ourselves and others. True love for God necessarily overflows into love for those God loves.
