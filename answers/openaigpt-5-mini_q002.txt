Short answer: Because David (and the Bible) wants more than legal relief from punishment — he wants his inner life healed. Pardon removes guilt; a clean heart removes the root that produced the sin and restores relationship, desire, and power to live differently.

Points to consider

- Context matters. Psalm 51 is David’s penitence after grievous sin. He knows a mere acquittal would leave the same inclinations and the same brokenness that led him into sin in the first place.

- Pardon versus inner renewal (two different realities)
  - Pardon = forgiveness, acquittal, removal of penalty and guilt. It answers “Will I be condemned?” and restores my standing before God.
  - Clean heart = moral/psychological/spiritual transformation. It answers “Who am I now? What do I love and want?” It changes motives, affections, and patterns of behavior.

- Why inner change matters
  - Prevents relapse. If only the external penalty is removed but the heart is unchanged, the same temptations and desires will often lead to repeated sin.
  - Restores relationship. True fellowship with God is not merely non-condemnation but mutual communion; that requires a renewed will and affection.
  - Produces fruit. A renewed heart results in changed conduct, obedience, and the “joy of thy salvation” (v.12). Forgiveness without transformation can produce a fragile, hypocritical faith.
  - Addresses shame and identity. Pardon clears the conscience, but a new heart heals identity and transforms how one sees God and oneself.

- Biblical/theological support
  - The Bible pairs legal forgiveness with internal renewal: Ezekiel 36:26 (“I will give you a new heart…”), and in the New Testament the work of the Spirit is presented as making people new (2 Cor 5:17; Titus 3:5). Classic Christian doctrine distinguishes justification (forgiveness) from sanctification (ongoing renewal); both are needed.

- Practical and pastoral implications
  - True repentance includes sorrow for sin, confessing it, and turning away — asking God not only to clear the record but to change the desires that led to sin.
  - Asking for the Holy Spirit’s sustaining power (v.11–12) recognizes that inner change is a gift God gives and sustains.

Conclusion
Pardon frees you from the penalty; a clean heart changes who you are. The psalmist asks for both because forgiveness alone would leave the cause of the failure intact. He wants restored fellowship, new affections, and the God-given power to live differently — not just an escape from guilt.
