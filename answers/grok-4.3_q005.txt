**Enemy-love is difficult primarily because it collides with evolved human psychology and observable social dynamics.**

Humans are wired for reciprocity and coalitional favoritism. We extend cooperation, empathy, and restraint most readily to kin, allies, and those who signal future reciprocity. Enemies, by definition, represent threats to resources, status, or safety. Hatred, avoidance, or calibrated retaliation often served survival in ancestral environments. The command in Matthew 5 to actively bless, do good, and pray for persecutors overrides that pattern without obvious immediate payoff. It feels costly in status, energy, and risk of exploitation.

### Sentimental?
It can function that way when treated as vague niceness or emotional warmth toward people actively harming you. That version collapses under scrutiny because it ignores power asymmetries and incentives. An abuser or aggressor often interprets non-retaliation as permission to continue. Sentimental readings also flatten the text: the verse does not command affection or trust, but specific actions—blessing, benefaction, and prayer—tied to God's impartial distribution of natural goods. Critics from Nietzsche onward have called this "slave morality," a revaluation that praises weakness as virtue because the strong cannot be constrained by it. That critique has bite where the teaching is used to demand unilateral disarmament without corresponding strength.

### Strategic?
Sometimes, but secondarily. In limited contexts, refusing escalation can de-escalate cycles of revenge, preserve reputation for fairness, or shift public perception. Historical examples include certain nonviolent campaigns where moral consistency created political leverage. Game-theoretic models show that strategies combining cooperation with retaliation (tit-for-tat variants) often outperform pure aggression or pure pacifism in repeated interactions. However, the biblical framing is not instrumental. It grounds the behavior in resemblance to divine action rather than expected returns. Treating it purely as strategy risks the same problem as any calculated virtue: it erodes when the calculation fails to deliver. Realpolitik traditions from Thucydides to modern statecraft treat enemy-love as either luxury or propaganda.

### Deeper moral strength?
This is the more robust interpretation. It demands independence from the other's conduct. Your response is not dictated by their provocation; it is set by an internal standard. That requires self-mastery—suppressing the immediate impulse to mirror harm, while still distinguishing benevolence from enablement. It is not passivity. The actions listed (bless, do good, pray) are positive and can coexist with boundaries, self-defense, or justice-seeking. The difficulty lies in execution: most people calibrate their conduct to the moral quality of the recipient, which makes their own character reactive rather than self-directed. Maintaining consistency here resembles other forms of discipline that look costly or irrational in the short term—delayed gratification, truth-telling under pressure, or consistent standards regardless of audience.

Empirical patterns reinforce why it rarely scales. Forgiveness research shows measurable psychological benefits for the forgiver (reduced rumination, better health markers), yet compliance remains low when stakes are high. Tribal and institutional behavior defaults to in-group bias and out-group suspicion because those patterns aggregate into durable cooperation within groups. Societies or individuals that practice indiscriminate enemy-love without institutional safeguards tend to be selected against.

The teaching's force comes from its asymmetry: it does not require the enemy to change first. That asymmetry is what makes it either profound or foolish depending on the agent's capacity to absorb cost without collapsing into resentment or self-deception. In practice, it is most coherent when paired with realism about human nature rather than denial of it.
