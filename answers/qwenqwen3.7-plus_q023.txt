In Isaiah 55:6–7, the prophet delivers one of the Bible’s most profound and urgent invitations to repentance. By explicitly calling for the forsaking of both “way” (outward conduct) and “thoughts” (inward imagination), the text presents a holistic vision of what it means to turn back to God. 

Here is a breakdown of why both are required, and what this implies about conduct, imagination, and the nature of divine mercy.

---

### 1. Why Both "Way" and "Thoughts"?
In biblical anthropology, a human being is not compartmentalized; the inner life and outer life are inextricably linked. 

*   **"Way" (Hebrew: *derek*):** This refers to a path, a road, or a manner of living. It represents habitual actions, lifestyle choices, and the visible trajectory of a person’s life.
*   **"Thoughts" (Hebrew: *machashabah*):** This refers to intentions, schemes, imaginations, and the underlying mindset or worldview that drives a person’s choices.

Repentance requires both because **the "thoughts" are the root, and the "way" is the fruit.** Merely changing outward behavior without changing inward motives leads to hypocrisy or temporary moralism. Conversely, claiming to have changed one’s inward thoughts while continuing to walk in destructive outward patterns reveals that the heart has not actually changed. True biblical repentance (*shuv*, meaning "to turn around") demands a complete 180-degree pivot of the whole person.

### 2. Implications for Outward Conduct ("Way")
The inclusion of "way" implies that **God cares deeply about tangible, observable reality.** 
*   **Sin has consequences:** Our actions impact others, our communities, and our own souls. God does not view sin merely as a private philosophical error, but as a destructive path that must be abandoned.
*   **Repentance must be visible:** True turning to God will inevitably produce a change in behavior. As John the Baptist and the Apostle Paul later echoed, there must be "fruit in keeping with repentance" (Matthew 3:8, Acts 26:20). You cannot claim to have returned to the Lord if you continue to walk away from Him in your daily habits.

### 3. Implications for Inward Imagination ("Thoughts")
The inclusion of "thoughts" implies that **God is not satisfied with mere behavior modification; He desires heart transformation.**
*   **The battle is in the mind:** Jesus taught that "out of the heart come evil thoughts, murder, adultery, sexual immorality, theft, false testimony, slander" (Matthew 15:19). If the inward imagination remains proud, greedy, resentful, or self-reliant, the outward "way" will eventually revert to sin. 
*   **Rejection of Legalism:** God does not want robotic obedience. He wants people whose very desires and thought processes have been renewed (Romans 12:2). Forsaking "thoughts" means surrendering our rationalizations, our cynical worldviews, and our prideful schemes, replacing them with trust in God’s ways (which Isaiah immediately contrasts in verse 8: *"For my thoughts are not your thoughts"*).

### 4. Implications for the Possibility of Mercy
The promise attached to this dual repentance is staggering: *"he will have mercy upon him... and he will abundantly pardon."* This reveals three vital truths about God’s mercy:

*   **Mercy is Accessible, but Urgent:** The phrase *"while he may be found... while he is near"* implies a window of grace. It is an urgent invitation, acknowledging that hardening one’s heart over time can make repentance impossible. However, for the one who chooses to turn *now*, God is present and ready.
*   **Mercy is Comprehensive:** Because God demands the turning of both "way" and "thoughts," His pardon covers both. He does not just forgive the outward crimes; He cleanses the inward guilt and reshapes the imagination. 
*   **Mercy is "Abundant":** The Hebrew word for "abundantly" (*harbe*) means to make great, to multiply, or to do something in large measure. God’s forgiveness is not grudging, minimal, or merely a legal acquittal. It is an overflowing, generous flood of grace. The dual requirement of repentance does not make mercy *harder to earn*; rather, it ensures that the recipient is truly open to receiving a grace that will completely transform them.

### Summary
Isaiah 55:6–7 teaches that true repentance is holistic. It requires abandoning the **visible path** of sin and the **invisible mindset** that justifies it. However, this high standard is not a barrier to keep people out; it is the very doorway through which God’s abundant, transformative mercy flows. God offers to not only forgive the past, but to fundamentally rewire the heart and redirect the life of anyone willing to turn to Him.
