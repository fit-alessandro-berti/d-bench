 This passage constructs a striking economic metaphor: the **heart** is a treasury (*thēsauros*)—a storehouse where moral capital is accumulated—and **speech** is the current drawn from that account. Jesus does not treat words as isolated events but as withdrawals from an existing balance. The “abundance” or overflow (*perisseuma*) suggests that when the mouth opens, the heart is simply tipping out what it has already stored. This is why Jesus rejects the Pharisees’ slander as inexplicable hypocrisy: vipers cannot produce honey, because venom is what they contain.

Regarding the nature of words, the text strongly supports **all three** dimensions, though it weights them toward revelation and symptom:

**1. Words as Symptoms**
Jesus uses the Pharisees’ speech as a diagnostic. Their accusation—that Jesus casts out demons by Satan—does not appear to them as random noise; it is the natural emission of a poisoned source. Just as a physician reads a symptom to identify a disease, Jesus reads their words to expose the evil “treasure” within. The mouth does not generate the evil; it externalizes what is already dominant in the heart.

**2. Words as Revelations**
The treasury metaphor is especially apt here because a treasury is usually hidden. Jesus insists that speech *unlocks* the storehouse and makes its contents public. What was concealed in the secret chambers of moral character is now “brought forth” (*ekballei*—literally “thrown out”) into the open. On judgment day, Jesus says elsewhere in the same discourse (Matt. 12:36–37), people will give account for every idle word *because* those words have revealed what their hearts were hoarding all along.

**3. Words as Choices**
Yet the passage does not reduce speech to mechanical venting. Jesus addresses the Pharisees as moral agents (“How *can ye*, being evil, speak good things?”). The verb “bring forth” implies active extraction; they are responsible for what they choose to draw from their treasury. Words are choices, but they are *constrained* choices: a person can only spend the currency he possesses. The Pharisees *choose* to speak slander, but they cannot choose to speak *good* slander, because their store lacks it.

**Synthesis**
The passage presents a fluid hierarchy: **words are choices that function as symptoms because they serve as revelations.** The direction of causality runs from the hidden to the manifest. One does not fill the heart by speaking; one speaks because the heart is already full. The frightening corollary is that speech is not merely a behavior to be managed but a window into an accumulated moral reality. To change what comes out of the mouth, one must alter what is deposited—and prioritized—within the treasury of the heart.
