# Enemy-Love: Sentimentality, Strategy, or Moral Strength?

This is one of the most radical ethical demands ever articulated, and you're right to press on why we resist it. Let me think through this carefully.

## Why It's Difficult to Take Seriously

Several forces work against it simultaneously:

**It offends our justice intuitions.** We have deep moral instincts that enemies *deserve* proportional response. Enemy-love can feel like it betrays victims, rewards wrongdoing, and dissolves meaningful moral distinctions. If you love everyone equally, have you abandoned the concept of justice altogether?

**It seems psychologically impossible.** Love isn't typically something we can command into existence. Telling someone to love the person who destroyed their family sounds not just difficult but almost *cruel* in its demand.

**It looks like weakness.** Every strategic framework we inherit - political, evolutionary, game-theoretic - treats enemy-love as exploitable naivety. The person who loves enemies appears to lose in every competitive scenario.

## Examining the Three Possibilities

### Is it sentimental?

This is the most common dismissal - that it's a kind of warm, vague niceness disconnected from reality. But the text itself undermines this reading. Jesus is speaking to people under Roman occupation. His audience includes people with *real* enemies who are doing *real* violence. This isn't advice given from comfort. And the demand is remarkably specific and active: *bless, do good, pray for.* These are concrete practices, not feelings. Sentimentality is passive and self-indulgent. This command is neither.

### Is it strategic?

There's a sophisticated case for this. Game theory research on iterated cooperation, the practical success of figures like King and Mandela, the way enemy-love can break cycles of retaliation - all suggest it sometimes *works*. Turning the other cheek can expose the aggressor's violence and shift the moral framework of the conflict.

But reducing it to strategy creates a contradiction. If you love your enemy *in order to* defeat them more effectively, you don't actually love them. Strategic "love" is just refined manipulation. The moment it stops working, you'd abandon it, which reveals it was never love at all.

### Is it moral strength?

This is where I think the passage pushes us, but in a way that needs careful unpacking.

## The Deeper Logic of the Text

The reasoning Jesus offers is extraordinary and often overlooked. He doesn't ground the command in:
- The enemy's hidden goodness
- The likelihood of a good outcome
- Your moral superiority in doing it

He grounds it in **the character of God**: *"He maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good."*

This is a remarkable theological and moral claim. God's posture toward creation is not governed by reciprocity. The sustaining of life - sun, rain, the basic conditions of existence - is given without regard to merit. Enemy-love is presented not as an extraordinary exception to normal morality but as **participation in the deepest structure of reality itself.**

This reframes the question entirely. The difficulty isn't that enemy-love is too idealistic for the real world. It's that our retaliatory instincts are too *small* for the real world as it actually is - a world sustained by indiscriminate generosity.

## Why "Moral Strength" Is Closest but Still Insufficient

I'd argue it's moral strength, but of a specific kind that challenges what we typically mean by strength:

**It requires seeing clearly.** You must recognize the enemy *as* enemy - acknowledging real harm, real opposition - and *still* choose love. This isn't denial or naivety. It's the hardest form of moral realism.

**It requires freedom from reactive determination.** If your enemy's hatred dictates your response, they control you. Enemy-love is the refusal to let another person's moral failure define your moral identity. In this sense, it is arguably the *most* sovereign posture available.

**It requires endurance without guarantee.** Strategic thinking promises results. Sentimentality promises good feelings. Enemy-love promises neither. It demands sustained moral action that may never be rewarded, recognized, or reciprocated.

## The Honest Difficulty

But I want to name something the passage doesn't resolve and that we shouldn't pretend is resolved:

Enemy-love exists in tension with the legitimate demands of justice, protection of the vulnerable, and resistance to evil. Bonhoeffer loved his enemies and participated in a plot to assassinate Hitler. Was that a failure of enemy-love or its deepest expression - loving the victims *and* the perpetrator enough to act?

The command doesn't give us a formula. It gives us a *orientation* - a fundamental direction of moral character that must be worked out in every particular situation with wisdom, courage, and often agonizing discernment.

## Conclusion

Enemy-love is not sentimental because it demands action in the face of real harm. It is not merely strategic because it doesn't depend on results. It is moral strength, but of a kind that redefines strength itself - **the capacity to act from fullness rather than reaction, from identity rather than injury.**

The deepest reason it's hard to take seriously may be that taking it seriously would require us to change not just our behavior toward enemies but our entire understanding of what goodness fundamentally *is*.
