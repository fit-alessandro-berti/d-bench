**1 John 4:20** is basically saying: you can’t fake it with God if you’re failing the open-book test with people. 

### Why love of neighbor is the “test” for love of God

1. **Visibility = accountability**  
   John’s logic is blunt: “whom he hath seen” vs “whom he hath not seen.” Your brother is right in front of you. His needs, flaws, and humanity are obvious. God is spirit, unseen. If you can’t manage love for the concrete, tangible person who cuts you off in traffic or disagrees with you at dinner, claiming to love an invisible, perfect God is suspect. The visible relationship is where love gets tested, proved, and practiced. It’s easier to profess love for a distant ideal than to show patience to the annoying person in your life.

2. **Shared image/likeness**  
   Earlier in 1 John 4, John grounds this in God’s nature: “God is love” (v8), and “we love because he first loved us” (v19). In Genesis terms, every “brother” bears God’s image. So hatred toward a fellow image-bearer is functionally hatred toward what God made and loves. You can’t claim devotion to the Artist while trashing His artwork.

3. **Love isn’t abstract to John**  
   For John, *agape* love isn’t a feeling or private spiritual vibe. It’s action: “let us not love in word or talk but in deed and in truth” (1 John 3:18). Deeds happen toward people you can see. So the neighbor becomes the lab where “love for God” either shows up or gets exposed as empty.

### The self-deception it’s exposing

John calls it straight out: “he is a liar.” The deception is **compartmentalization**. It’s the split where we think:

| The Lie I Tell Myself | What John Says It Actually Is |
| --- | --- |
| I can have a “vertical” relationship with God while my “horizontal” relationships are a mess | Spiritual bypassing — using religious language to dodge real-life love |
| Love is mainly about worship, prayer, theology — private things | Love is ethical and social first. Doctrine without people = hypocrisy |
| I don’t hate my brother, I just… avoid/ignore/hold contempt for them | John treats “not loving” and “hating” as the same category. Indifference counts |
| God understands my heart even if I’m bad with people | No, your treatment of people reveals your heart. God isn’t fooled |

So the self-deception is believing love can be disembodied — that you can claim intimacy with God while refusing the hard, ordinary work of loving flawed people. John says that’s not just inconsistent, it’s impossible. The claim to love God without loving your brother collapses under the weight of evidence.

**Bottom line**: In 1 John, love of God isn’t proved by religious intensity, but by how you treat the person you can actually see. If that’s missing, the “I love God” part isn’t real yet — it’s just words.
