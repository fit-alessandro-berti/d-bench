Certainly! Let’s break down **Matthew 12:34–35** and address your question step by step.

---

### **1. Connection Between Speech and Hidden Moral Stockpiles**

Jesus says:
> "O generation of vipers, how can ye, being evil, speak good things? for out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh."

- **"Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh"** is key here.  
- This means that what we say (our *speech*) is a direct reflection—*an expression*—of what is stored up within us (*the heart*).
- The "abundance" refers to the reservoir of thoughts, intentions, and values within a person.

So, **speech is not random; it reveals what is deeply rooted in one's character.**

---

### **2. Is Speech a Symptom, Choice, or Revelation?**

Let’s consider each option:

#### **a) Symptom**
- **Yes.** Speech is a symptom because it is an outward sign of inward realities. Just as a fever is a symptom of infection, our words reveal what is going on inside us.
- In this passage, Jesus is diagnosing people who claim to be good but produce evil words—their hearts are not aligned with their speech.

#### **b) Choice**
- **Also yes.** While some aspects of speech may be habitual or automatic, Jesus emphasizes that our words flow from the "abundance" of the heart. Choosing to fill our hearts with good things leads to good speech.
- So, while there is a sense of inevitability (symptom), there is also agency (choice): we can cultivate our inner treasure.

#### **c) Revelation**
- **Definitely.** The passage explicitly states that our words *reveal* what is in our hearts. They expose our true nature, whether good or evil.

---

### **Conclusion**

**Matthew 12:34–35** teaches that speech is both a *symptom* (reflecting inner reality), a *choice* (shaped by what we value and treasure), and a *revelation* (making the heart visible). The passage suggests that words are not arbitrary; they are the outward manifestation of the "abundance"—the moral stockpile—within us.

**In summary:**  
This passage connects speech with hidden moral stockpiles in the heart by asserting that our words are the natural outflow of what we treasure most. It suggests that speech is simultaneously a symptom, a choice, and a revelation of the heart’s contents.
