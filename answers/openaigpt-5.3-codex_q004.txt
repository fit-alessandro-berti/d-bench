Great question. **Proverbs 3:5–6 is not a command to stop thinking; it is a command about ultimate reliance.**

## What kind of trust is being asked for?

In Hebrew thought, “heart” means the center of will, thought, and desire—not just feelings. So:

- **“Trust in the Lord with all your heart”** = place your deepest confidence, loyalty, and security in God.
- **“Lean not on your own understanding”** = don’t treat your perspective as final, self-sufficient, or infallible.
- **“In all your ways acknowledge him”** = submit every sphere of life to God’s authority and wisdom.
- **“He shall direct your paths”** = God providentially guides the life of the one who depends on him.

So this is **whole-life, covenantal trust**, not a ban on thought.

---

## Humble self-distrust vs. anti-intellectualism

### Humble self-distrust (what Proverbs commends)
- “My reasoning is real but limited.”
- “I can think, plan, study—but I can be wrong.”
- “I seek God’s wisdom, correction, and moral direction.”
- “I use my mind as a servant under God.”

### Anti-intellectualism (not what this text teaches)
- “Thinking is bad.”
- “Evidence, learning, and wisdom don’t matter.”
- “Ignorance is spiritual.”
- “I reject careful discernment and call it faith.”

Biblically, wisdom literature (including Proverbs) constantly calls people to **understanding, instruction, prudence, and discernment**. So the verse cannot mean “don’t use your brain.” It means **don’t enthrone your brain**.

---

## A concise way to say it

Proverbs 3:5–6 teaches:  
**“Use your understanding, but do not *rest* your life on it; rest your life on God.”**  

Faith here is not irrationality—it is **rightly ordered rationality**: mind active, heart submitted, will obedient.
