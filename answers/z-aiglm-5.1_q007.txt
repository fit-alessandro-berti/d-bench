To understand Luke 6:37, it is crucial to recognize that when Jesus says, “Judge not,” He is not advocating for moral relativism or the abolition of ethics. The command is not a prohibition against seeing reality clearly; rather, it is a prohibition against a specific posture of the heart. 

Saying “moral judgments never matter” is a statement of philosophical nihilism or extreme relativism. It suggests that good and evil do not exist, or that human beings have no right to define them. Jesus, however, spent His entire ministry making razor-sharp moral distinctions—calling out the hypocrisy of the Pharisees, driving money-changers from the temple, and commanding people to "go and sin no more." 

The difference between His command and moral relativism lies in the vast chasm between **discernment** and the **appetite to condemn**.

### Discernment: The Faculty of Seeing
Discernment is the ability to perceive, distinguish, and evaluate. It is a necessary cognitive and spiritual faculty. 

*   **It is observational:** Discernment simply sees what is there. Just as a doctor diagnoses an illness not out of hatred for the patient, but to understand the reality of the disease, a discerning person recognizes a wrong action or a toxic situation.
*   **It is protective and practical:** Jesus Himself told His followers to be "wise as serpents and innocent as doves" (Matthew 10:16). Discernment tells you not to entrust your life savings to a known swindler, or not to let a destructive person unchecked into your community. 
*   **It separates the act from the identity:** Discernment says, "This action is wrong," or "This path is destructive." It evaluates the behavior or the fruit, but it does not claim to know the ultimate state of the person's soul.
*   **It leaves room for redemption:** Because discernment is focused on truth rather than finality, it naturally pairs with the second half of Luke 6:37: *“forgive, and ye shall be forgiven.”* Discernment identifies the fault; forgiveness leaves the door open for the person to change.

### The Appetite to Condemn: The Desire to Destroy
Condemnation, in the biblical sense, is not merely acknowledging that a wrong has occurred; it is a verdict of final rejection. An "appetite" to condemn implies that it is a craving—a desire that feeds the ego of the one doing the condemning.

*   **It is relational and ego-driven:** The appetite to condemn usually springs from a need to elevate oneself by pushing another down. When we condemn, we implicitly say, "I am fundamentally different from you—I am righteous, and you are worthless." This is the exact hypocrisy Jesus addresses later in Matthew 7: the person trying to remove a speck from their brother’s eye while ignoring the log in their own.
*   **It claims divine authority:** To condemn is to pronounce a final sentence on someone's worth, character, or ultimate destiny. It writes a person off as irredeemable. Jesus warns against this because final judgment belongs to God alone. When we condemn, we usurp a role we are not equipped for.
*   **It is terminal:** Discernment asks, "How do we address the wrong?" The appetite to condemn asks, "How do I dispose of the wrongdoer?" It seeks not restoration, but retribution and exclusion.

### The Key Distinctions

1.  **Motive:** Discernment is motivated by love, truth, and protection. The appetite to condemn is motivated by self-righteousness, fear, or malice.
2.  **Trajectory:** Discernment moves toward healing and restoration. Condemnation moves toward severing relationships and permanent alienation.
3.  **Self-Awareness:** Discernment is always accompanied by humility, recognizing one's own capacity for failure. Condemnation is always accompanied by blindness to one's own flaws.
4.  **Focus:** Discernment focuses on the *action* or the *impact*. Condemnation focuses on the *person's inherent worth*, reducing them to their worst moment.

### Conclusion
To say “moral judgments never matter” is to abandon the world to darkness, refusing to call evil by its name. But to give in to the appetite to condemn is to become the darkness ourselves, wielding self-righteousness as a weapon.

Luke 6:37 draws a line in the sand: You are allowed—indeed, required—to see the brokenness of the world. You must discern what is harmful, unjust, and untrue. But you must not cross the line into pronouncing a final, damning verdict on the broken people within it. Because, as Jesus warns, the measure you use to dispose of others will be the measure used to dispose of you.
