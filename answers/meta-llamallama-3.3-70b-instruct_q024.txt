The passage from Ezekiel 36:26-27 highlights a significant distinction between external compliance and internal transformation. External compliance refers to outwardly following rules, regulations, or statutes without a genuine change in one's nature or heart. This can be seen in religious or legal contexts where individuals might conform to expectations or laws without a deep, personal commitment or transformation.

On the other hand, receiving a "new heart" symbolizes an inner, profound transformation. This transformation involves a change in one's desires, motivations, and character, leading to a genuine commitment to follow God's statutes and judgments not out of obligation or fear, but out of a newfound nature that inclines towards righteousness and obedience. This new heart is not just about behavior modification but a change in the core of one's being, affecting how one thinks, feels, and acts.

The description of hardness as a "stony heart" that must be replaced with a "heart of flesh" is metaphorical and highlights the concept of spiritual hardness or callousness. In biblical contexts, the heart is often associated with the seat of emotions, will, and intellect, rather than just the physical organ. A "stony heart" represents a condition where an individual is unresponsive to spiritual truths, hardened against God's will, and perhaps resistant to change or repentance. This hardness is likened to stone, suggesting a rigidity and lack of responsiveness or feeling, contrasting with the pliability and sensitivity of flesh.

The use of this metaphor suggests that spiritual hardness is not just a superficial condition but a deep-seated issue that requires a fundamental transformation for change to occur. It's not about just "softening" one's attitude but undergoing a transformation that affects the very nature of one's being. This imagery implies that the process of sanctification or spiritual growth involves not just learning new rules or behaviors, but experiencing a radical, internal renovation that enables one to live according to God's will from the heart, rather than just superficially adhering to external standards.

In essence, the distinction between external compliance and receiving a new heart lies in the depth and nature of the transformation. External compliance may lead to outward righteousness, but it does not necessarily touch the inner man. Receiving a new heart, as promised in Ezekiel, signifies an internal transformation that leads to genuine, heartfelt obedience and a deep-seated desire to follow God's statutes and judgments, rather than mere external compliance.
