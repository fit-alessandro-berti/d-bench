The elder brother’s deepest problem is **not primarily his anger or envy, but a fundamental misunderstanding of his relationship with his father** — a relationship he sees as one of merit-based transaction rather than sonship grounded in love and grace.  

His speech reveals:  

1. **A transactional view of obedience** — “I serve you… I never transgressed… yet you never gave me a kid.” He views years of duty as currency that should have earned celebration and reward.  
2. **Resentment of grace** — He focuses on his brother’s unworthiness (“devoured your living with harlots”) and cannot rejoice in the restoration of a lost son, because his sense of fairness is violated.  
3. **Failure to recognize his own standing** — The father’s tender reply (“Son, you are always with me, and all that I have is yours”) highlights that the elder brother already possessed everything, yet lived like a servant.  
4. **A heart closed to mercy** — His refusal to join the feast shows discomfort not just with his brother, but with the father’s freely given love, which threatens his worldview where obedience earns favor.  

Thus, the core issue is **pride in his own righteousness** (Luke 18:9–14 comes to mind), which makes him unable to receive grace for himself and unwilling to celebrate grace for another. It is a spiritual blindness to the father’s heart — a problem deeper than anger or wounded fairness.
