Based on the text of Matthew 6:1 and its immediate context within the Sermon on the Mount, the moral danger lies not in **visibility itself**, but in the **craving to be seen** (the motive).

Here is an analysis of why the distinction matters:

### 1. The Grammatical Focus on Intent
The crucial phrase in the verse is "**to be seen of them**." In the original Greek, this construction (*pros to theathenai autois*) indicates purpose or intent. Jesus is not issuing a blanket prohibition against performing acts of charity in the presence of others; He is prohibiting performing them *for the purpose* of gaining an audience.

If visibility itself were the sin, the command would simply be "do not do your alms before men." By adding the qualifier "to be seen of them," Jesus identifies the heart's orientation as the locus of the moral failure.

### 2. The Contextual Tension (Matthew 5:16 vs. 6:1)
A few chapters earlier in the same sermon, Jesus gives a command that seems to contradict a literal ban on public goodness:
> "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven." (Matthew 5:16)

In 5:16, visibility is commanded. In 6:1, visibility is warned against. The only variable that changes between these two commands is the **end goal**:
*   **Matthew 5:16:** Be seen so that God is glorified.
*   **Matthew 6:1:** Do not be seen so that *you* are glorified.

This confirms that the moral danger is not the public nature of the act, but the appropriation of glory that belongs to God.

### 3. The Economy of Reward
The verse concludes with a warning: "otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven." This suggests a spiritual economy where reward is singular based on the audience you are playing to.

Jesus expands on this in the following verses (6:2–4), describing those who seek public praise as "hypocrites" (a word originally meaning "stage actor"). An actor performs to receive applause from the audience. Jesus argues that if you perform righteousness to receive applause from men, you have received your payment in full (human praise). There is nothing left for God to reward. The moral danger is **exhausting your reward** on temporary human approval rather than storing it up with God.

### 4. Visibility as a Temptation, Not a Sin
While visibility is not inherently sinful, this verse acknowledges that public goodness is **morally risky**. It acts as a trap for the ego. When an act of charity is public, it creates an opportunity for pride. Therefore, while the *craving* is the sin, the *visibility* is the temptation that often fuels the craving.

### Conclusion
In Matthew 6:1, the problem is **the craving to be seen**.

Visibility is morally neutral; it becomes dangerous only when it becomes the objective of the action. The verse teaches that true righteousness is defined by who you are trying to please: if you act for an audience of men, you are an actor seeking praise; if you act for an audience of One (God), you are a child seeking a reward from the Father.
