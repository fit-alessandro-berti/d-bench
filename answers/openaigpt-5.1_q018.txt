In Ephesians 4:31–32, “bitterness” and “forgiveness” are set over against each other because Paul is contrasting two *basic ways of relating to people*—two “inner climates” of the heart that inevitably shape speech, behavior, and community life.

### 1. What “bitterness” means here

The word translated “bitterness” is Greek *pikría*, which in this context doesn’t mean just “feeling hurt” once. It carries the idea of:

- a settled, sour spirit toward someone  
- deep, brooding resentment  
- hostility that has “set in” and hardened

That’s why it heads the list: “bitterness, and wrath, and anger, and clamour, and evil speaking, with all malice.” Bitterness is like the **root**, and the others are its **fruit**:

- *Wrath / anger* – emotional flare-ups and smoldering hostility
- *Clamor* – loud conflict, verbal fighting
- *Evil speaking* – slander, cutting words
- *Malice* – ill will, the desire to hurt or see someone hurt

Bitterness here is less a passing feeling and more a **chronic posture of the heart** that feeds those other sins.

### 2. Why it’s placed opposite forgiveness

Verse 32 answers verse 31: what do you do instead of bitterness?

> “Be kind… tenderhearted… forgiving one another, even as God in Christ forgave you.”

Forgiveness in Paul is not merely “deciding not to feel upset”; it is:

- releasing the claim to repayment or vengeance  
- reorienting your posture toward the person  
- imitating how God has related to you in Christ

So the contrast is:

- **Bitterness** – holding the debt, rehearsing the wrong, keeping the injury alive
- **Forgiveness** – releasing the debt, refusing to nurse the wrong, actively moving toward mercy

They are mutually exclusive postures: you cannot at the same time cling to bitterness and genuinely practice forgiveness “as God in Christ forgave you.” That’s why they are placed so sharply over against one another.

### 3. Is bitterness a feeling, habit, posture, or something contagious?

The text assumes a kind of progression:

- It **begins** as a response to hurt (a feeling).
- It **hardens** into a settled **disposition** (posture).
- It **expresses** itself in patterns of speech and behavior (habit).
- It **spreads** through a community (contagious).

Other biblical texts talk this way too. Hebrews 12:15 warns against a “root of bitterness” that springs up and “troubles you, and by it many be defiled.” That is communal language: bitterness is seen as **infectious**, not just private.

So in Ephesians 4:

- Bitterness is treated primarily as a **deep, chosen posture**—a heart-position you either keep or put away.
- That posture produces the visible behaviors listed with it.
- And because the whole section (4:25–32) is about life together in the body of Christ, Paul is especially concerned with its **contagious, community-destroying** effects.

### 4. Why the Christ-centered grounding matters

The last phrase is crucial: “even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you.”

Paul’s logic is:

- God had every right to hold your sins against you.
- Instead, for Christ’s sake, he released the debt and moved toward you in mercy.
- Therefore, to cling to bitterness toward others is to live **against** the very forgiveness that defines your own standing before God.

So bitterness and forgiveness aren’t just psychological opposites; they are **theological opposites**: one denies in practice what the other proclaims—namely, God’s forgiving grace in Christ.

To sum up: bitterness here is a hardened, resentful posture of the heart that issues in destructive habits and infects community life. Forgiveness is the Christ-shaped alternative posture. The text pits them directly against each other because you cannot live out God’s forgiveness toward others while continuing to nourish bitterness toward them.
