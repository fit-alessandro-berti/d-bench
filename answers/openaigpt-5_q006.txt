Short answer:
- The “self” Jesus calls us to deny is not your God‑given personhood but the self as master—your autonomous, self‑saving, self‑centering project (what Scripture elsewhere calls the “old self” or “flesh”). It’s the ego that insists on defining good and evil on its own terms, guarding status, comfort, and control.
- It sounds liberating to those tired of carrying that ego—of proving worth, managing image, and being ruled by compulsions—and offensive to those committed to autonomy, self‑definition, and winning on the world’s terms.

A bit more detail:

What “deny yourself” means
- Allegiance: Renounce the claim to be your own lord and place yourself under Jesus’ authority. It’s self‑disowning as ruler, not erasing your personhood.
- Desires and agenda: Say “no” to impulses, ambitions, and identities that compete with Jesus and his kingdom; say “yes” to his way even when it costs.
- Cross‑bearing: In the first century, “take up your cross” meant walking the path of shame and possible death. It’s a willingness to accept loss—reputation, security, even life—because Jesus is worth more.
- Life paradox: “Whoever would save his life will lose it… whoever loses his life for me and the gospel will save it.” The “life” (psyche) you try to secure on your own terms slips through your fingers; the life you entrust to Jesus is given back, deeper and eternal.

What it is not
- Not self‑hatred, personality erasure, or refusal of proper self‑care.
- Not asceticism for its own sake.
- Not submitting to abuse. The cross is Christ‑shaped obedience chosen for his sake, not enabling someone else’s sin.

Why it feels liberating
- Freedom from the tyranny of ego, comparison, and image‑management.
- Release from anxiety about securing worth and future—you receive identity as gift, not project.
- Sins and addictions lose their claim; suffering is not meaningless but shared with Christ.
- A clear purpose: love of God and neighbor, not endless self‑curation.

Why it feels offensive
- It confronts our culture’s creed of autonomy and self‑definition.
- It demands exclusive loyalty to Jesus and names some cherished desires “no.”
- The cross looks like weakness and failure; it insults pride and dismantles self‑reliance.
- It says you cannot save yourself—grace is humbling before it is healing.

In practice, denying the false self and finding true life in Christ looks like daily, concrete obedience—truth‑telling when a lie would protect you, generosity that costs, sexual integrity, forgiving enemies, confessing sin, enduring scorn for Jesus’ name. As many have put it, when Christ calls a person, he bids them come and die—and in that dying, they finally live.
