Based on Matthew 6:1 and the broader context of Jesus’ teaching in the Sermon on the Mount, the moral danger lies squarely in **the craving to be seen**, not the visibility of the act itself.

Here is a breakdown of why the motive is the central issue, and how this distinction shapes the moral meaning of the verse.

### 1. The Grammar of Intent
In the Greek text, the key phrase is *pros theathēnai autois* ("to be seen of them"). The construction indicates **purpose** or **goal**.

Jesus is not prohibiting actions that happen to be seen; He is prohibiting actions performed **in order to be seen**. The moral quality of the act is determined by the aim of the heart. If the aim is the applause of the audience, the act has been transactionally spent.

### 2. The Contrast with Matthew 5:16
To understand why visibility is not the problem, one must look just a few verses earlier. In Matthew 5:16, Jesus commands:
> “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven.”

Here, Jesus explicitly commands public visibility. The light is not meant to be hidden under a bushel. Therefore, the problem in Matthew 6:1 cannot be that the act is public.

The difference between the two verses lies in the **direction of the glory**:
*   **Matthew 5:** Visible works $\rightarrow$ Glory goes to **God**.
*   **Matthew 6:** Visible works $\rightarrow$ Glory is absorbed by the **Self**.

### 3. The Nature of the Danger: "Theatrical Righteousness"
The moral danger is what the church fathers and theologians have often called *vainglory* or *hypocrisy*.

When the "craving to be seen" is the motive, the act of goodness is no longer an act of worship or love; it becomes a **performance**. The Greek word for "hypocrite" (*hypokritēs*) used later in this chapter literally refers to a stage actor. The danger is that a person creates a pious persona—a mask—to gain social capital, religious status, or admiration from the audience, while the heart remains detached from God.

This is morally dangerous because it is a form of **idolatry**. The performer has swapped the Creator for the creature (the audience). They are seeking their validation from the court of public opinion rather than the court of Heaven.

### 4. The Issue of Reward
Jesus frames this issue as a transaction. He warns that if you give alms to be seen, "ye have no reward of your Father."

Jesus is essentially saying: **You get what you pay for.**
*   If you pay for the applause of men with your good works, you have received your reward in full (the attention you craved). The transaction is closed; there is no remaining spiritual merit.
*   If you give in secret (motivated by love for God and neighbor), you are laying up treasure in heaven.

### Conclusion
The verse suggests that public goodness is morally dangerous only when the audience becomes the **primary beneficiary of the glory**.

If the problem were visibility itself, the only moral option would be total isolation and secrecy, which would contradict the calling of believers to be "salt and light" in the world. The danger is not the gaze of the neighbor, but the **craving of the ego**.
