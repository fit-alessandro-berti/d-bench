**The image is sharp because of its extreme, absurd disproportion and the claustrophobic logic of vision: a tiny speck (mote) noticed in another’s eye while a massive timber (beam/plank) remains jammed in one’s own.**

It works through vivid hyperbole and physical impossibility. You cannot literally see clearly—let alone perform delicate eye surgery—with a log protruding from your face. The issue is not merely “you also have faults,” but “you are so impaired by a far larger obstruction that your scrutiny of the other person is blind, invasive, and ridiculous.” The visual comedy and moral horror reinforce each other: the confronter is both incompetent and self-important. The language is direct (“Thou hypocrite”) and sequential.

### How the passage critiques hypocrisy without forbidding moral correction
- **Hypocrisy condemned**: The problem is selective attention and false superiority—fixing others while ignoring (or refusing to acknowledge) one’s own larger failings. The beam is not a small oversight; it is the very thing that destroys clear perception and moral authority. Pointing out the mote while nursing the beam is exposed as pretense.
- **Correction still permitted—and even expected—once priorities are right**: The text does not say “never remove anyone’s mote.” It says *first* remove your own beam, *then* “shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote out of thy brother’s eye.” Self-examination and repentance restore sight and legitimacy; only afterward is fraternal correction appropriate. The goal of clearer vision is still mutual restoration, not permanent silence or moral relativism.

In short, the passage attacks blind, hypocritical judgment while protecting the possibility of lucid, humble help. The same metaphor that humiliates the hypocrite also charts the path for genuine moral agency: remove the beam, recover sight, then act.
