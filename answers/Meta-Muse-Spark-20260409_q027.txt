Short answer: **It isn’t false doctrine.** Jesus doesn’t say the Pharisee lied about fasting or tithing. What condemns him is the cluster of **pride + comparison + contempt + self-display** — all of it built on trusting in his own righteousness instead of God’s mercy. 

Luke actually tells you the target right in verse 9: *“to some who were confident of their own righteousness and looked down on everyone else.”* The parable illustrates that diagnosis.

### What condemns the Pharisee

You can break it into 4 interlocking moves:

| **Issue** | **What he does** | **Why it condemns** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Trust in self-righteousness** | “God, I thank thee that I am not as other men” | He treats his virtue as his own achievement. Righteousness becomes a resume to present to God. |
| **Comparison** | “...extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even as this publican” | He defines his standing by being better than others. His identity requires someone to be below him. |
| **Contempt** | “or even as this publican” | He despises the very person standing next to him in the Temple. Love of neighbor is absent. |
| **Virtue as self-display** | “I fast twice in the week, I give tithes...” | Good deeds become performance. He “prayed thus with himself” — the prayer is an audience with himself, not God. |

So it’s not *one* of those things. The engine is pride: *“trusted in themselves that they were righteous.”* Comparison, contempt, and self-display are the exhaust. The doctrine can be technically correct while the heart posture damns the whole thing.

### Why the tax collector’s posture is “truer”

The publican/tax collector was despised in 1st-century Judea — collaborators with Rome, often corrupt. Nobody expected him to be the example. But look at his posture:

- **Distance**: “standing afar off” — He knows he doesn’t belong up front. No presumption.
- **Physical humility**: “would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven” — Body language of shame and reverence.
- **Contrition**: “smote upon his breast” — A gesture of grief over his own heart.
- **Prayer content**: “God be merciful to me a sinner” — In Greek, *hilasthēti moi* — “be propitiated/merciful to me.” He asks for atonement, not a performance review.

**The key difference:** The Pharisee asks for nothing because he thinks he needs nothing. The tax collector asks for mercy because he knows he needs everything.

Jesus’ verdict: *“this man went down to his house justified rather than the other.”* Justified = declared righteous by God. It’s a courtroom term. The one who pleaded guilty walked out acquitted. The one who pled his resume walked out condemned.

### The theological point

The parable subverts how religion tends to work. Human systems rank people by visible virtue. Jesus says God’s kingdom works by grace, and grace can only be received by empty hands.

So what condemns the Pharisee? Using virtue to establish distance from others and closeness to God on his own terms.  
What justifies the tax collector? Throwing himself entirely on God’s mercy with no comparisons, no resume, no contempt for anyone else.

The line that sums it up comes right after: *“everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, and he who humbles himself will be exalted.”*Luke 18:14
