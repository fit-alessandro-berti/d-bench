This is an excellent and profound question that gets to the heart of biblical repentance. The pairing of **“way” (דֶּרֶךְ, *derek*)** and **“thoughts” (מַחְשָׁבָה, *maḥšāḇâ*)** in Isaiah 55:7 is deliberate and paints a complete picture of what it means to turn back to God.

Let's break it down:

### 1. The Two Dimensions of Repentance
*   **Outward Conduct (“his way”):** This refers to a person’s actions, habits, lifestyle, and visible behavior—their “path” in life. Forsaking one’s “way” means a tangible change in how one lives, treats others, and directs one’s energy. It’s the external fruit of the heart.
*   **Inward Imagination (“his thoughts”):** This goes deeper, to the internal source of the “way.” It encompasses desires, intentions, motives, fantasies, and the fundamental orientation of the mind and heart. Forsaking one’s “thoughts” means a transformation of one’s inner world—the purposes, plans, and perspectives that drive one’s actions.

### 2. Why Both Are Necessary: The Root and the Fruit
The prophet connects them because **you cannot have one without the other in genuine repentance.**
*   **Outward change without inward change is hypocrisy.** It’s merely behavioral modification, a social performance that leaves the heart unchanged. The “way” might look different temporarily, but the “thoughts” will eventually cause a return to the old path.
*   **Inward change without outward change is an illusion.** True conviction and a new way of thinking will inevitably express themselves in new actions. As a famous proverb says, “As a person **thinks** in their heart, so are they” (Proverbs 23:7). The inner “thoughts” are the engine; the outer “way” is the direction the vehicle travels.

Therefore, Isaiah calls for a **comprehensive revolution** of the person—both the internal compass and the external journey.

### 3. What This Implies About Mercy
This comprehensive view of repentance directly shapes our understanding of God’s mercy:
*   **Mercy Meets Us at the Point of Need:** God’s mercy isn’t offered to an abstract sinner, but to a whole person—someone tangled in both sinful actions *and* sinful patterns of thinking. His pardon addresses the entire problem.
*   **Mercy Enables the Transformation It Demands:** The call to forsake both ways and thoughts is not a prerequisite we must fulfill *before* approaching God. The command “Seek the Lord… Call upon Him” comes *first*. We come to God *in order to* receive the power and mercy to change. His nearness (“while he is near”) and His abundant pardon are what make the radical forsaking of our old self possible. Mercy is not the reward for repentance; it is the enabling grace *for* repentance.
*   **Mercy is Relational and Restorative:** The promise “he will have mercy… he will abundantly pardon” (v. 7) is given in the context of returning “unto the Lord.” This isn’t a legal transaction but a relational restoration. God pardons so that the relationship can be renewed. The forgiven person, having received mercy, is now free to walk in a new “way” guided by new “thoughts” aligned with God’s character.

### Summary
In essence, Isaiah 55:6-7 presents repentance not as mere guilt management, but as **a holistic re-orientation of life.** It involves turning from both the **external trajectory** (way) and the **internal source** (thoughts) of one’s rebellion. This underscores the gravity of sin—it corrupts both action and intention.

Yet, it simultaneously magnifies the glory of God’s mercy. His pardon is so “abundant” that it can cleanse both the stained hands and the corrupt heart. It is a mercy that invites us to come as we are, with our flawed ways and troubled thoughts, and promises to transform us from the inside out. The good news is that the One who demands such complete repentance is precisely the One whose mercy is deep enough to accomplish it.
