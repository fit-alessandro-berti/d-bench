Great question. In this parable, Jesus is not condemning **fasting, tithing, or moral seriousness** themselves. He is exposing a **heart posture**.

### What condemns the Pharisee?

Luke gives the key before the parable even starts:

- they **“trusted in themselves that they were righteous”**
- and **“despised others”** (v.9)

So it’s not mainly “false doctrine” in the abstract; it’s a spiritual orientation made of several intertwined sins:

1. **Self-righteous trust**  
   He grounds his acceptance before God in his own performance.

2. **Comparative righteousness**  
   “I am not as other men… or even as this publican.”  
   His identity is built by ranking himself above others.

3. **Contempt**  
   He doesn’t just confess virtue; he uses another person as a foil.

4. **Religious self-display before God**  
   His “prayer” is basically a self-report of merit, not a plea for mercy.

So yes—**pride, comparison, contempt, and virtue-as-self-display** are all present, and they all flow from the root: **trusting self rather than God’s mercy**.

---

### Why is the tax collector spiritually truer?

His body language and words reveal reality-based faith:

- **“standing afar off”** → awareness of unworthiness  
- **“would not lift up…eyes”** → reverence and shame, not performance  
- **“smote upon his breast”** → grief over sin  
- **“God, be merciful to me, the sinner”** (literally very emphatic) → total dependence on mercy

He does what the Pharisee does not: he brings **truth about himself** and **need before God**.  
That is why Jesus says he went home **justified**: not because sin is trivial, but because he receives righteousness as mercy, not as wages for moral achievement.

---

In short: the Pharisee’s problem is not that he is “too good,” but that he treats goodness as a claim on God and a weapon against others. The tax collector is “truer” because repentance and trust in mercy are the only honest footing before God.
