**It is difficult to take seriously because it systematically contradicts our instincts for reciprocity, status, justice-as-retribution, and group protection, while demanding a form of agency that looks like weakness until it is practiced.** It is not primarily sentimental, not reducible to strategy (though it can have strategic effects), and is best understood as a deeper form of moral strength—specifically the strength to refuse letting an enemy dictate the moral quality of your soul or actions.

### Why it resists being taken seriously
- **Instinct and emotional wiring.** Humans are quick to categorize “enemy,” magnify their threat or malice, and demand payback. The verses command the opposite directional energy (bless, do good, pray) toward those who are active agents of harm. That feels like betrayal of self or tribe, or like permission for further abuse. Contempt and righteous anger feel clarifying and energetic; enemy-love feels like self-erasure or masochism until examined carefully.
- **It collides with common notions of justice.** Most moral systems (and legal ones) treat desert and proportionality as central. “Love your enemies” does not abolish accountability, justice, or self-defense, but it refuses to let enmity become the organizing principle of the self. People hear it as “ignore wrongdoers” or “pretend everything is fine,” both of which are absurd and therefore casually dismissed.
- **Cultural scripts reward the opposite.** Stories, politics, and online discourse celebrate victory, humiliation of the out-group, and moral clarity that comes from identifying villains. A command to bless those who curse you sounds like noble-sounding coping language for losers, or a luxury belief for the safe. The Sermon on the Mount’s “that ye may be the children of your Father” framing (impartial providence: sun and rain on both good and evil) further undercuts the zero-sum scoring people prefer.
- **Risk of sentimentalization or weaponization.** When reduced to warm feelings or vague niceness, it becomes unserious. When weaponized (“you must affirm me while I harm you”), it becomes opportunistic. Both corruptions make the actual claim easier to mock.
- **It is hard to observe cleanly.** Genuine instances (consistent non-retaliatory goodwill under pressure) often look quiet or long-term; theatrical versions look performative. Failures are loud and attributed to the ideal itself.

### Sentiment, strategy, or deeper strength?
- **Not primarily sentimental.** The verbs are active and costly: do good, bless, pray. Sentiment is optional and often unreliable; the point is not how you feel but what stance you take and what you refuse to mirror. Feeling affection for someone actively persecuting you may never arrive—the demand is oriented action that refuses to let their hostility set your moral register.
- **Strategic effects exist but are secondary.** Non-retaliation, generosity under fire, and refusal to dehumanize can shame aggressors, create unexpected openings, reduce cycles of revenge, win over onlookers, or preserve coalitions (classically noted by figures who practiced forms of it). Jesus’ rationale is not “this will work for you socially or politically.” The stated purpose is character alignment with a God who sustains the cosmos impartially rather than running a patronage system of favors for the worthy. Treating it only as clever tactics collapses it into calculation, which is fragile when the tyrant does not respond or when the cost is high.
- **Deeper moral strength.** Enemy-love requires:
  - **Self-command** stronger than reactive rage or tribal solidarity.
  - **Imagination** that refuses to reduce the enemy to pure villainy or pure victimhood (they remain made in the image of the same Father who sends the rain).
  - **Asymmetry as power, not submission**: you stop letting their evil write the script of your response. You retain the capacity to pursue justice, protect the vulnerable, and set boundaries—all while refusing the complementary evil of hatred or curse-for-curse.
  - **Theological or teleological weight**: it is training for a different allegiance (children of this Father), proving that value is not conferred by mutual liking or utility. It is closer to courage than to softness: the courage of non-mirroring when mirroring would be easy and socially rewarded.

In short, it is hard to take seriously precisely because it is not a mood, a PR strategy, or a sentimental anesthetic. It is a claim about the kind of agency and likeness-to-God that human beings are capable of—one that treats the enemy’s hostility as real and troubling without allowing it the final word over your own moral form. People dismiss it because practicing it would require them to become less predictable, less vengeful, and more free than ordinary reciprocity allows. The difficulty is evidence of its demand, not its falsity.
