To understand what condemns the Pharisee and justifies the tax collector in Luke 18:9–14, we have to look at the framing of the parable and the contrast between how the two men approach God. 

The condemnation of the Pharisee is not due to a single flaw, but a toxic synergy of **pride, comparison, contempt, and the use of virtue as self-display**. Here is a breakdown of how each plays out, followed by why the tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer.

### What Condemns the Pharisee?

**1. Pride and Trust in Self (The Root)**
Verse 9 states Jesus is speaking to those who "trusted in themselves that they were righteous." The Pharisee’s fundamental error is theological anthropology—he believes he can generate his own righteousness and present it to God as a debt God owes him. He prays "with himself" (v. 11), indicating an echo chamber of self-congratulation. He does not actually need God; he uses God as an audience for his own greatness.

**2. Comparison and Contempt (The Fruit)**
The Pharisee’s righteousness is entirely relative. He says, "I thank thee, that I am not as other men are... or even as this publican." He does not measure himself against the perfect holiness of God; he measures himself against the worst examples of humanity. Furthermore, his comparison breeds contempt. The text notes he "despised others." Contempt is the natural byproduct of a pride that needs a lesser "other" to validate its own greatness.

**3. Virtue as Self-Display**
The Pharisee’s good works—fasting twice a week and tithing—are not inherently bad; in fact, they are commendable disciplines. What condemns him is that he weaponizes them as a resume. He treats God like an employer reviewing his credentials. He has no concept of grace because he believes he has perfectly fulfilled the law under his own power. His virtues are sterile because they have not produced humility or love; they have only produced arrogance.

*(Note: It is not "false doctrine" in the sense of believing in a false god or denying scripture. The Pharisee has orthodox theology regarding moral behavior, but his *practical* theology—how one stands before a holy God—is deeply flawed.)*

### Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?

The tax collector’s physical posture is a direct external manifestation of an internal spiritual reality. His approach is treated as spiritually truer for several profound reasons:

**1. Absolute Honesty**
The tax collector does not compare himself to the Pharisee (which, ironically, he would have won). He compares himself to God. His physical distance ("standing afar off") and downward gaze ("would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven") demonstrate a visceral awareness of the infinite gap between God's holiness and human sinfulness. Spiritual truth begins with honesty about one's actual condition. The Pharisee is self-deceived; the tax collector is lucid.

**2. Sorrow for Sin**
The act of "smote upon his breast" was a traditional sign of intense grief and anguish. The Pharisee felt no grief, only satisfaction. The tax collector recognizes that his sin is not just a breach of rules, but a personal offense against a holy God. True repentance requires a broken spirit, which God does not despise (Psalm 51:17).

**3. Reliance on Atonement, not Merit**
The tax collector’s prayer, "God be merciful to me a sinner," relies on a specific theological concept. The Greek word used for "merciful" here is *hilaskomai*, which is directly related to the concept of propitiation or atonement (the same word is used in Hebrews 9:5 for the "mercy seat" in the temple). 
The tax collector is essentially saying, "God, provide an atonement for me, a sinner." He brings no works to offer; he only brings his need. He appeals entirely to God’s unmerited grace. 

### Conclusion
The Pharisee is condemned because he views God as a judge to be impressed by human achievement, looking down on his fellow man in the process. The tax collector is justified because he views God as his only hope for rescue. 

Jesus concludes that the tax collector "went down to his house justified." Justification is a legal term meaning "declared righteous." The irony of the parable is that the man who tried to justify himself (the Pharisee) failed, while the man who admitted he had no justification (the tax collector) received it freely from God. The spiritually true posture is one of utter dependence on grace.
