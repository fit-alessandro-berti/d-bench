The short answer is *realistic*, and the distinction matters.

## Pessimism vs. realism about self-deception

A purely pessimistic reading would say: "Humans are rotten, end of story, so any hope of moral growth is naive." Jeremiah 17:9–10 is not quite doing that. The verb often rendered "desperately wicked" (*'āqār*) literally means **"incurably sick"** or "incurably wounded" — a medical metaphor, not just a moral one. (The Septuagint translates it with the word for *weak* or *ailing*.) The diagnosis is harsh, but it is a diagnosis pointing to a treatment — which arrives in v. 10: *God searches what we cannot.* The text sets up a problem and then immediately supplies the only adequate diagnostician.

So the move of the passage is:

1. We cannot fully know our own motives.
2. Therefore we should not pretend to.
3. Therefore we should invite the one who *can* to examine us (cf. Ps. 139:23–24; 1 Cor. 4:3–4; Heb. 4:12–13).

That is closer to realism than to cynicism. It is the Bible's version of what cognitive psychology now calls *motivated reasoning* — we are not neutral observers of ourselves; we are the interested party.

## How, then, should we talk about motives?

This is the harder question, and Jeremiah's own move suggests a few principles.

**1. Give up the demand for total self-transparency before speaking.**
A person is not required to have a fully clean, conscious account of their motives in order to act honestly. In pastoral or counseling contexts, demanding "tell me your *real* motive" can become a kind of inquisition that produces only more sophisticated self-deception. The text undercuts that demand at the root.

**2. Talk about motives in layers, not as singular.**
Self-deception thrives on clean, single-cause stories ("I did it for her good" / "I did it because I had to"). Honest speech about motives usually sounds like: *"I wanted X, and I also wanted Y, and I can't fully tell which was decisive, and I notice I'd prefer the nobler one to be the whole story."* The very fact that the cleaner story is more flattering is itself diagnostic.

**3. Trust *fruit* over claimed intention.**
Note where v. 10 lands: not on inner feeling, but on **"the fruit of his doings."** Motives can be indirectly tested by what an action actually produced — and not just by what the actor says it produced. This is why Jesus in the Sermon on the Mount keeps redirecting from the stated reason to the underlying orientation (anger, lust, the eye, the hand). He is not being perverse; he is bypassing a self-report he knows will be unreliable.

**4. Submit motives to a witness outside yourself.**
A friend, a confessor, a therapist, a community — the text implies the remedy is not better introspection but external searching. Not because another person sees you perfectly, but because another person's puzzlement is often the first signal that your self-narrative has gone smooth in the wrong places.

**5. End in prayer, not in self-confidence.**
The natural conclusion of Jeremiah 17:9–10 is not "therefore I will be more vigilant." It is *"Search me."* Which means the goal is not to become a better self-examiner — that is a losing game — but to be known by a God whose judgment is not distorted by self-interest. There is actually relief in this: you are not the final court of your own motives, and you don't have to be.

## A caution

One important guardrail: this passage can be *misused* as a club — "you just can't know your own heart, so trust the authority." That is not what Jeremiah is doing. He is warning the self-confident, not licensing the controlling. The whole arc of Jeremiah — toward the new covenant of Jer. 31:31–34 — is that the solution to a sick heart is not better surveillance but a transformed one. A right use of 17:9–10 should land people in grace, not in quiet despair about ever being legible to themselves or others.
