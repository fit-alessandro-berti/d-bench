In Ephesians 4:31–32, the Apostle Paul places bitterness and forgiveness in direct, stark opposition because they are mutually exclusive postures of the soul. You cannot hold onto both simultaneously; one will inevitably expel the other. 

To understand why they are contrasted so sharply—and to answer your question about whether bitterness is a feeling, a habit, a posture, or a contagion—we have to look at the nature of both words in their original context and in the broader logic of Paul’s argument.

### 1. Why Bitterness and Forgiveness Are Direct Opposites

**The Economics of Debt vs. Grace**
At its core, bitterness is an accounting term. It is the record of a wrong that has not been settled. When someone harms us, our sense of justice demands that a debt be paid. Bitterness is the refusal to tear up the invoice; it is holding the debt against the offender, demanding satisfaction.

Forgiveness (the Greek word *charizomai* used here) is the exact opposite. It literally means "to give grace" (*charis*). To forgive is to absorb the debt yourself, refusing to make the offender pay, and writing the loss off as grace. They are placed against each other because **bitterness demands payment, while forgiveness absorbs the cost.**

**The Theological Anchor**
Paul doesn’t just say "forgive because it’s nice." He gives a theological imperative: *"even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you."* The contrast is meant to invoke a sense of absurdity. If God, who is perfectly holy, chose to absorb the infinite debt of our sin through Christ, how absurd and arrogant is it for us—fellow sinners—to hold the finite debts of others against them? Bitterness is exposed here as spiritual hypocrisy; it is an attempt to claim grace for oneself while demanding law for one's neighbor.

### 2. Is Bitterness a Feeling, a Habit, a Posture, or a Contagion?

The short answer is: **It is all four, progressing from a feeling to a contagion, which is exactly why Paul commands us to eradicate it.**

**A Feeling (The Seed)**
Bitterness begins as a feeling. The Greek word used here is *pikria* (πικρία), which literally refers to a sharp, pungent, or acrid taste—like bile or undiluted wine. When you are wronged, the emotional taste is naturally bitter. Feeling the initial sting of betrayal, anger, or injustice is not a sin; it is a natural human reaction. 

**A Chosen Posture (The Root)**
However, Paul commands us to "put it away," which implies agency. You cannot easily command a feeling to stop, but you can command a posture to change. If bitterness is nursed, reflected upon, and justified, it transitions from a passing feeling to a chosen posture of the heart. You begin to define yourself by the wrong done to you. This is why the writer of Hebrews uses the exact same Greek word when he warns against the "root of bitterness" (Hebrews 12:15). A root is hidden, but it anchors the plant. A bitter posture becomes the lens through which you view the offender, and eventually, the world.

**A Habit (The Escalation)**
Look closely at the progression in verse 31: *"bitterness, and wrath, and anger, and clamour, and evil speaking... with all malice."* 
Paul shows us the anatomy of bitterness. It does not stay a silent, internal root. When a bitter posture is maintained, it becomes a habit that inevitably bears external fruit:
*   **Wrath and Anger:** The internal smoldering resentment boils over into active, hot-tempered rage.
*   **Clamour:** The anger moves from internal to vocal—shouting, arguing, and creating discord.
*   **Evil Speaking (Slander):** The habit of tearing the person down to others, assassinating their character to justify your bitterness.
*   **Malice:** The ultimate destination—the active desire to see the other person harmed or destroyed.

What started as a hurt feeling has now habituated the person into a destructive, malicious lifestyle.

**A Contagion (The Defilement)**
This brings us to the final, and perhaps most dangerous, aspect of bitterness: it is highly contagious. Paul is writing to a *church community*. Bitterness is never isolated. When you engage in "clamour" and "evil speaking," you are infecting others with your bitterness. You are pulling bystanders into your grievance, forcing them to take sides, and spreading the poison. 

Again, Hebrews 12:15 confirms this communal contagion: *"Looking diligently... lest any root of bitterness springing up trouble you, and thereby many be defiled."* The word "defiled" is a ceremonial term meaning "unclean." Bitterness spreads like a contagion, making the entire community spiritually sick and unfit for fellowship.

### The Cure: Tenderheartedness

Paul’s antidote to bitterness is not simply "stop feeling mad." It is *"be ye kind one to another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another."*

The word *tenderhearted* (euschymōn / eusplanchnos) literally means "having good bowels." In the ancient world, the bowels were considered the seat of compassion and deep feeling. To be tenderhearted is the exact opposite of a hard, calcified, bitter posture. It means keeping your heart soft and vulnerable, even after being wronged.

Paul recognizes that the only way to prevent bitterness from becoming a habit and a contagion is to cut it off at the root through forgiveness. Forgiveness is the act of taking the poison out of the wound before it infects the whole body. It is choosing to cancel the debt, not because the feeling of hurt has vanished, but because God in Christ canceled your infinitely greater debt first.
