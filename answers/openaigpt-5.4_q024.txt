External compliance is doing what is required from the outside. A “new heart” is inward transformation.

In Ezekiel 36:26–27, God is not merely saying, “I will make you behave better.” He is saying, “I will change the kind of person you are at the center.” That is the key difference.

## External compliance
External compliance means:

- obeying rules without loving them
- conforming in action while resisting inwardly
- doing the right thing from pressure, fear, habit, or social expectation
- appearing faithful while remaining unchanged within

A person can comply externally and still have a “stony heart.” In the Bible, the heart is not just emotion; it is the core of will, desire, thought, and moral response. So a hard heart is not simply a person who breaks rules openly. It can also be someone who keeps them outwardly but is inwardly unmoved, resistant, proud, or spiritually deadened.

## Receiving a “new heart”
Receiving a new heart means:

- new desires, not just new restraints
- responsiveness to God instead of resistance
- inward willingness instead of mere outward conformity
- obedience flowing from life within

That is why verse 27 says, “I will put my spirit within you, and cause you to walk in my statutes.” The obedience comes from God’s renewing work, not merely from stronger external control. The person does not just submit to God’s law as an outside demand; they are inwardly reoriented toward it.

## Why hardness is described like a material condition
The image of stone versus flesh is vivid because it shows that the problem is not superficial.

A stone heart suggests something:

- rigid
- cold
- unfeeling
- resistant to impression
- lifeless

A heart of flesh suggests something:

- living
- soft
- responsive
- capable of being moved
- open to God’s shaping

So hardness is described almost materially because sin is not treated as a minor defect or lack of information. It is a deep condition of the person. Stone does not “cooperate” into becoming flesh; it must be replaced. The metaphor emphasizes inability as well as resistance. God is not just repairing behavior; he is performing an act of re-creation.

## Why replacement, not mere adjustment
The passage says, “I will take away” and “I will give.” That language is radical. It suggests:

- the old heart is inadequate, not merely underperforming
- the problem is internal and structural
- true obedience requires divine intervention
- renewal is a gift before it becomes a practice

In other words, external compliance can polish conduct, but it cannot create spiritual life. A new heart is needed because the issue is not merely what people do, but what they love, trust, and are inclined toward.

## In short
External compliance changes behavior from the outside in.  
A new heart changes the person from the inside out.

Hardness is described like a material condition because Ezekiel is portraying the human problem as deep, resistant, and lifeless—something only God can remove and replace with living responsiveness to him.
