**Short answer:**  
The danger is not the *visibility* of the good deed itself, but the *desire* to be seen and praised for it. In Matthew 6:1 Jesus warns against doing “your alms before men, **to be seen of them**.” The problem is the **craving for human recognition** – the motive of gaining applause or honor from others. When that becomes the purpose, the act loses its spiritual worth and you forfeit “the reward of your Father which is in heaven.”

---

### Why the motive matters

1. **The text’s wording** – The Greek phrase “πρὸς τὸ θεαθῆναι αὐτοῖς” (pros to theathenai autois) literally means “for the purpose of being seen by them.” It is a *telos* (goal‑oriented) clause: the good deed is performed **in order to** be observed. That intention is what Jesusrebukes.

2. **The surrounding context** – The same pattern appears in the verses about prayer (6:5) and fasting (6:16). In each case the “hypocrites” (actors) love to perform their religious observance **publicly** “to be seen by men.” The repeated phrase shows the issue is not “public” versus “private” per se, but the **desire to be honored by people**.

3. **Reward theology** – Jesus says, “otherwise you have no reward of your Father which is in heaven.” The “reward” here is not a legalistic payout but the relational *presence* and *blessing* of God. When you already receive the praise of men, you have “received your reward” (Matt 6:2, 5, 16). The danger is that you trade the *lasting* reward of divine fellowship for the *fleeting* applause of people.

4. **Moral psychology** – Seeking human approval turns a good act into a **self‑glorifying performance**. It fosters pride, self‑righteousness, and a “show‑boat” spirituality that is more about reputation than love. That self‑centeredness corrupts the moral quality of the deed, making it “dangerous” because it can:

   - **Undermine genuine love** for neighbor (the good is done for the giver’s glory, not the receiver’s need).  
   - **Create a false sense of righteousness** (“I’m a good person because people see me giving”).  
   - **Block spiritual growth** – you become dependent on external validation rather than internal conformity to God’s will.

---

### Visibility itself is not the problem

The rest of the New Testament shows that good works *can* be done publicly without violating this principle:

- **Paul** tells the Philippian church to “let your **light** shine before men, that they may see your good deeds and **glorify your Father in heaven**” (Matt 5:16). The purpose is *God’s* glory, not the individual’s.  
- **The early believers** “sold their possessions … and **distributed to each as anyone had need**” (Acts 2:45). Their generosity was visible, but the motive was communal care, not personal acclaim.

Thus, a public act can be **morally neutral** (or even praise‑worthy) when it is done **for the sake of others and for God’s honor**, not for the sake of being seen and praised.

---

### Bottom line

- **Visibility** is not inherently morally dangerous.  
- **The craving to be seen** (the desire for human applause, honor, or recognition) *is* the danger, because it shifts the motive from love of God and neighbor to self‑glory, empties the act of its spiritual reward, and cultivates a proud,表演性的 (performance‑oriented) heart.

In Jesus’ teaching, the heart‑issue matters more than the outward setting. The “danger” lies in turning a good deed into a billboard for yourself, thereby trading the “reward of your Father” for the fleeting applause of the crowd.
