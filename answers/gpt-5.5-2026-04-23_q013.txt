Amos rejects the worship so sharply because the issue is not an imbalance between “religion” and “ethics.” The issue is that the worship itself has become false.

In Amos’s context, Israel is maintaining impressive religious observances—feasts, assemblies, sacrifices, songs—while tolerating or practicing injustice: exploitation of the poor, corrupt courts, dishonest gain, luxury built on oppression, and indifference to the vulnerable. Amos is saying that worship offered from such a life is not merely incomplete; it is fraudulent.

The sharpness of the passage comes from several related ideas:

1. **Ritual is being used to cover covenant betrayal**

Israel is acting as though sacrifices and festivals can compensate for injustice. But in the biblical covenant, worship of God and justice toward neighbor are not separable. To praise the God of righteousness while crushing the poor is to lie about who God is.

So God says, in effect: “Do not bring Me songs that deny Me in practice.”

2. **The worshipers are treating God as if He can be managed**

The people seem to assume that if they perform the right religious duties—offerings, music, festivals—God will be pleased, regardless of their social behavior. That is a kind of spiritual bribery: using worship as a transaction to secure divine favor while refusing divine character.

Amos exposes the fraud of thinking God wants ceremony more than obedience.

3. **The worship is aesthetically impressive but morally repulsive**

The passage mentions feast days, assemblies, burnt offerings, grain offerings, peace offerings, songs, and instruments. In other words, the worship is full, elaborate, and probably beautiful. But God calls it hateful noise because the beauty of the ceremony is contradicted by the ugliness of the people’s lives.

The problem is not that they sing. The problem is that their songs have become the soundtrack of injustice.

4. **God does not merely want “more justice added to worship”; He wants worship made truthful**

Amos does not say, “Keep your worship, but add a little concern for the poor.” He says:

> “Let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream.”

That image suggests something public, powerful, cleansing, and continuous. Justice is not a side project. It is the necessary evidence that Israel’s worship is directed to the true God.

5. **The phrase “your feast days” is important**

God does not say “My feasts” but “your feast days.” That implies that their religious life has been taken over by self-interest. What was supposed to be worship of God has become a human religious performance serving their own security, pride, and identity.

The moral and spiritual fraud being exposed is hypocrisy in its deepest form: using religion to appear faithful while refusing the righteousness of God. It is the fraud of honoring God with liturgy while dishonoring Him in the treatment of human beings made in His image.

So Amos 5:21–24 does not reject worship as such. It rejects worship that has become a lie—worship severed from justice, sacrifices offered by unjust hands, songs sung by people who will not repent. In that condition, “better balance” is too mild a remedy. The whole religious performance must be judged until righteousness flows like a mighty stream.
