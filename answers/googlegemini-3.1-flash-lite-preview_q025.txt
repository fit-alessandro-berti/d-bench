To address your question, it is essential to look at the Greek phrasing and the broader theological context of the Sermon on the Mount.

### 1. Is the problem visibility or intention? 
The consensus among theologians is that **the problem is the craving to be seen, not visibility itself.**

The verse warns against doing alms *“to be seen of them”* (Greek: *pros to theathenai autois*). The phrase "to be seen" is the crux of the moral danger. It implies that the act of charity has been transformed from an act of kindness (a gift to the needy) into a theatrical performance (a gift to the audience).

If the mere act of being seen were sinful, Jesus would have contradicted his own command later in the same chapter: *"Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven"* (Matthew 5:16). Since he explicitly tells followers to let their works be seen in one instance and hidden in another, the moral quality of the act is clearly determined by the **intent of the heart.**

### 2. Why is the "craving to be seen" morally dangerous?
In Matthew 6:1, Jesus highlights three specific moral dangers inherent in performing goodness for public approval:

#### A. The Corruption of the Transaction
When you perform an act of charity to gain praise, you have effectively "converted" the act. You are no longer giving to the needy; you are selling your goodness to an audience in exchange for the currency of reputation. Jesus’ warning—*"otherwise ye have no reward of your Father"*—is a stern way of saying that you have already received your payment. You traded eternal significance for the temporary satisfaction of human applause. It is a bad bargain.

#### B. The Displacement of God
To seek the approval of men is to subconsciously replace the "audience of One" with an audience of many. If your primary motivation is the perception of others, you have made the opinions of those people your ultimate authority and your source of validation. Theologically, this is a form of idolatry; you are looking to human beings to provide the affirmation and significance that rightly belongs to God.

#### C. The Erosion of Authenticity (Hypocrisy)
The word often used for these actors in the original text is *hypokritēs*, which originally referred to a stage actor who wears a mask. The moral danger here is that by performing goodness for status, you lose the ability to distinguish your true self from your public persona. Public goodness, when weaponized for status, creates a hollow moral character. You become a person who cares more about the *appearance* of virtue than the *reality* of virtue.

---

### The Moral Distinction
The distinction can be summarized as the difference between **Visibility as an Accident** and **Visibility as an Agency**:

*   **Visibility as an Accident:** You do good, and people happen to see it. Perhaps you cannot avoid being seen. In this case, if your heart remains turned toward God, you have not violated the principle.
*   **Visibility as an Agency:** You structure your kindness to maximize visibility. You choose the time, the place, and the method specifically to ensure you are observed. This is the "danger zone" Jesus describes.

**In summary:** The danger of public goodness is that it risks turning a spiritual act into a social maneuver. When we crave the spotlight for our deeds, we shift our focus from the *needs* of the person we are helping to the *perception* of the people observing us.
