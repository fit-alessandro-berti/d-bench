In the architecture of Ephesians 4:31–32, Paul uses a literary technique called **antithetical parallelism**. By placing bitterness and forgiveness in direct opposition, he isn't just offering a moral "to-do" list; he is describing two mutually exclusive ways of being human.

The text treats bitterness as a complex phenomenon that is simultaneously a **poisonous root**, a **chosen posture**, and a **social contagion.**

### 1. Bitterness as a "Root" (The Internal State)
The Greek word for bitterness (*pikria*) refers to a sour or pungent taste. In a first-century context, it often described a long-standing resentment that has "settled" in the soul. 
*   **Why it opposes forgiveness:** Bitterness is the "record of wrongs" kept in a permanent ledger. Forgiveness (*charizomai*), which shares the same root as "grace" (*charis*), is the act of canceling that debt. You cannot hold a debt and cancel it at the same time; therefore, bitterness and forgiveness are physiologically and spiritually incompatible.

### 2. Bitterness as a "Chosen Posture" (The List of Six)
Paul lists six vices in verse 31 that move from the internal to the external:
1.  **Bitterness:** The internal settled resentment.
2.  **Wrath:** The sudden flare-up of passion.
3.  **Anger:** The habitual hostile internal attitude.
4.  **Clamour:** The outward shouting or brawling.
5.  **Evil speaking:** Slander or "blasphemy" against others.
6.  **Malice:** The general desire to see another suffer.

By placing bitterness at the head of this list, Paul identifies it as the **foundational posture.** It is the "fuel" for the other five. The text treats it not as an involuntary feeling one *has*, but as something one must "put away"—implying a level of agency. It is a chosen refusal to process grief or offense through the lens of grace.

### 3. Bitterness as Contagious (The Social Dimension)
The command to "put away" these things is given to the *community* (the "you" is plural).
*   The progression in verse 31 shows how bitterness spreads: a bitter heart leads to angry outbursts, which lead to shouting (clamour), which eventually destroys the reputation of others (evil speaking). 
*   **The Contrast:** Forgiveness is also presented as a communal ecosystem. We are "tenderhearted" toward one another because we exist within a larger "macro-forgiveness" (God's forgiveness of us in Christ). If bitterness is a contagion that rots a community, forgiveness is the "immune system" that preserves it.

### 4. Why the Direct Opposition?
The placement is strategic because of the **Standard of Exchange.** 

The text ends with: *"even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you."* This is the "why." Paul argues that bitterness is actually a theological error. To remain bitter is to claim that another person's debt to you is more significant than your debt to God. 

Therefore, forgiveness is the only "antidote" to bitterness because it shifts the focus from the **offender's debt** to the **Believer's discharge.** He places them against each other to show that you cannot inhabit the grace of God (verse 32) while clinging to the malice of man (verse 31). One will inevitably drive out the other.

### Summary
The text treats bitterness as:
*   **A Feeling:** A sourness of soul.
*   **A Habit:** Something nurtured through "evil speaking."
*   **A Posture:** A refusal to extend the grace one has received.
*   **A Contagion:** Something that starts in the heart but ends in "clamour" that destroys the peace of the group.
