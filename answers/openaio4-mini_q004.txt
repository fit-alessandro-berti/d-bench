Proverbs 3:5–6 is calling for a wholehearted, God-ward dependence that is at once deeply spiritual and gratefully rational.  It is not a blanket rejection of thought or learning, but a refusal to rest the ultimate weight of our lives on our own unaided, pride-inflated “understanding.”

1. What kind of trust is being asked for?  
   •   A heart-trust (“with all thine heart”):  an all-of-you commitment to God’s character, promises and leading.  
   •   A surrender of control (“lean not unto thine own understanding”):  not because human reason is valueless, but because it is finite, fallible and often warped by sin and self-interest.  
   •   An active acknowledgement (“in all thy ways acknowledge him”):  every plan, project and decision is submitted to his wisdom by prayer, by waiting on his Word, and by seeking godly counsel.  
   •   A confidence in his direction (“he shall direct thy paths”):  the believer expects God actually to guide—with providence, conviction of the Spirit and sometimes wise circumstances or advisors.

2. Humble self-distrust versus mere anti-intellectualism  
   Humble self-distrust:  
     –   Admits human knowledge is partial, our reason prone to error and bias (Proverbs 14:12; Isaiah 55:8–9).  
     –   Earnestly asks God for wisdom (James 1:5), studies Scripture, learns from creation, history and community.  
     –   Uses reason as a servant under God’s revelation, evaluating every idea by his truth (Acts 17:11).  
     –   Is willing to change course when God’s leading or new information arises.

   Mere anti-intellectualism:  
     –   Dismisses study, critical thinking or expertise as inherently unspiritual.  
     –   Treats reason as the enemy of faith rather than a gift from the Creator.  
     –   Tends toward isolation from informed counsel or reliable data.  
     –   Often anchors itself in sectarian clichés rather than serious engagement with God’s world or Word.

3. Why the distinction matters  
   –   Proverbs elsewhere commends “getting wisdom” (2:1–6; 4:7) and collecting “counsel” (11:14).  
   –   Christ himself is called “the wisdom of God” (1 Corinthians 1:24), and he bids us to “love the Lord your God with all your mind” (Matthew 22:37).  
   –   True trust in God liberates us to study, plan and reason—yet always under the lordship of Christ, with due humility before his infinite wisdom.

In short, Proverbs 3:5–6 invites you to a trust that (a) refuses to lean in proud self-reliance and (b) gladly employs every God-given means—reason, study, prayer, counsel—under the supreme authority of his wisdom.
