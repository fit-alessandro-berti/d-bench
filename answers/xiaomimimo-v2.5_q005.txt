This is a profound and timeless question. The difficulty in taking enemy-love seriously often stems from a clash between our natural human instincts and a radical moral vision. Let's break down why it's hard, and then explore its true nature.

### Why It's So Difficult to Take Seriously

1.  **It Feels Sentimental and Unrealistic:** In a world that often operates on principles of power, self-defense, and tit-for-tat justice, loving an enemy can seem like a naive, weak, or even dangerous sentiment. It can be dismissed as "soft" or "impractical," something for an ideal world, not the real one.
2.  **It Provokes Cognitive Dissonance:** Our innate sense of fairness and desire for reciprocity rebels. We think, "Why should I give good to someone who gives me harm?" It feels like a violation of justice.
3.  **It's Misunderstood as Affirmation:** People often confuse love with approval or agreement. To "love your enemy" is not to condone their actions or pretend they are good. It's to will their good despite their wrong. This distinction is crucial and often missed, making the command seem morally obtuse.

### Is It Sentimental, Strategic, or Moral Strength?

It is **primarily a deeper form of radical moral strength**, but it can have strategic and transformative effects.

#### 1. It is NOT Merely Sentimental.
Sentiment is a feeling. This command is about a **choice** and a **posture of the will**. It is an act of profound self-discipline that goes against emotional impulse. The passage itself grounds it not in warm feelings, but in the character of God: "That ye may be the children of your Father..." The strength comes from aligning oneself with a divine, sustaining, and generous nature.

#### 2. It Can Be Incredibly Strategic (though that's not its primary aim).
History and psychology show that non-retaliation and blessing the enemy can be powerfully strategic. It can:
*   **De-escalate conflict** and break cycles of vengeance.
*   **Moralize a struggle**, winning the sympathy of bystanders and even the conscience of the oppressor.
*   **Preserve the humanity and integrity** of the one being persecuted, preventing them from becoming what they hate.
Think of Gandhi, Martin Luther King Jr., or Desmond Tutu—they understood that enemy-love (often framed as "unconditional love" or "agape") was the most potent weapon against dehumanizing systems. However, to see it *only* as a strategy would be to miss its heart.

#### 3. Its Core is Deeper Moral Strength.
This is where the true power lies. It is strength in several dimensions:
*   **Strength of Identity:** It defines who you are, regardless of how others treat you. You are a "child of the Father," defined by the character of God who "makes his sun rise on the evil and the good." Your actions flow from your deepest identity, not from your enemy's behavior.
*   **Strength of Love as Willed Good:** This love (*agape* in the Greek) is not an emotion but a commitment to the well-being of another. Praying for someone who persecutes you is an act of asserting their humanity and value before God, even when they deny yours. It is the ultimate refusal to let hatred define the relationship.
*   **Strength of Moral Vision:** It looks beyond the immediate conflict to the redemptive possibility. It believes no one is beyond redemption and that responding with good has the power to awaken conscience. This is a strength that trusts in a justice and mercy bigger than one's own.

**In summary:** The command is difficult because it calls us out of the comfortable, retaliatory world of retributive justice into the challenging, proactive world of restorative and divine love. It is not sentimental weakness; it is the **hardest and most potent form of moral courage.** It is the choice to absorb evil without becoming evil, to confront hatred with an active, seeking love that reflects the very nature of God as described in the passage.

As the parable of the wheat and tares (Matthew 13) suggests, God's love sustains both the good and the bad *for now*, allowing time for repentance and growth. To love your enemy is to partner with God in that patient, powerful, and world-changing perspective.
