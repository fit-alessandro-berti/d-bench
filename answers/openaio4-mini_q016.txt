At first glance the elder brother’s protest looks like anger or wounded fairness (and of course envy is in the mix).  But those are symptoms, not the root.

If you peel back his complaint you find that he cannot bear his father’s free, unmerited love.  He has lived on a “You owe me” model of son‐ship – “I’ve done all that service, I deserve that kid … ” – and he cannot countenance the idea that his younger brother gets the same lavish welcome without having earned it.  

In other words his real problem is a self‐righteous discomfort with mercy itself.  He has turned obedience into a transaction, and any gift not earned feels like an injustice.  The father’s boundless grace exposes him as a servant, not a beloved son, so he stays outside, stewing in anger, rather than entering the feast of free forgiveness and relationship.
