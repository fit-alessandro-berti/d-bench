The hatred of light in John 3:19–21 stems from a profound psychological and spiritual tension: the exposure of truth. Light, in this context, symbolizes divine truth, moral clarity, and the revealing presence of God. Those who "love darkness" do so because their actions, motives, or inner state cannot withstand scrutiny. The passage suggests a direct link between evil deeds and the aversion to light—evil thrives in concealment, while truth demands visibility.

### Key Dynamics:
1. **Truth as Exposure**
   Light reveals what darkness hides. For someone engaged in wrongdoing, the light doesn’t just illuminate actions—it exposes the *self*. The fear isn’t merely punishment but the shame of being *seen* as they truly are. Shame, then, is the emotional response to this exposure, a fear of being unmasked.

2. **The Desire for Concealment**
   The text states that those who do evil "do not come to the light, lest their deeds should be reproved." This implies a deliberate avoidance of accountability. Darkness offers the illusion of safety—no judgment, no confrontation with one’s own moral failures. The hatred of light, then, is a hatred of vulnerability.

3. **Truth as Alignment with God**
   Conversely, those who "do truth" (a phrase emphasizing integrity, not just factual accuracy) *come to the light* because their deeds are "wrought in God." Here, truth is not just an abstract ideal but a lived reality in relationship with the divine. The light is welcomed because there is no discrepancy between their inner state and outward actions.

### Deeper Implications:
- **Moral Psychology**: The passage reflects a universal human tendency—resisting what reveals our flaws. Light (truth) forces a reckoning, while darkness allows self-deception.
- **Shame vs. Conviction**: Shame drives one *away* from light (fear of exposure), while godly conviction (awareness of wrong with a path to repentance) draws one *toward* it.
- **The Paradox of Freedom**: Ironically, those who hide in darkness are enslaved to their secrets, while those who embrace the light find liberation in transparency.

In essence, light is hated because it disrupts the comfortable illusion of hiddenness. To love darkness is to love the self that remains unexamined—and to fear the self that might be transformed.
