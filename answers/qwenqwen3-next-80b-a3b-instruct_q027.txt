The Pharisee in Luke 18:9–14 is condemned not primarily for false doctrine, but for **pride, comparison, contempt, and the use of virtue as self-display**—all of which are deeply intertwined. Let’s break this down:

---

### **What condemns the Pharisee?**

1. **Pride (Self-Righteousness)**  
   The core issue is *trusting in himself that he was righteous* (v. 9). His prayer is not an act of worship or surrender to God, but a performance of self-congratulation. He doesn’t say, “God, I am grateful for your grace,” but “I thank you that *I* am not like others”—centering himself as the standard of righteousness.

2. **Comparison**  
   He doesn’t measure himself against God’s holiness (as Isaiah 6:5 or Isaiah 64:6 would suggest), but against other people—especially the publican. This is a hallmark of spiritual arrogance: using others’ failings to inflate one’s own sense of moral superiority.

3. **Contempt**  
   His language (“as this publican”) reveals disdain. He doesn’t just distinguish himself—he *looks down*. The Greek word for “despised others” (ἐξουθενοῦντας) in verse 9 is the same root as “contempt.” This isn’t mere observation; it’s dehumanization.

4. **Virtue as Self-Display**  
   He lists his religious practices (fasting twice a week, tithing everything) not as expressions of devotion, but as credentials—as if God is a cosmic auditor to whom he is submitting a report of merit. His piety becomes a trophy, not a tribute. He “prayed *with himself*” (v. 11)—a phrase suggesting internal monologue, not communion with God. His prayer is anthropocentric, not theocentric.

> **Note:** His doctrine (fasting, tithing) is not wrong—the Law commanded tithing and fasting was a devout practice. The problem is his *motivation and attitude*. He turns obedience into a self-justifying performance.

---

### **Why is the tax collector’s posture spiritually truer?**

1. **He stands “afar off”**  
   In Jewish temple symbolism, the Holy of Holies was the inner sanctum. The publican does not enter close to the altar, recognizing he is unworthy to approach God’s holiness. This embodies the biblical stance of repentance: “I am not worthy” (Matthew 8:8, Luke 5:8).

2. **He will not lift his eyes to heaven**  
   In prayer, lifting one’s eyes was a sign of confidence in divine hearing (cf. Psalm 123:1). But the publican does not presume. He cannot even look up—his sinfulness makes him feel beneath the gaze of God.

3. **He smites his breast**  
   A gesture of deep sorrow, grief, and self-condemnation (cf. Ezekiel 21:12, Luke 23:48). It is bodily, visceral, humble.

4. **His prayer is three words: “God, be merciful to me, a sinner.”**  
   Not: “I have tried,” “I’ve done better,” or “compare me to others.”  
   Just: “God, be merciful” — *Eleēson* (ἐλέησόν), *have mercy* — the cry of one who acknowledges he *has nothing to offer* but need.  
   And he calls himself a *sinner* (ἁμαρτωλός)—not “a sinner like others,” but *the* sinner—absolute, unqualified, without excuse.

> This is the essence of gospel humility: **No merit. Only mercy.**

---

### **Theological Takeaway**

Jesus says, “This man went down to his house *justified* rather than the other.”  
**Justification**—being declared righteous before God—is not earned by moral performance. It is granted by God’s mercy to those who admit their spiritual bankruptcy.

The publican, though despised by society, is received by God because he trusts *only* in mercy.  
The Pharisee, though religion’s model, is rejected because he trusts *in himself*.

This echoes the whole biblical theme:  
> *“God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble.”* (James 4:6; Proverbs 3:34)

The posture of the tax collector is spiritually truer because it is *receptive*—not performative. It is *dependent*—not self-sufficient. It is *God-centered*—not self-centered.

### **In summary:**
- **The Pharisee is condemned for**: *Pride, comparison, contempt, and virtue as self-display.*  
- **The publican is justified because**: He approaches God with *humble repentance*, recognizing his utter dependence on divine mercy.

And that, Jesus says, is the path to righteousness—not moral superiority, but brokenness before God.
