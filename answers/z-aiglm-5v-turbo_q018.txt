The passage in Ephesians 4:31–32 presents one of the starkest contrasts in all of Scripture. The reason these two concepts are placed so directly against one another is that, in biblical psychology, **bitterness and forgiveness are functional opposites regarding how we handle the past.**

To answer your question about whether bitterness is treated as a feeling, a habit, or a contagion: **It is treated as all three, but primarily as a spiritual toxin that starts as a feeling, hardens into a posture, and inevitably spreads.**

Here is a breakdown of why this text sets them against each other and how it defines the nature of bitterness.

### 1. The Structural Reason: "Put Off" vs. "Put On"
Paul uses a specific literary structure here common in his writings (similar to Colossians 3). He commands the believer to do two things simultaneously:
*   **Negative (v. 31):** "Put away" (Greek: *airō*) — literally meaning to lift up and carry off, or to strip off like a dirty garment.
*   **Positive (v. 32):** "Be ye..." (Become/Become ye) — implying the putting on of a new nature.

They are placed together because **you cannot wear two suits at once.** Bitterness is defined by the **retention** of a debt (holding a grudge). Forgiveness is defined by the **release** of a debt. They cannot coexist in the human heart any more than a vessel can be full of poison and pure water at the same time.

### 2. Analyzing the Nature of Bitterness
The Greek word used here is ***pikria*** (πικρία). Literally, it means "sharpness" or "acridity."

In the ancient world, this word was used for:
*   **Bitter plants/medicine:** Something that stings the palate.
*   **Salt water:** The undrinkable brine of the sea.
*   **Cold weather:** Biting frost.

When Paul lists bitterness first (before wrath, anger, clamor, etc.), he is identifying the **root** from which the others grow. Here is how the text views it:

#### Is it a Feeling?
Yes, but it is a **fermented feeling**. Anger (*thumos/orgē*) is often the initial heat of an offense. Bitterness is what happens when that anger is cooled and stored. It is no longer a fiery explosion; it has become a settled, long-term resentment. If anger is a fire, bitterness is the radiation that remains after the burn—it lingers and poisons the system.

#### Is it a Chosen Posture?
Absolutely. The text commands us to "put it away," which implies we have the agency to keep it or discard it. Bitterness is a **posture of protection**. When we are hurt, we naturally want to protect ourselves from being hurt again. We adopt a "hardened" posture. The text counters this with "tenderhearted" (*eusplanchnos*—literally "good bowels" or deep compassion). You must physically relax the "clenched fist" of bitterness to offer the open hand of forgiveness.

#### Is it Contagious?
This is where the text gets vivid. Notice the progression of symptoms in verse 31:
1.  **Bitterness** (Internal attitude)
2.  **Wrath/Anger** (Internal agitation)
3.  **Clamor** (External noise/shouting)
4.  **Evil Speaking** (Attacking others)

Bitterness does not stay inside. It demands expression. The text suggests that if you do not "put away" the internal bitterness, it will inevitably erupt into "clamor" (fighting) and "evil speaking" (slander/gossip). In this way, bitterness is highly contagious; it disrupts the peace of the entire community ("one to another").

### 3. Why Forgiveness is the Specific Antidote
The text doesn't just say "stop being bitter"; it says "forgive." Why? Because **bitterness is justice delayed (and perched).**

A bitter person is essentially saying, *"I will not let go of this wrong until balance is restored."*
Forgiveness is the act of saying, *"I am releasing my right to balance the scales; I am trusting God to handle the justice."*

Paul grounds this in theology: **"...even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you."**

*   **Bitterness holds the record of wrongs.**
*   **God, in Christ, shredded the record of wrongs.**

### Summary
Ephesians treats bitterness not merely as a bad mood, but as a **spiritual malignancy**. It is:
1.  **A feeling** that has soured into a disposition.
2.  **A posture** of hostility that hardens the heart against others.
3.  **A contagion** that spreads via gossip and strife.

It is placed against forgiveness because forgiveness is the only mechanism powerful enough to dissolve the acid of bitterness without inflicting further damage on the soul.
