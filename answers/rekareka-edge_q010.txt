In the context of Philippians 2:5–8, the praise for the power being discussed lies within the interplay of **humility** and **greatness**. Here’s how these elements work together and why the combination may be more unsettling than a simple demonstration of open domination:

---

### **1. Humility as a Powerful Basis for Greatness**
The passage explicitly praises humility. Instead of aspiring to be superior or trying to assert dominance, the figure mentioned presents a humility that positions himself **at the service of others** rather than the service of himself. This humility, combined with divine capability, suggests that greatness does not reside in self-exaltation but in selfless service. The ideal power here is **selfless greatness**—a virtue that transcends personal ambition or pride. This humility is not one of acquiescence but of deliberate, sacrificial action: being "in the form of God, who took upon him the form of a servant" (v. 6). This humility underpins a greatness that prioritizes the good of others over personal elevation.

---

### **2. Why Humility Joined with Greatness feels Unsettling**
While humility alone is laudable, the pairing of humility with **greatness** in this passage introduces complexities. Here’s why this combination may feel unsettling:

- **Tension between Humility and Power**: Founders of new movements (e.g., Jesus) often inspire respect for individuality and principles of justice, poverty, and humility. When great power is paired with unyielding humility, it can feel like a paradox. Does greatness without humility become glory-driven or controlling? Or does humility become an excuse for indolence, masking potential strength? This tension arises because humility, while essential, is not always compatible with unyielding authority.
  
- **Critique of Extremes**: The passage hints at a critique against overly extreme forms of power. If one becomes too zealous about greatness, it risks being arrogant or domineering. The ideal power here is balanced, acknowledging that greatness is not a weapon but a tool for service and unity. When humility is paired with triumphant dominance, it risks creating an environment where individuals or groups seek to wield power over others, even if it is cloaked in humility.

- **Religious Importance**: In many religious texts, humility is regarded as a virtue that humble individuals (e.g., Joseph, Jesus) gain authority through humility, not through authority over others. When great power is paired with humility, it can distort the spiritual message of humility, suggesting that rulers use humility as a tactic to further dominate.

---

### **3. Practical Implications**
If we apply this to modern society, the message challenges leaders—especially those who have achieved great success—to consider how their power is used. Should their influence drive excellence for the greater good, or is it better to reflect humility, even when achieving success? This question highlights the tension between personal achievement and societal responsibility, encouraging leaders to blend success with ethical stewardship, not just personal ambition.

In summary, while humility is celebrated as a virtue, its combination with greatness in this passage invites reflection on how power is exercised. The ideal is neither pure dominance nor pure humility—it requires a balance where both components serve mutual well-being. This critique and praise encourage us to think critically about the use of power, ensuring it centers on service and unity.

---

### **Final Thought**
The passage tspies that greatness is morally triumphantly achieved only when humility remains at its core. This interplay between power and humility is what makes the muscular Christian message timeless—the belief that true strength lies not in overwhelming others, but in serving others with humility and compassion. The discomfort arises from how our world—where power is often discussed through competing narratives of dominance and humility—requires us to consider the deeper spirit and ethics guiding our leadership.
