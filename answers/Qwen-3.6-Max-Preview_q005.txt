Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it runs counter to our deepest biological instincts, social conditioning, and intuitive sense of justice. It feels like a violation of the natural order: *protect yourself, reward friends, punish enemies.*

To answer your question directly: **It is none of those three exclusively, but rather a radical reorientation of identity that transcends sentimentality, strategy, and conventional morality.** However, we can analyze why it resists each of these categories and what it actually represents.

### 1. Why It Is Not Sentimental
Sentimentality implies a soft, emotional affection based on liking or compatibility. Enemy-love is explicitly *not* about feeling warm toward someone who harms you. In fact, the Greek word used in Matthew 5:44 for "love" (*agapao*) refers to a deliberate choice of will and action, not an emotion (*phileo* or *eros*).

- **The Difficulty:** We confuse love with approval. Loving an enemy does not mean approving of their actions or ignoring injustice. It means refusing to let their evil dictate your moral response.
- **Why It’s Hard:** Our emotions are reactive. When hurt, we feel anger, fear, or hatred. To choose *agape* requires overriding powerful emotional currents, which feels unnatural and even self-betraying.

### 2. Why It Is Not Merely Strategic
One might argue that loving enemies is strategic: it disarms them, breaks cycles of violence, or wins public sympathy. While these outcomes may occur, Jesus’ reasoning in verse 45 grounds the command not in utility, but in **ontology** (being): *"That ye may be the children of your Father."*

- **The Difficulty:** Strategy is conditional. If loving an enemy doesn’t work (i.e., they continue to persecute you), a strategic approach would abandon the tactic. But Jesus commands it regardless of outcome.
- **Why It’s Hard:** We live in a transactional world. We expect reciprocity. Enemy-love is non-reciprocal; it gives without guarantee of return. This feels foolish or weak in a competitive society.

### 3. Why It Is Not Conventional Moral Strength
Traditional moral strength often involves endurance, justice, or righteous indignation. Enemy-love goes further: it actively seeks the good of the one who wishes you harm. This is not stoic endurance; it is proactive benevolence.

- **The Difficulty:** Our moral intuition says, "Justice demands punishment." Enemy-love seems to suspend justice. But Jesus reframes justice: God’s justice includes mercy extended to the unjust (sun and rain on all). To mimic God is to participate in this broader, more difficult justice.
- **Why It’s Hard:** It requires humility. To pray for someone who despises you is to acknowledge your shared humanity and dependence on God’s grace, rather than asserting your moral superiority.

---

### So, What Is It? A Deeper Form of Moral Freedom

Enemy-love is best understood as **the ultimate assertion of spiritual freedom and identity.**

#### 1. It Breaks the Cycle of Determinism
When you hate your enemy, you allow them to control your inner life. Your emotions, thoughts, and actions become reactions to their behavior. You are enslaved by their evil.  
**Loving your enemy frees you.** You act from your own core values (reflecting God’s character) rather than reacting to theirs. This is true moral strength: the power to choose goodness independently of external circumstances.

#### 2. It Reflects Divine Identity
Jesus says, *"That ye may be the children of your Father."* The logic is mimetic: God loves indiscriminately (sun/rain on just/unjust). To be His child is to imitate this impartial, generous love.  
This is not sentimental; it is **theological**. It asserts that human worth is not determined by merit or behavior, but by being created and sustained by God. Therefore, even the "enemy" bears God’s image and deserves dignity.

#### 3. It Is a Counter-Cultural Witness
In a world governed by power, retaliation, and tribal loyalty, enemy-love is a revolutionary act. It exposes the futility of hatred and offers a glimpse of a different kingdom—one where reconciliation is possible, not because enemies deserve it, but because love is stronger than hate.

---

### Conclusion

Enemy-love is difficult because it demands:
- **Emotional discipline** over reactive feelings.
- **Non-transactional generosity** in a reciprocal world.
- **Humility** to see the enemy as equally loved by God.

It is not sentimental (it’s hard-willed), not merely strategic (it’s unconditional), and not conventional morality (it’s transformative). It is **the practice of divine likeness**: choosing to reflect God’s impartial, sustaining love in the face of hostility, thereby breaking the chain of evil and affirming your true identity as a child of God.
