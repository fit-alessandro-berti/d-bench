The passage from Philippians 2:5-8 praises a particular kind of power often referred to as "selfless," "sacrificial," or "kenotic" power (from the Greek word "kenosis," meaning "to empty"). This power is characterized by humility, service, and a willingness to surrender one's own status or interests for the benefit of others.

In this passage, the power being praised is that of Jesus Christ, who, despite being in the "form of God" and equal with God, chose to "empty himself" (or "made himself of no reputation" in the KJV) and take on the form of a servant. He humbled himself to the point of death on the cross, a death that was particularly shameful and humiliating in the Roman world.

This kind of power can be unsettling because it subverts traditional expectations of power and greatness. Typically, we associate power with dominance, strength, and control. In contrast, the power demonstrated by Jesus is quiet, unassuming, and sacrificial. It is a power that is not exercised through coercion or force, but through service and self-giving.

Humility joined to greatness can be particularly unsettling because it challenges our conventional notions of power and prestige. When someone who is great or powerful humbles themselves and serves others, it can be disorienting and even subversive. It forces us to reevaluate our assumptions about what it means to be powerful and to consider the possibility that true greatness may be found in humility and service rather than in domination or self-aggrandizement.

Furthermore, the fact that Jesus' humility and self-sacrifice were not signs of weakness, but rather a demonstration of his true power and character, can be particularly challenging to our conventional thinking. It suggests that true strength lies not in asserting one's own power or status, but in being willing to surrender oneself for the benefit of others.

In this sense, the power praised in Philippians 2:5-8 is not just a moral or ethical ideal, but a deeply subversive and transformative one. It challenges us to rethink our assumptions about power and greatness, and to consider the possibility that true power lies in humility, service, and self-sacrifice.
