• Its sharpness comes from the image hierarchy:

  - A mote (tiny splinter) is tiny and painful to remove,
  - a beam is huge and blocks vision.

  That contrast makes the accusation visual and immediate: you can’t honestly fix someone else’s tiny flaw while a major
  defect remains in yourself.

  The passage criticizes hypocrisy by exposing mismatch: someone who points out small sins in another while carrying a
  bigger sin themselves is not morally authoritative. The issue is not “don’t correct others,” but “don’t do it from a
  place of self-deception and superiority.”

  So it permits correction under a sequence:

  1. Self-diagnose first (remove the beam).
  2. Then correct others (remove the mote), with humility and legitimacy.

  That is why it attacks the hypocrite’s posture, not the act of moral correction itself. The goal is restoration, not
  domination.
