The movement marks the two sides of repentance.

- **“Cease to do evil”** is the negative side: stop the violence, corruption, and injustice that have made Judah’s worship offensive to God.
- **“Learn to do well”** is the positive side: acquire new habits, judgments, and practices that accord with God’s will.

“Learn” is important. Goodness is not treated as automatic once wrongdoing stops. It must be cultivated—discerned, practiced, and embodied. The following commands define what this goodness looks like:

- seek justice,
- correct oppression or defend the oppressed,
- secure justice for the fatherless,
- plead the widow’s cause.

Thus goodness in Isaiah is not mere restraint or private moral innocence. One can avoid personally committing an injustice while remaining indifferent to those harmed by it. Isaiah calls for active responsibility on behalf of the vulnerable.

The sequence can therefore be expressed as **from renunciation to restoration**: stop producing evil, then begin repairing what evil has damaged. Although the word *love* is not used in these verses, the commanded goodness is love in covenantal and social form—love practiced as justice, advocacy, and protection. It is not simply “do no harm,” but “learn to do the good your neighbor needs.”
