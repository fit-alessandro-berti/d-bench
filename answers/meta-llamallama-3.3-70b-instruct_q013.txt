The passage rejects worship so sharply because it is condemning the hypocrisy and superficiality of the Israelites' religious practices. The Israelites were going through the motions of worship, offering sacrifices and singing songs, but their hearts were not in it. They were not living justly and righteously, and their worship was not accompanied by a genuine desire to follow God's commands.

The moral or spiritual fraud being exposed here is the idea that one can separate worship from everyday life, and that ritualistic practices can substitute for genuine obedience to God. The Israelites were trying to appease God with their sacrifices and songs, while ignoring the injustices and oppressions that were rampant in their society. They were prioritizing religious rituals over righteous living, and God is saying that this is unacceptable.

By rejecting their worship so sharply, God is highlighting the fact that true worship must be accompanied by a commitment to justice, righteousness, and compassion. The passage is emphasizing that worship is not just about rituals and sacrifices, but about living a life that reflects God's values and commands. The "moral or spiritual fraud" being exposed is the attempt to use worship as a way to justify or distract from one's sinful behavior, rather than allowing worship to transform and guide one's life towards greater obedience to God.

In essence, the passage is saying that God is not interested in superficial worship, but in a deep-seated commitment to justice, righteousness, and compassion. The call to "let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream" is a call to live out one's faith in a way that brings about real change and justice in the world, rather than just going through the motions of religious ritual.
