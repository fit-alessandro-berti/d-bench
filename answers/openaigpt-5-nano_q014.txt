Short answer: the verse says the heart is tied to what you treasure. It’s a statement about alignment (and formation): your inner life gravitates toward whatever you value most. In practice it functions both as a diagnostic (your treasures reveal where your heart already is) and as a training program (you train your heart by choosing heavenly treasure).

How the three concepts fit together

- Possession (treasure): “Treasure” here means what you prize, store up, and value most—money, security, status, or heavenly realities. It’s not just what you own, but what you are willing to dedicate resources to.

- Attention: To treasure something is to fix your attention on it—to invest time, energy, and focus in pursuit or protection of it. Where you divert attention, your heart tends to follow.

- Love (the heart): In Matthew, the heart (kardia) is the center of a person’s loyalties, desires, and will—not merely emotions. Where your treasure is, there your love and allegiance will be.

Interpreting the clause

- The core claim: where your treasure is, there your heart will be also. This expresses a close link between what you value and where your heart goes. It can function as a diagnostic: a quick way to infer what someone truly loves or trusts by looking at where they invest their life.

- It also has a prescriptive, formative edge: if you want your heart to be with God and heavenly realities, invest in heavenly treasures. The act of laying up treasure in heaven is, in effect, a way to train your desires and loyalties to God.

Two ways to read the “train” idea

- Literal reading: the verse describes a condition—your heart follows your treasure. It’s a statement about how things tend to work.

- Practical/teaching reading: by choosing heavenly treasure (rather than earthly), you shape your heart over time. In that sense, treasure acts like a training device for the affections: you practice a kind of devotion that makes heavenly things your real treasure.

Why this matters for possession, attention, and love

- If you hoard earthly treasures, your heart becomes bound to security, status, and wealth. Anxiety, competition, or mistrust can follow because your affections are tied to fragile things.

- If you lay up treasures in heaven (e.g., generosity, justice, trust in God, acts of mercy), your heart is trained to care for God and his kingdom. Your attention shifts toward God’s purposes, and your loves align with heavenly realities.

Context note

- This passage sits with the larger Sermon on the Mount’s contrast between earthly and heavenly priorities (treasures, eye serving, serving God or Mammon). It also feeds into the tougher claim in 6:24: you cannot serve two masters. The heart is the ultimate chooser; treasure indicates (and shapes) where that chooser will give its loyalty.

Practical takeaway

- A simple diagnostic question: where is your treasure? Where you spend money, time, and energy often reveals what your heart already loves.

- A practical discipline: redirect some earthly treasures toward heavenly ones (prayer, generosity, practices that seek first God’s kingdom). Doing so invites your heart to be more at home with heaven.
