Galatians 6:1 presents a tightly woven ethic of community correction. To answer your two questions directly:

### 1. What moral posture is required?
The passage calls for a posture of **humble solidarity**. This is not detached judgment, nor is it permissive enablement. It is Spirit-led, grace-aware accountability characterized by:
- **Secured identity**: “Ye which are spiritual” does not mean “elite” but those whose standing and conduct are continually yielded to the Holy Spirit, not propped up by self-achievement.
- **Compassionate engagement**: The goal is not punishment or public shaming but the fallen brother’s return to wholeness and fellowship.
- **Controlled strength**: True meekness is not passive or weak; in biblical usage it is strength deliberately restrained for the sake of another’s good.
- **Sober self-awareness**: A clear-eyed recognition of one’s own vulnerability to the same temptations.

In short, the moral posture is that of a **grace-receiving corrector**: one who corrects because they themselves are held by grace, and who corrects in a way that mirrors how Christ restores rather than condemns.

### 2. Why are restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion kept together?
They form a **self-correcting triad** where each element guards against the distortions of the others:

| Element | Function | Danger if isolated |
|---------|----------|-------------------|
| **Restoration** (goal) | Brings the fallen back into right standing and community | Becomes moralistic legalism without meekness; becomes sentimentalism without truth |
| **Meekness** (attitude/method) | Ensures correction is delivered with gentleness, patience, and non-condemnation | Becomes harshness or pride without self-suspicion; becomes avoidance or false peace without a clear restorative aim |
| **Self-suspicion** (guardrail) | Keeps the corrector humble by remembering shared human frailty (“lest thou also be tempted”) | Becomes self-righteousness or performance-driven holiness without meekness or restorative purpose |

Together, they reflect the **gospel-shaped logic of the church**:
- We are restored by grace → therefore we restore others.
- We are forgiven in meekness → therefore we correct in meekness.
- We are tempted and fail → therefore we correct with sober self-awareness.

The triad prevents the common failures of church discipline: pride masquerading as purity, condemnation disguised as conviction, and isolation mistaken for holiness. Instead, it frames correction as a **communal, cruciform practice** where truth and grace hold each other, and no one stands above the mercy they themselves require.

### Practical-Theological Implication
Galatians 6:1 is not merely advice for conflict resolution; it is ecclesiology in miniature. The church is not a museum of saints but a hospital for sinners. Correction, when done rightly, is itself an act of spiritual care that preserves both the fallen and the correcting community from the twin snares of despair and self-deception.
