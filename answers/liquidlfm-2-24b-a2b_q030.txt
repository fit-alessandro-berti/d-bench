This passage from **1 Corinthians 13:4–7** offers a profound and poetic definition of *agape* (selfless, divine love), especially as it contrasts with the destructive qualities of human pride and sin. The extensive use of **negation**—listing what love "does not do"—serves several important purposes:

---

### **Why So Many Negations?**

1. **Clarifying True Love:**  
   By listing what love *does not* do, Paul clarifies what love truly is by explicitly excluding everything that opposes it. This method helps readers understand love not just by its positive traits, but by what it *rejects*—especially the vices of pride, envy, irritability, and suspicion.

2. **Highlighting the Opposition to Sin:**  
   Each negation names a common human failing or sinful attitude. By naming these specifically, Paul shows how love stands in direct opposition to the very things that divide, wound, and corrupt relationships.

3. **Emphasizing Humility and Selflessness:**  
   The negations repeatedly emphasize humility ("is kind," "is not puffed up"), patience ("suffereth long"), and lack of self-interest ("seeketh not her own"). These are antidotes to pride, selfishness, and possessiveness.

4. **Contrast and Remembrance:**  
   The structure creates a strong contrast between love and hate, selfishness and generosity, suspicion and trust. It’s easier to remember what love *isn’t* than what it *is*, especially for those learning about Christian virtue.

---

### **What Vision of Strength Is Proposed Here?**

1. **Strength Through Restraint:**  
   The passage suggests that true strength lies not in dominance, aggression, or self-assertion, but in restraint, patience, and forgiveness. Love is powerful because it chooses not to retaliate, not to boast, and not to give in to irritation.

2. **Endurance Over Emotion:**  
   The repeated phrase "endureth all things" presents strength as endurance rather than impulsivity. Love is not swayed by fleeting emotions or personal grievances—it remains steadfast.

3. **Humility as Power:**  
   The negations highlight humility as a core aspect of strength. To be strong is to be gentle, to bear others’ faults, and to rise above suspicion and envy.

4. **Love as a Transformative Force:**  
   Rather than being a passive emotion, love is depicted as an active force that transforms behavior and relationships. Its power is in its ability to overcome negative tendencies within ourselves and in others.

---

**In summary:**  
The negations in 1 Corinthians 13:4–7 serve to define love by what it rejects—pride, envy, irritability, suspicion—and thus reveal a vision of strength rooted in humility, patience, and enduring grace. This portrayal of love is not weakness, but rather the highest form of spiritual and moral strength.
