Short answer: the danger is not visibility itself, but the motive behind the visibility. The verse condemns doing acts of righteousness in order to be seen and praised by people.

What makes public goodness morally dangerous
- Motive matters more than audience. Jesus is not forbidding public acts of charity, prayer, or fasting per se. He is warning against performing them so that human observers will applaud you.
- The principle is about who you seek to please. If you act to gain the praise, approval, or prestige of others, your reward is human applause. Jesus says you “have no reward from your Father in heaven” in that case (Matthew 6:1).
- The danger is hypocrisy. The repeated pattern in this section contrasts authentic, God-directed righteousness with “the hypocrites” who perform pious acts to be seen by others (6:2, 6:5, 6:16). The public display becomes a mask rather than a genuine relationship with God.

How visibility relates to the broader message
- The context shows a consistent motif: acts of righteousness should come from a sincere relationship with God, not from a desire to impress people.
- At the same time, visibility is not categorically condemned. Jesus also says, “Let your light shine before others, that they may see your good deeds and give glory to your Father in heaven” (Matthew 5:16). The difference is: the audience and the aim. Public acts can glorify God when done to honor Him, not to feed personal pride.
- The instruction emphasizes secrecy in three common practices (almsgiving, prayer, fasting) to train the heart away from self-glorification toward reliance on God. The underlying assumption is that God sees in secret and will reward what is truly done for Him.

Practical implications
- Examine your motives: Are you aiming to honor God or to win human praise?
- If you do something publicly, aim to glorify God, not to draw attention to yourself.
- Cultivate humility and integrity: regular private devotion (prayer, generosity, fasting) can help keep motives aligned with God.
- Use public acts to set a positive example and to point others to God, not to enhance your own status.

In sum, the moral risk in Matthew 6:1 is the craving for human approval that can accompany public righteousness. Visibility can be legitimate when it serves God and others; it becomes dangerous when the point of the action is to be seen and praised by people.
