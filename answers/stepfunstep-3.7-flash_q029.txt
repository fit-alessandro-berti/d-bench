To address this question, we first ground the verse in its original context: Paul writes to the Galatian churches, fractured by Judaizing legalism (which demanded adherence to Jewish law for salvation) and rampant mutual judgment, to lay out a framework for grace-filled communal accountability. This instruction sits between his call to "bear one another's burdens" (Galatians 6:2) and his warning against self-deception, so it is deeply tied to the core Galatian theme that all believers stand equal before God, saved by grace alone, not by their own moral performance.

### Part 1: The required moral posture for correction
The posture prescribed here rejects both harsh legalism and permissive licentiousness, and is defined by three core features:
1. **Spirit-dependent humility, not self-righteous superiority**: The verse specifies that correction should be offered by those who are "spiritual" (Greek *pneumatikoi*), a term Paul uses elsewhere to describe believers walking in step with the Holy Spirit (Galatians 5:16-26), not a special class of morally elite Christians. The only qualification for correction is not sinlessness, but a commitment to relying on the Spirit rather than one's own fleshly desire to judge or dominate others. The corrector recognizes their own standing before God rests entirely on Christ’s work, not their own moral track record.
2. **Service-oriented, not punitive**: Correction is not an act of power, punishment, or community reputation management. It is an act of burdens-bearing, rooted in love for the person who has stumbled, with the sole aim of repairing harm to their soul and to the body of Christ. It is not about enforcing external rules, but about calling a fellow believer back to the wholeness they were meant to have in their identity in Christ.
3. **Non-judgmental, recognizing shared vulnerability**: The person being corrected is described as someone "overtaken in a fault"—caught off guard by sin, not willfully, obstinately rejecting the faith. The corrector approaches them as a fellow sinner, equally prone to temptation, rather than as a superior moral authority.

### Part 2: Why restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion are inseparable
These three elements are not optional add-ons, but mutually necessary components of biblically faithful correction, each protecting against a common failure of accountability:
1. **Restoration as the non-negotiable goal**
   The Greek term for "restore" (*katartizō*) carries the vivid imagery of setting a broken bone, mending a torn fishing net, or returning a damaged object to its original intended condition. It is inherently healing-focused, not punitive. If correction is separated from restoration, it devolves into public shaming, condemnation, or punishment, which alienates the person being corrected, drives them further from God and community, and fails to address the root harm of sin. Without restoration as the end goal, correction becomes an exercise in the corrector’s moral vanity, not an act of love.
2. **Meekness as the necessary mode of pursuing restoration**
   Meekness (Greek *prautēs*) is not weakness, but "strength under control": a humble, gentle, non-confrontational manner that speaks truth without crushing the other person. It flows directly from the shared identity of corrector and corrected as redeemed sinners: neither is better than the other, both stand forgiven by Christ’s work alone. If restoration is pursued without meekness, it becomes patronizing, harsh, or aggressive, causing the person being corrected to shut down, become defensive, and reject the very help being offered. Meekness ensures the person feels seen and loved even as their sin is confronted, making them open to being restored.
3. **Self-suspicion as the essential safeguard for both corrector and correction**
   The call to "consider thyself, lest thou also be tempted" guards against two deadly dangers:
   - First, it prevents the sin of self-righteousness, the subtle belief that one is morally superior to the person being corrected. As Jesus teaches in Matthew 7:3-5, those who focus on the faults of others while ignoring their own are unfit to help anyone else. Self-suspicion reminds the corrector they are just as vulnerable to temptation, and that their own standing rests on grace, not their own moral performance. Without this, correction devolves into judgment, a sin that disqualifies the corrector and harms the entire community.
   - Second, it protects the corrector from being tempted to commit the same fault they are addressing, or to use correction as an opportunity to elevate their own status in the community. Self-suspicion keeps the corrector honest, ensuring they confront sin only out of love for the other person, not out of a desire to feel better about themselves.

In short, these three elements are inseparable because they all flow from the core gospel truth that undergirds Galatians: all believers are equal sinners, saved entirely by grace, and called to love one another as Christ has loved us. Correction is only faithful when it is rooted in that truth, oriented to healing, carried out with humility, and guarded by self-awareness.
