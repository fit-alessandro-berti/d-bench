Short answer: both. Jeremiah 17:9–10 is soberly realistic about the ease of self-deception, not an invitation to cynical despair. It warns that our inward motives are easily hidden from ourselves, points us to God as the only true discerner, and calls for humility, repentance, and accountability.

Why both pessimistic and realistic
- Pessimistic tone: The verse uses strong language (“deceitful…desperately wicked”) to shock listeners into awareness. In its original context (Jeremiah condemning Judah’s unfaithfulness) it functions as a prophetic indictment: people think they are right but are morally blind.
- Realistic diagnosis: The statement names a real human problem—people do deceive themselves about why they act. That diagnosis is echoed throughout Scripture (e.g., Psalm 139:23–24; Hebrews 4:12; Romans 7–8) and accords with what psychology finds about bias and motivated reasoning.
- The theological balance: Scripture normally holds both truths together — human sinfulness and the image of God in people. Being “deceitful” does not make a person a mere object; it means we need God’s searching, correction, and grace.

How to talk about motives when the heart is an unreliable witness
1. Start with humility and tentativeness
   - Don’t give definitive pronouncements about motives. Use phrases like “I may be mistaken” or “help me understand.”
2. Distinguish behavior from motive
   - You can responsibly evaluate observable behavior and its consequences without claiming to know the inner motive absolutely.
3. Look for patterns and fruit
   - Motives are best inferred from consistent patterns over time, repeated choices, and the kinds of fruit actions produce (Galatians 5:22–23 model).
4. Test against Scripture and God’s character
   - Ask whether motives align with biblical love, justice, and humility. Invite the person to reflect in prayer and Scripture.
5. Use questions, not accusations
   - Ask open, non-defensive questions: “What were you hoping would happen?” “What made this decision feel right to you?” These invite self-reflection rather than shutting down conversation.
6. Invite accountability and confession
   - Create a context where people can admit mixed or wrong motives without fear—confession and correction are part of healthy formation.
7. Be pastoral and proportional
   - Aim for restoration and truth, not a win/loss. When motive may cause harm, act on the harm while offering a path for repentance.
8. Rely on communal discernment
   - Churches, mentors, or spiritual directors can help discern motives more fairly than a lone person’s judgment.
9. Attend to psychological realities
   - Be aware of cognitive biases (self-justification, rationalization). Encourage reflective practices (journaling, soul-searching prayer).
10. Remember God’s role
   - Jeremiah emphasizes that God “search[es] the heart.” We should not pretend to be the ultimate judge of others’ inner states; hold the judgment to God while pursuing truth and grace.

Practical phrases you can use
- “I’m wondering what drove that decision—could you tell me more?”
- “I’m noticing a pattern here; I worry about the effect it’s having. What do you think?”
- “I don’t want to assume I know your heart. Can we talk about what was behind that choice?”
- “From the outside it looks like X; help me understand if something else was going on.”

Brief summary
- Jeremiah is a corrective realism: humans are prone to self-deception; God alone fully knows motives. That should lead us to humility, serious self-examination, communal checks and balances, and a grace-filled approach when talking about others’ motives—focusing on behavior and fruit, asking good questions, and leaving ultimate judgment to God.
