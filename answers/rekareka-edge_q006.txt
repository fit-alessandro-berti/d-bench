The passage from Mark 8:34–35 describes a self that is being *denied by Christ*, reflecting a profound spiritual transformation required to follow Jesus. Here's how this fits and the reasons for divergent interpretations:

### **Understanding the Denial:**
1. **Collective Identity Rejection:**
   - The "self" being denied here is the individual's cultural, social, or even religious identity. Mark (50–70 CE) writes during a tumultuous time in Jewish history, where Pharisaic or imperial conformity was under heavy scrutiny. Denying one's "nationalistic" or " dodgeable" self aligns with Christ's mandate to embrace humility and sacrifice.
   - This rejection is not self-layered but a gift from Christ, transforming the follower into a servant (Mark 1:35–43), prioritizing spiritual life over personal or communal comfort.

2. **Cross-Cultural Freedom:**
   - "Take up his cross" symbolizes sacrifice, regression to humility, and abandonment of self-indulgence, which might be offensive to those preserving their pride or their autonomy. For others, this openness to transformation is liberating, allowing them to live a life more aligned with Christ's mission.

3. **Liberation from Dogma:**
   - The denial implies challenging widely held beliefs (e.g., Jewish biblical traditions, social status) to pursue truth. While ritualistic dogma is rejected, genuine truth becomes a light in darkness, offering clarity beyond superficialities.

---

### **Why It Sounds Liberating or Offensive:**
- **For converts or those seeking deep spiritual transformation:** This self-denial is celebrated as necessary for spiritual growth and alignment with Christ's path. It requires courage to abandon familiar comforts for the sake of mission.
- **For critics or those unfamiliar with Christian piety:** The demands of "denying oneself" can seem extreme or authoritarian, ignoring individual autonomy and hunger for comfort. In secular contexts, this might be viewed as impersonal or unfamiliar.
- **Contextual Relevance:** Some find the passage empowering—especially in a world where individuality is often commodified. Subverting cultural norms can be seen as liberating, granting space for faith-driven change.
- **Cultural sensitivities:**While resonant in many Christian traditions, the fierce rejection of societal identity replicates painful historical experiences (e.g., Jewish persecution). Deciphering this gift for liberation or oppression requires empathy and historical understanding.

### **Conclusion:**
The denial of a self rooted in pride, tradition, or comfort is a call to embrace humility, service, and the cost of faith. For some, this liberation; for others, a critique of cultural insularity. Whether seen as liberating or oppressive depends on one’s journey and interpretation of Christ’s love.
