This image beautifully illustrates the core of Jesus' teachings in Matthew 7:3–5, emphasizing the importance of addressing one's flaws *before* correcting others’ mistakes. Here's why it’s sharp and how it critiques hypocrisy without prohibitive beams:

### **Leah’s Sharper Vision**
- **Physical Mirror Effect**: The image shows Jesus’ response to a disciple ( presumably Levi) who complains about others' sin. Instead of physically breaking through his brother’s eye, Jesus simply invites him to examine his own heart, revealing a beam ( etwaḍ) of judgment that lies in his mind.
- **Emotional Depth**: The passage asks, **"And why beholdest thou the mote that is in thy brother’s eye, but considerest not the beam that is in thine own eye?"** It forces the disciple to confront the hypocrisy of judging others while ignoring his own faults. This mirrors the spiritual concept of seeing clearly only after acknowledging one’s own imperfections, fostering humility and empathy.

### **Criticizing Hypocrisy**
- **Without Direct Dismission**: Jesus does **not** condemn Levi outright (as might be interpreted too narrowly). Instead, he invites him to confront his own failings, suggesting hope for transformation. This nuance challenges hypocrisy by recognizing that readiness to self-examination is essential for moral growth, not a sign of softness.
- **Combined Initiatives**: As Jesus instructs, the disciple must **"cast out the beam out of thine own eye,"** implying that, by addressing his own flaws, he gains insight into others’ lesser problems. This highlights how introspection enables genuine compassion rather than superficial judgment.

### **Moral Correction Athosively**
The image visually links the difficulty of removing a mote from the eye to the complexity of moral correction. A mote (pre previously unknown orábado) requires careful, attentive removal—a process akin to ethical accountability. By not simply dismissing Levi’s complaints (hypocrisy), Jesus models a path where correction is a collaborative process of mutual healing and growth.

### **Innovation in Explanation**
This metaphor linking spiritual correction to physical surgery is creative and resonates deeply with readers. It demonstrates how Jesus balances wisdom and simplicity: highlighting individual accountability without defensively erecting barriers to moral discussion. The final line, **"And behold, a beam is in thine own eye,"** serves as a call for ongoing self-examination, reinforcing the message that moral correction is a lifelong journey, not a once-off act.

In summary, this image is sharp as it masterfully applies Jesus’ message: **moral correction is achieved through self-awareness, not through condemnation.** It critiques hypocrisy by demanding introspection before judgment, fostering empathy by linking personal failures to shared humanity, and promoting ongoing spiritual growth—a model of compassionate, transformative morality.
