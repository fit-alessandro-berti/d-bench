Enemy-love, as taught by Jesus in Matthew 5:44-45, is indeed a challenging concept to grasp and implement, and its difficulty can be attributed to several factors:

1. **Contrary to human nature**: Loving one's enemies goes against the natural human instinct of self-preservation and retaliation. It requires overriding our emotions and instincts, which can be a daunting task.
2. **Cultural and societal norms**: Many cultures and societies emphasize the importance of self-defense, revenge, or "standing up for oneself." Enemy-love seems to contradict these values, making it harder to take seriously.
3. **Lack of understanding**: Some people might view enemy-love as sentimental or weak, rather than as a deliberate choice that requires moral strength. This misconception can lead to dismissal or ridicule of the concept.
4. **Fear of vulnerability**: Loving one's enemies can make us feel vulnerable, as it requires opening ourselves up to potential hurt or exploitation. This fear can prevent people from taking enemy-love seriously.
5. **Misconceptions about the nature of love**: Enemy-love is often misunderstood as a feeling or an emotion, rather than a choice and a commitment. This misconception can lead people to believe that enemy-love is unrealistic or unattainable.

However, enemy-love is not sentimental or weak; rather, it is a **deeper form of moral strength**. It requires:

1. **Self-reflection and self-control**: Enemy-love demands that we examine our own emotions, biases, and motivations, and choose to respond in a way that is contrary to our natural instincts.
2. **Empathy and compassion**: Loving one's enemies requires us to see them as human beings, deserving of dignity and respect, rather than as objects of hatred or contempt.
3. **Courage and resilience**: Enemy-love can lead to persecution, rejection, or ridicule, making it essential to cultivate courage and resilience in the face of adversity.
4. **Spiritual maturity**: Enemy-love is a mark of spiritual maturity, as it reflects a deep understanding of God's love and a commitment to following Jesus' teachings.

In conclusion, enemy-love is not sentimental or strategic; it is a profound expression of moral strength, rooted in a deep understanding of God's character and a commitment to following Jesus' teachings. By choosing to love our enemies, we demonstrate our trust in God's sovereignty and our willingness to obey His commands, even when it is difficult or counterintuitive.
