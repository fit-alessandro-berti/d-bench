The elder brother's deepest problem can be seen as a combination of several factors, but arguably his most significant issue is an inability to grasp and accept the concept of grace and mercy. His anger and resentment stem from a sense of entitlement and pride in his obedience to his father's rules, which leads him to feel that he deserves more recognition and reward than his wayward brother. 

His perspective is rooted in fairness; he believes that his years of faithful service should earn him a special status or reward, whereas he perceives the celebration of his brother's return as an unjust snub to his loyalty. This wounded sense of fairness amplifies his envious feelings toward the attention and affection his brother receives. 

Ultimately, the elder brother's discomfort with mercy highlights a deeper spiritual malaise—he cannot rejoice in his brother's redemption because he is unable to see beyond his own merit and entitlement. Thus, while anger, envy, and wounded fairness play significant roles in his reaction, it is his pride and inability to embrace the mercy that his father represents that reflects his deepest problem.
