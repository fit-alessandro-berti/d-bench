Short answer: the verse is condemning the motive — doing good in order to be seen and praised by people — not all visibility of good. The problem is the craving for human approval, which corrupts the moral character of the act.

Why motive matters here
- Matthew 6 (part of the Sermon on the Mount) contrasts “hypocrites” who perform pious deeds “so that they may be seen” (Greek: ὅπως ὀφθῶσιν αὐτοῖς) with the person who serves God in secret and receives “reward…from your Father.” The emphasis is on intention: doing good in order to win applause replaces God as the intended audience and turns a self-giving act into self-promotion.
- That craving for visibility produces hypocrisy (appearance different from heart), makes the actor dependent on human praise, and corrupts the spiritual value of the deed. Jesus’s charge is about inner righteousness, not merely outward behavior.

Visibility itself is not condemned
- The New Testament elsewhere blesses public witness (e.g., “let your light shine” Matthew 5:16) and public ministry/care is often necessary (feeding the poor publicly to meet needs, public testimony, teaching, charitable institutions). Public good can inspire others, provide accountability, and meet communal needs.
- The difference is intention: making a deed known to serve others, teach, or encourage is morally different from doing it primarily to be admired.

Practical distinctions (how to apply it)
- Ask why: “Would I do this if no one knew?” or “Am I aiming to glorify God/help others, or to be admired?”
- If publicity serves the beneficiaries or a higher purpose (e.g., encouraging generosity, reporting charitable use of funds, advocacy), it can be appropriate.
- If it’s primarily to increase one’s status, cultivate humility: pray about motive, give discreetly when possible, accept necessary public roles as stewardship rather than self-promotion.

In short: the verse warns against the moral danger of seeking human applause; visibility is a neutral tool that becomes morally dangerous when the craving to be seen is the driving aim.
