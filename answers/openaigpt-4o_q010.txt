In Philippians 2:5–8, the kind of power being praised is the power of humility and self-sacrifice. This passage emphasizes the extraordinary nature of Christ's humility, despite his divine status. Jesus, although being in the form of God, chose not to cling to his equality with God as something to be exploited. Instead, he emptied himself, taking on the form of a servant and becoming obedient to the point of death on a cross.

This form of power—humility joined to greatness—can feel more unsettling than open domination because it challenges conventional understandings of power and authority. Dominant power often relies on coercion, control, and the explicit exertion of authority over others. It is predictable and fits with many societal expectations of what it means to be powerful.

On the other hand, humility combined with greatness subverts these norms. It introduces a paradox: the mightiest being choosing to become the least and serve others sacrificially. This form of power is unsettling because it reveals a strength that doesn't need to assert itself aggressively to be effective. It questions our assumptions about what true strength and leadership entail, suggesting that real power is found in service, love, and self-giving for the sake of others. This challenges individuals to reflect on their own views of power and consider the potential transformative impact of humility.
