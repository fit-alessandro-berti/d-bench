Hebrews’ “root” image is deliberate because bitterness is usually not first experienced as an obvious, outward sin. It’s more like something that **takes hold inwardly**, beneath the surface, and then later produces visible “fruit.”

**1) Root vs. act (hidden source vs. outward action)**
- An **act** is something you do in a moment (a word, a decision, a blow).
- A **root** is what **feeds** and **generates** later actions.  
So bitterness is described as a root because it tends to begin quietly—through repeated dwelling, refusing reconciliation, rehearsing hurts, and letting resentment “settle.” Only afterward does it work its way out into speech, attitudes, avoidance, conflict, and injustice.

**2) Delayed damage (it grows before it shows)**
Roots don’t produce immediate fruit. They grow underground over time, and the damage often becomes obvious only later:
- you may “act fine” while bitterness is developing,
- then suddenly it shows up as harshness, withdrawal, or retaliation.
That matches the warning: “looking diligently” is needed because you may not notice the danger until it has already started shaping you.

**3) Spreading effect (one resentment can defile others)**
The passage says a “root of bitterness” can “trouble you, and thereby many be defiled.” The metaphor suggests bitterness can:
- contaminate the atmosphere—your spirit affects the group,
- influence how you interpret events,
- cause others to take sides, adopt your suspicion, or be pulled into the same resentment.
So the damage isn’t limited to the individual who feels wronged; it can **infect relationships** and community health, like roots sending out effects that touch more than one plant.

In short: bitterness is called a “root” because it’s often **internal and formative**, it causes **gradual, hard-to-detect moral growth** that later becomes outward behavior, and it can **propagate**, affecting “many” beyond the first person.
