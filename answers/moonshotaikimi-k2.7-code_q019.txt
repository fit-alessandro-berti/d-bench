The passage exposes the **contradiction between our worship and our relationships**.

We use the same tongue, the same mouth, to:

- **bless God** — to call Him holy, good, and worthy of honor, and  
- **curse human beings** — who are “made after the similitude of God,” that is, made in God’s image.

James is saying: you cannot consistently honor God while despising, insulting, or verbally attacking people who bear God’s likeness. To bless God and curse His image-bearer is a kind of spiritual hypocrisy — it splits the heart in two. The same organ produces both “sweet water and bitter” (James 3:11).

### Why speech reveals moral disorder so quickly

Speech is revealing because it is **close to the heart**. James has already said the tongue is “a little member, and boasteth great things” (James 3:5) and “defileth the whole body” (3:6). Words do not require much time, strength, or planning; they slip out before we can filter them. Because of that, the tongue often shows what is really going on inside us — anger, envy, prejudice, contempt, pride — even while our public worship still sounds pious.

In other words, the mouth is like a window into the soul: **“Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh.”** When blessing and cursing come from the same mouth, it shows that the heart is divided — loving God in theory, but not loving what God loves. That is why James says, “My brethren, these things ought not so to be.”
