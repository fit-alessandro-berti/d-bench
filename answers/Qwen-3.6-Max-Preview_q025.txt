Based on the text of Matthew 6:1 and its immediate context in the Sermon on the Mount, the moral danger lies not in **visibility itself**, but in the **craving to be seen** (the motive).

Here’s a breakdown of why this distinction is crucial:

### 1. The Key Phrase: “To Be Seen of Them”
The Greek phrase translated as “to be seen of them” (*pros to theathēnai autois*) implies purpose or intent. Jesus is not condemning acts that are *observed* by others, but acts performed *for the purpose* of being observed. The problem is the **motive**: seeking human approval rather than divine approval.

### 2. Visibility Is Not Inherently Wrong
Elsewhere in the same sermon, Jesus says:
> “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven.” (Matthew 5:16)

This shows that visibility can be morally good when the goal is to point people to God. The difference is:
- **Matthew 5:16**: Good works are visible → People see them → They glorify **God**.
- **Matthew 6:1**: Alms are done visibly → People see them → They glorify **you**.

### 3. The Danger: Idolatry of Human Approval
The moral danger is that the actor replaces God with human audience as the source of validation. This turns an act of charity into an act of self-promotion. Jesus calls such people “hypocrites” (Matthew 6:2), a term originally referring to actors wearing masks—people performing a role for applause rather than living out genuine virtue.

### 4. The Consequence: Loss of Divine Reward
Jesus states that if you seek human praise, “you have no reward of your Father which is in heaven.” This doesn’t necessarily mean God punishes you, but that you have already received your full payment: human admiration. There is no further spiritual reward because the act was never directed toward God in the first place.

### Conclusion
The problem is **not visibility**, but **vanity**.  
Public goodness becomes morally dangerous when it is performed **to be seen** rather than **to serve**. The heart’s orientation—toward human applause or divine honor—is what determines the moral value of the act.
