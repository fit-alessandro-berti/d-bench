This passage from Matthew 6 (part of Jesus’ Sermon on the Mount) does not frame “treasure revealing the heart” and “treasure training the heart” as mutually exclusive. Instead, it uses the diagnostic logic of the present to argue for the transformative logic of the future, and the three dynamics of possession, attention, and love are tightly interwoven in this causal framework:

### First: Does the text say treasure reveals or trains the heart?
Both, but the primary rhetorical emphasis is formative (treasure trains the heart), with the revelatory (treasure reveals the heart) serving as a supporting diagnostic:
1.  **Revelatory (diagnostic) function**: As a straightforward observation of how human attachment works, the verse notes that your current treasure reliably exposes where your heart is already fixed. If you are uncertain whether your priorities align with God’s, you need only look at what you invest your time, money, and emotional energy in: that is your treasure, and it reveals your present loyalties. This is why the following verses’ warning against serving “God and mammon” (wealth/ultimate security) lands so sharply: you cannot claim divided devotion, because your treasure will expose where your true allegiance lies.
2.  **Formative (transformative) function, the primary emphasis of the passage**: The verse is part of an explicit exhortation: Jesus commands his listeners to stop hoarding “treasures on earth” (material wealth, status, or any finite, vulnerable source of security) and instead “lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven” (the reign of God, justice, compassion, fidelity to God). The causal “where your treasure is, there will your heart be also” is the promised outcome of that command: it is a guarantee that reorienting what you treasure will train your heart to align with that treasure, moving you from attachment to fleeting, corruptible earthly goods to love of eternal, divine goods. The future tense of the Greek phrase “there will your heart be also” (ἐκεῖ ἔσται καὶ ἡ καρδία ὑμῶν) confirms this is a forward-looking promise of change, not merely a description of current reality.

---

### How do possession, attention, and love tie together here?
These three dynamics form a closed, self-reinforcing loop rooted in the passage’s logic of attachment:
1.  **Possession as ultimate devotion**: The “treasure” in view is not neutral material goods, but whatever you hold as your ultimate source of security, worth, and loyalty. For the first-century audience, this was most often literal wealth (land, grain, silver) vulnerable to moth, rust, and theft, but the principle extends to any object of ultimate devotion: social status, professional achievement, even religious ritual if it becomes an end in itself. Possession here is not passive ownership: the objects we treasure exert a claim on us as much as we exert claim on them. A hoard of wealth demands time, effort, and loyalty to protect and grow it, just as a commitment to God’s reign demands investment of oneself in its values.
2.  **Attention as the bridge between possession and love**: Human finitude means our attention is a limited resource, directed inevitably by what we value. If your treasure is earthly, your attention will be monopolized by efforts to protect, acquire, and maintain it: you will worry about market crashes, theft, damage to your property, or the opinions of others that validate your status. If your treasure is heavenly, your attention will be directed to cultivating those goods: caring for the marginalized, seeking wisdom, building communities that reflect God’s justice. Attention is not a neutral act: sustained focus on an object shapes our desires and affections over time.
3.  **Love as the core of the heart’s orientation**: In the biblical imagination, the “heart” (kardia) is the seat of the will, covenant loyalty, and love, not just fleeting emotion. To “treasure” something is to love it, to be committed to it, to find one’s ultimate fulfillment in it. The three dynamics connect in a causal chain: What you possess as your ultimate treasure becomes the object of your love and loyalty; that love directs your finite attention to that treasure; and that sustained attention, in turn, trains your heart (your core will, priorities, and desires) to align more and more closely with that treasure. As the immediately following verses make explicit, this is why we cannot serve two masters: divided treasure splits our love, fragments our attention, and fractures our heart.

This loop also has explicit communal implications in the context of the Sermon on the Mount: in the first century, hoarding earthly treasure was almost always tied to systemic exploitation of the poor, so the possession of wealth as an ultimate good drew collective attention away from caring for the vulnerable, and perverted collective love toward self-enrichment rather than neighbor-love, training entire communities away from the justice of God’s reign.
