### Response

Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously not because it is sentimental or merely strategic, but because it challenges the very foundations of human instinct, social conditioning, and moral intuition.

At first glance, it may seem **sentimental**—a soft, idealistic notion that ignores the reality of conflict, injustice, and the pain caused by enemies. We are wired to protect ourselves, to retaliate when wronged, and to draw clear boundaries. To command love toward those who harm us appears, in a world governed by power and retribution, as unrealistic sentimentality.

Yet, to reduce it to sentimentality is to misunderstand its depth. It is not about feeling warm affection or emotional reconciliation in the moment. It is not dependent on emotion at all. Jesus does not say, “Love your enemies *because* you feel compassionate toward them,” but “Love your enemies”—a command that transcends emotion. It is an act of the will, a deliberate orientation of the heart and actions, even when hatred is justified.

Nor is it primarily **strategic**—though it may yield strategic results. Turning the other cheek, blessing those who curse you, and praying for persecutors can disarm hostility, expose injustice, and create space for transformation. In this sense, enemy-love can be a powerful nonviolent resistance, a form of moral subversion. But reducing it to mere strategy risks turning it into a tool rather than a transformation of self. If the goal is only to win or manipulate, the integrity of the practice collapses.

What makes enemy-love truly difficult—and profound—is that it demands a **deeper form of moral strength**. It requires the kind of courage that refuses to be shaped by the cycle of violence. It insists that hatred, no matter how justified it may seem, ultimately dehumanizes the one who harbors it. To love an enemy is to assert that they are still human, still capable of change, still within the reach of grace. It is to emulate a divine character—“that ye may be the children of your Father”—not by imitation in a superficial sense, but by participating in a divine ethic of generosity and impartiality.

This kind of love is not natural to human nature; it is supernatural in its depth. It is sustained not by emotional goodwill but by a vision of reality that sees beyond the immediate conflict to a larger story of redemption and justice. It is morally strong because it chooses long-term transformation over short-term vengeance, and it dares to believe that love can overcome hate—not by denying hate, but by refusing to let hate have the final word.

In this light, enemy-love is not weak, sentimental, or merely tactical. It is the most radical expression of moral courage—a quiet, relentless defiance of retribution, rooted in the conviction that God’s love is stronger than all forms of hatred.
