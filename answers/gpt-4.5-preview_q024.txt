The difference between external compliance and receiving a “new heart” lies in the depth and authenticity of change: external compliance is simply behavior modified from outside by rules or fear of consequences, whereas receiving a “new heart” means an internal, transformational renewal of one’s desires, motives, and disposition.

In describing hardness as a "stony heart," the passage emphasizes a material, almost tangible quality to spiritual resistance. A "stony heart" implies rigidity, insensitivity, and inability to respond genuinely to God or others—like stone, incapable of growth, feeling, or authentic love. By contrast, the "heart of flesh" symbolizes tenderness, sensitivity, responsiveness, and a capacity to genuinely desire and fulfill God's will out of inner conviction rather than external compulsion.

Thus, Ezekiel’s imagery underscores the depth of true transformation. It suggests that mere outward adherence, driven by obligation or coercion, is insufficient. Genuine obedience flows from inward renewal—a new set of desires shaped by God’s Spirit, reshaping one’s moral character from within, enabling authentic, joyful, and consistent obedience.
