The moral danger is **not visibility itself, but the craving to be seen**—the intentionality behind the act.

The verse hinges on a purpose clause: you are not to do alms *“to be seen of them”* (Greek: *pros to theathēnai autois*, literally “with a view toward being watched as a spectacle”). Jesus does not forbid being observed; he forbids **performing righteousness for an audience**.

Several clues in the text and its context make this clear:

**1. The intent determines the reward**
The warning is “otherwise ye have no reward of your Father.” In verses 2, 5, and 16, Jesus repeats the formula about hypocrites: *“they have their reward”*—meaning human applause is the full payment. The act is not evil because eyes are present; it is hollow because the heart has already cashed the check with human praise, leaving nothing to receive from God.

**2. The contrast with Matthew 5:16**
Just chapters earlier, Jesus commands: *“Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven.”* There, visibility is commanded—**provided the goal is God’s glory, not your own**. Matthew 6:1 targets the same visibility but with a corrupted motive. The problem is the theater of self-promotion, not the public stage.

**3. The hypocrisy that follows**
In the very next verses (6:2–6, 16–18), Jesus describes people who blow trumpets, pray on street corners, and disfigure their faces. These are not accidentally noticed; they engineer notice. The Greek *hypokritēs* (actor) fits precisely: they are playing the part of piety for spectators. The danger is that charity becomes costume.

**What makes it morally dangerous?**
- **It splits loyalty.** You cannot serve two audiences. Seeking human validation makes God redundant.
- **It corrupts virtue from within.** Almsgiving is meant to flow from compassion and love for God’s image; when filtered through vanity, it becomes self-advertisement wearing a mask of kindness.
- **It replaces reality with performance.** The “goodness” is no longer for the recipient or for God, but for the ego of the giver. The recipient becomes a prop.

So the verse warns against **performative righteousness**—goodness calculated as public relations. The eye of others is not the poison; the thirst for their admiration is. When that thirst drives the act, you have received exactly what you wanted, and lost what only the Father could give.
