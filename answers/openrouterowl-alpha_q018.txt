This pairing reveals that bitterness and forgiveness placed in opposition are not merely two things we feel — they are two ways of standing before reality, before the past, and before other people. Bitterness is treated here less as a fleeting emotion and more as something the whole person takes on. The Greek word *pikría* implies not just being upset, but becoming edged, corrosive, easily provoked into sin. The exhortation “be put away from you, with all malice” shows it is not a passive state; it settles in like poison, shaping how someone sees and speaks. Forgiveness, on the other hand, is presented not as ignoring wrong, but as a deliberate posture of release—tenderhearted instead of hardened, conscious of one’s own need for mercy. By placing them side by side, the text suggests that bitterness and forgiveness are not private feelings but social postures: one tightens and infects relationships; the other loosens and heals them. In this sense, both the breath of forgiveness are treated as contagious.
