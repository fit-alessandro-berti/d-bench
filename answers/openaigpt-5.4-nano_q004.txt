Proverbs 3:5–6 is asking for **a covenantal, faithful trust** in God—trust that is wholehearted and practical, not merely an emotion or a slogan.

### 1) What kind of trust is meant?
- **Total reliance (“with all thine heart”)**: not “partly God, partly me,” but a conscious decision to treat God as the ultimate authority for wisdom and guidance.
- **A posture of learning and submission (“acknowledge him in all thy ways”)**: you actively bring your decisions, plans, and interpretation of life *under God’s concern and direction*.
- **Dependence rather than self-sufficiency**: you accept that God’s guidance is needed because human understanding is limited and easily distorted.

### 2) Humble self-distrust (the kind Proverbs promotes)
This is **not** “I know nothing” or “thinking is bad.” It’s more like:
- “My understanding is limited and prone to error.”
- “Even when I’m confident, I may be missing what God sees.”
- “Therefore I will not treat my own judgment as final; I will verify and align it with God’s way.”

That is **intellectual humility**: trusting God does not erase reasoning; it keeps reasoning from becoming ultimate.

### 3) Distinguishing it from anti-intellectualism
**Anti-intellectualism** says (in effect):
- “Don’t think.”
- “Reasoning is inherently corrupt.”
- “Education/analysis is unnecessary or suspect.”

But Proverbs’ command is **specifically** about *leaning on one’s understanding as an alternative authority*, not about using understanding at all. The phrase “lean not unto thine own understanding” targets **self-reliant autonomy**—using your interpretation as the final court of appeal—rather than **the act of reasoning itself**.

### 4) Why the next verse matters
“In all thy ways acknowledge him, and he shall direct thy paths” implies that:
- you still *walk in ways* (i.e., make plans, choices, judgments),
- but you acknowledge God as you do so, and you expect guidance in the process.

So the “trust” is **faithful dependence + humility about the limits of your own judgment**, not a refusal of thinking.
