This passage from **Isaiah 55:6–7** is a powerful call to repentance, emphasizing both **external actions** (“way”) and **internal attitudes** (“thoughts”). Here’s an analysis of why both are included and what this implies:

---

### 1. **Repentance Involves Both “Way” and “Thoughts”**

- **“Way” (path/life):**  
  This refers to one’s outward conduct—how a person lives, acts, and treats others. It encompasses behavior, choices, and habits.
- **“Thoughts”:**  
  This addresses the inner life—the intentions, motivations, and mental attitudes behind actions.

By including both, the text acknowledges that sin is not only about what we do but also about what we think and desire. True repentance requires transformation at both levels.

---

### 2. **Implications for Outward Conduct**

- **Change in Actions:**  
  When someone “forsakes his way,” they turn away from harmful or sinful behaviors. This could mean ceasing to lie, steal, harm others, or pursue unjust ends.
- **Restoration of Relationships:**  
  Changing one’s way often leads to healing broken relationships and restoring trust with others and with God.

---

### 3. **Implications for Inward Imagination**

- **Transformation of Heart:**  
  “Let him return unto the Lord, and he will abundantly pardon” suggests that repentance must begin within—the heart must turn toward God, abandoning wickedness and embracing righteousness.
- **Renewed Desires:**  
  Repentance involves reorienting one’s desires and aspirations away from selfishness, pride, or evil, and toward God’s justice and mercy.

---

### 4. **Possibility of Mercy**

- **God’s Readiness:**  
  The promise of “abundantly pardon” shows that God’s mercy is vast and generous, available to those who truly repent.
- **Conditional on Genuine Change:**  
  The passage makes clear that God’s mercy is contingent upon genuine repentance—not just superficial regret, but real change in both action and thought.

---

### **Summary Table**

| Aspect      | What it Involves                                  | Example                                 |
|-------------|---------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| Way         | Outward actions, behavior                         | Stopping lying, stealing, violence      |
| Thoughts    | Inner attitudes, intentions                      | Turning away from hatred, envy         |
| Mercy       | God’s willingness to forgive when true repentance | Forgiveness after sincere change       |

---

### **Conclusion**

Repentance in Isaiah 55:6–7 is holistic—it calls for both external reform (“way”) and internal transformation (“thoughts”). This reflects the biblical understanding that sin affects every part of a person, and true forgiveness comes only when there is genuine change in both deed and desire. The passage assures us that God’s mercy is abundant for those who seek Him sincerely and turn away from wickedness in all its forms.
