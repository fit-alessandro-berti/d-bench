# The Elder Brother's Core Problem

Your options aren't mutually exclusive—they're layered—but the deepest issue appears to be **a fundamental misunderstanding of his relationship with his father**.

## The Revealing Detail

Notice what he says he's *never* received: "thou never gavest me a kid, that I might make merry with my friends."

He frames his obedience as a **transaction**—decades of service rendered without the promised reward. This suggests he's never grasped that:

- **He already has everything** ("all that I have is thine")
- His father's love wasn't conditional on earning it through compliance
- He's been free to celebrate all along

## Why This Matters

His anger, envy, and sense of wounded fairness all flow from this transactional logic. He's not actually upset about *justice*—he's upset that the payment structure doesn't match his expectations. He's made himself a servant when he could have been a son.

**Discomfort with mercy itself** is the symptom of this deeper wound. Mercy seems unfair *only if* you believe love must be earned.

## The Tragedy

The elder brother is in some ways more lost than the younger. The prodigal knew he'd strayed; the elder doesn't recognize his own alienation—from his father's heart and his own freedom.

The father's final words (implied: come celebrate) are an invitation to *finally* understand sonship.
