
Excellent question. You’ve touched on the profound paradox at the heart of this famous Christological hymn.

### 1. The Kind of Power Being Praised

The power being praised here is **kenotic power**—power expressed through self-emptying (from the Greek *kenosis* in verse 7: "made himself of no reputation"). It is:

*   **Authority through surrender:** Christ, "in the very nature of God" (form of God), does not see His divine status as something to be "grasped" or exploited for His own advantage. His equality with God is not a tool for domination.
*   **Power perfected in weakness:** His greatness is not displayed in a triumphant, unchallenged reign, but in His willingness to become a servant, to be obedient, and to suffer the most humiliating and excruciating death imaginable.
*   **Redemptive vulnerability:** The power is in the choice to become powerless. By entering fully into human fragility and suffering, He accomplishes a redemption that coercion or display of raw force never could. The power is *for* others, not *over* them.

This is the power of **love that sacrifices, not power that controls**. It is the power to transform from within, not to subdue from without.

### 2. Why Humility Joined to Greatness Feels More Unsettling Than Open Domination

This is the psychological and existential crux of your question. Open domination is a **familiar, external threat**. Humility-from-greatness is an **internal, destabilizing mirror**.

**Why open domination feels less unsettling:**
*   **Predictable & Containable:** It operates by clear rules of force and fear. You know your place, the boundaries, and the consequences of transgression. You can resist, rebel, or submit as strategies. It confirms the world as a hierarchy of power.
*   **Externalizes the Conflict:** The enemy is "out there." Your struggle is against a visible, opposing force. This can even forge solidarity among the oppressed.

**Why humility-from-greatness is profoundly unsettling:**
*   **It Inverts the Cosmic Order:** It shatters our deepest assumptions. If the ultimate, highest being chooses the path of the lowest servant, then our entire ladder of status, achievement, and power-mongering is revealed as absurd. It doesn't just change the rules; it declares the game itself meaningless.
*   **It Holds Up a Mirror:** This humility is not the humility of the weak, but of the **strong who could have chosen otherwise**. It exposes our own motivations. Our service is often conditional, our humility often a strategy. The humility of Christ is absolute and free, making our own look like self-serving performance.
*   **It Demands a Response, Not Just a Reaction:** Domination provokes a fight-or-flight reaction. Kenotic greatness provokes a crisis of identity. It asks, "If this is true greatness, what does that make me? What should I now become?" This internal call to transformation is more invasive and demanding than any external command.
*   **It Is Uncontrollable:** You cannot conquer or negotiate with this kind of power. You can only reject it (and thus remain in your pride) or surrender to its logic (which feels like death to the ego). There is no middle ground of grudging respect for a superior foe. It offers only love or indifference.

**In essence:** Open domination says, **"Bow before my strength."** Humility-from-greatness says, **"Become like me—in your very heart—and you will find your life."** The first is a challenge to your body; the second is a challenge to your soul. The second is therefore more unsettling because it doesn't just threaten your position—it offers to redefine your very being, and in doing so, it quietly accuses the foundations of the self you have built.

This is why the Cross is both the ultimate scandal and the ultimate wisdom. It presents a God who is not a sovereign to be feared, but a lover to be trusted—and that re-ordering of reality is what makes it so deeply, unsettlingly good.
