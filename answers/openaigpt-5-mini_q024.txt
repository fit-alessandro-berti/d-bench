Short answer
- External compliance is doing what God (or others) require outwardly — following rules, rituals, or moral behavior without a changed inner disposition.  
- Receiving a “new heart” is an inward, existential change of desires, affections and will so that obedience flows from love and delight in God rather than from fear, habit, or mere duty.  
- Ezekiel’s “stony heart” → “heart of flesh” language pictures the difference between a dead, unresponsive interior and a living, receptive one. The image of replacing stone with flesh stresses that true faith is not merely cosmetic repair of behavior but a radical renewal of the person’s inner life (something only God can do).

Why Ezekiel uses the “material” imagery
- In ancient Hebrew thought the heart (leb/lebab) is the center of thinking, feeling, choosing — what we would call will/affection. Calling it “stone” highlights hardness, insensitivity, and refusal; “flesh” suggests life, vulnerability, responsiveness.  
- The metaphor of replacement (remove the stony heart, give a heart of flesh) underscores the permanence and thoroughness of God’s work: habits and external compliance can be patched, but the fundamental orientation of a person must be transformed.  
- Stone cannot simply be softened into flesh; the point is the need for a creative, sovereign act by God to produce a new moral capacity. That’s why Ezekiel immediately pairs it with God’s promise to put his Spirit within them (v.27): spiritual renewal is capacitating, not merely instructive.

How this contrasts with mere external compliance
- External compliance: obeys rules but can remain self-centered, legalistic, hypocritical, or brittle. It often collapses when pressures change or motives are weak.  
- New heart: changes motives and affections so that keeping God’s statutes becomes natural, joyful, and sustained. Obedience becomes evidence of inner life, not a substitute for it.  
- Biblical parallels: Jeremiah 31:33 (law written on the heart), Jesus’ talk of new birth (John 3), Paul on the Spirit enabling obedience (Romans 8; 2 Cor 3).

Practical implications
- You can test whether change is chiefly external or inward by asking about desires, recurring temptations, the reasons for obedience, and the fruit (love, humility, patience).  
- Pastoral hope: God’s promise is not merely better behavior through education or discipline, but a new, living relationship effected by the Spirit.

Summary
Ezekiel’s contrast teaches that God’s covenant promise is about inner transformation — not just outward conformity. The “material” imagery dramatizes how radical and creative that change is: God will replace a dead, unresponsive center with a living, responsive one so that obedience springs from renewed life rather than mere external compliance.
