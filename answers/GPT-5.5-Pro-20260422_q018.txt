Bitterness and forgiveness are placed so directly against each other because they are **two opposite ways of carrying injury**.

Bitterness keeps the wrong alive as a grievance. Forgiveness receives God’s mercy as the deeper reality and then treats the other person from that mercy. Paul is not merely contrasting a bad emotion with a nice emotion; he is contrasting two moral-spiritual atmospheres.

In Ephesians 4:31, the movement seems to run from the inward to the outward:

> “bitterness, and wrath, and anger, and clamour, and evil speaking… with all malice”

Bitterness is listed first. It is the inward sourness, the retained resentment, the unresolved grievance. Then come wrath and anger, then clamour and evil speaking: the inner condition becomes noise, accusation, and relational damage. “Malice” names the settled ill will underneath it all.

So bitterness is **more than a feeling**, though it may begin as one. A person may feel hurt, shock, anger, or grief without yet being guilty of bitterness. But bitterness is what happens when pain is **kept, rehearsed, justified, and allowed to harden**. It becomes a way of seeing the offender, then a way of speaking, then a way of inhabiting the community.

That is why forgiveness is the direct opposite:

> “be ye kind one to another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another…”

Paul does not merely say, “Stop being bitter.” He says, in effect, “Let that whole economy of resentment be removed, and put on a different economy: kindness, tenderheartedness, forgiveness.”

Forgiveness here is not presented as pretending nothing happened. Nor is it mere emotional softness. It is grounded in something objective:

> “even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you.”

The pattern is: God has dealt graciously with you in Christ; therefore, do not make resentment the law by which you deal with others. Forgiveness is grace received becoming grace practiced.

So to your categories: the text treats bitterness as **a feeling that can become a habit, a chosen posture, and something contagious**.

It is a **feeling**, because bitterness has an emotional texture: sourness, resentment, inner offense.

It is a **habit**, because it can be nursed and repeated until it becomes one’s normal response.

It is a **chosen posture**, because Paul commands it to be “put away.” He is addressing what believers must no longer shelter or defend.

And it is **contagious**, because in this passage bitterness does not stay private. It spills into “clamour” and “evil speaking.” It infects the shared life of the body. Ephesians 4 is deeply communal: Paul has been speaking about truth, speech, unity, and not grieving the Holy Spirit. Bitterness is dangerous because it turns wounded people into wound-spreaders.

Forgiveness, then, is not simply the opposite emotion. It is the opposite **spiritual direction**. Bitterness says, “I will keep the wound as a claim against you.” Forgiveness says, “Because God has forgiven me in Christ, I will not let this wrong rule my heart, my speech, or my treatment of you.”

The sharp contrast is there because both bitterness and forgiveness deal with the same raw material: real wrongs, real wounds, real debts. Bitterness preserves the debt as poison. Forgiveness entrusts the debt to God and refuses to become what the wound would make of us.
