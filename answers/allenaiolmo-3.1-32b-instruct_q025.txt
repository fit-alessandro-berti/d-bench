Let’s break down the verse and the question:

> “Take heed that ye do not your alms before men, to be seen of them: otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven.”

This is Jesus speaking, warning against performing acts of righteousness (like giving alms) for the purpose of being seen by others.

### What makes public goodness morally dangerous here?

The danger isn’t simply that good deeds are done in public. The problem is the *motivation* behind the action. If someone gives alms to be seen by others—to gain praise, recognition, or status—the act is no longer pure goodness. It becomes self-serving.

- **If the motive is to be seen and admired by people,** the act is corrupted. It ceases to be about love for God or neighbor and becomes about oneself and how others perceive you.
- **The result, according to Jesus, is that such a person “has no reward from the Father in heaven.”** In other words, if your good deed is rooted in seeking human approval, you miss the true reward, which is spiritual and comes from God.

### Is the problem visibility itself, or the craving to be seen?

The problem is **not visibility itself**, but **the craving to be seen and praised by others**.

- **Visibility is not inherently bad.** Jesus himself performed many miracles and good deeds publicly. The Bible records them, and Jesus didn’t forbid public acts of goodness.
- **The issue is the motive.** If you give alms secretly, your motive can be pure: you want to help someone without seeking credit or reward.
- But if you give publicly *for the purpose of being seen*, your motive is impure. The act becomes about you, not about God or the recipient.

So, the verse is not condemning public good deeds, but rather warning against turning them into opportunities for self-glorification.

### In summary:

- **The moral danger** is when good deeds are done for the wrong reason—seeking human recognition rather than seeking to please God.
- **It’s not visibility itself that’s dangerous,** but the *craving to be seen and praised by people* that corrupts the act.

This teaching is echoed in other places in the New Testament, such as when Jesus says, “When thou prayest, thou shalt not be as the hypocrites are: for they love to pray standing in the synagogues and in the corners of the streets, that they may be seen of men... But thou, when thou prayest, enter into thy closet...” (Matthew 6:5-6). The principle is the same: focus on God, not on human approval.
