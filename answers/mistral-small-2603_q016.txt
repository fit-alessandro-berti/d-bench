The elder brother’s deepest problem is **pride in obedience**—specifically, the belief that his righteousness is transactional and deserves reward, while his brother’s repentance is treated as an exception that warrants mercy. His anger and envy stem from this underlying issue.

### Breakdown:
1. **Pride in Obedience**: He boasts, *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment"*—implying his service is meritorious and should be rewarded. His obedience is not out of love but a sense of entitlement.
2. **Wounded Fairness**: He resents the fatted calf being given to his brother, seeing it as unfair (*"thou never gavest me a kid"*). His sense of justice is skewed—he expects reward for duty, not grace.
3. **Discomfort with Mercy**: He cannot rejoice over his brother’s return because he doesn’t understand grace. His focus is on what *he* deserves, not on restoration or celebration.
4. **Anger and Envy**: These are symptoms of his deeper pride—they reveal his heart’s attachment to his own perceived righteousness over his brother’s redemption.

### Jesus’ Response:
The father’s words (*"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine"*) highlight the elder brother’s misplaced focus. He already has everything—yet he resents the father’s generosity because he sees love as a zero-sum game. His problem isn’t just anger; it’s a **failure to grasp the father’s heart**, which is the core of the parable (Luke 15:11–32).

**Conclusion**: The elder brother’s deepest issue is **pride in his own righteousness**, which blinds him to grace and fractures his relationship with the father and brother.
