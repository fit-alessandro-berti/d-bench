This is an excellent and perceptive question. The text from Matthew 6:19-21 primarily says that treasure **reveals** the heart, but in doing so, it implicitly describes a cycle where revelation can lead to training.

Let's break down the logic of the passage:

### 1. Revelation, Not Training (The Primary Reading)
The key is in the final, conclusive verse: **"For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also."**

*   **The Structure of the Argument:** Jesus starts with a command ("Do not lay up... But lay up...") and then provides the **reason** ("For..."). The reason explains the spiritual principle behind the command.
*   **The Grammar:** The phrase "there **will** your heart **be**" is a statement of fact, a prediction. It's not a command ("make your heart be there") nor a promise of future training ("your heart will learn to go there"). It's a diagnostic: your heart's true allegiance is *already manifested* by where you choose to place your treasure (your resources, energy, focus).
*   **The Metaphor:** Earthly treasure is subject to "moth and rust" and "thieves." Heavenly treasure is imperishable. The choice of where to invest reveals what a person values as ultimate and secure. If you invest in the temporary, it reveals a heart tied to the temporary. If you invest in the eternal, it reveals a heart anchored in the eternal.

So, the treasure is a **barometer** or **indicator** of the heart's pre-existing condition.

### 2. The Connection Between Possession, Attention, and Love
While the text focuses on revelation, it perfectly captures the dynamic cycle that *leads* to that revelation. Possession, attention, and love are tied together in a powerful, self-reinforcing loop:

*   **Possession Requires Attention:** What you "possess" or invest in (your "treasure") naturally demands your mental and emotional attention. If your treasure is wealth, you think about markets, security, and acquisitions. If your treasure is status, you think about reputation and influence. The object of possession **focuses your gaze**.
*   **Attention Cultivates Love:** Sustained attention shapes affection. The more you focus on something—nurturing it, planning for it, worrying about it—the more your desires and emotional energy become aligned with it. As the saying goes, "Your heart goes where your mind wanders." Attention is the pathway along which love travels.
*   **Love Motivates Possession:** This completes the cycle. The affection (love) that has been cultivated now becomes the driving force for further possession and investment. You seek to acquire and secure more of what you love. This then demands even more attention, and the cycle deepens.

**In the context of the passage:** Jesus is urging his listeners to **break the cycle** at the first step—the choice of *what to possess*. By consciously choosing to "lay up treasures in heaven" (through acts of mercy, justice, devotion, and love), you redirect the entire cycle.
*   This new **possession** (heavenly treasure) will command your **attention** (seeking first God's kingdom, as mentioned later in Matthew 6:33).
*   That sustained attention will, in turn, **train and cultivate your love** for God and eternal things.
*   And that growing love will then motivate you to further "possess" heavenly treasure.

### Conclusion
**The primary statement is that treasure reveals the heart.** Your external priorities are a window to your internal allegiances.

However, in describing this, Jesus outlines the very mechanism by which hearts are formed: **what we choose to possess (our investment) captures our attention, and what captures our attention ultimately claims our love.** The command to shift our treasure is a command to initiate a new, virtuous cycle that aligns our revealed heart with the kingdom of God. So, while the *diagnosis* is about revelation, the *prescription* is a form of intentional training through redirected investment.
