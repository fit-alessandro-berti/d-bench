In Matthew 12:34–35, Jesus provides a profound psychological and theological diagnosis of human behavior, explicitly linking the moral quality of our words to the hidden moral inventory of our inner lives. 

Here is how the passage connects speech to the heart’s stockpiles, and an analysis of whether words function as symptoms, choices, revelations, or all three.

### 1. The Connection: Speech and the "Hidden Moral Stockpiles"
The connection between speech and the heart is one of **inevitable overflow**. Jesus uses two key concepts to establish this:

*   **The Abundance (*perisseuma*):** This Greek word means "surplus," "overflow," or "that which remains over." Jesus is saying that the mouth is merely a spillway for the reservoir of the heart. When a cup is bumped, whatever it is full of spills out. If the cup is full of water, water spills; if full of poison, poison spills. Speech is not manufactured in a vacuum; it is the overflow of what has been accumulating inside.
*   **The Treasure (*thesauros*):** In verse 35, "treasure" refers to a storehouse or repository. We tend to think of treasure as something we hoard, but Jesus treats it as something we *cultivate*. A person is actively stockpiling either "good" or "evil" in their inner life through their thoughts, meditations, desires, and secret choices. Speech is the withdrawal from this treasury. 

Therefore, the passage suggests that **our words are the currency of our hidden stockpiles.** We do not speak out of character; we speak out of our true character. The Pharisees' harsh, blasphemous words against Jesus were not a momentary slip; they were the withdrawal of evil they had been hoarding in their hearts all along.

### 2. Words as Symptoms, Choices, Revelations, or All Three?
The passage demands that we view words as **all three**—symptoms, choices, and revelations—operating simultaneously. To separate them is to miss the depth of Jesus' diagnosis.

#### A. Words as Symptoms
Words are absolutely presented as symptoms. A symptom is an indicator of an underlying condition. 
*   Jesus addresses them as a "generation of vipers" whose venomous speech is a natural byproduct of their evil nature. 
*   Just as a cough reveals a respiratory infection, evil words reveal a moral infection. Jesus asks rhetorically, *"How can ye, being evil, speak good things?"* The implication is that they cannot. Their speech is symptomatic of their spiritual disease. If the root is rotten, the fruit will be rotten.

#### B. Words as Choices
While words are symptoms, they are not involuntary spasms; they are choices. 
*   The Greek word for "bringeth forth" (*ekballei*) in verse 35 often carries the connotation of an active, even forceful, expulsion. It is not passive. A person actively reaches into their treasury and pulls out what they will use.
*   Furthermore, the immediate context of this passage (verses 36-37) is Jesus warning that "every idle word that men shall speak, they shall give account thereof in the day of judgment." If words were merely involuntary symptoms devoid of choice, God would not hold us accountable for them. We are responsible for our words precisely because, while they flow from the heart, the act of speaking them is a willful choice. 

#### C. Words as Revelations
Words function as revelations because the human heart is hidden from plain view. 
*   The Pharisees were masters of religious disguise. Outwardly, they appeared righteous (which Jesus addresses later in Matthew 23:27 as "whited sepulchres"). However, their speech—specifically their accusation that Jesus cast out demons by the power of Satan—ripped the mask off.
*   Words *unveil* the true self. They expose what a person values, what they fear, what they love, and what they despise. The mouth is the interpreter of the heart, translating invisible moral realities into audible, undeniable realities.

### The Synthesis: How All Three Work Together
To fully understand this passage, we must see how these three concepts interact in a causal chain:

1.  **The Choice of the Heart:** Over time, a person makes countless internal choices about what to think, desire, and entertain. This forms the "treasure" (the moral stockpile) of the heart.
2.  **The Symptom of the Lips:** When a moment of pressure or provocation arrives (as when the Pharisees saw Jesus' miracles and felt threatened), the internal stockpile overflows. The words spoken are the symptom of the heart's condition.
3.  **The Revelation of the Self:** Through those symptomatic choices to speak, the hidden stockpile is revealed to the outside world. The person's true nature is unmasked.

**Conclusion:** 
Jesus is teaching that the tongue is not the initiator of moral evil; it is the transmitter. If you want to know the true moral condition of your heart, do not look at your intentions; listen to your words. Words are **choices** that act as **symptoms**, resulting in **revelations** of the hidden stockpiles of the soul.
