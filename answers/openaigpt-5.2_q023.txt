In Isaiah 55:6–7 repentance is described as turning from both a person’s **“way”** and **“thoughts”** because sin is not only *done* but also *designed*, *desired*, and *justified*.

### 1) “Way” = outward course of life (conduct, habits, loyalties)
The Hebrew *derek* (“way”) commonly means one’s path, pattern, or manner of life—what you actually pursue and practice. So repentance includes a concrete change in:

- actions and behaviors  
- routines and choices  
- the direction of one’s life (what one is “walking toward”)

Isaiah is not satisfied with verbal regret; the “wicked” must **forsake** (leave behind) the whole course of wrongdoing.

### 2) “Thoughts” = inner plans, intentions, and moral imagination
The Hebrew *machashavot* (“thoughts”) is more than passing mental images. It often carries the sense of:

- plans and schemes  
- intentions and motives  
- the inner reasoning by which someone *frames* their life (“how I see things,” “what I think is right,” “what I’ll do next”)

That’s why “the unrighteous man” must forsake not only acts but the *inner counsel* that produces them—what he wants, trusts, excuses, and plots.

### 3) The implication: repentance is holistic—external reformation without internal change is incomplete
By pairing “way” and “thoughts,” the text denies two common evasions:

- **Outward change without inward change**: cleaning up behavior while continuing to love, defend, or plan the same sin.  
- **Inward sentiment without outward change**: feeling remorse while keeping the same path.

Isaiah calls for a return to the Lord that reaches both **conduct** and **imagination**—the visible life and the invisible “inner world” from which the life flows.

### 4) The possibility of mercy: God pardons deeply because repentance goes deeply
The promise is striking: “he will have mercy… he will abundantly pardon.” The logic is:

- Sin runs through both what we *do* and what we *think*.
- Therefore repentance must address both.
- And because God is not offering a minimal pardon but an “abundant” one, even people whose sin is entrenched in patterns (**ways**) and rooted in the heart (**thoughts**) are not beyond forgiveness—if they truly “return.”

So the passage implies that God’s mercy is not limited to surface-level failures; it is offered for the whole person. But the return he calls for is likewise whole-person: a renounced path and a renewed mind turned back to him.
