To understand what makes public goodness "morally dangerous" in this verse, we must distinguish between the **act** (charity), the **setting** (public), and the **motive** (the desire for recognition).

The text suggests that the danger is not located in the visibility itself, but in the **displacement of the object of worship.**

### 1. The Problem of Motive: The "Craving to be Seen"
The primary danger identified by Jesus is the internal orientation of the heart. The Greek word used for "to be seen" is *theathenai*, from which we get the word "theater." This implies acting in a way that turns a moral deed into a performance.

When the motive is to be seen, the "good deed" undergoes a functional transformation:
*   **It ceases to be an act of love:** It is no longer about the recipient’s need, but about the giver’s image.
*   **It becomes a transaction with men:** The giver is not giving to God; they are "buying" social capital, reputation, or moral status from their peers.

Therefore, the danger is the **craving to be seen**, because that craving fundamentally changes the nature of the virtue from *selfless service* to *self-serving exhibitionism*.

### 2. The Problem of Visibility: Context vs. Intent
Is visibility itself the problem? The verse does not explicitly forbid people from seeing good works; in fact, elsewhere in the Gospels, Jesus commends those who act with integrity. 

The distinction lies in the **direction of the gaze**:
*   **Transparent Visibility:** A person does good, and others happen to see it. The visibility is a byproduct of the action. In this case, the glory remains with God.
*   **Performative Visibility:** A person ensures they are seen. The visibility is the *goal* of the action. In this case, the glory is stolen by the individual.

If visibility is the *goal*, the goodness becomes a mask. The "danger" is that the person begins to believe their own performance, effectively replacing a relationship with the Divine with a relationship with their own reputation.

### 3. The "Zero-Sum" Reward
The most sobering part of the verse is the warning: *"otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven."*

Jesus is presenting a moral law of spiritual economics. He suggests that **recognition is a finite resource.** If you perform a good deed to receive the praise of men, and you receive that praise, you have been "paid in full." You have extracted the value of your virtue from the social world, leaving nothing left to be credited to your account in the spiritual realm.

### Summary
The moral danger is **idolatry.** When public goodness is driven by the craving to be seen, the "self" becomes the deity being worshipped, and the "audience" becomes the altar. The visibility is merely the stage upon which this spiritual error is performed.
