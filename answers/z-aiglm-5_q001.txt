The passage in Genesis 4:6–7 offers one of the Bible's most vivid and psychologically acute portrayals of moral agency. It presents a picture of humanity not as a victim of instinct, but as a sovereign guardian of the self, capable of ruling over destructive forces.

Here is an analysis of how the text depicts evil and the responsibility placed on the individual.

### 1. The Picture of Evil: A Crouching Beast
The text answers your specific question about the nature of evil with striking imagery. Evil is presented not as an internal impulse (like a mood swing) nor as a habit, but as a **predatory beast**.

*   **The Imagery:** The Hebrew phrase translated "sin lieth at the door" uses a verb (*rōbēṣ*) that typically describes an animal crouching or lying in wait. Ancient Near Eastern parallels suggest the image of a demon or a lurking danger waiting to pounce. Evil is personified; it has a "desire" (an urge to dominate).
*   **External yet Intimate:** While the text depicts sin as an external force crouching at the "door" (perhaps the door of Cain’s heart or the literal entrance to his dwelling), it is intimately connected to his internal state. God notes Cain's anger and fallen countenance first. The implication is that Cain’s unmanaged internal emotions (jealousy, rage) have opened the door for a predatory, external force to enter.
*   **The Nature of the Threat:** Evil is depicted as an active antagonist. It does not merely suggest; it desires to consume. It is a powerful, chaotic force that seeks to master the person.

### 2. The Picture of Moral Agency: The Capacity to Rule
The text draws a picture of moral agency that is robust and demanding. Agency here is defined by the capacity to distinguish between emotion and action, and the power to enforce one’s will over destructive urges.

**A. The Separation of Feeling and Doing**
God asks Cain, "Why art thou wroth?" This question invites Cain to examine his internal state. The text validates that Cain *feels* anger, but it separates the feeling from the moral outcome. The moral failure is not in the anger itself, but in what Cain does with it. Agency is the ability to refuse to let temporary emotions dictate permanent actions.

**B. The "If/Then" Logic of Consequence**
The phrase "If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?" establishes a moral universe governed by predictable consequences. This rejects fatalism. Cain is not fated to be rejected; his standing before God is contingent on his choices. This places the burden of his current rejection squarely on his own shoulders.

**C. The Mandate to Rule**
The most significant statement on agency is the final clause: *"and thou shalt rule over him."*
*   **The Struggle:** The text acknowledges a power struggle. Sin "desires" Cain (wants to own him), implying that the moral life is a battle for dominance.
*   **The Authority:** Despite the ferocity of the "beast," the text assumes Cain has the inherent authority to win. He is told he *can* rule. The Hebrew phrasing can be read as a promise ("you will rule") or a command ("you must rule"). In either case, it asserts that the human will is higher in authority than the chaotic force of sin.

### 3. The Responsibility Placed on the Person
The text places total responsibility on Cain through two mechanisms: **warning** and **empowerment**.

1.  **The Warning:** By saying "sin lieth at the door," God removes the excuse of ignorance. Cain is warned that his anger is a gateway to something much worse. He cannot later claim he was overtaken by surprise.
2.  **The Empowerment:** Moral responsibility implies capacity. It would be unjust to command a man to "rule" over a force he cannot defeat. By issuing the command, the text implies that Cain possesses the spiritual and volitional strength to close the door on the beast.

### Summary
In Genesis 4:7, moral agency is depicted as **stewardship of the will**.

Evil is a crouching beast waiting to exploit human vulnerability. However, the text presents a view of the human person as a gatekeeper. We are responsible for our "doors"—our hearts and actions. We are not responsible for the existence of the beast (evil is a reality of the fallen world), but we are entirely responsible for whether we let it in. The text insists that while the *desire* of sin may be to consume us, the *decree* of God is that we can, and must, rule over it.
