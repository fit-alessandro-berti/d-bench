The command in Luke 6:37 is frequently reduced to a modern slogan meaning “never evaluate right and wrong,” but that reading collapses the distinction Jesus is actually making. **He does not abolish moral judgment; he redirects its posture, purpose, and endpoint.** The verse explicitly contrasts condemnation with forgiveness, which already presupposes that wrongdoing exists and can be named. If moral judgments never mattered, there would be nothing to forgive.

Here’s how this passage differs from moral relativism, and why the distinction between discernment and the appetite to condemn is essential:

### 1. The Biblical Context: What “Judge Not” Actually Targets
In Greek, κρίνω (*krinō*) ranges from “evaluate” to “pass sentence.” Paired immediately with “condemn” and “forgive,” Luke 6:37 is addressing a specific kind of judgment: **final, self-righteous, punitive assessment that mirrors divine condemnation rather than human wisdom.** 

Jesus elsewhere explicitly commands moral evaluation:
- *“Do not judge by appearances, but judge with right judgment.”* (John 7:24)
- He outlines processes for addressing sin in the community (Matthew 18:15–17)
- Paul instructs churches to exercise discipline for the sake of repentance and protection (1 Corinthians 5; Galatians 6:1)

The prohibition isn’t against moral clarity; it’s against using that clarity as a weapon of superiority, exclusion, or retaliation.

### 2. Why Moral Judgments Still Matter
Saying “moral judgments never matter” leads to ethical apathy or relativism. Luke 6:37 does the opposite: it **grounds moral realism in grace.** Forgiveness only makes sense if offense is real. The verse assumes:
- Actions have moral weight
- Communities need boundaries
- Truth matters enough to be spoken

What changes is not *whether* we discern, but *how* we hold that discernment: with humility, proportionality, and a commitment to restoration rather than ruination.

### 3. Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn
The key distinction lies in the **heart posture** behind the evaluation:

| **Discernment** | **Appetite to Condemn** |
|----------------|------------------------|
| Seeks truth with self-awareness | Seeks superiority or control |
| Aims at restoration, protection, or justice | Aims at punishment, exclusion, or vindication of self |
| Acknowledges personal blindness and fallibility | Assumes moral infallibility; ignores one’s own flaws |
| Is patient, contextual, and proportionate | Is rushed, absolute, and dehumanizing |
| Leaves ultimate sentencing to God | Usurps God’s role as final judge |
| Flows from love and responsibility (Eph 4:15; Gal 6:1) | Flows from fear, pride, or woundedness (James 4:11–12) |

The “appetite” language is crucial. Discernment is an act of stewardship; condemnation is an act of consumption. One asks, *“What is true, and how can truth serve healing?”* The other asks, *“Who deserves to be put in their place?”*

### 4. Practical & Theological Implications
- **Accountability remains necessary.** Healthy families, churches, and societies require moral boundaries. Discernment names harm; condemnation dismisses the possibility of repentance.
- **Forgiveness is not denial.** To forgive is to acknowledge wrong while refusing to let it dictate the future relationship or one’s own heart. It’s morally serious *and* merciful.
- **Spiritual humility guards discernment.** Jesus’ speck/log analogy (Matthew 7:3–5) shows that unchecked self-ignorance corrupts even accurate observations into hypocrisy.
- **Justice and mercy are not opposites.** Biblical ethics holds them together: *“Let justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like a mighty stream.”* (Amos 5:24) paired with *“He has shown you, O mortal, what is good. And what does the Lord require of you? To act justly and to love mercy…”* (Micah 6:8)

### In Summary
Luke 6:37 does not say moral judgments never matter. It says **moral judgments must never become idols, weapons, or substitutes for grace.** Discernment is a tool of love that names reality clearly and seeks redemption. The appetite to condemn is a posture of pride that uses reality to elevate oneself and diminish others. One builds community; the other fractures it. Christianity doesn’t call us to moral blindness, but to moral clarity held in merciful humility.
