The Pharisee was condemned primarily for **pride** (specifically manifesting as **self-righteousness**, which encompasses the use of virtue as self-display).

While the Pharisee engaged in other sinful behaviors, his spiritual condemnation centered on a mindset where he relied on his own moral perfection to secure salvation and justify himself against God without humility. This is best described by one of the options provided: **the use of virtue as self-display**. He claimed he was righteous ("not as other men are") because he could perform good deeds for others, but instead of acknowledging God's grace, he used his own works to position himself as superior. In doing so, he condemned the publican and effectively denied that true righteousness comes from faith alone rather than human merit.

As for why the tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer, it points to **humility** (or an open heart of contrition). While the Pharisee prayed with a focus on his own status and actions to show off his righteousness, the tax collector "looked down" at his feet in a posture of deep dependence and admission of sin. His humility showed that he recognized his total inability to do anything for himself and was relying entirely on mercy, making him eligible for judgment by grace rather than self-righteousness. True spiritual worth is found not in boasting one's own virtue but in admitting one's need for God’s forgiveness—a posture Jesus used to declare that the unjust was already justified before him.

In summary:
*   **Pharisee’s error:** Pride / Self-display (using good works to claim superiority).
*   **Tax Collector’s posturing:** Humility (admitting sin and looking for mercy without pretense).
