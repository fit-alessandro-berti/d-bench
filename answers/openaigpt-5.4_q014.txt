The passage says **both**, but the emphasis falls slightly on **treasure shaping the heart**, not merely exposing it.

## 1. “Where your treasure is, there will your heart be also”
Jesus does **not** say only, “Where your heart is, there your treasure goes,” though that is often true. He says the reverse:

- **Your treasure leads**
- **Your heart follows**

That means what you treat as valuable does not merely **reveal** your inner life; it also **directs** and **forms** it.

## 2. Treasure as revelation
At one level, treasure **reveals** the heart.

What you store up, protect, think about, and sacrifice for shows what matters most to you. If someone’s “treasure” is wealth, status, security, pleasure, or reputation, that tells you where the heart is already leaning.

So yes: **treasure is diagnostic**.

## 3. Treasure as training
But Jesus’ wording goes further. It suggests that investment creates attachment.

What you place your resources into:

- captures your attention,
- draws your concern,
- awakens fear of loss,
- produces desire for increase.

In that sense, **treasure trains the heart**. We become emotionally bound to what we store up.

If you invest in earthly things, your heart becomes tied to earthly instability:
- moth,
- rust,
- thieves,
- decay,
- anxiety.

If you invest in heavenly things, your heart is drawn upward toward God’s kingdom, permanence, and trust.

So treasure is not static. It is **formative**.

## 4. Possession, attention, and love
These are tightly linked in the text.

### Possession
“Treasure” means what you count as wealth, gain, security, or lasting good.

### Attention
Once something becomes your treasure, your mind returns to it:
- you watch it,
- measure it,
- protect it,
- worry over it.

Attention follows valuation.

### Love
And sustained attention ripens into attachment. What you continually guard and contemplate becomes beloved.

So the movement is often:

**valuation → investment → attention → attachment**

or more simply:

**what you prize, you watch; what you watch, you come to love.**

That is why Jesus warns about earthly treasure. Earthly treasure does not stay “outside” you. It pulls your heart into itself.

## 5. Why this matters in context
In Matthew 6, Jesus is teaching about undivided devotion to God. This passage leads toward:
- the “single eye” and “bad eye” (vv. 22–23),
- and “You cannot serve God and mammon” (v. 24).

So this is not just about money management. It is about **worship**.

Your treasure determines:
- what you trust,
- what you fear losing,
- what you serve,
- what finally commands your heart.

## Conclusion
Matthew 6:19–21 teaches that treasure both **reveals** and **forms** the heart, but Jesus especially stresses its **forming power**: what you lay up is where your heart will increasingly go.

Possession, attention, and love become tied together because whatever you count as treasure becomes the object of your watchfulness, and whatever holds your watchfulness gradually claims your heart.
