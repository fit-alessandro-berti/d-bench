Proverbs 3:5–6 is not calling for a refusal to think; it is calling for the **proper ordering of the mind and heart under God**.

### The trust being asked for

The Hebrew word for *trust* here (**בָּטַח**, *bāṭaḥ*) means **confident, reliance**—the kind of reliance you place in something strong enough to bear weight. “With all thine heart” means this trust is **total, personal, and affective**, not merely theoretical. It is covenant trust in Yahweh’s character, wisdom, and faithfulness.

“Lean not unto thine own understanding” uses the image of **leaning on a staff or crutch** (**שָׁעַן**, *šāʿan*). The warning is against making your own insight the final support on which your life rests. “In all thy ways acknowledge him” (**יָדַע**, *yādaʿ*) means to know, recognize, and own God’s lordship in every area of life. The promise follows: **“he shall direct thy paths.”**

So the trust commanded is:

- **Wholehearted** — involving the full self, not a compartmentalized “religious” part.
- **Submissive** — willing to be corrected and guided by God.
- **Practical** — applied “in all thy ways,” not only in crises or worship.
- **Confident** — resting on God’s reliability, not on one’s own cleverness.

### Humble self-distrust vs. anti-intellectualism

These two are not the same, and the proverb is careful not to collapse them.

**Humble self-distrust** says:
- “My understanding is finite, partial, and affected by sin, pride, and limited perspective.”
- “Therefore I will not make my own reasoning the ultimate court of appeal.”
- “I will submit my mind to God’s revelation and seek his wisdom.”
- It still *uses* reason, study, counsel, and experience—but under God’s authority.

**Mere anti-intellectualism** says:
- “Thinking is bad,” or “I don’t need learning, expertise, or careful reasoning.”
- It often masks pride: “My gut feeling is enough,” or “I have nothing to learn from anyone else.”
- It rejects the mind rather than redeeming it.

Proverbs itself rebukes both errors. The book commands the reader to pursue wisdom, instruction, and knowledge (Prov. 1:5; 4:7; 18:15), yet insists that the *fear of the Lord* is the beginning of wisdom (Prov. 1:7; 9:10). So the text expects you to use your mind—vigorously—but not to **trust** it as ultimate.

### In short

The proverb asks for **reliance on God as the sovereign guide of life**, not for the abandonment of intellect. Humble self-distrust places human reason under God’s wisdom; anti-intellectualism despises human reason. The first is faith; the second is folly.
