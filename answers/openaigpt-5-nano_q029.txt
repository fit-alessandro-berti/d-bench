Short answer: correction in Galatians 6:1 is to be done with humility and gentleness, for the sake of restoration, not humiliation. Those who correct are to be Spirit-led, and they must keep a careful eye on their own vulnerability to temptation.

More detail

- The moral posture asked for
  - Humble, gentle, and patient approach: “in the spirit of meekness” signals a soft, teachable, non-condemning attitude. It’s about restoring, not blasting or shaming.
  - Motivated by love and concern for the person’s welfare and for the church’s health.
  - Spirit-led discernment: those who are “spiritual” (mature in the Spirit) should use wisdom and self-control rather than zeal that becomes harsh or punitive.
  - Reality-check against pride: correction is not a badge of superiority. It requires a sense of dependence on the Spirit, truth spoken in love, and a desire for genuine repentance.

- Why restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion are tied together
  - Restoration: the aim is to bring the person back into right relationship with God and the community. The goal is healing and reconciliation, not punishment.
  - Meekness: without gentleness and humility, correction can harden the heart or be driven by arrogance. Meekness keeps the approach constructive and safe for repentance.
  - Self-suspicion (self-examination): “lest thou also be tempted” warns the one correcting to consider his own vulnerability. It guards against hypocrisy and pride (the very sins one condemns in another) and keeps the correction grounded in humility and dependence on God.
  - Together they form a corrective posture that is honest about sin, hopeful about grace, and careful about the tempter’s subtle siege on all of us.

- Practical implications
  - Start with self-examination and prayer; recognize your own susceptibility to the same temptation.
  - Address the matter privately and gently if possible (depending on the seriousness and context), aiming to win the person over with love.
  - If needed, involve others in a spirit of seriousness and care, but always maintain the aim of restoration.
  - Let truth be spoken, but let it be truth delivered in love and with the readiness to forgive and encourage renewal.

Cross-references that illuminate the same posture
- Matthew 18:15-17 shows a process of correction with the goal of reconciliation.
- Galatians 5:22-23 (fruit of the Spirit) grounds meekness, gentleness, and self-control in the Spirit.
- 2 Timothy 2:24-26 urges correction “in meekness, instructing those that oppose themselves,” so that God may grant repentance.
- James 5:19-20 echoes the healing and saving aim when someone wanders from the truth.

In sum, Galatians 6:1 calls for a correction that is spiritually mature, lovingly restorative, and spiritually vigilant—restoration pursued with meekness, and with a sober awareness that you too may be tempted.
