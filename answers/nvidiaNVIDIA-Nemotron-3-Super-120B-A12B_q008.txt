

The verse is using the concrete, observable love we show toward our brothers and sisters as a **litmus test** for the invisible, interior love we claim to have for God. In other words, if a person truly loves God, that love must inevitably show up in how he treats the people he can see; love of God cannot be hidden inside while the outward behavior stays unloving.  

When someone says, “I love God,” but then hates or neglects his brother, he is **deceiving himself**. The self‑deception being exposed is the belief that one can have genuine love for God while lacking love for one’s neighbor—a form of **moral hypocrisy / self‑justifying false piety**. It is a case of cognitive dissonance: the person holds two contradictory attitudes (professed love for God vs. actual hatred/indifference toward a brother) and resolves the tension by denying the reality of the latter, thereby pretending the love for God is genuine when it is not.  

So the verse treats love of the visible neighbor as a test of love of the invisible God because true divine love is inseparable from love of others; the self‑deception it exposes is the hypocrisy of claiming to love God while failing to love the people God has made—i.e., a false, self‑justifying profession of piety.
