### External compliance vs. a “new heart”

**External compliance** is *behavioral conformity*—doing what the law requires (or appearing to) because of pressure, habit, fear of consequences, desire for approval, or social identity. It can exist **without** inward love for God or inward agreement with what is commanded. In the prophets this is often exposed as obedience that is *mechanical*, *selective*, or *hypocritical* (right actions while the inner person remains resistant).

A **“new heart”** in Ezekiel is something deeper than improved behavior: it is a **new inner disposition**—new desires, new loyalties, a re-formed will. The “heart” in Hebrew thought is not mainly the emotions; it is the **control-center** of a person: thinking, valuing, deciding, and committing. So God isn’t merely asking Israel to *try harder*; he promises to **recreate** the inner source from which choices flow.

That is why the promise continues:

- “a new spirit I will put within you” (inner renewal), and then
- “I will put **my Spirit** within you, and **cause** you to walk in my statutes” (divine enabling).

External compliance can imitate obedience; a new heart produces obedience from a transformed will—obedience that is not just possible but fitting and desired.

### Why “hardness” is pictured like a material that must be replaced

Ezekiel’s image of a **“heart of stone”** versus a **“heart of flesh”** communicates *incapacity*, not just stubborn attitude.

A stone heart is:

- **insensitive** (doesn’t feel, doesn’t grieve, doesn’t respond),
- **unresponsive** (warnings and mercies don’t penetrate),
- **inflexible** (won’t yield),
- **lifeless** (no living movement toward God).

A heart of flesh (here meaning *living, soft, responsive flesh*, not “sinful flesh”) is:

- **tender** and **receptive**,
- capable of **repentance**, **faith**, and **love**,
- able to be “moved” and shaped by God.

So the metaphor implies that Israel’s problem isn’t merely that they need better instruction or stronger incentives; it’s that their inner condition is so resistant that it is like a dead material. That’s why the solution is not “repair” but **replacement**—an act of God as decisive as surgery or creation.

In short: **external compliance** changes what someone *does*; a **new heart** changes what someone *is* at the center, so that what they do flows from a renewed, living responsiveness to God by his Spirit.
