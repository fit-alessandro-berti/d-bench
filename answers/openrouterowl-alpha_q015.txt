# Analysis of Matthew 7:3–5

## What Makes This Image So Sharp

Jesus employs several deliberate rhetorical and literary techniques to create a striking, almost absurd visual effect:

**The homely image and amusing exaggeration.** The sudden shift from the abstract concept of contradictory criticism to a highly concrete, almost slapstick picture — a man with a massive log jammed in his own eye trying to perform delicate surgery on a speck in someone else's eye — is what gives the passage its punch. The disproportionate contrast between a tiny speck (κάρφος, *karphos*) and a wooden plank or beam (δοκός, *dokos*) would have made Jesus' audience laugh. People remember ideas anchored in vivid pictures.

**Irony as humor.** The humor reinforces the critique: the critic's claimed expertise ("let me pull out the mote") is entirely at odds with his situation. The sharper the visual absurdity, the harder it is for the listener to dodge the insight.

## How Hypocrisy Is Criticized Without Forbidding Moral Correction

This is theologically crucial, and Jesus handles it with precision:

1. **He targets *who* does the correcting, not the act itself.** The problem isn't the diagnosis of sin — "the mote in your brother's eye" is real and genuinely obstructive to sight. The problem is someone straining to fix tiny faults elsewhere while remaining blind to their own enormous ones.

2. **He prescribes a direct course of action.** "First remove the plank from your own eye." Only *after* self-correction does he say you will see clearly — implying that moral correction *is* legitimate once self-examination has preceded it.

3. **The goal is clarity, not avoidance.** The passage assumes correction can be necessary and good. What's being condemned is *premature, self-righteous* judgment — pointing out faults in others either without recognizing them at all or while ignoring their full weight in oneself. True moral clarity can only come after honest self-judgment.

## In Summary

The passage offers a rebuke of self-righteous hypocrisy — not a prohibition of correction, but a reordering of priority. Remove pride and self-blindness first. After honesty with oneself, discerning correction of others becomes possible.
