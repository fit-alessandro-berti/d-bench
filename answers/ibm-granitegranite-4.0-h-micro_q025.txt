In Matthew 6:1, Jesus is addressing the issue of hypocrisy in religious practice, specifically in the context of giving alms or charitable acts. The verse warns against performing good deeds with the intention of being seen by others, as this can lead to a lack of genuine reward from God.

The danger in public goodness, as described in this verse, is not merely the act of being visible or visible itself. Rather, it is the underlying motivation behind the act. The problem lies in the craving to be seen, which indicates a selfish motive. This craving suggests that the individual is more concerned with the approval of others than with the actual act of charity or righteousness itself. 

In essence, the danger is the potential for hypocrisy. When one's actions are motivated by a desire for recognition or praise from others, the act loses its true spiritual value. Such actions are not done out of genuine love for God or a desire to help others, but rather to gain social approval or to impress others. This can lead to a superficial faith that is not rooted in genuine devotion or love for God.

Therefore, the verse is cautioning against the danger of performing good deeds for the wrong reasons, rather than the danger of being seen. The focus should be on the sincerity of the act and the intention behind it, rather than on the visibility or recognition it might garner.
