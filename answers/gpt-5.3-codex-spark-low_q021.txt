• Pride in Proverbs here isn’t framed mainly as a “legal offense” (guilt) but as a mental/spiritual defect that distorts
  how a person sees reality.

  - Blindness: Pride isolates the person from feedback, correction, and shared reality. A haughty person assumes self-
    sufficiency (“I already know; I don’t need others”). That blocks learning, so error compounds until it becomes
    catastrophic.
  - Collapse: The verse’s “fall” is the inevitable outcome of that blindness: arrogance removes the internal checks that
    prevent risky, selfish, or reckless choices, then reputation, relationships, and judgment break down.
  - Moral psychology: The contrast with “the lowly” points to orientation more than status. “Lowly” means teachable,
    non-defensive, rooted in dependence, able to accept limits and correction. That posture reduces moral risk.

  So the proverb is saying: pride is dangerous because it degrades cognition before it destroys outcome. Arrogance first
  impairs perception (of God, others, consequences), then guarantees collapse. Humility, by contrast, preserves
  discernment and keeps someone aligned with wisdom because it stays connected—to reality, to others, and to
  accountability.
