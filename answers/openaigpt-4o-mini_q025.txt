In Matthew 6:1, the moral danger associated with public goodness revolves around the intention behind the acts of kindness rather than visibility itself. The core issue is the craving to be seen and acknowledged by others, which can lead to a desire for approval or recognition that overshadows the genuine act of charity.

When individuals perform good deeds for the purpose of being observed or praised, it shifts the focus from altruism to self-promotion. This undermines the intrinsic value of the act of giving, as it becomes more about one's image or reputation rather than the actual help being offered to others. The verse suggests that if the motivation behind the act is to gain visibility and esteem from others, then the spiritual reward from God is forfeited.

Thus, the danger lies in the motivation—performing acts of goodness for the sake of public recognition can corrupt the essence of charity and disconnect it from its true purpose. The emphasis is on the importance of sincerity and humility in one's actions, seeking to serve others without the need for acknowledgement.
