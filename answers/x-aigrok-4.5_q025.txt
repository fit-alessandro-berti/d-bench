**The problem is the craving to be seen (the motive), not mere visibility.**

Matthew 6:1 (KJV) warns: “Take heed that ye do not your alms before men, *to be seen of them*: otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven.” The infinitive phrase “to be seen of them” is purposive. It identifies the *intent*—seeking human applause as the reason for the act—as what corrupts it. Parallel warnings follow about prayer and fasting done “to be seen of men” (vv. 5, 16). In each case the “hypocrites” already receive their full reward in earthly recognition; the act is emptied of value before God.

Visibility itself is not condemned. Jesus performed public miracles and taught openly; the early church practiced public generosity (Acts 2–4) and later collections for the poor. The Sermon on the Mount does not require secrecy in every good deed; it forbids *theatrical* good deeds whose audience is human esteem rather than the Father who “sees in secret.” When the motive is pure, public acts can still glorify God (cf. Matt 5:16). When the motive is the craving for reputational reward, publicity becomes morally hazardous because it replaces God as the intended recipient of glory and reorients the doer toward self-display.

In short: the verse locates the danger in the heart’s desire for visibility *as reward*, not in the sheer fact of being observed. Motives determine whether the goodness is genuine piety or empty performance.
