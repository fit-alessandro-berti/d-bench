It is a common misunderstanding that Luke 6:37 suggests moral relativism—that we should ignore sin, or that it does not matter if others follow God’s laws. In reality, this verse warns against a **specific kind of judgment**: the self-righteous, punitive, and prideful posture intended to establish our own superiority over others.

The difference between Jesus’ teaching and the idea that "moral judgments never matter" is the distinction between **discernment** (identifying truth) and **the appetite to condemn** (imposing will).

Here is an exploration of how these concepts differ:

### 1. Discernment vs. Condemnation
*   **Discernment (The Duty of Truth):** To discern is to look at reality, moral law, and human actions with the hope of guidance or correction. When Jesus spoke against Judging, He did not mean we should ignore sin or lose our ability to spot deception. In fact, the Gospels are full of stories where Jesus identifies hypocrisy and calls people out on their wrongs (e.g., the Pharisees vs. the Poor). Discernment is an outward evaluation based on truth to help others grow into Christ’s likeness.
*   **The Appetite to Condemn (The Trap of Pride):** To condemn is an inward, hostile act rooted in a desire for power or status. It assumes that you know what God would have said about the person more than they know. It treats people as legal cases to be closed rather than friends to be loved.
    *   **Moral Judgment** asks: *"Is this right?"* (Truth)
    *   **Condemnation** asks: *"How can I make you wrong?"* (Pride)

### 2. The Source of Authority
Luke 6:37 is not teaching that humans do not have authority to uphold God’s moral standard; it is teaching that **humans do not have the final authority to pass judgment.**
*   **Moral Accountability:** As human beings, we are still morally accountable. We must choose right over wrong, and we must hold others accountable for their actions under God’s law. To deny the reality of sin because we won't judge anyone is to confuse grace with license.
*   **Divine Authority:** Jesus is pointing out that *only God* judges hearts. In Matthew 7:1–5 (contextually linked), Jesus warns those who "condemn others" without having done the same, or by using a legalistic standard to exclude others. He invites us to submit moral evaluation to His authority, rather than exercising it on our own terms.
    *   **Example:** You might discern that a coworker is stealing; this is moral reality. But condemning them publicly or acting as the judge in their spiritual life (i.e., deciding their eternal destiny) is usurping God’s role.

### 3. Motive: Pride vs. Humility
The key to the verse "Forgive" being the central promise lies in the motive of judgment. This is an expression of humility or pride.
*   **Appetite for Condemnation:** When we judge with an appetite, we often feel better for having done so and worse for being judged. It reinforces our identity as the "judge" (moral superior). In Luke 6:37, Jesus warns that if you operate from this mindset, *you* will be treated by others in the same way. The judgment is a reflection of our own hearts; we are more likely to receive what we give.
*   **Appetite for Mercy:** Discernment without condemnation implies loving another enough to tell them the truth but hating their pain enough not to crush their spirit. We discern truth because it is helpful, not because we can hurt or elevate ourselves above the other person.

### 4. "Judge Not" vs. "The Law of Justice"
It is important not to conflate Jesus’ words with an argument against justice itself. In Luke 6:37–38, Jesus warns us about how God will respond to our *posture*. If we are not willing to release people from judgment (showing mercy), then no one is going to do anything for us except judgment on their own heart.
*   **Discernment** recognizes that sin harms the whole person and society.
*   **Condemnation** tries to punish or isolate the person who committed it, often as a form of social control or religious ritual.
*   The command "Forgive" is not just passive; it is proactive mercy.

### Summary: How to Apply This Today
The verse does not suggest that moral boundaries should be ignored. It asks us to align our **moral discernment** with the purpose of restoration rather than retribution.

You are free to tell someone you shouldn't steal (Discernment). You might tell them they need help or correction (Appropriate Disciplinary Action). But you should not feel that *you* hold their soul in your hands, nor should you seek to hurt their spirit through condemnation unless God is using you as His messenger of truth and love.

**The bottom line:** We are accountable for keeping the moral law ("Judge"). But we are warned against judging the hearts and fates of others to establish our own righteousness ("Condemn").
