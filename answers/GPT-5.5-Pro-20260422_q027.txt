All of the options you name are involved, but they are not equal. The **root condemnation** is given in the first verse:

> He told the parable to those who **trusted in themselves that they were righteous** and **despised others**.

So the Pharisee is not condemned simply because he fasts, tithes, or cares about righteousness. Those things can be good. He is condemned because he turns righteousness into **self-trust before God** and **superiority over his neighbor**.

His error has several layers.

First, there is a kind of **practical false doctrine**. He may not be stating a formal heresy, but he is acting as though his religious performance can establish him before God. His prayer says “God, I thank thee,” but the substance of it is: “Here is why I am acceptable.” God becomes the audience for his self-verdict.

Second, there is **pride**. But it is not mere confidence or joy in obedience. It is pride that has moved from gratitude into self-justification. He is not saying, “God, you have had mercy on me and helped me walk in your ways.” He is saying, in effect, “I am not like those people.”

Third, there is **comparison**. His righteousness needs a contrast figure: “other men,” and especially “this publican.” That is spiritually revealing. He does not simply stand before God; he stands above another sinner. His moral identity is built by looking sideways.

Fourth, there is **contempt**. This is perhaps the ugliest visible fruit. The tax collector is not a person to be pitied, prayed for, restored, or loved. He is useful only as a foil. The Pharisee’s virtue has become anti-neighborly. That is why the opening says he “despised others.” A righteousness that makes one despise sinners has already departed from the heart of God.

Fifth, there is the **use of virtue as self-display**. His fasting and tithing are real religious acts, but in the prayer they become exhibits in a case for himself. He brings his virtues into the temple not as offerings, but as credentials.

So what condemns him “exactly”? Not virtue, not doctrine as such, not even moral seriousness. What condemns him is **self-justifying piety**: religious obedience turned into a basis for superiority before God and contempt toward others.

The tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer because he tells the truth about himself before God. He does not bargain, compare, excuse, or display. He “stands afar off,” will not lift his eyes, beats his breast, and says:

> “God be merciful to me a sinner.”

That posture is not praised because sin is better than virtue. It is praised because **truthful repentance is nearer to God than self-satisfied righteousness**.

The tax collector understands the actual relation between human beings and God: he needs mercy. He does not claim a right to be accepted. He casts himself on God’s compassion. In the temple, the place associated with sacrifice, forgiveness, and atonement, he asks not to be admired but to be covered by mercy.

The Pharisee says, “I am not like him.”
The tax collector says, “I am the sinner who needs mercy.”

That is why the tax collector goes home justified. He has not made himself righteous; he has come empty-handed to the God who justifies. The Pharisee, by contrast, is too full of his own righteousness to receive the righteousness that comes as mercy.

The parable’s reversal is sharp: the openly sinful man is spiritually closer to the truth than the religiously impressive man, because the former knows he needs grace, while the latter has turned grace into a mirror.
