Paul defines victory over evil not as defeating an enemy by the enemy’s methods, but as refusing to let evil determine one’s character and conduct:

> “Be not overcome of evil, but overcome evil with good.”

### What “victory” means

Evil wins twice when it harms someone and then turns that person into another agent of hatred, revenge, or cruelty. To “overcome evil with good” is to break that cycle through concrete action: feeding the hungry enemy, giving drink to the thirsty, and refusing personal vengeance (Romans 12:17–19).

This is not passive niceness. The verbs are active: **feed, give, overcome**. The believer does not merely avoid retaliation but positively seeks the enemy’s good.

### The “coals of fire”

Paul is quoting Proverbs 25:21–22. The image has been understood in several related ways:

- kindness may awaken shame and repentance;
- good treatment may make the enemy painfully conscious of wrongdoing;
- if the enemy remains unrepentant, judgment is left to God rather than personally inflicted.

It should not be read as, “Be kind because kindness is a subtler way to hurt people.” That would contradict the command’s purpose. The goal is genuine good, while the enemy’s conscience and final judgment are left to God.

### Weak, manipulative, or strong?

- **Not weak:** It requires restraint, courage, and freedom from the need to retaliate. Weakness may be unable to strike back; Christian mercy may have the power to retaliate and deliberately refuse.
- **Not manipulative:** If kindness is merely a strategy for humiliating or controlling the enemy, it is revenge in disguise.
- **Genuinely strong:** It resists evil without becoming evil. It seeks the offender’s need and possible restoration while entrusting justice to God.

This does **not** require pretending that wrong is harmless, remaining in danger, or obstructing lawful justice. Romans 12 rejects personal vengeance; Romans 13 immediately recognizes public authority’s role in restraining wrongdoing. One may set boundaries, seek protection, report abuse, and pursue justice without hatred or revenge.

Thus, victory is moral and spiritual: **evil is overcome when it fails to reproduce itself in the person it attacks, and when good is actively returned in its place.**
