The image is sharp because it combines **comic exaggeration, physical impossibility, and moral irony**.

- A **mote** is a tiny speck; a **beam** is a roof timber or plank. Someone with a timber protruding from his own eye offering delicate eye surgery to another is grotesquely absurd.
- The **eye** is especially effective imagery because it is both vulnerable and associated with sight and judgment. The person who claims to see another’s fault clearly is, in fact, visually incapacitated by his own.
- The wording exposes selective attention: he “beholdest” the speck in his brother but does not “consider” the beam in himself. The problem is not merely having faults, but being intensely perceptive about others and strangely unreflective about oneself.
- “Thou hypocrite” identifies the contradiction between the role he performs—clear-sighted moral helper—and his actual condition.

Yet the passage does **not** end with “never remove your brother’s mote.” It says:

> “**First** cast out the beam … **and then** shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote…”

The order matters. Jesus condemns correction that is self-blind, presumptuous, or performative—not all moral correction. Proper correction requires:

1. **Self-examination** before scrutiny of others.  
2. **Repentance** rather than assumed superiority.  
3. **Clearer judgment**, no longer distorted by one’s own unaddressed sin.  
4. **Brotherly help**, aimed at restoration rather than condemnation.

Thus the saying does not teach moral indifference. It teaches that one must cease playing the superior judge and become a repentant helper. The corrected person may still help remove the speck—but now with clearer sight, humility, and care.
