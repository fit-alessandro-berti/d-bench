The passage from Ezekiel 36:26–27 is one of the most profound descriptions of the "New Covenant" in the Hebrew Bible. It addresses the fundamental failure of the old system of law and points toward a radically different way God intends to relate to humanity. 

To answer your questions, we must look at both the theological mechanics of obedience and the visceral metaphors Ezekiel uses to describe the human condition.

---

### 1. External Compliance vs. Receiving a "New Heart"

The difference between these two concepts lies in the **source of motivation** and the **direction of transformation**.

**External Compliance (The Old Way)**
External compliance is behavioral modification driven from the *outside in*. It is adherence to rules motivated by fear of punishment, desire for social approval, a sense of duty, or attempts at self-justification. 
*   **The Flaw:** External compliance is inherently fragile. Because the underlying desires of the person have not changed, obedience lasts only as long as the external pressure remains. When the threat of judgment is removed, the behavior reverts. Furthermore, external compliance often breeds spiritual pride ("I followed the rules") or deep resentment ("These rules are a burden"). 

**The "New Heart" (The New Covenant Way)**
In Hebrew anthropology, the "heart" (*leb*) is not merely the seat of emotions; it is the command center of the human person—the seat of the intellect, the will, and the deepest desires. Receiving a "new heart" means transformation from the *inside out*.
*   **The Difference:** The primary difference is **agency and desire**. Verse 27 makes this explicit: *"I will put my spirit within you, and **cause** you to walk in my statutes."* Under the New Covenant, God does not just hand down a rulebook; He changes the reader. The law is no longer an external burden to be endured, but an internal desire to be fulfilled. Obedience becomes the natural, willing overflow of a transformed nature, driven by the indwelling Spirit of God.

---

### 2. Why Hardness is Described as a Material Condition

Ezekiel uses highly physical, almost surgical metaphors to describe a spiritual reality. He contrasts a "heart of stone" with a "heart of flesh." This is not accidental; it is designed to show the absolute impossibility of human self-reformation.

**The Heart of Stone (Calcified Rebellion)**
Why describe sin as a material condition of stone?
*   **Unresponsiveness:** Stone is cold, dead, and unfeeling. A stone cannot hear, cannot feel empathy, and cannot be reasoned with. A "heart of stone" represents a spiritual condition where a person has become completely desensitized to God, to truth, and to the suffering of others.
*   **Unyielding Nature:** You cannot bend or shape stone without shattering it. It represents stubbornness and rigid idolatry. (In the ancient world, idols were literally made of stone). 
*   **Spiritual Death:** Stone does not beat. It is a metaphor for spiritual death.

**The Heart of Flesh (Living Responsiveness)**
In this context, "flesh" (*basar*) does not mean "sinful nature" (as the Apostle Paul sometimes uses the word). Rather, it means *living, breathing tissue*. 
*   A heart of flesh is warm, pulsing, soft, and alive. 
*   It is sensitive to touch; it can be broken (which is the biblical metaphor for genuine repentance, as in Psalm 51:17). 
*   It is capable of love, rhythm, and life.

**Why it Must Be *Replaced*, Not Repaired**
This is the most crucial theological point of the passage. Ezekiel does not say, "I will polish your stony heart," or "I will teach your stony heart to be soft." 

You cannot train a rock to beat like a muscle. No amount of moral coaching, willpower, or external rule-keeping can turn stone into flesh. The condition is terminal. Therefore, God describes Himself as a divine surgeon: *"I will **take away** the stony heart... and I will **give** you a heart of flesh."*

This highlights the doctrine of **grace and regeneration**. Because the human condition is spiritually dead (stone), salvation and true transformation must be a monergistic work—initiated, powered, and completed entirely by God. He must excise the dead tissue and implant living tissue. 

### Summary
Ezekiel is declaring that God’s ultimate goal is not to produce perfectly trained robots who follow rules out of fear, but to create living, breathing children who love Him freely. External compliance fails because it tries to dress up a stone to look like a living person. The "new heart" is God’s miraculous surgery, removing the dead, unyielding nature of sin and replacing it with a living, sensitive nature that naturally desires to walk in His ways.
