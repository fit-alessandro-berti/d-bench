
This is a profound and rich question about the meaning of Matthew 12:34–35 and its implications for the relationship between internal character and external speech.

Let’s break down the passage and your question step by step.

### The Passage (NIV for clarity):

> “O generation of vipers, how can you, being evil, speak good things? For out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks.  
> A good man, out of the good treasure in his heart, brings forth good things; and an evil man, out of the evil treasure in his heart, brings forth evil things.”

### Key Phrases and Their Meaning

1. **“Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks.”**
   - This is a classic rendering of the Greek phrase (γρήσσεται ἐκ πλοῖου τοῦ καρδίας), indicating that a person’s inner state—what is in their heart—greatly influences what comes out of their mouth. It doesn’t mean speech is merely a passive reflection; rather, it is shaped by the dominant attitudes, feelings, and dispositions in the heart.

2. **“Good man … from the good treasure in his heart … brings forth good things. Evil man … from the evil treasure … brings forth evil things.”**
   - Here, Jesus equates a person’s inner “treasure” (a metaphor for what is stored in their heart—values, attitudes, motives) with the nature of what they say.

### How Does This Connect Speech to Hidden Moral Stockpiles?

The passage makes a direct causal link between the hidden state of the heart and the outward expression (speech). It suggests that the heart is like a storehouse—what you store there (your values, intentions, attitudes) determines what you produce outwardly (your words).

#### Does it suggest that words are:
1. **Symptoms?**  
   Yes, in a sense. The words are the outward manifestation or symptom of the internal state. Just as a fever is a symptom of illness, so speech is a symptom of the condition of the heart.

2. **Choices?**  
   Also yes. The passage implies that the heart’s condition is not just something hidden but actively shapes the person’s choices when speaking. It’s not that speech happens independently of the heart; rather, the heart’s “abundance” (either good or evil) influences the decision of what to say.

3. **Revelations?**  
   In a way, speech can reveal the state of the heart. If your heart is filled with goodness, your words will tend to reflect that; if it’s filled with evil, your words will be evil. Thus, speech can be a revelation of the inner condition.

But the passage is more than just saying words “reveal” the heart. It says the heart is the *source*—the *wellspring*—from which speech flows. Speech is not just a reflection, but an *expression* of the heart’s state.

#### Synthesis: All Three?

- **Symptoms:** Words are a visible sign of what is in the heart.
- **Choices:** The heart’s state actively shapes the choice of words (not all words are possible given the heart’s condition).
- **Revelation:** Speech is an outward display of the heart’s condition, making it a form of revelation.

So, the passage suggests that **words are all three**: symptoms of the heart’s state, the result of choices shaped by that state, and a form of revelation of the heart’s true condition.

### Jesus’ Point

By saying “out of the abundance of the heart,” Jesus is making a strong ethical claim: the foundation of morality is not just external rules or social conditioning, but what is cultivated and stored in the heart. The heart’s “treasure” (values, motives, attitudes) determines character and speech.

### Application

This means that:
- People are not just what they say, but what they *store* in their hearts.
- Change in speech must come from change in the heart—motives, attitudes, values.
- We can “reveal” our inner condition through our words, but the origin of our words is from within.

### In Summary

**The passage connects speech to the hidden moral stockpiles of the heart by showing that words are not independent of internal life. They are both the result of and the revelation of the heart’s condition. Speech is a product of the heart’s abundance (good or evil), a consequence of choices made from that abundance, and a way in which the heart’s state is revealed to others. Thus, words are symptoms, choices, and revelations—all stemming from the heart’s true nature.**

If you want to go deeper, you could explore how this relates to the broader biblical teaching on repentance (e.g., “For the mouth speaks what the heart thinks”—Proverbs 16:9), and the idea that true transformation begins in the heart (Matthew 5:5, 7).
