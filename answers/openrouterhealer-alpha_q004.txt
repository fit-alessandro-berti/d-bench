This is a profound distinction you're drawing—a crucial one for understanding biblical wisdom.

The trust called for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is **active, relational reliance on God's character and wisdom**, not a passive surrender of the mind. Let's break down the distinction:

**1. Humble Self-Distrust (As Presented in the Text):**
This is a *relational* posture. It recognizes the limits of human understanding—especially in complex moral, spiritual, or ultimate matters—while actively entrusting oneself to a wiser, trustworthy source. It’s not the absence of reason, but the **subordination of reason to a higher authority** when they conflict. The Hebrew word for "trust" (בָּטַח, *batach*) implies a confident reliance, like leaning your full weight on something solid. The text acknowledges our ways and understanding—it doesn’t deny them—but asks that we acknowledge God in all our ways (v. 6), inviting divine guidance into the process.

**2. Anti-Intellectualism (A Misreading):**
Anti-intellectualism would involve a *rejection* of reason, learning, or critical thinking as a matter of principle—seeing faith and intellect as inherently opposed. It would twist "lean not on your own understanding" into "abandon understanding altogether." This is foreign to the Hebrew wisdom tradition, which elsewhere celebrates skill, prudence, and knowledge (e.g., Proverbs 1:1–7). The problem here is not using the mind, but **presuming self-sufficiency**—trusting one’s own judgment as ultimate and final.

**Key Distinction:**
- **Self-distrust** is **relational and humble**. It says: *“My understanding is limited; I will seek and yield to a trustworthy, transcendent wisdom.”*
- **Anti-intellectualism** is **anti-relational and dismissive**. It says: *“Thinking is dangerous or unnecessary; I will simply believe without inquiry.”*

The proverbial vision is of a life **guided by trust in God’s wisdom**, which includes the responsible use of the mind He gave—while holding that mind with humility before His greater insight. It’s a call to **faithful dependence**, not intellectual abandonment.
