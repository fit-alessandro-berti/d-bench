The sharpness of the rejection in Amos 5:21–24 is intentional. God is not asking for a "better balance" because he is not dealing with a deficit of piety, but with a **surplus of hypocrisy**.

To understand why the language is so violent (“I hate,” “I despise,” “take away the noise”), we must look at the specific kind of moral and spiritual fraud being exposed.

### 1. The Fraud of "Ritual Substitution"
The people of Israel in Amos’s time were not ignoring God; in fact, they were meticulously observing the law. They were keeping the feast days, bringing the burnt offerings, and playing the music. 

The fraud was the belief that **ritual could substitute for righteousness.** They believed that the performance of religious duty acted as a "payment" or a "cover" for their ethical failures. In their minds, the smoke of the sacrifice rose up to appease God, regardless of how they treated the poor or the oppressed during the week. 

God rejects this because it treats worship as a transaction rather than a transformation. When ritual is used to mask injustice, the ritual itself becomes an insult to the One who commanded the justice in the first place.

### 2. The "Noise" of Insincerity
Amos uses a striking sensory shift in verse 23: *“Take thou away from me the noise of thy songs.”* 

Normally, music in the temple was described as "praise." Here, God calls it "noise." This exposes a profound spiritual truth: ** Worship is not defined by the quality of the music or the correctness of the liturgy, but by the alignment of the worshiper's life with the character of God.** 

When the heart is callous toward the suffering of others, the most beautiful melody becomes "noise" to God. The fraud being exposed is the idea that one can be "in tune" with God musically while being "out of tune" with God morally.

### 3. The Rejection of "Compartmentalization"
The Israelites had compartmentalized their lives into "Sacred Space" (the Temple) and "Secular Space" (the marketplace). 
*   **In the Temple:** They were devout, generous with their offerings, and humble.
*   **In the Marketplace:** They "trampled on the poor" and "turned justice into wormwood" (Amos 5:12).

By rejecting the worship sharply, God is declaring that **there is no such thing as a sacred space that is insulated from secular ethics.** He refuses to be "contained" within the temple walls. To God, the way a person treats the vulnerable is the only true metric of their worship.

### 4. The Solution: Justice as a "Mighty Stream"
The final verse provides the answer: *“But let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream.”*

The imagery of a "mighty stream" (or an ever-flowing torrent) contrasts with the "burnt offerings" of the previous verses. An offering is a momentary act—a spark of fire, a slaughtered animal. A stream, however, is constant, life-giving, and transformative.

**Summary:**
God does not ask for "balance" because balance implies that ritual and justice are two separate things that need to be weighed. Instead, God asserts that **justice is the prerequisite for worship.** Without righteousness, religious activity is not just "incomplete"—it is a fraudulent mask that mocks the nature of God.
