Luke gives the interpretive key before the story begins:

> Jesus told this parable **to some who trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and treated others with contempt** (Lk 18:9).

So the Pharisee is condemned not because fasting and tithing are false doctrine or bad practices (they aren’t), but because of what his “prayer” reveals about his *stance before God*.

### What condemns the Pharisee

**1) Self-trust as the basis of righteousness (self-justification).**  
The Pharisee presents his moral record as what secures his standing with God. The problem is not that he *has* virtues, but that he treats them as a kind of moral capital that makes him “the righteous man.” That is exactly what v.9 targets: “trusted in themselves that they were righteous.”

**2) Pride expressed as comparison.**  
His gratitude takes the form: “I thank you that I’m not like other men… even like this tax collector.” His “righteousness” is measured comparatively, not by God’s holiness. That’s a telltale sign of pride: he can feel secure only by *looking down*.

**3) Contempt for another worshiper.**  
Luke explicitly highlights this. The Pharisee’s spirituality includes contempt (“this publican”), which is the opposite of love of neighbor—and a strong indicator that his piety has become self-referential rather than Godward.

**4) Virtue used as self-display (even if subtly).**  
The text says he “stood and prayed thus **with himself**” (wording varies by translation, but it captures the sense). Even though he addresses God, the “prayer” functions as a recital of achievements. His focus is not God’s mercy but his own performance. The effect is self-congratulation in religious form.

Put simply: the Pharisee is *religiously observant but unrepentant*, and he uses religious observance to secure a verdict over himself (“righteous”) and over others (“not like them”).

### Why the tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer

The tax collector’s posture is treated as “truer” because it matches reality in two ways: **truth about himself** and **truth about God**.

**1) Truth about himself: he is guilty and needy.**  
He stands “afar off,” won’t lift his eyes, and beats his breast—signs of shame and sorrow. But the core is his sentence: “God, be merciful to me, a sinner.” He doesn’t negotiate, compare, or offer compensating virtues. He tells the truth.

**2) Truth about God: God justifies by mercy, not by résumé.**  
The verb behind “be merciful” (Greek *hilaskomai*) carries the sense of “be propitious / make atonement” in a sacrificial context. In the temple—where atonement is proclaimed by sacrifice—he appeals to God’s provision, not his own record. That is why Jesus can say he “went down… **justified**”: he is the one who entrusts himself to mercy.

### The point of the parable

The parable is not “bad man vs good man” in terms of external behavior. It is “self-justifying man vs repentant man.”

- The Pharisee is **confident without repentance** and **devout with contempt**.
- The tax collector is **humble in repentance** and **dependent on mercy**.

That’s why Jesus concludes with the general principle:

> “Everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, but the one who humbles himself will be exalted” (Lk 18:14).

So the Pharisee’s condemnation is fundamentally **prideful self-trust that expresses itself in comparison and contempt**, turning virtue into a warrant for self-approval. The tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer because it aligns with the basic reality of grace: *God justifies the one who stops defending himself and asks for mercy.*
