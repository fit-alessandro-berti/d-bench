The proverb from Proverbs 3:5–6 is calling for an inner trust in God, which is fundamentally different from mere anti-intellectualism or an arrogant disregard for one'self-doubt. Here's how to understand the distinction:

1. Trust in the Lord with all thy heart:
   - This is an inner, deep-seated faith and trust in God—what Christians might describe as having a personal relationship with God.
   - It implies complete reliance on God for guidance, strength, and direction.
   - One must be transparent with God in all aspects of life, implying an openness, honesty, and a readiness to surrender personal control where appropriate.
   - This trust is not passive; it often involves vigilant expectation that God's will and goodness will be evident in one’s life.

2. Do not lean on one's own understanding:
   - Rather than relying solely on one's own intellect, reason, or understanding, which can be limited or flawed.
   - Human wisdom may lead to pride or self-sufficiency, and can often fail to grasp the full complexity of life’s issues.
   - This doesn’t mean rejecting education or intellectual pursuits but recognizing that human understanding has its limitations.

The key distinction between this trust and anti-intellectualism is that the former doesn't oppose intellectual engagement or the use of reason. In fact, it often supplements one's faith by incorporating wisdom from both Scripture and sound reasoning to life's challenges. It’s recognizing that while our understanding can guide us, it's not an ultimate authority.

3. He shall direct thy paths:
   - This promise indicates God's active role in steering the course of one's life, helping to make wise decisions and resolves.
   - Trust here implies a submission to God, not a negation of individual capability or intellect.
   - It’s the hope that through God’s guidance, one can find the most righteous and favorable path.

The trust being advocated in these verses is not naive or blind, but humble and wise. It acknowledges the limitations of human understanding and the complexity of life. It's a form of spiritual discernment that interweaves the humanistic approach with a higher spiritual wisdom that comes from God. Trusting God does not dismiss the factual or logical thought processes but rather complements and transcends them by aligning a person’s decisions with divine will and purpose.

In summary, this trust sought is neither anti-intellectualism, which would denigrate the role of thought and reason, nor is it blind faith that fails to use one's understanding. Instead, it is an informed, humbly trusting faith that uses its understanding as one part of the equation while acknowledging that ultimately, God provides direction beyond natural comprehension. This is a balanced trust calling for intellectual engagement with faith, allowing God to shine through and guide the soul in a way that’s both rational and spiritually grounded.
