# The Visibility Test: Why Love Must Be Embodied

The argument of 1 John 4:20 has a precise logical structure, and unpacking it reveals what kind of self-deception is in view.

## The Logic of the Test

The verse does not say love of neighbor *is* love of God, nor that God is reduced to the neighbor. It says the neighbor is the **verifiable occasion** of love. John is making an epistemological point: love is not a private feeling toward an abstract object, but a way of *relating* — and we come to know what love is only through concrete encounter. The brother is the school, the gymnasium, the test site. The unseen God is the proper horizon, but our grip on that horizon is calibrated by the weight we carry with visible people.

If the scale won't register the person standing in front of you, why would you trust its reading of a distant object?

## The Self-Deception Exposed

What this verse is targeting is not mere hypocrisy (saying one thing and doing another) but something more interior: **the substitution of religious feeling for the actual shape of love.** Several layers of self-deception are at work:

**1. Mistaking warmth toward the abstract for love of the concrete.** The invisible God does not interrupt, contradict, or demand. One can have intense devotional feelings toward a God who never embarrasses, never disappoints, never asks for a cup of water. The self-deception is thinking these feelings constitute the same thing as the costly, vulnerable, particular love the gospel describes.

**2. Using the invisibility of God as a screen for projection.** Precisely because God is unseen, "love of God" can mean almost anything. The feeling is unfalsifiable from the inside. The neighbor, by contrast, is an unchosen reality who resists our categories. Hatred toward a brother is a datum the self must ignore, rationalize, or split off from the religious self-image.

**3. Confusing correct devotion with right relatedness.** The liar in this verse may pray beautifully, hold orthodox doctrine, and feel awe. The verse suggests this is precisely where the lie is most plausible: religious experience can *occlude* the absence of love rather than certify its presence. Piety becomes anesthetic.

**4. Hating in the name of piety.** Often the brother who is hated is hated for religious reasons — the "wrong" kind of person, the heretic, the impure, the ungrateful. The deception is that theological fervor is doing the work of love, when in fact it has become a more respectable mask for contempt.

## The Deeper Theological Reason

1 John has just made the daring claim that "God is love" (4:8, 16). Love is not something God has alongside other attributes; it is what God *is*. To claim to love God is therefore to claim participation in that nature — and participation is shown, not asserted. If the same person who claims divine love harbors hatred for a brother, the claim is not just morally inconsistent but *ontologically impossible*, because hatred belongs to a different nature.

There is also an incarnational logic at work in Johannine theology (cf. John 1:18; 14:9). The invisible God has been *made visible* — supremely in Christ, and now in the brother. To bypass the visible neighbor is, in effect, to insist on one's own version of God rather than God's own self-disclosure. The "love of God" that excludes the brother is, paradoxically, a *refusal* of the God who is love — a God who has bound Himself to us in flesh.

## What the Verse Refuses

Ultimately, 1 John 4:20 refuses a spirituality that wants vertical relationship without horizontal cost. It suggests that we cannot love what we have not seen by refusing to love what we have; the distance we maintain from the brother is the very distance that proves we have not drawn near to God. The liar is not primarily deceiving others. He is deceiving himself about the location of his own heart, mistaking a comfortable abstraction for the demanding, particular, visible love that the God of Israel and of Jesus Christ actually is.
