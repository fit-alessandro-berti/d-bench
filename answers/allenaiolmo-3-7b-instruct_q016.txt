
Let's analyze the passage from Luke 15:28–32—particularly focusing on the elder son (the one who "was angry, and would not go in")—to discern his deepest problem.

**Summary of the Narrative:**

- The father calls the son in to celebrate because a lost son (the younger one) has returned.
- The elder son is angry and refuses to come.
- The father goes out and pleads with him.
- The son responds by accusing his father: over the years he has served faithfully, never transgressed, but the father never gave him the "kid" (a special gift) so he could celebrate with friends.
- The father responds by saying the son is always with him and all he has is the son's.
- The father then notes it was right to rejoice because the lost son is found, but the elder son's response is to miss the joy of reunion because of his sense of being unappreciated and ungranted what he feels he deserves.

**Analysis of the Elder Son’s Deeper Problem:**

Let’s look at each possible answer:

1. **Anger:** The son is described as "angry," refusing to go in. But his core problem seems deeper than mere anger. Anger is a surface symptom, a reaction to something else.

2. **Envy:** The son resents that the younger brother, who was lost and thus "deserves" the celebration (and perhaps the special gift), received it instead. His bitterness stems from feeling that he has been unfairly denied what the younger brother gets for his return from loss.

3. **Wounded Fairness/Unfairness:** He feels he has faithfully served without reward—especially since the father never gave him the "kid," which the younger son apparently received despite his own failure. This sense of being overlooked or undervalued is central.

4. **Pride in Obedience:** While the son boasts of his obedience ("neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment"), it turns out that obedience has not brought him the reward or recognition he expected. His pride is not the root; rather, it’s the *disappointment* that comes from that obedience not being reciprocated.

5. **Discomfort with Mercy Itself:** Not directly. The father's mercy in celebrating the return of the lost son is precisely what the elder son finds hard to accept—his reaction is more about feeling unworthy than rejecting mercy itself.

**Best Answer: Envy or Wounded Fairness (Unfair Treatment)**

The elder son's *deepest* problem is **wounded fairness or a sense of being unfairly treated**. He feels that despite his loyalty and obedience, he has been denied the celebration, the special gift, and the shared joy that the father gave to his returned, wayward son. His anger is the outward expression of an internal wound: the sense that he deserves more recognition and reward for his devotion than the father has given him. This is classic "envious resentment" (though not simple envy, since he envies the *outcome* more than the younger son's actions), rooted in a feeling of being let down by fairness and grace.

**In summary:**  
The elder brother's deepest problem is **a wounded sense of fairness—feeling he has been unfairly overlooked or unappreciated compared to his younger brother**, even though he has been more dutiful. His anger is the symptom, not the cause. The real issue is the sense of injustice and unmet expectation.
