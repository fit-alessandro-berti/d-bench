Short answer: because light exposes what we want to hide. Light = truth, clarity, accountability; exposure threatens identity, reputation, comfort, and the self-deceptions that protect them. That threat produces fear, shame, and therefore an aversion — even active hostility — to being seen.

A more detailed unpacking

1. What “light” and “hate” mean here
- In John, “light” is both intellectual and moral: revelation, God’s truth, Christ’s presence that makes motives and actions visible (see John 1:4–9; 8:12).  
- “Hate” is strong Johannine language for aversion to that visibility — not merely dislike but an active resisting of being brought into the open. It functions rhetorically to underline how determined people can be to avoid exposure.

2. Why exposure is threatening
- Loss of social standing: being seen doing wrong can bring shame, stigma, rejection, loss of relationships, job, position. People naturally avoid those costs.  
- Identity threatened: if someone’s self-concept is built on a lie (e.g., “I’m a good person,” “I’m in control”), clear evidence to the contrary undermines the whole narrative.  
- Cognitive dissonance: new truthful information that conflicts with one’s beliefs is uncomfortable; avoiding light preserves psychological equilibrium.  
- Fear of punishment and consequences: legal, relational, or spiritual consequences push people to hide.  
- Vulnerability and pain: to be seen clearly often requires acknowledging weakness and admitting need — painful for the proud or the ashamed.

3. Shame vs. guilt
- Guilt focuses on a specific wrong and often motivates repair; shame targets the whole self (“I am bad”) and motivates hiding. Light tends to provoke shame where people have fused action with identity, so instead of confessing and repairing they evade the light.

4. Defensive strategies against light
- Rationalization, denial, minimization: reinterpret facts to avoid seeing wrongdoing.  
- Projection and blaming others: deflect exposure onto someone else.  
- Secrecy and compartmentalization: create dark corners where actions can continue unobserved.  
- Hostility toward truth-tellers: attack, discredit, or silence those who bring light.

5. The biblical point and pastoral hope
- John’s contrast is not merely diagnostic; it’s invitational: “he that doeth truth cometh to the light” — coming into the light is tied to healing, accountability, and deeds “wrought in God.” Jesus’ light exposes so that wrongs can be reproved and transformed, not merely punished.  
- Practically, that means creating communities of accountability and grace so people can risk exposure without being crushed by shame. True repentance and transformation require the painful but freeing experience of being known.

6. Practical implications
- For individuals: cultivate humility, seek honest feedback, learn to tolerate the temporary discomfort of truth, distinguish guilt (do something about it) from toxic shame (don’t hide).  
- For communities: combine truth-telling with restorative response; offer safe channels for confession, correction, and repair so light becomes a means of growth, not merely punishment.

Conclusion
People “hate” the light because it threatens everything they have built to protect themselves: reputation, lies, control, and a comfortable moral self-image. Yet in the Johannine view, light is the necessary condition for repentance, restoration, and life — painful at first but ultimately liberating.
