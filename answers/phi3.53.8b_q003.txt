Micah 6:8 sets forth three interconnected virtues—justice, mercy, and humility—as essential components for individual behavior and moral living directed towards God. Here is an exploration into why these virtues, as summarized in this verse, can be seen as formidable demands and how they correct and support one another:

1. **Justice:**
   Justice is the practice of fairness, equality, and righteous governance in interactions, treating others fairly and according to what is right and just. It requires an individual to act without favoritism or prejudice, upholding the law, and respecting the due rights of others. At face value, acting justly can seem daunting, as it often involves significant personal risk, especially when one has to challenge injustice or unethical practices. In contemporary society, where many face pressure to conform to immediate self-interest or the interests of a powerful group, promoting justice can be difficult.

2. **Loving Mercy:**
   Mercy is about showing compassion and leniency when appropriate, often towards those who have wronged us or who might not have the means to protect themselves. Love mercy means giving others second chances, offering forgiveness, and working towards their betterment. It often requires putting oneself in a vulnerable position and showing we care for others despite potential hardships this may entail. Mercy also implies understanding the struggles of others, which is counterintuitive to the sometimes harsh realities of life.

3. **Walking Humbly:**
   Walking humbly before God involves recognizing one's place in the divine scheme, fostering a sense of modesty and a recognition of one' extraterrestrial nature concerning the power and knowledge attributed to the divine being. It is about living with awareness of one’s limitations and being open to guidance and correction from a higher source.

Now, how these virtues demand and support each other:

- **Balancing Justice and Mercy:**
  - Justice and mercy can appear to be at odds; justice may sometimes require punishment, which could seem harsh, while mercy may demand leniency. However, true justice is tempered with mercy—it enacts fairness while always being compassionate.
  - Humility, through self-reflection and acceptance of one’s errors or prejudgments, helps in mercy. When individuals believe they do not know everything, they are more open to forgiveness and understanding others, which leads to more merciful actions.
  - Justice involves equitable treatment for all, recognizing that different people may need different support, much like mercy is applied differently based on circumstances.

- **Humbility and Justice:**
  - Humbly understanding one's own limitations prevents excessive self-righteous judgement, and thus allows fairness. It prevents one from passing harsh judgement upon others and oneself, enabling just action.
  - Recognition of one’s limitations means understanding the universal imperfection and the need for equity, which is at the core of just governance.

- **Humbility Fostering Mercy:**
  - Walking humbly with God often involves a sense of dependency, which is linked to an appreciation of the divine providence and the grace found in compassion. It can lead to an increased desire to act mercifully.
  - A humble stance towards God might also engender a deeper spiritual awareness, making someone more aware of the importance of showing love and mercy to fellow humans.

- **Self-Reflection and Forgiveness:**
  - Self-reflection—central to humility—can prompt individuals to examine their motivations for seeking justice or mercy, often revealing a desire to make amends or to genuinely right wrongs done by others.
  - Forgiveness, rooted in a humble understanding of oneself and one's place in the cosmos, can prevent the vindictiveness that often stems from feelings of superiority, thereby enabling individuals to show mercy instead of seeking retribution.

In summary, these virtues are interrelated and interdependent. Being just is not enough—it must be married with love and mercy, which is enabled and made more effective by humility. Humility facilitates justice by keeping one's ego from distorting fair judgment, and it promotes mercy by opening oneself up to compassion and empathy. Acting justly doesn't only require fair dealings with others but doing so with a heart aiming to heal and aid rather than solely enforce discipline. Walking humbly with God grounds this humility in spirituality, reminding the individual of the greater moral and ethical order they are part of and encouraging them to act justly, with mercy, and with modesty.

In the context of Micah 6:8, these virtues are not just aspirational but immediate imperatives for the individual's spiritual and communal conduct. They demand not just intellectually but also emotionally and spiritually engaging with the day-to-day aspects of life. These virtues challenge one to look beyond oneself, considering the welfare and dignity of every individual.

This challenging mandate—requiring justice, mercy, and humility—is comprehensive because each of these qualities must be practiced thoughtfully and consistently throughout life. Living up to this can be demanding because it often requires sacrifice, a re-thinking of one's values, and sometimes going against the immediate, self-serving impulses. It also demands a level of self-awareness and emotional intelligence that can only be cultivated over time through mindfulness, self-reflection, and continued growth both spiritually and morally. The act of living up to these standards can create ripples that extend beyond one's personal sphere, influencing relationships and interactions within one's community, hence reinforcing societal stability and compassion. The moral imperative of integrating justice, loving mercy, and humility demands commitment, empathy, and spiritual reflection, all of which deepen into wisdom over time, shaping not only individual character but also contributing to the fabric of a more equitable and kind society.

Understanding these facets together also helps clarify that they are not competitive virtues. Instead, they complement and reinforce each other; when one acts justly, mercy is often the natural extension, and humility ensures these virtues are kept in proper balance. They correct one another by ensuring that justice isn't blindly administered but is carried out with grace and a compassionate heart, and that mercy isn’t a tool for enabling injustice but is used with a keen sense of what's needed to foster true equality and fairness. Similarly, humility is a self-reflexive virtue that ensures that one’s sense of justice and capacity for mercy remain guided by a realistic understanding of oneself, precluding both arrogance and hypocrisy.

Therefore, to embody these virtues, one must engage in a lifelong journey of self-cultivation—actively practicing fairness and generosity without hubris, forgiving with a genuine heart, and remaining grounded in the reality of one’s true position within the larger societal and metaphysical context. It’s a holistic approach to ethics that requires attention to both the outer and inner dimensions of one’s life, leading to a moral character that genuinely reflects the interconnectedness and interdependence of all of humanity. The depth of these demands reflects the depth of transformation they aim to engender, moving us closer to a state of divine reflection and imitation on earth.

In essence, the combined requirement of these virtues compels individuals to engage in lifelong reflexivity regarding their moral purpose and action, aligning actions with a higher moral standard, which, in turn, creates a ripple effect that can significantly impact how society functions and interacts – promoting a culture of peace, empathy, and equitable living. By integrating these three components, Micah 6:8 essentially asks for a harmony between personal virtue and societal engagement for a deeper communal and spiritual good that extends beyond individual benefit. This formidable moral summation is not just a task; it is the embodiment of a spiritual and ethical calling that enriches us as individuals while nurturing the well-being of the wider community as we collectively aspire towards the betterment of our world.
