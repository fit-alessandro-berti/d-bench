The elder brother’s deepest problem in Luke 15:28–32 is **a combination of wounded fairness, resentment, and discomfort with mercy**—rooted in a **legalistic, self-righteous mindset** that struggles with grace. Here’s a breakdown of his core issues:

1. **Wounded Fairness (Perceived Injustice)**
   - He resents that his years of "faithful" service (v. 29) went uncelebrated, while his reckless brother’s return is met with extravagant joy. His complaint isn’t just about a lack of a party—it’s about feeling *unseen* and *unappreciated* in his own father’s eyes. His sense of fairness is tied to *earned* recognition, not *unconditional* love.
   - His language ("*these many years*") implies a ledger of debt: *"I’ve done my duty; where’s my reward?"* This reflects a **transactional relationship** with God/parent, not a relational one.

2. **Resentment Toward Mercy (Discomfort with Grace)**
   - His anger isn’t just about the party—it’s about the *kindness shown to the prodigal*. He frames his brother’s return as a *theft* of resources ("*devoured thy living with harlots*"), reducing mercy to a zero-sum game. His heart rejects the idea that joy isn’t finite; he believes his father’s love must be *divided* or *earned*.
   - His question (*"thou never gavest me a kid"*) reveals a **spiritual poverty**: he doesn’t see himself as already *richly provided for* (v. 31: *"all that I have is thine"*). His focus on *what he lacks* blinds him to *what he has*.

3. **Pride in Obedience (Self-Righteousness)**
   - He *explicitly* contrasts himself with his brother: *"I never transgressed thy commandment"* (v. 29). This is **performative righteousness**—his obedience is a *badge of honor*, not a posture of humility. His identity is tied to his *performance*, not his *belonging*.
   - His pride is subtle but deadly: he assumes his worth comes from *what he does*, not *who he is* as the father’s son. This is the opposite of the prodigal’s repentance (who admits his unworthiness).

4. **Anger as a Symptom (Not the Root)**
   - His anger is *secondary* to his deeper issues. It’s the *expression* of his wounded fairness and resentment, not the core problem itself. Anger is often a *mask* for fear (fear of irrelevance, fear of being unloved, fear of grace undermining his self-sufficiency).

5. **Discomfort with the Father’s Love**
   - The father’s response (*"Son, thou art ever with me"*) exposes the elder brother’s *fear of abandonment*—not because he’s been cast out, but because he *doesn’t trust* that the father’s love is *unconditional*. His obedience is a *negotiation*, not a *gift*.
   - His refusal to enter the feast symbolizes his **spiritual paralysis**: he can’t receive joy because he’s too busy *auditing* the father’s fairness.

---
### **The Core Issue: A Legalistic Heart**
The elder brother’s problem isn’t just *one* of these—it’s a **system of legalism** where:
- **Grace feels like theft** (because it disrupts his earned rewards).
- **Obedience is a currency** (not a response to love).
- **Fairness is a contract** (not a relationship).
- **Joy is conditional** (only for those who "deserve" it).

Jesus uses this story to expose how **religious self-righteousness** (like the Pharisees in Luke 15:1–2) can be *more resistant to God’s mercy* than outright sin. The elder brother’s sin isn’t lust or rebellion—it’s **refusing to celebrate the lost being found** because it threatens his *self-made identity*.

---
### **Key Takeaway for Us**
The elder brother’s struggle is a warning against:
- **Comparing our "righteousness" to others’ failures** (e.g., judging the "sinners" in church while ignoring our own pride).
- **Treating God’s love as a reward system** (e.g., "If I’m good enough, I’ll earn His favor").
- **Missing the feast of grace** because we’re too busy *keeping score*.

The father’s response (*"it was meet we should make merry"*) is a rebuke to legalism: **God’s joy isn’t earned—it’s given.** The elder brother’s growth requires him to *receive* his father’s love as a gift, not a wage.
