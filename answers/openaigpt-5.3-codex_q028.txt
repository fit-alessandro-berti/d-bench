Great question. That line is morally destabilizing because it **collapses the distance** between “the sinner” and “the judges.”

It does at least four things at once:

1. **Exposes hypocrisy**  
   The accusers treat the woman as a case, not a person. Jesus forces them to include themselves in the moral equation.

2. **Interrupts mob-righteousness**  
   In a crowd, people feel morally “clean” because responsibility is shared.  
   Jesus individualizes guilt: *you* throw the first stone—if *you* are sinless.

3. **Does not erase moral standards**  
   He does not say adultery is fine. He later says, “Go, and sin no more.”  
   So this is not moral relativism; it’s a challenge to **self-righteous punishment**.

4. **Reframes judgment under mercy**  
   Judgment is not forbidden absolutely (cf. “judge with right judgment,” John 7:24), but it is stripped of pride and vengeance.  
   Only those who stand under God’s judgment themselves can judge rightly.

### The deeper layer
It destabilizes because it reveals a truth we resist:  
we want justice for others and mercy for ourselves.  
Jesus demands the same standard for both.

So the sentence is not “never judge,” and not “sin doesn’t matter.”  
It is: **you cannot wield justice as a weapon while pretending you are outside the need for grace.**
