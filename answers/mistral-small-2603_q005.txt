This passage from the Sermon on the Mount is one of the most radical and challenging teachings in the Bible. Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously for several reasons, and the difficulty itself reveals something profound about human nature, morality, and the nature of love itself.

### **Why Enemy-Love Feels Unrealistic or Impractical**
1. **Instinctual Self-Preservation** – Humans are wired for reciprocity; we naturally love those who love us and retaliate against those who harm us. Enemy-love goes against our survival instincts, making it feel unnatural or even dangerous.

2. **Moral and Emotional Resistance** – Loving an enemy often feels like betrayal, weakness, or injustice. If someone has wronged us deeply, extending love can seem like excusing their behavior rather than addressing it.

3. **Cultural and Philosophical Skepticism** – Many modern philosophies (e.g., utilitarianism, social contract theory) prioritize self-interest or collective good over self-sacrificial love. Enemy-love can seem naive in a world that often rewards strategic power over moral purity.

4. **Misunderstanding of Love** – Love in this context isn’t about approval or emotional affection but about *goodwill*—a commitment to their well-being, even if they remain hostile. This is harder to grasp than sentimental love.

### **Is It Sentimental, Strategic, or Moral Strength?**
It’s **none of these in a shallow sense**—it’s something deeper:

- **Not Sentimental** – It’s not about warm feelings but about deliberate, costly action (e.g., praying for persecutors, blessing those who curse you).
- **Not Purely Strategic** – While some argue that enemy-love disarms conflict (e.g., Gandhi, MLK), Jesus isn’t prescribing it as a *means* to an end but as an *end* in itself—a reflection of God’s nature.
- **A Form of Moral Strength** – It requires immense inner freedom—the ability to act from love rather than reaction, to break the cycle of hatred rather than perpetuate it. It’s not weakness but the strongest possible response, rooted in divine perspective.

### **The Deeper Reason: Reflecting God’s Nature**
Jesus grounds this command in God’s own character: *"He maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust."* This means:
- **Unconditional Benevolence** – God’s love isn’t earned; it’s given to all, regardless of merit.
- **Divine Justice** – Enemy-love doesn’t negate justice (God remains just) but transforms the way we respond to injustice.
- **Transformative Power** – Love, not retaliation, has the potential to change hearts (as seen in the conversion of Saul/Paul, a persecutor turned apostle).

### **Conclusion: A Call to Radical Freedom**
Enemy-love is difficult because it demands we transcend our natural inclinations—but in doing so, it reveals a higher kind of strength: the strength to live by a different kingdom’s values. It’s not about being a doormat but about refusing to let hatred define us. As C.S. Lewis wrote:
*"Do not waste time bothering whether you ‘love’ your neighbor; act as if you did. As soon as we do this, we find one of the great secrets: when you are behaving as if you loved someone, you will presently come to love him."*
