The three‑fold command is demanding because each element attacks a different facet of human nature and together they form a complete ethical posture that no single virtue can achieve alone.

| Virtue | What it confronts | Why it is needed |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Justice** | The tendency to pursue self‑interest, to be unfair, or to ignore the rights of others. | Justice demands that we give each person what is due, establishing a baseline of right‑relationship with the world. Without it, society devolves into chaos or oppression. |
| **Mercy** | The propensity to be harsh, unforgiving, or to exact retribution beyond what is warranted. | Mercy softens justice, allowing room for repentance, forgiveness, and compassion. It prevents justice from becoming cold legalism that crushes the vulnerable. |
| **Humility** | The inclination toward pride, self‑exaltation, or reliance on one’s own wisdom. | Humility positions us under God, acknowledging our limits and the need for divine guidance. It ensures that both justice and mercy are applied with the right perspective, not from a place of ego. |

**How they correct one another**

1. **Justice without mercy** → becomes punitive, rigid, and can lead to oppression.  
2. **Mercy without justice** → becomes permissive, allowing wrongdoing to go unchecked.  
3. **Humility without either** → leads to self‑deception, where we think we are acting rightly while actually serving our own agenda.

When humility is present, we are less likely to demand justice in a self‑serving way and more inclined to extend mercy. Likewise, a humble heart is more receptive to the corrective prompt of justice and more open to extending mercy.

Thus the demand is holistic: it requires **balanced action** (justice), **compassionate response** (mercy), and **proper posture** (humility). Only by integrating all three can a person truly “walk humbly with thy God,” fulfilling the full intent of the passage.
