**Short answer:**  
Enemy‑love is hard to live out because it collides with three powerful, mutually reinforcing forces: (1) our natural psychology of *self‑preservation and tribalism*, (2) the cultural‑historical habit of treating “the other” as a threat to be neutralised, and (3) the way the command is framed in the Gospel – not as a sentiment or a tactic, but as a *radical re‑orientation of identity* toward the character of God. When we peel those layers away we see that the command is not sentimental, not merely strategic, but a deep‑rooted invitation to a new kind of moral strength that reshapes the very ground of what we call “love.”

Below is a step‑by‑step exploration of each layer, followed by a synthesis that shows why the difficulty is both real and, paradoxically, the very proof that the command is meant to be taken seriously.
