Bitterness is called a **root** because the writer is pointing to something **inward, hidden, and generative**, not just an outward bad action.

A **root**:

- is **below the surface**  
- grows quietly before anything is visible
- feeds the whole plant
- can spread and deepen over time
- eventually produces fruit, flowers, or damage that comes later

So the image suggests several things:

### 1) Bitterness begins internally before it becomes obvious
Bitterness is not first of all a public deed. It is a **settled inward condition**—a hardening of the heart, often from hurt, offense, injustice, or disappointment. At first it may be invisible, but it is still alive and growing.

### 2) Hidden moral growth can produce delayed damage
A root does its work in secret. Likewise, bitterness may seem manageable at first, but underneath it is shaping a person’s thoughts, speech, and reactions. Damage often appears **later**: in suspicion, cynicism, resentment, harshness, or unbelief. By the time the “fruit” is seen, the root has already been growing for some time.

### 3) One person’s bitterness can spread to others
Hebrews says that a root of bitterness can “trouble you” and “many be defiled.” That fits the root image well: a root is not isolated. It can affect the whole environment. Bitterness in one person often spreads through:

- words that poison relationships
- attitudes that infect a community
- unresolved offense that others absorb
- cycles of retaliation or mistrust

### 4) The warning is about what bitterness produces, not just what it feels like
The point is not merely that bitterness is unpleasant, but that it is **dangerous**. Left unchecked, it becomes a source of corruption, much like a root that nourishes a poisonous plant.

So the metaphor means: bitterness is a **hidden, growing moral corruption** that can remain unseen for a while, then produce serious damage, not only in the individual but in the people around them.
