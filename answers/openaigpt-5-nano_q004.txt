Short answer
- The verse is asking for a relational, wholehearted trust in God: a daily dependence on His character, promises, and guidance, not a mere intellectual assent to propositions.
- It does not call for anti-intellectualism. Rather, it calls for epistemic humility: do not rely solely on your own reasoning, but involve God in all your thinking and decisions.

What kind of trust is being asked for?
- Trust with the heart: In Hebrew thought, the heart (lev) encompasses will, desires, and the core of a person, not just feelings. So “trust with all your heart” means surrendering your whole self—will, desires, and plans—to God.
- Trust that reshapes decisions: This trust is practical, not merely devotional. It leads you to seek God’s guidance in concrete choices, not just to believe abstractly that God exists.
- Trust anchored in God’s reliability: The basis of the trust is who God is (His faithfulness, wisdom, goodness), not in your own cleverness or in luck.

What “lean not unto thine own understanding” means
- It warns against making human reason the ultimate rule. Your own understanding is limited, biased, and fallible.
- It invites you to test, correct, and supplement your thinking by divine revelation, wisdom, and community counsel.
- It does not forbid thinking or learning. It invites a higher standard of judgment: human understanding should be guided, corrected, and tempered by God’s truth.

What “in all thy ways acknowledge him” entails
- Acknowledge him in every area: decisions about work, relationships, finances, health, etc.—not just spiritual matters.
- Acknowledge him by seekingHis will through Scripture, prayer, and wise counsel; aligning motives with God’s purposes; and acting in ways that reflect trust in Him.
- The promise, “he shall direct thy paths,” suggests guidance and correction—often through a process that becomes clearer as you walk with God, rather than a single, calculable prescription.

Humble self-distrust vs anti-intellectualism
- Humble self-distrust (epistemic humility):
  - Recognizes the limits of human reason and the need for divine guidance.
  - Keeps the door open to learning, correction, and growth. It invites study of Scripture, wisdom from others, and praying for insight.
  - Is compatible with rigorous thinking, examination of evidence, and careful planning, all under God’s authority.
- Anti-intellectualism:
  - Tends to reject or devalue reason, evidence, and study, or to substitute faith as a blanket rejection of human knowledge.
  - Can lead to stubbornly clinging to preferences or traditions even when God’s revealed truth or wise counsel suggests otherwise.
  - Is at odds with the verse’s invitation to acknowledge God “in all thy ways” and to use wisdom as you live life.

How to live this out
- Before making a major decision, pair rational analysis with prayer and Scripture study.
- Seek counsel from wise, mature believers, and be willing to adjust your plans if God prompts or clarifies His direction.
- Regularly examine your motives: are you trusting God’s goodness, or are you clinging to your own perceived wisdom?
- Cultivate intellectual humility: admit when you don’t have the answer, seek further understanding, and submit your conclusions to God’s revealed truth.

In sum
Proverbs 3:5–6 invites a wholehearted, relational trust in God that orders all of life. It warns against relying solely on human understanding while not condemning thinking or learning. It champions humble self-distrust that seeks and follows God’s direction, rather than a blanket anti-intellectual stance.
