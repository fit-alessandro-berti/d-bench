Romans 12:20–21 defines “victory over evil” as **responding to hostility with active, beneficial good rather than retaliatory evil**.

- **“Therefore if thine enemy hunger, feed him; … thirst, give him drink”**: The passage directs the enemy’s unmet need toward kindness. This is not passive niceness; it’s concrete assistance.
- **“heap coals of fire on his head”**: In context, this is not primarily about cruelty or punishment. It echoes the idea that such goodness exposes the enemy’s moral situation, awakening conscience, and (in the biblical spirit) bringing about shame/repentance or at least leaving evil unable to keep its foothold. The “coals” image functions as a metaphor for the moral pressure of unexpected righteousness.
- **“Be not overcome of evil, but overcome evil with good”**: The real definition of victory is explicit: evil is not defeated by becoming like evil; it is defeated by **good that resists evil’s pattern**.

### Is the command weak, manipulative, or genuinely strong?
It’s **genuinely strong**—and not manipulative in the usual sense.

- **Not weak**: This requires discipline, self-control, and the willingness to absorb harm without mirroring it. Retaliation is easier; doing good “to” an enemy is costly.
- **Not manipulative**: The passage doesn’t teach “do good to get revenge” or to trick the person. The command is framed as **moral action** (“overcome… with good”), not as a scheme.
- **Strong in its moral force**: By refusing to retaliate, the believer interrupts the cycle of violence and deprives evil of its leverage. It also aims at the enemy’s good (or at minimum at their conscience), which makes the approach ethically serious rather than cunning.

So, the passage defines victory over evil as **refusing evil’s methods and countering them with tangible good**, trusting that this is the true way to “overcome” evil rather than to reproduce it.
