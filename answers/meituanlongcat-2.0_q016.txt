To understand the elder brother’s deepest problem, it is helpful to view the options you provided not as isolated choices, but as a descending root system. While anger, envy, wounded fairness, and discomfort with mercy are all present in the text, they are symptoms of a deeper, foundational issue. 

The elder brother’s deepest problem is **pride in obedience**, which creates a transactional worldview that makes **mercy** intolerable to him. 

Here is how the text reveals this progression:

**1. Anger and Envy are the Symptoms (vv. 28, 30)**
The elder brother is clearly angry and envious. He refuses to enter the party, and he bitterly contrasts his own faithlessness with his brother's debauchery. However, anger and envy are the visible smoke; they are not the fire. They are the emotional overflow of a deeper theological and relational distortion.

**2. Wounded Fairness is the Trunk (v. 29)**
When the father comes out to plead with him, the elder brother’s complaint is rooted in a skewed sense of fairness: *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee... and yet thou never gavest me a kid."* He views his relationship with his father as an economic transaction. He believes his obedience has accrued a debt that the father has failed to pay. His "fairness" is wounded because he operates on a merit-based system: *I obeyed, therefore I deserve a reward; he sinned, therefore he deserves punishment.* When the father violates this system of merit, the elder brother feels cheated.

**3. Pride in Obedience is the Root (v. 29)**
The true depth of his problem is revealed in his boast: *"neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment."* This is a stunning, self-righteous claim. In a few sentences, he has managed to reduce his father's love to a wage, his brother's life to a crime, and his own faithfulness to a burden. His pride in his obedience has blinded him to the fact that he has been living in his father's house, enjoying his father's provision, all along. He stayed home not out of love for his father, but out of a desire to earn assets for himself ("that I might make merry with my *friends*," not his father). 

**4. Discomfort with Mercy is the Ultimate Fruit (v. 30)**
Because his identity is built on his own merit, the father's mercy toward the younger brother is an existential threat to the elder brother. If the prodigal son can be restored without earning it, then the elder brother's years of "slaving" (a word he uses to describe his loving relationship with his father) are devalued. Mercy offends him because it renders his pride unnecessary. As long as the younger brother remains dead and lost, the elder brother's obedience looks like the superior path. Mercy destroys his illusion of superiority.

**Conclusion**
While the elder brother displays anger, envy, and a sense of wounded fairness, his deepest problem is **pride in obedience**. This pride turns his relationship with his father into a transaction, making him entirely **discomforted with mercy**. He would rather have justice (and his own self-righteousness) than a restored brother and a celebrating father.
