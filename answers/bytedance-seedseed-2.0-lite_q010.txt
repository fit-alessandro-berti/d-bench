First, it is important to note that interpretations of this Christian passage vary widely across denominations and individual faith traditions, but a common core reading of the power praised here centers on kenotic (self-emptying) power—a radical counterpoint to worldly, coercive power that accumulates status, control, and privilege. Unlike dominant cultural frameworks that frame power as the ability to dominate others or hoard resources, this passage lifts up a power exercised through voluntary humility: Christ, framed as possessing inherent divine greatness, chooses to set aside status to take on servanthood and ultimate suffering, prioritizing connection and care over self-preservation or hierarchical dominance. This power redefines strength as the capacity to humble oneself to lift others up, rather than to subjugate them.

As for why humility joined to greatness can feel unsettling to many (a common psychological and cultural reaction, though not a universal one, as many find this model life-giving as an alternative to violence of domination), there are two key reasons:
1. Open domination follows a predictable, familiar script. It operates within the hierarchical power structures most societies are socialized to recognize: those with power claim it, enforce their will, and draw clear lines between ruler and ruled. This dynamic is legible—we can name it, resist it, and orient ourselves around it. By contrast, humility from someone with unassailable greatness upends all unspoken rules of power. If someone who has every cultural or cosmic "right" to dominate chooses instead to serve, it shatters the assumption that self-serving power is the natural order.
2. This dynamic forces a personal reckoning. It holds up a mirror to our own small-scale choices to cling to our own privileges, or to exercise small forms of dominance in our relationships and communities to protect our own status. Unlike open domination, which we can dismiss as corrupt or unjust in others, self-emptying power from a great figure challenges our own acceptance of the world's hierarchical norms, making it uncomfortable to maintain our own unexamined choices to prioritize ourselves over others. For some, this unmooring of familiar power scripts can also spark suspicion, as it is so far removed from the self-serving power most people encounter regularly that it can feel ungraspable, as if it must hide a hidden agenda.
