In Ephesians 4:31–32 the two verses are not merely a list of “don’ts” followed by “dos.” They are a single, deliberate contrast between two ways of living after one has been wronged. Paul is finishing the larger argument of Ephesians 4—put off the old self, be renewed in mind, put on the new self—by showing what that renewal looks like in the church’s speech and relationships.

### Why bitterness and forgiveness are placed opposite each other

The verse sets up a before-and-after picture:

- **Old life:** bitterness, wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, malice  
- **New life:** kindness, tenderheartedness, forgiveness “even as God … hath forgiven you”

Bitterness is the root posture that produces the noisy fruit of anger, shouting, and slander. Forgiveness is the root posture that produces kindness and compassion. Paul places them side by side because they are two opposite responses to injury: one keeps a ledger of wrongs and lets it sour the heart; the other cancels the debt because God has already canceled ours in Christ.

### Is bitterness a feeling, a habit, a chosen posture, or contagious?

In this text, bitterness is **all of these, but it is most fundamentally a chosen relational posture** that soon takes over the other areas.

1. **A feeling**  
   The word *pikria* (“bitterness”) begins as a taste—something sharp, sour, unpalatable—and is used metaphorically for an inward disposition. Bitterness often starts with a real wound and an emotional reaction. The text does not deny the feeling; it forbids letting the feeling become a settled state.

2. **A habit**  
   The grammar matters: “Let … be put away” is an urgent command, while “be ye kind … forgiving” is a continuous command. Bitterness is not one bad moment but a pattern of returning to the injury, rehearsing it, and letting it shape how one speaks and acts. The list that follows—wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, malice—shows bitterness hardening into habitual speech and behavior.

3. **A chosen posture**  
   Because Paul commands believers to put it away, he treats bitterness as something we can repent of and resist. It is not an uncontrollable emotion that excuses bad conduct; it is a stance of the heart that can be brought to God and exchanged for grace.

4. **Something contagious**  
   Bitterness spreads through “clamour” and “evil speaking.” One resentful person can poison a household, a friendship, or a church by repeating the offense, recruiting allies, and souring the atmosphere. That is why Paul addresses the whole community: bitterness is not a private problem; it is a communal danger because it fragments the unity Christ has created.

### The key turn: “even as God … hath forgiven you”

Forgiveness here is not asked of us because the injury was small or because feelings have healed. It is asked because **we ourselves live by a forgiveness we did not earn.** The Greek word *charizomai* (“forgiving”) is related to *charis*, grace—to give freely, to release a debt. The command is not “stop hurting” but “become a conduit of the same grace you have received.”

So in this text, bitterness is the refusal of grace toward another; forgiveness is the extension of grace, modeled on God’s forgiveness in Christ. That is why they cannot coexist.
