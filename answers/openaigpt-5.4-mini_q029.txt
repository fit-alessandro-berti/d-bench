Galatians 6:1 requires a **restorative moral posture**, not a punitive one.

### What kind of posture?
The one who corrects must come as:

- **spiritual** — governed by the Spirit, not by pride, anger, or party spirit
- **meek** — gentle, restrained, and non-triumphalistic
- **self-aware / humble** — aware that they too are vulnerable to sin

So the posture is: **firm enough to correct, but humble enough to restore**.

### Why are restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion kept together?
Because Paul wants to prevent correction from becoming judgmental domination.

#### 1. **“Restore”** defines the goal
The aim is not to shame, win an argument, or exclude, but to **repair** what is broken.  
The word suggests mending or setting something back in place.

#### 2. **“In the spirit of meekness”** defines the manner
Even when correction is necessary, it must be done with **gentleness**.  
Meekness keeps correction from turning harsh, self-righteous, or humiliating.

#### 3. **“Considering thyself”** defines the attitude
The corrector must remember: **I am not immune**.  
This self-suspicion guards against hypocrisy and moral superiority. If I forget my own weakness, I will correct badly.

### The connection
Paul keeps them together because **true correction in the Christian life is both compassionate and cautious**:

- **restoration** without meekness becomes harshness,
- **meekness** without restoration becomes passivity,
- **self-suspicion** without restoration becomes fear,
- but together they produce **humble, healing correction**.

### In short
The required moral posture is:  
**a humble, Spirit-led readiness to heal a fellow believer while remembering one’s own fragility.**
