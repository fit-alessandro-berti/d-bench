Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously for a few overlapping reasons, but it’s not mainly sentimental. It’s best understood as a *deeper kind of moral strength* that people often misread through the lens of sentiment or usefulness.

### 1) People hear it as sentiment, not a real ethic
When Jesus says “love your enemies,” we often interpret “love” as a feeling—warmth, affection, emotional comfort. But enemies don’t reliably produce those feelings, so the command can seem naive or unrealistic. Many people assume the instruction is essentially “be nice,” rather than “practice a transforming kind of love” that can exist even without liking someone.

### 2) It feels strategically “irrational”—and people want security logic
Enemy-love also doesn’t align with the usual survival instincts: protect yourself, deter threats, retaliate when harmed. So it can sound impractical or even self-destructive—like it invites exploitation. If your moral framework is mainly about justice-as-retribution or security-as-deterrence, “pray for those who persecute you” may feel like surrender.

That said, Christian readings often argue it’s not denying the need for justice or protection; it’s directing the heart away from hatred and revenge.

### 3) It demands strength that contradicts our natural moral reflexes
The natural moral reflex after injury is: anger → resentment → dehumanization → retaliation. Enemy-love directly opposes that sequence. It’s hard because it asks for something costly: refusing to let hatred become your identity or your interpretation of reality.

That’s why it’s often described as *moral strength*: not weakness, not mere emotion, but the will to do good without surrendering your dignity or truth.

### 4) It threatens common “fairness” narratives
Many people want a clear equivalence: wrong deserves punishment, evil deserves defeat, enemies should be treated as less than fully human. Enemy-love forces a different standard—one grounded in God’s character: “he makes his sun… and sends rain…” to “evil and good.” In other words, it challenges the belief that moral worth is determined by whether someone is lovable or useful to you.

### So is it sentimental, strategic, or deeper moral strength?
**Most consistent answer: deeper moral strength.**  
Enemy-love is difficult because it isn’t primarily about producing a feeling or managing outcomes. It’s about resisting the moral corrosion of hatred and choosing beneficence even when hostility is deserved in the sense of being wrong.

**Strategic implications may follow** (it can disarm cycles of retaliation, break prejudice, witness to integrity), but the core is moral/spiritual: becoming “children of your Father,” shaped by divine impartial goodness.
