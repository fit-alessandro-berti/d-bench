Paul places bitterness and forgiveness against each other because they are rival ways of responding to injury within the Christian community.

- **Bitterness** (*pikria*) is more than a momentary feeling of hurt. It is hurt that has become sharp, settled, and resentful—a disposition that keeps an offense alive.
- **Forgiveness** is the opposite relational posture: releasing the claim to retaliate or to keep treating the offender according to the debt they owe, because God has forgiven us in Christ.

The contrast is especially clear in the flow of the passage:

> “Let all bitterness … be put away from you” (v. 31)  
> “And be kind … tenderhearted, forgiving one another” (v. 32).

Bitterness belongs with “wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, and malice.” Paul depicts it not as an isolated private emotion but as something that tends to develop outward expressions: angry eruptions, hostile speech, public conflict, and ill will. A resentful heart often becomes a destructive mouth and, eventually, a damaged community.

So the text treats bitterness in several connected ways:

1. **It begins in the realm of feeling.**  
   People are genuinely wounded. Paul is not denying that pain, injustice, or anger can be real.

2. **It can become a settled posture.**  
   “Bitterness” is not simply the first sting of being wronged; it is the cultivation or retention of resentment. The command to “put [it] away” means believers are not to make it their abiding disposition.

3. **It has habitual and moral force.**  
   Paul does not speak as though people have no agency before bitterness. His language calls Christians to renounce it—to refuse to feed it through rehearsing wrongs, hostile speech, retaliation, or malice.

4. **It is socially contagious.**  
   Though Ephesians 4:31 does not use the “root of bitterness” imagery found in Hebrews 12:15, it clearly views bitterness as community-damaging. It produces clamor and slander, grieves the Holy Spirit (v. 30), and tears at the unity of the body that Paul has been urging believers to preserve (4:1–16).

Forgiveness is therefore not mere emotional calm. The verb in verse 32 carries the sense of being gracious or freely giving pardon. It is grounded in a prior reality:

> “even as God in Christ forgave you.”

The Christian does not forgive because the offense was insignificant or because justice does not matter. Rather, the believer forgives as one who has received undeserved mercy from God. God’s forgiveness becomes both the model and the source of the church’s forgiveness.

That also means Paul is not necessarily commanding immediate restored trust, denial of wrongdoing, or the removal of wise boundaries. Forgiveness can coexist with truthful naming of evil, pursuit of justice, repentance, and caution. But it cannot coexist with the cherished desire to hold the offense against another in malice.

In short: **bitterness is woundedness hardened into resentful hostility; forgiveness is mercy extended from a heart softened by God’s mercy in Christ.** Paul sets them side by side because one corrodes the life of the body, while the other expresses the new life believers have received.
