The question about the difficulty of taking enemy-love seriously is both profound and philosophical, drawing from the profound teaching of Jesus in **Matthew 5:44–45**. This command, central to Christianity, urging us to love our enemies and bless them, transcends traditional notions of war, vengeance, or mere kindness:

---

### **Why Is Enemy-Love So Difficult to Take Seriously?**
1. **Emotional Considerations**:  
   - **Emotional Resonance**: Love requires empathy, understanding, and compassion. It challenges us to appreciate those who have caused harm or wrong, even those who are antagonistic. The act of loving an enemy involves acknowledging their humanity, forgiveness for their actions, and an openness to transformation. This emotional labor is stressful; it requires accepting conflict, pain, and potentially insurmountable odds for peace.

2. **Strategic Considerations**:  
   - **Cautious Approach**: Love does not always guarantee victory or protection. It does not always guarantee harm avoidance. The enemy may continue their behavior, requiring calculated resilience rather than aggression. This strategy might involve peace negotiations, mediation, or even noncompliance, challenging our natural instincts for retaliation or dominance.

3. **Moral Depth**:  
   - **Ethical Challenge**: Love as a moral command is not limited to individual ethics; it challenges the very core of societal structures and power imbalances. It requires operating in Alignment with what is justice AND benevolent, rather than benefiting from conflict. This depth involves imaginative justice, where outcomes prioritize peace and harmony rather than vengeance or division.

---

### **Is It Sentimental, Strategic, or a Deeper Form of Moral Strength?**
- **Sentimentalism**: Some argue that love for the enemy is driven purely by abstract morality rather than practical understanding. While it is a noble tendency, this interpretation often overlooks the psychological and strategic complexity required to sustain such love.
  
- **Strategic Reasoning**: This approach views love as a tool of peacebuilding, reconciliation, or negotiation. It may prioritize understanding motives and systems over immediate defense, aligning with sustainable peace-building strategies deemed useful in conflict resolution.

- **Deeper Moral Strength**: **Higher Morality**. Love for the enemy is one of the most profound and transformative virtues in Christianity. It requires courage, empathy, and sacrifice, embodying the Christian principle of love as acts of hope and resilience. By seeing enemy-love as a deeper moral strength, it addresses the intrinsic human tendency toward violence while promoting peace, healing, and justice.

**Conclusion**: Enemy-love is not easily mastered because it requires not just self-control but the capacity to bridge deep divides, transform adversities, and seek reconciliation. It is fundamentally a moral challenge that rewards those who can sustain it with empathy, strategy, and enduring hope.

--- 

This passage underscores the paradox of love—a gift that demands both vulnerability and resilience to truly materialize.
