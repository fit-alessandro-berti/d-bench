Great question. This image is sharp because Jesus uses **deliberate exaggeration** that is almost comical—and then turns it into moral diagnosis.

### Why the image is so vivid
- A **mote** = tiny speck (dust, splinter).
- A **beam** = large plank/timber.
- So Jesus paints an absurd scene: someone trying to do delicate eye work while a plank is sticking out of his own eye.
- The absurdity exposes how ridiculous moral pretension is when we ignore our own major faults.

In other words, the metaphor hits because it combines:
1. **Physical vividness** (eyes, pain, visibility),
2. **Scale contrast** (tiny speck vs giant beam),
3. **Irony** (the “helper” is blinder than the one he corrects).

### How it critiques hypocrisy—but not correction itself
Jesus does **not** end with “never remove your brother’s mote.”  
He says: **“first cast out the beam… then shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote.”**

So the passage forbids:
- self-righteous, selective judgment,
- correction used to elevate self,
- blindness to one’s own sin.

But it still allows—even expects:
- moral discernment,
- loving correction,
- restoration of others.

The key is **order and posture**:
1. Examine and repent of your own sin first.
2. Then correct others humbly, clearly, and helpfully.

So this is not a ban on moral accountability; it is a ban on **hypocritical accountability**.
