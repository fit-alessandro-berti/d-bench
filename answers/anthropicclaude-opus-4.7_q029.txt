# The Moral Posture of Galatians 6:1

Paul prescribes a posture that is almost paradoxical: the one doing the correcting must simultaneously be **strong enough to act** and **weak enough to tremble**. Three dispositions are fused—restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion—and none of them can be safely separated from the others.

## 1. Restoration, Not Exposure

The verb Paul uses (*katartizō*) is a surgical and mending word: it is used of setting a broken bone, mending fishing nets (Matt. 4:21), and refitting what has been torn. This governs the *aim* of the whole operation. The goal is not:

- to vent moral indignation,
- to establish the corrector's superiority,
- to punish, shame, or make an example,
- or even merely to rebuke.

The aim is the man's reintegration into wholeness. If your intended outcome is anything other than his healing, you are not doing what Paul commands, however accurate your diagnosis of his fault may be.

## 2. Meekness as the Only Safe Instrument

Meekness (*prautēs*) is not weakness or softness of speech; it is strength under restraint. A meek corrector is one who *could* crush but will not. This matters because correction is a dangerous instrument—like a scalpel, it heals in a steady hand and kills in a careless one. Harshness in correction often wounds more deeply than the original fault.

Notice also that meekness is a fruit of the Spirit (Gal. 5:23, just verses earlier). So "ye which are spiritual" does not mean the theologically advanced or the morally impressive; it means those actually walking in the Spirit, whose lives bear this specific fruit. Paul is screening the candidates: only the meek are authorized.

## 3. Self-Suspicion as the Guardrail

"Considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted." The Greek shifts here from plural ("ye") to singular ("thyself")—each corrector must do this personally. Paul requires the corrector to hold, in the same moment, two thoughts:

- *He has fallen.*
- *I could fall—perhaps by this very encounter.*

Why is this necessary? Because the act of correcting another is itself a temptation. It tempts toward pride ("I would never"), toward contempt, toward the subtle pleasure of being the judge rather than the judged. Self-suspicion is not false modesty; it is a realistic reading of one's own heart under the peculiar pressure of moral authority.

## Why the Three Must Stay Together

Separate any one from the others and the whole collapses:

| Combination | Result |
|---|---|
| Restoration without meekness | Officious meddling; surgery with a club |
| Restoration without self-suspicion | Condescension; the "helper" becomes the Pharisee |
| Meekness without restoration | Sentimental tolerance; the fault is left unaddressed |
| Meekness without self-suspicion | A gentleness that still looks down on the fallen |
| Self-suspicion without restoration | Paralysis; no one is ever helped |
| Self-suspicion without meekness | Anxious, guilt-driven correction that wounds |

The three together describe a person who **acts** (restoration), **acts gently** (meekness), and **acts humbly** (self-suspicion). This is why Paul frames it as a *spiritual* task: only the Spirit produces the rare composite of courage, tenderness, and humility required to correct a brother without harming him or oneself.

## The Deeper Logic

Underneath all three lies a single conviction: **the fallen man and the correcting man stand on the same ground.** Both are sinners, both are vulnerable, both are sustained only by grace. Correction in the biblical sense is not one person descending to rescue another; it is one beggar showing another where the bread is, knowing he could be hungry again tomorrow.
