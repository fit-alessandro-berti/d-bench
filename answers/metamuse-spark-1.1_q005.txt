Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously precisely because we *do* take evil seriously.

If someone has cursed you, hated you, used you - your first moral instinct isn't love, it's justice. And that instinct is not wrong. That's why Matthew 5:44-45 feels almost offensive on first reading.

It's difficult for three reasons:

**1. It violates reciprocity.** Human social life runs on reciprocity: do good to those who do good to you, do harm to those who harm you. Jesus explicitly rejects that equation in the verses right before this.

**2. It feels like betrayal.** To love an enemy can feel like betraying his victims - including yourself. If love means saying "what you did was okay," then it would be immoral.

**3. It seems impractical.** It doesn't work as immediate self-defense. Loving those who persecute you won't stop persecution in the moment.

So is it sentimental, strategic, or moral strength?

It can't be sentimental. Sentimental love is based on affection, attraction, or finding someone likable. The Greek word used here is *agape*, not *philia*. It doesn't mean "feel warm feelings about your enemies." It means will their ultimate good. You can will someone's good while still opposing them, telling the truth about them, and stopping them.

It is *partly* strategic, but not only that. There is a strategy to it. As Martin Luther King Jr., building directly on this passage, argued: hate multiplies hate. Enemy-love is the only force that can break a cycle of retaliation instead of just extending it. It refuses to let the enemy dictate what kind of person you become.

But at its core, Jesus frames it as a third thing: a deeper form of moral strength - and a reflection of character.

> "that ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven: for he maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good..."

The point of the sun and rain is this: God doesn't distribute common grace based on moral performance. If you are only good to people who are good to you, you aren't showing anything distinctive. You're just mirroring.

Enemy-love requires:

* **Strength to separate person from action.** You can still name an action as evil and unjust while refusing to reduce the person entirely to that evil.
* **Strength to refuse dehumanization.** It's easy to love humanity in the abstract. It's hard to not dehumanize the specific person who has hurt you.
* **Strength to keep your own soul intact.** Contempt and hatred are corrosive to the one who holds them. Praying for those who despitefully use you - the most difficult part of the verse - is not so much changing them, it's refusing to let their actions colonize your inner life.

In that sense, Jesus isn't calling for weakness. Sentimentality is weak - it collapses when tested. Enemy-love is strong because it can remain good under pressure, without needing the other person to be good first.

It does not mean you don't seek justice, set boundaries, or protect the vulnerable. Love without truth isn't love in this passage - it's just niceness.

The radical question of the passage is not "Can you like your enemy?" but "Can you want more for your enemy than just punishment - can you want their transformation, their own encounter with the sun and rain?"
