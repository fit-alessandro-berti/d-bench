### Why Are Bitterness and Forgiveness Placed So Directly Against Each Other?
The structure of these verses is a deliberate contrast, part of a broader pattern in Ephesians (and Pauline epistles generally) that emphasizes ethical transformation for believers. Ephesians 4 is focused on Christian unity and maturity, urging readers to "walk worthy of the vocation wherewith ye are called" (v. 1). Specifically:

- **The Contrast as Ethical Instruction**: Verses 31–32 form a "put off/put on" framework, echoing Ephesians 4:22–24, where Paul instructs believers to "put off" the old self (corrupted by deceitful desires) and "put on" the new self (created in God's likeness). Bitterness (along with wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, and malice) represents the "old" way of life—rooted in division, resentment, and self-centeredness. Forgiveness, kindness, and tenderheartedness represent the "new" way, modeled on God's forgiveness through Christ. This isn't random; it's a direct antithesis to illustrate that Christian living involves actively rejecting destructive attitudes and embracing restorative ones. Bitterness poisons relationships and community (a key theme in Ephesians, which stresses unity in the church body), while forgiveness heals and unites, reflecting God's redemptive work.

- **Theological Grounding**: The phrase "even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you" ties forgiveness to the gospel core. In Christian theology, human forgiveness isn't just a moral suggestion—it's an imitation of divine grace (cf. Matthew 6:14–15 or Colossians 3:13, which parallels this passage). Bitterness is positioned as the obstacle to this imitation because it festers inwardly and obstructs the outflow of grace. By juxtaposing them, Paul highlights that unchecked bitterness undermines the forgiveness believers have received, potentially leading to hypocrisy or spiritual stagnation. Commentators like F.F. Bruce (in *The Epistles to the Colossians, to Philemon, and to the Ephesians*) note this as a call to relational ethics in the early church, where grudges could fracture fragile communities amid persecution.

In short, the contrast serves as practical exhortation: bitterness erodes the unity and love that define the Christian community, while forgiveness embodies and extends Christ's redemptive mission.

### How Is Bitterness Portrayed—as a Feeling, Habit, Chosen Posture, or Something Contagious?
The text doesn't limit bitterness to a single category but portrays it in a multifaceted way, drawing from its Greek roots and biblical usage. The Greek word for "bitterness" here is *pikria* (πικρία), which can imply a sharp, acrid quality—like literal bitterness in taste—but metaphorically refers to resentment or a grudge-holding spirit. Let's break it down based on the verse and its context:

- **As a Feeling**: Yes, there's an emotional dimension. Bitterness often starts as a natural response to hurt, betrayal, or injustice (e.g., similar to how it's described in Acts 8:23, where Simon the Sorcerer is "in the gall of bitterness," implying a poisoned emotional state). In Ephesians, it's listed alongside "wrath" (*thymos*, explosive anger) and "anger" (*orgē*, simmering resentment), suggesting it's an affective state that can arise from interpersonal conflict. However, the text doesn't treat it as inevitable or uncontrollable—it's something to be "put away" (v. 31), implying agency over emotions.

- **As a Habit**: This fits well, as the imperative "let...be put away" (aorist tense in Greek, suggesting a decisive action) portrays bitterness as a pattern that can become ingrained if not addressed. It's not just a fleeting emotion but a habitual way of relating to others, akin to a vice that accumulates "with all malice" (v. 31). Paul warns against letting the sun go down on anger earlier in the chapter (v. 26), implying bitterness can harden into a routine mindset if fed by repeated rumination.

- **As a Chosen Posture**: Strongly emphasized here. The language of "put away" and "be ye kind" frames these as volitional choices, part of the ethical renewal in Christ. Bitterness isn't depicted as an unstoppable force but as an attitude believers can (and should) reject, much like choosing to forgive "as God...hath forgiven you." This aligns with the broader New Testament view that spiritual growth involves intentional decisions (e.g., Romans 6:13, yielding oneself to righteousness). It's a posture because it affects one's stance toward others—bitter people are less likely to be "tenderhearted" or forgiving.

- **As Something Contagious**: This is perhaps the most intriguing layer, though not explicit in these two verses alone. In the wider biblical canon, bitterness is often seen as infectious. Hebrews 12:15 warns of a "root of bitterness" that can "spring up" and "defile many," suggesting it spreads like a weed in a community, corrupting relationships and faith. Ephesians itself is concerned with church unity (e.g., vv. 1–16), so bitterness could be implied as contagious in how it fuels "clamour" (loud disputes) and "evil speaking" (slander), which divide groups. Early church fathers like John Chrysostom commented on this passage, noting how bitterness "poisons the soul" and extends to others, much like malice spreads. In modern psychological terms, this resonates with how resentment can create toxic environments, but the text's focus is spiritual contagion within the body of Christ.

Overall, bitterness is treated holistically—not just one thing, but an interconnected emotional, habitual, and volitional issue with potential to spread. It's something to actively eliminate to make room for forgiveness, which is presented as both a divine gift and a human responsibility.
