John makes the visible brother a test because love for God cannot remain merely inward, verbal, or mystical. God’s love necessarily takes concrete form in love for those God loves and commands us to love.

Several ideas are involved:

- **The visible brother provides a verifiable object of love.** It is easy to claim love for an unseen God because the claim cannot be directly observed. A brother, however, has needs, faults, demands, and inconveniences. Our treatment of him reveals whether our professed love is real.
- **Love for God includes obedience to God.** The next verse says, “he who loveth God love his brother also” (1 John 4:21). To reject the brother while claiming to love God is to reject God’s command while professing devotion to him.
- **God’s love produces love in those who receive it.** Throughout the passage, love originates with God and is manifested in Christ (vv. 9–10). Love of others is therefore not the price of God’s love but evidence that his love is “perfected” in us.
- **One cannot separate God from God’s children.** In context, “brother” especially means a fellow Christian. Hatred toward someone born of God contradicts love for the One who has begotten him (compare 1 John 5:1).

The self-deception exposed is **religious abstraction**: imagining that strong feelings, worship, correct language, or spiritual experiences amount to love for God while one remains contemptuous, indifferent, or malicious toward actual people. Such a person may be loving an idea of God—perhaps a God shaped to affirm the self—rather than the God who commands costly, embodied love.

This does not mean that love forbids disagreement, discipline, justice, or healthy boundaries. John’s contrast is between love and hatred: a settled refusal of another’s good. The point is that genuine love for the invisible God must become visible in patience, mercy, service, and concern for the brother before us. Otherwise, the profession “I love God” is contradicted by the life.
