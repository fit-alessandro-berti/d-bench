This verse from Luke 6:37 is often misunderstood as a blanket prohibition against *all* moral judgment, but it is actually a warning against the *spirit* of condemnation—particularly when it is driven by pride, self-righteousness, or a desire to control or belittle others. The key distinction lies between **healthy discernment** (which is biblical) and the **unhealthy appetite for condemnation** (which is not).

### **1. The Difference Between Discernment and Condemnation**
- **Discernment** is the ability to distinguish between truth and error, right and wrong, in a way that aligns with God’s standards. It is a **grace** given by the Holy Spirit (Hebrews 5:14) and is essential for spiritual health. A believer must be able to recognize sin, false teaching, and harmful behavior—not out of malice, but to protect the flock (Ezekiel 33:7-9).
  - Example: Jesus Himself made moral judgments (e.g., "You brood of vipers!"—Matthew 3:7; "Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees!"—Matthew 23:13-33). He did so with authority because He was God, but even His judgments were rooted in love and truth, not self-justification.

- **Condemnation** (in the negative sense of this verse) is a **self-serving, prideful, or legalistic** act of judgment that seeks to expose, shame, or control others rather than to correct or restore. It often stems from:
  - **Self-righteousness** ("I’m better than you")
  - **Fear** ("If I don’t judge, I’ll be judged")
  - **Control** ("I must expose their sin to maintain order")
  - **Self-justification** ("I’m doing this to prove my holiness")

  This kind of judgment is **not from God** but from a carnal, fleshly spirit (James 4:11-12). It is the opposite of the love and humility Christ modeled.

### **2. Why Does Jesus Warn Against Condemnation?**
- **It reveals the judge’s own heart.** When we condemn others, we often project our own unconfessed sins (Matthew 7:1-5). The Pharisees were quick to condemn others while ignoring their own hypocrisy (Matthew 23).
- **It blocks grace.** Condemnation hardens hearts and prevents people from receiving forgiveness (John 8:1-11). If we are always pointing out others’ flaws, we leave no room for them to see their need for Christ.
- **It invites the same backfire.** If we judge others with an unrepentant heart, we will be judged in kind (Matthew 7:2). The law of sowing and reaping applies here (Galatians 6:7-8).

### **3. How Does This Relate to Moral Judgment?**
Jesus is **not** saying that moral distinctions don’t matter—He is saying that **our motive and method** in making them must be pure. A believer must:
- **Judge sin, not people** (Romans 14:10-12). We can say, "That behavior is sinful," but not, "That person is damned."
- **Judge in love** (Galatians 6:1). Correction should aim at restoration, not destruction.
- **Judge with humility** (1 Corinthians 4:3-5). We must recognize that our own judgment is limited and that we will be held accountable for how we judge others.

### **4. Practical Examples**
| **Condemnation (Wrong Spirit)** | **Discernment (Right Spirit)** |
|----------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| *"You’re so selfish for not helping!"* (shaming) | *"I see that you’re struggling with generosity. Let’s pray about how we can support each other."* (restorative) |
| *"That teaching is clearly false."* (dismissive) | *"I’ve studied this passage differently. Let’s discuss it with humility."* (seeking truth) |
| *"You’re so judgmental for judging others!"* (hypocritical) | *"I know judging is dangerous, so I’ll pray before speaking."* (self-aware) |

### **5. The Role of Forgiveness**
The verse pairs condemnation with forgiveness because:
- **Forgiveness is the antidote to condemnation.** When we forgive others, we break the cycle of bitterness and legalism.
- **We are forgiven so that we might forgive.** If we refuse to forgive, we prove we haven’t truly experienced God’s grace (Ephesians 4:32).
- **Forgiveness is not approval.** It means we no longer hold the offense against them, but we can still set boundaries for their well-being.

### **6. When Is Moral Judgment Necessary?**
There are times when discernment *must* be exercised:
- **Against false doctrine** (1 Timothy 1:19-20, Titus 1:10-16).
- **Against sin that harms the church** (1 Corinthians 5:1-13).
- **Against personal sin in our own lives** (Galatians 6:1).

But even in these cases, the approach should be:
✅ **Gentle** (James 3:17)
✅ **Humble** (Proverbs 11:2)
✅ **Motivated by love** (1 Corinthians 13:4-7)

### **Conclusion**
Jesus’ warning is not against **moral discernment** (which is biblical) but against the **spirit of condemnation** (which is demonic). The difference is found in the **heart motive**:
- **Discernment** seeks **truth and restoration**.
- **Condemnation** seeks **control, pride, or self-justification**.

A mature believer will:
1. **Recognize sin** (when it is sin).
2. **Not delight in exposing it** (unless necessary for correction).
3. **Forgive freely** (because they have been forgiven much).
4. **Trust God’s justice** (not their own).

In this way, we walk in the balance of **righteous judgment** (John 7:24) without the **legalism of condemnation**.
