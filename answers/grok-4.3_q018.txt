The contrast in Ephesians 4:31–32 is deliberate and structural. Paul is not merely listing random vices and virtues; he is describing the “old self” (the former way of life, v. 22) versus the “new self” (created after the likeness of God, v. 24). Verse 31 gives the negative commands—“put away” (ἀρθήτω) all of these things—while verse 32 gives the positive replacement: be kind, tenderhearted, and forgiving. Bitterness sits at the head of the negative list because it is the settled disposition that makes the rest (wrath, anger, clamor, slander, malice) likely and that directly blocks the forgiveness commanded in the next verse.

### Why bitterness specifically is set against forgiveness

Forgiveness in this passage is not primarily an emotional state but an action of “gracing” someone (χαριζόμενοι)—releasing a debt or claim against them. Bitterness (πικρία) is the opposite posture: a sustained, resentful holding on. The text places them in direct opposition because they are functionally incompatible in community life. One cannot simultaneously nurse bitterness toward someone and extend the kind of forgiveness Paul describes. The bitterness functions as the internal barrier; forgiveness is the active removal of that barrier.

### How the text treats bitterness

Paul does not present bitterness as a purely involuntary feeling that simply happens to a person. The imperative “put away” treats it as something a believer can and must reject. This suggests it is at minimum a chosen posture or cultivated habit. 

At the same time, the broader biblical usage (including the warning in Hebrews 12:15 about a “root of bitterness” springing up and defiling many) indicates that Paul sees it as potentially contagious within the body. An embittered person tends to spread resentment, suspicion, and division. That is why it appears in a list of relational sins that damage the unity Paul has been urging throughout Ephesians 4.

In short, the text treats bitterness as:
- More than raw emotion (feelings of hurt or disappointment can arise without becoming πικρία).
- A chosen and maintained attitude that hardens into a habit.
- Something that spreads and poisons relationships, making it especially dangerous in the church.

The direct opposition to forgiveness is therefore both logical and practical: the posture of nursing offense prevents the posture of releasing it, and the community cannot maintain the “one another” life Paul describes if bitterness is allowed to remain.
