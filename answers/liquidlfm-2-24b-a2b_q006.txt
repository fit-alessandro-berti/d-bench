This passage from Mark 8:34–35 presents a profound call to radical discipleship that challenges conventional notions of self-identity and purpose. Here's a breakdown of the question:

---

### **1. What kind of self is being denied here?**

In this context, "denying oneself" refers to letting go of one’s **self-centered ambitions, desires, and identity** in favor of following Jesus. Specifically:

- **Denying personal ambition or status**: The individual must set aside their own goals, pride, or desire for recognition.
- **Denying self-preservation or comfort**: The call to "take up your cross" implies embracing suffering, sacrifice, and vulnerability rather than seeking ease or safety.
- **Denying autonomy**: The person must submit to Jesus’ authority over their life, not living for themselves but for His mission.

This is not about self-hatred or nihilism, but about reorienting one’s identity around service, humility, and alignment with God’s purposes (cf. Phil. 2:5–8).

---

### **2. Why is this liberating to some?**

For many, this message resonates deeply because it offers:
- **Freedom from the pressure to "make it"**: In a world obsessed with success, fame, or material security, the call to reject self-promotion can feel freeing. It shifts focus from human validation to divine purpose.
- **Hope in suffering**: The promise that "whoever loses his life for my sake and the gospel’s will save it" reframes sacrifice as redemptive, not futile. This can empower those who feel marginalized or powerless.
- **Community and meaning**: Following Jesus often involves belonging to a countercultural movement that values love, justice, and spiritual fulfillment over worldly gains.

---

### **3. Why is this offensive to others?**

Others may find this message troubling because:
- **Perceived anti-individualism**: Emphasizing self-denial can seem to suppress individuality, creativity, or personal aspirations, clashing with modern ideals of self-expression and autonomy.
- **Moral demands**: The idea that true life comes through loss (e.g., career, relationships, comfort) may feel harsh or guilt-inducing, especially to those who equate happiness with personal achievement.
- **Cultural resistance**: In societies that prioritize self-actualization and individualism, the call to "deny oneself" can appear regressive or incompatible with modern values like mental health prioritization or work-life balance.

---

### **Conclusion**

The tension lies in the **conflict between self-centeredness and self-giving**. To some, the passage is liberating because it offers a path to transcendence beyond ego-driven struggles. To others, it feels oppressive because it challenges deeply ingrained cultural narratives about success and identity. Ultimately, the interpretation hinges on how one understands the relationship between the self and the divine—or whether one sees life’s ultimate meaning as rooted in personal fulfillment or sacrificial love.
