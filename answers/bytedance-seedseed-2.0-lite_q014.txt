This passage affirms both that treasure reveals the heart and that treasure trains the heart, with possession, attention, and love woven into a mutually reinforcing, recursive dynamic that underpins Jesus’ core command in these verses:
### First: Treasure both reveals and trains the heart
The closing line’s explanatory conjunction ("For") grounds Jesus’ commands in a built-in alignment of heart and treasure that works in two directions:
1. **Treasure first reveals the heart**: The statement "where your treasure is, there will your heart be also" functions first as a diagnostic truth. If you are pouring your resources into storing up earthly wealth, status, or security, that investment reveals your heart’s deepest loyalties and desires are already oriented toward temporal, earthly things. Your treasure placement is a visible marker of your heart’s existing orientation.
2. **Treasure also trains the heart**: This is not merely a static observation—Jesus issues a command to shift your treasure-storing from earth to heaven, which presumes that intentionally placing your treasure in a new location will reorient your heart to follow. The alignment is not one-way: your heart leads you to place your treasure, but the act of investing your resources in heavenly good draws your heart to fix itself on that eternal realm, reshaping and training your desires over time.
### The tie between possession, attention, and love
These three forces are inseparable in the passage’s logic:
- **Possession**: "Laying up treasure" centers on what you do with the resources you steward: money, time, energy, influence. Storing up earthly treasure means hoarding these resources for your own temporal benefit; storing up heavenly treasure means deploying them for kingdom-focused good (the rest of Matthew’s Gospel frames caring for the vulnerable, loving God and neighbor, and investing in spiritual life as the only "treasure" that lasts).
- **Attention**: Where you place your possessions necessarily directs your focus. Earthly treasures demand constant vigilance: you must monitor for decay (moth, rust) and theft, so your attention is trapped in the anxiety of protecting temporary gains. Heavenly treasures, by contrast, draw your attention to the unshakable life of God’s kingdom, freeing you from that cycle of worry.
- **Love**: The heart’s core orientation of love is both the starting point and outcome of this dynamic. Your initial love—what you most deeply value and desire—leads you to place your treasure somewhere, so your treasure reveals the love that already orders your heart. But when you obey Jesus’ command to redirect your possessions to heavenly treasures, that intentional investment trains your love: your attention and resources oriented toward God’s kingdom deepen your love for those things, reordering your heart over time to fix it on what lasts, rather than what fades.
