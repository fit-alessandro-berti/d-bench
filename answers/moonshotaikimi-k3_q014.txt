## The sentence itself points to formation

Read literally, the saying asserts that treasure **moves** the heart, and only secondarily that it reveals it. Two features of the text press this direction:

- **The tenses.** In the Greek, the treasure *is* somewhere (present, *estin*); the heart *will be* there (future, *estai*). The treasure's location is treated as the settled fact; the heart's arrival is pending. The heart is portrayed as migrating toward the treasure over time — which is exactly what "training" looks like.
- **The logic of the command.** Verse 21 begins with "for" (*gar*) — it is the *reason* given for the imperatives in verses 19–20. If treasure merely *revealed* a heart that was already fixed, commanding you to relocate the treasure would be pointless; the heart would stay put. The command makes sense only if moving the treasure is a lever for moving the heart. Jesus is telling you to act on the asset because you cannot act directly on the affections.

So the primary claim is formative: **attachment follows investment.** This is deliberately counterintuitive. We tend to assume the opposite order — that we spend on what we already love, so that our spending is a readout of our heart. Jesus claims the current runs the other way: what you fund, store, and guard is what you will come to love. (Interestingly, Matthew also gives the reverse direction elsewhere — "the good person out of his good treasure brings forth good" (12:35). Taken together, the two sayings describe a feedback loop: the heart shapes its investments, and the investments reshape the heart. The Sermon intervenes at the actionable end of the loop — the hand, not the heart, can be commanded.)

The revealing function survives as a corollary: if you want to locate your heart, audit your treasure. But that is a diagnostic use of a formative law.

## How possession, attention, and love get welded together

The key is to notice that 6:19–24 is one continuous argument, and your three terms map onto its three movements:

- **Possession** — treasure on earth vs. treasure in heaven (vv. 19–21)
- **Attention** — the eye as the lamp of the body (vv. 22–23)
- **Love/allegiance** — no one can serve two masters (v. 24)

The chain works like this:

**Possession conscripts attention.** The details of verse 19 are not incidental. Earthly treasure is exposed to moth, corrosion (literally "eating"), and thieves who *dig through* mud-brick walls — which means it must be watched, insured, worried over. Vulnerable wealth captures the mind; vigilance is attention conscripted. This is why the passage flows without a seam into "do not be anxious" (v. 25): where your treasure sits determines your baseline anxiety, because the heart keeps checking on it.

**Attention, sustained, becomes love.** The eye saying is the mechanism. A "single" eye (*haplous* — undivided, and in this word-group often *generous*) fills the whole body with light; an "evil" eye — a Semitic idiom for the grudging, envious gaze (compare Matt 20:15) — fills it with darkness. Whatever holds your gaze ends up filling your interior. Attention is not neutral observation; it is formative. We become like what we steadily look at.

**Love, enthroned, becomes lordship.** Verse 24 states the outcome: "he will hate the one and love the other... you cannot serve God and mammon." Note that mammon is treated as a *master* (a *kyrios*) — the passage ends by revealing that what you thought you possessed has become your possessor. Treasure → gaze → love → master. That is the whole biography of an idol in four verses.

## What "training" looks like in practice

In Jewish idiom of the period, "treasure in heaven" had a concrete referent: almsgiving and deeds of mercy (Tobit 4:8–10; Sirach 29:11–12 assume the same storehouse imagery). Jesus is not advocating a sentiment but a transfer of assets. The encounter with the rich man in Mark 10 is almost a dramatization of Matthew 6:21 — "sell what you have, give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven; then come, follow me" — and the man's collapse proves the verse: "he went away sorrowful, for he had great possessions." His treasure was on earth, so his heart could not follow Jesus anywhere else. The order of Jesus' demand is the order of the saying: move the treasure first; the freed heart follows.

One last subtlety: Jesus keeps the self-interested framing — "lay up *for yourselves*." He does not suppress the acquisitive instinct; he reroutes it, guaranteeing the investment through "your Father who sees in secret" (6:4). The heart is trained not by renouncing treasure but by banking it with the Father — until the Father himself becomes the treasure, and the loop closes: possession placed with God, attention fixed on God, love given to God.

**In short:** the text says treasure *trains* the heart — the heart emigrates to wherever the assets are held — and therefore treasure also *reveals* the heart, as the visible trail of an invisible migration. Possession is the lever, attention is the conduit, and love is the sediment attention leaves behind.
