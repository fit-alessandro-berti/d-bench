# Repentance as Total Reorientation in Isaiah 55:6–7

## The Distinction Between "Way" and "Thoughts"

The pairing is deliberate and structurally complete. **"Way"** (*derek*) refers to the habitual path of conduct — the pattern of choices, associations, and behaviors that have become characteristic. **"Thoughts"** (*machasheboth*) refers to schemes, imaginations, and the interior reasoning that generates those behaviors. Together they form a merism covering the whole person: what a man *does* and what a man *devises*.

This matters enormously because it closes off a partial repentance. One might abandon outward wickedness while nursing inward desires, or one might cultivate pious feelings while unchanged in practical life. Isaiah's formulation **refuses both half-measures**.

## What This Implies About Human Corruption

The structure implies that sin operates on two levels simultaneously:

- The **will expressed outwardly** — relationships, transactions, habits of life
- The **imagination operating inwardly** — the workshop where sinful action is first conceived and where idolatry of the heart resides

This anticipates what Jesus makes explicit in the Sermon on the Mount: that murder begins in contempt and adultery in the wandering eye. The prophet understands that the **inner life is not neutral ground** requiring only behavioral correction on the surface.

## The Possibility of Mercy

Two remarkable things follow from the double demand:

**First**, the very completeness of what is required might seem to make mercy *less* likely — if both action and thought must change, who can qualify? But the passage reverses this: precisely because God reaches into the whole person, his pardon is correspondingly total. The Hebrew *yarbeh lisloach* — **"he will multiply pardon"** — is extraordinary. It is not merely that he will forgive; he will *exceed* what the penitent dares to hope for.

**Second**, the urgency of verses 6–7 ("while he may be found," "while he is near") suggests that **the capacity to repent is itself a moment of grace** not indefinitely available. The invitation to turn is simultaneously a gift. This removes any ground for presumption — delay is dangerous — while preserving complete ground for hope in the one who actually turns.

## The Integrated Logic

| Element | Dimension | What Must Change |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| "Way" | Outward | Conduct, habits, associations |
| "Thoughts" | Inward | Imagination, schemes, desires |
| "Return unto the Lord" | Relational | The direction of the whole self |

The word **"return"** (*yashuv*) ties it together. Repentance is not primarily self-improvement across two categories; it is **reorientation toward a Person**. The forsaking of way and thoughts serves the return, which is why mercy follows. God is not rewarding moral achievement but receiving a prodigal who has turned his face homeward — both his feet and his mind now pointed in the same direction.

The mercy is therefore **as comprehensive as the repentance demanded**: it covers what was done and what was imagined, because the God to whom the sinner returns is Lord of both the outward life and the secret heart.
