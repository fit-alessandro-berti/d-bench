Great question. Let’s break down Isaiah 55:6–7 and what it means to say that repentance involves both “way” (outward conduct) and “thoughts” (inward disposition).

**Text Review:**
> “Let the wicked forsake his way, and the unrighteous man his thoughts: and let him return unto the Lord, and he will have mercy upon him…”

**1. Forsake his way:**  
This refers to outward behavior—the patterns of action, choices, and lifestyle that define someone’s daily conduct. For the “wicked,” their “way” is the path of sin, rebellion, or harmful actions. To forsake one’s way is to stop doing the things that are wrong or harmful. It’s about changing what you do.

**2. Forsake his thoughts:**  
This refers to inward attitudes, motives, intentions, and desires. “Thoughts” here likely means the mindset that gives rise to wicked actions. For the “unrighteous man,” it’s not enough to just stop doing wrong things if his heart still harbors pride, anger, deceit, or other sinful attitudes. He must change how he thinks and what he desires.

**Implication about Outward Conduct and Inward Imagination:**
The verse implies that true repentance is holistic. It’s not just about outward reform or merely stopping bad behavior. Repentance also requires a transformation of the heart and mind—a turning away from evil intentions and desires. Both actions and attitudes must change.

- **Outward conduct** (the “way”) shows up in how we live and what we do.
- **Inward imagination** (the “thoughts”) shows up in what we desire, believe, and intend.

God is calling for a complete change, not just a behavioral one. The “way” is the fruit of changed “thoughts,” but both must be addressed.

**Possibility of Mercy:**
The promise in this passage is that if someone truly repents—changing both what they do and what they think—then God will “have mercy” and “abundantly pardon.” This shows that God’s mercy is available to those who pursue genuine repentance, not just outward religiosity or ritual. It’s for those whose hearts have been transformed.

**In summary:**  
Isaiah 55:6–7 teaches that true repentance involves both a change in outward behavior (forsaking one’s “way”) and a change in inward attitudes and desires (forsaking one’s “thoughts”). This dual transformation reflects the fullness of turning away from sin and returning to God. Only when both are addressed can a person fully experience God’s abundant mercy and forgiveness.
