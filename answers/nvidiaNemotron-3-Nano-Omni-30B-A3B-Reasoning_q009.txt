**Answer:**

This passage defines victory over evil not as retaliation or domination, but as **overcoming evil with good** — a profound, countercultural strength that is both courageous and transformative.

The command is **genuinely strong**, not weak or manipulative. Let’s break down why:

1. **It Requires Moral Courage**  
   To respond to hostility with kindness — to feed an enemy or give drink to someone who has wronged you — goes against natural human instincts. This is not passive submission; it is active, deliberate resistance to bitterness and vengeance. Choosing good in the face of evil demands emotional maturity, self-control, and deep conviction.

2. **It Refuses to Perpetuate the Cycle of Harm**  
   "An eye for an eye" may feel just, but it only fuels endless retaliation. By contrast, feeding or giving drink to an enemy "heaps coals of fire on his head" — a metaphor suggesting that kindness can provoke guilt, shame, or even change in the evildoer. The real victory lies in breaking the cycle, not escalating it.

3. **It Reflects Divine Character**  
   The passage invokes God’s justice and mercy, echoing Jesus’ command to "love your enemies" (Matthew 5:44). This isn’t human weakness — it’s a reflection of divine power. Only God can truly overcome evil; when we act in love, we participate in that divine victory.

4. **The Metaphor of "Coals of Fire"**  
   This vivid image suggests that kindness can be a powerful, transformative force. It may not defeat the enemy immediately, but it creates spiritual heat — conviction, discomfort, or awakening — that can lead to repentance or transformation.

In sum, **the command is strong** — not because it uses force, but because it overcomes evil at its root: through love, generosity, and moral triumph. It is the highest form of strength — the strength of Christ-like grace.
