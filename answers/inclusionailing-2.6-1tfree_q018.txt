The pairing is not accidental; it is structural.  
The text lines up two economies of relation—one that **withholds** and one that **releases**—and forces the contrast at the point where a person’s “inside” meets another person’s “outside.”

---

### 1. How the passage defines bitterness

Notice the cluster: *bitterness, wrath, anger, clamour, evil speaking, malice.*  
- **Bitterness** is first; functionally it is the root and the soil.  
- The others are its expressions: *wrath* and *anger* are the heat; *clamour* and *evil speaking* are the noise that broadcasts the injury; *malice* is the settled intention to diminish another.

In ordinary Greek usage *πικρία* (bitterness) is taste—something that has soured. In the LXX it marks undrinkable water, gall, a life turned acrid. By the time Paul uses it, “bitterness” is not simply a feeling that happens to you; it is:

- a **habit of rehearsing** an injury,  
- a **posture** that arranges the self against another in a fixed, defensive geometry, and  
- a **contagion**, because bitterness externalizes itself: it spreads by eliciting corresponding reactions (clamour, evil speaking, escalation).

So the text treats it as **all of the above**: feeling crystallized into conduct, a chosen stance sustained by repetition, and a social toxin that moves from person to person.

---

### 2. Why forgiveness is set directly opposite

The second sentence does not merely say “be positive”; it says **be kind, tenderhearted, forgiving**.  
- *Kindness* is active beneficence.  
- *Tenderheartedness* (literally “pitiable,” from the root for “pit” or “inner organs”) is vulnerability turned outward—capacity to feel another’s need without armoring.  
- *Forgiveness* is the decisive economic act: **release of the claim**.

If bitterness is a ledger that keeps a debt outstanding (“you owe me for this hurt”), forgiveness is the explicit cancellation of that ledger—even while memory may remain. It is not the denial of injury; it is the refusal to let the injury dictate future relation.

That is why the two are placed in binary opposition:  
- Bitterness **captures the future by means of the past**; it says the past controls what can happen next.  
- Forgiveness **surrenders the leverage of the past**; it says the past is named, named rightly as wrong, but will not monopolize tomorrow.

---

### 3. The ground of the alternative

The text drives the contrast to its ultimate base:  
*“forgiving one another, even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you.”*

There the choice snaps into focus:

- Bitterness **derives its ultimate authority from the self**—my grievance, my justice, my protection. It requires continual feeding: retaliation, rumination, rehearsing.  
- Forgiveness **derives its possibility from a prior gift**—a forgiveness already given that was not earned. To refuse forgiveness to another is, in the logic of the passage, to refuse the grammar by which one has oneself been addressed.

Thus the opposition is not just psychological (feeling vs. feeling) or merely ethical (rule vs. rule). It is **ecclesial and eschatological**: the community is to be a place where the circulation of grievance is broken by a prior circulation of forgiveness.

---

### 4. What the text is *not* doing

The passage does not say:

- “Suppress your anger.”  
- “Pretend the wrong did not matter.”  
- “Forgive because time will heal it anyway.”

Instead it **names bitterness as an active power with accomplices** and then **relocates the person in a different story**—one in which the cost of the injury has already been absorbed by a different party (Christ). That relocation makes forgiveness not a heroic virtue but a received rhythm: having been treated as the forgivable, you extend the same grammar to the other.

---

### 5. Short answer to your question

Bitterness is treated as **feeling hardened into habit, posture, and communicable atmosphere**.  
It is placed directly against forgiveness because both are **determinative acts about who controls the future**.  
Bitterness lets the past colonize tomorrow; forgiveness breaks that colonization by appealing to a gift that has already reset the ledger—so that kindness is no longer a risk but a recognizable home.
