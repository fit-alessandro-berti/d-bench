This verse identifies a specific kind of self-deception: the belief that love can exist in the abstract, untested by concrete demands.

**The logic of the test**

The visible neighbor is harder to love than the invisible God, not easier. God, unseen, makes no immediate claims on your patience, your resources, your pride. You can love an abstraction on your own terms—love it in prayer, in feeling, in theology—without ever being inconvenienced. The brother, seen daily, in his actual irritating or needy particularity, costs something. He interrupts, contradicts, asks for things. He is loved, if at all, at your own expense rather than his.

So the author reasons: if you fail the *easier*, more demanding-in-visibility test (love of the person right in front of you, whose need you cannot pretend not to see), how could you possibly be passing the harder, more demanding-in-reality test (love of God, whose demands are not concrete and therefore not obviously demanding)? The visible case is diagnostic because it cannot be faked as easily. Love of God, being unfalsifiable in daily experience, is exactly the kind of claim that self-deception loves to make—because nothing forces you to discover you're lying.

**The self-deception being exposed**

The specific illusion is that love can be *purely inward*—a disposition, a feeling, a claim about one's relation to the ultimate, that exists independently of behavior toward available others. This is attractive precisely because it's unaccountable. You can tell yourself you love God and feel sincere doing it, because God never shows up to be annoying, needy, or wrong in a way that costs you.

The brother is the reality-check because he is *given*—not chosen, not abstract, not able to be idealized away. Failing to love him isn't a separate failure alongside failing to love God; it's the *evidence* that the love of God was never operative in the first place, only asserted. The text doesn't say "you should also love your brother." It says: your claim to love God is *false*, demonstrably, by this test. The invisible love was fictional because it never had to prove itself against resistance—and the resistance is exactly what the visible brother provides.

In short: the verse exposes the self-deception of mistaking sentiment or belief *about* love for love itself, and it uses the visible/invisible asymmetry to show why that mistake is so easy to make and so easy to catch.
