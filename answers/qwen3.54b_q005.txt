This is one of the most profound paradoxes in human history: a command to love that seems antithetical to the fundamental nature of conflict and self-preservation. To take "love your enemies" seriously takes us out of the realm of psychology, into the realm of virtue ethics and theology. 

Is it sentimental? No. Is it strategic? Not primarily. **It is a deeper form of moral strength.** 

Here is why we struggle to take this seriously, and why those three categories don't fully capture the command.

### 1. Why it is so difficult to take seriously
Human beings are hardwired by evolution to care for their own and those who have cared for them. Nature dictates reciprocity: *if I treat you well, you will do the same.* If this rule of social exchange is broken—if one party refuses to reciprocate—the social contract breaks down, creating a cycle of defense.

We dismiss enemy-love when we confuse it with "liking" or "forgiving." The difficulty arises because:
*   **Emotional Reality:** It is psychologically exhausting to feel affection toward someone who is actively hurting you. This isn't sentimental; it is an emotional mismatch. The brain perceives the enemy as a threat to survival, which triggers fight-or-flight responses to protect oneself.
*   **Moral Complexity:** We often confuse "love" with "agreement." If I cannot agree with your behavior or your character, does that mean I am not allowed to love you? Jesus is challenging this binary.

### 2. Critiquing the Three Categories

**A. Is it Sentimental?**
Sentimentality implies a wishy-washy feeling: "I should feel love if God tells me to." 
The Gospel text explicitly refutes this when we read in Matthew 5:37 ("Not with the 'you' I call you..."). The Greek word for love here (*agapē*) is not an emotion that follows the heart; it is a will of action. If one treats it as sentimental, they will fall into hypocrisy—a performance of kindness without internal transformation. To take it seriously means acknowledging that *feelings* are unreliable, but *wills* are powerful.

**B. Is it Strategic?**
Many argue this is a strategy: "If I love my enemies, they cannot hurt me as much," or "I will look better in the public eye and win over the world." 
The question asks if it is strategic. A strategy is about achieving a specific end (peace, power, safety). Jesus' command ends with an identity declaration ("*that ye may be the children of your Father*"). The goal is not the outcome of the enemy's forgiveness; it is the state of the practitioner’s soul. Strategy implies a transactional exchange; this text calls for a unilateral act of will.

**C. Is it Moral Strength?**
This is the only category that fits the gravity of the text. To love one who persecutes you requires more than affection or tactics; it takes supreme moral discipline. It mirrors the nature of God, who does not look down on the good for the evil (impartiality). 
To love an enemy is to refuse to give them their due—refusal to let them dictate your emotional landscape. This is strength because it overrides fear and natural instincts.

### 3. Synthesis: Why this changes how we live
Taking "love of enemies" seriously means shifting focus from *what the enemy does* to *who I am*. 

The command is not about making people better (like a therapist); it is about making people holy (like a sower). When you are forced to love an enemy, and you fail to do so, your moral character breaks. But when you succeed, you transcend natural law.

**Conclusion:**
It is difficult to take seriously because we view "love" as an emotion. If viewed as **moral strength**, it becomes a discipline of the will, where one chooses *who they are* over *what feels right*. This does not require us to change our mind on how someone acts toward us; rather, it requires that even in the face of their actions, we do not let them win spiritually. It is a hard command because the universe is designed for reciprocity, but Jesus invites a community built on something far more powerful: grace.

**So, is it sentimental? No. Strategic? Not really. It is Moral Strength.**
