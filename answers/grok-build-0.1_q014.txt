The verse does not present a clean "either/or" between revealing and training the heart. It presents a dynamic relationship in which **treasure both reveals and shapes** the heart, with the stronger emphasis falling on the shaping effect.

### The Logical Direction of the Statement

The key line is: "For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also." 

Jesus is not primarily saying "your heart determines your treasure" (though that is true in other places). He is saying the reverse: **the location of your treasure exerts a gravitational pull on your heart**. This is why the commands in verses 19-20 are commands about *where to put* treasure, not merely commands to have the right attitude. Jesus is giving a practical means of reorienting the heart.

This creates a feedback loop:
- **Treasure reveals** the heart in the sense that we can diagnose the true state of our affections by observing what we actually protect, pursue, and worry about.
- **Treasure trains** the heart in the sense that sustained investment in something gradually captures the attention and moves the affections. The heart tends to follow the money, the time, and the emotional energy.

### How Possession, Attention, and Love Become Entangled

Jesus is working with a simple but profound psychological and spiritual mechanism:

1. **Possession** (what you treat as treasure) creates **attention**.  
   Earthly treasure, by its nature, requires vigilance. It can decay, be stolen, or lose value. Therefore it naturally pulls the mind toward itself. You check on it. You calculate about it. You become emotionally invested in its security. This is why Jesus immediately follows this passage with the command not to be anxious (6:25-34). Anxiety is often the emotional evidence that your treasure is in the wrong place.

2. **Attention** generates **love** (or at least affection and allegiance).  
   What we consistently attend to, we come to value more. This is not just modern psychology; it is basic to how the Bible understands the heart. The heart is not primarily the seat of spontaneous emotion but the center of one's commitments and attachments. Repeated attention strengthens attachment. Over time, what you possess begins to possess you.

3. **Love** then reinforces the original pattern of possession.  
   Once something has your heart, you will naturally seek to accumulate more of it. This is why Jesus treats "treasure on earth" and "treasure in heaven" as mutually exclusive orientations rather than merely different asset classes.

### The Implication

The verse is not just diagnostic ("show me where your money is and I'll show you your heart"). It is also **prescriptive**. By deliberately directing resources, energy, and risk toward things that cannot be corrupted or stolen (acts of love, generosity, the work of the kingdom, eternal realities), a person is actively retraining their own heart. The action precedes and shapes the affection.

This is why the surrounding context (not serving two masters, not being anxious) treats the location of treasure as a matter of lordship. The heart will serve whatever it is most invested in. The command to "lay up treasure in heaven" is therefore a strategy for freeing the heart, not merely a call to better accounting.
