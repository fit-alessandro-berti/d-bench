

**Short answer:**  
In Ephesians 4:31‑32 Paul pits *bitterness* (πικρία, *pikria*) against *forgiveness* (χαρίζομαι, *charizomai*) because they are two opposite **orientations of the heart** that shape the whole moral life of the believer. Bitterness is not merely a fleeting feeling; it is a settled, self‑reinforcing disposition that breeds the other vices Paul lists (wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, malice). Forgiveness, by contrast, is an active, grace‑grounded habit — a chosen posture of the will that releases the offender and opens the believer to the same mercy God has shown in Christ. The two are placed side‑by‑side to show that the Christian life must **replace** a corrupt interior habit with a new, Spirit‑formed one.

Below is a step‑by‑step unpacking of why the text treats them as direct opposites, what kind of reality each term denotes, and how the passage’s logic works.

---

## 1. Literary‑contextual setting

| Verse | Greek (key terms) | Rough English sense |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 4:31  | ἀποθέσθω ἀπ’ ὑμῶν πᾶσαν πικρίαν καὶ θυμὸν καὶ ὀργὴν καὶ κραυγὴν καὶ βλασφημίαν, σὺν πάσῃ κακίᾳ | “Let all bitterness, wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, be put away from you, together with all malice” |
| 4:32  | γίνεσθε δὲ εἰς ἀλλήλους χρηστοὶ, ἔμπνευστοι, χαριζόμενοι ἑαυτοῖς καθὼς καὶ ὁ θεὸς ἐν Χριστῷ χαριζόμενοι ὑμᾖν | “And be kind to one another, tender‑hearted, forgiving one another, just as God in Christ has forgiven you.” |

- The verse forms a **negative‑positive pair**: a list of vices to be “put away” (ἀποθέσθω) followed by a trio of virtues to be “put on” (γίνεσθε).  
- Paul’s pattern elsewhere (e.g., Col 3:8‑14; Eph 4:22‑24) is to **contrast the old self** (the “fleshly” habits) with **the new self** (the Spirit‑formed habits).  
- The contrast is not merely emotional; it is **ethical and communal**: the vices listed all disrupt fellowship; the virtues restore it.

---

## 2. What “bitterness” (πικρία) denotes

| Aspect | Evidence from Greek & usage | What it implies for the believer |
|--------|----------------------------|----------------------------------|
| **Lexical sense** | *πικρία* = “sharpness, acridity, resentment”; figuratively “a bitter, hostile attitude.” (Liddell‑Scott‑Jones; BDAG) | It is an **internal disposition** that colors perception and speech. |
| **Moral psychology** | In the Septuagint, *πικρία* often describes the lingering resentment of Israel after oppression (e.g., Deut 29:18; Ps 73:21). In Greco‑Roman moral writers (e.g., Seneca, Epistle 95) it is a **vice of the soul** that leads to “sharp words” and “quarrels.” | It is a **habit of mind** that, if nurtured, produces the outward sins Paul lists (wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, malice). |
| **Contagiousness** | The term is used in wisdom literature to warn that “a bitter root spreads and defiles many” (Heb 12:15, echoing Deut 29:18). The image is of a **poisonous plant** whose roots infect the surrounding soil. | Bitterness is **socially transmissible**: one person’s resentment can poison a whole community, prompting retaliation, gossip, and division. |
| **Volitional element** | While the feeling may arise involuntarily, the command “put away” (ἀποθέσθω) treats it as something the believer can **choose to relinquish** (cf. James 1:21: “put away all filthiness and rampant wickedness”). | It is **not merely a passive emotion**; it is a stance that can be rejected or cultivated. |

**Take‑away:** Bitterness functions as a **root attitude** — a settled, self‑perpetuating posture of hostility that, once allowed to linger, generates the cluster of outward sins Paul enumerates.

---

## 3. What “forgiveness” (χαρίζομαι) denotes

| Aspect | Evidence from Greek & usage | What it implies for the believer |
|--------|----------------------------|----------------------------------|
| **Lexical sense** | *χαρίζομαι* = “to grant as a favor, to show grace, to pardon.” (BDAG) | It is an act of **gracious giving**, not merely the absence of resentment. |
| **Theological grounding** | Paul ties forgiveness explicitly to the divine model: “as God in Christ has forgiven you.” The root is **χάρις** (grace). | Forgiveness is **grace‑mediated**; it flows from the believer’s receipt of God’s mercy. |
| **Volitional & habitual** | The imperative γίνεσθε (“be”) + present middle participle χαριζόμενοι calls for a **continual, habitual practice** (cf. Col 3:13: “bearing with one another and, if one has a complaint against another, forgiving each other”). | It is a **chosen posture** that must be cultivated, just like kindness and tenderness. |
| **Social effect** | Forgiveness breaks the cycle of retaliation; it restores *koinonia* (fellowship) and mirrors the reconciling work of Christ (2 Cor 5:18‑20). | It is **anti‑contagious**: where bitterness spreads defilement, forgiveness spreads healing and peace. |

**Take‑away:** Forgiveness is not a fleeting feeling of “letting go”; it is an **active, grace‑rooted habit** of extending pardon that directly counters the bitter disposition.

---

## 4. Why the two are placed in direct opposition

1. **Structural parallelism**  
   - The verse pairs a *negative list* (vices to be discarded) with a *positive triad* (virtues to be embraced). The first item in the negative list (*πικρίαν*) is matched with the first virtue in the positive list (*χάριζομαι*). This chiastic ordering signals that the apostle sees them as **functional opposites**.

2. **Causal logic**  
   - Bitterness is the *source* of the other vices: “bitterness … wrath … anger … clamor … evil speaking … malice.” If the root is removed, the fruits wither.  
   - Conversely, forgiveness is the *source* of the positive virtues: kindness, tender‑heartedness, and a forgiving spirit flow from the same grace that pardons us.  

3. **Anthropological realism**  
   - Human psychology shows that a lingering resentment tends to **color perception**, making neutral actions appear hostile, which then provokes angry speech and retaliation.  
   - Forgiveness reorients perception: it allows the believer to see the offender through the lens of Christ’s mercy, prompting gentle speech and patient bearing.

4. **Ecclesial health**  
   - The early churches Paul addressed were prone to factions, gossip, and mutual suspicion (cf. 1 Cor 1:10‑13; Gal 5:15). Bitterness would have been a palpable threat to unity.  
   - By commanding the Corinthians/Ephesians to “put away” bitterness and to “forgive one another,” Paul offers a concrete antidote to the very maladies tearing the community apart.

5. **Theological symmetry**  
   - Just as **God’s forgiveness** is the basis for our standing before Him (Eph 1:7; Col 1:14), **our forgiveness** is the evidence that we have inwardly appropriated that grace.  
   - Bitterness, by contrast, signals a failure to internalize the gospel’s reconciliation; it is a sign that the old self still reigns.

---

## 5. Summative answer to the question

- **Bitterness** in Ephesians 4:31‑32 is best understood as a **settled, hostile disposition of the heart** — a *habit* or *posture* that, if left unchecked, produces the outward sins Paul lists. It is not merely a passing feeling; it is a *contagious root* that can corrupt the whole community.  
- **Forgiveness** is the **active, grace‑driven habit of extending pardon** that directly opposes that hostile posture. It is a chosen virtue, empowered by the same mercy we have received in Christ, and it cultivates kindness, tenderness, and peace.  
- The passage treats them as direct opposites because they represent the **two possible spiritual orientations** a believer can adopt: either cling to the old self’s bitter, self‑justifying stance (which fuels discord) or put on the new self’s forgiving, grace‑filled stance (which builds up the body of Christ).

Thus, the juxtaposition is not a poetic flourish; it is a theological and ethical instruction: **remove the poisonous root of bitterness, and plant the life‑giving seed of forgiveness,** thereby shaping both inner character and outer communal life.
