## Why It's Hard to Take Seriously

Enemy-love strikes us as difficult—even offensive—for at least three reasons:

**It seems to betray justice.** Our moral instincts tell us that love should track desert. To love someone who has wronged you (or worse, wronged the vulnerable) feels like a failure of moral seriousness—as if you're saying the wrong didn't matter. Loving enemies can look like complicity.

**It seems psychologically impossible.** Love, we assume, is a feeling, and feelings can't be commanded. Telling someone to love their persecutor sounds like telling them to enjoy a toothache.

**It seems naive about power.** Critics from Nietzsche onward have suspected that enemy-love is slave morality—the resentment of the weak dressed up as virtue, a way of losing while claiming the moral high ground.

These objections deserve real answers, and the passage itself contains the beginnings of them.

## Not Sentimentality

Notice what Jesus actually commands: *bless*, *do good*, *pray for*. These are actions, not emotions. The Greek *agapē* here is not affection or approval—it's a settled orientation of will toward another person's good. You can love an enemy in this sense while still opposing them, naming their wrong, even resisting them. The command doesn't ask you to *feel* warmly toward your persecutor; it asks you to refuse to let their hostility dictate your conduct.

This matters because sentimentality is precisely what enemy-love is *not*. Sentimentality pretends the enemy isn't really an enemy. Jesus assumes the enemy is real—the curse, the hatred, the persecution are taken as given. The command operates *inside* honest acknowledgment of harm, not as a denial of it.

## Not Merely Strategic

There's a popular instrumental defense: enemy-love "works." It de-escalates conflict, converts opponents, breaks cycles of retaliation. Gandhi and King demonstrated something like this politically. And it's true as far as it goes—King explicitly argued that returning hate for hate multiplies hate.

But the text refuses to ground the command in results. The reason given is theological and almost shockingly indifferent to outcomes: *God sends sun and rain on the evil and the good alike*. The justification isn't "this will change your enemy" but "this is what God is like, and you are to be God's child." The sun rises on the unjust whether or not they reform. Enemy-love, on this reading, is participation in a divine generosity that precedes and exceeds merit—what theologians call grace.

If it were merely strategic, it would collapse the moment it stopped working. The command is harder than that: love even when it accomplishes nothing visible.

## A Deeper Form of Strength

Here's the strongest case that enemy-love is moral strength rather than weakness:

**It's the only act that breaks reciprocity.** Hatred is reactive—it lets the enemy write the script. The person who returns hate for hate is, in a real sense, controlled by their enemy. Enemy-love is the refusal to be determined by another's malice. It is, paradoxically, the most sovereign moral act available to someone being wronged.

**It requires seeing what hatred cannot see.** To love an enemy you must hold two truths simultaneously: this person has done evil, *and* this person is not reducible to that evil. That's a more demanding act of perception than either excusing them or demonizing them. Hannah Arendt and others have noted that demonization is cognitively easy; moral realism about an enemy's humanity is hard.

**It costs more than retaliation.** Bonhoeffer—who loved his enemies while joining a conspiracy against Hitler, and was hanged for it—called this "costly grace." Nietzsche's suspicion fails here: enemy-love practiced by Bonhoeffer or by civil rights marchers facing dogs and firehoses was not the resentment of the weak. It required more courage than violence would have.

## The Remaining Tension

An honest answer admits the command remains genuinely difficult, not just misunderstood. Real questions persist: Can it be demanded of victims of abuse without becoming a tool of their abusers? Does it apply to communities and nations or only individuals? The tradition itself wrestles with these—which is why enemy-love coexists uneasily with just-war theory, and why "love your enemy" cannot mean "stay with your abuser" or "never resist injustice."

Perhaps the best summary: enemy-love is hard to take seriously because we keep mishearing it as a feeling (sentimentality) or a tactic (strategy), when it's actually a discipline—the deliberate refusal to let evil set the terms of your soul. The sun-and-rain image suggests it's less about the enemy at all than about what kind of person, and whose child, you are becoming.
