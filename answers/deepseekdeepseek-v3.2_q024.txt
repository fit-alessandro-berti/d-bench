Excellent question. The distinction Ezekiel makes between **external compliance** and **receiving a "new heart"** is central to understanding the biblical concept of spiritual transformation—and why hardness is portrayed as a "material condition" needing replacement.  

---

### **1. External Compliance vs. New Heart**

**External compliance** refers to obeying laws or statutes outwardly, often motivated by:  
- Social pressure or fear of punishment  
- Cultural habit or tradition  
- Intellectual assent without deep personal alignment  
- Willpower-driven discipline  

But such compliance lacks inward *love for God* and genuine *desire* to follow Him. It’s fragile, prone to hypocrisy, and easily collapses under temptation or when circumstances change. In ancient Israel’s history, despite having God’s laws, they repeatedly broke them because their hearts were unchanged.

A **“new heart”** (and “new spirit”) signifies:  
- A fundamental change in a person’s inner nature  
- New affections, desires, and motivations aligned with God’s will  
- The capacity for sincere love for God and others  
- Obedience flowing naturally from identity and relationship, not mere duty  

Ezekiel 36:27 clarifies: God puts **His Spirit** within the person to *cause* them to walk in His statutes. The change is divinely empowered from within, not self-produced from outside.

---

### **2. Why Hardness Is Described as a “Material Condition”**

Ezekiel contrasts a **“stony heart”** with a **“heart of flesh.”** This is metaphorical but conveys concrete spiritual reality:

- **Stone** → hard, unyielding, cold, impervious to influence, dead, incapable of growth or sensitivity.  
- **Flesh** → living, soft, responsive, capable of feeling, pliable, warm, alive.

**Why like a material condition needing replacement?**  
1. **Diagnosis of human incapacity:** The biblical view is that sin isn’t just wrong actions, but a deeply ingrained condition of the human “heart” (the core of personality, mind, will, emotion). This condition cannot be fixed by education or discipline alone—it must be replaced, like swapping out a defective part.  
2. **Divine action required:** If the heart is “stone,” human effort cannot soften it. Only God can surgically remove the old heart and implant a new one.  
3. **Tangible change:** In prophecy, this speaks to Israel’s (and humanity’s) failure despite having the Law. The Law exposed their heart’s hardness but couldn’t soften it. The promise of a new heart is thus a promise of a **new creation**, not just reform.

---

### **3. Theological Implication**

This passage anticipates the **New Covenant** (Jeremiah 31:31–34; echoed in Hebrews 8). Under the Old Covenant, the law was written on stone tablets externally; under the New Covenant, God writes His law on hearts internally by the Spirit.

Thus, the difference is:  
- **External compliance** = trying to conform an unchanged heart to external rules.  
- **New heart** = God changing the heart so that obedience springs from renewed inner life.

In Christian theology, this is fulfilled through Christ and the gift of the Holy Spirit—regeneration, not just moral instruction.
