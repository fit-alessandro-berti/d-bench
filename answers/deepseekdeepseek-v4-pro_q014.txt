Jesus’ words in Matthew 6:19–21 describe a dynamic relationship between treasure and the heart. The text does not merely present treasure as a passive revealer of where the heart already is; it actively presents treasure as something that **trains** the heart, determining where the heart will eventually settle.

### 1. Does treasure *reveal* or *train* the heart?

The key phrase is: *“For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also”* (v. 21). The future tense “will be” (Greek ἔσται) is decisive. Jesus does not say, “Where your heart is, there your treasure is” (which would mean treasure merely reveals a pre-existing affection). He reverses the order: the location of the treasure **precedes and determines** the location of the heart.

This reversal is crucial because verses 19–20 are imperatives: *“Lay not up… but lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven.”* Jesus is commanding a deliberate relocation of one’s treasure. The logic is: if you transfer your wealth, security, and deepest investments from earth to heaven, your heart will inevitably follow. In this sense, treasure **trains** the heart. It pulls affections in its wake, shaping love, hope, and identity around whatever is stored up.

That doesn’t mean treasure has no revealing quality. Obviously, what a person currently stockpiles exposes the heart’s present loyalties. But the emphasis in this passage falls on agency and formation: you can direct your love by redirecting your possessions. The act of investing in heaven is not just a symptom of a heavenly heart; it is a means God has given to create one.

### 2. How do possession, attention, and love become tied together?

Jesus binds these three tightly—not only here but in the immediate context (6:22–24). Possession (treasure), attention (the eye), and love (the heart/devotion) form an unbreakable triangle.

- **Possession shapes attention.** What we own and accumulate demands maintenance, worry, and mental space. Earthly treasures lock our attention onto earth: moth, rust, thieves (v. 19). Heavenly treasures, by contrast, fix our gaze on the eternal (Colossians 3:1–2).  
- **Attention directs love.** In verse 22, Jesus says, *“The light of the body is the eye.”* In biblical thought, the “eye” often represents intention, focus, and desire. A “healthy” (literally “single”) eye generously fixed on God fills the whole self with light. An “evil” eye fixed on money (cf. 6:23) plunges a person into darkness. Where attention lingers, love grows—or withers.  
- **Love ultimately determines service.** Verse 24 makes it explicit: *“No man can serve two masters… Ye cannot serve God and mammon.”* The word “serve” implies a whole-person allegiance. Possessions, if treated as a treasure, claim our attention until they own our love, becoming a rival god.

Thus, the thread runs like this: **You invest your goods (possession) → your eyes and mind fix there (attention) → your deepest loyalty and affection follow (love).** Earth-bound treasure trains the heart to love earth, and heaven-bound treasure trains the heart to love God. Jesus treats them as a unified whole—because you cannot separate what you value from what you contemplate, nor what you contemplate from whom you worship.

### In Summary
Matthew 6:19–21 teaches that treasure is less a thermometer that takes the heart’s temperature and more a thermostat that sets the heart’s direction. Possession, attention, and love are not compartmentalized; they flow into one another. That is why Christ commands his followers to “lay up treasures in heaven”—not because heaven needs their money, but because their hearts need heaven.
