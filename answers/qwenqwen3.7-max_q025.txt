# The Moral Danger of Public Goodness in Matthew 6:1

## The Core Problem: Intention, Not Visibility

The Greek text resolves the question decisively. The phrase translated "to be seen of them" is **πρὸς τὸ θεαθῆναι** (*pros to theathēnai*) — a purpose clause. The construction doesn't describe a side effect; it describes **the aim**. The danger is not that others *happen* to see, but that one does good **in order to** be seen.

This distinction is confirmed just chapters earlier, in Jesus' own teaching:

> *"Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."* (Matthew 5:16)

Here, visibility is not merely permitted — it is **commanded**. The difference lies entirely in the intended audience and the intended glory. In 5:16, onlookers are meant to glorify God. In 6:1, the doer absorbs the glory themselves.

## What Makes It Morally Dangerous

The verse identifies a specific corruption: **the displacement of motive**. Several things happen when good deeds are performed for human applause:

1. **Virtue becomes transaction.** Almsgiving is no longer an act of compassion toward the poor; it is a purchase of social status using the poor as props.

2. **The audience shifts.** The Father is replaced by the crowd. The Greek word used for "hypocrites" in the surrounding verses (6:2, 5, 16) is *ὑποκριτής* — literally, a **stage actor**. The moral life becomes performance rather than authenticity.

3. **The reward is exhausted.** Jesus says such people "have their reward" (ἀπέχουσιν τὸν μισθὸν αὐτῶν) — the word *ἀπέχουσιν* is a commercial term meaning "paid in full." Human praise is the entirety of what they will receive. There is nothing left from God.

## A Deeper Layer: Righteousness Itself

Worth noting: the earliest manuscripts read **τὴν δικαιοσύνην** (*tēn dikaiosynēn* — "your righteousness") rather than "your alms." The principle encompasses **all moral action**, not merely charity. The warning is universal: any act of goodness — prayer, fasting, justice, mercy — can be hollowed out the moment it becomes a performance.

## Conclusion

Visibility is morally neutral. The craving to be seen is what poisons the act. The verse is not a call to secrecy for its own sake, but a call to **purity of motive** — to do good because it is good, for an Audience of One, rather than for the applause of the crowd.
