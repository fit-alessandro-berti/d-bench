In Mark 8:34–35, Jesus is referring to a self that encompasses not just physical desires or material possessions but also one’s ego, personal agenda, and the pursuit of worldly success and recognition. This "self" includes our identity shaped by external validation, societal expectations, and selfish interests—essentially, living for oneself rather than serving God and others.

**Why it sounds liberating to some:**

1. **Freedom from Conformity:** Denying this self allows individuals to break free from societal norms that often dictate how one should act, look, or live according to the standards set by peers, media, or culture. This can feel liberating because it empowers people to pursue their authentic paths aligned with spiritual values rather than conforming to external pressures.

2. **Spiritual Growth and Purpose:** Many find comfort in acknowledging that true fulfillment comes from living a life guided by faith and service to others. This denial points toward spiritual growth, emphasizing inner peace and contentment through devotion instead of material gratification.

3. **Renewed Identity:** Letting go of the self-centered identity helps individuals see themselves as part of something greater—a body of Christ—focusing on serving God’s purpose rather than their own ambitions. This shift can feel liberating because it instills a sense of belonging and mission beyond personal gain.

**Why it might be perceived as offensive to others:**

1. **Reframing Failure:** Denying this self requires individuals to recognize areas where they might have been living for themselves, such as greed, pride, or selfish ambition—qualities that many hold dear or believe are necessary for success and advancement in life. Seeing these traits as a failure can feel harsh if one interprets it as a call to suppress natural human tendencies.

2. **Fear of Self-Denial:** Some may view self-denial as a form of purgation rather than growth, fearing that admitting they have been living primarily for their ego or external validation is an admission of personal inadequacy or failure. This can be unsettling because it forces a re-evaluation of life choices and societal roles.

3. **Cultural Sensitivities:** In societies where individualism is highly valued, the concept of denying oneself could be seen as contradicting core values such as independence, achievement, and self-expression. It challenges deeply ingrained notions about personal responsibility and the pursuit of happiness through one’s own efforts and achievements.

Ultimately, Jesus’ call to deny this self invites a deeper spiritual awareness and commitment, which can resonate positively with those seeking meaning and purpose beyond material or social gains. For others, it might be perceived as unrealistic or even sacrilegious if misinterpreted as dismissing essential aspects of personal worth or societal functioning.
