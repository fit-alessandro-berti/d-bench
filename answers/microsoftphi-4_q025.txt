In Matthew 6:1, the warning about doing good deeds publicly primarily targets the intention behind the action rather than visibility itself. The moral danger lies in the motivation of desiring recognition or praise for one's good deeds. If the primary intention behind performing an act of kindness or charity is to gain social approval or enhance one's reputation, then the act loses its purity and sincerity, according to the teaching of Jesus.

The verse encourages altruism and genuine compassion without seeking reward or acknowledgment from others. The focus should be on pleasing God, who sees the heart and intentions behind actions, rather than men. When actions are performed for the sake of recognition, they become a form of hypocrisy, where the individual receives the admiration of people, substituting divine approval.

So, it's not the mere visibility that is problematic, but the craving or strong desire to be seen, admired, or praised by others for those actions. This craving shifts the emphasis from the actual good being done to the benefits one might receive in terms of social standing or personal pride. Thus, the verse calls for inward generosity—one's heart should be aligned with sincere intentions rather than external validation.
