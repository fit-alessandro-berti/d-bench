To name the elder brother’s “deepest” problem, we have to look beneath the emotional symptoms (anger, envy, the sense of unfairness) to the spiritual logic that makes those feelings inevitable.  The text gives us four decisive clues.

1.  **The grammar of “these many years” (v. 29).**  
    He rehearses his length of service as if time itself were a deposit he could withdraw.  The phrase is not a mere observation; it is an *accounting*.  When time is kept as a ledger, the self inevitably becomes the auditor and God the debtor.  The father’s final reply (“Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine”) exposes the unreality of that ledger: if everything already belongs to the elder son, then keeping a list of separate “extra” favors is a denial of belonging.

2.  **The word he will not say.**  
    He never calls the returning figure “brother.”  He points at him with a pronoun: “this thy son” (ὁ υἱός σου οὗτος).  The distance in the phrase is theological, not geographical.  To refuse to name someone as kin is to prefer a closed moral economy (“my deserts, my circle, my order”) to the open household the father keeps.

3.  **What he thinks the party celebrates.**  
    The elder brother interprets the feast as a verdict: his brother’s sin has been upgraded to celebrity.  He fears that mercy cancels accountability (“devoured thy living with harlots… thou hast killed for him”).  In that fear he reveals his deepest premise: fellowship with God is a *reward for non-transgression*, and therefore any interruption of that order—even for the sake of a lost person found—feels like an assault on justice.  Mercy, in this view, is not a virtue but an administrative hazard.

4.  **Where he stands at the end of the parable.**  
    The story stops with the father pleading, not with the elder son entering.  The omission is deliberate.  The problem is not that he is momentarily angry or envious; it is that he remains *outside* while claiming to be *inside*.  He mistakes proximity for intimacy and compliance for communion.

**Therefore, the deepest problem is not anger, envy, or even wounded fairness in isolation; it is a settled comfort with a closed system of merit that makes mercy itself intolerable.**  
The elder brother’s pride in obedience is real, but it is the *instrument* of a more fundamental refusal: the refusal to rejoice that what was dead is alive and what was lost is found.  He prefers a tidy righteousness to the dangerous, spacious joy of the father’s house.
