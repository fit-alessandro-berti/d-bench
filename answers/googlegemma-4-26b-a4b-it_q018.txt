The placement of these two verses creates a deliberate moral antithesis. Paul is not merely providing a list of "dos and don'ts"; he is presenting a **structural replacement therapy** for the human soul.

To understand why they are placed against each other and what nature bitterness takes, we must look at the Greek nuances and the theological logic of the passage.

### 1. Why are they placed against each other?
The juxtaposition is intentional because, in the economy of the New Testament, **forgiveness is not the absence of conflict, but the presence of a specific remedy.**

*   **The Zero-Sum Game of the Heart:** Paul suggests that the human heart has a finite capacity for "spirit." You cannot harbor the "malice" described in verse 31 and simultaneously inhabit the "tenderheartedness" of verse 32. They are mutually exclusive states of being.
*   **The "Put Away" vs. "Be" Dynamic:** Verse 31 uses the imperative *apothesthe* ("put away" or "strip off"), like taking off filthy, soiled clothing. Verse 32 uses the imperative *ginesthe* ("become" or "be"), like putting on a new garment. The text places them together to show that spiritual maturity is not just the *removal* of the bad, but the *replacement* of it with the good. If you only "put away" bitterness without "becoming" kind, you leave a vacuum that bitterness will eventually re-occupy.

### 2. Is bitterness a feeling, a habit, or a posture?
The text suggests that bitterness is actually **all three**, functioning as a progressive downward spiral.

**As a Feeling (The Seed):**
The list in verse 31 moves from internal to external. *Bitterness* (*pikria*) is the internal "sourness" of the soul. It is the initial emotional sting of being wronged. It is the feeling that "this is unfair."

**As a Habit (The Growth):**
As that feeling is nursed, it evolves into *wrath* (sudden outbursts) and *anger* (settled resentment). This becomes a habit of the mind. The "clamour" (shouting/noise) and "evil speaking" (slander) mentioned are the habitual *outward manifestations* of an internal state. Once these become the default way you react to stress or offense, they have become habits.

**As a Chosen Posture (The Root):**
The most damning term in the list is *kakia* (translated as "malice"). In Greek, this refers to a deep-seated disposition or a "wickedness of character." This is where bitterness ceases to be an involuntary feeling and becomes a **chosen posture.** It is the decision to hold onto the debt the other person owes you.

### 3. Is it contagious?
While the text doesn't use the word "contagious," it describes a **social contagion.**

Note the progression: It starts with a private emotion (*bitterness*), moves to an internal state (*malice*), and ends with social destruction (*clamour* and *evil speaking*). Bitterness is "contagious" in the sense that it destroys the *koinonia* (fellowship/community) that Paul is trying to build in the rest of the letter.

If one person in a community practices "evil speaking," it invites others to defend themselves with the same weapon, spreading the infection through the entire body.

### 4. The Theological "Why": The Vertical Standard
The reason Paul can demand this radical shift is found in the final clause: **"...even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you."**

He is arguing that the "cure" for the contagion of bitterness is not "willpower," but a realization of **vertical reality.** 

*   **Bitterness** says: "The debt is too large to be forgiven; justice must be served."
*   **Forgiveness** says: "The debt was too large for me to pay, yet it was canceled by Christ."

By placing them together, Paul is saying that the only way to stop the cycle of bitterness is to anchor one's identity in the forgiveness they have already received. You don't forgive because the other person deserves it; you forgive because you have become a person who has been forgiven.
