### **Textual Interpretation of Romans 12:20–21**

The passage says:

> “Therefore if thine enemy hunger, feed him; if he thirst, give him drink: for in so doing thou shalt heap coals of fire on his head. Be not overcome of evil, but overcome evil with good.”

#### **Defining Victory Over Evil**

1. **Victory Through Compassion (Not Aggression):**  
   The first part uses the imagery of feeding someone in hunger and giving drink to thirst. At first glance, this might seem passive or even self-sacrificing. However, the context is that the “enemy” is not an inanimate thing, but a human (or even spiritual) adversary. The command is not to defeat the enemy through violence or manipulation, but by meeting their basic needs—even those who oppose you.

2. **Victory Through Transformative Action:**  
   The second part—“heap coals of fire on his head”—is a metaphor for consequences that burn away evil. By extending kindness and meeting human needs, you are actually undermining their power over you (through anger, resentment, or cruelty). You are turning their attempts to harm you into opportunities to reveal their own weakness or to draw them into the cycle of retaliation they cannot win.

3. **Moral Strength, Not Physical or Manipulative Strength:**  
   The verse defines victory not as domination or retaliation, but as responding to evil with good. This is the classic “turn the other cheek” ethic: responding to aggression with non-aggression, and to hatred with love. This is strong because it requires self-control and moral courage.

#### **Is the Command Weak, Manipulative, or Strong?**

**1. Not Weak:**  
The command is not weak. Weakness would be surrendering to the enemy or responding in kind with more evil. Instead, this is an active, principled response that refuses to be corrupted by the adversary’s methods.

**2. Not Manipulative (as in Deceit):**  
While some critics might question whether “feeding your enemy” could be manipulative, the context of the Bible is one of mutual transformation. It’s not about trickery or entrapment, but about meeting human need—even for those who seek to harm you. Paul is teaching that the most effective way to defeat the power of evil (which often relies on division, hunger, and thirst for control) is to meet its deepest needs. This can actually expose its lack of true power over people’s hearts.

**3. Genuinely Strong:**  
This is strong because it is rooted in love and justice, not in hatred or revenge. It is strong in its commitment to non-retaliation and moral high ground. It acknowledges the reality of evil but refuses to be corrupted by it. The “coals of fire” are not literal but symbolic of the transformative consequences of responding to evil with goodness—eventually, the adversary’s power over you is destroyed by your integrity.

### **Conclusion**

Romans 12:20–21 defines victory over evil as **responding to evil with good, meeting the enemy’s needs with kindness, and allowing love and justice to expose and defeat the claims of hatred or power**. This is not a weak or manipulative tactic; it is a profoundly strong and ethical response that upholds the character of God in the world. It requires humility, courage, and a deep trust that the principles of love and justice are ultimately more powerful than the forces of evil.
