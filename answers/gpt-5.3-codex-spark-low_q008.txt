• In this verse John is making a practical test, not a contradiction:

  - God is unseen, but people are seen (especially neighbors who are specific, messy, vulnerable).
  - If someone truly loves God, that love will show up in behavior toward people; otherwise the claim of loving God is
    just verbal (“I say I love God”).

  So the “visible neighbor” functions as a proof point: love of God is not abstract feeling but observable, concrete
  action. John is exposing a form of religious self-deception where someone:

  1. Uses pious language (“I love God”) to define identity,
  2. But avoids costly love toward people he can actually see and touch,
  3. Yet still treats this as equivalent to loving God, which would make him a liar.

  It’s not only “not being nice.” It is a deeper split-self pattern: claiming loyalty to God while letting
  relationships, mercy, and justice toward others be controlled by resentment, pride, tribalism, or indifference. In
  short, the verse says genuine love of God is falsifiable by how you treat another human being.
