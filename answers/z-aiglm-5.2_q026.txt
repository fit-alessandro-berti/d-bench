This passage from Matthew establishes a direct, causal relationship between the invisible moral condition of a person’s heart and the visible nature of their speech. Jesus uses the metaphor of a treasury or storehouse to explain this connection.

Here is a breakdown of how the passage connects speech with "hidden moral stockpiles," and how it frames words as symptoms, choices, and revelations.

### 1. The Connection: Speech and Hidden Moral Stockpiles
Jesus uses the concept of "treasure" (from the Greek *thesauros*, meaning a storehouse or accumulated wealth) to describe the human heart. He connects speech to these stockpiles in two primary ways:

*   **The Principle of Overflow:** Jesus says, "out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh." "Abundance" here means the overflow of what has been stored up. Speech is not manufactured in a vacuum; it is the spillage of what a person has been accumulating in their heart through their thoughts, desires, and affections. 
*   **The Equivalence of Nature:** The passage establishes that the output always matches the input. A good stockpile yields good things; an evil stockpile yields evil things. Jesus’ rhetorical question—"how can ye, being evil, speak good things?"—implies that moral inconsistency is inevitable. Just as a water tank filled with poison cannot dispense fresh water, a heart stockpiling bitterness, pride, or malice cannot ultimately produce good speech.

### 2. Are Words Symptoms, Choices, Revelations, or All Three?
The passage suggests that words are **all three**, operating in a unified continuum. 

**Words as Symptoms**
In this text, words are primarily diagnostic. Jesus addresses the Pharisees as a "generation of vipers," diagnosing their internal spiritual rot based on their external speech. Just as a cough is a symptom of a deeper respiratory infection, evil words are the symptom of a diseased heart. The words themselves are not the root problem; they are the natural, organic outworking of a deeper moral condition. You cannot cure a viper's heart simply by telling it to speak kindly; the speech must change by changing the underlying stockpile.

**Words as Choices**
While speech is a symptom of an underlying condition, Jesus also uses the active phrase "bringeth forth." This implies a volitional act. 
*   We actively choose what we store in our hearts (what media we consume, what grudges we nurture, what virtues we practice).
*   We actively choose to "bring forth" those treasures into the world through our mouths. 
Even if a person's heart is evil, the act of speaking evil is a choice to unseal the treasury and distribute its contents. Thus, while we cannot easily change our nature by sheer willpower, we are held accountable for the choices we make in storing up and unleashing our moral inventory.

**Words as Revelations**
The heart is hidden and deceitful; no one can see another person's moral stockpile directly. However, speech acts as the spigot of the soul. Words are revelatory because they expose what is truly hidden in the dark. A person may perform outward religious duties or fake a good demeanor for a time, but under pressure, in a moment of anger, or in unguarded conversation, the mouth reveals the true state of the heart. Words tear the veil off the invisible soul.

### Synthesis
In Matthew 12:34-35, words are the **revelation** of a hidden condition, acting as the **symptom** of the moral health of the heart's stockpile, manifested through the conscious **choice** to "bring forth" what has been stored away. Jesus is teaching that to fix our speech, we cannot merely apply behavioral filters to our mouths; we must change the inventory of our hearts.
