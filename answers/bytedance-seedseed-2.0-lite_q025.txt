To answer this question, we first center the exact language and context of this verse (and Matthew’s broader teaching) to clarify that the moral danger lies in the craving to be seen, not visibility of good acts itself.
1.  The verse’s grammar explicitly pins the warning to motivation, not visibility: the warning is framed as “do not your alms before men, **to be seen of them**” — the phrase “to be seen of them” is a purpose clause that qualifies when public goodness becomes problematic. It is not that others might witness your charitable work, but that you perform that work *primarily to gain human admiration, status, or praise* that corrupts the act.
2.  This craving for recognition twists the entire point of the good deed. Alms in this context is a spiritual act of loving your neighbor and honoring God; turning it into a performance to feed your own ego exchanges the lasting, spiritual reward of aligning with God’s will for the fleeting, empty praise of other people. You forfeit any reward from God because you already claimed the only reward you sought: human validation.
3.  A parallel teaching in Matthew’s own gospel confirms visibility is not the problem. In Matthew 5:16, Jesus commands his followers: “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven.” Here, public visibility of good acts is actively endorsed, as long as the end goal is to glorify God, not to lift up the person performing the good. This contrast makes explicit that visibility of public goodness is neutral; the moral danger is the craving to center oneself as the recipient of praise for that goodness.
