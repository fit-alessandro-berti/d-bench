The elder brother's deepest problem in Luke 15:28–32 can be interpreted as a combination of anger, envy, and wounded fairness, stemming from a sense of entitlement and a rigid understanding of his own place in their father's affeasse. His sense of entitlement and expectation for his needs to be given priority have been violated when the father throws a celebratory feast for his younger brother' extramarital indulgence's return, and he is not given a gift. His fairness is wounded because he feels he has served faithfully and obeyed his father's commands without receiving recognition or reward, while mercy—specifically, the father's willingness to forgive and celebrate the younger brother's return—is discomforting to him.

In his response, he is not merely focused on the material issue of not receiving a gift, but also on the perceived injustice of his labor being overshadowed by the celebration of his brother's misdeeds. His anger seems directed not just at the unfairness, but also at his father's capacity to overlook his suffering and still prioritize reconcizing and celebrating his brother's actions that have caused him pain. The elder brother's emotions are complex, as he grapples with his loyalty and love for his brother, his fairness and expectation of rewards for his service, and his anger or indignation towards his father's seemingly inconsistent values. It's a poignant illustration of a person's struggle with guilt and unmet mercy from within a family, which often involves confronting deeply ingrained beliefs about what is deserved by the self when contrasted with others' actions or the generous, forgiving acts of those around them.
