Based on the grammatical structure and the theological implications of Matthew 6:19–21, the answer is that the text posits a **relational link** where treasure acts as both a **diagnostic tool** (revealing the heart) and a **directional guide** (training the heart).

Here is an analysis of how these dynamics function.

### 1. Does it reveal or train?
The verse concludes with a causal statement: *"For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also."*

**The Heart Reveals the Treasure (Diagnostic):**
In a strict grammatical sense, the verse functions as a diagnostic principle. Jesus is saying that if you want to know the true orientation of a person’s soul, you do not look at their stated intentions or their religious vocabulary; you look at their "treasure" (their resources, time, and security). The treasure acts as a mirror. If a person claims to love God but invests all their energy into earthly accumulation, the "treasure" has revealed a heart that is actually tethered to the temporal.

**The Treasure Trains the Heart (Directional):**
However, there is a psychological and spiritual movement implied here: the heart *follows* the treasure. The heart is not a static object; it is a reactive one. When we invest our resources into something—be it a cause, a person, or a pursuit—our attention, emotions, and affections are naturally drawn to follow that investment. By choosing to "lay up treasures in heaven" (acts of service, mercy, and faith), a person is effectively "training" their heart to value things that are eternal. In this sense, the movement of the treasure *pulls* the heart along with it.

### 2. The Triad: Possession, Attention, and Love
The text suggests a profound interconnectedness between what we own, what we notice, and what we value.

**Possession (The Anchor):**
Possession is the physical or temporal manifestation of our commitment. In the context of the passage, "treasure" refers to anything we deem necessary for our security, identity, or pleasure. Possession is the "where"—the location of our investment.

**Attention (The Mechanism):**
Attention is the bridge between possession and love. We cannot possess something without paying attention to it. To protect, grow, or enjoy a treasure, one must constantly monitor it. This constant mental and emotional "looking" is what shifts the heart's focus. If your treasure is earthly, your attention is perpetually consumed by anxiety (the fear of moth, rust, or thieves). If your treasure is heavenly, your attention is oriented toward the eternal.

**Love (The Destination):**
The "heart" in biblical idiom is the seat of the will and the center of affection (love). Love is the ultimate destination of the cycle. 

**The Cycle Works Like This:**
1. **Investment (Possession):** You put your resources into a specific sphere.
2. **Focus (Attention):** Because you have invested, your mind and emotions are constantly directed toward that sphere.
3. **Affection (Love):** Because your attention is constantly directed there, your heart develops an emotional and spiritual attachment to it.

### Summary
The text argues that **the heart is a follower, not a leader.** We often think we can decide to love God and then later decide how to spend our money. Jesus reverses this: He suggests that if we want to love God, we must first move our "treasure" toward Him. By shifting our possessions, we command our attention; by commanding our attention, we inevitably capture our heart.
