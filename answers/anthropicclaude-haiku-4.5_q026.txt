# Speech as Symptom, Choice, and Revelation

This passage operates on multiple levels simultaneously:

## As Symptom
The heart functions like a reservoir—words are what naturally overflow. Jesus isn't primarily condemning *choice* here but *inevitability*. The "generation of vipers" cannot speak good things "because" they are evil, suggesting an organic incapacity rather than mere selection. This frames speech as diagnostic: it reveals what's already present.

## As Choice
Yet the passage retains moral weight, which requires agency. If words were *only* symptoms (like a fever indicates illness), there would be no basis for Jesus's rebuke. The Pharisees are addressed as accountable agents, implying they *could* have cultivated different internal treasure. There's a strange doubleness: you cannot choose what comes out without first choosing what goes in.

## As Revelation
Perhaps most importantly, speech is *what makes the interior visible*. The passage doesn't say "your heart is evil"; it says your words *demonstrate* it. This gives speech an epistemic function—it's how the hidden becomes known, to others and perhaps to ourselves.

## The Tension Worth Holding
The passage suggests words aren't casual decorations applied to a fixed self, nor are they entirely free performances. They're the *inevitable expression of cultivated character*—which means change requires deeper work than mere restraint.
