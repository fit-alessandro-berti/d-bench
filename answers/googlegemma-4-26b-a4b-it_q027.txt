In analyzing this parable, it is important to note that the "condemnation" is not caused by a single factor, but rather a toxic synergy of several. However, if one must identify the root, it is **pride fueled by comparison.**

Here is a breakdown of the specific elements that condemn the Pharisee and why the tax collector’s posture is deemed spiritually superior.

### 1. What condemns the Pharisee?

While all the options you listed are present, they function in a hierarchy of causality:

*   **The Root: Pride (Self-Righteousness):** The text states the parable is for those who "trusted in themselves that they were righteous." The Pharisee’s fundamental error is placing his confidence in his own performance rather than in God’s grace. He is not praying *to* God so much as he is praying *at* God to validate his own resume.
*   **The Mechanism: Comparison and Contempt:** Pride in this context cannot exist in a vacuum; it requires a foil. The Pharisee uses the tax collector as a "prop" to heighten his own perceived sanctity. By saying, *"I am not as other men are... or even as this publican,"* he reveals that his righteousness is relative, not absolute. He is not measuring himself against the holiness of God, but against the failures of his neighbor. This leads directly to **contempt**, which is the outward expression of a heart that believes it is inherently superior to others.
*   **The Symptom: The Use of Virtue as Self-Display:** His fasting and tithing are objectively good acts. However, they become sinful when they are weaponized to build a wall between himself and his need for God. He uses his virtues as a shield against repentance.

**The verdict:** He is not condemned for his fasting or his tithing (which are commanded in the Law), but for **the theology of his heart.** He has replaced *dependence on God* with *reliance on self.*

---

### 2. Why is the tax collector’s posture spiritually truer?

The tax collector (publican) is treated as "justified" (rendered righteous in the sight of God) because his posture aligns with the fundamental requirement of a relationship with a holy God: **the recognition of spiritual bankruptcy.**

*   **The Recognition of Distance:** He stands "afar off." This is not just physical distance, but a spiritual realization of the chasm between a sinful human and a holy Creator. He does not presume to approach God on his own merits.
*   **The Rejection of Self-Defense:** Unlike the Pharisee, who offers a list of justifications, the tax collector offers no excuses. He does not say, "I am a sinner, *but* I am honest in my business." He offers a raw, unadorned confession: "God be merciful to me a sinner."
*   **The Cry for Mercy (Grace):** The Greek word used for "mercy" in this context (*hilaskomai*) implies a plea for propitiation—for the covering of sin. The tax collector understands that righteousness is not something he *achieves*, but something he must *receive*.

### Summary Comparison

| Feature | The Pharisee | The Tax Collector |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Focus of Prayer** | Self (His own achievements) | God (His own need) |
| **Standard of Judgment** | Comparison with others | Comparison with God's holiness |
| **Basis of Confidence** | Personal merit (Works) | Divine mercy (Grace) |
| **Spiritual Result** | Self-exaltation (Condemnation) | Justification (Salvation) |

The tax collector is "truer" because he acknowledges the reality of his condition. The Pharisee is "false" because he attempts to present a reality that does not exist: a state of self-sufficiency before a God who requires humility.
