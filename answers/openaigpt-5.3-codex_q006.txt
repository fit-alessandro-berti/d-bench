Great question. In **Mark 8:34–35**, the “self” being denied is not your God-given personhood, dignity, or value. It is the **self as sovereign**—the “I am my own lord” self.

### What “deny himself” means
Jesus is calling for:

- **Renouncing self-rule** (my will above God’s will)
- **Letting go of ego-centered identity** (status, image, control)
- **Surrendering self-preservation as highest good** (comfort/safety at any cost)

So this is not “hate yourself” or “erase your personality.”  
It is: *stop making yourself the center, and follow Christ as center.*

### Context matters
Right before this, Jesus predicts suffering, and Peter resists it. Then Jesus tells everyone: if you follow me, you must be ready for the same path—costly obedience (“take up your cross”).

In v.35, “life” (Greek **psyche**) can mean life/self/soul.  
So paradoxically: cling to life on your own terms, and you lose it; give your life to Christ, and you find true life.

---

## Why this feels liberating to some
For many, this is freedom from:

- the exhausting project of self-invention
- anxiety about image and success
- slavery to approval, ambition, and fear
- the burden of being your own savior

They hear: *You don’t have to carry the weight of being ultimate. You can belong to God.*

## Why this feels offensive to others
It confronts core modern instincts:

- personal autonomy (“my life, my truth, my choice”)
- self-expression as highest value
- suspicion of authority or surrender
- fear that “self-denial” means repression or abuse

So it can sound like a threat to freedom—unless one sees Jesus’ claim that surrender to him leads to **deeper** freedom, not less.

---

In short:  
Jesus is not asking you to deny your humanity, but your **self-centered lordship**.  
That is why it sounds like either bondage or liberation, depending on whether Christ is seen as rival to life—or source of it.
