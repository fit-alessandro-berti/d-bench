# The Sharpness of the Image

The passage works through **deliberate absurdity of scale**. A "mote" (Greek *karphos*) is a tiny speck—a splinter, a bit of chaff or dust. A "beam" (*dokos*) is a structural timber, the kind used to hold up a roof. The image asks us to picture someone with an entire log jutting out of their eye, squinting at a neighbor to help extract a piece of sawdust.

This is comedy as much as rebuke. It's absurd, almost slapstick—and that's the point. Hypocrisy often *is* absurd when seen clearly; it just doesn't feel that way from inside the hypocrite's perspective, where the speck in someone else's eye somehow looms larger than the timber in one's own.

The physical impossibility also does rhetorical work: **how could you possibly see well enough to perform delicate eye surgery on someone else while your own vision is blocked by a plank?** The metaphor isn't just about moral inconsistency—it's about *epistemic* inconsistency. Impaired judgment (obstructed by your own beam) is being trusted to make fine discriminations about someone else's minor faults.

## Correction Without Prohibition

Crucially, the passage does **not** say "never address your brother's fault." It says: *first* remove the beam, *then* you'll see clearly enough *to* remove the mote. The final clause—"then shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote out of thy brother's eye"—assumes the mote-removal will still happen. It's not forbidden; it's **sequenced**.

This distinguishes the passage from a simple "judge not" or "mind your own business" ethic. The problem isn't correction itself—it's:

1. **Disproportion** — treating your own major fault as invisible while magnifying someone else's minor one.
2. **Disqualification through self-blindness** — you literally cannot see clearly enough to help while impaired by your own obstruction.
3. **Hypocrisy** — the word Jesus uses directly ("thou hypocrite")—claiming moral authority you haven't earned through self-scrutiny.

The text assumes a community where people *do* correct one another—that's simply taken as normal spiritual practice. What it demands is **priority and self-examination first**, so that the correction that follows is accurate, humble, and actually helpful rather than a projection of one's own unaddressed sin. The end goal is stated plainly: clarity of vision *in service of* helping the brother, not avoidance of the relationship altogether.
