Matthew 12:34–35 uses the metaphor of a **moral vault** to describe the heart, and speech as its **overflow**. Jesus’s critique of his interlocutors hinges on an organic, causal relationship: what is stored within inevitably dictates what spills out. Let’s break down how the passage frames speech in relation to heart-condition, and whether words function as symptoms, choices, revelations, or all three.

### 🔹 How Speech Connects to Hidden Moral Stockpiles
The passage uses three key image clusters:
- `"treasure"` → implies accumulation, curation, and long-term storage of moral/spiritual resources.
- `"abundance"` → suggests saturation, fullness, and spontaneous overflow rather than forced or calculated output.
- `"bringeth forth"` → agricultural language indicating natural production, not mechanical repetition.

Together, these frames depict the heart not as a blank slate, but as a cultivated reservoir shaped by repeated affections, beliefs, and decisions. Speech is the visible discharge of that reservoir. You cannot draw good water from a poisoned well.

### 🔹 Are Words Symptoms, Choices, Revelations, or All Three?
The passage supports **all three**, but with important distinctions in emphasis and time-frame:

| Dimension | How the Passage Supports It | Nuance in Context |
|----------|----------------------------|-------------------|
| **Symptoms** | Strongest immediate emphasis. Jesus asks, *“how can ye, being evil, speak good things?”* Words function as diagnostic indicators. Like a fever signals infection, speech signals the underlying moral state. Good speech is impossible here, just as a healthy tree cannot bear rotten fruit. |
| **Revelations** | Explicitly implied. The heart’s “treasure” is hidden; speech makes it visible. The overflow *“brings forth”* what was stored, turning interior disposition into exterior reality. Words don’t just hint at the heart; they disclose it. |
| **Choices** | Indirect but foundational. The “treasure” didn’t assemble itself. Over time, repeated affections, interpretations, and moral decisions have filled the heart. While the moment of speaking feels like automatic overflow, it is the culmination of prior choices that shaped one’s moral orientation. Jesus isn’t denying human agency; he’s locating it upstream in character formation. |

### 🔹 Synthesis: All Three, but with Causal Priority
If ranked by the passage’s own logic:
1. **Revelation & Symptom** are the *immediate* functions of speech. Words reveal what is already there and symptomatically indicate the heart’s condition.
2. **Choice** operates *historically*. You don’t choose each word in isolation; you inherit the output because you’ve long ago chosen (consciously or habitually) what to treasure, what to believe, and what to love.

This aligns with Jesus’s broader teaching in Matthew 15:18–19 (*“out of the heart proceed evil thoughts, murders, adulteries...”*) and the wisdom tradition’s view of character as the wellspring of conduct (Prov. 4:23; 4:31). Modern psychology and virtue ethics echo this: repeated choices form dispositions, which then generate automatic outputs. Speech isn’t a neutral tool; it’s the exhaust pipe of the soul’s engine.

### 🔹 Conclusion
Matthew 12:34–35 does not reduce speech to merely one category. It presents words as **symptoms** of a hidden condition, **revelations** of what is stored within, and the **cumulative endpoint of prior moral choices**. The passage’s power lies in its refusal to treat speech as isolated utterances. Instead, it treats words as the visible crest of a moral wave, shaped by what one has long ago chosen to feed, what one has cultivated in secret, and what one finally cannot help but declare.
