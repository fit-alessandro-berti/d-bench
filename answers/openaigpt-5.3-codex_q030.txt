Great question. Paul’s list sounds “negative” (not proud, not envious, not irritable, not suspicious) because he is **dismantling a false idea of strength** that the Corinthians admired.

In Corinth, “strength” often looked like:
- status,
- spiritual showmanship,
- winning arguments,
- protecting your honor,
- being superior to others.

So Paul describes love by saying what it **refuses** to do. Why? Because those habits (envy, boasting, arrogance, touchiness, scorekeeping) are exactly the behaviors that destroy community while pretending to be power.

### What kind of strength is he proposing?

A **cruciform** strength (shaped by Christ), not an egoic one.

- **“Suffereth long”** → strength to absorb injury without becoming cruel.  
- **“Is kind”** → strength used for another’s good, not self-display.  
- **“Seeketh not her own”** → freedom from the compulsion to center oneself.  
- **“Not easily provoked”** → emotional discipline, not fragility.  
- **“Thinketh no evil”** (doesn’t keep a ledger of wrongs) → strength to release vengeance.  
- **“Endureth all things”** → resilient, covenantal fidelity.

So this is not weak sentimentality. It is **moral and spiritual muscle**: the power to govern the self, to tell the truth, to remain faithful, and to build others up rather than dominate them.

In short: Paul’s negations clear away counterfeit power so we can see real power—**love as enduring, self-giving strength**.
