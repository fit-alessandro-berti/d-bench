The trust asked for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is **a profound, relational dependence on God that involves the whole person—heart, will, and mind—while actively rejecting self-reliance as the final authority**. It is not a call to ignorance, but to a *humble epistemology* that places ultimate trust in God’s infinite wisdom over creaturely understanding.

To distinguish this from anti-intellectualism, we must define each:

### 1. **Humble Self-Distrust (Biblical Trust)**
This is the **positive** call of the verse:
- **Trust "with all thine heart"**: This is not blind faith, but a *total commitment* of the whole being—affection, will, and decision-making.
- **"Lean not unto thine own understanding"**: This is not a dismissal of *using* the mind, but of *leaning on* or *idolizing* one’s own fallen, limited, and often selfish reasoning as the ultimate standard.
- **"In all thy ways acknowledge him"**: Trust is expressed through *active submission* in every area of life, seeking God’s direction rather than relying solely on pragmatic or philosophical autonomy.
- **It is rooted in reverence and love for God**, recognizing that finite minds cannot fully grasp the infinite tapestry of life (cf. Isaiah 55:8–9).

This creates a **wise fool** paradox: The one who trusts God fully may be considered a "fool" by the world for not following the crowd’s wisdom, yet is truly wise (Proverbs 28:26, 29:18). It is not anti-intellectual; it is **meta-intellectual**—it questions the ultimate sufficiency of unaided human reason.

### 2. **Anti-Intellectualism**
This is **not** what the verse teaches:
- **Anti-intellectualism** is the *suspicion or dismissal of reason, learning, and intellectual pursuit* as ends in themselves or as valid paths to truth.
- It often stems from **distrust of education, expertise, or rational inquiry** itself—treating the mind as untrustworthy *simpliciter*.
- It can become a form of **prideful anti-rationalism**, where one’s own "common sense" or intuition is elevated over all else, which is actually the *opposite* of biblical self-distrust.

Biblical trust does **not** reject learning, science, or careful reasoning. Rather, it rejects the **autonomy** of human wisdom—its claim to be the final arbiter of truth apart from God.

### Key Distinction:
- **Humble self-distrust** says: "My understanding is fallen, limited, and prone to self-deception; therefore, I must continually submit it to God’s revealed and active Word and Spirit."
- **Anti-intellectualism** says: "Thinking is dangerous or unnecessary; we should rely on feeling, tradition, or anti-intellectual authority."

Historically, great Christian scholars like Augustine, Aquinas, Calvin, and Bavinck emphasized deep intellectual pursuit *precisely because* they trusted God with all their hearts—they saw rigorous thought as an act of worship (Romans 12:1–2).

### Conclusion:
The trust in Proverbs 3:5–6 is **a fiducial surrender** that acknowledges God’s omniscience and man’s finiteness, leading to a *deeper* engagement with truth, not a retreat from it. It is the antithesis of intellectual laziness or contempt; it is intellectual humility that *frees* the mind to seek truth more honestly, knowing that "the fear of the Lord is the beginning of wisdom" (Proverbs 1:7). It finds its ultimate fulfillment in Christ, who perfectly trusted the Father (John 5:19, 8:28), showing that true trust and true understanding are united in the person of Jesus.
