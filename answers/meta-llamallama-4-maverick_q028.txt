The sentence "He that is without sin among you, let him first cast a stone at her" from John 8:7 is morally destabilizing for several interconnected reasons, making it a profound and challenging statement. Let's examine the key aspects it addresses:

1. **Exposing Hypocrisy**: The statement directly confronts the self-righteousness of those about to stone the adulterous woman. By saying "He that is without sin among you," Jesus highlights the fact that the accusers are not morally superior to the accused. This challenges their hypocrisy, as they are about to condemn someone to death for a sin they themselves may have committed or may be capable of committing.

2. **Interrupting Mob-Righteousness**: The crowd is driven by a collective sense of righteousness, ready to execute judgment without mercy. Jesus interrupts this mob mentality by shifting the focus from the accused to the accusers, making each individual consider their own moral standing before taking action against another. This intervention challenges the notion that a group can collectively decide someone's fate without introspection.

3. **The Complexity of Judgment**: Jesus' statement doesn't outright forbid judgment but complicates it. It suggests that judgment should not be taken lightly and should be undertaken by those who are themselves morally impeccable, which is an impossible criterion to meet. This challenges the simplistic binary of judge and judged, pointing to the complexity of human morality.

4. **Highlighting Moral Ambiguity and Universality of Sin**: The statement implies that sin is universal ("among you"), suggesting that everyone has erred in some way. This universality of sin undermines the clear distinction between the sinner and the righteous, creating a moral ambiguity that complicates the act of judgment.

5. **Promoting Mercy and Reflection**: By challenging the accusers, Jesus encourages a moment of reflection. It allows for the possibility of mercy and understanding, rather than straightforward condemnation. This promotes a more nuanced approach to dealing with others' sins, one that considers the complexities of human frailty.

6. **Something Deeper - Challenging Legalism and Moral Absolutism**: At a deeper level, Jesus' statement challenges the legalistic and absolutist approach to morality represented by the Pharisees and the crowd. It questions the notion that law and morality can be reduced to clear-cut rules and punishments without considering the context, intentions, and complexities of human actions.

In summary, the sentence is morally destabilizing because it:

- Exposes the hypocrisy of those judging others.
- Interrupts the mob mentality that often accompanies collective judgment.
- Complicates the act of judgment by highlighting the universality of sin and the need for self-reflection.
- Challenges legalism and moral absolutism by promoting a more nuanced understanding of morality that includes mercy and acknowledges human complexity.

It doesn't simply forbid judgment but invites a more thoughtful, compassionate, and introspective approach to dealing with the sins of others.
