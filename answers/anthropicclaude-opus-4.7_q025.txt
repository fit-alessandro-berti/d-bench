# The Moral Danger in Matthew 6:1

## The Problem Is Not Visibility

Jesus cannot be condemning visibility as such. Only a few verses earlier, in the same Sermon on the Mount, He says:

> "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven." (Matt. 5:16)

So goodness is *meant* to be seen. A city on a hill cannot be hidden, nor should it try to be. If visibility itself were the corruption, Jesus would contradict Himself within a single discourse.

## The Problem Is the Motive — "To Be Seen"

The Greek phrase is *pros to theathēnai autois* — literally, "in order to be gazed upon by them." The construction expresses **purpose**, not mere consequence. The danger is not that others happen to see your goodness; it is that their seeing becomes the **reason** you do it.

This turns the moral act inside out:
- **Outwardly**: an act of mercy, piety, generosity.
- **Inwardly**: an act of self-display, using the needy or the sacred as a stage.

The alms still helps the poor person, but it no longer forms the soul of the giver. The deed becomes a costume.

## Why This Forfeits the Reward

Jesus' logic is strict: *"otherwise ye have no reward of your Father."* Why not?

Because a reward answers to an intention. If your aim was human applause, you already received exactly what you sought — the look, the admiration, the reputation. The transaction is closed. There is nothing left for God to reward, because nothing was ever directed to Him. As Jesus puts it bluntly three verses later about the hypocrites: *"they have their reward"* (v. 2).

## The Distinction in One Line

> **The question is not whether others see your goodness, but whether their seeing is what you were after.**

Two people can perform the identical act:
- One gives alms and is seen — and the seeing is incidental to a heart fixed on God and neighbor.
- Another gives alms *so that* he may be seen — and the goodness is swallowed by vanity.

The first is light shining naturally; the second is a lamp held up to illuminate the lamp-bearer.

## The Deeper Warning

Matthew 6:1 exposes a subtle corruption that is almost invisible from the outside — because it looks exactly like real virtue. That is what makes it dangerous. Gross sin announces itself; performed righteousness does not. One can spend a whole life doing good things and arrive before God having worked entirely for an audience that has already paid in full.

The remedy Jesus offers throughout the chapter is secrecy — not because secrecy is holier than openness, but because secrecy **purifies the motive**. When no one but God can see, only God can be the reason.
