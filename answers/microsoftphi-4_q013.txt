The passage from Amos 5:21–24 is part of a larger critique by the prophet Amos against the people of Israel during a time when they were experiencing prosperity and material blessings. Amos's message is a call for genuine piety and ethical behavior, rather than empty ritualism. Here’s why the passage rejects worship so sharply and what kind of moral or spiritual fraud it exposes:

1. **Superficial Worship**: The Israelites are engaging in religious rituals (feast days, solemn assemblies, offerings) without true devotion or a change in their moral character. Amos condemns this superficial worship because it's disconnected from justice and righteousness.

2. **Lack of Justice and Righteousness**: Amos emphasizes that God is not pleased with mere rituals if they are not accompanied by justice and righteousness. The people are more concerned with the outward forms of worship than with the underlying ethical demands of their faith.

3. **Complacency and Corruption**: The passage highlights a complacency among the people, who are indulging in the trappings of religious ceremonies while neglecting social justice, treating others unfairly, oppressing the poor, and accumulating wealth unjustly.

4. **Moral and Spiritual Fraud**: The "fraud" is in the false belief that ritualistic practices alone can secure God's favor. The people think that their offerings and feasts suffice, but Amos exposes that without a commitment to righteous living, these practices are meaningless to God.

5. **Call for Genuine Repentance**: Amos is calling for a transformative repentance that goes beyond ritual compliance. It’s a call to prioritize moral and ethical behavior—justice ("let judgment run down as waters") and righteousness ("and righteousness as a mighty stream")—as central elements of worship and community life.

In summary, the passage sharply rejects worship because it criticizes the disconnect between the people's religious practices and their moral failures. It calls for worship that is deeply rooted in justice and righteousness, rather than mere ritual conformity.
