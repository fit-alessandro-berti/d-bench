The passage is not calling for a “better balance” between ritual and ethics because, in the mind of the prophet, **the ritual is already a lie**—a performance that exists precisely to neutralize the demand of justice.  
What is being rejected, therefore, is not worship *as such*, but **worship that functions as moral currency**: the attempt to buy God off, to turn sacred acts into a shield against accusation.

### 1. The rejection is total because the fraud is total
The prophet piles up categorical negatives:  
*“I hate, I despise… I will not smell… I will not accept… I will not hear…”*  

This is not a suggestion to add social concern to liturgy.  
It is an **unmasking**: the very things that are supposed to draw near to God have become instruments of distance.  
When worship becomes a substitute for justice, it is not merely incomplete; it is **offensive**—not because God dislikes music or sacrifice in principle, but because here they have become **acts of contempt** toward the neighbor.

### 2. The specific fraud: “sacrifice as alibi”
The people are likely observing the prescribed feasts, offerings, and songs with meticulous care.  
But outside the sanctuary, the economic and judicial order is arranged to exploit the poor, pervert courts, and grind the needy into dust (see the surrounding context in Amos 5, 8:4–6).

The fraud works like this:  
- Worship creates a **moral glow** that reassures the worshiper of divine favor.  
- That glow is then used to **silence the conscience** and blunt the call for change.  
- God is treated as a **client** who can be pacified with gifts while the real business of life continues unchanged.

Thus the rituals are not “imbalanced”; they are **lies performed in sacred space**.  
To ask for “better balance” would be to assume the rituals are genuine and merely need supplementation.  
The prophet treats them as **counterfeit**—so counterfeit that God would rather have no ritual at all than ritual that masks violence.

### 3. The positive demand: worship poured out as justice
The famous line  
*“Let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream”*  
is not a separate add-on to religious life.  
It is **the shape that true worship must take** once it is stripped of fraud.

In the ancient imagination, “waters” and “streams” suggest:  
- **Continuous, public, unstoppable force** (not private, token charity).  
- **Life-giving power** that irrigates the whole land, not just the sanctuary.  
- **Judicial clarity** (judgment = *mishpat*, the just ordering of society) and *righteousness* (right relationships).

Here the prophet is doing something radical:  
He **redefines liturgical language**.  
“Running water” was often associated with the symbolism of temple purity and life.  
The prophet transfers that sacred force *out of the temple precincts* and into the streets, courts, and marketplaces.  
True worship “runs down” not in the blood of animals but in the **conduct of the community**.

### 4. The spiritual logic exposed
The passage reveals a perennial mechanism of religious self-deception:  
- Worship can become a **technology of control**, a way to manage God’s reactions.  
- It can serve as **moral anesthesia**, dulling the pain of injustice.  
- It can generate **social distinction**, allowing the powerful to display piety while withholding equity.

This is why the rejection is sharp:  
God refuses to be **domesticated** into a ritual vending machine.  
The divine “No” is actually a defense of God’s own holiness—because a holiness that sanctions exploitation is not holiness at all, but idolatry with a liturgical soundtrack.

### 5. The modern resonance
The text warns against any religious ecosystem in which:  
- **Performance replaces substance** (noise of songs while lives are broken).  
- **Sacrifice becomes transaction** (we gave, therefore we owe nothing more).  
- **Community worship hides communal injustice** (solemn assemblies that exclude the concerns of the marginalized).

The “moral fraud” is the **displacement of ethics by aesthetics**, the belief that God can be pleased with melody while ignoring misery.  
The prophet insists that God’s ears are closed to music until they are opened to the cry of the oppressed.

---

**In short:**  
The passage does not ask for balance because the worship described is not worship in God’s eyes—it is **a system of lies** that uses religion to evade the demand for justice.  
The sharp rejection is the only honest response to a lie that has become lethal.  
What God wants is not *additive* ethics but *transformative* worship: a life in which justice flows as freely and visibly as water, because the boundary between shrine and world has finally been abolished by righteousness.
