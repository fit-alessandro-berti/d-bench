# The Moral Posture of Correction in Galatians 6:1

## The Situation Described

Paul chooses his language carefully. The man is *overtaken* (προλημφθῇ) — not described as brazenly defiant but as one caught, surprised, perhaps ensnared before he fully understood what was happening. This matters because the nature of the fall shapes the nature of the required response. Paul does not describe a hardened rebel but a brother who has stumbled.

## Three Elements Held Together

Paul binds three things into a single command, and separating any one from the others corrupts the whole.

### 1. Restoration as the Aim
The word is *καταρτίζετε* — to mend, to set right, as a dislocated bone is reset or a torn net is repaired. The goal is not punishment, not exposure, not distancing. It is the *recovery of the person*. This fixes the entire purpose of the confrontation. Any correction not aimed at restoration has departed from what Paul commands.

### 2. Meekness as the Manner
The spirit of meekness is not optional atmosphere but the required instrument. And this is where the passage becomes searching, because meekness is not merely gentle tone. It is the absence of the desire to stand over another person. The meek corrector does not approach from above. He approaches as one who knows what the ground feels like.

This eliminates several counterfeits:
- Correction done with relish
- Correction done with cold superiority
- Correction done to establish the corrector's standing
- Correction done performatively, for an audience

### 3. Self-Suspicion as the Safeguard
"Considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted." This is the most remarkable element. Paul does not say *remember that you once sinned*. He says something far more unsettling: you are *presently vulnerable*. The very act of correcting another person is itself a moment of spiritual danger for the corrector.

Why? Because correction places you in a position of moral authority, and that position is precisely where pride, contempt, and self-righteousness enter most naturally. The man bending down to lift his brother up may discover in that very posture a secret satisfaction that he is the one standing.

## Why These Three Cannot Be Separated

Paul keeps them together because each one prevents the corruption of the others.

**Restoration without meekness** becomes domination — I will fix you, and you will know that I fixed you.

**Meekness without the aim of restoration** becomes mere niceness — a refusal to say the hard thing, which abandons the brother in his fault under the guise of gentleness.

**Restoration and meekness without self-suspicion** become the most dangerous counterfeit of all — a correction that looks right and sounds right but feeds the corrector's pride precisely because it appears so virtuous.

Self-suspicion is what keeps the entire operation honest. It forces the corrector to ask: *Am I doing this for him, or for me? Am I grieved, or am I gratified? Do I want him restored, or do I want to be the one who restored him?*

## The Underlying Anthropology

Paul's instruction rests on a particular understanding of human nature. He assumes:

- That **believers will fall** — he does not treat this as unthinkable
- That **fallen believers need others** — isolation is not the remedy
- That **those who help are themselves in danger** — no one stands on ground so firm that the act of helping another cannot become an occasion for his own stumbling
- That **spiritual maturity is shown not in immunity to temptation but in honest awareness of it**

The phrase "ye which are spiritual" does not mean *ye who are beyond falling*. It means *ye who are walking by the Spirit* — and walking by the Spirit includes knowing your own capacity for sin.

## The Heart of the Matter

The moral posture Paul requires is this: **you must care enough to confront, be humble enough to do it gently, and be honest enough to suspect your own motives in the very act of doing it.**

This is extraordinarily demanding. It is far easier to ignore the brother's sin (and call it grace), or to correct him harshly (and call it faithfulness), or to correct him gently while quietly congratulating yourself (and call it maturity). Paul closes off all three escapes.

What remains is a narrow path: genuine grief over a brother's fall, genuine desire for his recovery, genuine gentleness in the approach, and genuine fear that you yourself are not above the same fall — or above the subtler fall of pride that correction itself invites.

This is why restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion are kept together. They form a single discipline. Remove any one, and what remains is no longer correction. It is something else wearing correction's clothing.
