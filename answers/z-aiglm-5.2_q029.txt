Based on Galatians 6:1, the moral posture required for correction is one of **humble, Spirit-led compassion**. It is the posture of a spiritual physician rather than a prosecuting attorney. 

Here is a breakdown of the specific moral posture required, and why restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion must be kept together:

### The Moral Posture Required for Correction
To correct a brother or sister caught in sin, the apostle Paul outlines a specific disposition:
1. **Spiritual Maturity:** The correction is to be carried out by "ye which are spiritual." This does not mean spiritual perfection, but rather those who are walking by the Spirit (as outlined in Galatians 5). It requires emotional and spiritual stability; you cannot pull someone out of a ditch if you are sliding into it yourself.
2. **Meekness:** Also translated as "gentleness," meekness in the biblical sense is not weakness, but "power under control." It means having the authority and insight to identify a sin, but choosing the gentlest, most surgical method to address it. It refuses to shame, berate, or punish.
3. **Self-Suspicion:** The corrector must "consider thyself." This requires a posture of profound self-awareness and humility. The one correcting does not stand aloof as a morally superior being, but approaches the offender as a fellow fallible human. 

### Why Restoration, Meekness, and Self-Suspicion are Kept Together
Paul links these three elements because they form a necessary, interdependent triad. If any one of them is removed, the act of correction becomes spiritually toxic. 

*   **Restoration is the *Goal*.** The objective is not to expose, punish, or exile the sinner, but to "restore" them. The Greek word used here (*katartizo*) was a medical term used for setting a broken bone. Setting a bone requires a firm hand, but it must be done with extreme care to cause no further damage.
*   **Meekness is the *Method*.** If you pursue the goal of restoration without meekness, the correction easily devolves into harshness and condemnation. A harsh corrector might expose the sin, but in doing so, they crush the spirit of the sinner, driving them further into despair or rebellion. Meekness ensures the medicine does not kill the patient.
*   **Self-Suspicion is the *Safeguard*.** If you pursue restoration with meekness, but lack self-suspicion, you will inevitably fall into spiritual pride. You begin to think, "I am helping this wretched person because I am inherently better than they are." This is the exact pride that leaves a person vulnerable to the devil. Paul warns, "lest thou also be tempted." The moment a corrector forgets their own capacity for grave sin, they drop their guard and become targets for the very temptations they are trying to combat.

**In Summary:**
They are kept together because they reflect the reality of the gospel. We are all fallen (which demands self-suspicion), we are all in need of grace (which demands meekness), and God’s ultimate desire for fallen humanity is healing and reconciliation (restoration). Keeping these three together protects the offender from being crushed by self-righteousness, and it protects the corrector from being infected by pride.
