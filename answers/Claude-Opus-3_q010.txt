The passage from Philippians praises the power of humility and self-sacrifice exemplified by Jesus Christ. It highlights how Christ, despite being equal with God, willingly relinquished his divine status to take on human form as a servant. He then further humbled himself by submitting to a degrading death on the cross. 

This passage reveals a radically different conception of power than what is commonly admired in the world. Rather than the power to dominate others and exalt oneself, it elevates the power to humble oneself and sacrifice for others out of love. It's a power rooted in the strength of character to deny one's own privileges and prerogatives for a higher purpose.

In some ways, this humble, self-sacrificing power can feel more unsettling than blatant displays of dominance and superiority. Here's why:

1. It subverts expectations. We expect the strong and powerful to flaunt their status and lord it over others. Humility from the mighty defies the usual paradigms of power.

2. It convicts the conscience. Seeing someone with every right and ability to dominate others choose the path of humility and sacrifice instead can make us uncomfortably aware of our own selfishness and ego. It sets a standard we haven't met.

3. It challenges value systems. Praising humility as the supreme power and virtue goes against value systems built on pride, self-promotion, competition and domination of others. It calls into question what we consider important.

4. The motive feels suspect. We cynically suspect ulterior motives when the strong act weak. What are they really after? Humility can be interpreted as a ploy to gain sympathy and manipulate others.

5. It feels too good to be true. In a world used to self-interest, radical humility and self-denial seems too saintly to be real, triggering skepticism and unease. We doubt what we haven't personally seen.

So while brash domination is ugly, it fits the paradigms we know. Humility coupled with greatness is disruptive and hard to process. It challenges us to rethink our notions of power, calling us to a higher yet harder way of being. This passage presents Christ's humble sacrifice as the ultimate inversion of worldly power structures and the truest revelation of divine character.
