### Short answer  

- **“Judge not” (Luke 6:37) ** means *don’t pronounce a final, condemning verdict on a person’s character or worth.*  
- **Moral judgments do matter** — we must decide what is right or wrong, good or bad, and we are called to hold each other accountable in a loving, restorative way.  

The passage is not a blanket ban on all moral assessment; it warns against **the attitude of condemnation** that is self‑righteous, unexamined, and un‑merciful.

---

## 1. What “judge” means in Luke 6:37  

| Aspect | What the verse forbids | What it permits |
|--------|------------------------|-----------------|
| **Motivation** | *Self‑righteousness* – judging to elevate yourself or to feel superior. | *Humility* – judging to understand, to help, or to seek reconciliation. |
| **Scope** | *Final, condemning judgment* – “You are a sinner, you deserve punishment.” | *Discernment* – recognizing behavior, motives, and consequences without declaring a person’s eternal status. |
| **Action** | *Condemnation* – pronouncing a verdict that carries penalty or stigma. | *Forgiveness* – extending grace, offering a chance to change. |
| **Result** | “You shall be judged” – the same standard you apply comes back to you (the “golden rule” in reverse). | “You shall be forgiven” – the mercy you show is returned to you. |

In short, the verse warns **against a judgmental *spirit*** rather than **against moral discernment itself**.

---

## 2. Discernment vs. the Appetite to Condemn  

| Discernment (healthy) | Condemnation (unhealthy) |
|-----------------------|--------------------------|
| **Rooted in love** – seeks the good of the other (Gal 6:1). | **Rooted in pride** – wants to prove you’re “right.” |
| **Self‑examination first** – “Let us first remove the speck from our own eye” (Matt 7:3‑5). | **Selective blindness** – ignores one’s own faults while magnifying others’. |
| **Restorative aim** – aims to heal, reconcile, or guide toward repentance. | **Punitive aim** – aims to shame, ostracize, or enforce a rigid rule. |
| **Open to correction** – welcomes feedback and is willing to change. | **Closed to correction** – dismisses any challenge to the judgment. |
| **Context‑sensitive** – considers circumstances, intent, and cultural background. | **Rigid, absolutist** – applies the same verdict regardless of context. |

### Biblical illustrations  

1. **Jesus and the woman caught in adultery** (John 8:1‑11).  
   - He *did not* condemn her; he *challenged* the accusers, showing that judgment must be tempered with mercy.  
   - He still *recognized* the act as sin, but he redirected the focus from “you are guilty” to “let us consider the heart.”

2. **Paul’s instruction to the Galatians** (Gal 6:1).  
   - “Restore the one who is caught in a sin *in a spirit of gentleness*.”  
   - The goal is *recovery*, not condemnation.

3. **The early church’s discipline** (1 Cor 5:11‑13).  
   - The church *judged* a behavior (sexual immorality) and *took action* (exclusion), but the judgment was **based on clear teaching**, not on personal animus. The “judgment” was *corrective* rather than *condemning*.

---

## 3. Why the distinction matters for everyday life  

1. **Preserves relationships** – A condemning attitude drives people away; discernment invites restoration.  
2. **Models Christ‑like character** – Jesus repeatedly showed that truth and mercy are not opposites; they work together.  
3. **Avoids the “judge‑and‑be‑judged” trap** – When we adopt a harsh, unforgiving stance, we set ourselves up for the same harsh treatment (Matt 7:2).  
4. **Promotes genuine accountability** – True moral accountability asks, “How can we help this person change?” rather than “How can we punish them?”

---

## 4. Practical ways to practice discernment instead of condemnation  

| Situation | Condemning response | Discerning response |
|-----------|--------------------|----------------------|
| **A friend repeatedly gossips** | “You’re a terrible person; you’ll ruin everyone’s reputation.” | “I’ve noticed this pattern; how can we work together to keep confidences safe?” |
| **A coworker misses deadlines** | “You’re lazy and don’t care about the team.” | “I see the workload is heavy; what obstacles are you facing, and how can I help?” |
| **A family member holds a differing political view** | “You’re misinformed and morally wrong.” | “I value our relationship; can we share perspectives respectfully?” |
| **A church member struggles with addiction** | “You’re a sinner, you’re beyond help.” | “God’s grace is available; let’s connect you with support and pray together.” |

Key habits:

- **Pause before speaking** – ask, “Am I judging the person or the behavior?”  
- **Check your motive** – is the intent to help or to feel superior?  
- **Invite self‑examination** – “What part of this situation might I be overlooking?”  
- **Offer forgiveness first** – “I’m willing to extend grace; let’s see how we can move forward.”  

---

## 5. Summary  

- **Luke 6:37** condemns a *self‑righteous, final, condemning attitude* toward others, not the legitimate need to discern right from wrong.  
- **Discernment** is a loving, humble, and restorative evaluation of behavior and motives.  
- **Condemnation** is a prideful, punitive verdict that seeks to shame or punish rather than heal.  

Thus, moral judgments *do* matter—what matters is *how* we make them. The verse calls us to **examine ourselves first, extend mercy, and judge with the same standard we hope to receive**, thereby turning “judge not” into a pathway toward genuine, life‑giving accountability.
