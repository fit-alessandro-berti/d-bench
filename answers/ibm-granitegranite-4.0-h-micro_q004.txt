The passage from Proverbs 3:5-6 presents a nuanced concept of trust that is deeply rooted in faith and humility. To fully understand the nature of the trust being asked for, it's essential to distinguish between the two concepts you've mentioned: humble self-distrust and mere anti-intellectualism.

### 1. **Humble Self-Distrust**

This form of trust involves recognizing the limitations of human understanding and acknowledging that there are aspects of life and the universe that are beyond our comprehension. It is a form of trust that is grounded in humility, recognizing that we do not have all the answers and that there are things we cannot fully understand on our own. This kind of trust is not about doubting oneself or being overly cautious, but rather about placing one's confidence in a higher power or a greater wisdom that transcends human knowledge.

- **Characteristics:**
  - **Humility:** It requires a humble acknowledgment of one's own limitations and the vastness of the unknown.
  - **Dependence:** It involves a reliance on a higher power or divine wisdom, suggesting that there are aspects of life that are better understood through faith or spiritual insight.
  - **Openness:** It encourages openness to guidance and direction from sources beyond one's immediate understanding.

### 2. **Mere Anti-Intellectualism**

Anti-intellectualism, on the other hand, is characterized by a rejection of or mistrust in intellectual pursuits, knowledge, and reasoning. It often stems from a belief that certain forms of knowledge or reasoning are inherently flawed or unreliable. This stance can manifest in various ways, such as dismissing scientific evidence, rejecting logical arguments, or outright skepticism towards any form of intellectual endeavor.

- **Characteristics:**
  - **Dismissal of Knowledge:** It involves a general distrust or disdain for knowledge and intellectual pursuits.
  - **Skepticism:** It often includes a skeptical or dismissive attitude towards evidence-based reasoning and scientific inquiry.
  - **Closed-mindedness:** It can lead to a closed-mindedness, where individuals resist new ideas or information that challenges their preconceived notions.

### **Distinguishing Between the Two**

To distinguish between humble self-distrust and mere anti-intellectualism, consider the following points:

- **Source of Trust:** 
  - **Humble Self-Distrust:** Trusts in a higher power or divine wisdom, acknowledging that human understanding is limited.
  - **Anti-Intellectualism:** Lacks trust in any form of knowledge or intellectual reasoning, often dismissing evidence and logical arguments.

- **Attitude Towards Knowledge:**
  - **Humble Self-Distrust:** Accepts that there are limits to human knowledge but seeks guidance and direction from a higher source.
  - **Anti-Intellectualism:** Rejects the idea of seeking knowledge or understanding, often viewing intellectual pursuits as unnecessary or unreliable.

- **Motivation:**
  - **Humble Self-Distrust:** Motivated by a desire for guidance, wisdom, and direction, recognizing that there are aspects of life that are better understood through faith or spiritual insight.
  - **Anti-Intellectualism:** Motivated by a distrust of intellectual pursuits, often stemming from a belief that knowledge can be misleading or that certain forms of reasoning are flawed.

### **Application in Daily Life**

In practical terms, applying the concept of humble self-distrust as described in Proverbs 3:5-6 involves:

- **Acknowledging Limits:** Recognizing that there are aspects of life that are beyond our understanding and that we cannot control everything.
- **Seeking Guidance:** Turning to a higher power or spiritual wisdom for direction and guidance, trusting that there are insights and wisdom available that are not accessible through human reasoning alone.
- **Openness to Guidance:** Being open to the guidance and direction provided by a higher power, even if it challenges our preconceived notions or understanding.

In contrast, anti-intellectualism would involve rejecting this approach, dismissing the value of seeking guidance or understanding through intellectual means, and instead relying solely on personal intuition or skepticism towards any form of knowledge.

### **Conclusion**

In summary, the trust being asked for in Proverbs 3:5-6 is a form of humble self-distrust, characterized by humility, reliance on a higher power, and openness to guidance. This is distinct from anti-intellectualism, which is marked by a rejection of knowledge and intellectual pursuits. Embracing humble self-distrust allows individuals to acknowledge their limitations and seek guidance from a source beyond their own understanding, fostering a balanced and spiritually grounded approach to life.
