In Matthew 6:1, Jesus is addressing the **motive behind righteous acts**, not the visibility of the acts themselves. The core issue is the **craving to be seen and praised by others**—performing acts of goodness (like giving alms) primarily for human applause rather out of genuine devotion to God.

The verse warns that when good deeds are done **"to be seen of them"** (i.e., to gain public admiration), the reward is limited to human approval, and there is **"no reward from your Father in heaven."** The danger lies in a heart that seeks self-glorification rather than God’s glory.

Thus, the problem is **not visibility per se**, because Jesus later teaches that good works should sometimes be visible to glorify God (Matthew 5:16). The moral danger is the **internal motivation**—a performative piety driven by the desire for human praise, which replaces sincere faith and humility with hypocrisy.
