1. What “victory” looks like in Romans 12:20-21  

The verb Paul repeats—nikaō, “to conquer, win a victory” (from which Nike, “victory,” comes)—is the normal military term for defeating an enemy.  
• v. 21a  “Do not be conquered by evil” = Do not let the wrong done to you force you either to retaliate or to despair.  
• v. 21b  “Conquer evil with good” = Win by staying good and by answering your enemy’s need with active benevolence.

Thus the winner is the one who  ➔  (a) remains unsullied by the evil done to him, and  ➔  (b) has a real possibility of turning the opponent from enemy into friend.  The battlefield is moral and relational, not military.

2. What the “coals of fire” image means  

Paul is quoting Prov 25:21-22.  In the ancient Near-East a small brazier of live coals was often carried on the head when one borrowed fire from a neighbour.  “Heaping coals on his head” therefore pictures supplying the very thing a household needs to cook its food and warm itself.  In other words, you meet the person’s most basic need.  

But live coals also burn; they can awaken a stinging sense of shame.  The idea is not revenge but moral shock therapy: your kindness may sear the conscience of the wrong-doer so that he repents.  The enemy is not destroyed but reclaimed.

3. Is the command weak, manipulative, or strong?  

• Not weak.  It demands self-control, courage, and faith in God’s justice (v. 19 “leave room for God’s wrath”).  Anyone can strike back; few can love back.  

• Not manipulative.  The good is real good—food and drink, tangible help.  You do it whether or not the other ever repents (cf. Matt 5:44-45).  The possible effect (“coals”) is mentioned as encouragement, not as a sinister motive.

• Genuinely strong.  It wages a higher form of warfare that can  ➀ protect your own character,  ➁ stop the spiral of retaliation, and  ➂ offer the offender a way back to righteousness.  That is why Paul can borrow the secular word for victory and attach it to acts of love: goodness is the Christian’s most powerful weapon.

So Romans 12:20-21 defines victory as defeating evil without becoming evil and, whenever God allows, transforming the enemy by unexpected kindness. That is not weakness; it is moral conquest.
