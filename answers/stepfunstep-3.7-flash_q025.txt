The text of Matthew 6:1 explicitly frames the danger as rooted in the *craving to be seen by others*, not in visibility itself. To understand this distinction, we can parse the verse’s wording, its original context, and its place in Jesus’ broader teaching:
### First, the verse’s own framing makes motive the core issue
The phrase "to be seen of them" is a purpose clause in the original Greek: it specifies that the act of giving alms in public is only condemned when the intentional goal of the giver is to attract human admiration. Visibility is merely the context where this particular temptation is most acute, not the sin in itself. This is confirmed by Jesus’ own teaching elsewhere: in Matthew 5:16, he commands his followers, “Let your light shine before others, so that they may see your good works and give glory to your Father who is in heaven.” Public goodness is not inherently forbidden; its moral status depends entirely on the doer’s ultimate aim.
### Context clarifies the specific temptation Jesus is addressing
Matthew 6 is part of the Sermon on the Mount, where Jesus contrasts authentic, inward faith with the performative piety of first-century Jewish religious elites (the Pharisees). Almsgiving was a core covenantal duty in this context, and some elites would even have trumpets blown ahead of them when they gave to the poor, to ensure their generosity was public knowledge and boost their social status as devout, righteous people. Jesus is targeting this specific practice of performative charity: turning a sacred obligation to care for the poor into a self-serving performance to earn human honor.
### Why the craving to be seen is morally dangerous
The problem is not that other people witness the good deed, but that the desire for their approval becomes the driving purpose of the act, with three concrete harms:
1. **It corrupts the good act itself**: When the primary goal is to be admired, the deed stops being an expression of love for the person in need or obedience to God, and becomes a performance focused on the giver’s reputation. The needs of the recipient become secondary to the giver’s desire for social status. It is worth noting that Jesus does not say the almsgiving is entirely worthless: the poor person still benefits from the gift, regardless of the giver’s motive. The forfeited reward is the giver’s own spiritual compensation.
2. **It misplaces ultimate loyalty**: Seeking human approval prioritizes the fleeting, fallible judgment of other people over the eternal, perfect judgment of God. The giver is effectively serving an audience of human spectators rather than God, the true source of all goodness.
3. **It forfeits eternal reward**: Jesus states that those who seek human approval “have no reward from your Father which is in heaven.” Their reward is the earthly praise and status they sought, which they have already received in full – there is no eternal compensation for an act whose ultimate aim was worldly approval.
### Closing nuance
Jesus’ warning is not a call to never do good in public, but a call to vigilant self-examination. Public acts of charity, ministry, or moral courage naturally draw attention, and the human heart is prone to crave that attention. The warning here urges believers to guard their hearts against that temptation, ensuring that even when others see their good deeds, their ultimate aim is to honor God and serve others, not to build their own reputation.
