The power being praised here is the power of **self-emptying love**—a paradoxical strength that does not grasp or exploit its prerogatives but instead pours itself out for others. Christ, "being in the form of God," had every right to claim equality with God, yet He chose to "make Himself of no reputation," taking the "form of a servant" and dying a humiliating death. This is not the power of coercion or domination but the power of **kenosis** (from the Greek *kenoō*, "to empty"), where true greatness is measured by self-giving rather than self-aggrandizement.

Humility joined to greatness can feel more unsettling than open domination because it subverts our expectations of power. We are conditioned to associate power with control, prestige, and invulnerability—traits that dominate through force or intimidation. But Christ’s humility exposes the fragility of such power. It reveals that true authority lies not in crushing others but in **vulnerability**, not in self-preservation but in self-sacrifice. This kind of power is unsettling because it reveals the hollowness of worldly strength—it exposes that the most powerful act is not to take but to give, not to conquer but to serve. It challenges our instinct to measure greatness by what we possess rather than by what we surrender.

In a world that often equates power with domination, Christ’s example forces us to confront the radical alternative: that power is perfected in weakness (2 Corinthians 12:9). This is why it unsettles us—it demands a complete reorientation of our values.
