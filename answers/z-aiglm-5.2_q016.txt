While anger, envy, wounded fairness, and pride in obedience are all present in the elder brother’s reaction, his **deepest problem is his discomfort with mercy itself**, which is rooted in a transactional, merit-based relationship with his father. 

Here is a breakdown of how these elements interact to reveal his core issue:

**1. The Surface Symptoms: Anger and Envy**
Anger and envy are the immediate, visible emotions. He is angry (v. 28) and envious that his brother is getting a party while he has never received a goat to celebrate with his friends (v. 29). However, these are merely symptoms of a deeper theological and relational malfunction. 

**2. The Fuel: Pride in Obedience and Wounded Fairness**
The elder brother operates entirely within an economy of merit. Listen to his language: *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment"* (v. 29). 
*   He views his relationship with his father as an employer/employee dynamic, not a father/son dynamic. He uses the word "serve" rather than "live with" or "love." 
*   Because he believes he has earned his standing through flawless obedience, he views the father’s grace toward the younger son as a violation of justice. His wounded fairness dictates that bad behavior should be punished and good behavior should be rewarded. Unmerited grace breaks the rules of his universe.

**3. The Root: Discomfort with Mercy Itself**
Because the elder brother believes he has earned his righteousness, he fundamentally cannot accept grace—not for his brother, and ironically, not for himself. 
When the father shows mercy to the prodigal, the elder brother doesn't just feel slighted; he is morally offended. He resents that the father’s love is not a commodity to be purchased with good behavior, but a gift freely given. He is deeply uncomfortable with mercy because mercy levels the playing field. If the father can forgive the prodigal, it means the elder brother’s years of calculated obedience did not buy him a superior status of sonship—it only kept him physically present while his heart remained distant.

**The Ultimate Tragedy: Alienation in the Home**
The father’s response exposes the elder brother's deepest problem: *"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine"* (v. 31). 
The elder brother thought he was lacking something (a kid, a party, recognition), but the father points out that he already possessed everything. The tragedy of the elder brother is that he was **lost at home**. He had the father's presence but didn't value it. He wanted the *rewards* of the house, but he did not share the *heart* of the father. 

Therefore, his deepest problem is spiritual alienation. He resents mercy because he believes in a world built on transactions rather than grace. He is unable to rejoice in the resurrection of his brother because he has never understood that his own standing was always based on the father's love, not his own obedience.
