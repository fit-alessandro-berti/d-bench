Luke’s critique of the Pharisee is not mainly that he holds a few “wrong doctrines,” but that his entire posture toward God and other people is corrupted. Several things are going on at once:

### What condemns the Pharisee?
**1. Pride / self-righteousness**
He “trusted in himself that he was righteous.”  
That is the core issue. He treats righteousness as something he already possesses and can present to God.

**2. Comparison**
He defines himself by saying, “I am not like other men… or even this publican.”  
Instead of seeking God’s mercy, he ranks himself above others.

**3. Contempt**
Luke says the target audience “despised others.” The Pharisee’s prayer reveals that contempt: the tax collector is not a fellow sinner to be pitied, but a foil to boost his own moral standing.

**4. Virtue turned into self-display**
His fasting and tithing are not condemned in themselves. The problem is that they become credentials in a spiritual performance: “Look what I do; therefore I am acceptable.”  
So the issue is not the virtues, but the use of virtues as evidence of his own superiority.

### Is false doctrine involved?
Not directly in the sense of a formal theological error. The Pharisee’s “doctrine” is more a practical theology of merit: he behaves as though God justifies people on the basis of comparative moral success. That’s a spiritual error, but the passage focuses more on **moral posture** than on a specific doctrinal claim.

### Why is the tax collector’s posture spiritually truer?
Because he comes with **truth about himself** and **need of mercy**.

He does not argue his case, compare himself, or present achievements. He simply appeals to God’s grace:  
> “God be merciful to me, a sinner.”

That is spiritually truer because:

- it is **humble** rather than self-justifying,
- it acknowledges **sin** rather than minimizing it,
- it depends on **mercy** rather than merit,
- it is honest about his place before God.

### In short
The Pharisee is condemned not for fasting or tithing, but for **self-righteous pride expressed through comparison and contempt**, with virtue used as a means of self-exaltation.  
The tax collector is “truer” because he stands before God with no defense, no comparison, and no claim except mercy.
