The Pharisee in the parable is condemned by a combination of factors, but primarily by his pride, comparison, and the use of virtue as self-display. Here's a breakdown:

1. **Pride**: The Pharisee's prayer is filled with self-congratulation, where he thanks God for not being like other men, implying he believes himself to be superior. This self-exaltation is a form of pride, which is contrary to the humility that is foundational to a genuine relationship with God.

2. **Comparison**: By comparing himself favorably to others, especially to the publican (tax collector), the Pharisee reveals his focus on external appearances and his own perceived righteousness rather than a genuine hearts' desire for God. This comparison leads him to look down on others, which is condemned by Jesus as it reflects a heart that does not recognize its own sinfulness and need for mercy.

3. **Contempt for others**: The Pharisee's prayer shows contempt for the publican, lumping him in with "extortioners, unjust, adulterers," which demonstrates his lack of compassion and understanding. This contempt stems from his pride and self-righteousness, further distancing him from the heart of God, which is characterized by love, mercy, and grace.

4. **Use of virtue as self-display**: The Pharisee's virtues, such as fasting twice a week and giving tithes, are mentioned as part of his self-justification. Instead of performing these acts as sincere expressions of devotion to God, he uses them to flaunt his righteousness. This transforms his religious practices into a form of self-aggrandizement rather than genuine worship or service to God.

The tax collector's posture and prayer, on the other hand, are treated as spiritually truer for several reasons:

- **Humility**: The publican stands "afar off," indicating a sense of unworthiness to approach God closely, which reflects true humility. He does not compare himself to others or proclaim his own virtues; instead, he recognizes his sinfulness.

- **Recognition of sin**: By saying, "God be merciful to me a sinner," the publican demonstrates a profound recognition of his own sinfulness and his need for mercy. This acknowledgment is the first step towards repentance and receiving forgiveness from God.

- **Sincerity**: The publican's prayer is sincere and heartfelt, not a display for others or even for himself. His smiting of his breast is a physical expression of his internal anguish and repentance, showing a genuine desire for forgiveness rather than self-justification.

In essence, the Pharisee's condemnation stems from his external focus on his own perceived righteousness and his contempt for others, which is rooted in pride. In contrast, the tax collector's humility, recognition of sin, and sincere prayer for mercy demonstrate a truer spiritual posture, one that is grounded in humility and a genuine relationship with God.
