In Ephesians 4:31–32 Paul sets up a very deliberate contrast. On the one hand he names a cluster of interconnected vices—bitterness, wrath, anger, clamour (shouting), slander, malice—on the other, he summons the virtues that must replace them—kindness, tender-heartedness and forgiveness.  A few observations on why “bitterness” stands opposite “forgiveness,” and what kind of “thing” bitterness is in this passage:

1.  Bitterness as a settled, toxic disposition  
    •  The Greek word translated “bitterness” is pikria, literally “something that tastes sour or galling.”  In biblical usage it points beyond a momentary twinge of hurt to a deep-seated resentment that festers.  
    •  Paul includes it in a list with wrath (orgē), angry outbursts (thymos), shouting (kraugē), slander (blasphēmia) and malice (kakoē).  Those items build on one another—once resentment takes root it erupts in rage, abusive speech and malicious intent.  
    •  It is therefore more than a passing emotion; it has become a posture or habit of heart that shows itself in word and deed.

2.  A contagious poison in corporate life  
    •  Hebrews 12:15 warns of “a root of bitterness” springing up and defiling many.  Paul’s list in Ephesians 4 describes attitudes and behaviors that tend to spread through a community—one person’s grudge becomes gossip, then erupts as open conflict.  
    •  In that sense bitterness is socially contagious: it infects relationships and fragments unity.

3.  Forgiveness as the direct antidote  
    •  The verb Paul uses in v. 32—charizomai—is the same root behind “grace,” and signifies freely cancelling a debt or offence.  Forgiveness is not a passive feeling but an active, gracious choice to release another from what they owe you.  
    •  Kindness (chrestotēs) and tender-heartedness (eunoia) likewise describe moral dispositions that flow out of God’s own forgiving action “for Christ’s sake.”  Just as resentment is a deliberate clinging to past wrongs, forgiveness is a deliberate letting go.

4.  Why the direct opposition?  
    •  To show that you cannot harbor both postures at once.  A heart nursing bitterness will not (without God’s intervention) extend genuine forgiveness; conversely, a forgiving heart expels the root of resentment.  
    •  Paul’s practical point: churches (and individual Christians) must actively “put away” the old, poisonous mentality and “put on” Christlike mercy.  

In sum, Paul treats bitterness not as an involuntary flash of feeling but as a settled, destructive posture—one that easily becomes habitual and spreads among God’s people.  Forgiveness, by contrast, is the positive, grace-filled choice that uproots bitterness and knits believers back together, modeled on the forgiveness God has shown us in Christ.
