To answer this question, we must first root our reading of Mark 8:34–35 in its original 1st-century context, and then account for both the text’s liberating core and the ways it has been distorted to cause harm.

### What kind of self is being denied here?
First, it is critical to clarify what this denial is not: it is not a rejection of one’s inherent worth as a being created in God’s image (imago Dei), not a call to self-hatred, and not a rejection of all legitimate, God-given desires, needs, or identity. These are common, harmful misreadings of the text.

The self Jesus calls his followers to deny is the *autonomous, self-directed "false self" shaped by the values of the dominant world system*. In Jesus’ 1st-century Roman context, this was the self aligned with imperial values of power, wealth, domination, and self-preservation at all costs: the self that sought status and security by collaborating with oppressive systems, or that wanted to use Jesus and his movement as a means to its own ends (the disciples, for example, expected Jesus to be a political Messiah who would overthrow Rome and give them power and prestige in a new earthly kingdom).
The Greek word for "deny" (arneomai) is the same term Mark uses later to describe Peter’s denial of Jesus, framing this choice as a deliberate rejection of the self that would abandon Jesus and his mission of self-sacrifice for the world’s redemption when the cost becomes too high.

This denial is not self-erasure, but reorientation: it is the act of dethroning the self as the center of one’s life, so that one’s priorities and actions are aligned with God’s kingdom of justice, mercy, and love, rather than the world’s systems of oppression and self-aggrandizement. It is a rejection of the world’s lie that our worth is tied to our productivity, status, wealth, or ability to get what we want, so that we can live into the true, God-given self that flourishes in relationship with God and others.
Taking up the cross, too, is not a call to individualistic asceticism, but a public commitment to reject the values of empire: in Jesus’ day, a cross was a tool of public execution for rebels and people who challenged Roman power, so carrying a cross was a declaration that one was willing to face social rejection, loss of livelihood, or even death to stand with Jesus and his call to upend unjust systems.

### Why does this sound liberating to some and offensive to others?
#### Why it sounds liberating:
1. **It releases people from the crushing burden of the world’s impossible standards.** For people exhausted by the modern pressure to perform, achieve, and measure their worth by external markers of success (wealth, status, social media clout, productivity), denying the self that is obsessed with these metrics is radical freedom. It lets people rest in the inherent worth they already have as beloved children of God, rather than having to earn it through endless self-promotion.
2. **It offers an alternative identity for marginalized people.** For people crushed by racial, caste, economic, or gender-based oppression, this call affirms that their worth is not tied to their social status, their ability to produce wealth, or their compliance with the rules of the systems that oppress them. It roots their identity in God’s kingdom, where "the last will be first, and the first will be last" (Mark 10:31).
3. **It frees people from destructive, self-centered patterns.** For people trapped in cycles of addiction, abusive behavior, selfishness that ruins relationships, or consumerism that leaves them empty, denying the self ruled by these impulses is the path to life-giving freedom—for themselves and the people around them.
4. **It promises abundant life through self-giving.** The paradox that "whoever loses his life for my sake and the gospel’s will save it" resonates with people who have found that chasing pure self-interest leads only to emptiness and broken relationships. For people who have experienced the joy of giving themselves to a cause bigger than themselves (fighting for justice, caring for a loved one, serving a community), this promise describes the deep, lasting fulfillment Jesus came to bring.

#### Why it sounds offensive to others:
1. **It directly contradicts hyper-individualistic cultural values.** Modern Western culture elevates the autonomous self as the highest good, teaching that self-interest, self-preservation, and the pursuit of personal happiness are not just acceptable but mandatory. The call to deny the self, to put God and neighbor above one’s own desires, feels like a rejection of these core assumptions, and is often misread as a demand for self-erasure or a rejection of personal autonomy.
2. **It has been weaponized for centuries to justify abuse and oppression.** Religious and political authorities have repeatedly distorted this passage to demand that marginalized people accept their subjugation: enslaved people were told to deny their desire for freedom and submit to their enslavers, abused women were told to deny their own needs and submit to violent husbands, LGBTQ+ people have been told to deny their God-given identity as "selfish" or sinful, and poor people have been told their suffering is their own fault for not denying their "selfish" desire for economic security. For people harmed by these misuses, the passage is deeply offensive, as it is associated with the trauma of being told their inherent worth and legitimate desires are a sin.
3. **It is often misread as a call to passive acceptance of suffering.** For people already crushed by systemic injustice—poverty, racism, state violence, gender oppression—the call to "take up your cross" is frequently distorted into a demand that they accept their suffering as God’s will, rather than fighting for liberation. This misreading ignores that Jesus’ entire ministry was rooted in confronting oppressive systems (healing the sick, feeding the hungry, overturning the temple systems that exploited the poor), and that the cross he calls followers to carry is the cost of standing with the marginalized and challenging the powers that harm them, not a call to grin and bear injustice.
4. **It threatens the comfort of the powerful.** For people who benefit from current systems of empire, capitalism, and patriarchy—those who hold wealth, power, and status through the exploitation of others—the call to deny the self that hoards resources, to share with the poor, and to stand in solidarity with the marginalized feels like a direct threat to their way of life. It is offensive because it calls into question the legitimacy of the power and privilege they have built their lives around.

The tension between these two reactions is inherent to the text: it is a genuinely counter-cultural call that rejects both the world’s dehumanizing values and the distorted religious versions of that call that have been used to harm people. When read in its original context and in light of Jesus’ ministry of justice and love, it is a call to liberation, not oppression.
