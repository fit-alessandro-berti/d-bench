## The Architecture of the Metaphor

The passage works through two distinct images, and each contributes something different to your question:

**The overflow (v. 34):** *perisseuma* — the mouth speaks from the heart's *surplus*, what spills when a vessel is too full to contain its contents. This suggests speech is not so much drawn up deliberately as it is *forced out* by pressure from within. You can manage the tap for a while; you cannot indefinitely manage what's in the tank.

**The treasury (v. 35):** *thēsauros* — a storehouse, exactly your word "stockpile." A treasury implies three things: deposits made over time, an interior hidden from view, and withdrawals that can only consist of what was actually stored. No one speaks off the top of their head; they speak from the warehouse.

Together the images build a causal chain: accumulated deposits → stored contents → pressurized overflow → public expenditure.

## Words as Symptoms

The immediate context makes speech diagnostic. The Pharisees have just attributed the Spirit's work to Beelzebul (v. 24), and Jesus reads that utterance the way a physician reads a symptom — as surface evidence of an underlying condition. Verse 33 makes the epistemology explicit: "the tree is known by his fruit." And verse 36 sharpens it with the "idle word": careless, unguarded speech is the *purest* sample of the heart's contents, precisely because it bypasses the censor. Managed speech can be performance; the unguarded remark leaks the real inventory.

Note the address itself enacts the point: "brood of vipers" evokes the proverbial poison under the serpent's tongue (cf. Ps. 140:3). Jesus diagnoses their hearts *from their venomous words*.

## Words as Revelations

A symptom indicates; a revelation *discloses* something hidden. The storehouse door is closed — until the mouth opens it and shows the inventory to the world. This becomes eschatological in vv. 36–37: words function as self-generated evidence, a transcript each person produces against or for themselves at judgment. Speech doesn't merely hint at the interior; it externalizes it, and God treats the externalization as legally probative. There is a fitting irony here for the Pharisees, who specialized in pious vocabulary: formally religious words drawn from an evil treasury are still evil withdrawals. Goodness of speech is indexed to its source, not its polish.

## Words as Choices — But Upstream

Here the passage is most subtle. The rhetorical question — "*how can ye*, being evil, speak good things?" — introduces a note of real constraint. Speech flows from *being* (πονηροὶ ὄντες, "being evil"), and in the moment of utterance you cannot easily speak against the grain of a well-stocked heart. Yet the treasury metaphor locates freedom precisely there: treasures are accumulated by countless prior deposits — what you attend to, value, rehearse, indulge, forgive, resent. The decisive moral choices happen upstream of speech, in the stocking of the heart. Hence the broader canonical counsel: "Keep thy heart with all diligence; for out of it are the issues of life" (Prov. 4:23). Verse 33 itself — "make the tree good" — holds open that the stock can be changed, but only by changing the tree, not by tying artificial fruit to its branches.

## And a Fourth Category: Deeds

Your three options omit something the passage insists on. The Pharisees' accusation wasn't merely a *sign* of blasphemy — it *was* blasphemy, an act. Words do things: accuse, curse, bless, heal. That is why they carry judgment weight (v. 37). Speech is not epiphenomenal smoke from the inner fire; it is one of the ways the fire burns.

## Synthesis

All three, then, arranged in sequence — plus one:

| Deposit (choices, over time) | → | Contents (character) | → | Overflow (symptom) | → | Disclosure (revelation) | → | Accountability (deed) |

The passage's realism is that by the time a word reaches the lips, most of the moral work has already been done — or neglected. Its hope is that the treasury is not sealed: what is stored can still change, and with it, everything that spills out.
