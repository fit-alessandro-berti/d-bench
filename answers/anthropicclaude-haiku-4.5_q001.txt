# Moral Agency in Genesis 4:6–7

The passage presents a striking picture of moral agency—one remarkably modern in psychological insight, yet framed in archaic language.

## Evil as an External Force with Internal Consequences

The "sin lieth at the door" image is crucial. Sin appears as:
- **A creature or power crouching outside**, not yet internalized
- Something that *desires* Cain ("unto thee shall be his desire")
- Something Cain can still *rule over* ("thou shalt rule over him")

This is distinct from impulse, habit, or temptation in the usual sense. It's portrayed as an *active agent* that responds to human choice rather than merely arising from it.

## The Placement of Responsibility

The passage creates a tension worth noting:

**God identifies the problem before the deed**: Cain's anger and fallen countenance already signal a problem—not moral failure yet, but the *condition* from which it springs.

**Yet responsibility remains entirely Cain's**: The conditional structure ("If thou doest well...") places the outcome squarely in Cain's hands. The divine statement isn't fatalistic; it's diagnostic and hortatory.

## What This Suggests

The moral agent here is:
- **Capable of mastery** ("thou shalt rule over him")
- **Responsible for the threshold moment** before action crystallizes
- **Addressed as someone who can change course**—the warning implies the outcome isn't predetermined

The text seems to locate responsibility not in the *origin* of evil impulse but in the *response* to it at the threshold.
