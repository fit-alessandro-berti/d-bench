Interpretations of Isaiah 55:6–7 vary across Jewish and Christian theological traditions, but prominent shared and contextually rooted perspectives shed light on the pairing of "way" and "thoughts," and their connection to mercy:
### Why both "way" and "thoughts"?
The passage frames human identity and action as holistic, so repentance requires transformation of the whole person, not just one dimension. In the ancient Hebrew context of Isaiah’s writing, a person’s "way" referred to their observable outward conduct: their daily choices, treatment of others, and life patterns that are visible to their community. Their "thoughts" encompassed the inner life that fuels those outward actions: unspoken motivations, selfish desires, harmful biases, and distorted beliefs that shape how they show up in the world. 

Repentance (described here as "returning unto the Lord") cannot be only surface-level: changing outward actions without addressing the inner brokenness that spawned them is incomplete, and feeling inward remorse without changing harmful conduct is hollow. The pairing rejects the idea that someone can earn favor with God through performative good deeds while harboring corrupt inner intentions, or that inner piety alone excuses harm they cause others through their actions.
### Implications for outward conduct, inward life, and mercy
This dual call clarifies that both outward conduct and inward imagination are equally worthy of transformation, and equally covered by the promise of mercy. The demand to forsake both way and thoughts is not a rigid prerequisite to earn God’s pardon, but an invitation to step into the full freedom of the mercy being offered. The passage’s promise that God will "abundantly pardon" confirms that mercy encompasses the full scope of human brokenness: it addresses both the harm caused by outward actions and the flawed inner life that generated that harm.

Many traditions (including Jewish frameworks of teshuvah, or return, and Christian understandings of grace) add that this holistic repentance is not a human achievement alone: God’s nearness (named earlier in the passage) enables people to reorder both their outward choices and inner lives. The call to seek the Lord while he may be found frames mercy as accessible, not out of reach, for anyone willing to embrace whole-person transformation.
