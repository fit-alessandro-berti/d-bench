This passage from Matthew uses a powerful **economic metaphor** — the heart as a storeroom or treasury (*thesauros*) — to make a point about causality and identity. The connection between speech and the “hidden stockpile” in the heart is not casual; it’s presented as a natural, inevitable overflow.

Here’s how it breaks down:

1.  **The heart as a treasury:** A “treasure” is something accumulated over time — through habits, beliefs, affections, and choices. It’s not a passing mood but a settled state.

2.  **Speech as the inevitable overflow:** The Greek verb *ekballō* (to bring forth, to cast out) in verse 35 implies that words are *expelled* from within, like water from a well or treasure from a vault. They aren’t invented on the spot; they are revealed from what is already stored up.

Now, to your specific question — are words symptoms, choices, or revelations? The passage strongly suggests **all three, in a layered way:**

*   **Symptoms:** Yes. Just as a cough can be a symptom of a disease in the body, speech here is a symptom of the moral and spiritual condition of the heart. You can diagnose the storehouse by what comes out.
*   **Choices:** Yes, but indirectly. The “stockpile” itself is built by countless past choices — what one listens to, values, meditates on, and consents to. The immediate act of speaking is also a choice, but the *content* of that speech is drawn from a treasury built by previous choices.
*   **Revelations:** This is the core idea. The speech **reveals** the true nature of the treasury. In confronting the “generation of vipers,” Jesus isn’t just saying their words are bad; He’s saying their words *prove* their inner corruption. The hidden is made manifest.

**In essence, the passage frames a person’s words as the final, inevitable output of a moral economy.** The heart is the hidden factory; words are the product that is shipped out. You can’t get a good product from a corrupted factory, no matter how you package it. This is why Jesus’ rebuke is so cutting — He’s not just commenting on their rude speech; He’s exposing the source.

A contemporary analogy might be a computer’s operating system. Your heart (values, beliefs, affections) is the OS. Your words are the output on the screen. You can try to type good things (make moral choices in the moment), but if the OS is fundamentally flawed, glitches and errors will inevitably reveal the underlying code. True change requires updating the OS.

This is a sobering and diagnostic view of human speech. It shifts the focus from managing external behavior to cultivating the inner life. Would you like to explore how other parts of Scripture treat this theme of the heart as the source of life?
