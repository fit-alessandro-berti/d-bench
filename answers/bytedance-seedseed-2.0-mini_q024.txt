Let's break down the relationship between the old heart vs new heart and external compliance as it appears in the Book of Ezekiel and Old Testament thought:

**External Compliance vs. A New Heart:**
- **External Compliance:** This refers to merely outwardly following God's laws and commands without a corresponding change in one's inner life. It could involve obedience for reasons like fear of punishment, social pressure, or a desire to maintain a certain image. There is no transformation of the heart or motives; the actions are performed without a genuine internal alignment with God's will. For example, someone might keep the Sabbath simply because it's a rule to follow, not out of a personal relationship with God or a desire to honor Him. In the context of the Old Testament, the Israelites often engaged in such acts of external compliance while their hearts remained distant from God.
- **A New Heart:** This is a deep, internal transformation. When God gives a new heart, He changes the very core of a person's being. The desires, motives, and affections of the individual are redirected toward God and His ways. Obedience then flows naturally from this new inner state. It's not just about following rules externally but about having a genuine love for God and a willingness to do what He commands because one wants to, not out of obligation or fear. For instance, a person with a new heart would seek to love God and others not because it's a list of do's and don'ts, but because their heart has been renewed and they desire to live in accordance with God's nature.

**Hardness Described as a Material Condition:**
- In the ancient Near Eastern context and the Old Testament use of the term "heart" (Hebrew: leb), the heart was understood as the center of a person's identity, encompassing their thoughts, emotions, will, and moral character. Describing hardness as a material condition—such as a stony heart—serves to emphasize the severity and resistance to God's influence. Material things like stone are hard, unyielding, and difficult to change unless acted upon by an external force. This metaphor helps convey that spiritual hardness is not a trivial matter but a deep-seated, resistant state that blocks people from receiving God's truth and experiencing His transformative work. Just as a physical stony object cannot easily be shaped or softened without external intervention, a hard heart requires God's power to remove it and replace it with a soft, pliable heart of flesh that can respond to Him. Additionally, in the Old Testament, the replacement of the stony heart with a heart of flesh is often associated with the outpouring of God's Spirit (Ezekiel 36:27), which is an act of divine grace and renewal. **The imagery here suggests that the hardness of the heart is not just a psychological or moral state but a physical-like condition that needs to be transformed by God's power. It's not something that can be fixed by human effort alone; it requires God's intervention to bring about true spiritual renewal.**

In summary, external compliance focuses on outward actions without internal change, while a new heart involves a radical transformation of the inner person where obedience is motivated by love and a desire to please God. Describing hardness as a material condition helps to vividly illustrate the resistance and need for God's transformative power in renewing the heart.
