In Mark 8:34–35, the kind of self being denied is the self that the disciples have been living after being selected by Jesus Christ. This self is characterized by its prior allegiance to worldly desires and the flesh, which is often at odds with the teachings and demands of Christ. It represents an attachment to earthly concerns, personal comfort, and often materialistic, ego-driven behavior. When Jesus speaks of denying oneself, it implies a relinselessness to such things— a renouncement of self-centered existence in favor of a radical self-sacrifice for Christ and the Gospel's sake.

To some, following Jesus involves complete surrender and a letting go of the ego, social standing, and personal comforts, even if it means facing societal disapproval or hardship— it's an act of love and devotion towards God. This is liberating as it fosters spiritual growth, freedom from sin, and transformation towards a more compassionate, altruistic life. However, it could seem offensive or daunting to others who associate with the idea of self-denial in a way that it threatens or contradicts their conventional, comfortable lifestyle or worldly notions of success and fulfillment. The idea of losing one's life for the Christian's gospel can initially seem scary, demanding a complete reevaluation of their values and beliefs, which might feel like a loss. Some might interpret Jesus's teachings as an unfair expectation to risk one's hard-earned security for an uncertain or suffering path. But it is crucial to note that this is a transformative journey, and one doesn’t lose life but gains eternal life and spiritual fulfillment.
