The elder brother's deepest problem is a complex mix of emotions and attitudes, but at its core, it seems to be a combination of pride in obedience and discomfort with mercy itself, with a significant underlying issue of wounded fairness.

1. **Pride in Obedience**: The elder brother highlights his obedience and service to his father, emphasizing that he has never transgressed any commandment. This indicates a sense of pride in his obedience, suggesting that he believes his actions should be rewarded or at least recognized.

2. **Wounded Fairness**: He feels that the father's mercy and generosity towards the prodigal son are unfair compared to how he, the obedient son, has been treated. He points out that he has never been given a kid to make merry with his friends, despite his faithful service, while the prodigal son, who has squandered his inheritance, is celebrated upon his return.

3. **Discomfort with Mercy**: The elder brother's reaction also suggests discomfort with the idea of mercy itself, particularly when it is extended to someone he perceives as undeserving. He struggles to understand why his father would lavish such celebration and forgiveness on a son who has so blatantly disobeyed and wasted his inheritance.

Anger and envy are certainly present, as they are the visible emotions in his initial reaction. However, these emotions seem to stem from a deeper sense of injustice and a misunderstanding of the father's love and mercy. The elder brother's perspective is more legalistic, focusing on deserving and earning love and reward, rather than understanding the father's love as unconditional and generous.

Therefore, while anger, envy, and pride are all elements of the elder brother's response, his deepest problem appears to be a fundamental misunderstanding of his father's nature and the true meaning of love, mercy, and forgiveness.
