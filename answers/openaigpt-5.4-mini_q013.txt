Amos is not rejecting worship because worship is bad. He is rejecting **worship as a cover for injustice**.

In the context of Amos, Israel is doing the outward religious things—feasts, sacrifices, songs, assemblies—but at the same time the society is full of:

- exploitation of the poor
- corrupt courts
- bribery and oppression
- indifference to covenant obedience

So the problem is not “too much religion” in the abstract. The problem is **religion divorced from righteousness**. God says, in effect: *I will not be treated as if ritual devotion can compensate for moral corruption.*

### Why the rejection is so sharp
The language is intentionally severe because the worship itself has become **an insult**. If people offer sacrifices while trampling the vulnerable, the worship is not neutral—it is a form of hypocrisy. It creates the appearance of devotion while preserving the injustice God hates.

Amos is exposing a kind of **spiritual fraud**:

- **Outward piety without inward covenant faithfulness**
- **Public worship while private and social life remain unjust**
- **Sacrificial ritual used as moral laundering**
- **Religious performance replacing repentance**

### “Let judgment run down as waters”
This is the positive alternative: God wants a life where justice and righteousness are not occasional gestures but the prevailing, flowing reality—like an unstoppable stream. In other words:

- not ceremonies without ethics,
- but worship that is matched by justice,
- not sacrifice as cover,
- but righteousness as the fruit of true faithfulness.

### In one sentence
Amos condemns worship here because it is being used as a **religious mask for oppression**—a counterfeit piety that tries to honor God ceremonially while refusing the justice God actually requires.
