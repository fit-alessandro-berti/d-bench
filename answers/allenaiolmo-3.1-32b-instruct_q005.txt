That’s a profound question—one that has echoed through centuries of human thought and spiritual practice. Let’s break it down in light of the passage from Matthew 5:44–45.

**Why is enemy-love so difficult to take seriously?**

1. **Human Nature and Instinct:**  
   Most people are wired for self-preservation and to respond to hostility with defensiveness or retaliation. When someone hurts us, our natural response is to feel anger, fear, or the urge to strike back. Loving your enemy contradicts this instinct. It’s hard because it asks us to override our deepest impulses.

2. **Perceived Weakness or Naïveté:**  
   In many cultures, showing mercy or love to someone who has harmed you can be seen as weakness or foolishness. There’s a fear that such behavior will make you vulnerable to further exploitation.

3. **Social Conditioning:**  
   Societies often reward toughness and punish compassion toward adversaries. We are taught to “get even” or “stand up for ourselves,” not to turn the other cheek.

4. **Emotional Pain:**  
   When we are hurt, our wounds are fresh and real. It feels impossible, or even cruel, to respond with love when we are suffering. The emotional cost seems too high.

---

**Is it sentimental, strategic, or a deeper form of moral strength?**

Let’s consider each:

- **Sentimental:**  
   If enemy-love were merely sentimental, it would be about feeling good or being idealistic. But Jesus’s teaching here is not about feeling warm and fuzzy; it’s about a radical reorientation of action and attitude, even when it hurts. Sentimentality often stops at the surface, but this goes deeper.

- **Strategic:**  
   Some have interpreted this as a strategic move—turning the other cheek can disarm the enemy, create moral high ground, or break the cycle of violence. While there is wisdom in that, the passage’s language points to something more than clever tactics. Jesus frames it as a way to become “the children of your Father which is in heaven,” suggesting a deeper transformation.

- **A deeper form of moral strength:**  
   This is likely the most accurate framing. Enemy-love is not weakness; it is the ultimate test of strength. It requires discipline, courage, and the ability to transcend immediate gratification of anger or revenge. It is a form of moral maturity: to respond to evil with good (see Romans 12:17). It asks us not to suppress our feelings, but to act contrary to them when necessary, out of a conviction that this is the right way to live.

   Jesus points to the example of God, who “makes his sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sends rain on the just and the unjust.” God’s love is indiscriminate and unconditional. To imitate this is not naive or weak—it’s the highest form of strength, because it refuses to be corrupted by evil.

---

**In summary:**
Enemy-love is difficult because it goes against our instincts, our pain, and our cultural conditioning. It is not merely sentimental or strategic; it is a profound moral discipline that reflects the nature of divine love. By loving enemies, we do not become doormats—we become, in the words of Dietrich Bonhoeffer, “the real revolutionaries.” It is a deeper form of strength, rooted in the conviction that love, even for those who harm us, is the highest good.
