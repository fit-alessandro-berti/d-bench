

**Short answer:**  
The verse condemns the *motive* – the desire to be praised by people – not the mere fact that a good deed is seen. Visibility can become dangerous only when it serves (or is sought as) a means of gaining human approval; when the act is done purely for God’s glory, public exposure is not sinful in itself.

---

### Why the craving to be seen is the core problem

| Element in the verse | What it emphasizes | Why it matters morally |
|----------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **“Take heed that ye do not your alms before men,”** | The *action* of giving is placed **before** people (i.e., in a public setting). | The verse does **not** say “do not give alms at all” or “never let anyone see you give.” It merely warns against a particular *way* of doing it. |
| **“to be seen of them”** | The *purpose* or *intention* behind the action: to attract human notice. | This clause names the *motive* that makes the act spiritually vacant. The Greek word *θεάσθαι* (theasthai) means “to look at, to gaze upon, to be observed.” The danger lies in the *desire* to be the object of that gaze. |
| **“otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven.”** | The consequence: loss of divine reward. | If the reward sought is human applause, the heavenly reward is forfeited. The verse ties the moral defect directly to the *goal* of the act, not to its publicity. |

In other words, Jesus is warning against **self‑aggrandizement** disguised as piety. The “show” is merely the outward symptom; the inner disease is the craving for human esteem.

---

### Visibility is not inherently sinful

1. **Jesus’ own public ministry** – He healed, taught, and fed crowds openly (e.g., the Sermon on the Mount, Matthew 5‑7). His deeds were seen by many, yet He never sought personal glory; He consistently pointed the Father’s glory (John 12:28).

2. **Early‑church practice** – Acts 2:44‑45 describes believers sharing possessions “with all who had need,” a practice that was necessarily visible in the communal setting. The apostles commended this generosity (2 Cor 9:7) without condemning its publicity.

3. **Paul’s teaching** – He encourages believers to “let your light shine before others, that they may see your good works and glorify your Father in heaven” (Matt 5:16, echoed in Phil 2:15). Here visibility is *desired* precisely because it leads others to praise God, not the doer.

Thus, the New Testament holds a **dual standard**:  
- **Good works** may be public **provided** the ultimate aim is God’s glory and the edification of others.  
- **Good works** become spiritually empty when the *primary* aim is personal acclaim.

---

### How to discern the danger in practice

| Question | Indicates a healthy motive | Indicates a dangerous motive |
|----------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Why am I doing this?** | To obey God, love neighbor, or meet a genuine need. | To be admired, praised, or to boost my reputation. |
| **Who do I want to notice?** | God (and perhaps those who need help). | Primarily other people, especially those whose opinion I value. |
| **How would I feel if no one saw it?** | Satisfied that the need was met; joy in obedience. | Disappointed, frustrated, or feeling the act was “wasted.” |
| **What would I do if I knew I would never be thanked?** | Still do it, because it’s right. | Refuse or do it half‑heartedly. |

If the answer to the last two questions leans toward the second column, the act is vulnerable to the warning in Matthew 6:1.

---

### Summary

- **The problem is the *craving* to be seen**, i.e., the desire for human approval and praise.  
- **Visibility itself is morally neutral**; it becomes dangerous only when it serves (or is sought as) a vehicle for that craving.  
- A public good deed is virtuous when the heart’s goal is to glorify God and love others, not to collect human applause.  

Hence, Jesus’ admonition calls us to examine our *motives* before we examine our *audience*. When the motive is pure, the act can be done openly without losing the heavenly reward; when the motive is impure, even a secret act would be spiritually fruitless.
