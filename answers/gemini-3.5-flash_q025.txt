Based on Matthew 6:1, the moral danger of public goodness lies not in **visibility itself**, but entirely in **the craving to be seen**—specifically, the motivation behind the action. 

Here is a breakdown of why this distinction is crucial, what makes this craving morally dangerous, and how the rest of the Sermon on the Mount supports this view.

---

### 1. Visibility is Not the Problem (The Context of Matthew 5:16)
We know visibility itself is not the issue because just one chapter earlier, Jesus commands his followers to be visible:
> *"Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."* (Matthew 5:16)

If visibility were inherently sinful, Matthew 5:16 and Matthew 6:1 would contradict each other. The difference between the two verses lies in **the ultimate recipient of the glory**:
* In Matthew 5, goods works are seen so that others **glorify God**.
* In Matthew 6, good works are performed so that the doer is **glorified by men**.

### 2. The Craving to Be Seen: "Theatrical" Righteousness
The Greek phrase translated as "to be seen of them" is *pros to theathēnai autois*. 
* The root word here is *theaomai*, from which we get the English word **"theater."** 
* Literally, Jesus is warning against doing good deeds "to be put on display as a spectacle" or "to be performed for an audience."

When goodness is done for an audience, it ceases to be an act of love or devotion and becomes a performance. The moral danger of this craving manifests in three major ways:

#### A. It Corrupts the Nature of the Virtue
When you give alms to the poor *to be seen*, the needy person is no longer the recipient of your love; they are merely a prop in your self-promotional drama. The act of charity is hollowed out; it is weaponized for social status. What looks like selflessness is actually extreme selfishness.

#### B. It Replaces God with Human Judgment
By seeking the applause of onlookers, the performer makes humanity—rather than God—the ultimate judge of what is good. This shifts the focus of faith from a vertical relationship (the soul and God) to a horizontal transaction (the actor and the audience).

#### C. It Results in a Cheap, Fleeting Reward
Jesus warns that those who do good for public praise "have their reward" (*apechousin ton misthon auton*—a Greek commercial term meaning "paid in full"). The moral danger here is spiritual bankruptcy. By settling for the cheap currency of human admiration, the individual forfeits the eternal, transformative reward of intimacy with "your Father which is in heaven."

### Summary
Public goodness is morally dangerous because **the human heart is highly susceptible to performative piety.** 

Matthew 6:1 does not demand that we hide our good deeds in a bunker; rather, it demands that we audit our motives. The danger is not that people *see* us doing good, but that we *depend* on their sight to validate our worth. True goodness, in the Christian framework, is done for an audience of One.
