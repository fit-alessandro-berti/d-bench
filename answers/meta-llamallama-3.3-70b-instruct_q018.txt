What a rich and thoughtful question! The juxtaposition of bitterness and forgiveness in Ephesians 4:31-32 is indeed striking. Let's break down the possible implications of this pairing:

1. **Feeling vs. Choice**: Bitterness is often an emotional response to perceived injustices, hurts, or frustrations. Forgiveness, on the other hand, is a choice, a deliberate decision to release resentment and let go of the need for retaliation or revenge. By placing bitterness and forgiveness in contrast, the text highlights the idea that we have agency in choosing how we respond to difficult situations.
2. **Habit vs. Mindset**: Bitterness can become a habitual pattern of thinking and behavior, while forgiveness requires a mindset shift. The text encourages readers to "put away" bitterness, implying that it's a habit that can be broken, and to adopt a new mindset characterized by kindness, tenderheartedness, and forgiveness.
3. **Chosen Posture**: The phrase "be ye kind one to another" suggests that forgiveness is not just a one-time decision, but a continual posture or attitude. Bitterness, in contrast, is often a self-focused, inward-turned posture that can lead to further conflict and division. The text invites readers to choose a posture of forgiveness, humility, and kindness.
4. **Contagious Nature**: Bitterness can be contagious, spreading quickly and infecting relationships. Forgiveness, too, can be contagious, creating a ripple effect of kindness, understanding, and reconciliation. By placing bitterness and forgiveness in opposition, the text may be warning against the dangers of allowing bitterness to spread, while encouraging the spread of forgiveness and kindness.
5. **Spiritual dynamic**: In a deeper sense, bitterness can be seen as a manifestation of the "old self" (Ephesians 4:22), while forgiveness represents the "new self" created in Christ (Ephesians 4:24). The text may be highlighting the spiritual dynamic at play, where bitterness is a fruit of the flesh, while forgiveness is a fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:19-23).

In summary, the text treats bitterness as a complex phenomenon that can be a feeling, a habit, a chosen posture, or something contagious. By placing it in direct opposition to forgiveness, the author emphasizes the importance of choosing a posture of kindness, tenderheartedness, and forgiveness, which is rooted in the example of God's forgiveness through Christ.
