The literary structure you've identified is sharp, and it reveals something theologically important: the text does not permit us to remain passive. The movement from *ceasing* to *learning* is not merely rhetorical progression—it is a deliberate assertion about the nature of righteousness.

The Hebrew verb in "learn to do well" (*lamad*) is striking. It implies *acquisition of skill*, the kind of discipline that comes through repetition and practice. This is not instinct; it is formed habit, cultivated character. The prophets elsewhere use this language—*Hosea* 10:12 speaks of "plowing" righteousness, *Isaiah* 32:17 of "the effect of righteousness" as a quiet, practiced habit. Goodness, in this vision, is not the absence of wrongdoing but the *presence* of active virtue.

This resolves your question in favor of the latter option: goodness is not mere restraint. The text makes this explicit in the following imperatives: "seek judgment, relieve the oppressed, judge the fatherless, plead for the widow." These are not negative prohibitions but positive engagements with concrete injustice. To "do well" is to enact justice for the vulnerable—to make one's life structurally oriented toward the flourishing of those the ancient Near Eastern social order most easily discarded.

Theologically, this reflects a robust prophetic understanding: God's covenant demand is not merely ethical non-interference but *active love* expressed in justice. The prophets repeatedly collapse the distance between ritual and ethical obedience (see Isaiah 1:11–17, where God rejects sacrifice while demanding justice; Micah 6:6–8; Amos 5:21–24). Righteousness is relational and behavioral—it is learned, practiced, and directed outward.

So the text answers your question by showing that the negative command ("cease to do evil") is the门槛 (threshold), not the destination. It clears the ground. Then the positive command ("learn to do well") builds something on that cleared ground—something that requires intention, effort, and care for the neighbor. Goodness, in Isaiah's vision, is restless, active, and oriented toward the margins.
