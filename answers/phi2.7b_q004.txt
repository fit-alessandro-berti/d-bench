 The trust being asked for here is blind trust in a higher power. The proverb suggests that individuals should not rely on their own understanding and interpretations of situations, but rather trust in a higher power to guide their paths. This kind of trust requires humility and the acknowledgement that one's own understanding and interpretations may not always be correct or complete.

This is not the same as anti-intellectualism, as the proverb does not dismiss the value of reason or the importance of seeking knowledge and understanding of the world. Rather, it emphasizes that seeking knowledge and understanding should not be the end goal, but rather seeking guidance and direction from a higher power.

In summary, humble self-distrust is the act of acknowledging and accepting that one does not have all the answers, and being open to the possibility that others or a higher power may have greater insights and guidance. Blind trust, however, involves placing complete faith and reliance on a higher power, without question or skepticism.
