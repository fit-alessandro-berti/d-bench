The moral posture required is one of **humble, gentle restoration grounded in honest self-awareness**. Paul is not forbidding correction, but he is forbidding a spirit of superiority, harshness, or condemnation when doing it.

### The required moral posture
- **Restorative, not punitive:** The goal is to “restore” (a term used for setting a broken bone or mending nets). The focus is healing and re-integration, not shaming or excommunicating.
- **Meekness, not severity:** “In a spirit of meekness” means approaching the person with gentleness, patience, and the same underserved kindness one has received from God. Meekness is power under control—strength governed by love.
- **Self-suspicion, not self-righteousness:** “Considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted.” The corrector must maintain a sober view of their own frailty. The danger of temptation is real, and no one stands except by grace.

### Why restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion are kept together
These three are a unified package because they protect both the person being corrected and the person doing the correcting:

1. **Meekness is the only spirit in which true restoration works.** Harsh, prideful correction crushes the fallen (2 Cor. 2:7) and often drives them further away. Meekness mirrors Christ, who restores gently (Matt. 11:29). Without meekness, “restoration” becomes a weapon.

2. **Self-suspicion fuels meekness and makes restoration genuine.** When I remember that I too am capable of the same fall, superiority evaporates and is replaced by empathy. I no longer approach as an accuser but as a fellow beggar showing another where to find bread. This self-awareness keeps the tone gracious and the goal merciful.

3. **Together they guard against the corrector’s sin.** Paul’s warning is sharp: the moment I think I am beyond a particular sin, I am most vulnerable to it. Restoration without meekness is pride; meekness without self-suspicion is shallow sentiment. Keeping them bound together ensures the act of correction remains an act of spiritual health for everyone involved, not a gateway to further temptation.

In short, the only safe posture for correcting another is to do it with the tender hands of a surgeon who knows he has the same disease.
