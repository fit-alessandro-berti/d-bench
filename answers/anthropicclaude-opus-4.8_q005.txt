# Why Enemy-Love Is Hard to Take Seriously

The difficulty isn't accidental—it cuts against several deep human instincts at once.

## Why We Resist It

**It feels like a denial of justice.** When someone wrongs us, our sense of fairness demands a response. Loving the offender can seem to dissolve the moral distinction between victim and perpetrator—as if the wrong didn't matter. We suspect that enemy-love asks us to pretend evil isn't evil.

**It seems to make us prey.** A world where good people love their enemies while bad people exploit them looks like a world rigged in favor of cruelty. The command appears naive about how power actually works.

**It threatens our identity.** Much of who we are is defined against what we oppose. To love the enemy is to lose a clarifying boundary that tells us who we are.

## Is It Sentimental?

No—and the text resists that reading. Notice it doesn't say *feel warmly* toward enemies. It commands *actions*: bless, do good, pray. Jesus is not asking for an emotional surge but for a stance of active goodwill that persists even when affection is absent. Sentimentality is cheap because it costs nothing; this costs everything precisely because the feeling isn't there.

## Is It Strategic?

Partly, but reducing it to strategy distorts it. Yes, enemy-love can disarm hostility and break cycles of retaliation. But the passage gives a different rationale: *"that ye may be the children of your Father."* The justification is not "it works" but "this is what your Father is like." Strategy would abandon the practice the moment it stopped paying off. This refuses that exit.

## The Deeper Reading: Moral Strength

The image of God's sun and rain falling on "the evil and the good" reframes everything. Divine generosity is **indiscriminate**—not because evil and good are the same, but because love is not *rationed according to desert*.

This is the hard core of the teaching:

- **It separates love from merit.** Most of our loving is a quiet transaction—we give affection to those who earn it. Enemy-love severs that link entirely.
- **It requires immense self-possession.** To respond to hatred without being conquered by it demands more strength than retaliation, not less. The reactive person is governed by their enemy; the one who can love is free.
- **It maintains the moral distinction it seems to erase.** Loving the enemy means treating them as a person who could still be otherwise—which actually *takes their moral agency seriously*, rather than reducing them to their worst act.

## The Honest Difficulty

This remains genuinely hard, and it shouldn't be made to sound easy. It is not a refusal to resist injustice, nor a command to remain in harm's way. It is a refusal to let an enemy define what you become.

That is why it reads less as sentiment or tactic than as a demanding form of strength—the capacity to keep the door of one's own humanity open, even toward those who have slammed theirs shut.
