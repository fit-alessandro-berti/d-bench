The passage Matthew 12:34-35 from the Bible presents a profound insight into the relationship between speech and the hidden moral inclinations or "treasure" within a person's heart. It suggests that a person'sinternal moral disposition is reflected or even revealed through their speech. Here's a breakdown that connects speech with hidden moral stockpiles in the context of the options given:

1. Symptoms: Speech can act as symptoms that hint at the underlying moral compass of an individual. When someone consistently produces speech that is harmful or good, it may indicate their true character and inner feelings. Their words are a manifestation of their internal moral state, acting as indicators (symptoms) of their deeper inclinations.

2. Choices: The passage also implies that speech is a choice individuals make. This reflects the idea that people consciously or subconsciously select from the moral resources (treasure) within their hearts, which then determines the nature of the words they use. This choice is influenced by their inherent disposition and moral inclinations, whether good or evil. It is a manifestation of their will and moral principles.

3. Revelations: Lastly, speech can serve as a revelation of one's internal moral "treasure", unveiling their true character or the sincerity of their intentions. Through their words, people can disclose their genuine selves; good intentions will manifest in benevolent speech, while malevolent ones unveil deceitful or harmful motives. Speech is a way of sharing one's inner moral "treasure" with others, exposing it for all to see.

The passage does not limit speech to only one of these functions; it encapsulately suggests that speech embodies all these aspects—it is symptomatic of one'sinternal moral state, is a conscious or subconscious choice based on that state, and is an unveiling of one's true nature to the external world. Through their words, individuals not only exhibit but also actively reveal their accumulated moral virtues or faults. The implication is that the content and tone of our speech serve as a direct line into our inner character, making our communication not just an outward action, but an expressive act of our internal moral wealth (or lack thereof).

To summarize, the passage connects speech and hidden moral stockpiles by considering our words as symptoms that expose our inner moral inclinations, as choices made from the resources of our hearts, and as revelations that bring one's true character to light. Speech is tightly intertwined with the good or evil within us, serving as a multifaceted reflection of our deeper selves. The message of the verse resonates with the idea that we are responsible for the impact of our speech and that it may serve as a beacon of our true nature—reflecting, showing, and sometimes laying bare what resides within our hearts.
