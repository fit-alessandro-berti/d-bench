Short answer: Isaiah 55:6–7 says repentance must touch both how we live (our ways) and what we think and desire (our thoughts). God is ready to forgive when a person truly turns from both outward conduct and inward motives.

Key points

- What “way” means: In biblical language Derek (way) refers to a life-path, a pattern of behavior and choices. To forsake one’s way is to abandon the sinful lifestyle and the habitual acts that define it.

- What “thoughts” mean: The term here points to inner attitudes, plans, and beliefs—the motives and rationalizations that drive us to sin. To forsake one’s thoughts means renouncing the rebellious ideas and self-centered purposes that shape a person’s life.

- Why both are needed: Outward reform without inward renewal is incomplete (and often short-lived). Inner disobedience will undermine external changes, while outward changes without a changed heart may be only superficial. True repentance involves a reorientation of life (actions) and a reorientation of the mind and desires (motives).

- The implication for mercy: God promises abundant pardon to those who turn from both their ways and their thoughts toward Him. The mercy is comprehensive—God forgives not just outward sins but also the inner dispositions that produced them. The gift of mercy is offered to “the wicked” and “the unrighteous,” but it requires turning to the Lord.

- The urgency: The calls to seek the Lord and to call on Him “while he may be found, while he is near” stress that mercy is available now, but the opportunity is not guaranteed forever.

- Broader context hint: Isaiah 55 frames God’s thoughts and ways as higher than ours (55:8–9). The invitation to repentance thus involves aligning both conduct and mind to God’s higher purposes, under the expectation that He will respond with mercy and pardon.

Practical takeaway

- If you’re reflecting on repentance, ask:
  - Are there patterns of behavior you need to abandon (ways)?
  - Are there motives, plans, or beliefs you need to reorient toward God (thoughts)?
  - If you turn to the Lord with both heart and life directed toward Him, you can trust His abundant mercy and pardon.
