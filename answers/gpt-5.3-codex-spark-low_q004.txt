• The passage is asking for relational trust in God as the final authority for direction, not a rejection of thinking.

  ### What kind of trust?

  - Active trust:
      - Depend on God’s character, wisdom, and sovereignty, especially when you must choose or act.
      - Bring your decisions, motives, and outcomes under God’s guidance (“in all your ways”).
      - Be willing to be corrected, led, and redirected.
  - Humility, not stupidity:
      - “Lean not unto thine own understanding” means do not make your private reasoning the ultimate ruler.
      - It calls for epistemic humility: your perspective is partial, fallible, and limited.
      - The command assumes you still think, plan, and discern—just with God as the higher interpretive frame.

  ### Humble self-distrust vs. anti-intellectualism

  - Humble self-distrust (biblical intent):
      - Uses reasoning, but submits it to God’s truth and command.
      - Seeks wisdom from Scripture, community, correction, prayer, and experience.
      - Acknowledges limits and asks for divine guidance.
  - Anti-intellectualism:
      - Rejects inquiry, evidence, or disciplined thought.
      - Treats faith as incompatible with intellect.
      - Can become passive or cynical (“I’ll just believe, not think”).

  ### Practical litmus test

  If this trust makes you more responsible, wiser, teachable, and discerning—it’s faithful humility.
  If it makes you less thoughtful, less accountable, and suspicious of all learning—it has become anti-intellectualism,
  which the verse does not require.
