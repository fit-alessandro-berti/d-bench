

**Answer**

In Ezekiel 36:26‑27 the prophet contrasts two ways God deals with Israel’s rebellion:

| Aspect | What the text says | What it means |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| **External compliance** | “I will put my spirit within you, and cause you to walk in my statutes, and ye shall keep my judgments, and do them.” | God gives the people a *spirit* that enables them to *obey* the law **from the outside** – they are still the same people, but now they are compelled to act in accordance with God’s commands. The change is primarily behavioral: they *do* what the law requires. |
| **Receiving a “new heart”** | “I will give you a new heart, and put a new spirit within you… and I will take away the stony heart out of your flesh, and I will give you a heart of flesh.” | God performs an *inner* regeneration: the hardened, rebellious disposition (the “stony heart”) is removed and replaced by a soft, responsive “heart of flesh.” The change is ontological – the person’s inner nature is transformed so that obedience flows from within, not merely from external coercion. |

### 1. Difference between external compliance and a new heart

|  | External compliance | New heart |
|---|---------------------|-----------|
| **Source of change** | God’s Spirit *enables* outward obedience (the person still has the same heart). | God’s Spirit *replaces* the inner disposition (the heart itself is renewed). |
| **What changes** | Behavior – the person *does* the law because they are compelled to. | Nature – the person *wants* to do the law because their desires are altered. |
| **Biblical illustration** | Ezekiel 36:27 (“cause you to walk in my statutes… keep my judgments”). | Ezekiel 36:26 (“take away the stony heart… give you a heart of flesh”). |
| **Corresponding NT theme** | *Walking in the Spirit* (Gal 5:22‑25) – the Spirit helps us keep in step with God’s moral law. | *Being born again* (Jn 3:3‑8) – receiving a new heart that makes us desire God’s will. |
| **Result** | Temporary or situational obedience; can lapse if the external restraint is removed. | Permanent, internal transformation; obedience persists because the heart itself is renewed. |

### 2. Why “hardness” is described as a material condition that must be replaced

- **“Stony heart” ( לב־אבן, *lev‑even*)** is a metaphor for a heart that is **hard, insensitive, and unresponsive**—like a stone that does not yield to pressure.  
- In the ancient Near Eastern mindset, a stone represented something **impervious, dead, and unable to produce life** (cf. Job 41:15‑16, where Leviathan’s heart is “as hard as a stone”).  
- God’s promise to *“take away the stony heart out of your flesh”* therefore signifies the removal of that **impervious, lifeless disposition** and its substitution with a *“heart of flesh”* ( לב‑בשר, *lev‑basar*), which is **soft, pliable, and capable of feeling and responding**.  
- The image works because the problem with Israel’s rebellion is not merely a failure to know the law; it is a *hardness of the will* that prevents the heart from beating in tune with God. Replacing the stone with flesh changes the *material* of the inner person so that the heart can now *beat* (i.e., feel, desire, and obey) in response to God’s Spirit.  

Thus, the “hardness” is spoken of in material terms because the prophet is illustrating that the obstacle to obedience is not a superficial lapse of behavior but an **intrinsic, stony‑like condition of the heart** that must be **replaced** by a soft, living heart before true, lasting obedience can occur. This mirrors the New Testament teaching that we need a **new heart** (Ezek 36:26; cf. 2 Cor 5:17; Rom 12:2) rather than mere external reform.
