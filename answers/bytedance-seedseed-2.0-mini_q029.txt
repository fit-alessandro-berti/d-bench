Let's break down the key elements of Galatians 6:1 and explore the moral posture required for correction:

### Moral Posture Required for Correction
Galatians 6:1 prescribes a specific and intentional moral posture for correction. Firstly, the person being corrective is described as "spiritual," but this does not imply self-righteousness. Instead, "restore such an one in the spirit of meekness" means the one correcting must approach with humility rather than arrogance. The term "meekness" emphasizes a calm, gentle, and non-condemning attitude. This is crucial because correction done in a spirit of pride or harshness often pushes the offender away rather than leading to restoration. The corrective individual is also commanded to "consider thyself," which fosters a posture of self-awareness and vigilance against the potential of falling into the same fault themselves. This is a form of self-suspicion—an acknowledgement of personal vulnerability, which humbles the corrector and keeps them from acting as if they are above sin.

Why restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion are kept together:
1. **Restoration**: The overarching goal of the correction is to restore the fallen brother or sister, not to shame or abandon them. Restoration is rooted in the church's responsibility to care for one's members and promote unity and redemption within the community.
2. **Meekness**: Meekness ensures the restoration is carried out in a way that speaks truth to the offender without alienating them. Correction without meekness can lead to resentment or defensiveness, hindering genuine healing and growth. Meekness balances correction with empathy, recognizing the humanity of the one who has sinned and the need for grace.
3. **Self-suspicion**: The command to consider oneself is intentionally paired with restoration and meekness to prevent hypocrisy. If the person correcting fails to reflect on their own vulnerability to the same fault, they risk having a self-righteous attitude, which not only undermines the effectiveness of their correction but also potentially leads them to fall into the same sin. Self-suspicion serves as a check on the corrector's heart, keeping their actions motivated by love and humility rather than judgment. 

In summary, Galatians 6:1 teaches that correction should be restorative, humble, and grounded in self-awareness. This holistic approach ensures that correction serves the offender's growth in faith while protecting the spiritual well-being of the community and the integrity of the corrective process.
