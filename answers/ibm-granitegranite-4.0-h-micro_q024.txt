The passage from Ezekiel 36:26-27 speaks about a profound spiritual transformation that God promises to His people. The difference between external compliance and receiving a "new heart" lies in the depth and nature of the change.

**External Compliance vs. Receiving a New Heart:**

1. **External Compliance:** This refers to the act of merely following the rules, laws, or commands without any change in the heart or inner disposition. It is akin to going through the motions, performing actions as required, but without any genuine understanding, motivation, or transformation. Such compliance can be superficial and may not lead to genuine spiritual growth or a change in one's character. It is often driven by fear of punishment or desire for reward, rather than a genuine love for God or a desire to live according to His will.

2. **Receiving a New Heart:** This represents a profound, internal transformation where God replaces a "stony heart" (a heart that is hard, unyielding, and resistant to change) with a "new heart" (a heart that is soft, pliable, and open to change). This new heart is characterized by a genuine desire to please God, a willingness to obey His commands out of love and conviction, and a transformed disposition towards others. It is a change that comes from within, driven by a renewed spirit and a renewed relationship with God. This transformation is not merely about external actions but about a change in the very nature of the individual, enabling them to walk in God's statutes and keep His judgments.

**Why is Hardness Described as a Material Condition?**

The description of hardness as a material condition is a metaphorical way to convey the depth and nature of the spiritual condition of the people. Here are a few reasons why this metaphor is used:

1. **Immutability:** Hardness is a material property that does not change easily. It signifies something that is resistant to change, unyielding, and stubborn. In the context of the human heart, this metaphor suggests that the people's spiritual condition was deeply entrenched and resistant to change. They were bound by their own sinful nature, which made them resistant to God's call for repentance and transformation.

2. **Symbol of Spiritual Condition:** Just as a stone heart is hard and unyielding, the metaphor suggests that the people's hearts were spiritually hardened. They were spiritually dead, unable to feel the love of God, unable to discern His will, and unable to respond to His call for repentance. This hardness symbolizes a lack of spiritual vitality and a disconnect from God.

3. **Need for Transformation:** The metaphor underscores the need for a radical transformation. Just as a stone must be broken or replaced to allow for new growth, the people's hearts needed to be replaced with a new, softer, and more pliable heart. This transformation is not merely a superficial change but a complete overhaul of the spiritual condition, enabling them to walk in God's statutes and keep His judgments.

4. **Contrast with Spiritual Renewal:** The contrast between hardness and a new heart highlights the difference between mere external compliance and true spiritual renewal. The hardness represents the status quo, where people are merely going through the motions of religious observance without any genuine change in their hearts. The new heart, on the other hand, represents a genuine spiritual renewal, where the individual is transformed from within, enabling them to live according to God's will and purpose.

In summary, the difference between external compliance and receiving a "new heart" lies in the depth and nature of the change. The hardness described in the passage is a metaphorical representation of a spiritually hardened condition that needs to be replaced with a new, soft, and pliable heart, symbolizing a profound transformation that comes from God's grace and the indwelling of His Spirit.
