Jesus tells you the target up front:

> he spoke this parable to some **who trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others**.

So the Pharisee is not condemned simply for:

- believing in fasting,
- tithing,
- or even thanking God.

Those are not the problem in themselves. The problem is the whole spiritual posture behind them.

## What condemns the Pharisee?

### 1. **Self-trust**
The clearest issue is that he **trusts in himself that he is righteous**.

He is in the temple, but his prayer is basically self-approval. He names his moral record as the ground of his confidence. Even though he says “God, I thank thee,” the substance of the prayer is: *I am not like them; I do more; therefore I stand right.*

So yes, **pride** is central.

### 2. **Comparison as a basis for righteousness**
He measures himself **against other sinners**:

- not extortioners,
- not unjust,
- not adulterers,
- not even “this publican.”

That is spiritually false because righteousness before God is not established by outperforming other people. Comparison lets him feel clean without actually standing honestly before God.

So yes, **comparison** is part of the condemnation.

### 3. **Contempt**
The text explicitly says he **despised others**. That matters a lot.

His problem is not merely private self-satisfaction. His righteousness needs an inferior class beneath him. He does not just confess his obedience; he uses another person as a dark background to make himself shine.

So yes, **contempt** is directly condemned.

### 4. **Virtue used as self-display**
His fasting and tithing are real virtues, but he turns them into a performance of self. The issue is not virtue, but **virtue as self-exaltation**.

He is not presenting himself to God for mercy; he is presenting his résumé.

So yes, **the use of virtue as self-display** is also part of the problem.

### 5. **Not mainly false doctrine in the narrow sense**
If by “false doctrine” you mean some formally incorrect theological statement, that is not the main emphasis here. Jesus does not say, “The Pharisee held the wrong proposition.” He exposes a **false spiritual stance**: self-righteousness.

That said, there is a deeper doctrinal error embedded in it: he misunderstands how a person is right before God. He acts as though human moral achievement can be possessed and displayed as grounds for justification.

So: **not primarily doctrinal error in form, but a doctrinally distorted heart**.

## Why is the tax collector’s posture spiritually truer?

Because he stands before God in **truth**.

### 1. **He does not evade judgment**
He says:

> God be merciful to me, a sinner.

He does not compare himself with someone worse. He does not offset guilt with achievements. He names his need directly.

That is spiritually true because before God, the first truth is not “I am better than others,” but “I need mercy.”

### 2. **His bodily posture expresses inward reality**
He stands afar off, will not lift his eyes, and beats his breast. These are signs of repentance, shame, and grief over sin. Jesus is not praising mere external gestures, but gestures that match the heart.

The Pharisee’s body is upright and composed; the tax collector’s posture is broken. The latter is truer because it corresponds to his real condition before God.

### 3. **He appeals to mercy, not merit**
This is the decisive contrast.

- The Pharisee comes with credentials.
- The tax collector comes with need.

And Jesus says the tax collector goes home **justified**. Why? Because justification is received by humble appeal to God’s mercy, not secured by self-display.

### 4. **Humility is closer to reality than pride**
The tax collector may be morally worse in public reputation, but he is spiritually more truthful because he is not pretending self-sufficiency. Humility is not just a nice attitude here; it is **accurate vision**.

He sees:
- God is holy,
- I am a sinner,
- I need mercy.

That is reality. The Pharisee, by contrast, sees himself through a flattering distortion.

---

## In short

The Pharisee is condemned **chiefly for self-righteousness**—a combination of:

- **pride**,
- **self-trust**,
- **comparison with others**,
- **contempt for others**,
- and **turning virtue into self-exalting display**.

The tax collector is spiritually truer because he stands before God without illusion, without comparison, and without claims—only with repentance and a plea for mercy.

That is why **he**, and not the Pharisee, goes home justified.
