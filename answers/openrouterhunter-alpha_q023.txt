This is an excellent observation. The distinction between "way" and "thoughts" in Isaiah 55:7 is profound and intentional. It reveals that biblical repentance (*teshuvah* in Hebrew) is a holistic, 180-degree turn—not a superficial adjustment, but a transformation of both our **behavior** and our **identity**.

Let’s break it down:

### 1. "Let the wicked forsake his **way**" – Outward Conduct & Life Direction
- **"Way"** (Hebrew: *derekh*) signifies one’s habitual course of life, chosen path, and outward behavior.
- To forsake one’s *way* means to abandon a lifestyle of injustice, selfishness, or rebellion against God’s design. It’s a change in direction—what you **do** and where you’re **going** in life.
- This addresses **conduct**—the tangible, observable pattern of choices that harm oneself, others, or one’s relationship with God.

### 2. "And the unrighteous man his **thoughts**" – Inward Imagination & Mental Framework
- **"Thoughts"** (Hebrew: *machashavah*) refers not just to fleeting ideas, but to one’s plans, schemes, inner reasoning, and even the secret imaginations of the heart.
- To forsake one’s *thoughts* means to dismantle the **internal architecture** that justifies or produces the wicked *way*. It’s to reject the self-centered logic, the prideful strategies, the hidden resentments, or the distorted desires that fuel outward sin.
- This addresses **character**—the root system beneath the fruit. It’s not enough to prune the branches (behavior) if the root (thinking, desiring, imagining) remains poisonous.

### What This Implies About Repentance
1. **Integrity of Action and Intention**: God rejects a repentance that is only behavioral compliance without inner transformation ("these people honor me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me," Isaiah 29:13). Conversely, we cannot claim inner devotion while clinging to a sinful lifestyle. True repentance aligns the inner self with the outer self under God’s reign.
   
2. **The Depth of Mercy’s Requirement**: The call to abandon both *way* and *thoughts* shows that God’s mercy is not a cheap pardon that ignores the depth of human brokenness. It addresses the **whole person**—the sinner as an agent (what they do) and as an author (what they plot and imagine). Mercy, therefore, meets us at the core of our rebellion.

3. **The Possibility of Transformation**: The very invitation implies that people *can* forsake both their way and their thoughts. It speaks to God’s power to renew the mind (cf. Romans 12:2) and redirect the life. Mercy here is not just forgiveness but enabling grace for change.

### The Promise of Mercy
The result of this holistic repentance is staggering: **“He will have mercy… for He will abundantly pardon.”**
- **“Abundantly pardon”** (Hebrew: *yirbeh lisloach*) is intensive—the pardon is multiplied, overflowing, excessive.
- Why such lavish mercy? Because the repentance described is not a transaction (“I’ll stop doing this if you forgive me”), but a **return to relationship**—“let him return to the LORD.” God’s mercy flows not because we’ve perfected our ways or purified all our thoughts, but because we have turned to Him in fullness—acknowledging that both our conduct and our inner life need His redemption.

In short, Isaiah 55:7 teaches that **real repentance is a surrender of the whole self—the visible life and the hidden imagination—and God’s response is a mercy that meets that totality with an abundance that overwhelms both our deeds and our doubts.**
