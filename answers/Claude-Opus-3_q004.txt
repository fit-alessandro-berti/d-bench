This passage from Proverbs 3:5-6 calls for a deep, wholehearted trust in God rather than relying solely on one's own understanding. However, we need to distinguish this trust from naive anti-intellectualism.

The trust being advocated here is not a rejection of reason, learning or critical thinking. After all, the book of Proverbs itself is full of exhortations to seek wisdom and understanding. Rather, it is a humble acknowledgment of the limits of human understanding compared to the wisdom and knowledge of God. 

The phrase "lean not on your own understanding" warns against intellectual pride and self-sufficiency. It cautions that human reason alone is not enough to navigate life's complexities. Our perspective is always limited and prone to blind spots.

However, this is not a call to switch off one's mind and blindly follow religious precepts. True faith is not irrational or anti-intellectual. God has given us the faculty of reason and expects us to use it. But reason must be coupled with a reverent, teachable spirit that looks to God for ultimate guidance.

The key is the attitude of the heart - is it self-reliant and proudly independent, or humbly submitted to God, acknowledging Him in all our ways? Humble trust is not a resignation of the intellect, but a recognition that all of our faculties, including the mind, are to be submitted to God and reliant on His wisdom.

In summary, the passage advocates a posture of intellectual humility and daily dependence on God's guidance, not a rejection of human learning and reason altogether. Rightly understood, faith and reason go hand in hand, with human understanding operating within the larger framework of trust in an all-wise God. It is self-reliant pride, not the intellect itself, that this proverb warns against.
