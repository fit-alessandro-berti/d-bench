Isaiah 55:6–7 presents repentance as something deeper than stopping a few bad actions. It reaches both **“way”** and **“thoughts.”**

## 1. “Way” = outward conduct
The **way** is a person’s path, manner of life, behavior, habits, and chosen course.  
So when the text says, **“Let the wicked forsake his way,”** it means repentance includes a real turning from sinful practice.

This implies:
- repentance is not merely regret,
- not just words,
- not just religious feeling,

but an actual change in how one lives.

## 2. “Thoughts” = inward life
The **thoughts** refer to the inner world: motives, desires, imaginations, intentions, plans, and attitudes.  
Thus **“the unrighteous man his thoughts”** shows that sin is not only external. It begins and lives within.

This implies:
- God cares about the heart, not only visible actions,
- a person may leave certain outward sins while still clinging inwardly to pride, lust, bitterness, unbelief, self-justification, or rebellious desire,
- true repentance must include inner renewal, not merely behavioral reform.

## 3. Why both are mentioned
Because human sin has both:
- an **external expression** (“way”),
- and an **internal source** (“thoughts”).

The “way” is often the fruit; the “thoughts” are the root.  
If only the outward way changes, repentance can be superficial.  
If someone claims inward sorrow but does not forsake his way, repentance is unproven.

So the passage calls for a whole-person turning:
- from sinful deeds,
- from sinful desires and reasoning,
- back **unto the Lord**.

## 4. What this says about mercy
The striking thing is that this severe call to repentance is joined to a large promise:  
**“and he will have mercy upon him… for he will abundantly pardon.”**

That means:
- God’s mercy is available even to those whose lives and hearts are both corrupted,
- no one is excluded because his sin has been both inward and outward,
- the call to forsake both way and thoughts does not close the door of hope; it opens it.

In fact, the breadth of repentance matches the breadth of pardon.  
God asks for a real turning, but he promises a rich forgiveness. The sinner is not told merely to improve, but to **return**—and God receives the returning sinner with mercy.

## 5. The deeper implication
This passage suggests:
- outward morality alone is insufficient,
- inward sincerity alone is insufficient,
- repentance is a return of the whole person to God.

And mercy is not reluctant or small.  
The phrase **“abundantly pardon”** means God forgives beyond what sinners expect. His mercy is greater than both the evil **way** and the evil **thoughts** that must be forsaken.

In short:  
Repentance involves both **way** and **thoughts** because sin is both outward and inward. Therefore true repentance must be both behavioral and heart-deep. And the promise is that the one who thus returns to the Lord will find not hesitation, but abundant mercy.
