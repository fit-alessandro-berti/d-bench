The elder brother’s deepest problem is **a fundamental misunderstanding of grace**, which manifests as a prideful, legalistic view of his relationship with the father. He sees his obedience as a commodity that entitles him to reward, and he resents mercy because it undermines his sense of earned fairness. 

While anger, envy, and wounded fairness are surface emotions, they stem from a core issue: he does not see himself as a beloved son who already possesses everything, but as a dutiful servant who expects recognition. His pride in obedience blinds him to the father’s joy in redemption and makes him uncomfortable with mercy itself—mercy that violates his transactional worldview. 

Thus, the deepest problem is **a self-righteous heart that cannot rejoice in grace**, and that prefers to exclude rather than welcome the repentant.
