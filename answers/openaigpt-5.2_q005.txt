Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it collides with several of our strongest moral instincts at once—instincts that are not all “bad,” but that easily harden into something smaller than what Jesus is commanding.

## Why it’s so hard

1. **We equate love with warmth or approval.**  
   If “love” means affection, attraction, or endorsement, then “love your enemy” sounds either dishonest (“I don’t feel that”) or morally confused (“should I approve of harm?”). In Matthew 5, love is closer to *willing the good* of the other—actively seeking their true good under God—without pretending their evil is good.

2. **Our sense of justice wants repayment.**  
   Enmity triggers a powerful moral logic: wrongs demand payback, at least in the form of withholding goodwill. Enemy-love feels like letting the guilty off the hook. But Jesus isn’t abolishing justice; he’s forbidding *personal vengeance and dehumanization* as the way we “balance the books.”

3. **Fear and self-protection are real.**  
   When someone has harmed you, openness can feel unsafe. Enemy-love can sound like “keep yourself exposed.” But love doesn’t require naïveté. You can pray for an enemy and still set boundaries, seek protection, and pursue lawful accountability. Love is not the same as access.

4. **Resentment can feel like moral clarity.**  
   Anger and contempt can masquerade as righteousness. They may start as a protest against wrong, but they easily become an identity: “I’m the kind of person who never forgives *that*.” Enemy-love threatens that identity by insisting the enemy remains human and still answerable to God.

## Is it sentimental?

Not in the way Jesus frames it. The rationale is not “be nice” but “that you may be children of your Father… for he makes his sun rise on the evil and on the good” (vv. 44–45). That’s not sentiment; it’s *imitation of God’s indiscriminate beneficence*.

Enemy-love is often **emotionally cold at first**—more like fidelity to a moral decision than a feeling. It may begin as refusing to curse, refusing to repay evil with evil, refusing to delight in their downfall, and choosing to pray. None of that requires sentimental affection.

## Is it strategic?

It can have strategic effects, and Jesus likely expects them:

- It **breaks cycles of retaliation** that keep communities trapped.  
- It **exposes injustice** by refusing to mirror it.  
- It can **disarm** an opponent, not by manipulation but by removing the expected fuel of hatred.

But if it’s only strategy—“I’ll love so they change”—it becomes a technique of control. Jesus’ command isn’t contingent on the enemy’s response. The point is faithfulness to the Father’s character, not guaranteed outcomes.

## Is it a deeper moral strength?

Yes—enemy-love is a form of strength because it requires at least three hard things at once:

1. **Strength to name evil without becoming it.**  
   Love doesn’t mean calling harm “fine.” It means resisting the temptation to let another’s sin author your own (through vengeance, cruelty, or contempt).

2. **Strength to remain free inside.**  
   Hatred chains you to the enemy; your inner life starts orbiting their offense. Enemy-love is a refusal to hand over that control. It’s not excusing; it’s refusing to be possessed by the injury.

3. **Strength to seek the enemy’s true good.**  
   The “good” you will for them is not necessarily comfort; it may be repentance, restraint, and justice. Praying for an enemy can mean praying they be stopped, exposed, and changed—because that is genuinely good for them and for their victims.

## Why Jesus links it to being “children of your Father”

In the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus isn’t merely raising the ethical bar; he’s describing a family resemblance. The Father gives life-sustaining goods (sun, rain) even to those who misuse them. Enemy-love is acting in a way that reflects that same generosity: not because the enemy deserves it, but because God is like that—and his children learn to be like him.

So enemy-love isn’t sentimental softness or mere social technique. It’s **a refusal to let evil set the terms of your heart**, and a deliberate participation in the Father’s way of being good in a world where goodness is often not returned.
