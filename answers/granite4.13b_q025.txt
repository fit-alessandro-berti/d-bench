The passage from Matthew 6:1 highlights a subtle yet profound moral issue concerning the intention behind acts of charity and almsgiving. The phrase "Take heed that ye do not your alms before men" suggests two interrelated concerns:

1. **Visibility (Public Display):** The first concern is about how these charitable actions are performed—specifically, whether they are done to be seen by others rather than out of genuine altruistic intent. When good deeds are performed publicly with the primary aim of garnering praise or admiration from people, there exists a risk that the motivation for such acts may become corrupted. This public display can turn altruism into a kind of showmanship, where the focus shifts from helping those in need to impressing others and receiving external validation.

2. **Craving to Be Seen (Intent Motivation):** The second concern is related but distinct: it's about the underlying craving or motive—to be recognized, appreciated, or praised by people—rather than a pure desire to serve others for their intrinsic benefit. This craving can distort the purpose of charitable giving from being selfless and compassionate toward an act that seeks personal gratification or social affirmation.

In essence, both visibility itself and the accompanying craving to be seen contribute to what Matthew identifies as morally dangerous—one turning altruism into performance art, and the other transforming benevolent intent into a quest for external recognition. The verse cautions against these tendencies by emphasizing the importance of sincerity in acts of charity, urging that such actions should stem from faithfulness and humility rather than from a desire for people's approval or admiration.
