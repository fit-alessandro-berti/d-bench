The power praised here is **kenotic** power—self-emptying, cruciform power. It is not the power to subjugate, impress, or coerce, but the power to *descend*, to relinquish, to absorb violence without returning it. Paul describes a figure who possesses the absolute status of divinity—“the form of God,” equality with God—yet treats that status not as a possession to be guarded, but as a resource to be poured out. The power on display is perfected not in conquest but in obedience, not in triumph but in the shame of a slave’s death. It is servant-power: greatness that has become small in order to rescue, not to rule.

This kind of humility joined to greatness is more unsettling than open domination for at least three reasons.

**First, it dissolves our categories.** Open domination is legible. We understand a tyrant; we know how to read a throne, a sword, a threat. We can locate ourselves in that story—as victims, collaborators, or rebels. But a King who empties himself into the form of a servant short-circuits the social grammar of power. He is neither the oppressor we fear nor the weakling we pity. He occupies a paradoxical position that our hierarchies cannot process, and that cognitive dissonance feels uncanny. We are confronted by a strength that looks like weakness, which makes the world feel unstable.

**Second, it is morally invasive.** A despot judges you from above with laws and punishments; you can hate him and feel righteous. But humility joined to greatness judges you from below. When the highest imaginable being takes the lowest place, He does not accuse you—He exposes you. The light of that humility reveals the desperation with which we cling to our own reputations, rank, and control. It is far more comfortable to be dominated by someone obviously corrupt than to be loved by someone perfectly humble, because the former allows us the alibi of victimhood, while the latter convicts us of our own pride and demands that we *imitate* Him. The text begins, “Let this mind be in you.” This power is contagious; it replicates itself in the observer. That is terrifying.

**Finally, it overturns the economy of force.** Open domination can be met with counter-force. But how do you fight a sovereignty that surrenders? How do you defeat a ruler who refuses to grasp at His rights, who absorbs your murderous violence into His own body and answers it with obedience unto death? It is unsettling because it is *irresistible in a way that force is not*. It does not play by the rules of the game; it ends the game. The cross is humanity’s attempt to extinguish this inconvenient greatness, yet the hymn implies that this very extinguishing is the means by which God redeems. Such power is too deep, too backward, too free to be managed by human fear. It does not dominate us; it unsettles us because it comes to *change* us.
