While the elder brother exhibits all the traits mentioned—anger, envy, and a sense of wounded fairness—his deepest problem, according to the theological heart of the parable, is **a lack of relational sonship.** 

Essentially, he is a "lost son" who never left the house. His struggle can be broken down into three core dimensions:

### 1. The "Employee" Mindset (Pride in Obedience)
The elder brother’s own words betray how he views his father: *"Lo, these many years do I **serve** thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment."* 

The Greek word for "serve" here is *douleuo*, meaning "to serve as a slave." He does not see himself as a son enjoying an inheritance; he sees himself as a compliant laborer earning a wage. His deepest problem is that he believes his father’s love and resources are things to be **earned** through performance rather than **inherited** through relationship. Because he operates on a merit-based system, he is offended by grace.

### 2. Discomfort with Mercy (The Heart of the Pharisee)
Contextually, Jesus told this story to the Pharisees who were grumbling about Him eating with sinners (Luke 15:1-2). The elder brother’s discomfort with mercy is actually a rejection of the Father’s character. 

By refusing to go into the feast, he is separating himself from his father just as much as the younger brother did by going to the "far country." He is "out" while the sinner is "in." His problem is that he values **justice over restoration**. To him, a restored brother is a threat to the moral order, whereas to the father, a restored son is the only thing that matters.

### 3. Isolation in the Midst of Abundance
The father’s response identifies the elder son’s tragic blind spot: *"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine."*

The elder brother complained that he was never given a "kid" (a young goat) to celebrate with his friends. The father points out that the brother already owned the "fatted calf." He was living in spiritual poverty while surrounded by wealth. He was so focused on his "duty" and his brother’s "sin" that he never actually enjoyed his father's presence.

### Conclusion: Which is the "Deepest" Problem?
The deepest problem is **discomfort with mercy itself.** 

Anger, envy, and pride are merely the symptoms. The root cause is a heart that is fundamentally aligned with **Law** rather than **Love**. He cannot rejoice in his brother's life because he has built his entire identity on being "better" than his brother. If the father is merciful to the "unworthy," then the elder brother’s "worthiness" becomes irrelevant—and that is the one thing his ego cannot handle. 

**He would rather stayed outside in the cold with his "fairness" than go inside and share his father's joy.**
