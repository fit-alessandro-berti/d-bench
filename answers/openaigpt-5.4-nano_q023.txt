In Isaiah 55:6–7, repentance is framed as a turning in two directions: **away from one’s “way”** and **away from one’s “thoughts.”** The point is that genuine repentance is not merely behavior-change, but a whole-life redirection.

### 1) “Way” = outward conduct (patterns you practice)
“Way” points to the *course of life*—what a person repeatedly does: habits, actions, moral conduct, lifestyle. Asking the wicked to “forsake his way” means they must stop doing what they have been doing, not just feel bad about it.

### 2) “Thoughts” = inward imagination and moral reasoning
“Thoughts” includes the inner life that generates actions: plans, desires, rationalizations, imaginations, and the way someone thinks about God, people, and what they take to be right or desirable. “Forsake…his thoughts” implies repentance reaches the *sources* of behavior—not only the results.

### 3) Repentance therefore involves both action and inner motives
By naming both, the text implies:
- **Outward conduct without inward change is incomplete.** You can stop some behaviors while still harboring the same corrupt mindset and intentions.
- **Inward change is not merely private feelings.** If the “thoughts” are truly forsaken, they will eventually produce different “ways.” The inner and outer cannot be cleanly separated.

In other words, repentance is both **moral reform** (new actions) and **spiritual transformation** (a new way of thinking that leads to those actions).

### 4) Mercy is still offered—because the turn must include the “whole person”
The promise—“he will have mercy upon him… abundantly pardon”—means God’s forgiveness is not limited to people who have already cleaned up their outward life. The call is precisely to those who are still described as wicked and unrighteous.

But it’s also not cheap grace: the “return unto the Lord” is portrayed as a real reorientation that involves:
- leaving the **wrong path** (“way”),
- disowning the **wrong mental pattern** (“thoughts”),
- and turning back to God.

So mercy is possible because God welcomes repentance; and repentance is described as whole-direction change—**outward conduct shaped by outward action, and inward imagination shaped by inward renewal**—not one without the other.
