In the flow of Jesus’ argument, it’s doing **both**—but with a clear emphasis on **treasure directing (training) the heart** rather than merely “diagnosing” it.

### 1) The grammar points to the heart *following* the treasure
Matthew 6:21:

> “For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also.”

In Greek the verb is future: **“there your heart will be”** (ἔσται). That future sense naturally reads as: *the heart goes after the treasure.* In other words, what you treat as “stored up” and secured becomes the place your inner life gravitates toward.

That doesn’t exclude “reveals,” but it makes “forms/directs” the main thrust: **choose where you put treasure, and your heart will move there.**

### 2) “Treasure” here is not just what you own; it’s what you *store up*
Jesus is not simply saying, “If you have money, your heart is bad.” The key verbs are:

- “Lay up” / “store up” (θησαυρίζω): *to stockpile, to bank, to invest for the future*.

So the issue is not mere possession, but **what you are treating as your security, your payoff, your future, your “safe place.”** That’s why moth/rust/thieves matter: earthly treasure is fragile and ultimately unsecured.

### 3) Treasure ties possession, attention, and love together
Jesus links them because “treasure” functions as an organizing center for life:

**a) Possession → attention**  
If you store wealth on earth, you must watch it, maintain it, protect it, worry over it, upgrade it. Earthly treasure *demands* attention because it is vulnerable (“moth… rust… thieves”). Your mind keeps returning to what can be lost.

**b) Attention → affection (love)**  
What you repeatedly attend to tends to become what you care about. Biblically, “heart” isn’t only emotions; it’s the center of desire, will, and loyalty. If your attention is continually pulled toward your earthly storehouse, your affections and priorities settle there.

**c) Affection → deeper investment (more treasure)**  
Once the heart is there, you invest more there—time, money, planning, risk, anxiety, identity—creating a reinforcing loop.

So “treasure” is the hinge that binds together:
- **what you accumulate**
- **what you monitor**
- **what you love**
- **what you become loyal to**

### 4) How “reveal” fits without being the main point
Jesus’ statement also works diagnostically: if you want to know what your heart is attached to, look at where you are “laying up”—what you are actually banking on.

But the structure of the passage is primarily exhortational:
- **Don’t** store up on earth.
- **Do** store up in heaven.
- **Because** your heart will follow what you store up.

So it’s not merely “your treasure shows your heart,” but “your treasure **sets the direction** of your heart.”

### 5) The surrounding context confirms it’s about loyalty and worship
A few verses later Jesus says:

> “You cannot serve God and mammon” (6:24)

That clarifies the theme: treasure isn’t neutral; it tends to become a master. “Heart follows treasure” is another way of saying: **what you treat as ultimate security becomes the object of your loyalty.**
