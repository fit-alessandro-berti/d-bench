The short answer is that the text suggests treasure **reveals** the heart, but the context of the Sermon on the Mount implies that treasure **trains** the heart.

To understand how these concepts interact, we must look at the mechanics of the verse: how possession, attention, and love coalesce.

### 1. Treasure Reveals the Heart (The "Diagnostic" View)
Verse 21 is a diagnostic statement: *"For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also."* 

In this sense, the text treats the heart as a secondary follower. It posits that human beings are fundamentally "value-seeking" creatures. We do not choose a treasure and then decide to love it; rather, we instinctively gravitate toward what we value, and our hearts inevitably settle where we have invested our time, energy, and resources. 

Seen this way, your checkbook and your calendar are not the *causes* of your heart's orientation; they are the *evidence* of it. If you want to know what you truly love, you simply look at where your "treasure" has been deposited. The treasure acts as a mirror.

### 2. Treasure Trains the Heart (The "Formative" View)
While the verse functions as a diagnostic, the command in verses 19–20 ("Lay not up... but lay up") functions as a discipline. 

If Jesus commands us to store treasure in heaven, he is acknowledging that placing value in a certain domain has a gravitational pull on the human soul. By intentionally investing in "heavenly" things (charity, righteousness, justice, eternal relationships), we are engaging in a spiritual discipline. 

In this view, the heart is not a static object that simply reveals itself; it is a muscle that is formed by the environment in which it is placed. If you "invest" in earthly things (consumerism, ego, material security), your heart is trained to become anxious, greedy, and fragile (subject to moth, rust, and thieves). If you "invest" in heavenly things, your heart is trained to become eternal, generous, and secure.

### 3. The Trinity of Attachment: Possession, Attention, and Love
The text weaves three components together into a feedback loop:

*   **Possession (The Treasure):** This is the physical or metaphorical object of your pursuit. The text identifies "treasures" as tangible manifestations of our goals.
*   **Attention (The "Breaking Through"):** Whether it is the thief obsessed with stealing or the person obsessed with securing their wealth, treasure demands constant surveillance. You cannot look at your treasure without your attention being occupied by its vulnerability (moth, rust, thieves). Whatever you possess claims a share of your mental bandwidth. 
*   **Love (The Heart):** In biblical thought, the "heart" is the command center of the will and affections. 

**The Synthesis:**
The text argues that **there is no such thing as neutral possession.** 

Because human attention is finite, the act of "laying up" treasure creates a tether. The possession requires attention, and that steady, repetitive attention creates an emotional bond (love). Therefore, if you possess earthly things, you must pay them attention, and because you pay them attention, you will inevitably grow to love them. 

The tragedy, Jesus warns, is that you have chained your heart to things that are structurally incapable of surviving (moth/rust). You are training your heart to love a dying thing, which ensures that your own heart will eventually experience the same "corruption" as the treasure it is tied to. 

**Conclusion:** 
The text says treasure **reveals** your current attachments, but it serves as a command to **train** your heart by changing your investments. You are essentially being told to "re-anchor" your heart by moving your treasure, so that your heart follows the treasure into a realm where both can be preserved.
