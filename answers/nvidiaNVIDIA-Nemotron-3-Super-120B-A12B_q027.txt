

**Short answer:**  
The Pharisee is condemned chiefly by **pride – the self‑exalting, works‑righteous attitude that turns his prayer into a display of his own virtue**. This pride inevitably produces the other sins mentioned (he compares himself with others, despises the tax‑collector, and treats his religious observance as a merit to be boasted about).  

The tax‑collector’s posture is judged “more true” because it is the opposite: a humble, broken acknowledgment of sin and a total reliance on God’s mercy rather than on any personal achievement.

Below is a step‑by‑step explanation of why pride is the core fault, how the other options flow from it, and why the tax‑collector’s stance is the biblical model of true prayer.

---

## 1. The text (Luke 18:9‑14)

| Verse | Content (ESV) |
|-------|---------------|
| 9‑10  | Jesus tells the parable “to some who trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and looked down on everyone else.” |
| 11‑12 | The Pharisee’s prayer: “God, I thank you that I am not like other men… I fast twice a week; I give tithes of all I possess.” |
| 13    | The tax‑collector, standing far off, will not even lift his eyes to heaven, but beats his breast and says, “God, be merciful to me, a sinner.” |
| 14    | Jesus concludes: “I tell you, this man went down to his house justified rather than the other… For everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, and the one who humbles himself will be exalted.” |

The key contrast is **self‑justification vs. self‑emptying**; the moral of the story is stated in v. 14.

---

## 2. Why “pride” is the decisive fault

| Option | How it relates to the text | Why it is *not* the primary cause |
|--------|----------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **False doctrine** | The Pharisee’s theology implies that righteousness is earned by fasting, tithing, and moral superiority. This is a *works‑righteous* view, which is indeed erroneous. | The parable does not condemn him for holding a wrong *belief* per se; it condemns the *attitude* that flows from that belief—namely, his self‑exaltation. Jesus could have rebuked a doctrinal error directly (cf. Matt 23:23), but here the focus is on the heart’s posture. |
| **Pride** (self‑exaltation, self‑righteousness) | The Pharisee begins his prayer with *“I thank you that I am not like other men…”* – a classic formula of self‑praise. He then enumerates his religious deeds as grounds for merit. The whole prayer is an act of **self‑justification** (Greek *dikaioō* “to declare righteous”) that relies on his own performance. | This is the core sin Jesus highlights: pride leads to every other fault listed. |
| **Comparison** | He explicitly compares himself with “extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even as this publican.” | Comparison is a *symptom* of pride: when you think you are superior, you naturally measure others against yourself. The parable does not say comparison is evil in itself (Paul uses comparison positively in 1 Cor 4:6), but here it follows from a proud heart. |
| **Contempt / despising others** | The introduction notes that the Pharisee “looked down on everyone else.” His prayer ends with a subtle disparagement of the tax‑collector. | Contempt is the *outward expression* of an inward pride; it is the social manifestation of the same self‑exalting attitude. |
| **Use of virtue as self‑display** | He fasts, tithes, and mentions these deeds as reasons for God’s favor. | This is another way of naming the prideful motive: he turns genuine religious practices into a *badge* of self‑worth. The problem is not the acts themselves (fasting and tithing are commended elsewhere) but the *purpose* behind them—self‑glorification. |

**Conclusion:** All the other descriptors (false doctrine, comparison, contempt, virtue‑as‑self‑display) are *effects* or *manifestations* of the underlying pride (self‑exalting, works‑righteousness). The parable’s punchline—*“everyone who exalts himself will be humbled”*—points directly to pride as the root sin that brings divine opposition.

---

## 3. Why the tax‑collector’s posture is “more true”

1. **Humility before God** – He “stood far off,” would not lift his eyes to heaven, and *beat his breast* (a culturally recognized sign of deep contrition, cf. Joel 2:12‑13; Psalm 51:17). His posture acknowledges his own unworthiness without trying to earn merit.

2. **Confession of sin** – His petition is *solely* for mercy: “God, be merciful to me, a sinner.” There is no self‑justification, no list of good works, no comparison with anyone else.

3. **Dependence on grace** – He throws himself entirely on God’s compassion. This aligns with the biblical pattern of *faith* that receives justification (see Romans 3:23‑24; Ephesians 2:8‑9).  

4. **Jesus’ explicit verdict** – In v. 14 Jesus says the tax‑collector “went down to his house justified.” The Greek verb *dikaioō* is used here in the passive sense: God declared him righteous *not* because of his deeds but because of his humble, repentant heart.

Thus, the tax‑collector exemplifies the opposite of the Pharisee’s fault: *humility* (the antidote to pride) and *reliance on divine mercy* (the antidote to works‑righteousness).  

---

## 4. Theological take‑away

- **Pride (self‑exalting, works‑righteous) is the sin that God opposes** (James 4:6; 1 Peter 5:5).  
- **True prayer** is marked by honest confession, dependence on God’s mercy, and a heart that does not seek to earn favor by religious performance.  
- The parable does *not* condemn fasting or tithing per se; it condemns *using* those disciplines as a foundation for self‑justification.  
- The tax‑collector’s attitude is the model for all who would be justified: “God, be merciful to me, a sinner” (cf. the Publican’s Prayer in the early church, the Jesus Prayer, and the continual refrain of the Psalms).

---

### Bottom line
- **What condemns the Pharisee?** His **pride**—the self‑exalting, works‑righteous attitude that turns his religious observance into a display of personal merit. This pride inevitably produces false doctrine, comparison, contempt, and the misuse of virtue as self‑display.  
- **Why is the tax‑collector’s posture truer?** Because it expresses humility, honest confession of sin, and total dependence on God’s mercy— the very opposite of pride and the posture that Jesus declares leads to justification.
