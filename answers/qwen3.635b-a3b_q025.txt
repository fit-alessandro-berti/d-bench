The verse condemns **the craving to be seen, not visibility itself**. The moral danger lies in the *purpose* behind the act, not its public nature.

**1. The Text Points to Motive, Not Mere Visibility**  
The Greek behind “to be seen of them” is a purposive clause (`πρὸς τὸ κατακριθῆναι αὐτούς` / `πρὸς τὴν δόξαν τῶν ἀνθρώπων` in broader Synoptic parallels), meaning “*in order that you may be noticed/praised by them*.” Jesus is not warning against public goodness per se, but against performing it as a transaction for human approval. Visibility is merely the vehicle; the craving for recognition is the infection.

**2. The Historical-Moral Mechanism**  
In the ancient honor-shame culture, being “seen” was synonymous with gaining social capital. When alms-giving becomes a performance, it shifts from an act of love or obedience to a bid for status. The moral danger is therefore **idolatry of the self**: the practitioner exchanges divine commendation for earthly applause, making human praise the functional idol. The act is still outwardly good, but inwardly bankrupt.

**3. Why Visibility Isn’t the Problem**  
Jesus elsewhere explicitly endorses public visibility when rightly ordered:  
> “Let your light shine before others, that they may see your good works and glorify your Father in heaven.” (Matt 5:16)  
The difference is the *direction of glory*. Public goodness becomes dangerous only when visibility is pursued as the end, not the means. When the craving to be seen replaces love of God and neighbor as the driving motive, the moral integrity of the act collapses.

**4. The Loss of “Reward”**  
The forfeiture of heavenly reward doesn’t stem from being observed, but from *trading* a spiritual reward for an earthly one. As Jesus elaborates in the following verses (6:2–4), the hypocrites “have received their reward in full” because they consciously chose human recognition over God’s approval. The moral danger is ultimately a displacement of worship: the heart bows to the crowd instead of to the Father.

**In short:** The verse identifies the *craving to be seen* as the moral danger, not visibility itself. Public goodness is only perilous when it is instrumentalized for self-glorification. When visibility serves love and divine obedience, it remains virtuous; when it becomes the goal, it becomes spiritual bankruptcy.
