This passage from the Sermon on the Mount is widely considered one of the most radical and demanding ethical teachings in history. It strikes at the very core of human instinct.

To answer your question, we must look at why this command is so counter-intuitive, and then distinguish between the three categories you proposed: sentiment, strategy, and strength.

### 1. Why is enemy-love so difficult to take seriously?

The primary reason we struggle to take this command seriously is that it contradicts our fundamental biology and our innate sense of justice.

**The Biological Barrier:**
Evolutionarily, human beings are wired for "reciprocal altruism" and tribalism. We are built to protect our kin and repel threats. When someone hates or persecutes us, the amygdala triggers a "fight or flight" response. "Loving" the threat feels like a betrayal of our survival instinct. To take enemy-love seriously requires overriding the most primal parts of our brain.

**The Justice Barrier:**
Most human concepts of justice are retributive. We believe in balance: if you inflict pain, you "deserve" punishment. Loving an enemy feels like an imbalance of the moral scales; it feels as though the perpetrator is "getting away with it." To love an enemy feels like condoning their evil, which violates our deep-seated need for wrongs to be righted.

**The Definition Barrier:**
We often confuse "love" with "affection." If we interpret Jesus' command as a demand to have warm, fuzzy feelings toward a torturer or an oppressor, the command is impossible—and arguably pathological. Because we assume love is an emotion we cannot control, we dismiss the command as an idealistic exaggeration rather than a practical ethic.

### 2. Is it sentimental, strategic, or a deeper form of moral strength?

The text itself offers clues that help us categorize this kind of love.

**Is it Sentimental?**
**No.** Sentimentality relies on feelings—fondness, pity, or sympathy. But Jesus commands specific *actions*: "bless," "do good," "pray."

In the Greek context of the New Testament, the word used for love here is *agape*. Unlike *philia* (brotherly affection) or *eros* (romantic desire), *agape* is not a feeling; it is a volition. It is a determined seeking of the other’s good regardless of how we feel about them. You cannot command an emotion, but you can command an act of the will. Therefore, Jesus is not asking his followers to "like" their enemies or to pretend they aren't dangerous; he is asking them to will the good of those who wish them harm. To view this as sentimental is to misunderstand the nature of the command.

**Is it Strategic?**
**Partially, but not primarily.**
There is a strategic element to enemy-love. Martin Luther King Jr., a student of this very text, famously noted that returning hate for hate multiplies hate, adding "deeper darkness to a night already devoid of stars." Strategically, enemy-love breaks the cycle of vengeance. It disarms the oppressor and can shame them into repentance (as seen in non-violent resistance movements).

However, verse 45 suggests the motive is not merely strategic: *"That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven."* The logic here is one of **imitation**, not utility. We do not love enemies because it works; we love enemies because that is what God is like. If the love were only strategic, it would cease if the enemy refused to change. The text demands a love that persists regardless of the outcome.

**Is it a deeper form of moral strength?**
**Yes.** This is the ultimate answer the text provides.
Most moral systems operate on a balance sheet: Good for good, evil for evil. This is the moral standard of the world, and it requires a certain amount of strength to maintain fairness.

However, Jesus describes a "higher righteousness." It requires a terrifying amount of moral strength to absorb injustice without returning it. The image of the sun rising "on the evil and on the good" portrays a God who is not reactive. God does not withdraw his gifts (sun and rain) based on human behavior. He is constant, generous, and sovereign.

To love an enemy is to refuse to let the enemy dictate your behavior. If you hate those who hate you, you have become a mirror of your enemy. If you love them, you break the chain. This is a form of moral strength that asserts: *Your evil is not powerful enough to turn me into an evil person.*

### Summary

Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it demands we abandon the ledger of justice we use to navigate the world.

It is **not sentimental**—it does not require us to feel affection.
It is **not merely strategic**—it is not a manipulation tactic, but a reflection of divine character.
It is **a profound form of moral strength**—the strength to imitate the Creator by refusing to let the "unjust" determine the limits of our love. It is the power to will the good of the other, even when the other is wielding a sword.
