This is an excellent and crucial question. The teaching in Luke 6:37 is often misunderstood as a call to moral relativism or a blanket rejection of all evaluation. In reality, it draws a profound distinction between two fundamentally different postures: **discernment** and **condemnation**.

Let’s explore the difference.

### 1. The Posture of the Heart: Humility vs. Pride
*   **Discernment** arises from a place of **humility**. It begins with the recognition that "I, too, am flawed and in need of grace." It seeks to understand truth, assess situations, and navigate relationships wisely. It can acknowledge sin or error without deeming the person irredeemable. Its goal is **restoration** (Galatians 6:1).
*   **Condemnation** (the "appetite to condemn") arises from a place of **pride**. It creates a hierarchy where the judge sits in a position of moral superiority. Its goal is often **exclusion, punishment, or self-elevation**. It writes a person off, focusing on their failure as their defining identity.

### 2. The Object of Focus: The Act vs. The Person
*   **Discernment** can evaluate **actions, ideas, or consequences**. We discern that theft is wrong, that a policy is unjust, or that a behavior is harmful. This is not only permitted but necessary for healthy communities and moral living. The verse does not say "Do not evaluate anything."
*   **Condemnation** judges **the person**. It moves from "This act was wrong" to "You are worthless/irredeemable." This is the "judging" and "condemning" that Jesus forbids, because it usurps a divine prerogative (Romans 12:19) and destroys the possibility of mercy.

### 3. The Desired Outcome: Redemption vs. Retribution
*   **Discernment**, when coupled with love, desires the **redemption and growth** of the other person. Like a doctor diagnosing a sickness to provide a cure, it identifies a problem to help solve it.
*   **Condemnation** often desires **retribution or vindication**. It feels a satisfying sense of justice in the other’s punishment or downfall. It says, "They got what they deserved."

### 4. The Relationship to Grace: Recognition vs. Rejection
This is the core of Luke 6:37. The mechanism Jesus outlines is one of **reciprocal grace**: "the measure you give will be the measure you get."
*   If your life is governed by an **appetite to condemn**, you place yourself outside the flow of grace. You declare a system of pure justice for others, and that very system will be applied to you—a terrifying prospect, as "all have sinned" (Romans 3:23).
*   If your life is governed by **discernment tempered with forgiveness**, you actively participate in the economy of grace. You acknowledge your own need for it, which enables you to extend it. This doesn't make moral judgments disappear; it makes them **humble, sorrowful, and ultimately oriented toward healing**.

**In summary: Moral judgments absolutely matter. We are constantly called to discern right from wrong, truth from falsehood, wisdom from folly.**

What Jesus cautions against is the **heart posture of the condemner**—the one who judges with a sense of gleeful superiority, who writes people off, who sees others' failures as affirming their own virtue.

**A good test is this:** Does my discernment lead me to pray for this person, to feel sorrow over the brokenness, and to seek a path toward restoration? Or does it lead me to gossip, to write them off, to feel a sense of righteous satisfaction in their downfall?

The former is the work of a disciple. The latter is the "judging" and "condemning" from which we are warned to abstend. Discernment is a tool for wisdom; condemnation is a weapon of the ego.
