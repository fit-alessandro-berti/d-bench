Luke 6:37 is often cited as a demand for moral relativism—the idea that we should never evaluate the actions of others. However, in the context of the broader New Testament and the specific language used, this verse does not abolish moral standards. Instead, it addresses the **posture of the heart** and the **usurpation of divine authority.**

The difference between "judging" (as forbidden here) and "discernment" (as encouraged elsewhere) lies in the distinction between evaluating a fruit and sentencing a soul.

### 1. Discernment vs. The "Judge’s Bench"
The Greek word for "judge" in this context is *krinete*, which can refer to the act of passing a final judicial sentence. 

*   **Discernment** is the ability to distinguish between right and wrong, truth and falsehood. Jesus actually commands discernment later in the same chapter when he says, "Every tree is known by its own fruit" (Luke 6:44). To know that a tree is producing "bad fruit," one must make a moral evaluation.
*   **Judging (Condemnation)** is the attempt to sit in the seat of God. It is the move from saying "That action is harmful" (discernment) to saying "That person is worthless and beyond redemption" (condemnation). 

### 2. The Appetite to Condemn
Luke 6:37 specifically targets the **appetite for superiority.** The "judge" in this verse is someone who finds satisfaction in the failures of others because it reinforces their own sense of righteousness. 

*   **Discernment is outward-looking:** It seeks to protect the community, uphold truth, and help the offender return to the right path. 
*   **Condemnation is inward-looking:** It seeks to elevate the self by pushing the "other" down. 

When we have an *appetite* to condemn, we are looking for reasons to exclude and punish. When we use discernment, we are looking for the truth so that we may navigate life according to God’s will.

### 3. The Mirror of Mercy
The second half of the verse ("forgive, and ye shall be forgiven") provides the key to the first half. Jesus is establishing a principle of **reciprocity.** 

If moral judgments never mattered, forgiveness would be unnecessary. You only forgive what you have first identified as a wrong. Therefore, Jesus assumes we will recognize sin. However, he demands that our recognition of sin in others be filtered through the recognition of our own need for mercy.

Moral judgments matter because they define the boundaries of the Kingdom of God; however, they become toxic when they are divorced from the humility of the "forgiven sinner."

### 4. The Goal: Restoration vs. Destruction
The ultimate difference lies in the **goal of the evaluation**:
*   **The Condemner** evaluates in order to write a final chapter. The goal is the "sentence"—the social or spiritual disposal of the person.
*   **The Discerner** evaluates in order to bring healing. Like a doctor "judging" a disease in a patient, the goal is not to hate the patient, but to identify the illness so that health (forgiveness/restoration) can be applied.

### Summary
Luke 6:37 does not suggest that "anything goes." Rather, it warns that **how we measure Others is how God will measure Us.** If we use morality as a weapon to destroy others (condemnation), we invite that same destruction upon ourselves. If we use morality as a guide for our own lives while extending the same mercy to others that we hope to receive (forgiveness), we fulfill the Law of Christ.
