John isn’t playing word-games here but is pointing out a very practical, even empirical, test of whether “my love for God” is real or just talk.  A few observations help us see what he’s exposing:

1.  You can’t directly observe someone’s inner feeling toward an invisible Being.  
    •   My claim “I love God” is private, subjective, unverifiable.  
2.  You *can* observe how I treat my neighbor, whom I see every day.  
    •   Do I feed the hungry, forgive the irritating, show compassion to the weak?  
3.  Jesus himself tied love of God to love of neighbor: “A new commandment I give you… that you love one another” (John 13:34); “Inasmuch as you did it to one of the least… you did it to me” (Matt. 25:40).  
4.  So 1 John 4:20 uses the visible, testable reality of neighborly love as the only concrete evidence we have for genuine love of the unseen God.  

What self-deception is at work?  

–  Hypocrisy: I proclaim deep devotion to God while nursing bitter feelings toward people I actually see and meet.  
–  Spiritual vanity: I flatter myself that my lofty affections are so “spiritual” they need no earthly proof.  
–  Moral compartmentalization: I separate “religion” (what I say in church) from “real life” (how I behave next door).  

John’s point:  if your love of God doesn’t change the way you treat your brother or sister—if it doesn’t lead you to acts of kindness and forgiveness—you have deceived yourself.  You’re holding up an empty claim.  The only honest measure of your love for an unseen God is your love for the people you do see.
