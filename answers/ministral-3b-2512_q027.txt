In **Luke 18:9–14**, the Pharisee’s condemnation lies primarily in **pride, self-righteousness, and the misuse of virtue as self-display**—though elements of **comparison and contempt** also play a role. Here’s a breakdown of the key issues and why the tax collector’s posture is spiritually superior:

### **1. What Condems the Pharisee?**
The Pharisee’s sin is **self-centered righteousness**—he presents himself as superior not because of genuine humility but because he **uses his good deeds as a shield against others’ sins**. His prayer is not a humble confession but a **boastful declaration of his own righteousness**, which Jesus explicitly condemns:

- **False Doctrine (Implied but Not the Core Issue):**
  While the Pharisee’s external practices (fasting, tithing) were often misused to justify pride (e.g., John 9:24, where he refused to acknowledge Jesus’ authority), the **core problem is not his specific religious practices but his heart’s attitude**. The tax collector’s posture is not about whether he tithed or fasted but about his **relationship with God**.

- **Pride & Self-Righteousness:**
  The Pharisee’s prayer is a **self-aggrandizing performance** ("I thank thee... that I am not like others"). His righteousness is **external and performative**, not a heart transformed by God. Jesus says he was **"justified"** (declared righteous) *not* because of his deeds but because he **recognized his sinfulness** (cf. Rom. 3:23, 6:23).

- **Comparison & Contempt:**
  - He **compares himself to others** ("not as other men are")—this is a sin of **self-comparison**, which the Pharisee does not escape (e.g., he assumes his deeds make him superior).
  - He **despises the publican** (implicitly, by his boastful posture), treating him as inferior. The tax collector, however, **avoids judgment of others** (he doesn’t even look up).

- **Virtue as Self-Display:**
  The Pharisee **uses his good works as a weapon against others** ("I am not like them"), making his righteousness **dependent on his own performance** rather than God’s grace. The tax collector, in contrast, **abandons all self-righteousness** and **confesses his sin**—not to impress God but to receive mercy.

### **2. Why Is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?**
The tax collector’s posture reflects **true repentance and humility**, which Jesus highlights because it **contrasts with the Pharisee’s hypocrisy**. Key reasons:

- **He Recognizes His Sinfulness:**
  The tax collector does **not** claim to be better—he **confesses his guilt** ("God be merciful to me a sinner"). This is **genuine repentance**, not self-justification.

- **He Avoids Self-Righteousness:**
  Unlike the Pharisee, he **does not compare himself to others** or **use his deeds as a shield**. He simply **asks for mercy**, trusting God’s grace.

- **His Posture Is Humble & Broken:**
  - He **stands far away** (symbolizing his distance from God’s presence).
  - He **smites his breast** (a gesture of deep sorrow, not pride).
  - He **does not look up** (he does not seek God’s approval but receives it passively).

- **Jesus’ Teaching on Justification:**
  The Pharisee was **"justified"** (declared righteous) **not by his deeds but by God’s grace**—because he **recognized his need**. The tax collector, though a sinner, **received mercy** precisely because he **abandoned his self-righteousness**.

### **3. Theological Implications**
This passage teaches that **true righteousness is not about external deeds or self-comparison but about a heart transformed by God’s grace**. The Pharisee’s sin was **missing the mark of humility**—he thought his good works made him acceptable, while the tax collector **realized his unworthiness and trusted in God’s mercy**.

**Key Scripture References:**
- **Romans 3:23** – *"All have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."*
- **Romans 4:5** – *"To the one who works, wages are not credited as a gift, but as an obligation."*
- **Luke 18:14** – *"For everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, and the one who humbles himself will be exalted."*

### **Conclusion**
The Pharisee is condemned for **pride, self-righteousness, and the misuse of virtue as self-display**, while the tax collector’s posture is spiritually superior because it **embodies true repentance, humility, and trust in God’s mercy**. Jesus’ point is not about the tax collector’s specific sins (though he was a sinner) but about **the heart’s posture**—whether one seeks to **justify themselves or receive grace**.
