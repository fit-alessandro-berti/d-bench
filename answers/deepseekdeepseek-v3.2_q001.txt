In Genesis 4:6–7, God addresses Cain after he has become angry and downcast because his offering was not regarded as favorably as Abel’s. These verses present a compact but profound picture of moral agency and the nature of sin.

**1. Picture of moral agency**
God begins by asking Cain two rhetorical questions, implying that Cain is responsible for his own emotional state (“Why are you angry? Why is your face downcast?”). This immediately frames Cain as a moral agent capable of self-reflection and choice.

The heart of the statement—“If you do well, will you not be accepted? And if you do not do well, sin lies at the door”—presents moral life as a binary of **doing well** versus **not doing well**.  
Agency lies in the act (“do”), and the consequence (“accepted” or not) follows from the quality of the action. God affirms that acceptance is available if Cain chooses rightly, removing any sense of predetermined rejection.

**2. Nature of evil depicted**
The text describes sin (חַטָּאת, *chatta’t*) as something that “lies at the door” (רֹבֵץ, *rovetz* — often translated “crouching”). This image is animal-like—a lurking, predatory presence waiting at the threshold of opportunity.  
Evil is portrayed here as **an external force or beast-like impulse** that seeks to overpower Cain, yet it is also tied to his own actions (“if you do not do well…”). Thus, sin is both a **temptation from outside** (something at the door) and **activated by one’s choices**.

**3. Responsibility and mastery**
God says: “Its desire is for you, but you must rule over it.” This echoes the language used of Eve’s desire for her husband in Genesis 3:16, suggesting a struggle for domination.  
Sin “desires” to control Cain, but **the responsibility and capacity to master it rest squarely with Cain**. The text places the weight of moral victory on human will—the person is called to exercise self-rule.

**Conclusion**
Moral agency in this passage is depicted as a conscious choice between doing well and doing wrong, with the outcome depending on that choice. Evil is personified as a crouching beast at the door—an impulse or temptation that arises when one “does not do well,” but it is not an unconquerable fate. Responsibility is explicit: though sin desires to dominate, **the human must and can rule over it**.  
The text rejects passive victimhood and affirms that, despite sin’s lure, humans retain the power and obligation to choose rightly.
