The hinge of the whole passage is one easily missed verb: **"learn."**

"Cease" can be obeyed in a moment. It is a single act of will — stop, desist, drop it. But the Hebrew *limdu heiteiv* ("learn to do well") comes from the root *l-m-d*, the same root that gives us *talmid*, "disciple." Isaiah doesn't say *do* good; he says be *apprenticed* into it. Goodness here is durative — something acquired through instruction, repetition, failure, and habit. You can cease evil by an act of renunciation; you can only do well by a process of formation.

**Why the negative comes first.** The order isn't arbitrary. Verse 15 has just explained why God won't hear their prayers: "your hands are full of blood." You cannot plead the widow's cause while your own hands are implicated in her affliction. Ongoing evil is an active obstruction — it has to be cleared before anything can be built. But notice what that implies: clearing the ground is not the harvest. A person who merely stops doing evil has produced a vacancy, not a virtue. The text refuses to treat the moral life as subtraction.

**What "doing well" actually consists of.** The four imperatives of verse 17 are telling — *seek justice, relieve the oppressed, judge the fatherless, plead for the widow*. Every one is social, particular, and directed at people with no power and no advocate. "Seek justice" (*dirshu mishpat*) even borrows the verb normally used for seeking *God* — Isaiah quietly relocates the object of religious pursuit from the sanctuary to the courtroom. And "judge the orphan," "plead for the widow" are skilled acts: investigating a case, securing a fair verdict, arguing on behalf of someone who cannot argue for herself. This is not sentiment. It requires knowing *who* is vulnerable and *what* their situation is — competence in other people's lives, not just management of one's own.

**The larger frame.** Verses 11–15 have God sick of sacrifices, incense, festivals, and prayers. So the "washing" of verse 16 is not a ritual ablution but an ethical one — Isaiah is redefining purity itself. The movement from *cease* to *learn* is the passage's whole theology in miniature: true religion is not the absence of defilement but the presence of justice.

So to your question directly: the text clearly rejects goodness as mere restraint. Restraint is necessary and preconditional — but it is the doorway, not the house. The biblical writers return to this two-beat rhythm constantly: "Depart from evil, and do good; seek peace, and *pursue* it" (Psalm 34:14) — as if peace runs from you and must be chased. Amos gives the same structure: "Hate the evil, and love the good, and establish judgment in the gate" (5:15). Even Aristotle, from a very different direction, lands on the same insight: we become just by doing just acts. Virtue is a formed capacity, not a default state we revert to once evil is removed.

There's an implicit anthropology in that little word "learn": human beings do not drift naturally into justice once their hands are stilled. Goodness has to be taught, practiced, and sustained — which is why the prophets repeat themselves, and why communities of formation matter.

The distinction, finally, is this: restraint keeps your hands off other people; learned goodness puts your hands to work *for* them. A man who never harms a widow but never pleads her cause has obeyed verse 16 and failed verse 17. James, writing a commentary on Isaiah without naming it, preserved exactly this: "Pure religion and undefiled before God... is this, To visit the fatherless and widows in their affliction, *and* to keep himself unspotted from the world" (James 1:27). Both halves. But the visiting — the learned, practiced, seeking love — is the point toward which the unspottedness is aimed.
