The text moves from "cease to do evil" to "learn to do well" to convey that true righteousness involves more than just refraining from wrongdoing. The prophet Isaiah is highlighting the difference between negative holiness (avoiding evil) and positive holiness (actively pursuing good).

The initial command to "cease to do evil" (v. 16) emphasizes the importance of stopping harmful behaviors. However, this is only a starting point. The follow-up command to "learn to do well" (v. 17) suggests that true goodness involves actively engaging in righteous actions.

This progression from restraint to practice implies that goodness is not merely the absence of evil, but a proactive and practiced form of love and justice. It requires effort, dedication, and a commitment to doing what is right. The specific examples provided in verse 17 – seeking judgment, relieving the oppressed, judging the fatherless, and pleading for the widow – illustrate the kinds of actions that embody this positive holiness.

In essence, the text suggests that goodness is not simply a matter of not doing bad things, but of actively working towards the well-being and flourishing of others, particularly the vulnerable and marginalized. This understanding of goodness as a practiced form of love and justice is a recurring theme throughout the Bible and is central to the prophetic message of Isaiah.
