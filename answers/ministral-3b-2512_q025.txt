In **Matthew 6:1**, the danger of public goodness lies not so much in the *visibility* itself as in the **motivation behind it**—specifically, the **craving for human approval, praise, or recognition**. Here’s why:

1. **The Problem is the *Purpose*, Not the *Action***
   - The verse warns against doing good deeds *for the sake of being seen* (i.e., to gain human admiration). The act of almsgiving or charity is not inherently wrong, but the *intent* behind it is. Jesus is not condemning the act of giving but the *motive* of seeking external validation.
   - The Greek word for "seen" (*eidō*) here refers to being *observed* by others, which can easily become a means of self-promotion rather than a response to God’s call.

2. **The Danger of Human Approval**
   - The warning is that if your goodness is done *for men* (to be seen, praised, or rewarded by them), you are not truly seeking the reward of God. The Father in heaven does not reward *human* recognition but the *heart* of the giver.
   - This aligns with Jesus’ broader teaching in the Sermon on the Mount (e.g., **Matthew 5:16**)—true righteousness is not about being noticed by others but about being a light that *shines for God’s glory*.

3. **The Contrast: God’s Hidden Reward**
   - The alternative is to do good *without fanfare*, trusting that God sees and rewards the heart (cf. **Psalm 145:18-19**). The Father’s reward is not in the applause of men but in His own knowledge of a sincere heart.

### **Key Takeaway**
The moral danger is **not the act of being seen**, but the **desire to be seen**—i.e., the shift from serving God to serving human approval. Jesus is calling believers to align their motives with His, ensuring their goodness is done *for God’s sake alone*, not for the praise of men.
