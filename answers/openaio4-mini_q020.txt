Bitterness in Hebrews 12:15 is pictured as a “root” rather than a single “act” to stress three things:  

1. Hidden moral growth  
   – A root begins unseen below the surface. Bitterness often starts as a small, private resentment or woundedness in the heart. Left unchecked it draws nourishment from every disappointment or injustice you nurse. By contrast, a one-time angry outburst is obvious, easy to identify and address.  

2. Delayed and deepening damage  
   – A root may lie dormant for a season then suddenly send up shoots that choke other plants. So bitterness can lie under the surface of your soul for weeks or months before it erupts in harsh words, coldness, judgmentalism or depression. And because it goes down deep—feeding on pride, unmet expectations or self-pity—it is harder to pull out than to deal with a single wrong action.  

3. Contagious defilement  
   – Many wild plants share the same rootstock underground. A single toxic root can poison an entire patch. In the same way, one person’s unchecked bitterness pollutes a church family or workplace. Gossip, suspicion and cynicism spread from heart to heart, defiling relationships long before anyone realizes where it began.  

By calling it a root, Hebrews warns us not just to trim off the visible fruit of bitterness (e.g. harsh words, sulking) but to dig down—to confess hurts, forgive offenders, seek God’s healing—so that the poison plant never springs up at all.
