**The Pharisee is condemned primarily by prideful self-righteousness—trusting in his own righteousness—expressed through comparison, contempt, and the use of genuine virtues as self-display and self-justification. False doctrine is not the issue.**

Jesus frames the parable explicitly in verse 9: it is spoken “unto certain which trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others.” The concluding principle in verse 14 reinforces this: “everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, but the one who humbles himself will be exalted.” The text itself identifies the core problem as self-trust in one’s righteousness combined with contempt for others.

### The interconnected failures in the Pharisee’s posture
- **Pride / self-righteous trust**: He approaches God as one who has no need of mercy. His “thanksgiving” is self-congratulatory; he lists what he is *not* and what he *does*, treating his performance as the ground of his standing before God. There is no confession of sin and no petition for grace. Classic commentators (including Calvin) isolate exactly these two defects: trusting in himself that he was righteous, and despising others. Without them, the fasting and tithing would not have been rejected.
- **Comparison and contempt**: The prayer is structured comparatively—“I am not as other men are… or even as this publican.” He uses the tax collector as a foil to elevate himself. The introduction’s “despised others” is enacted in real time.
- **Virtues as self-display**: Fasting twice a week and tithing everything were (and are) legitimate acts of devotion that went beyond the letter of the Law. The problem is not the practices but their function in the prayer: they become credentials presented to God and to himself, instruments of self-justification rather than expressions of gratitude or obedience. The phrase “prayed thus with himself” (πρὸς ἑαυτόν) further underscores the self-directed, self-absorbed quality of the speech—whether taken as “by himself” (standing apart) or “to/with himself.”

False doctrine is not in view. The Pharisee correctly identifies certain sins and practices real virtues. His error is not theological content but the heart posture that turns even correct religion into a platform for self-exaltation and disdain.

### Why the tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer
The tax collector’s physical and verbal stance matches reality and the divine economy of justification:
- He stands afar off, will not lift his eyes, and beats his breast—visible signs of contrition and unworthiness.
- His only words are a pure plea for mercy: “God be merciful to me a sinner” (ἱλάσθητί μοι τῷ ἁμαρτωλῷ)—an appeal for propitiation/mercy addressed to the one he knows he has offended, with no mitigating comparison or claim of relative goodness.

He is declared justified *rather than* the other precisely because he relies solely on God’s mercy rather than on personal merit or superiority. This is the gospel contrast running through Luke: the humble who know their need receive grace; the self-exalting are left with their self-made righteousness, which God does not accept as grounds for justification. The tax collector’s posture is “truer” because it corresponds to the actual human condition (sinners in need of mercy) and to the only basis on which God justifies (his own gracious mercy, not human performance or comparative ranking).

In short, the parable does not critique virtue, fasting, tithing, or even accurate moral distinctions. It critiques the proud self-righteousness that turns those things into self-display, comparison, and contempt—and it holds up the humble, mercy-dependent sinner as the one who goes home justified.
