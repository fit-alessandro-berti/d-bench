The apostle Paul’s letter to the Ephesian church addresses communal ethics and unity. In Ephesians 4:31–32, he lays out a sharp contrast between bitterness and forgiveness, urging believers to put away the former and embrace the latter. The placement of these two ideas back-to-back reveals a deliberate rhetorical and theological choice. 

The Greek word for "bitterness" (pikria) carries both a literal and metaphorical meaning. Literally, it can refer to a bitter-tasting substance, but theologically and contextually, it denotes a deep-seated resentment, malice, or ill will that festers in a person’s heart. Its contrast with "forgiveness" in this passage suggests that bitterness is not merely a fleeting feeling but a persistent and toxic disposition that undermines the unity Paul elsewhere advocates in Ephesians (see 4:1–16). 

Further, Paul uses the verb anairō, meaning "to put away," "remove entirely," or "cast out." This shows that bitterness is framed not as an inevitable emotion but as a choice believers can reject. Its pairing with "malice" (kakoētheia) underscores its role as a deliberate orientation toward harming others, rather than a fleeting emotion. 

Paul’s language of "putting away" also implies action—bitterness is not to be suppressed but actively discarded. When linked to forgiveness, it becomes clear that rejecting bitterness is not merely about managing feelings but about aligning with Christ’s example. Verse 32 ties forgiveness to God’s own generosity through Christ, positioning it as a transformative, outward practice rather than an internal state alone. Thus, bitterness is contagious: by clinging to it, believers become agents of division, propagating a spirit of hostility that thrives on resentment rather than grace. 

Moreover, the mention of "all bitterness" and "all malice" shows these are pervasive, habitual attitudes, not isolated moments. To "put them away" requires a conscious choice to adopt a different posture—one of kindness, tenderness, and forgiveness. This posture is similarly active—it involves actively choosing to extend grace, even when it is hard, as God did for believers through Christ. In this way, bitterness and forgiveness become opposing modes of relating to others: bitterness poisons relationships from within, while forgiveness heals and nurtures them from without.
