**This verse from Luke 6:37 (paralleled in Matthew 7) is not a call to moral relativism or indifference to right and wrong.** It warns against a specific *spirit* and *practice* of judgment and condemnation—particularly the hypocritical, self-righteous, and unmerciful kind—while assuming that moral reality and discernment remain essential.

### The Verse in Context
Jesus is teaching in the Sermon on the Plain (or Sermon on the Mount in Matthew). The immediate follow-up in Matthew 7:3-5 is the vivid image of the plank in your own eye versus the speck in your brother's: remove your own glaring fault first, *then* you may see clearly to help your neighbor. The passage continues with the principle of reciprocity ("the measure you use will be measured to you") and emphasizes mercy, forgiveness, and generosity. 

The Greek words involved (*krinō* for "judge" and *katakrinō* for "condemn") carry nuances of evaluation, decision, and pronouncing doom. Jesus is not prohibiting all evaluation. Elsewhere he explicitly says to "judge with right judgment" (John 7:24), calls out hypocrisy and false teaching, tells the woman caught in adultery to "go and sin no more" (John 8:11), and overturns tables in the temple. The apostles exercise discernment and church discipline. The Bible repeatedly praises wisdom, testing spirits, distinguishing good from evil, and mature judgment (Proverbs, Philippians 1:9-10, Hebrews 5:14, 1 Corinthians 2:15—"the spiritual person judges all things").

The verse is therefore **not** saying "moral judgments never matter" or "anything goes." That would collapse into relativism, rendering concepts like sin, repentance, justice, holiness, and love incoherent. Christianity is built on objective moral reality grounded in God's character. Instead, Luke 6:37 addresses *how* fallen humans tend to wield moral awareness: with pride, selective blindness, and a desire to elevate ourselves by tearing others down.

### Discernment vs. the Appetite to Condemn
- **Discernment** is humble, truth-seeking, self-aware evaluation aimed at restoration, protection, or wisdom. It begins with self-examination ("first take the plank out of your own eye"). It uses evidence and moral standards (ultimately God's), seeks clarity rather than superiority, and operates with mercy where possible. Think of a skilled surgeon diagnosing disease in order to heal, or a parent wisely guiding a child. It recognizes that actions and teachings have consequences—protecting the vulnerable, maintaining justice, calling sin what it is (in love, per Galatians 6:1), and pursuing truth. Mature discernment is cognitive, spiritual, and constructive. It leaves ultimate judgment of hearts and eternal destinies to God (James 4:12) while still making necessary distinctions in this life.

- **The appetite to condemn** is an emotional, prideful *hunger* to pronounce doom, shame, exclude, or punish. It is often hypocritical (Romans 2:1-3), driven by resentment, insecurity, self-righteousness, or the desire for power and status. It focuses on others' faults while ignoring one's own. It weaponizes morality rather than submitting to it. This spirit treats people as beyond redemption, delights in their downfall, or uses their sins to feel superior. It is affective and destructive rather than restorative. Jesus repeatedly condemns this Pharisaic tendency: they tithed mint and cumin but neglected justice, mercy, and faithfulness (Matthew 23).

The difference is one of posture, aim, and self-knowledge:
- Discernment says, "This action/idea/teaching is wrong, and here's why—now how can truth, repentance, or justice serve love and restoration?" It includes the possibility of forgiveness and sees one's own vulnerability ("there but for the grace of God").
- The appetite to condemn says, in effect, "They are bad and deserve to be crushed/shamed/excluded—look how righteous I am by comparison." It rushes to finality and often lacks mercy.

Jesus links this directly to forgiveness: "Forgive, and you will be forgiven." The reciprocal principle ("with the measure you use, it will be measured to you") is sobering. A merciless, condemnatory spirit invites the same standard upon oneself before a holy God. This does not eliminate accountability or consequences (civil justice, church discipline in Matthew 18, parental correction, or personal consequences of sin remain). It targets the *heart* that plays God by eagerly damning others while remaining unrepentant itself.

In short, Luke 6:37 calls believers to a merciful, humble realism about human sin—including our own—rather than naive permissiveness or self-exalting judgmentalism. Moral judgments matter deeply; what we do with them, and the spirit in which we make them, matters even more. Discernment builds up and aligns with love of truth and neighbor. The appetite to condemn tears down and reveals love of self over God and others. This teaching is demanding precisely because it requires us to judge ourselves first and most rigorously.
