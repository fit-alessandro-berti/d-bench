This passage operates on all three levels you suggest, and the metaphor of "treasure" (θησαυρός) is what links them into a coherent picture. Let me try to unfold what the text is actually doing.

## The Heart as a Storehouse

The pivotal image is the heart as a *thesauros*—a treasury or stockpile. This is not the language of a passive vessel but of an active repository that has been *amassed* over time. The word translated "abundance" (περίσσευμα) reinforces this: it denotes surplus, overflow, the accumulation of more than enough. The heart, on this view, is an *arsenal of character*—a place where moral material has been gathered, hoarded, and stored until it can be brought forth.

This is significant because it means the heart's contents are not spontaneous. They are the product of accumulation, which implies a history of choices, inputs, and dispositions that have been allowed to settle and consolidate.

## Words as Symptom

The dominant note in v. 34—"out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh"—is symptomatic. Speech is depicted as the *natural overflow* of an already-existing condition. Like a container too full to hold its contents, or a fountain that cannot help but bubble up what lies beneath, the mouth simply releases what the heart has already produced. The sequence is: *what fills → what spills.*

This is why Jesus can frame the earlier question rhetorically: "How can you, being evil, speak good things?" The implicit answer is *you cannot*—or at least, you cannot do so sustainably. Good words may occasionally be coaxed out, but the long-term pattern of speech will betray the inner reality. Words here function diagnostically, like fever indicating infection.

## Words as Choice

But if the heart is a *treasury*, then someone has been deciding what to put into it. The "good man" and "evil man" (ὁ ἀγαθός / ὁ πονηρός) are not merely described as states—they are moral identities, and such identities are formed. The verb ἐκβάλλω ("brings forth") is the same word used for *casting out* demons; it implies an active, even forceful production, not mere passive leakage.

So while the immediate text emphasizes what flows out, the treasury metaphor pulls our attention backward: how did this stockpile get here? This opens the door to the whole scriptural logic of formation—where one *treasures* shapes what one *becomes* (cf. Matt 6:21, where the location of one's treasure determines the orientation of the heart). The heart, then, is a record of choices, and speech is the *performance* of those choices.

## Words as Revelation

The context in Matthew 12 is decisive here. Jesus has just been accused of working *by Beelzebub*—a charge that, on its surface, looks like a theological judgment against evil. The Pharisees believe they are *speaking good things* (defending God, condemning the demonic). Jesus' response is that the very accusation reveals what they actually are: vipers. Their words are not measured against their own self-understanding but against what the words disclose.

This is where speech becomes revelatory in a more radical sense: words disclose the inner state even *to the speaker*. The Pharisees do not seem to recognize the contradiction between their claim and their character. Speech functions as an x-ray—penetrating the self-deception that the hidden heart is otherwise able to maintain. As v. 36 follows up, on the day of judgment men will give account for *every idle word*, because idle words are not actually idle; they are diagnostic.

## The Synthesis: A Vicious and Virtuous Loop

The three functions are not alternative readings but a single dynamic:

1. **What you have treasured** (accumulation through choice)
2. **Produces what overflows** (symptomatic expression)
3. **Which discloses what you have become** (revelation of identity)

And critically, this disclosure is not neutral—it loops back. To have one's words reveal the corruption of one's heart is itself a moral event. The Pharisees, in speaking, are not just *described* as evil; they are *constituted* as such before themselves and before God. Speech, on this view, is *performative* of the very self it reveals.

## A Further Implication

The "hiddenness" of the stockpile matters theologically. The heart is invisible, and this is precisely why speech is necessary as revelation. If the heart were transparent, no test would be needed. But because we can deceive ourselves about our inner state (and others certainly cannot see it), God has appointed speech as a kind of involuntary testimony. We are, in this sense, *exposed by our own tongues*.

This makes the passage both a warning and an invitation: one cannot control what the mouth ultimately produces by merely policing the mouth itself. The reform must go deeper, to the treasury—because whatever is stored in abundance will, eventually, come out.
