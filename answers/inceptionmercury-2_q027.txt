**Short answer:**  
The Pharisee is condemned not because he teaches a false doctrine, but because his prayer reveals a *pride‑filled, self‑righteous posture* that turns the virtues he boasts about into a public display of superiority.  The tax‑collector’s posture is “spiritually truer” because it is marked by *humility, contrition, and a dependence on God’s mercy* rather than on his own merit.

---

## 1.  What the Pharisee is actually condemned for

| Possible fault | Does the text support it? | Why it matters |
|----------------|--------------------------|----------------|
| **False doctrine** | No.  The Pharisee’s prayer contains no theological error; he affirms the same God, the same commandments, and the same practices (fasting, tithing) that the Jewish law requires. | The parable is not a polemic against the law or against Pharisaic theology; it is a moral teaching about the *heart* behind the law. |
| **Pride / self‑righteousness** | ✔︎  He opens with “I thank you, O God, that I am not like other men…” (Lk 18:13).  The whole prayer is a litany of personal merit. | Pride is the “root of all sin” (Prov 16:18) and the chief obstacle to genuine repentance. |
| **Comparison / contempt** | ✔︎  He explicitly *compares* himself with “other men” (extortioners, unjust, adulterers) and with the publican.  The comparison is meant to *exalt* himself and *devalue* the other. | The New Testament repeatedly warns that “the one who exalts himself will be humbled, and the one who humbles himself will be exalted” (Matt 23:12). |
| **Use of virtue as self‑display** | ✔︎  He turns his fasting and tithing into a *badge* of righteousness (“I fast twice a week, I give tithes of all that I possess”).  The prayer is a *performance* rather than a petition. | Jesus condemns “practicing righteousness before men to be seen by them” (Matt 6:1‑2).  The Pharisee’s prayer is exactly that. |
| **Contempt of others** | ✔︎  The phrase “and despised others” (the Lk’s introduction) captures his attitude toward the publican and, by extension, toward all “sinners.” | Contempt blocks the grace that can flow from God to the repentant. |

**Bottom line:** The Pharisee’s sin is *pride expressed through comparison and self‑glorification*.  The parable’s focus is on the *inner disposition* that turns good works into a means of self‑exaltation, not on any doctrinal error.

---

## 2.  Why the tax‑collector’s posture is “spiritually truer”

| Feature of the collector’s prayer | Biblical / theological significance |
|-----------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Standing “afar off”** (Lk 18:13) | Physical distance mirrors *spiritual distance* from the “self‑justifying” crowd; he does not seek applause. |
| **Refusing to lift his eyes to heaven** (Lk 18:13) | He does **not** try to *show* his piety; his focus is inward, on his own condition. |
| **Beating his breast** (Lk 18:13) | The ancient gesture of *contrition* (cf. Jer 31:19; Ps 51:3).  It signals genuine sorrow for sin. |
| **“God, be merciful to me, a sinner”** (Lk 18:13) | He acknowledges *sin* and *dependence* on divine mercy, not on his own works. |
| **No mention of works** | The prayer is *empty of boasting*; it leaves the question of “what I have done” to God’s judgment. |

### 2.1  Humility vs. self‑exaltation

- **Humility** is the *condition* for God’s grace (Matt 5:3; James 4:6).  The collector’s prayer meets that condition: he *recognizes* his unworthiness and *asks* for mercy.
- **Self‑exaltation** (the Pharisee’s prayer) is the *obstacle*: it places the *self* as the basis of justification.

### 2.2  Faith‑like justification

The Greek word rendered “justified” (δικαιωθῆναι) in Lk 18:14 is the same term used in Paul’s doctrine of *justification by faith* (Rom 3:24, 5:1).  The collector’s “justification” is *declarative*—God declares him righteous **because** he trusts in God’s mercy, not because of his own merit.  The Pharisee’s “justification” would be *self‑declarative*—he tries to *declare* his own righteousness, which the Spirit rejects (John 5:24).

### 2.3  The “kingdom value” of the parable

Jesus often used “the lowly” and “the humble” as *kingdom‑type* people (Matt 20:16; Luke 14:11).  The tax‑collector, the *social outcast*, embodies the kingdom’s upside‑down logic: the one who “knows his need” is the one who *receives*.

---

## 3.  A concise theological formulation

> **The Pharisee’s sin is pride‑driven self‑righteousness, expressed through comparative boasting and the use of virtue as a public badge.**  
> **The tax‑collector’s prayer is spiritually truer because it is a posture of humility, contrition, and dependence on God’s mercy—conditions that make a person receptive to divine justification.**

## 4.  Supporting voices from the tradition

| Source | Key point |
|--------|-----------|
| **St. Matthew (Catena Aurea, 12th c.)** | “The Pharisee, by his very words, shows that he has not repented, but only counted his works.” |
| **St. Augustine, *Sermon 70*** | “The pride of the Pharisee is the very opposite of the humility that the Gospel demands.” |
| **John Calvin, *Commentary on Luke 18*** | “The publican’s prayer is simple and sincere; it is the prayer of a man who knows his need for grace.” |
| **Modern scholarship (N. T. Wright, *Luke for the Gospel of the Kingdom*, 1992)** | “The parable is a critique of *self‑justifying religiosity* and a celebration of *the repentant sinner* as the model of true piety.” |

---

## 5.  Practical take‑away

When we read the parable today, the focus is not on *what we do* (fasting, giving) but on *why we do it*.  A good deed becomes a *spiritual liability* when it is used to elevate the self; a humble confession of need becomes a *spiritual asset* when it opens us to God’s mercy.  The Pharisee’s “justification” is *self‑generated* and therefore false; the tax‑collector’s “justification” is *God‑generated* and therefore true.
