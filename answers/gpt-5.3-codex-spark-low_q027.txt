• The central fault is pride/self-righteousness, expressed as contempt and self-display (boasting), with comparison as
  its method.

  - Not primarily false doctrine in this verse. The Pharisee recites orthodox piety (fasting, tithing), but turns
    religion into self-promotion.
  - His prayer is wrong in structure: it is a report on his achievements over others, not a plea for mercy.
  - The key problem is that he defines himself as righteous and “above” the tax collector, effectively placing himself
    as judge over God’s mercy.
  - “Despised others” is decisive: he has separated himself morally instead of approaching God in dependence and
    humility.

  The tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer because:

  1. Humility – he does not claim status or merit, just admits need (“a sinner”).
  2. Honest self-knowledge – no comparison, no self-justification.
  3. Trust in mercy, not works – he asks for God’s compassion, not recognition.
  4. Correct attitude before God – Jesus frames justification as God’s gracious response to repentance, not as reward
     for visible religion.

  So the contrast is between:

  - Pharisee = “I have done enough”
  - Tax collector = “I need grace.”

  That distinction is what makes the tax collector “justified” in Jesus’ parable.
