All of the things you mention are visible in the elder brother: he is angry, he is envious, he cries “It isn’t fair!”, he boasts of lifelong obedience, and he recoils at the father’s mercy.  But in Jesus’ story those are successive layers of the same root problem.  Strip the layers off and the core is this:

The elder brother is living by **merit**, not by **grace**.  
Because he thinks relationship with the father is earned wages, he cannot rejoice when the father gives unearned favour to another.

1. Pride in his obedience  
   • “These many years I have served you … I never transgressed …”  
   • He defines himself by performance and therefore feels entitled.

2. Wounded sense of fairness  
   • “You never gave me even a kid …”  
   • Merit-thinking inevitably measures and compares.  When grace overturns the accounting, it feels “unfair.”

3. Envy and anger  
   • Grace to the younger brother looks to him like loss to himself, so he burns with resentment.

4. Discomfort with mercy  
   • Because he does not live by mercy himself, mercy shown to another offends him.

All the surface reactions grow out of the single tap-root of **self-righteousness—confidence in his own record rather than delight in the father’s love.**  That is why Jesus uses him to portray the Pharisees and scribes (Luke 15:1-2): people who “serve” God impeccably yet cannot celebrate when notorious sinners are received by sheer grace.

So the elder brother’s deepest problem is not anger or envy as such; those are symptoms.  His deepest problem is the pride of merit-based righteousness, which makes him incapable of sharing the father’s joy and therefore incapable of accepting the father’s mercy to others—or ultimately to himself.
