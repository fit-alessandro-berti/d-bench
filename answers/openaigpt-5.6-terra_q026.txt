Matthew 12:34–35 presents speech as flowing from an inward “store” or “treasure” of the heart.

- **“Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks”**: The word translated “abundance” suggests an overflow. Speech is not merely accidental noise; what fills the heart tends eventually to spill into words.
- **“Treasure”** refers to a stored supply or repository. The heart, in biblical language, is not only the seat of emotion but the center of thought, desire, will, loyalties, and moral orientation. A person accumulates an inner stock through what they love, believe, dwell on, and choose.

So the passage treats words as **all three**:

1. **Symptoms**  
   Speech can be evidence of an existing inward condition. Malice, slander, contempt, deceit, or hardened unbelief are not just isolated verbal slips; they may indicate what has been accumulating within.

2. **Choices**  
   Jesus does not portray people as helpless machines whose mouths merely emit whatever is inside. His larger teaching holds people responsible for their words (see Matt. 12:36–37). Speech is morally accountable action. What one says is chosen, even if those choices are strongly shaped by the heart’s habits.

3. **Revelations**  
   Words disclose the heart. In the immediate context, religious leaders have attributed Jesus’ works to Satan (Matt. 12:24). Their accusation reveals not simply a mistaken sentence but a deeper hostility toward God’s work. Their speech exposes their moral and spiritual posture.

The image is therefore dynamic: the “treasure” produces speech, but repeated speech also belongs to the process by which a person forms and reinforces that treasure. Good words arise from a heart being filled with what is good; evil words reveal and nourish an evil store.

Jesus’ point is not that every careless phrase gives a complete diagnosis of a person, nor that a person cannot repent or change. Rather, sustained patterns of speech are spiritually significant because they show what one has been storing within.
