
This proverb, originating from the Bible (Proverbs 16:18–19), suggests that pride and haughtiness are not only moral sins but also detrimental forces that can lead to one's downfall and blindness to the plight of others. This comparison elucde the moral psychology of arrogance as not only a defiice of character but as an inherently self-destructive and divisive attitude that fosters disconnection with the realities of the less fortunate. Pride is linked to blindness not as a literal inability to see but as a metaphorical inability to see or acknowledge the struggles, sufferings, and realities of those who are less fortunate. The contrast with the "lowly" suggests that humility opens one's eyes to the inequities of life, thus creating empathy and connection with others who are less privileged. By prioritizing humility, one can see the world from a more holistic perspective that recognizes the struggles and vulnerabilities of all people, as opposed to a self-centered worldview driven by pride, which perpetuates isolation and indifference. Arrogance, on the other hand, obscures the struggles of others, preventing a comprehensive understanding of the societal issues at hand and leading to a fragmented community where only the fortunate, like the "proud," exist. The proverb emphasizes that personal fortunes are intertwined with the social structure and hence, in being humble, one could potentially contribute more effectively to societal wellbeing, which ultimately could lead to more substantial, shared success rather than division and fall. It implies that arrogance and pride lead to a self-centered mindset, blinded to larger societal realities, which can catalyze one's own 'fall', or downfall, by fostering discord and imbalance in the community.
