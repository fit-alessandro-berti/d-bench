The Pharisee is primarily condemned for pride, comparison, contempt, and using virtue as a form of self-display. While his doctrine is technically correct—fasting and tithing are not wrong in themselves—his righteousness is rooted in self-confidence, superiority over others, and public demonstration rather than humble dependence on God. He uses religion not to connect genuinely with God but to elevate himself above others.

The tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer because it embodies authentic humility, genuine self-awareness, and total reliance on divine mercy. Rather than presenting accomplishments to God, he recognizes his moral poverty and dependence. Thus, the parable sharply contrasts self-righteousness with true humility, suggesting that genuine spirituality emerges not from moral performance or comparison, but from honest recognition of one's need for God's mercy.
