James exposes the contradiction of **claiming reverence for God while despising His image in other people**.

To bless God is to acknowledge His worth, holiness, and authority. But to curse people—especially in the sense of contempt, slander, dehumanizing speech, or wishing harm—is to treat as worthless those whom God has made “after the similitude of God.” The contradiction is this:

> The same mouth honors the Creator while dishonoring His likeness in the creature.

James is not saying humans are equal to God. He is saying that humans bear God’s image, so our treatment of them reveals whether our worship of God is morally coherent. You cannot sincerely exalt God while casually degrading what God has marked with dignity.

Speech reveals moral disorder quickly because the tongue is an immediate outlet of the heart. Words often expose what is already within: pride, bitterness, contempt, envy, anger, self-righteousness. Jesus says similarly:

> “Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh” — Matthew 12:34.

So the problem is not merely “bad language.” The tongue is a symptom of a deeper dividedness. James is concerned with a person whose worship and character do not match—praise in one moment, cruelty in the next.

That is why he says, “My brethren, these things ought not so to be.” It is unnatural and morally disordered, like a spring producing both fresh and bitter water. The mouth has become double: it performs devotion toward God while practicing violence toward neighbor.

In short, James is exposing **the hypocrisy of worship without love**, and speech reveals it quickly because words are where the hidden condition of the heart becomes audible.
