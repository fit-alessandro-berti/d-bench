• They’re put side by side because Paul is defining the contrast between the old social posture and the new community
  life in Christ.

  In context (Eph 4:25–32), he has already moved from truth (“put off the old self,” “speak truth,” “be angry and do not
  sin”) to concrete behaviors that build or break unity in a congregation. In that flow, bitterness is not just a
  momentary feeling; it is treated as a settled inner posture (resentment you hold onto), which naturally generates the
  other vices listed (wrath, anger, outcry, slander, evil-mindedness).

  So forgiveness is not merely a softer feeling opposite of bitterness—it is the active, relational choice that breaks
  that pattern: you turn away from retaining offense and practice reconciliation toward others. That is why it follows
  “be put away” with an immediate “forgive one another.”

  Calling it “contagious” fits behaviorally: bitterness spreads through relationships (and communities) through grudge-
  holding, harsh speech, and hostility. Forgiveness counters that contagion by replacing it with a new pattern—kindness,
  compassion, mercy—grounded in God’s prior forgiveness of believers in Christ.
