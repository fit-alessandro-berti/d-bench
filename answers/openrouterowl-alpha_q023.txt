You're asking a profound interpretive and theological question about the nature of repentance, drawing on the text of Isaiah 55:6–7. My purpose is to be helpful, and discussing theological concepts from religious texts is a reasoning exercise that aligns with that purpose, so let me explore the tension between outward and inward restoration with you.

**I would approach the mention of "wicked" and "unrighteous" as anthropological labels.**

In this prophetic context, the "way" (*derek*) refers to one's path or manner of life—it is not merely physical movement, but the trajectory of one's behavioral choices and social patterns. The man of the world may think his 'way' is fixed and determined by culture, economy, habit, and the chaos of life. The "thoughts" (*machashabah*) are the inward counsels, the plans, and the reasoning of the mind. And the prophet presents a radical turn for both: to forsake the external path and the internal thoughts.

What does that imply about outward conduct, inward imagination, and the possibility of mercy?

**The Nature of the Outward Conduct**

The world often judges the righteousness of man according to external compliance—I would go further and say that external compliance is a functional necessity of social existence. The prophet acknowledges this. The "way" must be forsaken. But simply changing one's “way” is superficial. A man can alter his conduct out of fear, coercion, or pragmatism without ever touching the root issue. He becomes a “good citizen” by social metrics, but his inner world remains unaddressed.

The possibility of mercy, however, is not contingent on the perfection of one's conduct. The text says, "let him return unto the Lord." Not “let him perfect himself,” but “let him return.” The return requires acknowledging that one’s habitual way was not leading to life, and that one's framework for thinking was flawed.

**The Nature of the Inward Imagination and Thoughts**

The imagination and thoughts are where the battle for the soul is truly waged. It is here that intentions, motivations, and deeply held beliefs reside. The man of the world assumes his thoughts are his own, autonomous and neutral. The prophet challenges this, suggesting that the inward man is as active—and as in need of turning—as the outward man. If the thoughts remain unaddressed, the outward change will inevitably revert. New wine cannot be poured into old wineskins; a new path cannot be sustained by old thoughts.

This implies that mercy is available. It is a return to a Creator whose forgiveness is characterized by "abundantly pardon." This contrasts sharply with the world of economics and transaction, where a man might "forgive" a debt out of loss mitigation or strategic calculation.

**The Possibility of Mercy**

This is where the anthropological struggle comes in. We have a being who must forsake both the external path and the internal counsel. The text is a conditional promise: **IF** he returns, **THEN** God will have mercy. This implies that the initiation of the turn belongs to man—a free agent making a volitional choice—but the sustaining of that turn, the actual "abundant pardon," is an act of divine grace. 

The possibility of mercy implies a rupture in the cycle of human autonomy. Man cannot save himself by his way, nor can he redeem himself by his thoughts. Total depravity—or, in more secular terms, total self-interest—means that both the external path and the internal compass are broken. The "abundant pardon" suggests that God's resources are not scarce or limited; they are superabundant. He does not forgive out of economic necessity, but out of inexhaustible mercy. This forgiveness replaces the need for human self-justification. A man can stop performing and simply trust the promise.

In this view, repentance becomes an economic and spiritual reality. The "wicked" and the "unrighteous" are not just people who do bad things or think bad thoughts, but those who operate outside the covenant of grace. When they "return," they are stepping out of the economy of scarcity and into the economy of grace.

The mention of both "way" and "thoughts" implies that the whole person is addressed. The outward conduct and the inward imagination must both be surrendered, not to a particular culture or ideology, but to a personal God who responds to a "return." This is not about behavioral modification or cognitive restructuring for the sake of productivity; it is about transformation by grace.

Therefore, mercy is not earned by the perfection of either path or imagination; it is received by the act of returning. It is an act of volition that acknowledges the bankruptcy of both the external and the internal self, and looks outward to a God who "abundantly pardons."
