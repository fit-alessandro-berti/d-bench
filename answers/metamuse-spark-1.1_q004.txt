In Proverbs 3:5-6, the trust being asked for is not blind or anti-rational. The verse itself assumes you *will* use your understanding — it just tells you what not to do with it.

The Hebrew helps:

> **"Trust [batach] in the Lord with all thine heart [lev]; and lean not unto thine own understanding [binah]. In all thy ways acknowledge [yada] him, and he shall direct [yashar - make straight] thy paths."**

1.  **Batach** means to have confident security, like leaning your full weight on something that will not give way.
2.  **Lev** is not just emotion in Hebrew. It is the center of intellect, will, and emotion — the whole inner person.
3.  **Lean** is the image of a man putting all his weight on a walking staff. The staff may be useful, but it is not made to be your only support.

So the kind of trust is:

**Whole-hearted, not half-hearted.** "With all thine heart" — not compartmentalized trust where you trust God on Sunday and your own calculations the rest of the week.

**Relational and dependent, not just intellectual assent.** It's covenant trust — looking to a Person for guidance, not just to a principle.

**Submissive and acknowledging.** "In all thy ways acknowledge him" — *yada* means to know attentively, to recognize his claim and presence in every domain of life — work, money, relationships, decisions.

That leads to the distinction you asked about:

### 1. What it is NOT: Anti-intellectualism

Anti-intellectualism says: Reason is bad, thinking is unspiritual, God wants you to turn your mind off and take a blind leap.

If that were the meaning, Proverbs would contradict itself. The same book says:

> "Get wisdom, get understanding" - Prov. 4:5
> "The heart of him that hath understanding seeketh knowledge" - Prov. 15:14

Proverbs *commands* the vigorous use of the mind, planning, counsel, prudence, learning.

Anti-intellectualism makes an idol of ignorance.

### 2. What it IS: Humble self-distrust

Humble self-distrust says: Reason is good and God-given, but it is limited, finite, and self-interested. It is not ultimate. It is not autonomous.

Leaning not on your own understanding means:

*  **Recognizing finitude:** You do not see all ends, all consequences, all hearts — including your own. Isaiah 55:8-9
*  **Recognizing fallibility:** Your reasoning can be shaped by fear, pride, desire, and habit. Your own understanding feels objective to you when it isn't.
*  **Recognizing final authority:** The question is not *whether* you will think, but *where* you place final weight. Will your understanding sit in judgment over God's wisdom, or will God's wisdom sit in judgment over your understanding?

Think of it this way:

> **Anti-intellectualism says:** "My understanding is worthless, so I won't use it."
> **Humble self-distrust says:** "My understanding is valuable, but it is not sufficient to be my support. I will use it fully, but I will not rest my full weight on it."

A person living this does not think *less*. He often thinks *more* — he seeks counsel, studies, plans, weighs — but with an open hand. He can hold his best conclusions with conviction and with humility at the same time, willing to be corrected by God through Scripture, through wise community, and through providence.

And the promise is not that such trust will make the path always easy, but that he will *yashar* — make straight, clear, directed — the path itself.
