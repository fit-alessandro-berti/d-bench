In Isaiah 55:6–7, repentance is described as turning from sin at **both the outer and inner levels**:

- **“Way”** = a person’s visible pattern of life, habits, actions, and moral direction.
- **“Thoughts”** = the inward plans, desires, assumptions, and self-justifying patterns of the heart and mind.

So the text is saying repentance is not merely **changing behavior** while keeping the same heart, nor merely **having the right feelings** while continuing in the same conduct. It calls for a whole-person turning back to God.

### What that implies

#### 1. Outward conduct matters
“Let the wicked forsake his way” means evil is not only something believed or intended; it is also something **lived out**. Repentance includes abandoning sinful practices, public patterns, and chosen directions.

#### 2. Inward imagination matters too
“Let the unrighteous man his thoughts” shows that sin reaches deeper than deeds. The “thoughts” include:
- sinful motives
- proud self-reliance
- distorted desires
- plans opposed to God
- hidden rationalizations

God is not asking only for external reform, but for a renewed mind and heart.

#### 3. Mercy is available because repentance is relational
The promise is striking: “he will have mercy upon him… he will abundantly pardon.” This means repentance is not a way of earning forgiveness, but the posture that **returns to the Lord who is ready to forgive**.

The passage emphasizes:
- God is near
- God may be found
- God welcomes the one who returns
- God’s mercy exceeds the sinner’s guilt

### In short
Repentance involves both “way” and “thoughts” because God addresses the whole person: **what we do and what we are inwardly becoming**. And the promise of mercy means that even deep, comprehensive sin is not beyond pardon when a person truly turns back to the Lord.
