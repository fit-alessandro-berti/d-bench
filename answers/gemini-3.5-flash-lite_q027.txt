To answer this question accurately, one must look closely at both the mechanics of Jesus’ parable and the specific framing Luke provides in verse 9. 

The text states that Jesus directed the parable at **“certain which trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others.”** 

While the Pharisee is guilty of almost every option listed in your question, the root condemnation—and the reason the tax collector is justified—comes down to a fundamental theological error regarding the nature of righteousness and grace. 

Here is an analysis of the Pharisee’s faults and the tax collector’s posture.

---

### Part 1: What condemns the Pharisee?

The Pharisee is not condemned for keeping the Law. Fasting twice a week (more than the mandatory minimums) and tithing on "all that I possess" (going beyond strict legal requirements) are presented as facts. His condemnation arises from *why* he does these things and *how* he relates to God and man. Specifically, his downfall is a combination of your list, anchored by two primary drivers:

#### 1. Pride and Self-Justification ("Trusted in themselves")
The Pharisee’s prayer is actually a monologue addressed to himself (*pros heauton*). He uses the pronoun "I" four times. He treats his righteousness as an achievement generated by his own willpower and moral stamina, rather than as a gift from God. By trusting in his own moral resume, he effectively makes God a debtor to him rather than him a debtor to God.

#### 2. Comparison and Contempt (“Despised others”)
The Pharisee’s identity does not rest on who God is, but on who he is *not*. He defines his holiness by looking down on others ("not as other men are... or even as this publican"). Comparison is the engine of self-righteousness: to feel righteous, the Pharisee requires someone else to be unrighteous. This inevitably leads to *contempt*—the active looking-down-upon and dehumanization of his neighbor. 

#### 3. Virtue as Self-Display
His religious disciplines (fasting, tithing) are weaponized. Instead of acts of devotion, gratitude, or love for neighbor, they are used as evidence in his self-trial to prove his superiority. He treats grace as a merit badge.

#### What about "False Doctrine"?
Strictly speaking, the Pharisee holds orthodox Jewish beliefs. He thanks God, acknowledges God as the source of his morality (nominally, at least), and practices traditional piety. Therefore, his condemnation is **not** about holding incorrect theological propositions, but about **misunderstanding the posture of the human heart before a holy God.** His doctrine is orthodox, but his *application* of it is thoroughly corrupted by legalism and self-inflation.

---

### Part 2: Why is the tax collector’s posture spiritually truer?

The tax collector (publican) represents the exact inverse of the Pharisee. He is spiritually "true" for three reasons:

#### 1. Ontological Truth (Acknowledging Reality)
The tax collector comes with no defense because he has none. He is standing before the Creator, and his posture—standing afar off, eyes downcast, beating his breast—is simply an accurate assessment of reality. He is a sinner standing before a holy God. The Pharisee is living in a delusion; the tax collector is living in the truth. 

#### 2. The Nature of His Plea (*Ilastheti*)
In Greek, the tax collector’s prayer is: *Theos, hilastheti moi toi hamartolou*. 
Literally translated, it means: **"God, be propitiated [or merciful] to me, the sinner."** 
*   He uses the definite article: *the* sinner. He isn't comparing himself to the Pharisee; in his universe, there is only God and himself. 
*   The word for "be merciful" (*hilaskomai*) is deeply theological. It is the same root used for the mercy seat on the Ark of the Covenant where blood was sprinkled to cover sins. The tax collector is asking for atonement, sacrifice, and unearned mercy. He throws himself entirely on the mercy of the court, offering no bribes of good works.

#### 3. Surrender of Self
The tax collector empties himself. He brings no resume, no comparative analysis, and no demands. 

---

### Conclusion

Jesus’ verdict—that the tax collector went home **justified** (declared righteous by God) rather than the Pharisee—shocks the original audience. 

The Pharisee is condemned because **he uses religion to elevate himself above others and isolate himself from God's grace.** He doesn't need mercy because he believes he is already good. 

The tax collector is justified because **he bypasses moral self-help and appeals directly to divine mercy.** As Jesus summarizes elsewhere in Luke’s Gospel: *"Everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, and he who humbles himself will be exalted"* (Luke 18:14). The tax collector’s posture is truer because it is the only posture appropriate for a creature standing before his Creator.
