This verse touches on a subtle but important distinction in moral motivation. Let's unpack it.

The verse doesn't condemn **public good deeds themselves**—elsewhere, Jesus says "let your light shine before others" (Matthew 5:16). The danger here is specifically the **motive**:

> "...to be seen of them..."

The Greek phrase **πρὸς τὸ θεαθῆναι αὐτοῖς** (*pros to theathēnai autois*) means "for the purpose of being observed by them." It’s not the visibility that’s wrong, but **making visibility the goal**.

Jesus is diagnosing a **transactional mindset**: if you do good primarily for human applause, you've already received your "reward" in full—the fleeting admiration of others. The deeper, spiritual reward (communion with God, inner transformation, eternal significance) is forfeited because the act wasn't oriented toward God.

**Analogy:**  
It’s like writing a heartfelt letter to someone you love, but your real intention is to post it on social media for likes. The act of writing isn’t wrong—but if the primary audience shifts from the recipient to the public, the intimacy and meaning change entirely.

So the moral danger lies in **the craving to be seen**—using goodness as a tool for self-glorification rather than as an expression of love, justice, or devotion. Visibility becomes dangerous only when it corrupts motive.
