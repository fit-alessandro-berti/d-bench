Isaiah 55:6–7 names both **“way”** and **“thoughts”** because repentance is not merely a change in visible behavior, nor merely a private change of opinion. It is a whole-person turning.

The **“way”** is one’s path: the practiced pattern of life, the habits, choices, relationships, and public conduct by which a person walks. So repentance means the wicked person must actually **forsake the road he is on**. It is not enough to feel regret while continuing in the same direction.

The **“thoughts”** are the inward plans, desires, imaginations, calculations, and self-justifying stories that give rise to that way of life. Sin is not only in the feet and hands; it is also in the mind’s hidden architecture. The unrighteous person must forsake not only what he does, but also the inward world that makes evil seem reasonable, desirable, inevitable, or excusable.

So the verse is saying: repentance reaches both the **outer path** and the **inner imagination**.

That matters because outward conduct often grows from inward thought. A person walks a certain way because he has first imagined the world a certain way: “This will satisfy me,” “God will not see,” “I cannot change,” “Mercy is impossible,” “My way is better.” Isaiah calls the sinner to abandon both the visible rebellion and the inward falsehood that sustains it.

But the command is not merely negative: **“let him return unto the Lord.”** Repentance is not just leaving sin; it is coming home to God. One does not forsake wicked ways into a moral vacuum. One turns from one way to another, from one mind to another, from self-rule to the Lord.

The promise is striking: **“he will have mercy… he will abundantly pardon.”** The depth of the required repentance is matched by the abundance of divine mercy. God does not say, “Return after you have made yourself pardonable.” He says, in effect, “Return, and I will show mercy.” The sinner’s thoughts may say, “I have gone too far,” but God’s next words answer that: **“my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways”** (Isaiah 55:8). Human beings may imagine mercy as narrow, reluctant, or exhausted; God reveals His mercy as higher, freer, and more abundant than our imagination.

So Isaiah 55:6–7 implies three things:

**Outward conduct must change.** Repentance cannot remain an inward sentiment while the old “way” continues.

**Inward imagination must change.** Repentance includes forsaking the thoughts, desires, plans, and lies that made sin livable.

**Mercy remains genuinely possible.** The call to forsake sin is framed by an invitation to seek, call, return, and be pardoned. God exposes the whole disease, not to destroy hope, but to offer a whole mercy.
