**The problem is the craving to be seen, not visibility itself.**

Matthew 6:1 does not condemn public goodness or the simple fact that others may notice righteous acts. It condemns a specific purpose: performing those acts *in order to be seen by people* and thereby gain their admiration or praise.

The Greek makes this clear. The phrase is πρὸς τὸ θεαθῆναι αὐτοῖς (*pros to theathēnai autois*), a purpose construction meaning “in order to be seen/noticed by them.” The verb *theaomai* carries a theatrical sense—spectating, viewing as a spectacle—which is the root of our word “theater.” Jesus is warning against turning acts of righteousness into a performance staged for a human audience.

This is reinforced by the immediate context. The same chapter repeatedly contrasts those who already “have their reward” (human praise) with those whose Father, who sees in secret, will reward them. The danger is hypocrisy: the outer act of goodness becomes a means of self-promotion rather than genuine obedience or charity. When the craving for being seen drives the action, the heavenly reward is forfeited because the motive has already claimed its earthly payment.

Jesus is not against public witness. Just one chapter earlier, in Matthew 5:16, he commands, “Let your light shine before others, so that they may see your good works and give glory to your Father who is in heaven.” The difference is the intended recipient of the glory. In 5:16 the goal is that observers glorify God; in 6:1 the goal is that observers glorify (or at least notice and praise) the doer. Visibility is morally neutral; the heart’s aim determines whether the act remains pure or becomes spiritually empty.

In short, public goodness becomes morally dangerous in this verse only when it is co-opted by the desire to be seen and applauded. The craving for recognition corrupts the deed, turning it into self-serving theater rather than true righteousness before God.
