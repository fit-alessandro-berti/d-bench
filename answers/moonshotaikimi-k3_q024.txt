## First, what the "heart" actually is

In Hebrew anthropology, the *lev* (heart) is not primarily the seat of emotion but of will, thought, and decision — the control center of the person. So "a new heart" doesn't mean new feelings; it means a new governing orientation, new loves at the root of the self. That reframes both of your questions.

## External compliance vs. the new heart

External compliance is conformity of behavior without conformity of desire. The law stands *outside* the person as pressure — fear of punishment, hope of reward, social expectation, ingrained habit. The will stays divided: you do what is required while wanting otherwise. This produces a recognizable syndrome:

- Obedience experienced as constraint rather than freedom
- Fragility — the compliance collapses when external pressure lifts or costs rise
- Hypocrisy — the performance of righteousness alongside inner alienation ("this people honor me with their lips, but their heart is far from me," Isa 29:13)
- The minimum-threshold question, "what must I do?" instead of "what do I love?"

The new heart *relocates* the law. Jeremiah's parallel New Covenant text makes it explicit: "I will put my law in their inward parts, and write it in their hearts" (Jer 31:33). What was an external standard becomes an internal desire. Duty and delight converge, so obedience becomes self-expression — doing what you now actually want. That's why Scripture describes this state as freedom rather than a better discipline.

## Why the material language of stone

Your instinct is right — the passage deliberately materializes a moral condition, and there are layered reasons:

**1. The problem is constitutional, not behavioral.** If the problem were ignorance, teaching would fix it; if habit, discipline; if circumstance, better conditions. Stone says the *organ of response itself* is defective. Jeremiah 17:9 calls the heart "incurable" — the Hebrew word means exactly that. You don't repair stone; you replace it. (Commentators often note Ezekiel is describing, millennia early, essentially a heart transplant: removal and implantation, not renovation.)

**2. Stone cannot soften itself.** The imagery enforces inability and divine initiative — hence the relentless first-person verbs: *I will* gather, sprinkle, give, take away, put, cause. Strikingly, Ezekiel 18:31 had *commanded* "make yourselves a new heart"; Ezekiel 36 promises what was commanded. Augustine's prayer captures the logic: "Give what you command, and command what you will."

**3. Hardness genuinely behaves like a material process.** Scripture treats it as cumulative — a moral sclerosis. Repeated refusal desensitizes (Isa 6:10, the heart made "fat," i.e., coated and dull; 1 Tim 4:2, the conscience "seared," literally cauterized). Stone is the end-state of a thousand small refusals: cold, heavy, unimpressionable. God's word strikes it and makes no dent. The material metaphor isn't decoration; it's diagnosis.

**4. The stone tablets allusion.** Possibly the most elegant layer: the old covenant was inscribed on *tablets of stone* — God's will externalized in the hardest possible medium. The stony heart is a perverse mirror: the human interior has become as impervious as the law's own material. Paul makes the connection explicit in 2 Corinthians 3:3 — the new covenant is written "not on tablets of stone but on tablets of flesh, that is, of the heart," by the Spirit. God moves the inscription from the stone *in front of* the person *into* the person. (Note that "flesh" here is positive — living, responsive, bleeding tissue — not Paul's later negative use of *sarx*.)

## Verse 27: the mechanism and the goal

The passage gives two gifts in sequence: a "new spirit" (the human disposition renewed, v. 26) and "my Spirit" (God's own indwelling presence, v. 27). The resulting obedience is *caused* by God yet genuinely *performed* by the person — "ye shall keep my judgments, and do them." Philippians 2:12–13 holds the same structure: work it out, *for* God works in you both to will and to do.

And the telos is not improved morality but covenant: "ye shall be my people, and I will be your God" (v. 28). External compliance can exist between strangers, even enemies. The new heart exists inside a relationship — which is why the chapter insists this is done "not for your sakes" (v. 32). A heart capable of love can't be achieved under contract; it has to be given.
