In Luke 18:9-14, several aspects of the Pharisee's attitude and actions lead to his condemnation in the narrative of the parable:

1. **Pride:** The Pharisee's prayer is primarily about himself and his own righteousness. His approach shows a sense of superiority over others, suggesting a proud heart. The essence of his prayer is self-congratulatory rather than humble or truly reverent to God.

2. **Comparison and Contempt:** The Pharisee directly compares himself to others, notably saying, “I am not as other men are, extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even as this publican.” This not only shows his judgmental attitude but also his contempt for others, which is antithetical to many of Jesus' teachings on humility and love for one's neighbor.

3. **Use of Virtue as Self-Display:** His recitation of his righteous acts (fasting twice a week, giving tithes of all his possessions) is used more as a demonstration of his piety to justify himself rather than as genuine acts of faith and devotion. This transforms what could be seen as virtues into mere displays for self-justification and self-glorification.

None of these points directly accuse the Pharisee of false doctrine in the technical sense; he is not incorrect in practicing fasting and tithing, which were prescribed practices. However, the manner and the spirit in which he does them, and his attitude towards others, are what lead to his condemnation.

Conversely, the tax collector is seen as spiritually truer because:

1. **Humility:** He stands "afar off" and does not even feel worthy to lift his eyes to heaven, indicating a humble recognition of his own unworthiness and smallness before God.

2. **Repentance:** His prayer is simple and penitent: “God be merciful to me a sinner,” showing his acknowledgment of his sins and his need for God's mercy. This displays true self-awareness and contrition, attributes valued in spiritual teachings for genuine reconciliation and justification with God.

3. **Focus on Mercy Rather than Merit:** Unlike the Pharisee who enumerates his supposed righteous deeds, the tax collector focuses solely on seeking God's mercy, which aligns with the biblical teaching of salvation not being earned by deeds but being a gift of grace.

Jesus concludes that the tax collector returned to his home justified rather than the Pharisean. In this context, “justified” means being declared righteous before God. The justification here is not based on a tally of good deeds but rather on the repentant and humble seeking of God's mercy. This parable is a powerful testament against the dangers of spiritual pride and self-righteousness and an endorsement of humility, repentance, and the mercy of God as the true paths to righteousness.
