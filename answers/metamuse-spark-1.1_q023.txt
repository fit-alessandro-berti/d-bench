In Isaiah 55:6-7, repentance is described as a complete turn, because sin itself is complete — it lives in two places.

> Let the wicked forsake his way, and the unrighteous man his thoughts

There are two Hebrew words paired here intentionally:

**1. "Way" - *derek* [dֶּרֶךְ]**
This is a road, a journey, a habitual course of life. It is the outward, observable pattern of conduct — what a person *does* repeatedly. The *rasha* - the wicked - is defined by a wayward way.

**2. "Thoughts" - *machashaboth* [מַחֲשָׁבָה]**
This is not a fleeting thought. It is device, scheme, calculation, imagination, inner reasoning. The *ish aven* - literally "man of iniquity / trouble" - may not look as openly wicked, but his inner world is plotting, justifying, imagining evil.

This is Hebrew parallelism, but not just repetition. It is diagnosis from the outside in:

Why both?

**Outward conduct is not enough.** If a man only forsakes his *way* but keeps his *thoughts*, he has only changed his behavior for a season. The root is still there, and the way will return. That is mere reformation.

**Inward imagination is not enough.** If a man says he has changed his *thoughts* but keeps his *way*, his repentance is imaginary. True thoughts produce a new road.

Biblical repentance — *shuv*, to return — is not just turning *from* something, but turning *to* Someone. God wants the whole person.

Jesus makes this same connection later: adultery is both in the bed and in the heart [Matthew 5:28], murder is both in the hand and in anger [Matthew 5:21-22].

This is why verse 8 follows immediately:

> "For my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways, saith the Lord."

Our problem is both in our thoughts and our ways. And so God's answer must be higher than both.

That leads to the promise of mercy:

> ...and let him return unto the Lord, and he will have mercy upon him; and to our God, for he will abundantly pardon.

Two things are striking:

**1. Mercy is available for both.** God does not say "I will pardon the way but not the thoughts." Many people believe God might forgive what they *did* but not what they *are* inside — their secret imagination, resentments, fantasies, plans. This text says He calls for and pardons both.

**2. The pardon is abundant — *yarbeh lisloach*, literally "He will multiply to forgive."** The Hebrew idea is not just that He forgives a lot, but that He *multiplies* forgiveness to match multiplied sin. The depth of the diagnosis requires depth of grace.

"Seek ye the Lord while he may be found" does not mean God is hiding and then disappears. In the context of Isaiah 55, this is the exile invitation — God is now near through His prophetic word, offering covenant restoration. There is an urgent window to respond. Today is that nearness.

In short: God requires forsaking the outer way and the inner thought because sin reigns in both. And He promises abundant pardon because His mercy reigns over both.
