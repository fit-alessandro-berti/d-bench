Luke 6:37 is not about *eliminating* moral judgments entirely but about shifting the *intent* and *focus* of our judgments. The passage distinguishes between **discernment** (a necessary and godly act of evaluating truth) and **condemnation** (a sinful act of judging others with harshness, bitterness, or uncharitable motives). Here’s how they differ:

---

### **1. Discernment vs. Condemnation**
- **Discernment** (a biblical virtue, e.g., *Proverbs 12:15*; *1 Corinthians 12:10*) is the ability to distinguish between truth and error, good and evil, with wisdom and love. It is a *positive, constructive* act—like a doctor diagnosing a disease to prescribe healing, or a parent correcting a child to guide them toward righteousness.
  - *Example*: A pastor discerns that a church member is struggling with addiction and seeks to help them without judgmentalism.

- **Condemnation** (the opposite of grace, *Romans 8:1*) is the act of *rejecting* someone or something with unmerciful harshness, often fueled by pride, envy, or self-righteousness. It is a *negative, destructive* act—like a judge pronouncing guilt without mercy, or a sibling tearing down a sibling’s efforts.
  - *Example*: A coworker who dismisses a peer’s honest feedback because they fear their own inadequacies.

The command in Luke 6:37 does **not** say we should never discern truth—it says we should *avoid* condemning others with a heart of judgment. Discernment is allowed; condemnation is forbidden.

---

### **2. The Difference in Motive**
The key phrase is *"judge not"* (not *"don’t judge"*), which highlights the *attitude* behind the act. Jesus is not forbidding all moral evaluation but warning against judging with:
- **Self-righteousness** (*Matthew 7:1-5*): The sin of assuming we are better than others, so we have the right to pass judgment.
- **Harshness** (*James 4:11*): Judging with a heart of bitterness, instead of grace.
- **Ignoring the Golden Rule** (*Matthew 7:12*): Treating others as we would not want to be treated.

**Discernment** is like a *light* that reveals truth, while **condemnation** is like a *blinding glare* that shuts down grace. Both can involve "judging," but only one is commanded to stop.

---

### **3. The Role of Forgiveness**
The verse ties judgment to forgiveness (*"forgive, and ye shall be forgiven"*). Forgiveness is the antidote to condemnation because:
- It releases others from your judgmental grip.
- It models the same mercy you seek (*Matthew 5:44*).
- It aligns with God’s heart (*Luke 6:36*), who forgives us despite our sins.

Forgiveness does not mean ignoring wrongdoing (e.g., a thief should not be treated as innocent), but it means refusing to let bitterness or pride fuel your response.

---

### **4. Practical Application**
- **Discernment Example**:
  - You notice a friend struggling with addiction. You *judge* their actions as harmful to themselves and others, but you *discern* the need for help and act with compassion.
- **Condemnation Example**:
  - You *judge* the same friend’s struggle as "their fault" and *condemn* them by withholding support, assuming you’re "better" than they are.

The difference is in the *heart* behind the evaluation. Jesus does not say we should never evaluate—he says we should never condemn with a heart of judgment.

---
### **Key Takeaway**
Luke 6:37 is not about moral neutrality but about **purity of motive**. Discernment is a gift from God; condemnation is a sin. The command is to *avoid* the latter while embracing the former—with humility, love, and a focus on grace.
