• In Mark 8:34–35, “himself” is not mainly the basic biological person, but the self that is centered on self-protection
  and self-possessiveness:

  - my priorities and comfort,
  - my reputation and status,
  - my control over my life,
  - my claims to be the measure of what’s true or right.

  It is the ego-driven, self-directed way of living that says, “I must preserve my life on my own terms.”

  So “deny himself” points to saying: I choose to follow Jesus in a way that displaces self-rule with allegiance to God
  and the kingdom.

  Why it sounds liberating to some:

  - It promises a real freedom: you stop living by fear, comparison, and constant self-justification.
  - It reorders life around meaning, love, and service rather than performance and status.
  - It offers identity in something deeper than survival—faithfulness, purpose, and resurrection hope.

  Why it sounds offensive to others:

  - It can sound like a command to suppress personality, joy, or dignity.
  - Some hear “lose your life” and imagine emotional self-erasure, passivity, or passivity-based suffering.
  - In cultures that prize self-assertion, it feels like a threat to autonomy.

  A key tension is intentional: the verse is not asking for destruction of the person, but for a conversion of identity—
  from “I live to preserve me” to “I live in loyal response to Jesus, even at cost.”
