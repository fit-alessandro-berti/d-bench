In Matthew 6:1, the moral danger of public goodness lies in its unique power to corrupt the motive of the doer. To answer your question directly: **the problem is not the visibility itself, but the craving to be seen.** 

Jesus is not condemning the public nature of an act; He is condemning the orientation of the heart. Here is a deeper look at why public goodness is morally dangerous, and why the "craving" is the true issue.

### 1. Why Public Goodness is Morally Dangerous
Public goodness is dangerous because it offers an immediate, tangible reward—human approval—that can easily replace God as the ultimate object of our affection and motivation. 

*   **The Snare of Transactional Righteousness:** When you do good in secret, the only reward you can hope for is a spiritual or eternal one from God. But when you do good publicly, you can immediately "cash in" your righteousness for social capital—praise, respect, or influence. The danger is that the act of devotion to God becomes a commodity used to purchase human approval.
*   **The Illusion of Virtue (Theatrical Righteousness):** The Greek word used for "to be seen" is *theathēnai*, which is the root of the English word "theater." In the very next verse (Matt 6:2), Jesus calls those who do this "hypocrites" (*hypokritēs*), a Greek term used for stage actors who wore masks. Public goodness is dangerous because it allows a person to play the part of a worshiper or a saint without actually being one. It creates an illusion of intimacy with God for the benefit of an audience.
*   **The Nullification of True Reward:** Jesus says that if you do your alms to be seen, "ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven." The danger is the permanent forfeiture of divine approval. The person trades an eternal, weighty reward for a fleeting, hollow one. Once the applause fades, the transaction is complete; God owes them nothing else.

### 2. The Problem is the Craving, Not the Visibility
Jesus makes it clear that the moral failing hinges on the *purpose* of the act: doing it "to be seen of them." The visibility of the act is merely the medium through which the craving is fed. 

We know visibility itself is not the problem because of the broader context of Scripture. Just one chapter earlier, in Matthew 5:16, Jesus commands the exact opposite circumstance: *"Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."* 

How can Jesus command us to let men see our good works in Matthew 5, but warn us against doing good before men in Matthew 6? The difference lies entirely in the craving of the heart:

*   **In Matthew 5** (Visibility without the craving): The good work is public, but the goal is that the audience would look *past* the doer and glorify God. The doer is a window; God is the light.
*   **In Matthew 6** (Visibility driven by the craving): The good work is public, and the goal is that the audience would look *at* the doer and glorify them. The doer becomes the light, absorbing the glory meant for God.

Furthermore, Jesus Himself lived a highly public life of goodness. He healed in synagogues, fed thousands in the open, and taught on hillsides. His goodness was intensely visible, but His motive was never the craving for human approval. He sought only the will of the Father.

### Conclusion
Public goodness is like walking near the edge of a cliff. The cliff (visibility) is not inherently sinful, but it creates the perfect environment for a fatal fall (pride and self-worship). Jesus warns us to "take heed" (pay careful attention) because He knows the human heart is desperately susceptible to the intoxicating praise of others. The craving to be seen is the problem because it is a form of idolatry—it makes the approval of man the ultimate treasure, displacing the reward of the Father.
