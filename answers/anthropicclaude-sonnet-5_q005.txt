This teaching is difficult to take seriously because it cuts against something we experience as basic moral common sense: that love, respect, and good treatment should be *responses* to how people act. We think enmity should be met with resistance, and that failing to resist evil is a kind of complicity with it. So "love your enemies" sounds either naive—as if it denies that some people really are dangerous or unjust—or manipulative, a strategy for disarming opponents while still secretly regarding them as enemies.

**Why it's not sentimental**

Sentimental love wants to feel affection; it's about the emotional state of the lover. But the text specifies concrete actions—bless, do good, pray for—not feelings. You can do these while feeling nothing like affection, even while still calling the person an "enemy" (the term isn't retracted). This is a discipline of the will, not a feeling. It doesn't require you to think the enemy is good, or to stop resisting their actions in the world; it requires that your basic orientation toward their existence not be reducible to their treatment of you.

**Why it's not (merely) strategic**

If it were strategic—love your enemies because it disarms them, wins them over, gets better outcomes—then it would be conditional on working. But the passage grounds it in nothing about outcomes at all. It grounds it in what God does: sun and rain distributed without moral bookkeeping. The reasoning is theological/ontological, not consequentialist. If loving your enemy stops "working" (they remain hostile, or exploit your goodwill), the command doesn't dissolve. That's a sign it isn't strategy dressed up as ethics.

**Why it looks like a deeper moral strength**

The hard core of the passage is this: ordinary morality (love your neighbor, hate your enemy) makes your moral behavior *reactive*—determined by the other person's behavior toward you. Enemy-love asks you to make your moral behavior *self-determined*—rooted in your own character and, ultimately, in imitation of something like unconditioned generosity. That's why it's linked to becoming "children of your Father": the claim is that indiscriminate care (sun/rain on evil and good alike) is what divine, ungrudging existence actually looks like, and that human moral maturity means acting from a similarly unconditioned source rather than from tit-for-tat.

That's also why it's *hard*: reactive morality is almost automatic—it's how retaliation, grudges, and tribal loyalty work, and it feels like justice. Non-reactive love requires you to hold your treatment of others separate from your feelings about them, sustained without an external payoff (their reform, your safety, social approval). It's not a technique for defeating enemies; it's a demand that your moral center of gravity not be located in your enemy at all—that they not have the power to determine your character. That is a stronger, more demanding claim than either sentimentalism or strategy, and it's why the passage keeps unsettling people who otherwise think they have gotten Christian ethics "figured out."
