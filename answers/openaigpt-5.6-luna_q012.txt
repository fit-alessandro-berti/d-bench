Jeremiah 17:9–10 is both pessimistic and realistic, but its main concern is not that human beings are incapable of goodness or self-knowledge. It is that human self-understanding cannot be treated as a final authority.

In Jeremiah’s context, Judah is tempted to trust in human strength, political alliances, and its own judgment rather than in God (see 17:5–8). “The heart” in biblical language means more than emotion; it includes the mind, will, desires, and moral center. The claim is that this inner life is capable of disguising its own motives. We can sincerely believe that we are acting from justice, love, or principle while self-interest, fear, pride, or resentment are also involved.

The verse “who can know it?” is answered in verse 10: God searches and tests the heart. The passage’s point is not that motives are wholly unknowable, but that they are not transparently known by the person who has them. God’s judgment is needed because human self-assessment is partial and easily manipulated.

So one should speak about motives with **humility rather than either confidence or cynicism**:

- About oneself: “I may have mixed motives,” “I think part of this is fear,” or “I need to examine whether I am protecting my ego.”
- About others: distinguish observable actions from speculative motives. Say, “That decision had this effect,” or “I wonder whether fear influenced it,” rather than confidently declaring, “You did this because you are selfish.”
- About both: allow motives to be tested over time by patterns, consequences, sacrifices, consistency, and willingness to receive correction.

The heart’s unreliability does not mean that introspection is useless. It means introspection needs external tests: God’s word, prayer, wise and honest community, accountability, and attention to the “fruit” of one’s actions. Motives may be mixed, but they are not irrelevant; verse 10 still speaks of God judging people according to their ways and deeds.

Thus the passage offers a sober anthropology: people are prone to self-deception, but they are not beyond moral responsibility or growth. The appropriate posture is neither “I know my motives perfectly” nor “no one can ever know anything about themselves,” but: **I should seek truth about my motives while remaining open to being corrected by God and by reality.**
