Jesus uses a vivid, humorous, and hyperbolic metaphor in this passage to make his point about hypocrisy and self-righteousness. The image of someone trying to remove a speck from another person's eye while having a log or beam in their own eye is intentionally absurd and exaggerated. 

A few things make this metaphorical image so sharp and effective:

1. The vast difference in scale between a speck/mote and a beam/log. It highlights how the hypocrite's own faults are glaringly huge compared to the minor faults they criticize in others.

2. The idea of offering to fix someone else's small issue while being oblivious to one's own huge, obvious failing. This paints the hypocrite as lacking all self-awareness.

3. The impossibility of the task - you can't get close enough to remove a speck from an eye if you have a log protruding from your own eye. It shows the foolishness and futility of hypocritical fault-finding.

4. The physical comedy of the visual - it's an amusingly ludicrous image that lightens the mood while still landing the point.

At the same time, Jesus doesn't forbid identifying issues in others' lives altogether. He says "first" take the log out of your own eye, "then" you will see clearly to take the speck out of your brother's eye. 

The implication is that we shouldn't ignore faults or sin, in ourselves or others. But we must deal with our own shortcomings first before presuming to correct others. And when we do address others' faults, it must be with humility, gentleness and a keen awareness of our own failings - not with hypocrisy or self-righteousness.

So in a few memorable lines, Jesus humorously exposes the hypocrisy of harsh, judgmental fault-finding while still allowing for humble, gracious moral correction once we've examined ourselves. It's a call to moral integrity and humility rather than a blanket prohibition on identifying right and wrong.
