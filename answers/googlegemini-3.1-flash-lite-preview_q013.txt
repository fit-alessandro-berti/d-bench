The sharpness of God’s rejection in Amos 5:21–24 is rooted in the biblical concept of **covenantal integrity.** For the prophet Amos, worship is not an end in itself; it is a response to a relationship with God defined by justice. 

The passage moves beyond a request for "better balance" because, in the view of Amos, the Israelites were not merely out of balance—they were committing a fundamental spiritual fraud.

### 1. The Nature of the Fraud: The "Ritual-Ethical Dissonance"
The fraud being exposed is the belief that religious ritual can function as a "bribe" or a "cover" for moral corruption. 

In the ancient Near East, as in many later religious contexts, there was a temptation to view God through a transactional lens: if one performs the correct sacrifices, sings the right hymns, and observes the appointed feasts, God is "obligated" to provide blessing and protection. 

Amos dismantles this by highlighting a blatant contradiction:
*   **The Liturgy:** The people are engaging in "solemn assemblies," singing elaborate hymns ("the melody of thy viols"), and offering expensive sacrifices ("the fat beasts").
*   **The Life:** The society, however, was defined by the trampling of the poor, the perversion of justice in the courts, and the exploitation of the vulnerable (Amos 5:10–12).

God rejects the worship not because ritual is inherently evil, but because **it is being used to sanctify an unholy life.** It is a spiritual fraud because it offers God the "husk" of devotion while withholding the "kernel" of obedience.

### 2. Why Not "Balance"?
The passage rejects worship so sharply because "balance" implies two equally important, separate spheres: one for religion and one for social morality. Amos argues that these are not separate.

If one attempts to "balance" oppression with hymn-singing, the result is not devotion, but blasphemy. To offer a sacrifice while holding a bribe in one’s hand is to insult the recipient of the sacrifice. By demanding that "judgment run down as waters," Amos is asserting that **justice is the prerequisite for worship.** 

If the stream of justice has dried up, the "stream" of religious ritual becomes a stagnant, offensive pool. Asking for "balance" would suggest that the temple ritual still has value despite the injustice; Amos insists that until the injustice is remedied, the ritual does not just lose its value—it becomes an object of divine hatred.

### 3. The Reversal of Roles
The language—"I hate, I despise"—is intentionally jarring. It reverses the human expectation of what God desires. The people believed they were "pleasing" God with their festivals; Amos tells them they are actually making God nauseous ("I will not smell in your solemn assemblies").

This passage serves as a diagnostic tool for spiritual health:
*   **The Religion of Narcissism:** The songs and feasts were likely satisfying the worshippers’ own desire for status, community, and the feeling of piety.
*   **The Religion of Covenant:** Genuine worship, in the prophetic tradition, must align the human heart with the character of God. Since God is a God of justice, any worship that ignores the marginalized is, by definition, divorced from God.

### Conclusion
Amos 5:21–24 rejects the separation of the "sacred" (rituals) from the "secular" (social justice). It exposes the spiritual fraud of **"sacrificial substitution,"** where religious performance is used to anaesthetize the conscience. 

The prophet is not anti-worship; he is anti-hypocrisy. He is calling for a radical integration where the "mighty stream" of justice is the necessary condition that allows the "waters" of worship to flow freely once again. Without that justice, the noise of the choir is simply a distraction from the cry of the oppressed, which God is actually the one listening to.
