To understand why God rejects this worship so absolutely—rather than just asking for a "better balance"—we have to understand what worship actually is, and what it had become in the hands of the people Amos was addressing. 

Amos is not reacting to a slight imbalance, like a student who focuses too much on math and not enough on reading. He is diagnosing a **spiritual counterfeit**. In the biblical worldview, worship without justice is not just incomplete; it is an active, offensive rebellion against God’s character.

Here is a breakdown of why the rejection is so sharp, followed by the specific moral and spiritual fraud Amos is exposing.

---

### Part 1: Why no call for "balance"?

**1. Worship and justice are not two separate columns; they are the same currency.**
To ask for "better balance" implies that worship and ethics are two separate buckets, and Israel simply had too much in the worship bucket and not enough in the justice bucket. But in the covenantal theology of the Old Testament, love for God *is* love for neighbor. You cannot have one without the other. If you remove justice, you don't have "unbalanced worship"—you have a completely different religion. 

**2. The worship had become a cover-up.**
God doesn’t hate feasts, sacrifices, or music (He invented them). But when these acts are performed by hands that crush the poor, they cease to be acts of devotion and become acts of **smokescreening**. The sharp rejection is because the worship itself has been weaponized. It is an attempt to bribe God, appease conscience, and impress society while continuing to exploit the vulnerable. 

**3. Prophetic shock therapy.**
The Northern Kingdom of Israel was experiencing an economic boom under King Jeroboam II. They felt incredibly blessed and secure. They believed their lavish worship was proof that God was pleased with them. Amos uses violent, jarring language ("I hate, I despise," "noise of your songs") to shatter this delusion. A polite request for "more balance" would not wake a sleeping, arrogant, self-assured nation.

---

### Part 2: The Moral and Spiritual Fraud Exposed

Amos is pulling back the curtain on several layers of deep religious fraud. 

**Fraud #1: The "Alibi" of Piety**
The Israelites were using religion as an alibi. By showing up at the temple, singing loudly, and offering expensive animals, they were creating a public relations campaign for their own righteousness. The fraud is the belief that *performing religious duties grants a person a moral exemption from how they treat the marginalized.* They wanted the identity of "God's chosen people" without the ethical burden of actually acting like God's people.

**Fraud #2: The Transactional Bribe**
In the Ancient Near East, there was a common mentality that the gods were like cosmic vending machines: you put in a sacrifice, you get out a blessing. Amos exposes this as a fraud. God cannot be bought. The people believed that the "fat beasts" (v.22) would compel God to accept them, regardless of their greed and corruption. God is saying, "Your luxury does not impress me when it is built on the backs of the poor." 

**Fraud #3: Aestheticism over Substance**
Notice the language in verse 23: *"Take thou away from me the noise of thy songs; for I will not hear the melody of thy viols."* God doesn't call it music; He calls it *noise*. The fraud here is substituting emotional, aesthetic experience for moral substance. They likely had excellent musicians and beautiful liturgies, but because their hearts were harboring injustice, the most beautiful worship music in the world sounded like fingernails on a chalkboard to God. 

**Fraud #4: Oppression as the Unseen Offering**
If you read the chapter right before this (Amos 4:1-3), you see that the wealthy elite were crushing the poor, exploiting the needy, and living in grotesque luxury. When they brought their offerings to the temple, those offerings were essentially bought with the blood of the oppressed. They were offering to God the stolen wages of the poor. This is the ultimate fraud: parading stolen goods before the God of justice and expecting His applause.

### The Climax: "Let judgment run down as waters..."
In verse 24, God tells them what He actually wants: *justice* (mishpat) and *righteousness* (tsedaqah). 
*   **Justice (Mishpat)** is the active, systemic protection of the vulnerable, ensuring the poor aren't crushed by the courts or the economy. 
*   **Righteousness (Tsedeq)** is right-relationship, having a life and a society aligned with God's character.

God wants these to be like a "mighty stream"—a relentless, powerful, overflowing, unstoppable force. 

**Summary:** 
Amos rejects their worship sharply because it was a masquerade. The fraud was the illusion that God could be duped by stagecraft, sacrifice, and songs, while the daily reality of their society was a violation of everything He stands for. True worship is not an event you attend; it is the reflection of a life—and a society—aligned with the character of God.
