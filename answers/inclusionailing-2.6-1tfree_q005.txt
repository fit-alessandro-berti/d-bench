The difficulty with enemy-love is that it **refuses to be a technique**; it is not a strategy for conflict resolution, nor is it a feeling we can manufacture on demand. In its biblical form—as you quoted—it is a **revolutionary orientation of the self** that collides with our most basic intuitions about power, justice, and survival.

Here is why it feels so hard to take seriously, and why it is neither mere sentimentality nor mere strategy, but a deeper form of moral strength:

---

### 1. It Defies the Logic of Reciprocity
Human moral systems are largely built on **exchange**: you scratch my back, I’ll scratch yours; you harm me, I am licensed to retaliate (or at least to withhold goodwill). Enemy-love breaks this economy. It gives without guarantee of return, often to those who will continue to curse or persecute.  
This makes it **psychologically counter-intuitive**. We are wired to detect threat and respond in kind; to do otherwise feels like self-betrayal or self-endangerment. So we instinctively translate love into something safer—tolerance, civility, passive avoidance—and call that “loving your enemy.” But the text speaks of *blessing*, *doing good*, and *praying*—active, costly gestures.

---

### 2. It Refuses to Reduce the Enemy to a Category
To love an enemy is to refuse to let them remain merely an **obstacle or a symbol**. Our enemies are usually people we have turned into abstractions (“the oppressor,” “the heretic,” “the criminal”) so we can hate them cleanly. Enemy-love forces us to see them as **complex, suffering, image-bearers**—which is far more destabilizing than simple hatred.  
This is why sentimentalized versions of the command fail: they soften the enemy but leave our own sense of moral superiority intact. Real enemy-love softens **us**; it admits that we, too, are capable of the same cruelty, and that we, too, depend on grace.

---

### 3. It Is Not Strategic (in the Ordinary Sense)
Some try to defend enemy-love as **prudential**: *If you love your enemies, they may become friends; it breaks cycles of violence.*  
There is truth here—nonviolent resistance, for example, can expose injustice and create moral jiu-jitsu. But the biblical command is not conditional on results. Jesus says to love enemies **so that you may be children of your Father**, not so that you may convert them. The sun rises and rain falls without asking whether the fields are “deserving.” If we love only for strategic payoff, we are still treating the enemy as a means to our security—a subtle form of control, not love.

---

### 4. It Is a Deeper Moral Strength Because It Transforms Power
The strength of enemy-love is not weakness; it is **the strength to refuse to mirror the enemy’s methods while still confronting the evil they do**.  
- Hatred often claims strength but is actually **reactive slavery**: you are bound to the person you hate, your mood set by their actions.  
- Enemy-love claims **freedom**: you act from a center that the enemy cannot dictate.  

This is why it is terrifying: it strips away the victim’s moral excuse to become a mirror-image of the oppressor. It says: *Your cruelty does not get to define the moral universe.*  
In this sense, it is deeply **political**—not in the sense of winning elections, but in refusing to let violence have the final word about what it means to be human.

---

### 5. It Is Eschatological, Not Utopian
Finally, the difficulty comes from **impatience**. We want love to fix everything now. But the text connects enemy-love to the character of a Father whose justice includes both sunrise and rain—**gifts given before moral accounting**.  
This suggests enemy-love is not a tool to create a perfect world immediately, but a **sign of a different order already breaking into this one**. It is strength enough to **withhold the final judgment** that belongs to God, and to act in the present as if the future promised in the resurrection is already real.

---

### Summary
Enemy-love is difficult because it:
- **Refuses reciprocity**  
- **Refuses dehumanization**  
- **Refuses conditional calculation**  
- **Refuses to let evil have the last definitional word**  

It is not sentimental (it faces reality without flinching), not merely strategic (it does not require reciprocation to be valid), but a **deeper moral strength**: the capacity to remain human—and to recognize shared humanity—even when every instinct says to become monstrous in return.
