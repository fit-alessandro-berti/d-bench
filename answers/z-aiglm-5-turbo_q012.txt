To answer your first question: Jeremiah 17:9–10 is primarily **realistic about self-deception**, though its diagnosis is indeed grim. To call it merely "pessimistic" is to miss the theological cure that is embedded in the very next verse. 

Here is a breakdown of why it is realistic rather than just pessimistic, followed by how we should practically talk about our motives in light of it.

### Part 1: Realistic, Not Merely Pessimistic
Pessimism assumes the worst with no hope of improvement. Realism describes things exactly as they are, regardless of how uncomfortable that reality is. 

The Hebrew word translated "deceitful" (*aqash*) carries the imagery of a crooked, winding path or a braided trap. It means the heart is an active distorting agent. It does not merely *have* deceit in it; it *is* deceitful. It is a funhouse mirror that bends our self-perception. 

When the passage says the heart is "desperately wicked" (incurably sick in Hebrew), it is stating a factual human condition: our internal motivations are so compromised by self-interest, self-preservation, and pride that we cannot objectively evaluate ourselves. If you ask a corrupt judge to judge his own corruption, he will acquit himself. That is not pessimism; that is an accurate assessment of a conflict of interest.

Furthermore, verse 9 asks a rhetorical question: *"Who can know it?"* Verse 10 provides the answer: *"I the Lord search the heart."* The passage does not leave us in the dark. It shifts the burden of knowing our motives from ourselves (where it will fail) to God (where it succeeds). Therefore, it is profoundly realistic—it diagnoses the disease and immediately points to the only Physician who can cure it.

### Part 2: How to Talk About Motives
If the heart is an unreliable witness, how do we discuss our motives without falling into either brutal self-flagellation or willful self-justification? Here are four principles derived from this passage:

**1. Speak with "Qualified Humility" Rather Than Absolute Certainty**
Because the heart is deceitful, we should be incredibly cautious about declaring our own motives as purely virtuous. Instead of saying, *"I did this entirely out of love,"* the realistic Christian says, *"I hope this was loving, but I know I am capable of mixed motives."* We refuse to play defense attorney for our own hearts. We acknowledge that even our good deeds often have a shadow of pride, fear, or a desire for approval attached to them.

**2. Shift the Focus from the "Root" to the "Fruit"**
Notice that verse 10 says God judges "according to the fruit of his doings." While God searches the invisible root (the heart), we are called to evaluate the visible fruit (the actions). If you are unsure whether your motive in helping someone was genuine love or a desire to look good, look at the *fruit*. 
*   Did the help actually benefit them? 
*   Did you demand gratitude afterward? 
*   Were you resentful when they didn't respond the way you wanted? 
We cannot accurately interrogate our hidden motives, but we can objectively observe the trajectory and impact of our actions. Talk about what you *did* and the *impact* it had, rather than speculating too deeply on *why* you did it.

**3. Invite External Evaluation**
If your heart has a blind spot, you cannot see the blind spot by looking harder with the same eyes. You need an outside perspective. Talking about motives should be a communal exercise. We should invite trusted friends, spouses, or church community members to speak into our lives: *"Does this decision seem wise to you? Do you detect any arrogance in how I handled that situation?"* Proverbs 27:17 and Galatians 6:1 assume that we need others to help us see what we cannot see in the mirror.

**4. Pray the Prayer of Relinquishment**
When discussing motives, the ultimate posture is not to figure yourself out, but to hand yourself over to the One who already has. Psalm 139:23–24 is the perfect liturgical response to Jeremiah 17:9: *"Search me, O God, and know my heart! Try me and know my thoughts! And see if there be any grievous way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting!"*

When talking to God or others about why you are doing something, you can honestly say: *"I am doing this, and I believe it is the right thing to do. But I know my heart well enough to know it might be deceiving me. So I am asking God to search me, and I am asking you to help keep me accountable."*

### Summary
Jeremiah 17 is not a passage meant to paralyze us with cynicism, but to liberate us from the exhausting work of defending our own righteousness. Once you accept that your heart is an unreliable witness, you stop trying to prove how good you are, you stop trusting your gut implicitly, and you rest in the fact that God's judgment of you is based on His perfect searching, not your flawed self-awareness.
