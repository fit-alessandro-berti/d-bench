While all the options listed (anger, envy, wounded fairness, pride in obedience, and discomfort with mercy) describe real dimensions of the elder brother’s attitude, his **deepest problem is a fundamentally transactional view of his relationship with his father—which ultimately makes him hostile to grace and blind to true sonship.**

His deeper problem can be understood through three cascading layers, moving from surface symptoms to the spiritual root:

---

### 1. The Symptoms: Anger and Envy
Anger and envy are what appear on the surface (v. 28). He is furious that the prodigal receives a fatted calf while he claims he never even got a young goat. However, anger and envy are *reactions*, not the underlying condition. They are the friction created when his worldview collides with his father’s generosity.

### 2. The Mindset: Pride in Obedience and Wounded Fairness
Looking deeper, the elder brother operates entirely on a system of **merit, performance, and legalism**. 
* **"Lo, these many years do I serve thee..."** (v. 29): The word he uses for "serve" is tied to slavish labor (*douleuo*). He does not see himself as a beloved son enjoying a family inheritance; he sees himself as a dutiful employee earning wages.
* **"...neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment."** He relies on his moral record as his currency. 
* **Wounded Fairness:** Because he believes relationship with the father is a transaction (Obedience = Reward), the father’s joy over the returned prodigal feels like an absolute injustice. If a lawbreaker gets a feast for free, then the elder brother's lifetime of hard work feels wasted.

### 3. The Root Cause: Discomfort with Grace and Alienation from the Father's Heart
The elder brother’s *deepest* problem is that **he does not actually know or love his father, and therefore he cannot stomach mercy.**

* **He lives as an orphan in his own home:** The father gently reminds him, *"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine"* (v. 31). The elder brother could have enjoyed the father's presence and resources at any time. But he didn't want the father; he wanted the father's goods to "make merry with *my* friends" (v. 29). He is just as distant from the father's heart as his brother was in the far country—he just stayed in the yard.
* **He rejects grace because grace destroys his self-righteousness:** If acceptance is a free gift of grace given to repentant sinners, then his perfectionism and moral performance confer no special status. Mercy threatens his entire identity, which is built on being "better" than his brother.
* **He severs relational ties:** He tells his father, *"this thy son"* (refusing to say "my brother"). The father has to correct him: *"this thy brother."* By rejecting mercy, he rejects both his brother and his father.

---

### Conclusion
The elder brother's deepest problem is **his refusal to relate to his father on the basis of love and grace rather than duty and debt.** 

He is comfortable with *law* (where everything is earned and calculated), but profoundly **uncomfortable with *mercy*** (where love is freely given). In trying to earn what was already freely his as a son, he turns his loyalty into slavery, his obedience into resentment, and his father's house into a prison.
