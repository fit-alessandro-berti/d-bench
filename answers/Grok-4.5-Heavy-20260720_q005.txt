**Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously precisely because it is neither sentimental softness nor clever strategy first and foremost. It is a demanding form of moral strength that runs against deep human wiring and ordinary moral intuition.**

### Why it feels almost unserious
Human beings are built for reciprocity and coalition. Evolutionary psychology, everyday experience, and moral psychology converge here: we extend empathy and goodwill far more readily to in-group members; threat detection (amygdala-driven) and fairness heuristics push us toward retaliation or at least cool distance when someone harms or hates us. Lex talionis—“eye for eye”—is intuitive; extending the same sun and rain to the unjust feels like moral collapse or self-betrayal. Trauma, moral injury, and real-world track records amplify this. History is littered with people who practiced unilateral goodwill and were crushed or exploited. In high-stakes or zero-sum settings, enemy-love can look like low dominance, surrender of agency, or spiritual bypassing. Cultural allergy to anything that smells of weakness only intensifies the resistance.

The difficulty is therefore not a bug in the teaching; it is evidence of how high the bar is.

### Not primarily sentimental
A shallow reading treats “love your enemies” as warm feelings or vague positivity. The text itself resists that. The verbs are active and costly: bless, do good, pray for those who persecute you. The motive is not emotional uplift or self-soothing. It is ontological: “that ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven,” the one who already shows impartial providence. This is agape as willful goodwill—choosing the good of the other without requiring affection, approval, or trust—rather than sentiment. Feeling-based versions are easy to dismiss; the action-oriented, character-forming version is harder to wave away.

### Strategic benefits are real but secondary
Enemy-love can function strategically. Nonviolent resistance (Gandhi, King, aspects of South Africa’s transition) has sometimes disarmed opponents, exposed injustice, and broken cycles of retaliation in public moral spheres. Game-theoretic work on iterated cooperation even notes that occasional forgiveness can stabilize cooperation better than pure tit-for-tat. Psychological research on forgiveness (releasing chronic resentment) consistently shows benefits to the practitioner—lower stress, better mental and physical health—because hatred is metabolically and cognitively expensive. Holding the hot coal burns the hand that holds it.

Yet the Matthean text does not ground the command in results. Strategy is contingent on context and power dynamics; pure pacifism against totalitarian or predatory threats has often failed catastrophically. The primary logic is filial resemblance and self-mastery, not utility.

### Deeper moral strength
What remains is the most rigorous reading: enemy-love as a high form of moral and psychological strength. It requires overriding limbic threat responses with prefrontal discipline, refusing to let the enemy dictate the shape of one’s character, and practicing freedom from the corrosive grip of resentment. Hatred and revenge, however justified they feel, bind the agent to the offender. Willing the good of the hostile person (while still opposing the harmful action) is anti-fragile: it keeps the moral center of gravity inside the self rather than outsourcing it to the enemy’s behavior.

This does not collapse into moral equivalence. One can restrain the unjust, protect the vulnerable, pursue institutional justice, and still refuse personal hatred or the desire for the enemy’s destruction as an end. The ethic is primarily interpersonal and discipleship-oriented (Sermon on the Mount under empire), not a ready-made foreign policy or call to unilateral disarmament against existential threats. Serious practitioners—from early Christians under persecution to figures such as Bonhoeffer wrestling with the limits—have always felt the tension with other duties.

Nietzsche’s critique in *On the Genealogy of Morality* remains the sharpest counter: that such love is slave morality, an inversion born of powerlessness and covert resentment. The strongest reply is not denial of the risk of self-deception, but the observation that genuine versions demand more energy, courage, and self-command than either hatred or calculated reciprocity. They expand the moral circle without erasing distinctions and aim at a freedom that hatred cannot deliver.

In short, enemy-love is hard to take seriously because it is anti-instinctual, risky, and exacting. It is not sentimental fluff. Strategy is optional and context-dependent. At its core it is a rigorous form of moral strength: the refusal to be defined by one’s enemies and the disciplined imitation of an impartial generosity that does not wait for desert. Whether one accepts the theological framing or translates it into secular terms of self-mastery and anti-fragile agency, the difficulty itself is part of the point.
