Enemy-love is hard to take seriously because it sounds, at first, either **naive** or **impossible**.

Many hear it as sentimental: “Just be nice to everyone.” But in Matthew 5:44–45, Jesus is not describing soft feeling. He does **not** say, “Approve of your enemies,” or “Pretend evil is not evil.” He says: **love, bless, do good, pray**. These are disciplined acts of the will, not sentimental emotions.

It is also not merely strategic, though it can have strategic effects. Loving an enemy may sometimes disarm hostility or break cycles of revenge. But Jesus gives a deeper reason: **“that ye may be the children of your Father.”** The model is God, who gives sun and rain to both just and unjust. Enemy-love is therefore not mainly a tactic for success, but a way of sharing in the character of God.

Why is it so difficult?

1. **It offends our sense of fairness.**  
   We want goodness rewarded and evil punished. Enemy-love seems to blur that distinction.

2. **It threatens the ego.**  
   We often build identity through opposition: who is against us, who deserves our anger. Loving an enemy feels like surrendering moral superiority.

3. **It is costly.**  
   Retaliation is instinctive; benevolence toward an enemy requires self-mastery, patience, and vulnerability.

4. **It is easily misunderstood.**  
   People confuse love with excusing abuse, abandoning justice, or refusing boundaries. But enemy-love does not forbid judgment, protection, or resistance to evil. It forbids hatred and vengeance as the governing posture of the heart.

So is it sentimental, strategic, or moral strength?

It is **deep moral strength**. Specifically, it is the strength to refuse imitation of evil. The enemy hates, curses, injures; the natural response is to mirror that pattern back. Enemy-love breaks the chain. It says: “Your wrongdoing will not determine the shape of my soul.”

In that sense, enemy-love is one of the highest forms of moral freedom. It does not deny justice, but it refuses to let justice decay into revenge. It does not depend on warm feelings, but on a transformed vision of what human beings are before God.

So the command is difficult to take seriously precisely because, if taken seriously, it asks for something radical: not weakness, not passivity, not sentimentality, but a greatness of soul that resembles divine generosity.
