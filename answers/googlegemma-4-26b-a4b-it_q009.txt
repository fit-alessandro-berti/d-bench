To understand how Romans 12:20–21 defines victory, one must look past modern definitions of "winning" (which usually involve subjugation or destruction of the opponent) and look toward the biblical definition of "victory" (which involves the preservation of one's own character and the transformation of the opponent).

### 1. How does this passage define victory over evil?

In this passage, victory is not defined by the **defeat of the enemy**, but by the **defeat of the evil impulse within the self.**

*   **Victory is Moral Integrity:** If you respond to evil with evil, the evil has "overcome" you. You have allowed the enemy’s tactics to dictate your character. You have become a mirror of the very thing you despise. Therefore, victory is the ability to remain uncorrupted by the malice directed at you.
*   **Victory is Disarming the Opponent:** The goal is not to crush the enemy, but to break the cycle of retaliation. By responding to hunger with food, you disrupt the logic of the conflict. You deny the enemy the "victory" of turning you into a monster.
*   **The Paradox of the "Coals of Fire":** This phrase is often debated. Most scholars suggest that "heaping coals of fire" refers to the restorative power of shame or conviction. When someone expects a fight and receives kindness instead, the cognitive dissonance creates a "burning" sense of shame or a realization of their own wrongdoing. Thus, victory is achieved when the enemy’s hostility is converted into repentance.

### 2. Is the command weak, manipulative, or genuinely strong?

At first glance, the command to feed an enemy might seem **weak** (submissive) or **manipulative** (using kindness as a weapon to induce guilt). However, a deeper analysis suggests it is **genuinely strong.**

#### Why it is NOT Weak:
Weakness is characterized by an inability to act; it is being driven by fear or reflex. Responding to an insult with a meal requires immense **self-mastery**. It takes very little strength to strike back—it is a primal, reflexive impulse. It takes profound psychological and spiritual strength to override the survival instinct (fight or flight) and choose a counter-intuitive, sacrificial action. This is "strength under control."

#### Why it is NOT Manipulative:
Manipulation seeks to control an outcome for selfish gain. While the "coals of fire" might result in the enemy feeling guilty, the command is not predicated on *forcing* them to change. The command is about the *actor's* response, not the *target's* reaction. True manipulation asks, "How can I make him feel bad so I can win?" This command says, "How can I act with goodness so that evil has no foothold in me?" The focus is on the virtue of the believer, not the tactical exploitation of the enemy.

#### Why it is Genuinely Strong:
The strength of this command lies in its **asymmetric nature.** 
*   **It breaks the cycle:** Evil thrives on reciprocity (eye for an eye). By refusing to reciprocate, you break the chain of causality that keeps evil moving through the world.
*   **It asserts agency:** An evil person tries to control your emotions and actions. If they make you angry, they have won. By choosing to be kind, you assert total agency over your own soul. You are no longer a victim of their behavior; you are an architect of your own character.

### Summary
Victory, according to Paul, is **the refusal to let evil dictate the terms of your existence.** It is a "strength" that does not seek to destroy the person, but to destroy the malice, choosing the difficult path of grace over the easy path of retaliation.
