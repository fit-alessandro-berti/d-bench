The parable identifies the problem at the outset: Jesus tells it to people who “trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others.” That is the governing diagnosis. The Pharisee’s fasting and tithing are not condemned as practices in themselves. They were recognizable forms of Jewish piety, and the text does not say that his statements about his conduct were factually false.

What is condemned is the way those virtues function in his prayer.

- **His prayer is really a self-assessment.** He says, “I thank thee,” but the substance of the prayer is a list of his own credentials. God is scarcely addressed as the one from whom mercy and righteousness must be received.
- **His righteousness is comparative.** “I am not as other men are… or even as this publican.” Comparison becomes the basis of his confidence. He does not stand before God as a needy sinner but as a superior person.
- **His comparison becomes contempt.** The publican is not merely an example of conduct he rejects; he is an object of disdain. This is why the introductory description joins “trusted in themselves” with “despised others.”
- **His virtue becomes self-display.** Fasting and tithing may be good, but they have been converted into a claim on God and a reason for despising another person. The problem is not virtue but virtue used as a certificate of personal worth.

Thus, the Pharisee’s error is not chiefly “false doctrine” in the sense that he holds the wrong view about fasting or tithing. It is a false understanding of righteousness and of his relation to God: he treats righteousness as a possession that distinguishes him from inferior people, rather than as a gift for which a person remains dependent on divine mercy.

The tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer because it corresponds to reality as Jesus presents it. He stands “afar off,” will not raise his eyes, beats his breast, and prays, “God, be merciful to me, a sinner.” His bodily posture expresses contrition and dependence. More importantly, his words make no claim of comparative goodness. He does not say, “I am better than others,” nor does he offer a record of compensating achievements. He asks God for mercy.

The phrase can also be rendered, “God, be merciful to me, the sinner.” Whether or not one presses the definite article, the point is clear: he accepts the truth of his guilt and places himself entirely at God’s mercy. His humility does not earn justification; it is the posture that receives it. Jesus is not saying that shame, lowered eyes, or beating one’s chest mechanically produce forgiveness. He is contrasting self-reliance with repentant trust.

Nor does the parable imply that tax collecting and exploitation are morally acceptable, or that the tax collector is better in every respect than the Pharisee. It gives no account of his later conduct. Its point is the basis on which each man approaches God. The Pharisee approaches with a résumé; the tax collector approaches with a plea.

That is why Jesus says the tax collector went home “justified rather than” the Pharisee. The decisive contrast is:

- **self-made righteousness versus received mercy;**
- **superiority over others versus repentance before God;**
- **religious performance as self-display versus honest dependence.**

The Pharisee’s good deeds do not make him condemned; his reliance on them, his comparison, and his contempt reveal that he has closed himself to mercy. The tax collector’s humility is truer because it tells the truth about the human condition before God: whatever one’s moral record, one stands in need of grace.
