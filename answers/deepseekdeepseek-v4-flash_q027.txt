This is one of the most surgical passages in Scripture for diagnosing the human heart. To answer your question directly: **the Pharisee is condemned by a compound sin where pride, comparison, and the use of virtue as self-display are all symptoms of a single root—self-justification.**

Let’s break down your options and then examine the tax collector.

### What Exactly Condemns the Pharisee?

The options you listed are not mutually exclusive; they are a chain of causation. But the *root* is **trusting in himself** (Luke 18:9). Jesus explicitly frames the parable for an audience that "trusted in themselves that they were righteous." Everything else flows from that.

1.  **False Doctrine?** Not in the sense of a wrong creeds. The Pharisee believes in God, the Law, tithing, and fasting. His *doctrine* was largely orthodox. Where he goes wrong is in his *soteriology*—his understanding of how one is made right with God. He believes his own performance earns and secures God's favor. This is self-salvation, which is indeed a profound doctrinal error (salvation by works).

2.  **Pride?** Yes, this is the immediate engine. Pride is the internal disposition that says, "I am inherently better or have achieved more than others." The Pharisee's prayer is not a report of gratitude; it’s a performance for an audience of one—himself (he "prayed thus with himself").

3.  **Comparison?** This is the *mechanism* of the Pharisee's pride. He doesn't stand *before God*; he stands *beside the tax collector*. His identity depends on being "not like other men." Comparison is the toxic fuel. He cannot know his own worth without diminishing another's.

4.  **Contempt?** This is the *direct fruit* of comparison. The Greek word for "despised" others (v. 9) implies "looked down on" or "set at nothing." His praise of God is, in practice, a demonization of the tax collector. "Even as this publican" is not a prayer; it’s a sneer.

5.  **The Use of Virtue as Self-Display?** This is the *performance*. He fasts and tithes—genuinely good acts—but he uses them as a trophy. They become a shield against the need for mercy and a ladder to look down on others.

**The core condemnation:** The Pharisee is condemned because **he asks for nothing and receives nothing.** He comes to God with a full account book, demanding payment, not seeking grace. He is "locked into" himself. He is his own standard, his own judge, and his own savior. He leaves the temple just as he entered—forgiven of nothing, because he never asked.

### Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?

The tax collector’s posture is not just "more humble"; it is **more accurate.** It corresponds to reality.

1.  **Spiritual Honesty (Truth in the inward being):** The tax collector knows the fundamental truth of the human condition before a holy God: "I am a sinner." He doesn't compare himself to the Pharisee. He doesn't say, "At least I’m not a Pharisee." He stands alone before God. This is the beginning of true knowledge.

2.  **Total Dependence (The Essence of Faith):** His prayer, "God, be merciful to me, a sinner," is a cry of absolute helplessness.
    - The Greek word for "be merciful" is *hilaskomai*, which is rich with temple imagery. It means "be propitiated" or "be the place of atonement for me." He is essentially saying, "Be the mercy seat for my sin."
    - He offers no resume. He does not say "I have repented enough" or "I will do better." He casts himself entirely on God's character, not his own. **This is faith.**

3.  **The Perfect Receiving Posture:** The opposite of self-justification is justification as a gift. The tax collector is open-handed. He has nothing to give, only everything to receive. Jesus declares he "went down to his house justified." The Greek word *dedikaiomenos* is in the perfect tense—he was pronounced righteous, and he *remains* righteous. He walked in a sinner and walked out a saint, not because he changed his moral record, but because God imputed righteousness to him in response to his cry for mercy.

### The Deep Answer

The Pharisee is condemned because his prayer is a **monologue of self-congratulation** disguised as a dialogue with God.
The tax collector is justified because his prayer is a **genuine dialogue of desperation** that opens the door for Divine intervention.

Jesus is not condemning virtue (fasting and tithing are good). He is condemning the **functional atheism** of trusting in virtue. The Pharisee trusted his own righteousness; the tax collector trusted in God’s mercy. One was his own savior; the other had no savior but the Lord.

**In short:** The tax collector was "truer" because he told the truth about himself and the truth about God. The Pharisee lied about himself (he was not righteous) and thereby misrepresented God (as a debtor, not a giver of grace).
