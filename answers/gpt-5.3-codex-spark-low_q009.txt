• The passage defines victory over evil as a moral and spiritual strategy, not a power move.
  It says evil is not defeated by matching it, but by refusing to let it control your actions.

  Romans 12:20–21 says to answer hostility with generosity and kindness, and closes with: “Do not be overcome by evil,
  but overcome evil with good.”
  So victory is:

  1. Not domination (you win by harming them).
  2. Not passive withdrawal (you still act).
  3. Transformative witness (your good action exposes and dismantles evil’s hold).

  The “heaping coals of fire” phrase is traditionally read as letting your kindness expose the wrongdoer’s conscience,
  shaming them into repentance, and demonstrating a higher standard of life.
  So it is generally genuinely strong, because it demands self-control, courage, and moral discipline in the face of
  real harm. It is not manipulative if it’s not aimed at provoking guilt for control; it is strategic and gracious moral
  resistance. It becomes manipulative only if used to secretly engineer someone’s shame rather than to reflect
  integrity.
