**The craving to be seen, not visibility itself.**

### What the verse is actually saying

Jesus is not condemning every public act of goodness. He is condemning the *motivation* that turns a good deed into a performance designed to win human approval. The key Greek phrase is *πρὸς τὸ θεαθῆναι αὐτοῖς* (“in order to be seen by them”). The danger is not that the act is visible, but that it is *done with the deliberate aim* of being noticed and admired.

This is confirmed by the immediate literary context:

- The verse is the heading for the entire section on “piety before men” (Matthew 6:1–18).
- Jesus then gives three examples: **charity** (vv. 2–4), **prayer** (vv. 5–15), and **fasting** (vv. 16–18).
- In each case the pattern is the same: the *hypocrites* (literally “play-actors”) perform the act in the most conspicuous place possible *so that* people will praise them.
- Jesus’ alternative is not “never let anyone see you,” but “do it in such a way that the desire for human applause is not the driving force.”

### Why the craving for visibility is morally dangerous

1. **It changes the audience and therefore the reward**  
   Jesus says: “otherwise you have no reward *with your Father who is in heaven*.”  
   The deed is still “good” in its external form, but the moral and spiritual value has been cashed in for a cheaper currency—social approval. You have already received your reward (*apechousin*, v. 2, 5, 16—the same commercial term for “they have received payment in full”). There is nothing left to be rewarded by God.

2. **It is a subtle form of idolatry**  
   The heart that needs to be seen has made other people’s opinion its functional god. It is seeking the glory that belongs to God alone (cf. John 5:44; 12:43). This is why Jesus calls such people “hypocrites”—they are acting a part whose real purpose is self-glorification disguised as piety.

3. **It corrupts the character**  
   Repeatedly performing good deeds for applause trains the soul to value appearance over reality. Eventually the person becomes incapable of doing good when no one is watching. The external act may still be impressive, but the inner life is hollow.

### Visibility is not the problem

Jesus Himself performed many miracles and acts of mercy in public. He taught crowds, healed in synagogues, and was crucified in the most public place possible. The apostles likewise did their good works “before men” (Matthew 5:16—“let your light shine before others, so that they may see your good works and give glory to your Father who is in heaven”). The difference is motive:

- Matthew 5:16 → Do good *so that God will be glorified*.
- Matthew 6:1 → Do not do good *so that you will be glorified*.

The same visible act can be commanded in one verse and warned against in the next because the moral danger lies in the heart, not in the visibility.

### Practical test Jesus gives

- If no one ever knew you did it, would you still do it?
- If the only reward you received was God’s quiet approval, would that be enough?

If the answer is no, then the act—even if it looks like “alms”—has become morally dangerous, exactly as Jesus warns in Matthew 6:1.

**Summary:**  
The verse does not forbid public goodness. It forbids the *craving to be seen* as the motive for goodness. That craving steals the deed’s eternal value, turns virtue into表演, and exchanges the reward of the Father for the cheap applause of men. Visibility is neutral; the hunger for an audience is deadly.
