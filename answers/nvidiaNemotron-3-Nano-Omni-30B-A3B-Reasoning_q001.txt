**Answer**

Genesis 4:6‑7 presents a **moral‑agency picture that emphasizes personal choice and responsibility**.  

| Aspect | How the text portrays it |
|--------|--------------------------|
| **Nature of sin** | Sin is **“lying at the door”** – a latent, potential force that can enter a person’s life, but it is **not an external beast or impulse**. It is a condition that can be **invited or resisted** through one’s own decisions. |
| **Evil as impulse/beast** | The passage does **not** treat evil as a wild, uncontrollable beast. Instead, it frames it as a **possibility that arises from the heart** (“if thou doest not well”). The “desire” mentioned is the **inner longing** that can lead a person toward wrongdoing, but that desire is **subject to the person’s will**. |
| **Habit / temptation** | While “habit” and “temptation” are related concepts, the verse does **not** present them as the primary source of sin. It points to the **initial moral decision** (“if thou doest well… if thou doest not well”). The “door” metaphor suggests a **threshold** that a person can open or keep shut. |
| **Responsibility** | The text places **full responsibility on the individual**: <br>• **Self‑assessment** – “Why art thou wroth?” (a call to examine one’s own attitude). <br>• **Choice** – “If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?” (a conditional promise). <br>• **Consequence** – “If thou doest not well, sin lieth at the door” (the outcome depends on the person’s action). <br>• **Authority** – “thou shalt rule over him” (the person must **master** the sin, exercising dominion over it rather than being mastered by it). |
| **Overall picture** | Evil is **latent potential** that becomes actual when a person **chooses** to act contrary to what is right. The moral agency is **active**: the individual must **recognize** the danger, **choose** the good path, and **exercise self‑control** to keep sin from entering. The verse therefore stresses **personal accountability** and the **capacity to rule over** (i.e., master) one’s own sinful inclinations. |

**In summary:**  
- Evil is depicted as a **potential condition** that can “lie at the door,” not as an external, unstoppable force.  
- The person bears **full moral responsibility**: he must **self‑examine**, **choose** righteousness, and **exercise dominion** over any sinful desire that arises. The passage thus portrays moral agency as **choice‑driven** with a clear call to **rule over** sin rather than be ruled by it.
