This is an excellent question that gets to the heart of the passage. The trust described in Proverbs 3:5–6 is not a blind leap into the dark, but a specific kind of trust that indeed requires careful distinction.

The kind of trust asked for here is **relational, covenantal, and holistic trust.** It is the trust a follower places in a wise, loving, and sovereign guide who has a proven track record (in the biblical context, God's faithfulness to Israel).

Here is how humble self-distrust differs from mere anti-intellectualism:

### 1. The Object of Trust
*   **Humble Self-Distrust:** Its focus is on the **limitation of the self**. It says, "My own perspective, no matter how intelligent or well-researched, is finite and flawed. I might be wrong." The trust is then placed in a superior, external source of wisdom and goodness (here, "the Lord").
*   **Anti-Intellectualism:** Its focus is on the **distrust of a method or tool** (reason, evidence, scholarship). It often says, "Thinking is inherently suspect, and intellectual pursuit is a prideful distraction from true faith." The object of trust may shift to raw emotion, authority alone, or a simplistic reading that rejects all interpretation.

### 2. The Relationship to Understanding
*   **Humble Self-Distrust:** Does **not** ask you to "lean not unto thine own understanding" by abandoning your mind. Instead, it asks you to **subordinate** it to a higher authority. You use your understanding as a tool, but you hold it with open hands, acknowledging its limits. This is **Proverbs 1:7** in action: "The fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge." The intellect is engaged but submitted.
*   **Anti-Intellectualism:** Often **abandons** the tool of understanding altogether in matters of faith. It can create a false dichotomy between "heart" (trust) and "head" (reason). This is foreign to Proverbs, which is a book *of wisdom* that deeply values disciplined thinking, learning, and insight (e.g., Prov. 18:15, 2:1-6).

### 3. The Resulting Path
*   **Humble Self-Distrust:** Leads to **"acknowledging him in all thy ways."** This means bringing your intellect, your plans, your analysis, and your decisions into a dialogue with your faith. It's integrative. Your reason serves your relationship with God.
*   **Anti-Intellectualism:** Can lead to a faith that is disconnected from daily life and decision-making ("ways"), or one that requires checking your brain at the door of the sanctuary. It often creates an unnecessary conflict between faith and learning.

### A Helpful Metaphor:
Imagine you are navigating a ship. **Reason and experience** are your maps, compass, and knowledge of the stars. **Humility** is the captain acknowledging that the sea is vast, storms are unpredictable, and even the best charts might have errors. **Trust in the Lord** is like trusting the ship's experienced designer and owner, who is also in radio contact with you, to guide you through unfamiliar waters.

*   **The humble self-distrustful sailor** uses his instruments diligently but is ready to alter course based on the owner's guidance.
*   **The anti-intellectual sailor** might throw his compass overboard, declaring it useless, and just steer by "feeling." Or he might stare at the compass, refusing to listen to any radio communication, trusting the tool alone.

In summary, **Proverbs 3:5-6 calls for a trust that is deeply intellectual in its humility and relational in its orientation.** It is the antithesis of arrogance in one's own grasp of truth, but it is also the affirmation that our God-given faculties are best used when submitted to and guided by God Himself. It is faith that thinks, and thought that believes.
