**Answer:**

1. **The logical link**  
   - The verse sets up a *causal* relationship: **love of the visible (the brother) → love of the invisible (God)**.  
   - If someone truly loves the unseen God, his heart must be oriented toward the visible expressions of God’s character—namely, other people.  
   - Conversely, if he *hates* or *disparages* the person he can see, his claim to love an unseen deity is internally inconsistent. He cannot genuinely love God while rejecting the very “image” of God that is present in his neighbor.

2. **What self‑deception is exposed**  
   - **Hypocrisy / “religious” pretense** – The man is pretending to have a sincere, internal devotion while his outward behavior betrays animosity. The verse calls this a *lie* because the claim (“I love God”) is not backed by the evidence of his treatment of the neighbor.  
   - **Self‑justification** – He may rationalize his hatred (“I’m angry for a good reason”) and still claim divine love, thereby deceiving himself about the true condition of his heart. The text shows that love cannot be compartmentalized; it must be holistic.  
   - **Divisive “us vs. them” mindset** – By treating the neighbor as a separate category from God, the verse exposes a false dichotomy. It reveals that the love of God is not an abstract, isolated feeling but a relational love that necessarily includes love for the people God has placed in our lives.

3. **Why the neighbor is the test**  
   - **Visibility** – The brother is a concrete, observable reality. Love for the invisible requires a *tangible* outworking; otherwise love remains a mental abstraction.  
   - **Imitation of God** – In the biblical narrative, loving the neighbor reflects the character of the God who loves us (e.g., “Love your neighbor as yourself” – Leviticus 19:18; Matthew 22:39). If we cannot love the visible image of God’s love, our love for the invisible source is suspect.  

**In short:** The verse uses the visible neighbor as a *litmus test* because authentic love for the unseen God must manifest in concrete, outward love for the people we can see. It exposes the self‑deception of claiming divine love while harboring hatred or indifference toward the very individuals God has placed in our sight.
