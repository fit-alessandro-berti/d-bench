Excellent and nuanced questions—let’s explore the subtleties at work in this parable.

### 1. **What Condemns the Pharisee?**

The parable doesn’t charge the Pharisee with doctrinal error or flagrant immorality; rather, it highlights **attitude and heart posture**. The most direct condemnation centers on:

#### **a. Pride and Self-Righteousness**
- The Pharisee “trusted in [himself] that [he] was righteous,” which is explicitly introduced in verse 9.
- He rehearses his virtues not in gratitude, but seemingly as a ledger of superiority (“I fast… I tithe…”).
- His prayer is less a plea and more a presentation of his spiritual résumé.

#### **b. Comparison and Contempt**
- He defines his own righteousness by contrast: “God, I thank thee, that I am not as other men are…” and specifically “or even as this publican.”
- Despising others is highlighted as a root issue (“despised others”—v. 9).

#### **c. Virtue as Self-Display**
- The Pharisee’s prayer is performative, focused on his spiritual accomplishments before God and others, rather than on God’s mercy or his own need.

#### **d. Lack of Humility**
- Unlike the tax collector, he shows no self-suspicion or awareness of personal fault.

**Summary:**  
It is not simply the Pharisee’s external practices (which are, in themselves, good), nor is it a matter of false doctrine. The core condemnation is:
- **Prideful self-reliance**  
- **Comparative contempt for others**  
- **Treating virtue as a ground for justification with God**  
- **A lack of humility and dependence on God’s mercy**

### 2. **Why Is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?**

The publican (tax collector), by contrast, exemplifies the heart-response that God honors:

#### **a. Humility**
- He “would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven,” showing awareness of his unworthiness.

#### **b. Contrition**
- “Smote upon his breast” (physical sign of repentance and grief for sin).

#### **c. Confession and Dependence**
- His prayer is: “God, be merciful to me, a sinner.”
- He pleads for mercy, not merit.

#### **d. Absolute Honesty**
- He does not excuse or minimize his sin but stands bare before God.

**Summary:**  
His posture is “spiritually truer” because:
- It recognizes the reality of his sin and need for grace.
- It depends wholly on God’s mercy, not self-justification.
- Jesus says *“this man went down to his house justified rather than the other”* because **humble repentance, not proud achievement, is the condition for justification before God.**

### **Synthesis**

- The Pharisee is condemned not for his virtues, but for turning them into tools of pride, comparison, and self-salvation.
- The tax collector’s posture is truer because it aligns with the reality of all humans before God: in need of mercy, unable to justify ourselves.

**In summary:**  
**Pride, comparison, and treating virtue as self-display are what actually condemn the Pharisee.** The tax collector models the only posture that leads to justification—humble, honest, dependent repentance. This is at the heart of the gospel: “those who exalt themselves will be humbled, and those who humble themselves will be exalted” (Luke 18:14).
