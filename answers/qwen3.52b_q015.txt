### 1. The Sharpness of the Image: Visual Metaphor and Cognitive Contrast

The sharpness of Matthew 7:3–5 lies in its **visual metaphor** designed to highlight a cognitive disproportion between two scales of reality:

*   **Scale (Relative Size):** The text establishes an analogy by contrasting the "mote" (a speck of dust) against the "beam" (a massive object). To a human observer, both are objects you can see with the naked eye. However, when comparing them directly, the beam is objectively much larger. By using these two specific images, the passage forces a reader to confront the *disparity in perception*.
*   **Perspective (The Lens of Judgment):** The image sharpens because it illustrates that human judgment often fails because we focus on the small "mote" while our own internal standards are obscured by "the beam." This visual metaphor is used to make a logical claim: if you cannot see the mote clearly (if you don't see your own sin), how can you judge others fairly? It highlights a **cognitive bias** inherent in human vision.
*   **The Magnifying Glass Analogy:** The "eye" functions as the magnification tool. A sharp image is achieved when we realize our own vision is limited by where we focus. By showing the two images side-by-side, the passage demonstrates that judgment isn't just about *accuracy*, but about who or what we are looking at. If you are looking at a beam (your heart/ego) instead of a mote (sin), your view is blocked.

### 2. Criticizing Hypocrisy Without Forbidding Correction

The passage addresses hypocrisy without banning moral correction in two distinct ways:

*   **Hypocrisy Defined:** The term "hypocrite" here refers to those who judge others with harshness while simultaneously maintaining their own ego ("Thou hypocrite"). In this context, hypocrisy means judging by your own standards rather than recognizing the objective reality of God’s law. It implies a person who condemns others for minor faults ("mote") but fails in self-reflection due to a similar blindness ("beam"). The author critiques this **inconsistent moral behavior**.
*   **Moral Correction as a Prerequisite, Not a Punishment:** The text does not forbid moral correction; it frames it as a necessary logical step. By commanding the reader to "first cast out the beam," the passage clarifies that one cannot simply ignore self-correction to focus on others. Instead, one must remove the internal obstacle (the beam) before external judgment.
    *   **The Sequence:** The logic dictates removing the larger impediment (the own eye/ego) first, then the smaller issue (the mote/others). This avoids moral correction becoming a "license" to continue judging while ignoring self-confrontation.
    *   **The Goal:** "Shalt see clearly." Moral correction is not about changing an external act of worship; it is about fixing one's internal lens ("eye") so that spiritual clarity returns ("thou shalt see"). By demanding this order, the text maintains high moral standards without banning any form of ethical correction—it simply demands that we do not judge *other* people until we are willing to judge *ourselves*.

### Conclusion
Matthew 7:3–5 uses a sharp visual contrast of size to highlight human perception and judgment. It criticizes hypocrisy by labeling the act of judging others while failing to reflect on oneself as inconsistent. However, it does not forbid moral correction; rather, it establishes that spiritual clarity can only be achieved through self-removal before addressing external issues. This approach prevents hypocrisy from becoming passive negligence, ensuring that the removal of the "beam" happens before judgment is ever applied to a "mote."
