Repentance in Isaiah 55:7 requires forsaking both the “way” and the “thoughts” because Scripture views sin as a problem that affects the whole person—outward actions and inward disposition. The two are not treated as separate options but as connected realities that must both be addressed.

### The Distinction and Its Significance

- **“His way”** refers to a person’s outward conduct and course of life—the habitual patterns of behavior, decisions, and practices. In biblical language, a “way” is a road or path one travels. To forsake one’s way means to stop walking in the direction of sin in practical, observable terms. This is the external, visible dimension of repentance.

- **“His thoughts”** refers to the inner life: the reasoning, imagination, desires, plans, and motives of the heart. In Hebrew thought, the “thoughts” often include the intentions and mental frameworks that give rise to action. Forsaking the thoughts means refusing to continue feeding or justifying sin internally.

The prophet places both side by side because they are organically linked. Actions flow from the heart (see Proverbs 4:23; Jeremiah 17:9), yet the heart is also shaped and hardened by repeated actions. Partial repentance—changing behavior while keeping the same inner world, or feeling different inside while continuing in the same patterns—is not what God calls for here.

### Implications for Outward Conduct

By requiring the wicked to forsake his *way*, the text makes clear that repentance cannot remain merely internal or emotional. Genuine turning to God will eventually show itself in changed behavior. A person cannot continue in the same sinful habits, relationships, or lifestyle and claim to have returned to the Lord. The call is concrete: stop walking in that direction.

### Implications for Inward Imagination

By also requiring the forsaking of *thoughts*, the passage guards against superficial or hypocritical change. God is not satisfied with outward reformation that leaves the mind and heart untouched. The imagination must be brought under the lordship of God—wrong desires must be rejected, sinful reasoning must be abandoned, and the inner life must be reoriented toward Him. This is why the text speaks of the “unrighteous man” forsaking his thoughts; even if someone manages to clean up their external life for a time, if the thoughts remain corrupt, the root problem has not been dealt with.

### Implications for the Possibility of Mercy

This double requirement actually magnifies the mercy being offered rather than restricting it. The passage does not say that only those with minor sins or already-improved lives may return. It addresses the *wicked* and the *unrighteous*—those whose ways and thoughts are thoroughly corrupt—and still promises that if they return, God “will have mercy” and “will abundantly pardon.”

The inclusion of both “way” and “thoughts” shows that mercy is available even when the problem is deep and comprehensive. God does not demand perfect performance before offering pardon; He demands honest turning. The fact that He calls people to deal with both the visible and the invisible dimensions of sin demonstrates that His forgiveness is not cheap or superficial. He is willing to pardon those who come to Him with nothing to offer except their sin, provided they are willing to relinquish it in both its external and internal forms.

In short, the verse presents repentance as a whole-person return. The outward conduct must change, the inward imagination must be surrendered, and when both occur in a genuine turning to the Lord, mercy is promised in abundance.
