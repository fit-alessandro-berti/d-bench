In Philippians 2:5-8, the Apostle Paul is praising the "great humility" demonstrated by Jesus Christ, which is a profound form of spiritual and divine power. This passage is encapsulating the essence of Christ's incarnation and his ultimate act of submission and sacrifice for humanity'se salvation. Here's a breakdown of the concepts and their implications for the nature of power being praised:

1. **Formlessness in Godhood:** Paul speaks of "formlessness in God" when referring to Jesus before his incarnation. This implies an omnipotent and divine nature, but one that is not constrained by human form or physical power. It's a divine essence that transcends human limitations, suggesting a power that is limitless and divine, not limited by ego or worldly ambition.

2. **Rejection of Equal Glory:** Jesus did not cling to or take pride in His divine glory. He chose to "make himself of no reputation," which means that He refused the claim to equality with God in terms of power and exaltation. This act can be seen as an extraordinary demonstration of humility and the rejection of arrogance.

3. **Humble Servitude:** He took on human form ("made in the likeness of men") and chose to serve others ("as a man"). In this form, He embraced the role of a servant, which is contrary to the expectations of a divine being on earth. This is an act of selflessness and puts others before His own.

4. **Self-Humbility:** Jesus deliberately chose a lowly profile ("humbled Himself"), choosing to serve rather than lord over others, despite having all power. This humility is not simply a lack of strength but an active choice to embrace a position that emphasizes service, love, and respect for others.

5. **Eternal Obedience:** The willingness of Jesus to endure "the death of the cross" signifies an unwavering commitment to God's will and humanity's greater good. Death, especially in the form of crucifixion, was an ultimate sacrifice, a form of total surrender for the benefit of others, which signifies the depth of His love and humility.

The kind of power being praised here is not dominating power or might that imposes itself upon others. Rather, it's a gentle, sacrificial, and self-effacing form of strength that puts others first—an act of love, servanthood, and submission.

**Why humility joined to greatness feels unsettling compared to open domination:**

- **Selfless Service vs. Self-aggrandizement:** Societies often celebrate leaders who take center stage, emphasize their own accomplishments, and elevate their status above others. Jesus's decision to empty Himself and serve reflects a radical break from this conventional power ethic. It subverts expectations that those with greatness should also seek to rule or dominate, rather than use their position to help, uplift, and empower others.

- **Reality vs. Illusion:** Greatness typically carried with it a sense of entitlement and authority. Jesus, however, chose not to exploit His divine strength for his gain but to become obedient and mortal. This reality can be unsettling for those accustomed to a relationship with power structured around hierarchy, where those at the top view themselves as separate and superior to those beneath them.

- **Grace Over Gratitude:** The idea that He would perform great deeds not with the expectation of reward or recognition but out of love and grace challenges the transactional nature of leadership and authority. It suggests that true greatness can arise from genuine humility, not just from seeking and maintaining power.

- **Sacrifice of Power:** By giving up His right to God's exalted status, Jesus demonstrated that true power lies in what can be sacrificed for others. This reverses the normal dynamics where those with power seek to hold onto it, sometimes regardless of the cost to others. Jesus's choice shifts the perspective on authority from one of control and retention to one of service and generosity.

- **Empathy and Identification:** When a being of greatness walks humbly alongside us, it can be deeply unsettling because it blurs the lines between the powerful and the powerless, the ruler and the ruled. It humanizes the omnipotent and suggests that rulership can be grounded in empathy and shared humanity.

- **Moral Transcends Authority:** Jesus's humility despite His capacity shows that true authority can be grounded not in dominance, but in moral purity, love, and sacrifice. This power is based not on what can be imposed, but what can be shared and extended to others without expectation.

In summary, Jesus's form of greatness serves as a divine example, showing that true power is a servant's heart and a life lived in love and service, not in seeking elevation. It challenges human conventional wisdom, unsettling those who believe that power is synonymous with domination and control. It suggests that authority grounded in humility, love, and service can be far more enlightening and edifying, pointing toward a higher and truer way to live an impactful and effective life for the common good.
