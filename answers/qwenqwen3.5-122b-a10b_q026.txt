This passage establishes a causal and diagnostic relationship between the internal moral state of a person (the heart) and their external expression (speech). It argues that speech is not an isolated event but the inevitable overflow of what is stored within.

Here is an analysis of how the passage connects speech to the heart, followed by an evaluation of whether words function as symptoms, choices, or revelations.

### 1. The Connection: Speech as the Overflow of the Heart
The text uses two primary metaphors to explain this connection: **abundance** (v. 34) and **treasure** (v. 35).

*   **The Heart as a Warehouse:** By using the word "treasure" (*thesauros*), Jesus implies that the heart is a storage facility. Moral character is not formed in the moment of speaking; it is accumulated over time through thoughts, desires, and affections. This "stockpile" represents the moral inventory of a person.
*   **Speech as the Output:** The phrase "out of the abundance... the mouth speaketh" suggests a hydraulic or agricultural principle: what fills the container must eventually spill over. You cannot speak good things if your internal reservoir contains only evil. The mouth does not create content; it releases what is already there.
*   **The Impossibility of Counterfeit:** Jesus asks, "How can ye, being evil, speak good things?" This rhetorical question establishes a law of spiritual physics: the nature of the output must match the nature of the source. A "generation of vipers" (evil source) cannot produce "good things" (good output) because the mechanism of production is corrupted.

### 2. Are Words Symptoms, Choices, or Revelations?
The passage suggests that words function as **all three**. To separate them would be to misunderstand the holistic nature of the text.

#### **Words as Symptoms (Diagnostic)**
The text treats speech as a medical indicator of spiritual health.
*   **Evidence:** When Jesus calls the Pharisees a "generation of vipers" based on their words, He is diagnosing their condition. Just as a cough is a symptom of a lung infection, evil speech is a symptom of a corrupted heart.
*   **Function:** Words allow an observer to assess the invisible state of the speaker. If the "fruit" (words) is bad, the "tree" (heart) is bad.

#### **Words as Choices (Volitional)**
While the *source* of the words is the heart, the *act* of speaking is volitional, and the *content* of the heart is the result of prior choices.
*   **Evidence:** The text speaks of a "good man" and an "evil man." These are moral identities formed by choices. One chooses what to store in the "treasure" of the heart through what they love, believe, and practice. Furthermore, "bringing forth" implies an active release.
*   **Function:** While a person cannot force a good word out of an evil heart, they are responsible for the accumulation of the treasure that makes that word possible.

#### **Words as Revelations (Epistemological)**
The passage suggests that speech unmasks the true identity of the speaker.
*   **Evidence:** The text implies that the heart is hidden ("abundance of the heart"), but the mouth makes it public. The words reveal the "treasure" that was previously concealed.
*   **Function:** Words strip away pretense. The Pharisees may have tried to appear righteous, but their speech revealed their true hostility toward Jesus.

### Conclusion
Matthew 12:34–35 posits that **words are symptoms, choices, and revelations simultaneously.**

*   They are **symptoms** because they indicate the underlying health of the soul.
*   They are **choices** because they are the result of a will that has chosen what to value and store over time.
*   They are **revelations** because they make the hidden moral reality visible to others.

Ultimately, the passage argues that ethical reform cannot be achieved merely by policing speech (the mouth). Because speech is the overflow of the heart, true change requires a transformation of the "treasure" stored within. One cannot speak good things until one has first become a good person by filling the heart with good things.
