Luke 6:37 is not a blanket prohibition on recognizing right from wrong. It is a warning against a *posture* of the heart—one that is eager to condemn others while excusing oneself. The difference between this command and the claim “moral judgments never matter” is the difference between mercy and moral abdication.

### 1. The passage targets a spirit, not all evaluation
Jesus says, “Judge not, and ye shall not be judged: condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned: forgive, and ye shall be forgiven.” In the same chapter he immediately adds the image of trying to remove a speck from your brother’s eye while ignoring the log in your own (Luke 6:41–42). The problem is not seeing the speck; it is the hypocrisy, the blindness to one’s own faults, and the refusal to show mercy.

Elsewhere Jesus himself makes strong moral judgments: he calls religious leaders “hypocrites,” cleanses the temple, and tells his disciples to judge with “righteous judgment” (John 7:24). So the text cannot mean that moral discernment is forbidden. It means that the *manner* and *motive* of judgment matter deeply.

### 2. Why this is different from “moral judgments never matter”
If moral judgments never mattered, there would be no reason to forgive—because forgiveness presupposes a real wrong. You do not forgive someone for something you regard as morally neutral. Jesus assumes that people *do* wrong, that harm is real, and that victims have legitimate grievances. His command is to release the debt rather than to pretend the debt never existed.

Saying “judge not” also does not mean “all behavior is equally valid.” It means: do not place yourself in the seat of final condemnation over another human being. Only God can hand down a final verdict on a soul. Human beings can recognize evil, but we cannot look into a heart and pronounce a person utterly worthless.

### 3. Discernment versus the appetite to condemn
The key distinction is between **discernment** and the **appetite to condemn**.

**Discernment** is the careful, humble work of telling good from evil, truth from falsehood, wisdom from folly. It is necessary for justice, for protecting the vulnerable, for correcting friends, and for choosing how to live. Discernment says: “This action is harmful; this choice is wrong; this path leads away from flourishing.” It can be severe about sin without being contemptuous toward the sinner. It is motivated by love of the good and concern for the person.

**The appetite to condemn**, by contrast, is a hunger to pronounce judgment. It delights in finding fault, in exposing shame, in ranking oneself above others. It treats moral failures as opportunities for self-exaltation rather than occasions for repair. Where discernment looks at an action and asks, “What is true here, and what should be done?” the appetite to condemn looks at a person and asks, “How can I put you in your place?”

You can see the difference in how each handles the same facts:

- **Discernment** might say: “That behavior is dishonest, and it needs to stop. I have been dishonest too, so I will speak with humility and a desire for restoration.”
- **Condemnation** says: “You are a liar. You are beneath me. You deserve to be cast out.”

The first upholds moral truth; the second feeds on moral superiority.

### 4. The measure you use
Luke 6:38 adds that the measure you give will be the measure you receive. This is the heart of the warning. If you judge others with rigidity and no mercy, you are asking the universe—and God—to apply that same rigid standard to you. Since everyone fails, only a merciful standard leaves any room for human beings to stand.

So the command does not erase morality. It changes how we hold it. It forbids the pleasure of condemnation while still requiring the courage of discernment. We are to see clearly, speak truthfully, and yet lean toward forgiveness rather than final verdict.
