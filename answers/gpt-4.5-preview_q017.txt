Light is hated because it exposes truth—and truth, by nature, reveals what many prefer hidden. The hatred for light emerges from a deep human discomfort with exposure, with being seen in vulnerability, and especially in wrongdoing. Truth acts as a penetrating illumination that strips away our façades and leaves our motives and actions fully visible. This transparency challenges our self-perceptions and often contradicts the comforting narratives we craft about ourselves.

John's imagery vividly captures the psychology of shame and concealment. People "love darkness" not merely out of a preference for evil itself, but because darkness shields them from the discomfort of scrutiny. Shame, the painful awareness of failing a moral or social standard, intensifies the desire to remain unseen, fueling a powerful impulse toward concealment. It’s easier to live with internal contradictions, hypocrisies, or wrongdoing when they remain hidden, unacknowledged even to oneself. Light, as truth, exposes precisely these contradictions and imperfections, compelling confrontation with uncomfortable realities.

Yet, paradoxically, the passage suggests that those who “do truth” embrace exposure, not because they are perfect, but because openness before God allows genuine transformation. Rather than a threat, exposure becomes liberation, freeing them from the oppressive burden of secrecy and self-deception.

Thus, hatred of light arises from an unwillingness to confront our true selves openly. Conversely, embracing the light becomes an act of courage and trust, acknowledging that authentic transformation can only occur when we allow ourselves to be genuinely and humbly seen.
