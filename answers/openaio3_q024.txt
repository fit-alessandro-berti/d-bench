1. External compliance  

• What it is  
  – Conforming one’s behaviour to an outside standard: “Do this, don’t do that.”  
  – It can be produced by fear of punishment, social pressure, habit, or sheer will-power.  

• What it is not  
  – It is not a change in the seat of decision-making (in Hebrew thought the “heart,” לֵב/לֵבָב).  
  – Therefore it is fragile; remove the pressure and the obedience evaporates (cf. Isa 29 :13, “this people draw near with their mouth … while their hearts are far from me”).  

2. “A new heart … a new spirit”  

• What it is  
  – An inward renovation of motives, loves, and dispositions produced by God’s own Spirit.  
  – The law is no longer merely over the person but written inside the person (Jer 31 :33; 2 Cor 3 :3).  
  – Because the desire has been altered, obedience becomes spontaneous (“I will cause you to walk …”).  

• Why it is needed  
  – Israel’s history had shown that a perfect law plus external incentives still yielded chronic unfaithfulness.  
  – The exile (Ezekiel’s setting) proved that the problem was not lack of information but a damaged moral core.  

3. Why call the old heart “stone” and the new one “flesh”?  

Hebrew imagery is concrete. A “stony” heart means:  

• Insensitive – stone feels nothing; a hard heart cannot feel God’s instruction or neighbour’s pain.  
• Inflexible – stone does not bend; the will is locked in obstinacy (cf. Pharaoh’s “hardened” heart, Ex 7–14).  
• Lifeless – stone is inanimate; no inner vitality responds to God.  

By contrast a “heart of flesh” is:  

• Sensitive – able to be “pricked” or “written on.”  
• Pliable – capable of being shaped by God’s statutes.  
• Living – it throbs, grows, and relates.  

The metaphor treats hardness almost as a physical condition because, from Ezekiel’s vantage point, Israel’s rebellion was not a set of isolated acts but a built-in, systemic defect. Like a calcified organ, it could not be rehabilitated; it had to be replaced.

4. Summary  

External compliance = rule-keeping driven from the outside.  
New heart = inward transformation driven from the inside by God’s Spirit.  

The “stone/flesh” language dramatizes the contrast: merely chiselling rules onto a slab of granite changes nothing; replacing the granite with living tissue makes obedience the natural outflow of a renewed, responsive life.
