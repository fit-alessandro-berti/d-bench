The elder brother’s deepest problem in Luke 15:28–32 is a complex mixture, but most fundamentally it centers on **discomfort with mercy itself**, which manifests through *anger, envy, wounded fairness,* and *pride in obedience.*

Here’s an analysis of each element in context:

1. **Anger:**  
   The elder brother is explicitly described as angry and refusing to enter the celebration. His anger is a surface symptom of a deeper issue.

2. **Envy:**  
   He resents the younger brother’s return and the celebration in his honor, revealing envy—the younger brother received forgiveness and joy, which he feels is undeserved.

3. **Wounded sense of fairness:**  
   The elder brother complains about never receiving a kid to celebrate with his friends, highlighting a perceived injustice. He feels he has been loyal and obedient for years without receiving similar kindness or reward.

4. **Pride in obedience:**  
   He emphasizes his faithful service and compliance with his father’s commands, revealing pride in his own righteousness and perhaps a belief that obedience should earn reward or privilege.

5. **Discomfort with mercy itself:**  
   Underlying all of the above is his inability or unwillingness to embrace the father’s mercy toward the repentant younger brother. He struggles with the father’s generous forgiveness and joy, seeing it as unfair or improper.

**In summary:**  
While the elder brother's anger, envy, sense of wounded fairness, and pride are evident, these are manifestations of a deeper spiritual and emotional problem: **his discomfort with mercy and grace—he cannot rejoice in the father's compassion because it challenges his view of justice and righteousness.** This bars him from fully participating in the celebration of restoration and forgiveness.

Jesus uses this parable to illustrate how the self-righteous can reject God’s grace toward repentant sinners because mercy conflicts with their sense of fairness and pride. The elder brother's heart is closed to mercy, which is the root of his problem.
