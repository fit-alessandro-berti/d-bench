Amos rejects the worship so sharply because the issue is not **imbalance** but **contradiction**.

The people are not being told, “Worship is good, but don’t forget ethics.” They are being told that their worship has become **offensive** to God because it is being used to **cover injustice**. Their sacrifices, songs, and festivals are not merely incomplete; they are **fraudulent**. They act as though religious performance can compensate for oppression, dishonesty, and social corruption.

That is why the language is so absolute:

- “I hate, I despise…”
- “I will not accept…”
- “I will not hear…”

This is covenant lawsuit language. God is exposing a people who still maintain the **forms of devotion** while violating the **substance of devotion**.

### The fraud being exposed
The moral and spiritual fraud is something like this:

1. **Trying to honor God ritually while disobeying Him socially**  
   They gather for worship, but their society is marked by injustice. So their liturgy says, “We belong to God,” while their public life says the opposite.

2. **Using religion as moral cover**  
   Worship becomes a way to feel righteous without becoming righteous. It functions as camouflage for exploitation.

3. **Separating piety from justice**  
   They appear spiritually serious, but they have detached love of God from love of neighbor. Amos says that separation is false from the start.

4. **Assuming God can be pleased by gifts while ignoring victims**  
   Sacrifice is treated almost like a transaction: offerings are given, so divine favor is expected. Amos destroys that idea. God wants a just community, not ceremonial bribes.

### Why not ask for “better balance”?
Because in this situation, “balance” would suggest that worship and justice are two independent goods needing proper proportion. Amos’s point is deeper: **worship without justice is not worship at all**. It has become noise.

That is why verse 24 is not an added extra:
> “Let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream.”

Justice and righteousness are not substitutes for true worship; they are evidence that worship is real. If they are absent, the songs themselves become unbearable hypocrisy.

### In short
Amos is exposing **religious hypocrisy at a structural level**: a community that keeps sacred ceremonies while tolerating exploitation, and that expects God to accept devotion from hands stained by injustice. The sharp rejection shows that God is not asking for a little less ritual and a little more morality; He is rejecting a counterfeit spirituality that uses worship to evade repentance.
