### The Passage (NIV for clarity):

> “Trust in the Lord with all thine heart; and do not lean on your own understanding.  
> In all your ways acknowledge him, and he will make your paths straight.”

### What Kind of Trust is Being Asked For?

**The kind of trust here is radical, total, and surrendering trust—not just in some aspects of life, but with “all thine heart.”** This is a *spiritual trust* in the God of Israel, characterized by:

- **Reliance on Divine Guidance Over Human Wisdom:**  
  The verse says, “Trust in the Lord with all thine heart; and *do not* lean on your own understanding.”  
  This is not about rejecting knowledge or intelligence. Instead, it’s about recognizing that human reason and insight are limited, and that God’s wisdom surpasses ours. We are to place our ultimate confidence in God, not in our own plans or intellect.

- **Full-hearted Surrender:**  
  The phrase “with all thine heart” suggests an unreserved, wholehearted dependence on God. It is not partial trust (like trusting God in some decisions but relying on yourself in others), but absolute, all-encompassing trust.

- **Active Acknowledgement:**  
  The second part, “In all your ways acknowledge him,” means that as you walk through life, you should continually give God credit for your direction and success. This ongoing awareness keeps the trust alive and prevents arrogance or self-sufficiency.

### Distinguishing Humble Self-Distrust from Mere Anti-Intellectualism

#### 1. **Humble Self-Distrust (What Proverbs 3 is Asking For)**
- **Definition:**  
  This is a posture of acknowledging one’s own limitations and submitting to a higher authority (God) out of genuine humility.
- **Motivation:**  
  It comes from recognizing that you are not in control of everything. You admit the limits of your knowledge, wisdom, and power.
- **Characteristics:**  
  - Openness to correction and guidance  
  - Willingness to let go of self-made plans  
  - Confidence in God’s ability to lead and provide  
  - Not anti-intellectual—still uses reason and experience, but knows when to defer to something greater  
- **Example:**  
  A scientist who trusts their training but also admits, “There are things I don’t understand, and maybe God sees something I can’t. I’ll let Him guide me in this area.”

#### 2. **Mere Anti-Intellectualism**
- **Definition:**  
  This is a rejection of reason, knowledge, and experience, not based on faith but on arrogance or fear of criticism.
- **Motivation:**  
  It’s not about trust in God, but about saying, “I won’t use my mind or evidence because I want to be different or avoid being proven wrong.”
- **Characteristics:**  
  - Disregard for personal knowledge and experience  
  - Refusal to engage in critical thinking or problem-solving  
  - Potential for naivety or being manipulated  
  - Not rooted in humility but in pride or rebellion against learning  
- **Example:**  
  Someone who refuses to consider scientific evidence because “I’m supposed to just believe without thinking.” This is not humble trust in God, but a closed-minded approach.

### Summary Table

|                             | Humble Self-Distrust (Proverbs 3) | Mere Anti-Intellectualism |
|-----------------------------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Basis for Trust             | Humility, recognition of limits   | Rejection of knowledge, pride |
| Use of Intellect            | Balanced; uses intellect in service to God | Abandonment of intellect altogether |
| Motivation                  | To glorify God, follow His will  | To be different, avoid scrutiny |
| Relationship to God         | Surrender and dependence         | Dismissive or perfunctory |
| Outcome                     | Growth, guidance, peace         | Stagnation, confusion     |

### Conclusion

**Proverbs 3:5–6 calls for humble self-distrust:** a willingness to step aside from one’s own understanding, recognizing God’s sovereignty, while still using one’s intellect in a God-honoring way. It is not about being anti-intellectual, but about wisely placing the main burden of trust in God, guided by both faith and reason when appropriate.

In short:  
> **True trust in the Lord here is humble, not anti-intellectual—it’s trusting in the Lord most of all, while still using your mind in a way that acknowledges your limits.**
