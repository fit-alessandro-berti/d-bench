This is a profound and beautiful question that gets to the heart of biblical repentance. Isaiah 55:6–7 distinguishes between **“way” (דֶּרֶךְ, *derekh*)** and **“thoughts” (מַחֲשָׁבָה, *machashavah*)** to show that true repentance is holistic—it must reach both outward behavior and inward disposition.

---

### **1. “Way” — Outward Conduct & Life Pattern**
- The “way” refers to habitual actions, lifestyle, and the general direction of one’s life.
- For the “wicked,” this could mean patterns of injustice, dishonesty, exploitation, or open rebellion against God’s commands.
- Forsaking one’s “way” means turning from *what one does*—changing conduct, stopping harmful practices, and redirecting one’s life toward God’s path.

---

### **2. “Thoughts” — Inward Imagination & Motivation**
- “Thoughts” here are not just fleeting ideas, but **the inner plans, motives, intentions, and reasoning** that shape the “way.”
- The “unrighteous man’s thoughts” might include selfish ambitions, hidden lusts, rationalizations for sin, pride, or distorted views of God and others.
- Forsaking one’s “thoughts” means surrendering one’s inner world to God—allowing Him to reshape how one thinks, desires, and perceives reality (cf. Romans 12:2).

---

### **3. The Connection Between the Two**
- Jesus later emphasized this in Matthew 5:21–28: murder begins with anger, adultery begins with lust. Outward sin often springs from inward thought patterns.
- If we only change behavior without addressing heart motivations, repentance is incomplete—like pruning weeds without pulling the root.
- God looks at the heart (1 Samuel 16:7), so He calls for transformation at the source.

---

### **4. What This Implies About Mercy**
- The promise—**“He will have mercy… He will abundantly pardon”**—is astonishing precisely because it addresses both layers of our fallenness.
- God’s mercy is not offered on the condition of perfect external compliance alone, but to those who turn their whole being—actions and thoughts—toward Him.
- The word “abundantly” (or “richly”) in Hebrew (*yirbeh*) suggests overflowing, multiplied pardon—sufficient to cover both manifest sins and hidden faults.

---

### **5. The Invitation’s Urgency**
- “While he may be found… while he is near” reminds us that this opportunity for repentance is both real and time-bound—God makes Himself accessible, but we must respond.
- The call to forsake both “way” and “thoughts” implies that no one is beyond hope: whether our sin is outwardly scandalous or inwardly concealed, God’s mercy reaches it.

---

**In summary:**  
Isaiah teaches that true repentance is **integrated**—it involves turning from wrong actions *and* surrendering the inner thoughts that produce them. God’s mercy extends to forgive and transform both, inviting us into a renewed relationship where outward life flows from an inwardly changed heart. This is the beauty of the gospel’s call: it meets us as whole persons, and restores us wholly.
