Short answer: this passage ties speech to the inner moral life, and the relation is causal, evidential, and revelatory. Words come from the heart, reveal the heart, and also show the moral state of a person before God.

How the passage works
- Heart as the source: The “heart” is the center of a person’s desires, affections, thoughts, and will. The “treasure” is what a person stores up there—what they love, trust, and rely on.
- Abundance and overflow: “Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks” means what fills the heart flows outward in speech. A heart filled with good things produces good speech; a heart filled with evil produces evil speech.
- Treasure as stockpile and habit: The terms “good treasure” and “evil treasure” imply a stored-up repertoire of inner resources formed by repeated choices, not just a one-time feeling. What you repeatedly value and rehearse inwardly eventually surfaces outwardly in language and action.

Is speech a symptom, a choice, or a revelation? It’s all three, in a connected way.
- Symptom (evidence): Your words expose what your heart is full of. You can discern a person’s spiritual state by what comes out of their mouth.
- Choice (moral formation): The heart’s contents are shaped by choices—habits, loves, loyalties. What you cultivate inwardly (treasure) will determine what you speak. You can choose what you store up in your heart.
- Revelation (moral judgment): The words themselves reveal the truth about a person and, in Jesus’ teaching, will incur divine judgment—“By your words you will be acquitted or condemned” (Matt 12:37; cf. James 3:9–12).

Contextual note
- The passage sits in a rebuke of hypocritical talk by the Pharisees. Jesus is emphasizing genuine righteousness that flows from an inward condition of the heart, not merely external speech or ritual.
- Related themes: Luke 6:45 (“good man…out of the good treasure of his heart brings forth what is good”; Prov 4:23 on guarding the heart); James 3 on the tongue as a bridle for the whole body; and Matt 6:21 about where one’s treasure is, there the heart will be.

Practical takeaway
- To change what you say, attend to what you treasure in your heart. Fill your heart with Scripture, prayer, worship, and love for God so that your speech will naturally reflect that inward abundance.
- Be aware that words reveal and shape moral reality before God, so strive for integrity in both inward dispositions and outward speech.
