The danger is not **visibility as such**, but **visibility sought as the moral point of the act**.

In Matthew 6:1, the key phrase is “to be seen of them.” Jesus is addressing a shift in the act’s intended audience and purpose:

- The apparent audience is God and the person helped.
- The actual audience becomes other people.
- The desired “reward” becomes admiration, status, or reputation.

The act may remain outwardly good, but its moral center has been displaced. Giving becomes a way of purchasing honor; prayer becomes a performance; fasting becomes a means of displaying spiritual seriousness. In that sense, public goodness is dangerous because it can turn devotion and generosity into self-promotion.

This also explains why the verse need not contradict Matthew 5:16—“let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father.” Public action can be appropriate when its purpose is faithful witness or the good of others. The difference is not simply whether people see, but **what the actor wants their seeing to accomplish**:

- Are they led to glorify God and receive help?
- Or are they led to admire the benefactor?

Jesus’ emphasis on secrecy in the following verses is therefore a safeguard against the craving for recognition. It exposes whether one would still do the good thing without applause. “Your Father, which seeth in secret” means that divine approval, rather than public acclaim, is to be the decisive reward.

A further textual note: the KJV reads “your alms” in 6:1, but many manuscripts and modern translations read “your righteousness,” with almsgiving introduced specifically in verse 2. On that reading, Jesus gives a general principle about religious goodness: **good works are morally endangered when being seen becomes their governing motive, not merely an incidental consequence.**
