# The Two-Fold Turning of Repentance

Isaiah does not say merely, "Let the wicked forsake his way." He adds, "and the unrighteous man his thoughts." The prophet refuses to let repentance be half a thing. It must reach both the hands and the heart, both the path the feet walk and the country the mind inhabits.

## Why "Way"?

The "way" is the visible life—conduct, habit, the road worn by repeated choice. A man's way is what his neighbors see: his dealings, his speech, his pleasures, his company. Scripture frequently frames sin as a road (Ps. 1:6; Prov. 14:12), because sin is not usually a single act but a direction, a groove cut deeper by each step. To forsake the way is to stop walking it—to break habits, abandon companions of evil, quit the trade of iniquity.

But if only the way is forsaken, the man becomes a hypocrite. He changes his route while keeping his map.

## Why "Thoughts"?

The "thoughts" are the inward imagination—the secret counsel of the heart, the daydreams, the ambitions, the grudges rehearsed in private. Here is the workshop where outward deeds are first forged. "As he thinketh in his heart, so is he" (Prov. 23:7). The Lord says through this same prophet that His thoughts are not our thoughts (Isa. 55:8, the very next verse)—so repentance is not only a correction of behavior but a surrender of one's entire interior furniture to God's mind.

If only the thoughts were forsaken without the way, repentance would be mere sentiment—regret without reformation. But if only the way were forsaken without the thoughts, it would be mere moralism—reformation without regeneration.

## What This Implies

**Outward conduct matters.** God is not satisfied with a secret change of mind that leaves the wicked man still walking his wicked way. Repentance must be visible, practical, measurable by the abandonment of actual sins.

**Inward imagination matters more than we suppose.** God claims jurisdiction over the mind itself. Lust, envy, pride, bitterness, vain ambition—these are not mere private matters but matters for repentance. The heart is not a sanctuary exempt from God's scrutiny; it is the first place He seeks.

**Mercy is "abundant"—but not cheap.** Notice the proportion: God requires a thorough turning (way *and* thoughts), and answers it with *abundant* pardon. The greatness of the mercy is not diminished by the thoroughness of the repentance; rather, the thoroughness of the repentance is made possible by the greatness of the mercy. He who commands the turning gives the grace for it, and meets it with a pardon larger than the sin.

## The Nearness That Will Not Always Remain

The passage opens with urgency: "while he may be found... while he is near." The implication is sober. There are seasons when God draws near in unusual readiness to be found, and these seasons do not last forever. To delay repentance on the assumption that God will always be equally accessible is to presume upon a nearness that is, in fact, a gift of the moment.

So the call is: turn now, turn wholly—feet and mind, conduct and conscience—and find that the God you feared to approach pardons more abundantly than you dared to hope.
