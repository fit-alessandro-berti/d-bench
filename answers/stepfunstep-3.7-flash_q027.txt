### Answering the first question: What condemns the Pharisee?
First, **false doctrine is not the primary issue here**. The Pharisee’s practices (twice-weekly fasting, full tithing of his income) are all faithful observance of the Mosaic Law, so he is not teaching heresy or incorrect theology about God’s commands. Jesus is not condemning religious observance itself, but the corrupted heart posture that animates it.
The Pharisee is condemned for an interconnected bundle of sins rooted in pride, all tied to his foundational error of trusting in his own righteousness rather than in God’s mercy:
1. **Pride as the core root**: The parable’s prologue explicitly frames the story for people who "trusted in themselves that they were righteous"—the Pharisee’s entire orientation is self-reliance, the belief that his own works can earn him God’s favor and make him superior to others.
2. **Comparative self-righteousness**: He defines his goodness not by his own relationship with God, but by comparison to others. He opens his prayer by thanking God he is not like "extortioners, unjust, adulterers," and explicitly singles out the tax collector as a benchmark for how much better he is. His righteousness is relative, dependent on being better than people he deems sinful, rather than absolute, rooted in alignment with God.
3. **Contempt for others**: The prologue notes the target audience "despised others," and the Pharisee’s prayer drips with this contempt. He uses the tax collector—a figure viewed as the ultimate traitor and sinner in 1st-century Jewish society, for collaborating with Roman occupiers and extorting his own people—as a prop to elevate himself, reducing other people to categories of sin rather than seeing them as fellow Image-bearers in need of grace.
4. **Using virtue as self-display**: The Greek text notes the Pharisee "prayed thus with himself" (πρὸς ἑαυτὸν προσηύξατο), meaning his audience is not God, but himself and the other worshipers in the temple. His good works are not offered to God as worship; they are paraded as evidence of his own superiority. His prayer is a performance of piety designed to draw human approval, not a humble address to the God he claims to thank.
These traits are inseparable: his pride leads him to compare himself to others to feel superior, that comparison breeds contempt for those he deems inferior, and he leverages his legitimate good works to show off that superiority instead of recognizing those works as gifts from God meant to humble him, not boost his ego.

### Answering the second question: Why is the tax collector’s posture spiritually truer?
The tax collector’s posture—standing far off, refusing to lift his eyes to heaven, smiting his breast (a traditional Jewish gesture of contrition), and crying out only "God be merciful to me, a sinner"—is spiritually truer because it aligns perfectly with the biblical reality of human sinfulness and God’s grace, and rejects every false posture the Pharisee embraces:
1. **It rejects comparison and contempt**: The taxiller does not look to others (even the Pharisee, who is outwardly more "righteous") to justify himself. He does not list any good works, does not compare himself to "worse" sinners, does not despise anyone. He only acknowledges his own sinfulness, no exceptions.
2. **It rejects self-trust**: He knows he has no righteousness of his own to offer God. He does not appeal to his observance of the Law, his generosity, or his moral standing—he recognizes that all humans are sinners, and no amount of good works can earn God’s favor. He comes to God with empty hands, relying entirely on mercy.
3. **It is a genuine address to God**: Unlike the Pharisee’s self-directed performance, the tax collector’s cry is a raw, honest appeal to God alone. He does not perform for an audience; he acknowledges his unworthiness to even look directly at God’s presence, and asks only for the mercy he knows he does not deserve.
This posture reflects the biblical principle that "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble" (James 4:6). The Pharisee’s pride leads him to trust in himself, so God rejects his self-righteousness. The tax collector’s humility leads him to trust in God’s mercy alone, so God declares him justified—right with God—not because of his works, but because of his repentant heart.
The parable’s core lesson is that justification before God does not come from outward religious observance or a track record of "being better than others," but from honest acknowledgment of our sin and reliance on God’s grace.
