The dual command to forsake both **“way”** (conduct/actions) and **“thoughts”** (intentions/intellect) provides a comprehensive definition of biblical repentance. It suggests that true transformation is not merely a behavioral adjustment, but a holistic realignment of the entire human person.

Here is an analysis of what this implies regarding conduct, imagination, and the nature of mercy:

### 1. Outward Conduct: Forsaking the "Way"
The "way" refers to a person’s lifestyle, habits, and external trajectory. In the Hebrew context, a "way" is the path one walks—the visible track of one’s life.
*   **The Implication:** Repentance requires a tangible change in direction. It is not enough to feel regret; there must be a cessation of the specific actions that constitute "wickedness." If a person continues to walk the same path while claiming to be repentant, their repentance is considered hollow.
*   **The Limitation of Conduct Alone:** If repentance only involved the "way," it would be mere **moralism** or legalism—an attempt to fix the symptoms of sin without addressing the cause.

### 2. Inward Imagination: Forsaking "Thoughts"
The command to forsake "thoughts" (the Hebrew *machashabah*, implying schemes, purposes, or designs) moves the requirement from the limbs to the mind.
*   **The Implication:** This addresses the "inner sanctum" of the human person—the imagination, the will, and the secret motives. A person can follow the "right way" outwardly (appearing righteous) while harboring "unrighteous thoughts" (malice, greed, or pride) inwardly.
*   **The Root of Sin:** By including "thoughts," the text acknowledges that outward sin is merely the fruit; the root is the internal imagination and the intent of the heart. True repentance requires a "re-thinking" of one's values and a surrender of the mental frameworks that justify sin.

### 3. The Synthesis: Holistic Transformation
By linking "way" and "thoughts," Isaiah 55:6–7 implies that **integrity is the union of the internal and the external.** 
*   To change the *way* without the *thoughts* is **hypocrisy** (acting righteous while remaining corrupt).
*   To change the *thoughts* without the *way* is **sentimentality** (feeling sorry without the courage to change).
True repentance is a "turning" (*shuv*) that happens in the mind and is proven in the feet.

### 4. The Possibility of Mercy: The "Why" Behind the "How"
The structure of the verse creates a profound tension: the requirement is total (mind and body), yet the promise is "abundant."

*   **The Scope of Mercy:** The text does not say, "If you fix your thoughts and your ways, you will earn your way back." Rather, it says, "Let him return... and He will have mercy." The difficulty of the requirement (changing both mind and action) underscores the necessity of God's grace. Because humans struggle to perfectly align both thought and deed, they are entirely dependent on a God who "abundantly pardons."
*   **The Nature of Pardon:** The word "abundantly" (*rab*) suggests a mercy that is disproportionate to the sin. The "abundant pardon" is the divine response to the "total surrender." When a person offers their whole self—both the messy internal thoughts and the flawed external ways—God meets that total surrender with a total restoration.

### Summary
The inclusion of both "way" and "thoughts" implies that **God is not looking for a change in behavior, but a change in being.** He seeks a person whose internal compass (thoughts) and external journey (way) are both recalibrated toward Him. The promise of "abundant pardon" ensures that even though this task is total and demanding, the grace available to those who attempt it is even more vast.
