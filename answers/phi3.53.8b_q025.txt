The passage from Matthew 6:1 in the context of the New Testament appears to critique a particular attitude towards charitable acts and almsgiving. The verse reads, "Take heed that ye do not render your alms before men to be seen; or else it savors of flattery; therefore it is also given unto the hypocrites: for in vain will that which a man have wrought for the approval of other men.” This verse is often interpreted as a caution against performing acts of charity or goodness with the sole intention of seeking recognition or praise from others rather than out of genuine compassion and piety. Let's break down the issue of moral danger in public goodness according to this verse:

1. **Visibility and Flattery**: The primary concern within the verse seems to be not the visibility of charitable acts per se, but the motivation behind them. When acts of giving are done with the intention of being seen or recognized by others, it may be a form of flattery or vanity. The danger, in this view, is not the act of giving itself, but the desire to gain external validation or social status, which can manifest as vanity. The focus on the external reward rather than acting for the sake of helping others indicates a performative mindset rather than a charitable heart.

2. **Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation**: The distinction here lies between intrinsic and extrinsic motivation. Intrinsistic reasons are motivated by a love for doing good for its own sake, while extrinsic motivation seeks external rewards or recognition. When giving is motivated by public visibility, the action becomes extrinsically motivated, and the risk is that it's perceived as transactional or self-serving. This extrinsic incentive can undermine the purity of one'thy intentions and potentially foster a culture where help is given only when there’s an expectation of praise or reciprocal exchange.

3. **Complicity of Hypocrites**: The verse refers to hypocrites, who may present a facade of piety while their real motives are anything but sincere. In such cases, public charitable acts might simply be a manipulation to manipulate others’ perceptions rather than a genuine concern for the welfare of others. The actions thus fail to foster a sense of moral integrity and instead perpetuate dishonest facades.

4. **Corrupt Influence on Altruism**: If public charity becomes primarily about social image, it risks corrupting the very notion of altruism, which ideally is selfless concern for the well-being of others without seeking personal gain. Actual altruism is based on empathy and a selfless act of kindness, independent of how visible or recognized it may be.

5. **The True Motive of Devotion**: The primary focus of worship and religious practice, according to the verse, is to glorify God, which includes demonstrating God's love and virtues through ethical living. When the intent behind charitable acts shifts to self-aggrandizement, the true purpose of service and gratitude towards God is obscured. Worship and good deeds should stem from a heart aligned with divine will, not human appraisal.

6. **The Call for Authentic Devotion**: The Bible, in various verses, seems to advocate for doing good for God as it's the right thing (James 4:8), without a heckling crowd to witness or be impressed. The moral danger here is not the charity, but when charitable acts cease to be an expression of faith and instead become a performance: "Each of you must pray, as far as you know the person in front of you" (James 5:4), suggesting that acts of kindness and charity should come from a genuine relationship with God, rather than from a desire to impress fellow humans.

In short, the verse does not criticize sharing or helping publicly per se, but rather the superficial aspects of motivation—either to garner visible approval or to avoid public disfavor. The underlying ethical teaching appears to encourage charitable deeds when done from a place of genuine love and commitment to faith and community, rather than when driven by the craving for visible recognition or personal enhancement.

This perspective suggests that the problem rests not within the visibility of good actions but in the self-serving intent that can undermine the authenticity of the goodness:

- **Visibility as Motivator**: When visibility becomes the driver for generosity, one runs the risk of motivated by self-interested desires (e.g., personal gratification, reputation enhancement), thus potentially compromising the altruist’s integrity.

- **Craving to be Seen**: The desire for acknowledgment can transform acts of kindness into empty rituals, where the act serves the individual's ego rather than the needs of those being helped. Actions driven by a deep respect for and commitment to the well-being of others—regardless of their audience—are less morally problematic according to this verse.

To reconcile these concerns, the Bible often promotes modesty, humility, and the internalization of the motivation behind actions as a way to counteract the temptation of self-aggrandizement. Generosity and altruism should ideally flow from a sincere faith that values helping others unconditionally and silently, as opposed to seeking spotlight or accolades. This internal motivation aligns with living genuinely by God's standards, which is the heart of the spiritual growth and moral living that the verse advocates.

As a result, the verse invites individuals to reflect deeply on their intentions, encouraging actions rooted in a true spiritual connection rather than a performative, external necessity. The moral stance taken here emphasizes the importance of sincerity in the demonstration of goodness rather than the superficial allure of external acknowledgement or the vanity of appearing virtuous. It implies that charity should not be about the observer's adulation, but about the right act being performed from a place of genuine benevolence and devotion. This way, public charity doesn't lose its moral value as long as the primary intent isn’t performing for an audience but rather fulfilling a heartfelt religious and ethical obligation.

Remember, these interpretations are rooted in theological contexts, and various religious traditions may have different perspectives on the same issue. Furthermore, modern ethical considerations also offer additional dimensions that the ancient texts alone may not fully capture. However, the key moral directive, at its essence, leans toward a sincere and heartfelt practice of good deeds rather than performative or vanity-driven actions.
