### How Romans 12:20–21 defines victory

Paul defines victory not as defeating your enemy, but as refusing to let evil set the terms. 

**Victory = "overcome evil with good"**  
The passage gives a two-part strategy:

- **Tactical level**: Do specific good to your enemy — feed, give drink. You respond to their need as a human, not to their hostility as an enemy.
- **Strategic level**: "Be not overcome of evil." Evil wants to replicate itself. If someone’s malice makes you malicious, evil just won twice. You’ve been overcome. Victory is breaking that chain.

The "coals of fire" line sounds violent, but most scholars read it in light of Egyptian and Jewish background: carrying burning coals in a clay pot on your head was how you received fire to restart your hearth. It was a picture of urgent need and shame that leads to repentance. You’re not burning them — you’re creating a situation where your kindness confronts their conscience. The “burning” is conviction, not revenge.

### Is the command weak, manipulative, or strong?

**It’s not weak.**  
Weakness would be avoidance: “just ignore them” or “don’t rock the boat.” Paul tells you to engage directly with your enemy’s needs. That takes more self-mastery than retaliation. Retaliation is reactive and predictable. Feeding an enemy requires you to govern yourself when your instincts want payback.

**It’s not manipulative.**  
Manipulation uses good deeds as a tool to force a specific outcome — “I’ll be nice so they feel guilty and apologize.” Paul doesn’t promise that outcome. The command is to *do* the good, not to *control* them with it. The “coals” result is a possible effect, not the motive. The motive is verse 21: don’t be overcome. You’d do the good even if they never change. The text gives God jurisdiction over justice in Romans 12:19, the verse right before: “Vengeance is mine, I will repay, saith the Lord.” Your job isn’t payback or payback-by-niceness. 

**It’s genuinely strong because:**  

1. **It keeps moral agency**: You don’t let someone else’s evil decide who you become. 
2. **It’s asymmetrical warfare**: Evil expects hostility back. Unexpected good disrupts the whole script. It’s the hardest move to counter.
3. **It aims higher than winning**: The goal isn’t “I win, you lose.” The goal is “evil loses.” If your enemy stops being an enemy because your good disarmed him, that’s evil defeated. If he doesn’t, you still weren’t overcome — evil still lost its bid to spread into you.

So Paul’s definition of victory is *contagion in reverse*. Evil spreads when people return evil for evil. Good spreads when someone absorbs the blow and sends back food instead. That’s not passivity. That’s strength under command.
