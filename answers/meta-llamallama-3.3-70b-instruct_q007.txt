This verse from Luke 6:37 is often misunderstood as suggesting that moral judgments should never be made, implying a relativistic approach to ethics where everything is equally acceptable. However, a closer examination reveals that Jesus' teaching here differentiation between judgment that leads to condemnation and the act of discernment or making moral judgments.

1. **Discernment vs. Condemnation**: The key difference lies in the intent and outcome of one's judgments. **Discernment** involves making distinctions between right and wrong, wise and unwise, with the intent of understanding, guiding, or correcting. It is about evaluating situations, actions, or behaviors based on moral principles, often with the goal of improvement or prevention of harm. Discernment is essential for personal growth, maintaining healthy relationships, and ensuring societal order.

2. **Condemnation**, on the other hand, goes beyond discernment. It involves not just recognizing a wrong but taking a step further to condemn the person, often with a sense of finality and without regard for redemption or rehabilitation. Condemnation can lead to exclusion, division, and hurt, rather than healing and restoration. The verse warns against this form of judgment, which not only fails to correct but also damages.

3. **The Appetite to Condemn**: Jesus' warning against judging and condemning touches on the human tendency to judge others harshly, often out of a self-righteous or hypocritical stance. This "appetite to condemn" can stem from a desire to elevate oneself by putting others down, to distract from one's own flaws, or to assert moral superiority. Such an approach to judgment lacks empathy, mercy, and awareness of one's own fallibility.

4. **Forgiveness and Mercy**: The latter part of the verse, "forgive, and ye shall be forgiven," underscores the importance of adopting a merciful and forgiving attitude towards others. This does not mean ignoring wrongdoing but rather choosing to address it with grace and the hope of restoration. Forgiveness is a process that recognizes human error, acknowledges hurt, and seeks healing for all parties involved.

5. **Practical Application**: In practice, this teaching encourages individuals to approach situations with a critical yet compassionate eye. It means being able to discern right from wrong without rushing to condemn others. By choosing discernment over condemnation and opting for forgiveness, individuals can foster a more supportive and less judgmental community. This approach also promotes personal humility and recognition of one's own capacity for error, leading to a more genuine and empathetic form of judgment when it is necessary.

In summary, Luke 6:37 does not dismiss the importance of moral judgments but instead highlights the need for these judgments to be made with discernment, empathy, and a predisposition towards mercy and forgiveness, rather than with a condemnatory spirit. This distinction is crucial for building a society that values both justice and compassion.
