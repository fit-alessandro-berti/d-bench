Excellent question! Let’s look closely at **Matthew 6:1**:

> “Take heed that ye do not your alms before men, **to be seen of them**: otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven.”

Here’s how we can break it down:

### 1. **What’s the real problem?**
- The verse does **not** strictly condemn doing good deeds where others can see—they *will* see sometimes.
- Instead, Jesus specifically warns against *doing your alms in order to be seen by others* (“to be seen of them”).

### 2. **Is visibility the issue?**
- **No,** visibility itself is not inherently wrong.
    - Sometimes, good deeds inevitably become public. For example, in Matthew 5:16 (the previous chapter), Jesus says:  
      > “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works and glorify your Father which is in heaven.”
    - The emphasis in 5:16 is on God being glorified, not yourself.

### 3. **What makes public goodness dangerous?**
- **Motivation:** The real moral danger is *the craving to be seen*—doing good not out of love or obedience to God, but to win *human* approval or praise.
- Such acts arise from pride or self-interest, not compassion or devotion.
- When people perform “alms” (charity, good works) primarily to look righteous or gain prestige, those actions lose their spiritual value before God (“you have no reward…”).

### 4. **Summary**
- **It’s not the act of being seen that is dangerous, but the desire to be seen, the hunger for applause, and the focus on self-glorification.** That motivation corrupts the act’s goodness and earns no reward from God.

---

#### **In sum:**
> Public goodness becomes morally dangerous when it is *performed for the sake of public visibility*—that is, out of a craving to be seen and admired, rather than genuine love for God or neighbor.
