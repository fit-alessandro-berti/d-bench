Luke 6:37 says:  
> "Judge not, and ye shall not be judged: condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned: forgive, and ye shall be forgiven."

At first glance, this verse might seem to suggest that one should never make moral judgments about others. However, this interpretation can be misleading. The verse is not dismissing all forms of judgment or discernment but is addressing the *attitude* and *spirit* behind judgment, and the consequences of harsh condemnation.

### Difference between moral judgment and condemnation

- **Moral judgment (discernment)** involves recognizing right and wrong, making wise and informed decisions, setting boundaries, and guiding oneself and others toward good and away from harm. This kind of judgment is necessary and healthy. For example, discerning harmful behaviors from beneficial ones can protect relationships, communities, and personal integrity. Jesus himself often made moral discernments in his teachings.

- **Condemnation** refers to a harsh, unforgiving, and self-righteous attitude that seeks to condemn, shame, or exclude others. Condemnation often comes with pride, lack of mercy, and an intent to punish or degrade. It can close the door to understanding, grace, and reconciliation. This verse admonishes against this kind of judgment, warning that when you condemn, you invite similar condemnation toward yourself.

### The passage's focus

- The verse urges believers **not to judge with a condemning or hypocritical spirit**. The key is *how* one judges: Are we humble and merciful, acknowledging our own imperfections, or are we arrogant and harsh, eager to punish?

- The call is to **forgive rather than condemn**, showing grace as we have received grace.

- Jesus sets a reciprocal principle: those who withhold harsh judgment and instead forgive will themselves receive mercy and forgiveness.

### Summary

- **Discernment** = healthy, wise judgment, with humility and mercy, aimed at truth and love.
- **Condemnation** = harsh, unforgiving judgment, often hypocritical and self-righteous.

Luke 6:37 warns against the latter, not the former. It invites us into a spirit of grace rather than a spirit of judgmentalism. Moral judgments matter, but they must be made with a heart of love, mercy, and forgiveness—not condemnation.
