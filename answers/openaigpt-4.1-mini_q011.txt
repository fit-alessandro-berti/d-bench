The progression in Isaiah 1:16–17 from “cease to do evil” to “learn to do well” highlights an important theological and ethical insight about goodness. It suggests that goodness is not merely the absence of wrongdoing (restraint), but involves actively cultivating and practicing love, justice, and mercy.

**Here’s why:**

1. **From Negative Command to Positive Action:**  
   “Cease to do evil” is a command to stop harmful behavior — a form of restraint or avoidance of sin. It implies that wrongdoing must be removed from one’s life. However, stopping evil alone is insufficient.

2. **Learning and Practicing Goodness:**  
   “Learn to do well” implies that goodness requires intentional effort, growth, and education. It is not automatic or innate but something to be developed. The phrase suggests an ongoing process of moral formation and virtue cultivation.

3. **Active Engagement with Justice and Mercy:**  
   The following verses clarify what doing well entails: “seek judgment, relieve the oppressed, judge the fatherless, plead for the widow.” These are concrete actions involving justice, care for the vulnerable, and advocacy. Goodness here is portrayed as active love expressed in social responsibility.

4. **Goodness as Love and Justice, Not Mere Restraint:**  
   The text rejects a minimalistic view of morality as simply avoiding sin. Instead, true goodness involves intentionally embracing and practicing justice and compassion. It’s a transformative way of life aimed at repairing relationships and society.

**In summary:**  
Isaiah’s message moves from stopping evil (a minimal baseline) to learning and performing good (an active pursuit of justice and mercy). This suggests that goodness is more than mere restraint; it is a practiced, lived expression of love and justice. The faithful are called not just to refrain from evil but to embody righteousness by seeking justice and caring for the marginalized.
