Short answer:
- The Pharisee is condemned for self-righteousness: trusting in himself and despising others. Pride, comparison, contempt, and turning virtue into self-display are all symptoms of that root.
- The tax collector’s posture is “truer” because it accords with spiritual reality: he acknowledges sin, asks for mercy, and rests on God’s atoning grace rather than his own record.

Explanation:
- Luke signals the target: “to some who trusted in themselves that they were righteous and treated others with contempt” (v. 9). That double posture—self-trust and contempt—is what Jesus exposes.
- The Pharisee’s prayer reveals it. He “prayed…to himself” (can mean to/about himself), listing “I… I… I…,” comparing horizontally (“not like other men… or even this tax collector”), and using thanksgiving as a platform for self-congratulation. Fasting and tithing are good in themselves; the fault is turning them into grounds of standing before God, measured against other sinners rather than God’s holiness. That is the essence of self-justification.
- The tax collector’s stance—standing far off, eyes down, beating his breast—is the bodily form of repentance. His single petition, “God, be merciful to me, a sinner,” uses a verb (be propitiated) that evokes atonement in the temple setting. He asks, not boasts; he looks Godward, not sideways. This is faith: entrusting oneself to God’s mercy rather than one’s merit.
- Hence the verdict: “This man went down to his house justified rather than the other.” Jesus is not praising tax collecting; he is praising repentance. The issue is not doing good works but relying on them. “Everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, but the one who humbles himself will be exalted” (v. 14).

In sum: The Pharisee’s error is self-justification (root) expressed as pride, comparison, contempt, and virtue-as-display (fruit). The tax collector’s way is spiritually true because it aligns with who God is (holy and merciful) and who we are (sinners in need of grace), and so it receives justification as a gift.
