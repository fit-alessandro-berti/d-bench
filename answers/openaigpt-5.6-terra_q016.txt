The elder brother’s deepest problem is not simply anger or envy, though both are present. It is a **heart that relates to the father transactionally rather than lovingly**—and therefore cannot rejoice in the father’s mercy.

He says, in effect:

- “I have *served* you.”
- “I have *never* disobeyed.”
- “You never gave *me* a goat.”
- “This *your* son squandered your property.”

His vocabulary is revealing. He sees himself less as a beloved son than as a dutiful servant earning wages. And he refuses to call the returned man “my brother”; he says, “this son of yours.” The father gently corrects both errors: “**Son**, you are always with me … and this **your brother** was dead and is alive.”

So the elements named in the question fit together:

- **Anger** is the outward reaction.
- **Envy** appears in his resentment of the celebration.
- **Wounded fairness** is his stated complaint: the undeserving receive what the faithful have not received.
- **Pride in obedience** is the basis on which he thinks he can make a claim against his father.
- **Discomfort with mercy** is the decisive spiritual issue: he cannot accept a grace that honors the repentant undeserving.

The elder brother does not merely want justice; he wants a moral order in which his obedience gives him standing over his brother. Mercy feels like an insult because he treats his own faithfulness as a claim on the father rather than as life within the father’s house.

That is why the father’s answer does not argue that the younger son deserved the feast. He did not. Nor does he deny the elder son’s position: “All that I have is yours.” Instead, he calls him into the father’s own joy: “It was fitting to celebrate.” The question is whether the elder brother will value being with the father and sharing the father’s joy more than being proved morally superior to his brother.

In Luke’s setting, the elder brother especially mirrors the religious critics who objected that Jesus welcomed sinners (Luke 15:1–2). Their problem was not zeal for holiness by itself, but an inability to rejoice when God restores the unworthy.
