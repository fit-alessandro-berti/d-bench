Luke 6:37 is not a call to moral blindness. If “moral judgments never matter,” then forgiveness becomes nonsensical (you can’t forgive what isn’t wrong), wisdom is impossible, and the passage itself collapses—because Jesus is clearly commanding a specific *kind* of moral posture. The verse forbids a **spirit** of condemnation, not the faculty of discernment.

Here is the distinction:

### 1. Discernment evaluates actions; condemnation writes off people
**Discernment** is the necessary work of distinguishing true from false, healthy from toxic, wise from foolish. Scripture everywhere commands it: Jesus told his disciples to “beware of false prophets” (Matt. 7:15); Paul instructed churches to correct members living in unrepentant sin (1 Cor. 5); Proverbs praises the person who “hates evil” (Prov. 8:13). Discernment looks at behavior, ideas, or fruit and makes a judgment *about them*. Crucially, it can do this without deciding that the other person is beyond redemption or that the observer is inherently superior.

**The appetite to condemn** (Greek *krinō* in its judicial/punitive sense) is different. It is not satisfied with saying, “That action is harmful and wrong.” It seeks to assign final worth: *You are trash. You are finished. I am the righteous one, and you are the reject.* It is prosecutorial, not protective. Where discernment asks, “What is true here, and how can this be made right?” condemnation asks, “How can I prove I’m not like *them*?”

### 2. Discernment serves love; condemnation serves the ego
Notice the parallelism in Luke 6:37:
- Judge → you will be judged
- Condemn → you will be condemned
- Forgive → you will be forgiven

Jesus is describing a **reciprocal posture**. The person who lives with a measuring stick always pressed against others will eventually find that same stick pressed against themselves. Discernment can be exercised in humility—“There but for the grace of God go I.” But the appetite to condemn is self-righteousness disguised as morality. It uses another’s failure as fuel for self-elevation.

This is precisely the context of the “beam and speck” passage (Luke 6:41–42). Jesus does not say, “Don’t notice the speck.” He says, “First remove the beam from your own eye, **and then** you can see clearly to take the speck from your brother’s eye.” Discernment is not banned; hypocrisy in judgment is.

### 3. Moral judgment *does* matter—otherwise Jesus is a hypocrite
If Luke 6:37 meant “never call anything sinful,” then Jesus violated his own command repeatedly. He called the Pharisees “hypocrites” (Matt. 23). He told the woman caught in adultery, “Go, and **sin no more**” (John 8:11)—which requires recognizing that adultery is wrong. He drove money-changers from the temple with a whip, making a fierce moral judgment about their corruption.

But observe the pattern: Jesus **discerned** sin clearly and **refused to condemn** the sinner to destruction. To the adulterous woman he said, “Neither do I condemn you.” He separated the evil act from the person’s eternal worth. That is the line between discernment and condemnation.

### 4. The heart-test: Can you forgive?
Here is the practical test Luke offers. If your “moral judgment” leaves you unable or unwilling to forgive, you have crossed from discernment into condemnation. Discernment can coexist with mercy—“That was wrong, and I still extend grace to you.” Condemnation cannot tolerate mercy, because mercy undermines its verdict.

So the verse is not saying morality is an illusion. It is saying:
- Do not appoint yourself as the final judge of another’s soul.
- Do not relish the role of executioner.
- Apply to others the same grace you need for your own failures.

In short: **Discernment is a tool for truth and protection; the appetite to condemn is a hunger for superiority.** One builds boundaries with humility. The other builds thrones for the self. Luke 6:37 targets the latter—while leaving the former not only intact, but necessary for a life that can genuinely forgive.
