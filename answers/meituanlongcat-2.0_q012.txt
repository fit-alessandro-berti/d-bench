Jeremiah 17:9–10 presents a profound tension between the human capacity for self-deception and the divine ability to perceive truth. To answer your questions, it is helpful to look at the text through both a theological and a psychological lens.

### Is the passage mainly pessimistic about human nature, or realistic about self-deception?

The passage is **realistic about self-deception**, though it uses language that sounds deeply pessimistic to modern ears. 

**The Pessimistic Element:**
The language is undeniably harsh. The Hebrew word translated "desperately wicked" is *anesh*, which can also be translated as "incurable" or "sick." The text asserts that the human heart—the biblical center of the mind, will, and affections—is fundamentally sick and deceitful. This rejects any naive optimism that humans are inherently good or perfectly capable of self-governance. 

**The Realistic Element:**
However, the core of the verse is not just that humans are "wicked," but that the heart is "deceitful." The primary danger highlighted is not just moral failure, but **self-deception**. The heart lies to itself. We are highly skilled at justifying our actions, convincing ourselves that selfish motives are altruistic, and reframing our vices as virtues. 

Therefore, the passage is realistic rather than purely pessimistic. A purely pessimistic view would say, "Humans are evil, and there is nothing to be done about it." A realistic view says, "Humans are deeply prone to deceiving themselves about their own goodness, which makes self-diagnosis impossible." The purpose of this realism is not to drive the reader to despair, but to drive them away from self-reliance. It is a diagnostic statement meant to show why human beings need an outside perspective.

### How should someone talk about motives if the heart itself is an unreliable witness?

If the heart is an unreliable witness, talking about motives requires a shift from pure introspection to external verification. Here are several ways to approach this:

**1. Evaluate the "Fruit" (Actions over Feelings)**
Verse 10 states that God gives every man "according to the fruit of his doings." Actions are often a more reliable indicator of true motives than internal feelings. A person may feel entirely justified in their anger, but if the "fruit" of that anger is destruction and broken relationships, the underlying motive is likely selfishness. When evaluating motives, it is wiser to look at the tangible outcomes of an action rather than the internal narrative that accompanied it.

**2. Rely on External Standards**
If the internal compass is broken, an external map is required. In the Judeo-Christian tradition, this means measuring one's motives against the objective standard of Scripture and divine law. In a broader philosophical sense, it means measuring motives against established ethical frameworks, logic, and the consensus of the community, rather than relying on how "pure" a motive feels in the moment.

**3. Cultivate Epistemic Humility**
If the heart is deceitful, anyone claiming "I know my motives are completely pure" is likely a victim of the very deceit Jeremiah describes. Talking about motives should be done with profound humility. Instead of saying, "I am doing this purely out of love," it is more accurate to say, "I believe my motive is love, but I am aware that I am capable of self-deception, so I am open to correction."

**4. Invite Outside Counsel**
Because we have blind spots, we cannot see our own motives clearly. Trusted friends, mentors, spiritual directors, or therapists act as mirrors. They can often see the true fruit and underlying drivers of our actions long before we can. Proverbs 11:14 echoes this: "Where no counsel is, the people fall: but in the multitude of counsellors there is safety."

**5. Submit to Divine Searching**
Theologically, verse 10 is the ultimate comfort to the problem of verse 9. If the heart is unknowable to the self, it is entirely known to God. The proper response to the deceitful heart is not to stop examining motives, but to invite God to search them. The Psalmist prays similarly in Psalm 139:23-24: "Search me, O God, and know my heart: try me, and know my thoughts: And see if there be any wicked way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting."

In summary, Jeremiah 17:9–10 is a realistic warning against the human capacity for self-justification. It teaches that we cannot trust our own hearts to give a true account of our motives. Therefore, talking about motives requires looking at objective fruit, relying on external truth, maintaining humility, seeking community, and ultimately relying on God to reveal what we cannot see in ourselves.
