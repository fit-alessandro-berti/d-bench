This passage from Isaiah highlights an important theological and ethical truth - that goodness and righteousness are not merely about avoiding evil, but actively pursuing what is right and just. The progression from "cease to do evil" to "learn to do well" suggests that true morality requires more than simply refraining from wrongdoing. It calls for the cultivation of positive virtues and habits.

The Hebrew word translated as "learn" here is למדו (limdu), which carries the sense of training, practicing and becoming accustomed to something. So the idea is that doing good must become second nature through repeated practice, not just a now-and-then occurrence. 

The specific examples given of doing well reinforce this:
- "Seek judgment" - Pursue justice actively
- "Relieve the oppressed" - Take action to help the downtrodden 
- "Judge the fatherless, plead for the widow" - Stand up for the vulnerable who lack protectors

These are not passive acts but require effort, engagement and commitment to the wellbeing of others, especially the marginalized. They paint a picture of goodness as other-centered, self-giving love.

The passage suggests that avoiding evil is the necessary first step, but it is incomplete on its own. Real righteousness means developing a character oriented toward justice, mercy and sacrificial concern for others. It means learning the habits of proactive goodness.

Theologians have noted how this connects to the biblical understanding of holiness. To be holy as God is holy (Lev 19:2) is not merely to be morally pure but to reflect God's own character of compassionate love and zeal for justice. As this passage indicates, that requires training the heart, not just adhering to a list of "thou shalt nots."

So in summary, Isaiah is calling the people not simply to moral restraint, but to embody a practiced, passionate commitment to the good of others, especially the needy - to make justice and compassion instinctive and habitual. That is the full biblical vision of the righteous life. It suggests goodness is ultimately about emulating the proactive, others-focused love that defines God's own character.
