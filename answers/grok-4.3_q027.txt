The Pharisee is condemned primarily for **pride** that manifests as self-trust and contempt for others. The text itself frames the parable around those who “trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others” (v. 9). Everything else flows from that root.

### Why the Pharisee is rejected
- **Self-trust, not false doctrine**: The Pharisee is not teaching doctrinal error. His statements about fasting and tithing are factually correct under the law. The problem is not *what* he has done but *why* he is presenting it to God—as evidence that he is already righteous and therefore needs nothing from God.
- **Comparison and contempt**: He explicitly uses the tax collector as a foil (“even as this publican”). His prayer is not directed toward God so much as it is a performance of superiority. The phrase “prayed thus with himself” is telling; the audience is ultimately himself and any human observers.
- **Virtue as self-display**: Fasting and tithing become tools for establishing his own righteousness rather than expressions of love for God or neighbor. This is the opposite of the righteousness Jesus describes elsewhere (e.g., Matthew 6:1–6, 16–18), where genuine piety is done secretly before the Father.

In short, the Pharisee’s prayer is not really a prayer at all. It is a religious resume read aloud to God. He approaches God on the basis of his own record rather than on the basis of mercy.

### Why the tax collector is justified
The tax collector’s posture and words are treated as spiritually truer because they embody the opposite posture:
- He refuses to lift his eyes, beats his breast, and stands at a distance. These are classic signs of shame and repentance, not self-assertion.
- His single sentence (“God be merciful to me, the sinner”) contains no comparison with others and no appeal to his own merits. He identifies himself as *the* sinner, not merely *a* sinner among many.
- He throws himself entirely on God’s mercy (*hilaskomai* carries the sense of propitiation or atonement). This is the posture Jesus consistently honors throughout the Gospels: the person who knows he has nothing to bring except his need.

Jesus’ verdict—“this man went down to his house justified rather than the other”—directly links justification with humility and the acknowledgment of sin, not with religious performance. The tax collector receives what he asked for (mercy); the Pharisee receives what he implicitly asked for (a confirmation of his own righteousness), which is ultimately nothing.

### Summary
The condemnation is not mainly doctrinal inaccuracy or even the virtues themselves. It is the **self-justifying heart** that turns virtue into a platform for superiority and that refuses to come to God empty-handed. The tax collector’s posture is “truer” because it matches the actual condition of every human being before a holy God and therefore becomes the only ground on which mercy can be received.
