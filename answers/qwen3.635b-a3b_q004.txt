The trust commanded in Proverbs 3:5–6 is **wholehearted, relational reliance on God’s wisdom and sovereignty**, characterized by conscious dependence, volitional submission, and the refusal to treat human reasoning as the final court of appeal. It is not passive resignation, uncritical belief, or emotional sentimentality. Rather, it is an active orientation of the will that acknowledges God as the ultimate source of truth, direction, and safety.

To answer your second point precisely:

### 🔹 What is meant by "humble self-distrust"?
The Hebrew verb translated "lean not" (שָׁעֵן, *sha'an*) means to rest one’s weight upon, take refuge in, or depend on. The text does not say "abandon your understanding" but warns against making it your ultimate foundation. "Humble self-distrust" (better termed **epistemic humility of reliance**) acknowledges:
- The **finitude** of human perspective: we see in part (1 Cor. 13:12).
- The **fallenness** of human reasoning: sin distorts judgment and biases our conclusions (Jer. 17:9).
- The **relational posture** of dependence: we continue to think, plan, and analyze, but we submit our insights to God’s authority rather than trusting them to infallibly guide us.

This is not self-negation or despair. It is the recognition that human understanding is a *derived* gift, not an *autonomous* standard.

### 🔹 Why this is not anti-intellectualism
Anti-intellectualism devalues or rejects the intellect itself, often framing faith and reason as inherent opposites. Proverbs 3:5–6 does none of this:
1. **It assumes understanding exists and is usable.** The warning is against *exclusive reliance*, not against use. Wisdom literature throughout Scripture commands study, reflection, and discernment (Prov. 18:15; 24:3–4; Ps. 111:10).
2. **It corrects, does not cancel.** "Lean not" is a directional warning against idolizing human perspective, not a command to discard it. Faith engages the mind but refuses to make it the ultimate arbiter of truth.
3. **It pairs humility with stewardship.** Verse 6 commands us to "acknowledge" (יָדַע, *yada*; to know, recognize, publicly confess) God in all our ways. This implies bringing our learning, plans, and analyses *before* Him in submission, not leaving them behind at the door of religion.

### 🔑 Key Distinctions
| Humble Self-Distrust (Prov. 3:5–6) | Anti-Intellectualism |
|-----------------------------------|----------------------|
| Subordinates reason to revelation | Rejects or devalues reason |
| Uses the mind in dependence on God | Abdicates the mind to emotion or dogma |
| Acknowledges limits without despair | Assumes intellect is inherently oppositional to faith |
| Leads to discernment & wisdom | Often breeds unexamined certainty or irrationalism |

### ✅ In Summary
The trust Proverbs 3:5–6 asks for is **reliance without reliance on self**. It is a volitional resting of one’s life-weight on God’s character and wisdom, while faithfully stewarding the mind He gave. "Humble self-distrust" is the acknowledgment that our understanding is finite and fallen enough to mislead us if made autonomous; it is not a rejection of reason, but a refusal to idolize it. The passage calls us to bring our intellects to God, not ban them, trusting that He will rightly order what the human heart cannot fully discern.
