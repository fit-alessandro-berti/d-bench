

**Short answer:**  
In Matthew 12:34‑35 Jesus treats speech as **both a symptom and a revelation of the moral condition stored in the heart**, and, because the heart’s “treasure” is itself the product of ongoing choices, the utterance can also be understood as a **choice** that either confirms or reshapes that inner store. In other words, the verse sees words as **symptoms + revelations + the outward expression of prior choices**.

---

### 1. What the “treasure of the heart” means  

| Element | Biblical usage | What it implies for speech |
|---------|----------------|----------------------------|
| **Heart (καρδία)** | The inner seat of thoughts, desires, motives (e.g., Ps 51:10; Jer 17:9). | The hidden reservoir where virtues or vices accumulate. |
| **Treasure (θησαυρός)** | A storehouse of value — can be good (e.g., Matt 6:20) or evil (e.g., Matt 6:19‑21). | The moral “stockpile” that has been built up over time through habitual attitudes and decisions. |
| **Abundance (πληθύνω)** | Overflow; when the store is full, it spills out. | When the heart’s moral content reaches a critical mass, it inevitably finds expression. |

Thus, the “treasure” is **not a static inventory but the cumulative result of repeated moral choices** (acts of kindness, honesty, selfishness, deceit, etc.).  

---

### 2. Speech as a **symptom**  

- **Medical metaphor:** Just as a fever signals an underlying infection, words signal the state of the heart.  
- **Jesus’ logic:** “Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh” (v. 34) – if the heart is evil, evil speech will appear; if the heart is good, good speech will appear.  
- **Implication:** Observing someone’s speech can diagnose the hidden moral condition without needing direct access to the heart.

---

### 3. Speech as a **revelation**  

- **Epistemic function:** The utterance makes the otherwise invisible interior visible to others (and to God).  
- **Divine judgment:** Jesus uses this principle to expose the Pharisees’ hypocrisy: their outward religiosity masks an evil heart, and their words reveal that mismatch.  
- **Revelatory power:** In the broader Matthean context (chap. 12), Jesus contrasts “the kingdom of God” with “the kingdom of Satan” by showing that the source of one’s words indicates whose kingdom one serves.

---

### 4. Speech as a **choice** (the feedback loop)  

- While the verse emphasizes that the heart’s content drives speech, the New Testament elsewhere stresses that **speech can also shape the heart** (e.g., James 3:5‑10; Eph 4:29).  
- Choosing to speak truthfully or lovingly reinforces good treasures; choosing to speak lies or malice reinforces evil treasures.  
- Therefore, the act of speaking is **not purely passive** — it is a moral decision that either affirms or modifies the existing store.

---

### 5. Synthesis: All three dimensions  

| Dimension | How the verse supports it |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| **Symptom** | “Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh” – speech follows the heart’s condition. |
| **Revelation** | The spoken word makes the hidden heart known to listeners (and to God). |
| **Choice** | The heart’s abundance is itself the result of prior choices; speaking can further choose to reinforce or alter that abundance. |

Consequently, Matthew 12:34‑35 presents speech as **the outward manifestation of an inner moral treasury**, a **diagnostic sign** of that treasury, a **means by which the treasury is disclosed**, and an **action that can either confirm or transform** the treasury through ongoing moral decisions.

---

### Practical take‑away  

- **Self‑examination:** Monitor your own speech as a window into your heart’s stockpile.  
- **Spiritual discipline:** Cultivate good treasures (prayer, Scripture, acts of mercy) so that overflow yields wholesome words.  
- **Relational discernment:** When others’ words consistently reveal bitterness, deceit, or hostility, it signals an underlying evil store that may need confrontation, prayer, or pastoral care.  

In short, the passage does not force us to pick one label; it invites us to see words as **symptomatic, revelatory, and choic‑laden** all at once.
