The key to Luke 6:37 is that Jesus isn’t telling us to abandon all moral evaluation; he’s warning us against a self-righteous, joyless, punitive spirit.  In other words, there’s a world of difference between

  • Discernment—honest, humble seeking of what is right, coupled with mercy and a willingness to be corrected yourself  
  • The appetite to condemn—a self-appointed throne from which you pronounce final, punitive sentences on others  

1. Context of “judge not”  
   – Jesus is speaking to people weary of religious elites who condemned others without mercy, often to mask their own faults.  
   – In Matthew’s parallel (7:1–5) he immediately goes on to warn against hypocrisy: “First take the plank out of your own eye, and then you will see clearly to remove the speck from your brother’s eye.”

2. “Discernment” vs. “Condemnation”  
   a) Discernment:  
      • Means “to judge between”—to recognize right and wrong, to make wise choices, to hold one another accountable out of love.  
      • Paul urges believers to “test everything; hold fast to what is good” (1 Thess. 5:21).  
      • John 7:24: “Do not judge by appearances, but judge with right judgment.”  
      • Discernment submits itself to God’s standards, stays open to correction, and seeks restoration.  
   b) Condemnation:  
      • Seeks to exclude, shame, punish—often for the sake of feeling morally superior.  
      • Closes a heart to repentance or dialogue.  
      • Lays on the guilt, but offers no path to grace.

3. Why moral judgment still matters  
   – Jesus confronted immorality again and again (e.g. the woman caught in adultery), not to drive sinners into deeper shame but to offer forgiveness and call people to new life.  
   – The early church practiced church discipline (1 Cor. 5) alongside forgiveness and restoration (Gal. 6:1).  
   – To say “judge not” doesn’t erase moral truth; it changes the posture from “I have found you guilty” to “Here’s how love calls you to healing.”

4. How to practice godly discernment  
   • Examine your own heart first—are you driven by love for the other person or by your own need to feel superior?  
   • Gather facts carefully; listen before leaping to conclusions.  
   • Speak truth in love (Eph. 4:15): clear about standards, but tender toward the struggler.  
   • Leave ultimate judgment to God—he alone can read hearts perfectly.  
   • Always pair correction with a hand extended in forgiveness.

In Luke 6:37 Jesus is steering us away from a carnivorous kind of “justice” that delights in others’ downfall, and toward a redemptive justice that holds fast to truth while leaving space for mercy. Moral judgments still matter—they safeguard love and truth in the community—but they must arise from compassion, humility, and the same forgiveness we ourselves have received.
