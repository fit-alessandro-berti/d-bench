In Mark 8:34-35, Jesus is teaching His disciples the necessity of denying themselves in order to follow Him, using the metaphor of taking up one's "cross." The "cross" here symbolizes the hardships, struggles, and ultimate sacrifice that must be embraced in the path of following Him and spreading the gospel. This concept of self-denial can be seen as liberating and daunting for different reasons:

**Liberation Perspective:**

1. **Relational Freedom:** Denying oneself often means letting go of personal pride, selfishness, and attachment to material possessions and societal status. This relinse, from self-interest, can lead to a deeper, more authentic connection with others and with God.

2. **Spiritual Transformation:** By putting aside personal desires, the individual is opened up to spiritual growth and transformation. This process can foster a sense of inner peace and contentment that stems from being aligned with a higher purpose.

3. **Mission Commitment:** The call to follow Jesus inherently includes a commitment to spread the gospel, which can be a deeply meaningful and liberating mission. Many find freedom of purpose, knowing they are part of something significant and eternal.

4. **Love and Service:** Denying one's own comfort can lead to living out a life of love and service for others and for Jesus' teachings, which can be tremendously fulfilling on a moral and emotional level.

5. **Eternal Perspective:** Considering the larger scope of eternity, choosing the cross can seem like a small price to pay when weighed against the possibility of eternal salvation and the promise of everlasting life with God.

6. **Suffering with Jesus:** Embracing the cross also means sharing in the suffering of Jesus, which, according to Christian belief, is a pathway to redemption and reconciliation with God.

7. **Fulfilling Prophecy and Teaching:** Some may find liberation in realizing that Jesus' call aligns with age-old prophetic expectations and divine teachings, which often emphasize sacrifice, selflessness, and ultimate discipleship.

**Why It Might Be Offensive:**

1. **Personal Autonomy:** The notion of denying oneself can sometimes feel like an infringement upon personal freedoms and choices. Many value the ability to make decisions independent of external pressures and may see self-denial as sacrificing their own desires and way of life.

2. **Practical Implications:** The idea of actively seeking suffering as a means to salvation might appear practical. People often seek joy and avoid pain, so the idea of embracing suffering can be unsettling.

3. **Cultural and Personality Differences:** Western cultures, in particular, prize individualism and personal achievement. The concept of self-denial might clash with these values, making it seem alien or even repressive.

4. **Fear of Failure:** Denying oneself and the potential to fail in the disciple’s role might be intimidating. Fear of not living up to the expectations can deter individuals from embracing this calling.

5. **Misunderstanding the Teachings:** Misconceptions about Jesus' message, such as equating it with asceticism or extreme self-denial, can lead to the perception that it's about depriving oneself rather than finding the right balance between faith and daily life.

6. **Uncertainty about Salvation:** The assurance that following Jesus will lead to salvation is not universal; some may wrestle with doubts and fears about the afterlife and salvation itself.

7. **Interpretation of Sacrifice:** If not understood correctly, self-denial can be seen as self-destructive or misplaced, and in a way that appears unhealthy or disproportionate—particularly if one's sense of identity is strongly attached to their abilities and roles in life.

**Conclusion:**

Regarding the balance between these perspectse the feeling of liberation arises from surrendering to a higher purpose and the potential transformation associated with personal and spiritual development. The offensiveness primarily stems from cultural norms that prize self-gain and independence, fear of the consequences of putting oneself and failures, as well as misunderstandings or misinterpretations of what self-sacrifice entails in this context. The subjective nature of these responses reflects the diversity of background, belief, and personal values held by each individual. For some, embracing this path can be a liberating and life-affirming choice, while for others it may feel misaligned with their understanding of what is best for themselves both individually and as part of a broader social system. Communicating the deeper, more balanced intent behind the call to take up one's cross and understand Jesus' message about the purpose of suffering and sacrifice is key in responding to the concerns and unease of others regarding self-denial.
