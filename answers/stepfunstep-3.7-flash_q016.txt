To answer this question, we first have to situate the elder brother in the context of Jesus’s parable, told to self-righteous Pharisees and scribes who resented Jesus’s welcome of "sinners" (represented by the prodigal son). While the elder brother does exhibit anger, envy, a wounded sense of fairness, and discomfort with his father’s mercy to his wayward sibling, all of these are surface symptoms of a far deeper root: **pride in his own obedience, which frames his relationship with his father as a transactional, works-based exchange rather than a gracious familial bond.**
Let’s break down why the other options are secondary to this core issue:
1. **Anger** is his immediate emotional reaction to the feast for his brother, but it is triggered by the deeper grievance that the father is "wasting" grace on someone he sees as unworthy.
2. **Envy** of the celebration his brother receives stems from his belief that his years of faithful service entitle him to that favor more than the prodigal. He does not resent the feast itself so much as the fact that it is given to someone he deems undeserving.
3. **Wounded fairness** is not a godly commitment to justice, but a self-righteous standard that measures worth by moral performance. He sees his own obedience as a merit that should be rewarded, and his brother’s rebellion as a disqualification from restoration. This standard only exists because he trusts in his own works as the basis of his standing with the father.
4. **Discomfort with mercy itself** is a direct consequence of this pride: if the father can freely forgive and restore a son who squandered his inheritance, the elder brother’s years of striving are rendered meaningless as a ticket to special status. He cannot tolerate unmerited grace because it exposes that his standing as a son was never something he earned—it was a gift all along.
The elder brother’s own words reveal this core issue: he tells his father, "Lo, these many years do I serve thee [using the Greek word *douleuō*, meaning to serve as a slave, not a son], neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment." He has reduced his relationship with his father from that of a beloved son to a hired servant who trades good behavior for reward, and resents when the father extends grace outside of this transactional framework. The father’s reply cuts to the heart of his delusion: "Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine." The elder brother already has full access to his father’s love and provision, but his pride in his own obedience blinds him to this gift, and makes him unable to rejoice in his father’s joy or his brother’s restoration.
