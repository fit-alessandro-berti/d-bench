The moral danger in Matthew 6:1 lies **not in visibility itself, but in the craving to be seen**. Jesus' warning is fundamentally about *motivation*, not *method*.

### 1. The Greek makes intention explicit
The phrase "to be seen of them" translates `εἰς τὸ θεάσεσθαι αὐτούς` ("in order to be seen by them"). The preposition `εἰς` (`eis` + infinitive) marks **purpose**. Jesus isn't saying "avoid public places"; he's saying "don't perform righteousness *for the sake of* human applause." The sin is teleological: doing good to curate reputation rather than to obey and love God.

### 2. Why the craving is morally dangerous
- **Idolatry of human approval:** When people become the primary audience, God is displaced. Goodness shifts from worship to performance, making reputation an idol.
- **Corruption of the act:** Compassion aimed at visibility often lacks genuine love. It can breed pride, exploit the vulnerable as props, and turn mercy into social or spiritual currency.
- **Severance from divine communion:** Jesus contrasts earthly validation with "reward from your Father in heaven." This "reward" isn't a transactional wage but the fellowship, affirmation, and eternal joy of being aligned with God's heart. Performative righteousness cuts the act off from its only true source and end.

### 3. Jesus doesn't condemn visibility per se
Throughout the Gospels, Jesus:
- Performed miracles publicly
- Fed thousands in plain sight
- Commissioned the church to proclaim and do good openly
- Later commended public confession of faith (Matt 10:32-33)

What he condemns here is **religious theater**: acts designed to manufacture human admiration rather than reflect God's character. The call to give "in secret" (v. 4) is a spiritual safeguard, a discipline to protect the heart from the subtle but corrosive desire for applause.

### In short
Visibility is morally neutral; the craving to be seen is spiritually lethal. When goodness is done to be witnessed by humans rather than offered to God, it ceases to be righteousness and becomes self-worship. Jesus' warning targets the heart's orientation: true almsgiving looks upward in trust, not outward in expectation.
