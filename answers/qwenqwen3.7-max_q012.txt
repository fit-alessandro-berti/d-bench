The passage from Jeremiah 17:9–10 is one of the most penetrating anthropological statements in ancient literature. To answer your questions, we must look at the text not merely as a theological decree, but as a profound observation of human psychology and epistemology (how we know what we know).

Here is an analysis of whether the text is pessimistic or realistic, followed by a framework for how to discuss motives when the "heart" cannot be trusted.

---

### Part 1: Pessimism vs. Realism
The passage is best understood as **diagnostic realism** rather than fatalistic pessimism. 

**Pessimism** suggests that because human nature is flawed, effort is futile, and despair is the only logical response. **Realism**, however, accurately maps the terrain of a problem so that it can be navigated, managed, or cured. Jeremiah is acting as a physician delivering a harsh but necessary diagnosis.

Two Hebrew words in verse 9 highlight this realism:
1. **"Deceitful" (*aqob*):** This word means slippery, uneven, or tricky. It is the same root used to describe Jacob (the "supplanter" or "trickster"). Jeremiah is pointing out that the human mind is an expert at rationalization. We do not usually sin while admitting we are being evil; we sin while convincing ourselves we are being justified, pragmatic, or even righteous.
2. **"Desperately wicked" (*anush*):** More accurately translated as "incurably sick" or "mortally wounded." Jeremiah is not just making a moral judgment; he is making a medical one. The human ego is infected with a terminal blind spot.

Modern psychology strongly validates Jeremiah’s realism. Concepts like *cognitive dissonance, confirmation bias, self-serving bias,* and *rationalization* all describe the exact "deceitfulness" Jeremiah identifies. The heart is not an objective observer; it is a defense attorney hired by the ego. Therefore, expecting the heart to provide an objective assessment of itself is a category error. 

The realism of the passage is ultimately hopeful because it leads to Verse 10: **The introduction of an Objective Auditor.** If the heart is sick, the cure cannot come from within the heart. It requires an external, omniscient standard (God) and empirical evidence (fruit).

---

### Part 2: How to Talk About Motives When the Heart is Unreliable
If the heart is an unreliable witness—acting as both the defendant and the judge in the courtroom of the self—how do we evaluate or discuss motives, whether our own or others'? 

Jeremiah 17:10 provides the exact methodology: *"I the Lord search the heart... even to give every man according to his ways, and according to the fruit of his doings."* 

Here are four rules for discussing motives based on this text:

#### 1. Shift from "Root" to "Fruit" (Impact over Intent)
Because the "root" (the internal motive) is hidden and subject to severe self-deception, human beings must evaluate the "fruit" (the external actions and outcomes). 
* **How to talk about it:** When confronting someone (or yourself), do not get bogged down in debates about what was *intended*. Instead, look at the *impact*. You cannot see a person's heart, but you can see their "ways" and their "doings." If a person claims their motive was pure, but the consistent fruit of their actions is destructive, the fruit is the truer witness. 

#### 2. Practice Epistemic Humility Regarding Your Own Motives
If the heart is deceitful above all things, you must hold your own self-assessments with deep suspicion. 
* **How to talk about it:** When explaining your own motives, use the language of humility and limitation. Instead of saying, *"I know my heart was in the right place,"* a Jeremian approach says, *"My intention was to help, but I know I am capable of self-deception. How did my actions actually land with you?"* This invites external correction into the blind spots of your own heart.

#### 3. Look for Patterns ("His Ways"), Not Isolated Incidents
Verse 10 notes that God judges according to a person's *"ways"* (patterns of behavior over time). Self-deception is highly effective in isolated incidents, but it is very difficult to maintain a facade over a lifetime of patterns.
* **How to talk about it:** When trying to discern someone's true motives (e.g., in hiring, marriage, or leadership), ignore their stated intentions and look at their historical "ways." How do they treat people who can do nothing for them? How do they handle power? How do they respond to criticism? Patterns bypass the deceitful heart and reveal the true character.

#### 4. Reserve Ultimate Judgment of Motives for God
The text draws a strict boundary between human jurisdiction and divine jurisdiction. Humans are tasked with observing the *fruit*; God claims the exclusive right to search the *heart*.
* **How to talk about it:** We should refrain from playing God by declaring, *"I know exactly why you did that; you did it out of jealousy/malice."* We can say, *"Your actions were harmful and violated our agreement."* We address the actionable behavior and leave the ultimate, hidden architecture of the soul to the Divine Auditor. This prevents us from becoming arrogant and presumptuous.

### Summary
Jeremiah 17:9-10 is not a counsel of despair; it is a **liberation from the echo chamber of the ego**. 

By declaring the heart an unreliable witness, the text frees us from the exhausting task of trying to perfectly psychoanalyze ourselves and others. It tells us to stop trusting our own press releases. Instead, it directs us to look at the tangible reality of our actions ("the fruit"), to submit to the correction of community and truth, and to trust that ultimate justice and true self-knowledge rest safely in the hands of a God who sees what we cannot.
