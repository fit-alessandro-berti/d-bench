The passage in Matthew 12:34–35 establishes a profound connection between speech and the moral state of the heart, emphasizing that words are not random or independent actions but natural outflows of an individual’s inner character. Here’s how the passage links speech to hidden moral "stockpiles" in the heart, and how it frames words as symptoms, choices, and revelations:

---

### **1. Speech as a Symptom**  
The verse explicitly states that words reflect the heart’s condition:  
> *“Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh”* (Matthew 12:34).  
Here, speech functions as a **symptom**—a visible sign of an underlying reality. Just as a fever reveals an infection, the content of a person’s speech exposes the moral and spiritual state of their heart. If the heart is "evil" (filled with malice, hypocrisy, or wickedness), speech will inevitably reflect that evil. Conversely, a "good" heart produces speech that aligns with its goodness. The words are a diagnostic indicator of what lies hidden within.

---

### **2. Speech as a Choice**  
While the heart’s influence is primary, the verse also implies human agency. The mouth "speaketh" because it is an instrument of choice, shaped by the heart’s inclinations. However, this choice is not entirely free; it is **determined by the heart’s preexisting state**. The heart’s "treasure" (its dominant values and desires) guides the decision-making process. Thus, speech is a **choice rooted in the heart’s character**. Jesus’ harsh condemnation of the crowd as a "generation of vipers" underscores that their speech was not merely accidental but a deliberate expression of their corrupted nature.

---

### **3. Speech as a Revelation**  
The passage positions speech as a **revelation** of the heart’s hidden contents. Words make the invisible visible. Jesus had previously warned, *“Every idle word that men shall speak, I will judge it”* (Matthew 12:36), suggesting that speech discloses what was meant to remain concealed. The heart’s "treasures" (its deepest moral and spiritual holdings) are laid bare through speech. This revelation has judgmental consequences, as it exposes hypocrisy or righteousness, aligning with Jesus’ teaching that one cannot hide their true character from God or others.

---

### **Synthesis: All Three Roles**  
The passage synthesizes these three dimensions:  
- **Symptom**: Speech signals the heart’s condition.  
- **Choice**: Words are deliberate expressions shaped by the heart.  
- **Revelation**: Speech discloses the heart’s hidden state, both to others and to divine judgment.  

Jesus’ argument is not merely descriptive but **normative**—it warns that moral transformation must begin internally. Superficial compliance (e.g., avoiding overt evil speech) is insufficient; true righteousness requires a heart regenerated by God (cf. Matthew 15:18–20). The verse thus ties moral speech to the broader Christian call to cultivate inner virtue, as the heart’s "treasure" determines the quality of every word and action.

In summary, Matthew 12:34–35 teaches that speech is a multifaceted expression of the heart’s moral stockpiles—both a mirror of inner reality and a testament to one’s character before God and others.
