### What Condemns the Pharisee
First, we can immediately eliminate **false doctrine**: The Pharisee’s practices—fasting twice weekly, tithing all his possessions—align with traditional Jewish religious law. His error is not theological, but attitudinal.

The core and exact condemnation is **pride: specifically self-righteous pride**—the belief that his own moral observances made him worthy of God’s favor without needing His mercy. This pride manifests through the other listed traits, all interconnected:
1.  **Comparison**: He structures his prayer around contrasting himself with "extortioners, unjust, adulterers, and this publican" to elevate his own status, using these groups as a baseline for his supposed righteousness. Comparison itself is not inherently sinful, but here it serves his prideful goal of justifying himself.
2.  **Contempt**: As noted in the opening verse, he "despised others"—explicitly dismissing the publican (a reviled tax collector working for Roman occupiers) as spiritually inferior, a direct product of his self-exalting pride.
3.  **Virtue as self-display**: He lists his fasts and tithes not as an act of grateful worship to God, but as a way to advertise his own righteousness. His virtuous deeds become a tool for self-promotion rather than humility before God.

While all these are present, the root condemnation is pride: he trusted entirely in his own works instead of God’s grace to earn a right standing with God.

### Why the Tax Collector’s Posture is Spiritually Truer
The tax collector embodies the humble, grace-dependent posture Jesus teaches as essential for spiritual approval, in direct contrast to the Pharisee:
- He rejects self-justification entirely: he stands afar off, avoids lifting his eyes to heaven (a gesture that would signal confidence in his own worth), and smites his breast—a traditional Jewish gesture of repentance and grief over sin.
- His prayer is a raw, humble confession: *"God be merciful to me a sinner."* He acknowledges his own sinfulness and begs for God’s mercy, rather than relying on his own good deeds to earn favor.
- Jesus’ closing pronouncement confirms this: the tax collector "went down to his house justified" (declared righteous by God) because he humbled himself, while the Pharisee—who exalted himself in his own works—was not. This aligns with Jesus’ broader teaching that *"whosoever exalteth himself shall be abased; and he that humbleth himself shall be exalted"* (Luke 14:11).
