In Ezekiel 36, the contrast is not between “having laws” and “having no laws,” but between **obeying God outwardly** and being **inwardly remade so that obedience arises from a changed disposition**.

### External compliance

External compliance can mean doing what God commands for reasons such as fear, social pressure, habit, self-interest, or religious appearance. A person may conform in visible behavior while remaining resistant, indifferent, proud, or alienated inwardly.

Israel had received God’s statutes, sacrifices, priests, land, and covenant signs. Yet Ezekiel repeatedly says that the people had profaned God’s name through idolatry, injustice, and rebellion. The problem was not simply that they lacked information about God’s will. They knew the commands. The deeper problem was that they did not have hearts responsive to God.

So “a new heart” does not mean merely acquiring new rules or trying harder. It means receiving a new inner orientation: a will that is turned toward God rather than away from him.

### “Heart” in Ezekiel

In biblical language, the “heart” is more than the seat of feelings. It includes the center of a person’s:

- thinking and understanding  
- desires and loves  
- choices and intentions  
- moral responsiveness  
- loyalty and worship  

A “new heart” therefore means a renewed self at its deepest level. God does not merely ask for better religious performance; he promises to change the source from which conduct flows.

### Why call it a “heart of stone”?

The imagery of stone describes hardness as something real, settled, and resistant—not merely a passing bad mood.

A heart of stone is:

- **unresponsive**: like stone, it does not feel or receive what should move it;  
- **resistant**: it cannot easily be shaped, corrected, or softened;  
- **dead or inert**: it lacks the living responsiveness proper to a covenant relationship with God;  
- **durable in rebellion**: the problem has become ingrained, not superficial.  

The metaphor also conveys human inability. A stone cannot make itself into living flesh. If the heart has become stone-like, the remedy must be more radical than advice, discipline, or external pressure. It must be an act of God: “I will take away … I will give … I will put.”

### “A heart of flesh” does not mean a merely physical or sinful heart

“Flesh” here is contrasted with stone, not with spirit. In this setting, “a heart of flesh” means a heart that is living, soft, receptive, and responsive. It is capable of being moved, instructed, corrected, and faithful.

The point is not that God replaces spirituality with bodily desire. It is that he replaces dead hardness with living sensitivity.

### The role of God’s Spirit

Verse 27 expands the promise:

> “I will put my spirit within you, and cause you to walk in my statutes.”

The new heart supplies inward renewal; God’s Spirit supplies sustaining power and direction. Obedience is still real obedience—“ye shall keep my judgments, and do them”—but it is no longer presented as merely human effort attempting to meet an external standard. It is the fruit of God’s renewing presence.

This is why Ezekiel does not dismiss God’s law. The law remains good; the problem is the hardened people who cannot faithfully live it. God’s answer is not to lower his standards, but to transform his people so they can walk in them.

### In context

Ezekiel 36 addresses Israel in exile, whose sin had brought judgment and whose restoration would display God’s holiness among the nations. The promise is corporate—God will restore his people—but it reaches into the deepest personal level: heart and spirit.

Thus the passage describes salvation and restoration not as cosmetic reform but as a kind of re-creation. Hardness is treated like a material condition because sin is portrayed as more than isolated wrong actions. It has become a settled condition of the inner person. God’s promise is therefore not simply, “Behave better,” but, “I will make you new from within.”
