This passage from Amos delivers a searing critique precisely because the issue isn't a lack of "balance" but a profound moral and spiritual contradiction. The sharp rejection exposes a specific kind of fraud: **the use of religious ritual to mask, and even legitimize, systemic injustice and oppression.**

Here’s a breakdown of the fraud being exposed:

### 1. **The Fraud of Substitute Morality**
The people believed that performing the correct rituals—feasts, sacrifices, songs—fulfilled their duty to God, almost like a cosmic transaction. They thought ritual piety could substitute for ethical behavior. God’s answer is a categorical rejection: He hates their festivals because they have become a diversion. The fraud is the belief that *orthodoxy* (correct worship) can replace *orthopraxy* (correct action toward one's neighbor).

### 2. **The Fraud of Sacred Hypocrisy**
The worship services were likely filled with sincere emotion and beautiful music ("the noise of thy songs," "melody of thy viols"). Yet, this very sincerity became a fraud because it created a false sense of divine favor while the worshipers were actively denying justice in the marketplace and courts (Amos 5:10-12). The worship wasn't merely "imbalanced"; it was a **delusional smokescreen** that allowed the worshipers to feel spiritually secure while practicing wickedness. It’s the fraud of using God’s name to sanction a way of life that contradicts His character.

### 3. **The Fraud of Ritual as Anesthetic**
The elaborate religious calendar served as a moral anesthetic, dulling the conscience. By being scrupulous in worship, they could avoid confronting the glaring injustice in their economic and social systems. This is why the language is so violent: God isn't asking for a tune-up of their worship; He is declaring it **morally poisonous and nauseating**. He will not be pacified or bribed by rituals offered by hands stained with injustice.

### 4. **The Core Inversion: What God Truly Demands**
The climax in verse 24 reveals the heart of the matter. God doesn't want better songs or more fervent sacrifices; He demands a total reordering of society. The metaphors of "judgment" (*mishpat*) and "righteousness" (*tsedaqah*) as unstoppable natural forces are powerful. They signify:
*   **Justice:** Fair legal processes, protection of the vulnerable, and the correction of wrongs.
*   **Righteousness:** Right relationships, equity, and moral integrity in all dealings.

God is saying that true worship is not an isolated religious activity but a life that flows with justice. The rituals of the temple were empty because they did not flow into and produce justice in the city streets.

### In Essence:
The fraud is **religious compartmentalization**. It’s the attempt to worship a God of justice in a way that conveniently ignores justice. Amos, speaking for God, exposes this as not just poor balance but as a fundamental betrayal of the covenant. The rejection is sharp because the sin is severe: it makes a mockery of God’s character and exploits the vulnerable while using God’s own名义 (name) to sanctify the oppression.

It’s a warning that **liturgy without ethics is not just incomplete; it is an abomination.** God would rather have the noise of hypocrisy silenced entirely than continue under the pretense of worship. The only acceptable "sacrifice" is a life poured out like water for justice.
