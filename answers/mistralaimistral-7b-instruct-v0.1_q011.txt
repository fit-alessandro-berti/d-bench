 The text moves from "cease to do evil" to "learn to do well" to emphasize that true goodness goes beyond mere restraint from doing harm. It suggests that goodness is not just about avoiding evil, but also about actively doing good. The commands to "seek judgment, relieve the oppressed, judge the fatherless, plead for the widow" indicate that goodness involves practicing love and justice towards others. Therefore, the text presents goodness as a proactive and compassionate attitude towards others, rather than just a negative avoidance of evil.
