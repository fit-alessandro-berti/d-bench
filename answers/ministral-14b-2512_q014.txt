This passage from **Matthew 6:19–21** is part of Jesus’ Sermon on the Mount, where He contrasts earthly and heavenly priorities. The text does **not explicitly say that treasure *trains* the heart**, but it does imply that **treasure *reveals* the heart**—and by extension, that **where our treasure is, our heart will follow** (i.e., our affections are shaped by what we value).

Here’s how **possession, attention, and love** are tied together in this passage:

### 1. **Treasure Reveals the Heart (Diagnostic Function)**
   - Jesus is not just warning against greed but **exposing what the heart already loves**. The phrase *"where your treasure is, there will your heart be also"* (v. 21) is a **description of reality**, not a command to change the heart directly.
   - Just as a person’s **possession** (what they own) reflects their priorities, their **attention** (what they focus on) and **love** (what they desire) follow suit. If someone hoards earthly wealth, their heart is already set on it—even if they claim otherwise.
   - This aligns with Jesus’ broader teaching in the Sermon on the Mount (e.g., *"No one can serve two masters"*—Matt. 6:24), where **external actions (like storing up treasure) flow from internal desires**.

### 2. **Treasure Trains the Heart (Formative Function)**
   - While the text doesn’t explicitly say *"treasure trains the heart,"* the **implication is that misplaced treasure *corrupts* the heart** over time.
   - Earthly treasures (money, possessions, status) are **perishable** (moth, rust, theft—v. 19), meaning they **cannot satisfy** and will **distract the heart** from eternal things.
   - Conversely, **heavenly treasure (righteousness, wisdom, generosity, faith)** is **eternal and secure** (v. 20), suggesting that **redirecting our treasure *shapes* our heart** toward God.
   - This is why Jesus later says, *"But seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness, and all these things will be added to you"* (Matt. 6:33). **What we pursue becomes what we love.**

### 3. **The Connection Between Possession, Attention, and Love**
   - **Possession (What we own)** → Reflects **attention (What we focus on)** → Determines **love (What we desire most)**.
     - If you **possess** much wealth, you likely **attend to** it (planning, protecting, worrying about it), which **trains your heart to love** it more than God.
     - If you **possess** heavenly-minded values (generosity, humility, prayer), your **attention** shifts there, and your **heart follows**.
   - This is why Jesus warns against **hoarding** (v. 19) but encourages **giving** (e.g., Matt. 6:1–4, 19–21). **Generosity is not just an action; it’s a heart-posture that reorients our treasure.**

### 4. **Theological Implications**
   - **Idolatry of Possessions**: Earthly treasure becomes an **idol** when it **replaces God as the object of ultimate trust and desire** (cf. Col. 3:5).
   - **Heavenly Treasure as Worship**: Storing up treasure in heaven (through obedience, generosity, and faith) is **an act of worship**, aligning the heart with God’s kingdom.
   - **The Heart’s Natural Movement**: The text assumes that **the heart will always pursue what it treasures**—so **changing what we treasure changes where our heart goes**.

### **Conclusion: Does It Train or Reveal?**
- **Primarily, it *reveals***—Jesus is **diagnosing** where the heart is already set.
- **Secondarily, it *trains***—because **what we treasure shapes our desires**, and **redirecting our treasure (e.g., through giving, prayer, faith) reorients the heart**.
- The passage is both a **warning** (earthly treasure corrupts) and an **invitation** (heavenly treasure transforms).

**Practical Application:**
- **Ask:** *"What do I spend most time, money, and energy on?"* (This reveals your heart.)
- **Repent & Redirect:** If earthly treasures dominate, **practice generosity, prayer, and kingdom-mindedness** to train your heart toward heaven.
