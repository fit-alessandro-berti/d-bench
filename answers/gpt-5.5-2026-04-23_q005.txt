Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it cuts against several deep human instincts at once.

First, it cuts against **self-protection**. When someone harms us, hatred can feel like armor. Resentment gives the injured person a sense of control: “I will not be fooled again.” To love an enemy can feel like lowering one’s guard.

Second, it cuts against our sense of **justice**. We often assume that loving someone means excusing them. So “love your enemies” sounds like moral weakness: letting the guilty escape judgment. But in Matthew 5, Jesus is not saying evil is unreal or that enemies are harmless. He names them plainly: those who curse, hate, exploit, and persecute. Enemy-love does not deny evil; it refuses to become evil in response.

Third, it cuts against **tribal identity**. It is easy to love “our own.” Jesus says even tax collectors and pagans do that. The difficult command is to desire the good of someone outside one’s circle, someone who may not reciprocate. That threatens the boundaries by which we often define ourselves.

So is enemy-love sentimental? Not in Jesus’ sense. Sentimentality avoids the cost of truth. Enemy-love, as Jesus teaches it, sees the enemy clearly and still refuses hatred. It is not a warm feeling toward the person who wronged us. It is a disciplined willing of their good: praying for them, refusing revenge, blessing rather than cursing, doing good where possible.

Is it strategic? It can have strategic effects. Non-retaliation can expose injustice, break cycles of vengeance, and sometimes transform the offender. Much of nonviolent resistance depends on this insight. But in Matthew 5, enemy-love is deeper than strategy. Jesus grounds it in the character of God: “He makes his sun rise on the evil and on the good.” God’s generosity is not limited to the deserving. To love enemies is to imitate the indiscriminate mercy of the Father.

That means enemy-love is best understood as a deeper form of **moral strength**.

It requires strength because it refuses the easiest responses: revenge, contempt, dehumanization, and bitterness. It does not mean allowing abuse to continue. It does not forbid boundaries, justice, or protection of the innocent. One can love an enemy and still restrain them, testify against them, or resist their wrongdoing. But one does so without wishing their destruction as a person.

In Christian terms, enemy-love is not weakness because it is not passivity. It is active goodness toward the undeserving. It is the power to remain free from the enemy’s moral logic. The enemy says, “I will make you hate.” Jesus says, “Do not let them determine what kind of person you become.”

So the command is difficult because it asks for more than politeness, tolerance, or emotional niceness. It asks for a transformation of the heart: to seek justice without hatred, to resist evil without becoming cruel, and to mirror the mercy of God toward those who least deserve it.
