The text does both, but its exhortational force especially emphasizes that **treasure trains and draws the heart**.

The Greek is quite direct:

> “Where your treasure is, there **your heart will be** also”  
> (*hopou gar estin ho thēsauros sou, ekei estai kai hē kardia sou*).

Notice the order: treasure *is* somewhere; the heart *will be* there. The future tense expresses an assured correspondence, though it need not describe a strict chronological sequence. Jesus is saying that the heart reliably follows what a person treats as treasure.

### Treasure reveals the heart

What we call “treasure” already discloses what we value, trust, and hope in. Spending, saving, and sacrificing reveal our practical priorities more clearly than stated ideals often do.

In that sense, treasure is diagnostic: **show me what you regard as your security and gain, and I can see the direction of your heart.**

### Treasure also trains the heart

But Jesus does not merely say, “Inspect your treasures to discover your heart.” He commands:

> “Lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven.”

That implies deliberate practices can redirect the heart. Giving, serving, seeking God’s kingdom, and investing in what endures are not only expressions of existing love; they also cultivate love. We become attached to what we repeatedly support, sacrifice for, protect, and hope in.

Thus the movement is something like:

1. **Possession or investment creates a stake.**
2. **A stake attracts attention and concern.**
3. **Repeated attention deepens attachment.**
4. **Attachment increasingly governs the heart.**

This is why money and possessions are spiritually formative. Whatever we treat as “our treasure” occupies our thoughts, evokes fear or hope, and eventually claims our loyalty.

The surrounding context reinforces this. Jesus moves from treasure (vv. 19–21), to the eye and perception (vv. 22–23), to serving God or mammon (v. 24). Treasure shapes what we attend to, and attention shapes whom we love and serve.

So the relationship is reciprocal: **the heart chooses its treasures, and those treasures then shape the heart.** Jesus’ warning is that earthly treasure binds the heart to what is fragile and perishing; heavenly treasure binds it to God and his enduring kingdom. This is not about purchasing salvation, but about directing one’s life toward what one truly trusts and loves.
