This passage from Matthew 12:34–35 powerfully connects speech with the hidden moral condition of the heart in several intertwined ways. Let’s break down how it relates speech to the “treasure” in the heart, and whether words are symptoms, choices, revelations, or all three.

### 1. Speech as a Symptom
The passage says, “out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh.” This suggests that what a person says is a product of what is already inside them—their innermost desires, attitudes, and moral dispositions. Speech here is like a symptom: it reveals what is stored up in the heart. Just as a doctor can diagnose an illness by symptoms, so too can we infer the state of a person’s heart by what they say.

### 2. Speech as a Choice
However, the text also implies an element of choice. A “good man out of the good treasure of the heart bringeth forth good things; and an evil man out of the evil treasure bringeth forth evil things.” Here, people are not merely passive; they draw from the kind of “treasure” they have chosen to accumulate. The heart is not just a passive storehouse but reflects the moral choices a person has made over time. In this sense, speech is not just a symptom but also a deliberate act—a choice to bring forth from one’s inner store.

### 3. Speech as a Revelation
Most fundamentally, the passage presents speech as a revelation or disclosure of the heart. The mouth “speaks what is in the heart.” Speech reveals, uncovers, or lays bare the moral condition that was previously hidden. It is a window into the inner person. Thus, words are not just the result of or the choice from one’s heart, but also a means by which the heart is revealed to others (and perhaps to oneself).

### Conclusion: All Three
Therefore, in Matthew 12:34–35, words are:
- **Symptoms** of what is already present in the heart,
- **Choices** made by drawing from the treasure we have accumulated,
- **Revelations** that disclose the state of our inner being to others.

In other words, speech is a complex interplay of inner disposition, moral agency, and disclosure. Our words both reveal and express what is inside us, and in speaking, we also make choices that can reinforce or even change our inner condition.
