Excellent question. The difficulty in taking enemy-love seriously stems from a conflict between **natural human instincts** and the **radical, counter-intuitive nature of Jesus’ command**. Let's unpack the three possible interpretations you suggest—sentimental, strategic, and moral strength—to understand why it’s challenging and what it truly represents.

---

### **1. Why It’s Difficult to Take Seriously**
- **Psychological Instinct:** Our default response to threat or hostility is self-protection—fight, flee, or retaliate. Enemy-love directly opposes this survival-based impulse.
- **Cultural Norms:** Most societies valorize justice as reciprocity (“an eye for an eye”) and celebrate defending one’s honor. Loving enemies seems to undermine justice and dignity.
- **Practical Risk:** It feels unsafe and naive—it opens us to further exploitation or harm.
- **Emotional Burden:** Sustaining love toward someone who wishes you ill requires immense emotional resilience, which can feel exhausting or even unjust.

---

### **2. Is It Sentimental?**
**No.** If “sentimental” means a soft, passive emotion divorced from reality, this command is not that.  
- It’s **active**: “*do good* to them,” “*pray* for them.”  
- It’s **unsentimental** because it doesn’t ignore the evil done; it chooses a response that transcends mere retaliation.  
- It’s grounded in the **nature of God** (Matthew 5:45), not in a naïve optimism about human goodness.

---

### **3. Is It Strategic?**
It can be viewed as **strategic in a transcendent sense**, but not in a worldly, calculating way.
- **Worldly strategy** might use kindness to manipulate or disarm an enemy. Jesus’ command isn’t tactical in that sense—it’s a principle to live by regardless of outcome.
- However, it *does* have transformative potential: breaking cycles of vengeance, creating moral surprise, and possibly converting hostility. But the motive isn’t primarily to win; it’s to **embody divine character**.

---

### **4. Is It a Deeper Form of Moral Strength?**
**Yes, this is the core interpretation.** Enemy-love is:
- **A Reflection of God’s Nature:** The rationale given (“that ye may be children of your Father”) ties it to God’s impartial grace—He gives sun and rain to all, good and evil. This love is an imitation of divine generosity, not human sentiment.
- **A Liberation from Hatred:** It frees the person from being defined by the enemy’s actions. The moral strength lies in **self-possession**—choosing one’s response based on identity (as God’s child) rather than reaction.
- **The Peak of Virtue:** It goes beyond fairness (love for friends) to **agape**—unconditional, willful goodwill. This requires deep courage, discipline, and a commitment to a higher order of ethics.

---

### **Conclusion**
Enemy-love is **difficult** because it demands overcoming instinct, culture, and fear. It is **not sentimental**—it’s an active, deliberate choice. It is **not primarily strategic** in a self-serving way, though it can transform relationships. Ultimately, it is **a deeper form of moral strength**—a reflection of divine character that requires profound inner fortitude and redefines what it means to be righteous.

In practice, taking it seriously means viewing it not as a fragile emotion but as a **disciplined practice of will**, rooted in the belief that how we respond to evil ultimately shapes our own humanity and aligns us with a God who loves even His enemies.
