The passage from Amos 5:21-24 presents one of the most stark and uncompromising rejections of worship in the Hebrew Bible. Understanding why requires looking at the text's historical context, its theological critique, and the specific "fraud" it exposes.

### Why the Sharp Rejection?
Amos was a prophet from Judah who prophesied to the northern kingdom of Israel around 760–750 BCE, during a period of great prosperity and religious activity. Yet this was also a time marked by:
1.  **Social Injustice:** Widespread oppression of the poor, the needy, and the righteous (see Amos 2:6-7, 4:1, 5:10-12, 8:4-6). The wealthy exploited the vulnerable for gain.
2.  **False Security:** The people equated their external religious rituals—festivals, sacrifices, and songs—with divine favor and protection. They trusted in the **form** of worship rather than its **substance**.

The sharp rejection is not merely about "balance" (e.g., "worship less, be moral more"). Instead, it makes a radical theological statement: **God rejects worship that is disconnected from justice and righteousness.** The rituals had become an end in themselves, a way to "cover" or compensate for a life of oppression, as if God could be bribed or fooled by ceremonial performances. The prophet is saying such worship is not just insufficient—it is an abomination, because it treats the sacred as a tool for unrighteousness.

### The Moral and Spiritual Fraud Being Exposed
The fraud is **hypocritical worship**: the practice of external religious rites while internally, and systemically, violating the very moral law those rites are meant to honor.

1.  **The Fraud of Substitution:** They were substituting **ritual for righteousness**. They offered sacrifices and sang psalms with their lips while their hands were full of violence and their economy was built on exploiting the poor. The worship was fraudulent because it was meant to replace, not express, a just life.
2.  **The Fraud of Self-Righteousness:** The rituals had become a source of pride and a perceived claim on God's favor. "We are God's people; we keep the feasts and offer the sacrifices." This created a smug confidence that masked their own sin and moral corruption. They used worship as a badge of identity rather than a call to transformation.
3.  **The Fraud of Externalism:** God, through the prophet, declares that the **external performance** (the smell of sacrifices, the sound of songs) is irrelevant if the **internal condition** and **external conduct** are corrupt. The worship is fraudulent because it addresses the superficial while ignoring the essential. It is a worship of the **ritual** but not of the **God** who demands justice (see Micah 6:6-8 for a parallel).
4.  **The Fraud of Ignoring Systemic Evil:** The text does not condemn personal immorality as much as it indicts a **societal system** that tolerates and perpetuates injustice. The fraud is collective: a community that engages in worship while structurally oppressing the vulnerable is engaged in a profound spiritual lie.

### The Positive Vision and Command
The ending of the passage provides the key to its understanding. The rejection of worship is paired with a clear, positive demand: **"But let justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like an ever-flowing stream."** (v. 24, NRSV).

This is the crux. The prophet is not saying worship is bad; he is saying that **righteousness and justice are the proper forms of worship and the true expressions of a relationship with God.** The pouring out of judgment (v. 24a) is against those whose worship is a fraudulent cover for unrighteousness.

### Conclusion
The passage rejects worship sharply because the worship described was **inherently fraudulent and insulting** to God. It was a self-serving performance that:
*   Denied the true requirements of God's covenant (justice, love, humility).
*   Masked a life of systemic oppression.
*   Reduced the holy to a ritualistic transaction.

The spiritual fraud is the **hypocrisy of claiming to honor God while actively dishonoring His image in the vulnerable.** The prophet's message is timeless: authentic faith cannot be separated from a commitment to justice and righteousness. True worship is lived, not merely performed.
