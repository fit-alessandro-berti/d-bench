### 1. **What kind of power is being praised here in Philippians 2:5–8?**

The passage describes **the power of self-sacrificial humility**—a power that is paradoxical yet profoundly spiritual.

- **The Power in Christ’s Life:**  
  The verse celebrates the “mind” (way of thinking, attitude) that was in Christ Jesus.  
  - *“Who, being in the form of God…”* — Christ had the divine authority, the fullness of God (Colossians 1:15).
  - *“…but made himself of no reputation, and took upon him the form of a servant…”* — Instead of claiming his divine rights, he emptied himself, becoming like a human and a servant.
  - *“And being found in the likeness of a man, he humbled himself…”* — He did not use his divine status to dominate but submitted fully to death on the cross.

So, the power being praised is **the power of humility coupled with divine authority**: the ability to set aside one’s natural privileges and exalted status for the sake of others, even unto death.

This is the **power of self-giving love and obedience**, which is the ultimate demonstration of God’s character and the Christian’s calling. It’s the power that comes from letting go of ego, pride, and the desire for control, to serve and redeem others.

---

### 2. **Why does humility joined to greatness feel unsettling?**

This combination feels unsettling because it upends our usual human expectations and biases:

#### a) **The Conflict of Expectations**
- **Greatness** usually implies power, dominance, and the right to be in charge.
- **Humility** suggests surrender, weakness, and deference to others.
- When someone (or something) who is truly great chooses humility, it challenges our sense of normalcy. We expect a king to rule, a god to demand worship, but here, the God-man chooses to be a servant.

#### b) **The Unsettling Paradox**
- **Power without arrogance:** The passage shows that real power is not about asserting dominance but about letting go of it. This is unsettling because we’re conditioned to expect leaders to be proud, to “prove their greatness” through force or status.
- **Obedience over dominance:** Christ’s willingness to die on the cross for sinners is a radical act of love that defies self-preservation. It’s unsettling because it’s costly in the most basic sense—losing life, status, even divine identity, for the sake of others.

#### c) **Philosophical and Spiritual Discomfort**
- In many cultures and philosophies, humility is seen as a virtue for the weak, while greatness is for the powerful. The combination in Christ makes both seem unnecessary—he is both.
- This challenges our own identities: Are we meant to seek power and recognition, or to seek humility and service?

#### d) **A Call to Repentance and Reorientation**
- The unsettling nature of this example is precisely its invitation. If Christ, who is without sin, can humble himself so completely, how should we who are broken and full of sin respond? It calls us to reassess what kind of power we pursue and what kind of humility we cultivate.

---

### **In Summary:**
**The power being praised is the radical, God-given power of humble obedience—using one’s full divine authority not for self-advantage but for self-sacrifice and service.**  
It feels unsettling because it reverses our usual expectations of greatness and humility, forcing us to confront what kind of “power” we value and how true greatness is measured not by dominance but by the willingness to die for others.

This unsettling quality is, in fact, its point: it shakes us out of complacency and points us toward a truer, deeper form of strength—one that is rooted not in control, but in the surrender of self for the sake of others.
