In Mark 8:34–35, the self that is being denied refers to the ego-driven or self-centered aspects of a person's identity. This includes a life governed by personal desires, ambitions, and societal expectations that prioritize individual gain and comfort over deeper spiritual commitments and values. The call to "deny oneself" emphasizes a shift from self-serving pursuits to embracing a life centered around following Jesus and aligning with the teachings of the gospel.

This idea can be seen as liberating to some because it encourages letting go of burdensome expectations and the constant striving for personal success or validation. For individuals feeling trapped by societal pressures or personal ambitions, the message of self-denial and commitment to a greater purpose can provide a sense of freedom and the promise of deeper fulfillment through a relationship with God and community.

Conversely, this concept can be offensive to others, as it challenges deeply ingrained notions of individualism and personal autonomy. The exhortation to take up one's cross signifies accepting hardship, sacrifice, and potentially suffering for one’s faith, which can be perceived as counterintuitive or even harsh in a culture that often values self-promotion and individual success. Thus, the call to lose one's life for the sake of Christ and the gospel is a radical departure from conventional understandings of success and happiness, leading to differing reactions based on one's perspective and values regarding self-identity and purpose.
