### Why “love your enemies” feels so hard to take seriously

When Jesus tells his followers to *love* those who curse, hate, and persecute them, he is not offering a warm‑fuzzy feeling. He is asking for a **re‑orientation of the will** that runs against the grain of ordinary human psychology. The difficulty can be traced to at least four interlocking factors:

| Factor | What it does | Why it makes the command feel unrealistic |
|--------|--------------|--------------------------------------------|
| **In‑group/out‑group bias** | We automatically categorize people as “us” or “them.” | Enemies are coded as a threat, not as a neighbor to be loved. |
| **Self‑preservation & revenge** | The brain’s alarm system (amygdala) pushes us toward fight‑or‑flight. | Loving a “dangerous” other feels like risking safety. |
| **Moral emotions (anger, contempt, indignation)** | These emotions reinforce boundaries and signal that a norm has been violated. | The presence of anger makes the idea of love seem “soft” or naïve. |
| **Cultural narratives of power** | Modern societies often celebrate strength through dominance or “winning.” | The command to turn the other cheek can look like surrender. |

All of these forces make the **practice** of enemy‑love cognitively and emotionally costly, which is why many people either dismiss it as sentimental or treat it as a strategic ploy. Yet the difficulty is precisely what makes the command a test of moral character.

---

## Three ways the command can be read

### 1. Sentimental love – “a warm feeling”

*What it looks like:*  
“Love” is reduced to a warm fuzzy emotion—something we *feel* toward a friend, a lover, or a pet. When that feeling is missing, the command appears impossible or even absurd.

*Why it fails:*  
If we wait for affection to arise naturally, we will be waiting forever for most enemies. The “sentimental” reading collapses the *action* of love into an *emotion* and then judges the command by that standard. It also invites the criticism that such love is naïve and can be co‑opted to excuse harm (“I love you, so I won’t hold you accountable”).

*Biblical note:*  
The passage does **not** ask us to *feel* affection for an enemy; it asks us to **bless, do good, and pray**. These are *acts of the will* that shape the feelings over time.

---

### 2. Strategic love – “a clever tactic”

*What it looks like:*  
Loving enemies is framed as a *means* to a desired end—peace, social stability, political advantage, or the softening of an opponent. In this view, the command is a “smart” policy: “If we love them, they’ll stop fighting us.”

*Why it falls short:*  
When love is purely instrumental, it remains contingent on its payoff. If the strategy stops working, the love evaporates. Moreover, strategic love can be manipulated: it may become a PR move that masks power imbalances, as when a dominant group “loves” a minority in order to absorb or silence it.

*Biblical note:*  
The reason given for loving enemies in Matthew 5:45 is *not* “so that they will stop persecuting you,” but rather **“that you may be children of your Father in heaven.”** The aim is *conformity to God’s character*, not a pragmatic bargain.

---

### 3. Deep moral strength – “a virtue that transforms”

*What it looks like:*  
Enemy‑love is seen as an **excellence of character**, a moral virtue that demands the full integration of reason, emotion, and will. It is the practice of *agape*—the selfless, impartial goodwill that mirrors God’s own love for the just and the unjust alike.

*Why it is the deepest reading:*  
- **It aligns with the *telos* (purpose) of moral development.** Aristotle called the person who can love even those who harm him *magnanimous* (great‑souled). Aquinas placed “charity” (agape) as the “form” of all virtues, the animating force that makes other moral actions truly good.  
- **It is a *transformative* practice.** By repeatedly acting toward an enemy in ways that are kind, forgiving, and just, we reshape our affective dispositions. The feeling of love can follow the act, not precede it.  
- **It mirrors divine impartiality.** The passage’s concluding clause—“He makes his sun rise on the evil and on the good, and sends rain on the just and on the unjust”—is a theological claim: to love like God is to extend goodwill *universally*, without regard to merit. This is not sentimentality; it is a *character* shaped by the reality of God’s own love.

---

## How the three perspectives interact

In practice, a mature Christian (or any moral agent striving toward enemy‑love) will **experience all three** elements:

1. **Sentiment** – a growing **empathy** that arises as we repeatedly act lovingly.  
2. **Strategy** – the **peace‑building** outcomes that naturally follow when enemies are treated with respect.  
3. **Moral strength** – the **virtue** that forms a new habitus, making it easier to act rightly even when emotions are hostile.

The key is that the **primary driver** is the third: the *virtue* of agape, which *uses* and *refines* the sentimental and strategic dimensions. If the primary aim is simply to *feel* good (sentiment) or to *win* (strategy), the practice becomes hollow. When the aim is to become more like God—impartial, generative, and gracious—the other two become natural by‑products.

---

## What makes this a “deep moral strength”?

- **Moral courage:** It requires defying the innate flight‑or‑flight response and choosing a path that feels risky.  
- **Self‑transcendence:** It moves the agent beyond self‑interest toward a *common good* that includes even those who oppose us.  
- **Integrative rationality:** It combines *reason* (recognizing our shared humanity) with *emotion* (cultivating compassion) and *will* (acting despite dislike).  
- **Transformative effect:** As we practice enemy‑love, we often see “enemies” become *neighbors*—the social fabric is rewoven, sometimes in ways that outlast the original conflict (e.g., the Civil Rights movement, post‑apartheid South Africa).  

Philosophically, this aligns with **virtue ethics**: moral strength is not a rule to be followed or a calculation of outcomes, but a **character trait** that is cultivated through repeated action. The *Sermon on the Mount* can be read as a curriculum for forming that character.

---

## Take‑away: The command is demanding, but it is not impossible

- **Difficulty is built in** because it confronts our deepest psychological biases.  
- **Sentiment alone** makes it feel naïve; **pure strategy** makes it a utilitarian tool.  
- **True enemy‑love** is a **deep moral strength**—a cultivated virtue that aligns us with God’s impartial goodness and reshapes our emotions, relationships, and societies.

When we understand love for enemies as a **practice of becoming like the Father who sends sun and rain to all**, the command moves from an abstract ideal to a concrete discipline. It is costly, it often feels counter‑intuitive, and it calls for the kind of moral stamina that only a life of prayer, community, and intentional moral formation can sustain.

In short, the difficulty is precisely what makes enemy‑love a test of moral depth rather than a sentimental fantasy or a clever tactic. It is a *strength* that grows through the daily decision to bless, do good, and pray for those who do us harm—mirroring the very love that the God of the universe extends to every soul, regardless of merit.
