1.  What the Greek sentence actually says  

   • "Where your treasure is (ἐστιν, present),  
     there your heart will be (ἔσται, future) also."

   The present-tense ἐστιν locates the treasure now; the future-tense ἔσται indicates that the heart will move to that location.  
   Grammatically, therefore, the treasure precedes and draws the heart; the heart is trained by the treasure.

2.  Does it only train, or also reveal?  

   a. The act of depositing treasure is itself the expression of an existing preference—so observers can already “read” the heart from the treasure (revelation).  

   b. Yet the future verb points beyond mere disclosure: every fresh deposit reinforces the orientation of the heart and pulls it further in that direction (formation / training).

   The verse therefore works both ways, but its sharper edge is formative: “Choose carefully what you value, because your heart is going to end up living there.”

3.  How possession, attention and love are tied together (Mt 6:19-24)

   Jesus builds a three-step progression:

   a. vv 19-21 – Treasure (possessions)  
      What you store up determines the arena in which your heart will live.

   b. vv 22-23 – Eye (attention/perception)  
      The “eye” is the lamp of the body; whatever the heart has learned to prize becomes what the eye keeps looking at, filling the whole person with either light or darkness.

   c. v 24 – Two masters (love/loyalty)  
      Continued attention ripens into service. One finally gives personal allegiance, not just loose interest, to either God or Mammon. “You cannot love (ἀγαπᾶν) both.”

   Thus:

   Possession → directs Attention → forges Love/Loyalty.

   What we accumulate seizes our gaze; what we keep gazing at wins our heart.

4.  Practical force of the saying

   • Earthly treasure is insecure (moth, rust, thieves).  
   • Heavenly treasure is secure (God keeps it).  

   Because the heart inexorably follows the treasure, the only way to safeguard the heart is to relocate the treasure. The command is not merely to feel differently about money but to act differently with it (“lay up … in heaven”) so that the heart can be trained toward God.

Conclusion  

Matthew 6:19-21 teaches that treasure both discloses and disciplines the heart, with the emphasis on its disciplining power. Where you decide to park your value today will drag your heart there tomorrow; possession shapes attention, and sustained attention matures into love. Jesus therefore orders a change of investment so that the disciple’s heart may finally lodge where it belongs—with God.
