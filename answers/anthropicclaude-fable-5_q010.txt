## The Power Being Praised

The passage describes what theologians call *kenosis*—self-emptying. But notice what kind of power this actually presupposes. The hymn insists that Christ possessed equality with God and "thought it not robbery"—that is, his divine status was not something seized or clung to anxiously, the way a usurper grips a stolen crown. The humility only means anything because the greatness is real and secure.

So the power praised here is:

- **Power that does not need to display itself.** It is so complete that it can afford concealment. Insecure power must constantly perform; this power can wear the form of a servant without losing anything essential.
- **Power exercised as descent rather than ascent.** Every worldly model of power moves upward—accumulation, conquest, status. This moves deliberately downward, step by step: God → servant → man → obedience → death → *the* death, crucifixion, the death reserved for slaves and rebels.
- **Power that is identical with self-gift.** The text suggests that emptying oneself is not the suspension of divine nature but its truest expression. Strength is revealed precisely in the capacity to renounce its privileges.

## Why This Unsettles More Than Domination

Open domination, strangely, is *legible*. We understand the tyrant. We know how to relate to him—fear, flattery, resistance, escape. Domination confirms our existing map of the world: power takes, the strong rule, the weak submit. It may crush us, but it doesn't confuse us.

Humility joined to greatness disturbs the map itself, for several reasons:

**1. It removes our excuses.** A tyrant's power makes no claim on my character—I can hate him with a clear conscience. But voluntary condescension from genuine height implies a standard. The passage opens with "Let this mind be in you." The example is also a summons. Domination asks for my submission; this asks for my *imitation*, which is far more invasive.

**2. It cannot be resisted by ordinary means.** You can fight force with force, or at least die opposing it with your dignity intact. But how do you defend yourself against someone who has already surrendered everything? Power that descends to the cross has placed itself beneath retaliation. Nietzsche saw this clearly and hated it for exactly this reason—he sensed that the apparent weakness was a more formidable kind of strength, one that conquers by refusing to compete on the expected terms.

**3. It exposes the fragility of our own self-assertion.** Most human striving is an attempt to climb away from servanthood, obscurity, and death. Here is someone who, possessing the summit, walked down voluntarily. That implies the summit was never what we thought it was—that our whole upward scramble may be a misreading of what greatness is. Open domination flatters our ambitions (we'd do the same if we could). Kenosis indicts them.

**4. It suggests we are loved from a height we cannot repay.** Domination creates debtors who resent; condescension of this kind creates debtors who can never settle the account. Gratitude on this scale is humbling in a way that fear never is. Fear preserves the self; this kind of grace reorganizes it.

In short: domination threatens what we *have*. Humility-from-greatness threatens what we *are*—our definitions of power, success, and selfhood. The tyrant's boot is terrible, but it leaves our worldview standing. The servant's basin and towel, held in hands that could command angels, quietly demolishes it.
