This is a profound and important distinction. The verse isn't calling for moral neutrality or the abandonment of judgment; it’s addressing the **posture** and **purpose** behind our judgments.

Let’s explore the difference between **discernment** and the **appetite to condemn.**

---

### **Discernment: The Ability to See Clearly**
Discernment is about **assessment, understanding, and evaluation.** It’s a necessary faculty for navigating the world.  
- It asks: *What is true? What is right? What is harmful or helpful?*  
- It’s often done with humility, openness to correction, and a desire for truth or restoration.  
- It considers context, intent, and consequence.  
- It’s oriented toward **love, justice, or wisdom**—not toward self-aggrandizement or punishment.

**Example:** A parent noticing a child’s behavior is harmful and guiding them toward kindness—that’s discernment. A doctor diagnosing an illness to treat it—that’s discernment.

---

### **The Appetite to Condemn: The Desire to Pass Final Sentence**
Condemnation, in the way the verse warns against it, is about **finality, superiority, and punishment.**  
- It asks: *How can I label, blame, and dismiss this person?*  
- It’s often rooted in pride, fear, or a desire to control.  
- It closes off dialogue, mercy, and redemption.  
- It focuses on the **fault** while ignoring the **full person**—their complexity, capacity for change, or shared humanity.

**Example:** Public shaming without seeking context. Reducing someone to their worst act. Judging in a way that says, “You are irredeemable.”

---

### **The Key Difference in the Verse**
The verse isn’t saying, “Never evaluate right from wrong.”  
It’s saying: **Do not adopt the stance of a final judge who condemns without mercy.**

This is rooted in a theological idea: *Only God is the ultimate judge.* When humans take on the role of condemner, we usurp that position and often corrupt it with our limited perspective, pride, or bias.

In Christian ethics, this ties to the idea of **restorative justice** over **retributive justice.**  
- Restorative justice seeks healing, reconciliation, and correction—not just punishment.  
- It asks: *How can what is broken be made whole?*  
- It requires discernment to see what is broken—but rejects the appetite to simply condemn and discard.

---

### **A Metaphor: The Judge vs. The Diagnostician**
A **judge** pronounces a final sentence: “You are guilty. Here is your punishment.”  
A **diagnostician** assesses: “Here is what is happening. Here is what needs to heal.”

The verse is inviting us to move from the first stance to the second—in how we relate to others, and even in how we relate to ourselves.

---

### **Why This Matters**
If moral judgments never mattered, then justice, morality, and even love would lose their meaning. The verse doesn’t erase moral reasoning—it **humbles it.** It says:  
*Discern wisely, but do not condemn mercilessly.*  
*Evaluate, but leave room for mercy.*  
*See clearly, but remember that you, too, will need forgiveness.*

In short:  
**Discernment is seeing with clarity and compassion.  
Condemnation is seeing with a gavel and no heart.**

The verse calls us to one and warns against the other—not because judgment doesn’t matter, but because the *way* we judge matters profoundly.
