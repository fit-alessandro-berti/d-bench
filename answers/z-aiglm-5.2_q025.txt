Based on Matthew 6:1, the moral danger of public goodness lies not in the act being seen, but in the corruption of the doer's motives. Here is a breakdown of the specific dynamics at play in the verse:

**The Morally Dangerous Element**
Public goodness becomes morally dangerous because it introduces a competing reward system. When a good deed is done in public, the doer is immediately eligible for a earthly reward: the praise, admiration, and social elevation of other people. The danger is that this temporal reward can replace the eternal reward of "your Father which is in heaven." 

When the primary goal becomes securing human approval, the act of charity (alms) ceases to be about the recipient or about obedience to God; it becomes a transactional performance for social capital. The goodness is stripped of its moral weight because it is no longer an act of love or devotion, but an act of self-promotion.

**The Problem: Visibility vs. Craving**
The problem is explicitly the **craving to be seen**, not the visibility itself. 

The phrase Jesus uses is *"to be seen of them."* In the original Greek, the word used for "to be seen" (*theathēnai*) is related to the word from which we get "theater." Jesus is warning against turning life into a stage play where the practitioner is the actor seeking applause. The moral failure is located in the *intent* (the craving for human validation), not the *circumstance* (the deed happening to be witnessed).

This distinction is vital when looking at the broader context of Jesus' teachings. In Matthew 5:16, just one chapter earlier, Jesus commands His followers: *"Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."* 

In Matthew 5:16, visibility is good because the motive is to point others to God. In Matthew 6:1, visibility is dangerous because the motive is to point others to oneself. 

Therefore, the verse teaches that doing good *in* public is not inherently wrong, but doing good *for* the public's approval is spiritually fatal, as it causes the practitioner to forfeit God's reward in exchange for the fleeting approval of men.
