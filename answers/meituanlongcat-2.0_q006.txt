In Mark 8:34–35, Jesus is not asking for a superficial act of self-improvement. The “self” he calls us to deny is the **autonomous, self-curfed ego**—the part of us that seeks to be the centre of its own universe, that clings to its own safety, glory, and control. In the biblical tradition, this is the “old self” or the “false self,” the identity built on what we achieve, possess, and perform apart from God. To deny this self is to disown its claim to ultimacy; to “take up my cross” is to embrace the way of sacrificial love, even when it leads to suffering and loss. The life one “loses” is the life spent frantically trying to secure meaning on one’s own terms.

This sounds **liberating** to many because it frees them from the exhausting tyranny of self-management. The ego constantly demands to be fed, defended, and compared to others. By releasing the grip on that false self, a person discovers a deeper identity rooted in God’s love, not in their own achievements or image. It is the relief of no longer having to be their own god—the end of a desperate chase for significance that can never be satisfied. Paradoxically, letting go of that self is the doorway to truly living: “whoever loses their life for me and for the gospel will save it.” The burden of a fragile ego is exchanged for the peace of belonging wholly to Christ.

It sounds **offensive** to others because it strikes at the heart of modern Western culture’s highest value: the sovereign self. We are taught from childhood to “look out for number one,” to follow our hearts, and to define our own truth. A command to die to oneself feels like cultural betrayal and personal annihilation. The cross symbolises shame, failure, and the end of autonomy—the very things our self-help culture promises to avoid. Moreover, Jesus’ words are not generic spiritual wisdom; they demand exclusive allegiance to him, which clashes with a pluralistic mindset that sees all paths as equally valid. To be told that your instinct to “save your life” is actually the way to lose it offends human pride and the deep-seated belief that we are the authors of our own meaning. What one person hears as freedom from a prison, another hears as an attack on their very identity.
