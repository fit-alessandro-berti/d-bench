The elder brother’s deepest problem in Luke 15:28–32 is **pride in obedience**, which manifests as a **distorted sense of merit and a misunderstanding of relationship, love, and grace**—and in this context, this pride directly fuels his anger, envy, and discomfort with mercy.

Let’s unpack this carefully:

### 1. **He sees obedience as a transaction, not a relationship**
The elder brother says, *“These many years I have served you”* (v. 29). This is not just a statement of duty—it’s a performance of moral superiority. He frames his life as a long-term contract: *“I obeyed, therefore I deserve.”* He believes that his obedience has earned him rights, rewards, and recognition—like a **kid** for his friends to celebrate. His service is not rooted in love or intimacy with his father, but in merit-based expectation.

This reveals **pride in obedience**—not humility, not gratitude, but a sense of self-righteous achievement.

### 2. **He is envious and resentful of the younger brother’s restoration**
He doesn’t just object to the celebration; he *defines* the younger brother in the worst possible terms: “*which hath devoured thy living with harlots*” (v. 30). He reduces his brother’s past to a moral failure, ignoring grace, repentance, and redemption. He can’t rejoice in the younger brother’s return because he sees only the sin, not the transformation.

This envy stems from **comparative morality**: *“I’ve never strayed; I’ve served; he wasted everything, and yet he gets the fatted calf.”* This shows he doesn’t see forgiveness as a reason for joy—but as a threat to his own status.

### 3. **He struggles with mercy itself, not just the outcome**
The father’s answer—*“This thy brother was dead, and is alive again; and was lost, and is found”*—is a declaration of *reversal*, of *restoration*, of *mercy*. But the elder brother can't grasp this. He cannot enter into the joy of mercy because he sees mercy as *injustice*—a reward given not to those who earned it, but to those who failed.

This reveals a **deeper spiritual issue**: **he cannot rejoice in grace because he believes it invalidates his own worth**. He fears that if mercy is freely given to sinners (even repentant ones), then his own efforts mean nothing.

### 4. **Why “pride in obedience” is the root, not just anger or envy**
- **Anger** and **envy** are symptoms—products of deeper pride.
- **Wounded fairness** suggests he feels treated unjustly, but the real issue is that he sees fairness as earned reward, not as relationship.
- The father doesn't rebuke him for *not* being angry; he calls him *“son,”* and reminds him: *“Thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine”* (v. 31). The father offers him full inheritance and belonging—yet the elder brother *refuses* the celebration, refusing to enter into fellowship.

This suggests he values **status and recognition** more than relationship, which is the essence of pride in obedience.

---

### Conclusion:
> **The elder brother’s deepest problem is pride in obedience**—a belief that righteousness is a personal achievement that earns reward, status, and entitlement. This pride prevents him from rejoicing in mercy, from embracing grace, and from seeing his father as a loving father rather than a demanding master.  
>  
> He does not just resent the younger brother; he *cannot* experience the joy of the father’s heart because he does not love the father like a son—but like a servant seeking wages.

This parable, therefore, is not just about the prodigal son’s repentance, but also about **the danger of self-righteous religiosity**—the danger of serving God out of duty, not love, and of misunderstanding grace as a threat to moral integrity.
