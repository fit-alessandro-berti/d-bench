The parable in Luke 18:9–14 is a masterful critique of self-righteousness and a celebration of humility before God. The condemnation of the Pharisee is not primarily about **false doctrine** (his beliefs about God, the Law, or morality were largely correct) but about his **attitude, motives, and relational posture** toward God and others. Here’s a breakdown of why he is condemned and why the tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer:

### **What Condemns the Pharisee?**
1. **Pride and Self-Righteousness**
   - He boasts in his own righteousness ("I thank thee that I am not like other men") rather than acknowledging his need for God’s grace.
   - His prayer is a **monologue of self-congratulation**, not a dialogue with God. He speaks "with himself" (v. 11), implying his prayer is more about self-affirmation than communion with God.

2. **Contempt for Others**
   - He **despises others** (v. 9), particularly the tax collector, whom he explicitly compares himself against ("or even as this publican").
   - His righteousness is **performative and comparative**—it exists only in contrast to the sins of others, not in relation to God’s holiness.

3. **Virtue as Self-Display**
   - His fasting and tithing are **not acts of devotion but of self-promotion**. He treats them as **merit badges** rather than gifts of grace.
   - His virtues are **transactional**—he expects God to owe him something for his obedience, rather than approaching God as a dependent sinner.

4. **Lack of Humility**
   - The tax collector’s posture (standing afar off, not lifting his eyes, beating his breast) reflects **true humility and repentance**.
   - The Pharisee, by contrast, stands **boldly in the temple**, assuming his right to be there based on his own merits.

### **Why the Tax Collector’s Posture is Spiritually Truer**
1. **Acknowledgment of Sin**
   - The tax collector does not compare himself to others; he **confesses his own unworthiness** ("God, be merciful to me, a sinner").
   - His prayer is **raw and honest**—he doesn’t hide his sin but brings it before God.

2. **Dependence on God’s Mercy**
   - He does not rely on his own righteousness but **pleads for God’s grace** ("be merciful").
   - His posture (afar off, eyes down, beating his breast) symbolizes **brokenness and contrition**, not self-justification.

3. **True Worship vs. False Performance**
   - The Pharisee’s worship is **performative**—he does the right things but with the wrong heart.
   - The tax collector’s worship is **transformative**—his heart is aligned with God’s holiness, even if his actions are minimal.

### **Key Theological Point**
Jesus is not condemning **righteousness itself** (the Pharisee was technically obedient to the Law) but **the Pharisee’s **self-justifying, comparative, and prideful approach to it**. The tax collector, though a "sinner" by societal standards, is **justified** because he humbly relies on God’s mercy rather than his own performance.

This parable echoes Jesus’ teaching in **Matthew 23:12**: *"Whoever exalts himself will be humbled, and whoever humbles himself will be exalted."* The Pharisee is humbled because he trusted in himself; the tax collector is exalted because he trusted in God’s grace.
