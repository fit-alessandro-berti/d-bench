This passage uses a powerful and universal metaphor: light as truth and exposure, and darkness as concealment. The hatred of light isn’t about the light itself being harmful, but about what the light *does*—it reveals.

Here’s a breakdown of the relationship between truth, exposure, shame, and the desire to hide:

**1. The Fear of Exposure:**
Light makes things visible. In a moral or spiritual sense, it brings hidden thoughts, motives, and actions into the open. For someone whose life is built on secrecy, deception, or self-deception, this exposure is terrifying. It threatens the false narrative they have constructed for themselves or for others. The desire not to be seen clearly is a desire to maintain control over one's own image and to avoid the consequences that truth might bring.

**2. Shame as a Protective Reaction:**
Shame is the deep, painful feeling that we are fundamentally flawed or unworthy. When our "evil deeds" (actions that violate our own conscience or a moral standard) are brought to light, shame is often the immediate emotional response. It’s a painful experience. To avoid this pain, a person may instinctively avoid the light that triggers it. The darkness becomes a safe haven where the shame doesn't have to be felt or confronted.

**3. The Defense of the False Self:**
Many people construct an identity—a "false self"—that is respectable, moral, and in control. This self-image often requires hiding our struggles, failures, and darker impulses. The "light" of truth threatens to dismantle this carefully built persona. Staying in the "darkness" allows one to perpetuate the illusion, at least for a while, and avoid the painful process of authentic change.

**4. The Freedom in "Doing Truth":**
The passage contrasts those who hate the light with those who "doeth truth." This isn't just about being honest; it's about *living* in a way that aligns with reality and integrity. Such a person doesn't need to fear the light because their life is already oriented toward it. Their deeds are "wrought in God"—done with a transparent conscience and a desire for alignment with what is good and true. For them, light isn't a threat but a validation and a guide.

**Why Light is Hated, in Short:**
The light is hated not for what it *is*, but for what it *does*. It **exposes** what we want hidden, **triggers shame** we want to avoid, and **threatens the false self** we want to preserve. The preference for darkness is, in essence, a preference for the comfort of illusion over the potentially painful, but ultimately liberating, reality of truth.

This dynamic plays out everywhere—from personal relationships where we hide our faults, to societal structures where injustices are obscured. The passage suggests that true moral and spiritual health requires a courageous move *toward* the light, trusting that being known fully is the first step toward genuine healing and wholeness.
