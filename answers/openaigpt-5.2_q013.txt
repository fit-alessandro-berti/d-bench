Amos isn’t saying, “You worship too much—add a little more ethics.” He’s saying the *worship itself has become a lie*, because it is being used to sanctify a life and a society that directly contradict God’s covenant.

### 1) The worship is not “incomplete”; it is *covenant-treason dressed up as devotion*
In Amos 5 the problem isn’t a minor imbalance between ritual and morality. The surrounding verses describe a culture of systemic injustice:

- courts and public decision-making are corrupt (“you who turn justice to wormwood,” 5:7)
- the powerful “hate him who reproves in the gate” and “abhor him who speaks the truth” (5:10)
- the poor are trampled, taxed, and dispossessed (5:11–12)

So when such a society gathers for “feast days,” “solemn assemblies,” sacrifices, and music, those acts are no longer neutral religious activities. They become **public claims** that “we are right with God,” while their daily practices deny Him.

That is why the response is not, “Do justice *also*,” but, “I hate… I despise… I will not accept… take away the noise.” It’s the language you use when something holy has been turned into an instrument of deception.

### 2) The fraud exposed: using worship as a moral cover and a bribe
The passage unmasks a particular kind of spiritual fraud: **treating worship as a transaction that compensates for disobedience**, or as a religious performance that grants moral legitimacy.

They are effectively saying:

- “We’re bringing offerings, so God must be pleased.”
- “We’re keeping festivals, so our society must be blessed.”
- “We’re singing, so we must be faithful.”

Amos says God won’t even “smell” (i.e., accept) the offerings—language that in the sacrificial system normally signifies divine pleasure. Here it signals the opposite: **ritual is being offered as if God can be bought or managed while His justice is being rejected.**

### 3) Why the sharp rejection? Because worship without justice becomes *anti-worship*
In the Bible, worship is not merely “religious expression”; it is covenantal allegiance. If the worshiper’s life contradicts God’s character—especially by harming the vulnerable—then the ritual doesn’t merely “fall short.” It **reverses its meaning**:

- Songs become “noise” (5:23), not because music is bad, but because it is *praise weaponized to silence truth*.
- Sacrifices become offensive, not because sacrifices are wrong, but because they are **covering ongoing exploitation**.

This is why similar prophetic critiques (Isaiah 1; Micah 6) don’t ask for “better balance” either: the issue is not quantity but *integrity*. When worship is used to mask injustice, it functions like perjury in God’s name.

### 4) “Let justice roll down…” means: let public life match your liturgy
“Judgment” (justice) and “righteousness” are not private feelings here; they refer to the **actual ordering of society**, especially the courts (“the gate”) and economic life. Amos’s point is that genuine worship would produce a community where justice is as steady and unstoppable as water in a floodplain—not a rare, token gesture.

**In short:** Amos rejects the worship so sharply because it is not merely imbalanced; it is *hypocritical worship functioning as moral laundering*—religious ceremony used to claim God’s favor while violating the very things God’s worship is meant to honor.
