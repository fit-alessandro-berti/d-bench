James is exposing a **split inside the worshiper**:

- **With the same tongue we “bless” God as Father** (public piety, reverence, orthodoxy),
- **and we “curse” people who bear God’s likeness** (contempt, hatred, dehumanizing speech).

That is an inner contradiction because **honoring God while dishonoring His image is logically and morally inconsistent**. To curse an image-bearer is, in effect, to assault what God has stamped as reflecting Himself. It treats God as worthy of praise in the abstract while treating what He values and resembles in the concrete as worthy of disdain. James’ “ought not so to be” is not mere etiquette; it’s a claim that such speech betrays a **divided heart**—a life trying to run two moral orientations at once: worship and contempt.

### Why speech reveals moral disorder so quickly
1. **Speech is the quickest “leak” of the heart.**  
   Words often come out before deliberation; they expose what is already present—anger, pride, envy, bitterness, malice. This matches the broader biblical diagnosis: “out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks” (cf. Matt 12:34; Luke 6:45).

2. **The tongue is hard to control and constantly in use.**  
   James has just emphasized that the tongue is small but powerful, like a spark or rudder (James 3:3–6). Because we speak so frequently and reflexively, the tongue becomes an unusually sensitive indicator of spiritual health.

3. **Words don’t just express character; they enact it.**  
   Cursing is not only reporting what one feels—it is *doing* something: diminishing, condemning, shaming. So moral disorder becomes visible immediately in the social and relational damage speech causes.

In short: James points to the contradiction of **reverencing God while reviling God’s likeness**, and he highlights speech because it is the most immediate, unguarded, and morally active expression of what the heart is actually devoted to.
