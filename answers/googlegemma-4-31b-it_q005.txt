The difficulty in taking "enemy-love" seriously stems from the fact that it contradicts the most fundamental biological and psychological instincts of the human species. To love an enemy is to act against the grain of evolutionary survival.

Here is an analysis of why this command is so challenging and what nature of strength it actually requires.

### Why it is difficult to take seriously

**1. The Conflict with Justice**
Most people equate "love" with "approval" or "affinity." From a human perspective, justice is defined as *reciprocity*: kindness for kindness, and punishment for harm. When we are told to love those who persecute us, our internal sense of justice screams that this is unfair. We feel that by loving an enemy, we are condoning their evil or betraying the victim within ourselves.

**2. The Fear of Vulnerability**
Love, by definition, requires an opening of the self. To love an enemy is to lower your shields in the presence of someone who has already proven they are willing to strike. To the rational mind, this looks like tactical suicide.

**3. The Confusion of Terms**
We often mistake *agape* (the unconditional will for the good of another) for *philia* (friendship/affection). If we think Jesus is commanding us to *feel* warm, fuzzy emotions toward a murderer or a tyrant, the command seems delusional or impossible.

---

### Is it Sentimental, Strategic, or Moral Strength?

#### It is NOT Sentimental
Sentimentality is a feeling; it is passive and often shallow. If enemy-love were sentimental, it would be a mere suggestion to "be nice." However, the text describes specific *actions*: blessing, doing good, and praying. These are volitional acts of the will, not fluctuations of the heart. Sentimental love vanishes when the enemy strikes back; the love described here is designed to persist *because* the enemy strikes back.

#### It is NOT (Primarily) Strategic
While there is a strategic element—loving an enemy can sometimes disarm them or win them over (the "heaps of burning coals" mentioned in Romans 12:20)—reducing this command to a strategy makes it a manipulation. If you love an enemy only to change their behavior, you are still controlling them. The passage emphasizes that the motivation is not the enemy's reaction, but the imitation of the Father: *"That ye may be the children of your Father."* The goal is the transformation of the *lover*, not necessarily the *beloved*.

#### It IS a Deeper Form of Moral Strength
Enemy-love is the ultimate expression of moral agency because it is the only form of love that is **entirely independent of the object.**

*   **Love for friends** is a reaction to the goodness of the other.
*   **Love for enemies** is an assertion of the goodness of the lover.

This is a "superior" strength because it breaks the cycle of causality. In a world of "eye for an eye," the victim is a slave to the aggressor; the victim's behavior is merely a reaction to the aggressor's cruelty. By loving the enemy, the individual ceases to be a mirror and becomes a source. 

### Conclusion: The "Imago Dei"
The passage concludes by pointing to the sun and the rain. Nature does not "audit" the worthiness of the soil before it rains. By practicing enemy-love, a human transcends their animal instinct for tribalism and revenge and begins to operate on a divine frequency. It is a form of strength that refuses to let the evil of another person dictate the moral character of the self.
