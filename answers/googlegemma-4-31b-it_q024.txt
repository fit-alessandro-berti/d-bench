Based on the text of Ezekiel 36:26–27 and the broader biblical context, here is an analysis of the difference between external compliance and an internal transformation, as well as the significance of the "stony heart" metaphor.

### 1. External Compliance vs. a New Heart

The primary difference between these two states is the **source of motivation** and the **location of the power** to obey.

*   **External Compliance (The Law from Without):** External compliance occurs when an individual follows rules because of fear, social pressure, or a desire for reward. In the context of the Old Covenant, the Law was written on stone tablets (Exodus 31:18). While the Law was holy and just, it functioned as an external standard. It could tell a person *what* to do, but it could not provide the *desire* or the *ability* to do it. Compliance without a "new heart" is often characterized by legalism, hypocrisy, or a struggle against one's own nature.
*   **The New Heart (The Law from Within):** A "new heart" represents an internal revolution. When God says, *"I will put my spirit within you,"* He is describing a shift from external coercion to internal inclination. The "new heart" does not merely follow the rules to avoid punishment; it follows the rules because its nature has been changed. Obedience becomes a byproduct of a new identity. The desire to "walk in my statutes" is no longer a burden imposed from the outside, but a natural expression of the Spirit working from the inside.

### 2. Why Hardness is Described as a Material Condition

The use of "stony heart" (material) versus "heart of flesh" (organic) is a powerful metaphor for the spiritual state of the human will.

**The Nature of Stone (The Stony Heart):**
Stone is used here to describe three specific conditions:
*   **Impermeability:** A stone cannot absorb water. Similarly, a "stony heart" is unresponsive to the "living water" of God's word or the conviction of the Spirit. It is closed off and stubborn.
*   **Inflexibility:** Stone does not bend; it either stays rigid or it breaks. A hard heart is one that refuses to repent, apologize, or adapt. It is fixed in its own pride and self-will.
*   **Lifelessness:** Stone is inorganic. It has no pulse, no breath, and no capacity for growth. A heart of stone is spiritually dead—incapable of genuine love or spiritual vitality.

**The Nature of Flesh (The Heart of Flesh):**
In this context, "flesh" does not refer to sinful human nature, but to **living, breathing, responsive tissue.**
*   **Sensitivity:** A heart of flesh can feel. It is capable of contrition, empathy, and conviction.
*   **Malleability:** Unlike stone, living tissue can be shaped and molded. A heart of flesh is teachable and responsive to the guidance of the Holy Spirit.
*   **Vitality:** Flesh is alive. It represents a heart that is beating in synchronization with the will of God.

### Summary
The passage argues that human effort is insufficient to achieve true holiness. You cannot "polish" a stone to make it a living heart; you must **replace** it. The transition from "stone" to "flesh" signifies that salvation is not a matter of trying harder to comply with external rules, but of receiving a fundamental change in nature—a divine transplant that enables a person to love what God loves and desire what God desires.
