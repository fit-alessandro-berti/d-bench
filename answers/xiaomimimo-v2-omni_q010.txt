This passage portrays a **power of self-emptying love** (kenotic power) — the power to relinquish privilege, status, and force in order to identify fully with human vulnerability. It is the power of divine authority expressed through servant-hood, obedience, and sacrificial surrender.  

**Why humility with greatness is more unsettling than domination:**  
Open domination confirms our ordinary expectations — power asserts itself, demands submission, and maintains distance. It fits a predictable script of authority.  
But when ultimate greatness chooses humility, it **subverts the very framework** we use to understand power. This creates several unsettling effects:

1. **It exposes our own motives** — Domination allows us to either submit or resist, but humility invites us into a relationship that requires voluntary self-giving. We must examine whether we seek power over others or the power to love.

2. **It disarms our defenses** — Resistance against domination can be principled or proud; but resistance to humble love feels morally and spiritually hollow, even when we want to assert our own will.

3. **It redefines greatness** — The humility of Christ reveals that true power is not in controlling others but in willingly entering their condition to transform it from within. This reverses human instincts for prestige and control, creating cognitive and moral dissonance.

4. **It demands a response without coercion** — Domination forces a choice between obedience or rebellion. Humility presents a love so profound that the only fitting response is transformation from within — which is far more demanding on the human heart.

In short, domination can be opposed from a place of pride or principle, but the humility of Christ meets us in a way that challenges the very foundations of our understanding of power, self-interest, and love — leaving us without a comfortable position from which to judge or resist.
