## The Two Dimensions of Repentance

The pairing in verse 7 is deliberate and reveals something about the nature of sin and turning from it. Notice the parallel structure:

- "Let the **wicked** forsake his **way**"
- "the **unrighteous man** his **thoughts**"

This is Hebrew parallelism, but not mere repetition. The two lines address different layers of the person.

### "Way" — Outward Conduct

The Hebrew *derek* (way, path, road) refers to the observable pattern of life: actions, habits, the direction one's feet actually walk. To forsake one's "way" is to abandon a course of behavior—what others can see and what leaves tracks in the world.

### "Thoughts" — Inward Imagination

The Hebrew *machashebah* points to plans, intentions, reckonings, the interior reasoning that *precedes* and *justifies* the way. This is the workshop where conduct is first manufactured.

## What the Pairing Implies

**1. Sin is rooted before it is acted.** By naming thoughts alongside ways, the text refuses to let repentance be merely behavioral reform. A man can change his "way" while keeping his "thoughts"—altering conduct out of fear or convenience while the inner allegiance remains unchanged. Scripture treats that as incomplete. The imagination must turn, not only the feet.

**2. The order matters.** The verse moves outward-to-inward (way, then thoughts), which mirrors how repentance is often experienced: we first stop the visible sin, then discover the deeper work of dismantling the thoughts that fed it. Mere behavior management without renewed thinking tends to relapse.

**3. Genuine return is total.** The next clause—"let him return unto the Lord"—suggests that forsaking way and thoughts together *is* the turning toward God. You cannot keep a secret inner attachment and call it return.

## The Possibility of Mercy

Here is the striking weight of the passage. After demanding this thorough turning, the response is not proportionate judgment but lavish grace:

> "he will have mercy... he will **abundantly** pardon."

The Hebrew suggests God "multiplies to pardon." The contrast is intentional and runs through the larger context (vv. 8–9): God's thoughts are not our thoughts. A human creditor, having been wronged in both deed *and* intent, might forgive the deed grudgingly. God pardons both abundantly.

So the very thoroughness of the demand magnifies the mercy. Repentance reaches all the way down—but mercy reaches further still.

## Summary

| Element | Refers to | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| "Way" | Outward conduct | Visible behavior must change |
| "Thoughts" | Inward imagination | The hidden source must change too |
| "Return" | The whole person | Repentance is total, not partial |
| "Abundantly pardon" | God's response | Mercy exceeds the depth of sin |

The passage assumes sin is deep—but insists mercy is deeper, and available *now*, "while he may be found."
