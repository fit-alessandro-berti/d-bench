The passage refuses balance because what it's confronting isn't a deficiency to be corrected but a counterfeit to be exposed. You can't balance something fake with something real—you can only reject the fake.

**The nature of the fraud**

The fraud here is using worship as a substitute for justice rather than as its source. The people of Israel were performing their religious obligations meticulously—feasts, offerings, music—and presumably feeling righteous about it. But this religious activity was running *alongside* exploitation, not transforming it. The poor were being crushed (Amos says so elsewhere: selling the needy for a pair of sandals, trampling the head of the poor into the dust), and the worship continued unbothered.

This is what makes it fraud rather than mere imbalance. The worship wasn't a partial good that needed supplementing with justice. It had become a *cover*—a way of maintaining a relationship with God while violating the very people God cares about. The offerings functioned as a kind of payment, as if devotion could be transacted independently of how one treats one's neighbor.

**Why rejection rather than refinement**

A request for "better balance" would concede that the worship had value as far as it went. But God's hatred ("I hate, I despise") signals something stronger: this worship is not neutral or insufficient—it is offensive, because it is being used to deceive. It tells a lie about who the worshipper is. It dresses injustice in liturgical clothing.

There's a logic to the violence of the language. When a thing is being used to falsify reality—to make oppressors feel reconciled to God while they oppress—then half-measures only confirm the deception. The only way to break the lie is to strip the worship away entirely and reveal what's underneath.

**The deeper point about what worship is for**

The closing demand—"let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream"—isn't an addition to worship. It's a redefinition of what acceptable worship *is*. The flowing water image is telling: justice isn't supposed to be a ceremony performed on appointed days but something continuous, abundant, and self-sustaining, like a river that doesn't stop. Ritual is episodic; the prophet wants something that runs all the time.

So the fraud exposed is the assumption that God can be satisfied by religious performance disconnected from moral conduct—that you can give God the songs and keep the injustice. The sharpness of the rejection insists that these were never two separate accounts. To honor God *is* to do justice. Worship that doesn't issue in righteousness isn't incomplete worship; it's the opposite of worship, however correct its form.
