The saying does both, but its main rhetorical force is that **what you treat as treasure will draw your heart after it**.

> “Where your treasure is, there your heart **will be** also” (Matt. 6:21).

The future wording—“will be”—is important. Jesus does not merely say, “Your treasure shows where your heart already is.” He says that the location of one’s treasure is where one’s heart comes to reside. In that sense, treasure can **train** or direct the heart.

At the same time, the treasure one chooses also **reveals** the heart: what one protects, accumulates, worries about, studies, and sacrifices for discloses one’s actual loves and loyalties. So the relation runs both ways:

- **The heart chooses treasure**: desires and convictions shape what we value.
- **Treasure shapes the heart**: what we invest in gains our attention, concern, imagination, and affection.
- **Therefore treasure also exposes the heart**: our investments reveal what has become central to us.

## “Treasure” is more than possessions

The immediate image includes material wealth—things vulnerable to moth, corrosion, and theft. But “treasure” means whatever is stored up as one’s security, gain, status, or lasting good. It may include money and property, but also reputation, achievement, comfort, power, or even religious recognition.

Jesus contrasts two places of investment:

- **Earthly treasure** is insecure and perishable: it can decay, be taken, or fail to deliver what it promised.
- **Heavenly treasure** is secure because it belongs to God’s enduring kingdom.

“Heaven” here is not simply a private afterlife bank account. In the surrounding Sermon on the Mount, it concerns living before the Father—giving, praying, forgiving, trusting God, seeking his kingdom, and valuing his approval rather than public reward (Matt. 6:1–18, 25–33).

## Possession, attention, and love

Jesus sees a practical moral psychology: **we give our hearts to what we count as valuable**.

If someone places treasure in a particular place, that place becomes important to them. They watch it, think about it, protect it, fear losing it, and organize decisions around it. Investment produces attention; sustained attention nourishes attachment; attachment becomes love or allegiance.

That is why the issue is not merely, “What do you own?” but, “What owns your concern and trust?”

A person may possess earthly goods without making them their treasure. The problem is not responsible ownership as such, but treating possessions as ultimate security, identity, or source of life. The verses that follow sharpen this point:

> “The eye is the lamp of the body” (6:22–23)  
> “You cannot serve God and mammon” (6:24).

The “eye” concerns one’s way of seeing and valuing; “serve” concerns ultimate allegiance. Wealth becomes spiritually dangerous when it ceases to be a tool and becomes a master.

## The point of the command

“Lay not up… but lay up” is therefore a call to relocate one’s investments. Jesus does not only command a feeling of detachment. He calls for concrete practices that direct the heart toward God’s kingdom.

That can include generosity, mercy, hidden faithfulness, seeking God’s approval, and using material goods for purposes that endure beyond their temporary possession. Such practices do not purchase salvation; rather, they enact and cultivate trust in God instead of trust in perishable stores.

So Matthew 6:19–21 is not merely diagnostic—*look at your treasure and discover your heart*—though it is that. It is also formative—*place your treasure with God, and your heart will increasingly follow*.
