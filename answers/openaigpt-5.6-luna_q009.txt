Romans 12:20–21 defines victory over evil not as getting even, humiliating an enemy, or forcing him to surrender. Victory is refusing to let another person’s evil determine one’s own character and conduct.

The surrounding context makes this clear:

- “Repay no man evil for evil” (v. 17)
- “Avenge not yourselves” (v. 19)
- “If it be possible…live peaceably” (v. 18)
- “Be not overcome of evil, but overcome evil with good” (v. 21)

The enemy’s hunger and thirst are met with real, practical good. The Christian does not merely refrain from revenge; he actively resists evil by doing what evil itself cannot do—showing undeserved mercy.

### What about “heaping coals of fire”?

The phrase comes from Proverbs 25:21–22 and has been interpreted in two related ways:

1. **Shame leading to repentance:** unexpected kindness may awaken remorse in the wrongdoer.
2. **God’s judgment:** if the enemy refuses repentance, the kindness highlights and exposes his guilt before God.

In either case, the “coals” are not permission to help someone while secretly enjoying his pain. They are not a psychological trick: “I will be kind so that I can hurt you more effectively.” Such manipulation would contradict the command to love and bless enemies. The act must be genuine, while any repentance or judgment belongs to the enemy’s conscience and to God.

### Is this weak?

No. It is not weakness, because it requires more than retaliation. Retaliation is often automatic; mercy requires self-command, courage, and freedom from the desire to be controlled by one’s enemy. The person who can answer hatred without becoming hateful has not been conquered by evil.

This also does not mean tolerating abuse, abandoning justice, or refusing protection. Romans 12 does not require pretending that evil is harmless. One may set boundaries, seek legal protection, expose wrongdoing, and pursue justice. What is forbidden is personal vengeance and the deliberate return of evil for evil.

Thus the passage presents a strong form of victory:

- evil does not reproduce itself through me;
- the enemy does not determine my moral identity;
- good is done even when it is undeserved;
- the possibility of the enemy’s repentance is preserved;
- final vengeance is left to God.

The command is therefore genuinely strong, but its strength is cruciform rather than coercive: it defeats evil by refusing to become its instrument.
