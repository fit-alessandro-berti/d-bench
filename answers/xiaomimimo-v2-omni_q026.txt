This passage from Matthew 12:34–35 presents a profound and integrated view of the relationship between the inner life and outward speech. It suggests that words are **all three**—symptoms, choices, and revelations—interwoven in a causal chain rooted in the moral and spiritual condition of the heart.

Here’s how the passage connects speech to the hidden “treasure” of the heart:

1. **Speech as a Symptom**  
   The phrase “out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh” implies that speech naturally **overflows** from what fills the heart. In this sense, words are like a thermometer—they indicate the internal temperature of the soul. If the heart is full of anger, deceit, or impurity, speech will reflect that; if full of compassion, truth, and wisdom, speech will likewise manifest it. The imagery of an “abundance” suggests that speech is not calculated but emerges from what is already stored within.

2. **Speech as a Revelation**  
   The heart is described as a “treasure” (θησαυρός) — a storehouse of values, affections, and moral commitments. Speech **reveals** what that treasure consists of. In calling the Pharisees a “generation of vipers,” Jesus is exposing their true nature, which their religious words tried to conceal. Thus, words can either reveal or betray the heart’s true alignment. They make visible what is otherwise hidden.

3. **Speech as a Choice**  
   Though overflow is natural, there is still agency implied: “bringeth forth” (ἐκβάλλει) suggests an active bringing out. The “good man” actively draws from good treasure; the “evil man” from evil. While the heart’s contents shape speech, the metaphor of treasure also implies accumulation—choices over time that fill the heart with good or evil. Thus, current speech choices both reveal and perpetuate the heart’s condition.

**Conclusion**  
Jesus’ teaching here is not merely psychological but deeply moral and spiritual. Words are **symptomatic** of the heart’s condition, **revelatory** of one’s true character, and **the result of ongoing choices** that fill the heart’s storehouse. The passage warns that superficial speech cannot long conceal a corrupt heart, and that true righteousness begins within. In this way, speech becomes a diagnostic tool for the soul’s health and a witness to the moral treasures we have chosen to cultivate.
