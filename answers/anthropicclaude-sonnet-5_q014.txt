This passage is doing something more precise than simply saying "you can tell what someone loves by what they own." Read closely, it's making a causal claim, not merely a diagnostic one.

## Revelation vs. Formation

If this were pure revelation — treasure as symptom — the verse would read something like "there your treasure is, for there your heart is already." The heart would be the fixed, prior fact, and treasure would be its footprint.

But the grammar runs the other direction: "*For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also.*" The heart's location is the future consequence, not the given premise. Treasure comes first in the sentence and first in causality; the heart *follows* it, migrates toward it, is *drawn* into its orbit.

So the text is not offering a test to diagnose an already-formed heart. It's offering a warning about a process: **put your treasure somewhere, and your heart will relocate there over time, whether you intend it or not.**

## Why This Makes Sense Practically

Think about what "treasure" actually requires of a person. To possess a treasure — especially an earthly one, vulnerable to moth, rust, and thieves — is to enter into an ongoing relationship of vigilance with it. You have to:

- **Check on it** (is it still there, is it decaying, is it safe)
- **Protect it** (locks, storage, defenses against theft)
- **Think about it** (plan around it, worry about it, arrange life to preserve it)

This is *attention* — repeated, habitual, involuntary attention. And attention is not neutral. What you attend to repeatedly becomes what you *care about*, because caring is partly constituted by sustained attention. You don't attend to something for years and remain indifferent to it.

And love — in the sense Matthew means by "heart" — is what attention becomes when it deepens and personalizes. The thing you've watched over, feared losing, organized your days around, stops being merely useful and starts being *dear*.

## The Chain

So the logic isn't:

> heart (fixed) → reveals itself through → treasure (chosen)

It's:

> treasure (chosen) → demands → attention (habitual) → produces → heart (formed)

Possession *requires* attention as a maintenance cost. Attention, sustained, *becomes* love. Love is *retroactively* what the treasure earns from you, not what it merely exposes.

This is why the passage frames it as counsel about where to *lay up* treasure — imperative, forward-looking — rather than as a diagnostic tool for self-knowledge. It's not "look at your treasure to discover your heart." It's "choose your treasure carefully, because your heart has no choice but to follow it home."

The corruptibility of earthly treasure (moth, rust, thieves) isn't incidental to this argument — it's the whole point. A heart that follows a decaying, stealable object becomes a heart shaped by anxiety, defensiveness, and eventual loss. A heart that follows something incorruptible is *formed* differently — not because it started differently, but because what it had to keep attending to was different.
