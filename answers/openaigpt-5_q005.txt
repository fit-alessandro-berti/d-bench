Enemy-love is hard to take seriously because it cuts against our deepest intuitions about fairness, safety, and belonging. It looks naïve in a world where harm is real and power is uneven. But in Jesus’ teaching it isn’t sentimental softness and it isn’t merely a tactic; it’s a morally strenuous way of willing the good of an offender while refusing to mirror their evil, grounded in the character of God.

Why it feels unreasonable
- Justice instinct: We’re wired for reciprocity. Loving an enemy seems to erase moral boundaries and “let them get away with it.”
- Threat and trauma: When we’ve been wounded, our bodies remember. Fight/flight makes tenderness toward a threat feel dangerous, even disloyal to our own pain.
- Group identity and honor: Hating a foe bonds the in‑group. Enemy-love can look like betrayal of one’s people.
- Social signaling: Retaliation signals strength; mercy can be read as weakness and invite further abuse.
- Short-term costs, long-term goods: Love of enemies rarely “works” immediately. The benefits (de-escalation, transformation) are delayed and uncertain.
- History of misuse: Abusers and unjust systems sometimes quote “love your enemies” to silence victims. That misuse makes the command suspect.

What enemy-love is not
- Not sentimentality: Jesus does not command warm feelings. He specifies concrete goods—bless, do good, pray. Love is willing and acting for an enemy’s true good.
- Not enabling harm: Loving an enemy doesn’t mean trusting them, avoiding legal consequences, or abandoning protection of the vulnerable. You can love an offender and still set firm boundaries, tell the truth, seek justice, and use legitimate means to stop harm.
- Not conflict avoidance: It can involve sharp confrontation aimed at restoration, not revenge.

What it is, biblically
- Imitation of God: “He makes his sun rise on the evil and the good.” The standard is God’s indiscriminate benevolence—giving what sustains life even to those who misuse it. Loving enemies displays family resemblance to the Father.
- A discipline of the will: Blessing those who curse you, doing good to haters, and praying for persecutors trains the heart out of retaliation and into mercy without lying about the wrong.
- A refusal to let evil set the terms: You do not become what you hate. You insist on the other’s humanity (and yours), even while confronting their sin.

Is it strategic?
- Often, yes. Nonretaliatory goodwill can disrupt cycles of escalation, expose injustice without making new victims, and open doors to repentance. Movements led by figures like Martin Luther King Jr. and Desmond Tutu show its power to transform public life.
- But it isn’t only strategy. If the enemy never changes, enemy-love is still right because it aligns us with God’s character and keeps our own souls from being conquered by hatred.

Why it’s a deeper form of moral strength
- It requires self-mastery: restraining vengeance when it feels justified.
- It requires courage: risking misunderstanding and possible further injury while pursuing truth and protection.
- It integrates justice and mercy: seeking accountability and the enemy’s ultimate good at once.
- It hopes beyond the visible: trusting God’s judgment and timing more than immediate payback.
- It re-humanizes: choosing to see an enemy not as a monster but as a damaged image-bearer capable of repentance—without denying harm.

How to practice it without naivety
- Name the wrong plainly. Love tells the truth.
- Set and enforce boundaries. Distance, legal action, and protective measures may be acts of love.
- Seek justice nonvengefully. Aim for restoration where possible; for restraint and consequences where necessary.
- Pray for the offender’s good—especially their awakening and reform. Prayer also protects your heart from corrosion.
- Do small, concrete goods where appropriate: refuse to slander, refuse to gloat, meet basic needs if they are in your power and it is safe to do so.
- Stay in community. Enemy-love practiced alone can be unsafe; the church (or a trusted community) should share the discernment and costs.

In short: Enemy-love is difficult because it confronts our fears and our sense of fairness. Read sentimentally, it’s dangerous; taken merely as strategy, it’s manipulative. In Jesus’ vision it is a rugged virtue—costly, courageous, truth-telling love that mirrors the Father’s own and, as a byproduct, often proves the most powerful way to break the cycle of harm.
