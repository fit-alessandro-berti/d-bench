## Pessimism or Realism?

The passage reads less like despair about humanity and more like a *diagnosis*—and diagnoses are only useful if they're accurate. A few things in the text itself push toward the "realistic about self-deception" reading:

**The Hebrew is about crookedness, not pure evil.** The word translated "deceitful" (*ʿāqōb*) means crooked, twisted, uneven terrain—it's the same root as Jacob's name, the heel-grabber and supplanter. And "desperately wicked" (*ʾānūš*) is better rendered "incurably sick"—it's a medical term Jeremiah uses elsewhere for untreatable wounds. So the claim isn't "humans are maximally evil" but "the heart is sick in a way it can't diagnose itself."

**The rhetorical question is the point.** "Who can know it?" The problem identified isn't wickedness per se—it's *opacity*. The heart is an unreliable narrator of its own story. That's an epistemological claim before it's a moral one.

**Verse 10 is the answer, not just a threat.** The pairing matters: the heart can't be known *by us*, but it can be known. And notice what God judges by—"according to his ways, and according to the fruit of his doings." Even the divine assessment cashes out in conduct and consequences, not in some private inner essence detached from action.

It's also worth remembering the context: Jeremiah is speaking to people who were confidently religious while practicing injustice. The verse targets *self-congratulation*—people whose hearts assured them they were fine. That's a precise observation about motivated reasoning, not a blanket condemnation.

## How to Talk About Motives, Then

If the heart is an unreliable witness, several practices follow:

**Speak about your own motives provisionally.** "I think I'm doing this because X, but I might be wrong" isn't false modesty—it's accuracy. Modern psychology largely agrees: we confabulate reasons after the fact and are poor introspectors. Paul's line in 1 Corinthians 4 echoes this: "I know nothing against myself, yet am I not hereby justified."

**Judge by fruit, including your own.** Verse 10 offers the method: ways and fruit. If you claim a motive of love but the pattern of outcomes is control, the pattern is better evidence than the self-report. This works on yourself too—your track record knows things your introspection doesn't.

**Be slow to assign motives to others.** If you can't fully read your own heart, you certainly can't read someone else's. This counsels describing behavior ("you said X, which hurt") rather than diagnosing intent ("you wanted to humiliate me").

**Invite outside examination.** The verse's logic implies you need a vantage point you don't have. For Jeremiah that's God ("Search me, O God, and know my heart," as Psalm 139 puts it); practically it also means trusted friends, confessors, therapists—people positioned to see your blind spots.

**Avoid the cynical overcorrection.** "The heart is deceitful" doesn't mean every kind impulse is secretly selfish. That reading is itself a kind of false certainty—claiming to know the heart is *always* bad, when the text's point is that you don't fully know it at all. The honest posture is neither "my motives are pure" nor "all motives are rotten," but "my motives are mixed and partly hidden from me, so watch the fruit and stay open to correction."

In short: the verse is realistic, and its realism is liberating rather than crushing—it frees you from the exhausting pretense of perfect self-knowledge and redirects attention to what can actually be examined: ways, fruit, and a willingness to be searched.
