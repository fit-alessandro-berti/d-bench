1 John 4:20 is part of a broader passage (1 John 4:7–21) that emphasizes the inseparable connection between loving God and loving one’s neighbor. The verse exposes a form of **self-deception**—specifically, the idea that one can claim to love God while simultaneously harboring hatred or indifference toward others. Here’s why this test works and what kind of deception it reveals:

### **1. The Logic of the Test: Love as a Unified Whole**
John’s argument hinges on the **unity of love**—the idea that love for God and love for humanity are not separate but **interdependent expressions of the same divine nature**. Jesus Himself taught this in Matthew 22:37–39, where loving God and loving neighbor are the two greatest commandments.

- **Visible love as evidence of invisible love**: If someone claims to love God but does not love their neighbor (whom they *can* see), their profession of love for God is **hypocritical**. The visible act (or lack thereof) of love for a fellow human being **contradicts** their claim to love the invisible God.
- **The impossibility of selective love**: John’s question—*"How can he love God whom he hath not seen?"*—implies that **true love cannot be compartmentalized**. If one cannot love what is visible (a fellow human), how can they genuinely love what is invisible (God)?

### **2. The Self-Deception Exposed**
This verse reveals **three key forms of self-deception**:

#### **A. The Deception of Empty Professions**
- Many people **verbally affirm** their love for God (e.g., through prayer, worship, or theological correctness) but **fail to act** in love toward others.
- John exposes this as **lip service**—a hollow claim that lacks real substance. True love must be **demonstrated**, not just declared.

#### **B. The Deception of Moral Selectivity**
- Some people believe they can **pick and choose** which commandments to obey. They may prioritize "spiritual" acts (e.g., prayer, Bible study) while neglecting "practical" ones (e.g., kindness, justice, mercy).
- John’s point is that **love is not divisible**. If you don’t love your neighbor, you don’t truly love God, because God’s nature is love (1 John 4:8, 16).

#### **C. The Deception of Self-Righteousness**
- A person might **judge others** for their lack of love while **excusing their own hardness of heart**. For example:
  - They might condemn a neighbor for hatred but **ignore their own bitterness, prejudice, or indifference**.
  - They might claim to be "spiritual" while **ignoring material needs** (e.g., poverty, suffering, injustice).
- John forces them to confront the **inconsistency**—if they truly loved God, they would **manifest that love in tangible ways** toward others.

### **3. Theological Foundation: God’s Nature and Our Reflection**
John’s argument rests on two key truths:
1. **God is love** (1 John 4:8, 16). To claim to love God is to **participate in His nature**.
2. **Love is active and relational** (1 John 3:16–18). If we do not love our brothers and sisters, we **do not know God** (1 John 4:20).

Thus, **hating or neglecting a neighbor is proof that one does not truly know or love God**, because such hatred **contradicts God’s character**.

### **4. Practical Implications**
This verse challenges believers to:
- **Examine their hearts**—Do their actions match their professions of faith?
- **Reject hypocrisy**—True faith must be **lived out** in love for others.
- **Fight self-deception**—We often **trick ourselves** into thinking we’re righteous when we’re not. John’s words are a **mirror** to expose this.

### **Conclusion**
1 John 4:20 is not just about **external behavior**—it’s about **the authenticity of one’s relationship with God**. The test of loving the visible neighbor **reveals whether our love for the invisible God is real or merely pretentious**. Self-deception thrives when we **separate love into categories**, but John insists that **true love is holistic**—it cannot be divided between God and humanity.
