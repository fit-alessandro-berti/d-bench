This passage presents one of the most profound psychological and spiritual principles in the Sermon on the Mount. To answer your question directly: **The text primarily argues that treasure *trains* (or anchors) the heart, though it simultaneously functions as a diagnostic tool that *reveals* the heart.**

However, the rhetorical force of the passage leans heavily toward the **formative** aspect (training). Jesus does not merely describe a static reality; he issues a command ("Lay up...") based on a causal principle ("For where your treasure is..."). He is instructing his listeners that by shifting their investments, they shift their affections.

Here is an analysis of how possession, attention, and love are tied together in this text.

### 1. Reveal vs. Train: The Dynamic of Verse 21
The third verse ("For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also") is the rationale for the commands in verses 19 and 20.

*   **The Diagnostic (Reveal):** If we observe a person’s life, their "treasure" (what they save, spend, and protect) accurately displays where their heart currently resides. If a person is obsessively securing earthly wealth, their heart is already bound to the earth. In this sense, possession reveals the heart.
*   **The Formative (Train):** However, the imperative mood of verses 19–20 suggests that the *placement* of treasure influences the *position* of the heart. The Greek word *kardia* (heart) refers not just to emotion, but to the seat of the will, intellect, and moral center. Jesus is teaching that the heart is not a fixed statue; it is a mobile weight that is pulled by gravity. By commanding his disciples to "lay up" treasures in heaven, he is providing a spiritual discipline. If you invest in the eternal, your heart will be pulled toward the eternal.

**Conclusion:** The text suggests a reciprocal feedback loop. While your heart dictates where you put your treasure, the act of placing your treasure reinforces and trains the heart to stay there. The primary point of the passage is **training**: *Do this action so that your heart becomes safe.*

### 2. The Triangle: Possession, Attention, and Love
In this passage, possession, attention, and love are not separate categories; they are three facets of the same spiritual orientation.

#### A. Possession as the Anchor (Treasure)
In the ancient world, "treasure" was not just currency; it was security. It was what you hid away for the future.
*   **The Tie:** Possession represents where you place your **trust**. To "lay up" treasure is to decide where your future safety lies.
*   **The Connection:** You cannot possess something without committing resources to it. By deciding to possess heavenly treasure (through generosity, justice, and faith), you are physically and economically tethering yourself to the Kingdom.

#### B. Attention as the Locus (The Heart)
The "heart" (*kardia*) in the Bible is the center of attention. It is where the mind dwells.
*   **The Tie:** Attention is the currency of the heart. You cannot attend to two masters (Matt 6:24).
*   **The Connection:** The text states that the heart *will be* where the treasure is. This implies that our focus is drawn to what we value. If you possess earthly treasure, your attention is consumed by protecting it from "moth and rust" and "thieves." If you possess heavenly treasure, your attention is consumed by God’s kingdom. Possession dictates the direction of your gaze.

#### C. Love as the Motivation (Affection)
Love is the desire for the good of the object.
*   **The Tie:** We love what we value, and we value what we possess.
*   **The Connection:** In verse 19, Jesus warns against earthly treasure because it is corruptible. Implicitly, he is saying: *Do not love what cannot last.* When you tie your possession to the eternal, your love is protected from decay. Love follows the treasure because love seeks security. If your treasure is safe in heaven, your love is safe in heaven.

### Summary of the Connection
The text ties these three together through the mechanism of **security**:

1.  **Possession** is where we store our security (Treasure).
2.  **Attention** is where we look for our security (Heart).
3.  **Love** is how we relate to that security.

Jesus is arguing that we cannot separate our economic choices from our spiritual state. We do not simply "love God" in the abstract; we love God through where we deposit our trust.

Therefore, the text is a call to **spiritual engineering**. It suggests that if you want your heart to be fixed on heaven, you must practically, tangibly, and financially "lay up" treasure there. By changing the possession, you train the attention, which ultimately aligns the love.
