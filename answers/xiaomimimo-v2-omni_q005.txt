This is a profound question that gets to the heart of one of the most challenging and counterintuitive teachings in the Gospels. The difficulty in taking "enemy-love" seriously stems from several understandable human perspectives:

1.  **From a "Sentimental" View:** If love is understood primarily as warm, affectionate feeling, then loving enemies seems impossible and even dishonest. We cannot command our emotions in that way, and to pretend to feel fondness for someone who wishes us harm can indeed feel like naive sentimentality.

2.  **From a "Strategic" View:** If love is seen as a tactic for winning over opponents or ensuring peace, it can be undermined when the "enemy" does not reciprocate. When the strategy appears to fail (the persecutor continues persecution), the love can be dismissed as foolish or ineffective.

However, the teaching here points to something deeper than sentiment or strategy—**a form of moral and spiritual strength rooted in the nature of God Himself.**

Jesus grounds the command in the character of the Father: *"That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven."* The model is God's own indiscriminate grace—the sun and rain that sustain all life, regardless of moral worth. This is not sentimentality; it is **sovereign, sustaining goodness.**

Therefore, enemy-love in this context is:

*   **A Discipline of the Will:** It is a conscious choice to seek the good of another, even when feelings are absent. It's an act of obedience that aligns the human will with the divine will.
*   **A Break from the Cycle of Retribution:** It refuses to be defined by the enemy's actions. By blessing instead of cursing, the person seizes moral autonomy and breaks the chain of hatred.
*   **Participation in God's Nature:** To love as God loves is to exercise a creative, life-giving power that seeks to redeem rather than destroy. It is the strength to see the "enemy" not just as an obstacle, but as a person who—like all humanity—is sustained by the same sun and rain, and who exists under the same ultimate judgment and mercy of God.

So, it is not sentimental (it doesn't require fond feeling), and while it can have strategic consequences (breaking cycles of violence), it is fundamentally **a deeper form of moral strength.** It is the strength to love *in the way God loves*—proactively, generously, and without requiring the worthiness of the beloved. It is perhaps the most demanding and supernatural form of strength there is.
