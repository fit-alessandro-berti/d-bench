Because Isaiah is calling for a whole-person return to God.

- “Way” (Hebrew: derek) means one’s path, habits, and outward conduct—the visible life you are walking.
- “Thoughts” (Hebrew: machshevot) means one’s plans, motives, desires, and inner counsels—the invisible life that drives the visible one.

Sin runs through both; so repentance must forsake both. Outward reform without inner change is hypocrisy, and “right thoughts” that never alter behavior are empty. The parallel terms anticipate the next verses (Isaiah 55:8–9): our ways and thoughts are not God’s. Repentance is abandoning our own paths and counsels to return to his.

Implications:
- Outward conduct: It must change—leave the old path.
- Inward imagination: It must be surrendered—renounce the self-made plans, cravings, and narratives that opposed God.
- Mercy: When the return is whole (outer and inner), God’s response is whole—he has compassion and “abundantly pardons.” His pardon is not grudging or partial; it covers both deed and intent, and it is available now (“while he may be found”), pressing the urgency to seek him without delay.
