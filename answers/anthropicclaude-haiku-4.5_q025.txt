# The Target of the Warning

The distinction you're drawing is crucial—and the verse itself seems to point toward **motivation rather than visibility per se**.

## The Evidence Points to Motive

The phrase "to be seen of them" functions as a *purpose clause*, not merely a description of what happens. Jesus isn't saying "if people happen to see your good works," but rather "if your aim *is* to be seen."

Consider:
- The "reward" you forfeit isn't social approval (that's explicitly theirs to keep)—it's God's reward
- The problem isn't that others witness the act, but that you've already received your payment in full through their approval
- There's no moral prohibition in the verses that follow against public fasting, praying, or giving—only against doing them *for an audience*

## Why This Matters

If visibility itself were the sin, then:
- Whistleblowers and activists couldn't act publicly
- Community service would be inherently corrupted
- Jesus couldn't commend the disciples' "light on a hill"

**But craving to be seen** hollows out the action—you've substituted an internal moral orientation (serving others, honoring God) with an external one (managing your reputation).

## The Harder Question

That said, separating pure motive from the *pleasure* of being seen is nearly impossible. Maybe the verse warns less about a clean distinction and more about **what you're primarily after**—the thing that would genuinely pain you if no one knew.

What's your intuition: can the two really be separated?
