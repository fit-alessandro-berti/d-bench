The difficulty in taking "enemy-love" seriously stems from the fact that, at first glance, it appears to violate the fundamental laws of social survival. To the skeptical observer or the weary human heart, this commandment often seems to ask for the impossible or the absurd. However, if we analyze it through a theological, psychological, and ethical lens, we can determine that the command is **not sentimental and not strategic**, but rather an expression of **profound moral strength.**

Here is an analysis of why enemy-love is difficult to take seriously, and why those three categories have theological significance.

### 1. Why it is *not* Sentimental (The Misunderstanding)
The primary reason enemy-love is ridiculed is because human beings conflate love with *affection* rather than *action*.
*   **The Trap of Emotion:** If "loving your enemy" required you to feel warm, fuzzy validation for your enemy, the command would indeed be foolish. No amount of moral discipline can manufacture genuine emotional attachment toward a threat.
*   **The Reality of Will:** In Christian ethics (specifically the *agape* tradition), love is a verb, not just a noun. It is a decision of the will to act for the good of another, regardless of feeling.
*   **Why it's Hard:** People do not take it seriously because they expect a change in emotion first. When they realize the command demands obedience even when feelings of resentment persist, they dismiss it as "sentimental wishful thinking."

### 2. Why it is often *suspected as* Strategic (The Secular Logic)
In a world governed by transactional ethics, a command that breaks transactional rules looks like a long con.
*   **Transactional Justice:** Human society generally operates on "lex talionis" (an eye for an eye) or reciprocal goodwill. If I do good, you do good back. If I forgive, I hope you repent.
*   **The Suspicion of Manipulation:** When Jesus commands this, the immediate suspicion is that it is a tool to disarm an opponent or turn the tables in a negotiation. "Thank you, Lord!" said the world, for that "foolishness" makes us look weak enough to be manipulated, or strong enough to manipulate others.
*   **The Failure of Utility:** It only fails this "strategic" label when it yields no earthly benefit. If I forgive my enemy when it damages my reputation or safety, it ceases to be a strategy for survival and becomes a surrender of self-preservation.

### 3. Why it is actually **Profound Moral Strength** (The Reality)
The text itself describes the motivation for this difficulty. It says, *"That ye may be the children of your Father."* This indicates that the command is about **divine identity imitations.**

**True moral strength is rejecting the lowest common denominator.**
*   **The Cost:** It is easier to kill, curse, or demand justice. It requires attention and emotional energy to do good to someone who hurts you. To "tend" to your enemy requires the muscles of patience that the muscles of vengeance relax.
*   **The Revelation:** By loving the enemy, the lover is not trying to protect themselves, but trying to protect the *other*. It is an act of lifting the burden of the enemy's sin (or perceived sin) rather than seeking to burden them with your own retaliation.
*   **The Impossibility:** It is impossible to do a loving, active deed toward someone you hate until you have overcome your own ego. Therefore, the one who can do so has conquered a higher degree of self-control.

### Conclusion: The Difficulty of the Immaculate
Why is it difficult to take seriously? Because it challenges the **human ego.**

1.  **It denies the Right to Retribution:** We believe we have a moral right to be treated better than others because we are good; this command asks us to release that right.
2.  **It endangers the Self:** It asks us to de-escalate when the environment is escalating. It is a quiet, internal struggle that is invisible and unglamorous compared to the flash of justice.

Ultimately, enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because **it is under-resourced.** It asks for the values of God in a world that offers the values of men. It cannot be sentimental because it persists despite pain; it cannot be strategic because it offers no material reward; therefore, it must be **supernatural moral strength.**

If it were anything else, it would be a tool of power. Instead, it attempts to be a tool of **humility**, which is the hardest thing in the world to wield seriously.
