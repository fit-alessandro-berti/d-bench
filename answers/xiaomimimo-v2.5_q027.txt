This is a rich passage, and I think the honest answer is: **all of those elements are present, but the parable's own framing narrows the diagnosis to a specific heart posture.** Let me walk through them.

---

## What the text actually identifies

Jesus introduces the parable with a programmatic sentence: he told it *"to certain who trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others."*

That framing already points to a cluster of intertwined sins rather than a single neat label:

1. **Self-trust as the root.** The Pharisee's confidence rests on his own performance — "I fast... I give tithes." The problem isn't that fasting or tithing are bad; it's that he treats them as the *basis* of his standing before God rather than as grateful responses to grace already received.

2. **Comparison and contempt as the fruit.** He doesn't just quietly thank God; he defines his righteousness *against* another person — "not as other men are, or even as this publican." The word "despised" in the introduction tells us this isn't neutral self-assessment; it's looking down.

3. **Virtue as self-display.** Jesus notes the Pharisee "stood and prayed *thus with himself*." There's a performative quality — he is constructing a self-portrait before God rather than actually addressing God. The prayer is, in a sense, addressed to an audience of one: himself.

So "false doctrine" isn't quite the right frame — the Pharisee likely affirmed correct theological propositions. The failure is **doxastic and affective**: he has fundamentally misunderstood his *position* before God, mistaking moral achievement for the grounds of acceptance.

---

## Why the tax collector's posture is "truer"

The tax collector's posture is treated as spiritually truer for several interlocking reasons:

- **He begins where every human actually stands.** "God, be merciful to me, a sinner" is a recognition of the basic asymmetry between creature and Creator. He doesn't perform self-assessment; he simply tells the truth.

- **He has no resort to self.** Standing "afar off," not lifting his eyes, beating his breast — every element communicates that he brings *nothing* to the transaction except his need. He has no résumé to present.

- **He addresses God, not himself.** Unlike the Pharisee's self-congratulatory monologue, the tax collector's prayer is genuinely directed *outward* — toward the only source of mercy. This is what prayer actually is.

- **He relies on mercy rather than merit.** The Greek word behind "merciful" (*hilaskomai*) carries overtones of atonement and propitiation — he's not asking God to acknowledge his efforts but to *cover* his failure.

The result — "this man went down to his house justified" — is startling precisely because it inverts every religious expectation of the original audience. Justification comes not through the accumulation of religious credentials but through honest dependence.

---

## The deeper logic

If you step back, the parable illustrates a principle that runs through the whole biblical narrative: **God resists the proud but gives grace to the humble** (Proverbs 3:34; James 4:6). The Pharisee's sin is not that he is morally disciplined — it's that his discipline has become a closed loop of self-reinforcement. He has effectively removed God from the equation; if he is already righteous by his own accounting, mercy becomes unnecessary, and God becomes merely an audience for his self-justification.

The tax collector, by contrast, has nothing left *but* God — and that naked dependence is precisely the posture in which grace can actually be received.

In short: the Pharisee's error isn't any single item on your list; it's the **total architecture of self-sufficiency** that makes all the others possible. The tax collector's truth isn't his emotions or his gestures — it's his彻底 (thorough) refusal to trust in anything except God's mercy.
