Great question. Let’s break down the passage and what it reveals about the Pharisee and the tax collector, and what exactly condemns the Pharisee in Jesus’ parable.

**The Scene Recap:**  
The Pharisee prays with self-congratulation, contrasting himself with others—specifically the tax collector—whom he despises. He enumerates his religious practices (fasting twice a week, giving tithes) as if these make him superior. The tax collector, meanwhile, stands at a distance, doesn’t even look up, and beats his breast, pleading for God’s mercy as a sinner.

---

### What Condemns the Pharisee?

Let’s look at the possible options you listed:

#### 1. **False Doctrine**
The Pharisee doesn’t explicitly state any false teaching; he’s talking about his own righteousness and practices. The problem isn’t doctrine per se, but his *understanding* of righteousness.

#### 2. **Pride / Self-Righteousness**
This is the clearest and most central issue. Jesus often criticizes the Pharisees for pride and self-righteousness (see, e.g., Matthew 23). The Pharisee’s prayer is about *himself*—how he is better than others, how he measures up. His focus is on his own virtue rather than on God or on his need for forgiveness. The prayer is self-centered, not God-centered.

#### 3. **Comparison**
The Pharisee’s prayer is all about *comparison*: “I am not like other men... or even as this tax collector.” He defines his righteousness by how he stacks up against others. Jesus will later say, “Why do you call me ‘Lord, Lord’ and do not do what I tell you? Everyone who comes to me and hears my words and does them, I will show you what he is like: he is like a man who built a house on the rock... But the one who hears my words and does not keep them is like a man who built a house on the ground...” (Luke 6:46-49). True righteousness isn’t about being better than others, but about being right with God.

#### 4. **Contempt**
He clearly looks down on the tax collector, calling him “even as this publican,” as if the tax collector is the lowest of the low. Contempt for others is a symptom of his pride and self-righteousness.

#### 5. **Use of Virtue as Self-Display**
This is closely related to pride and comparison. The Pharisee’s acts of fasting and tithing are not wrong in themselves, but he uses them to display his righteousness, to *advertise* his superiority. He’s more interested in appearing righteous than in actually *being* righteous before God.

---

### Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture “Spiritually Truer”?

The tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer because:

- **Humility**: He doesn’t see himself as righteous or better than others. He sees himself as a sinner in need of mercy.
- **Contrition**: He “smote upon his breast,” a sign of repentance and sorrow for sin.
- **Dependence on God’s Mercy**: He asks for mercy, acknowledging his need for God’s grace.
- **Honesty about Sin**: He doesn’t hide or excuse his sin; he admits it openly.
- **Focus on God, not Self**: His prayer is about his own need, not about how he compares to others.

Jesus’ point is that *genuine* repentance and humility lead to justification, not outward religious performance or self-righteousness.

---

### In Summary

**What condemns the Pharisee?**
- **Primarily: Pride and self-righteousness, combined with the use of virtue as self-display and contempt for others.**
- It’s not just the acts (fasting, giving tithes) but the *attitude* behind them.

**Why is the tax collector treated as spiritually truer?**
- Because he is humble, repentant, honest about his sin, and seeks God’s mercy rather than trying to prove his own righteousness.

Jesus is saying: *True righteousness is not about how good we are compared to others, but about how we stand before God—humble, contrite, and in need of His grace.*
