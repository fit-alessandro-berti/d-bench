“Cease to do evil” is only half of what God wants.  Isaiah immediately adds “learn to do well” to show that the prophet is not calling for mere abstention from wrong-doing; he is calling for an active, trained pursuit of justice and compassion.

1. A deliberate progression  
   • Wash / make clean (remove impurity)  
   • Put away evil / stop doing it (negative repentance)  
   • Learn to do well (positive formation)  

   The verbs move from removing dirt, to abandoning evil acts, to acquiring new skills.  The climax is the verb לִמְדוּ (limdu) “learn,” which in Hebrew always implies disciplined training—what a disciple does with a teacher, or a soldier with a drill-master.  Goodness is therefore something that must be acquired and practiced, not simply the state that remains when evil is absent.

2. “Doing well” is immediately defined  
   “Seek justice, straighten (relieve) the oppressed, judge (defend) the orphan, plead for the widow.”  
   These four infinitives spell out what “learning to do well” entails: public justice, advocacy for the powerless, and personal involvement with those without social protection.  It is a practiced form of love and social righteousness (mishpɑṭ and tsedɑqɑh), not private moral innocence.

3. Israel’s covenant ethic  
   The Triad “widow-orphan-sojourner” appears throughout the Torah (e.g., Ex 22:22; Deut 24:17).  Isaiah invokes it to remind Judah that covenant loyalty is measured in concrete acts of care.  If the people merely stop sinning but do not step into these covenant responsibilities, they are still unfaithful.

4. The danger of a moral vacuum  
   Merely stopping evil leaves a void; unless the void is filled with positive righteousness, injustice quickly returns (compare Jesus’ parable of the unclean spirit, Mt 12:43-45).  Isaiah therefore couples the negative and positive commands so the reform is complete.

5. Implication for the definition of goodness  
   • Goodness is not simply restraint.  
   • Goodness is an acquired, communal practice of justice, mercy, and advocacy.  
   • It must be “learned” because human beings are not naturally disposed to it; they need formation—teaching, habituation, and concrete obediences.

Hence the text moves from “cease to do evil” to “learn to do well” to teach that genuine repentance demands more than stopping sin; it demands an active, disciplined pursuit of love and justice toward one’s neighbor.
