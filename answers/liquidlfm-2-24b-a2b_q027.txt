This passage from **Luke 18:9–14** presents a parable contrasting two men who go to pray in the temple—one a Pharisee, the other a tax collector (publican). Jesus uses their attitudes and actions to teach about humility and true righteousness. Let's break down what specifically is condemned in the Pharisee and why the tax collector's posture is seen as more spiritually authentic.

---

### What Condemns the Pharisee?

**1. Pride and Self-Righteousness:**  
The Pharisee stands and prays *to himself*, declaring his own righteousness ("God, I thank thee, that I am not as other men are..."). This is not prayer but self-praise—a form of spiritual pride.

**2. Comparison and Contempt:**  
He explicitly compares himself favorably to "other men," especially mentioning their sins (extortioners, unjust, adulterers), thereby putting himself above them. This attitude breeds contempt for others.

**3. False Doctrine/False Righteousness:**  
His self-righteousness is based on works (fasting, tithing) rather than genuine repentance or humility before God. He believes his external piety makes him righteous in God’s sight, ignoring his inner sinfulness.

**4. Use of Virtue as Self-Display:**  
By listing his religious acts (fasting, tithing), he turns his virtues into badges of honor, seeking approval from others and from God.

---

### Why Is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?

**1. Humility and Self-Awareness:**  
The tax collector stands "afar off" and "would not lift up his eyes unto heaven." This posture symbolizes humility and recognition of his own unworthiness.

**2. Confession and Contrition:**  
He does not boast or compare himself to others; instead, he simply asks for mercy ("God be merciful to me, a sinner"). This reflects true repentance and dependence on grace.

**3. Lack of Self-Righteousness:**  
He does not list any good works or claim moral superiority. His posture shows he knows he needs forgiveness, not self-justification.

**4. True Righteousness Comes Through Faith, Not Works:**  
Jesus says, "This man went down to his house justified rather than the other" (v. 14). Justification comes through faith and humility, not by human effort or self-righteousness.

---

---

### Conclusion

**The Pharisee is condemned primarily for pride, self-righteousness, comparison, contempt, and using his virtues as a means of self-display.** The tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer because it is marked by humility, confession, and reliance on God’s mercy—not on his own merit.

**In summary:**  
- **Condemned:** Pride, self-righteousness, comparison, contempt, self-display.  
- **Spiritually truer:** Humility, confession, dependence on grace.

This parable teaches that true righteousness is not about outward displays of piety, but about a humble heart that recognizes its need for God’s mercy.
