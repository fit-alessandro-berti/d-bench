Amos rejects the worship so sharply because the problem is **not imbalance**. It is **contradiction**.

The people are not being told, “You have too much ritual and not enough ethics; adjust the ratio.” They are being told, “Your worship has become false because your public life denies the God you claim to worship.”

In Amos, the issue is that Israel’s religious life is flourishing while justice is collapsing. The festivals continue. Offerings continue. Music continues. Assemblies continue. But the poor are being crushed, courts are corrupted, the needy are exploited, and the powerful are secure in their piety. So the worship is not merely deficient. It has become a lie.

The sharpness comes from this: **worship is supposed to express covenant fidelity, but here it is being used to conceal covenant betrayal.**

When God says, “I hate, I despise your feast days,” the pronoun matters. They may be religious feasts, even formally correct ones, but God calls them **your** feasts. They have been severed from his character. They are no longer signs of communion with him; they are religious performances staged by people who refuse his justice.

The fraud being exposed is something like **ritual laundering**.

The people are trying to offer God sacrifices, songs, and ceremonies while withholding righteousness from their neighbors. They are treating worship as though it could compensate for injustice, as though liturgy could bribe God, as though beauty in the sanctuary could cancel brutality in the marketplace or the courtroom.

That is why God rejects the smell of offerings and the sound of songs. The sacrifices are not neutral. The music is not harmless. In context, they have become part of the deception. They allow an unjust society to feel holy. They give oppressors a religious self-image. They make people think they are near to God while they are actively resisting what God loves.

So Amos is not saying, “Add justice to your worship schedule.”

He is saying, “Without justice, this is not worship.”

The final line makes that clear:

> “But let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream.”

“Judgment” here means justice: right order, fair dealing, public rectitude. “Righteousness” is not merely private virtue but covenant faithfulness embodied in social life. Amos wants justice not as an occasional charitable gesture, but as a flowing, public, unstoppable reality.

The moral fraud is pretending to honor God while dishonoring the human beings whose cause God defends.

The spiritual fraud is imagining that God can be approached aesthetically, ceremonially, or emotionally while his moral will is being refused.

In Amos’s logic, God cannot “accept” such worship without becoming an accomplice to the lie. That is why the language is not moderate. A call for “better balance” would imply that the worship is basically valid but needs supplementation. Amos says the opposite: worship that masks injustice is counterfeit, and God’s rejection of it is itself an act of truth.
