The parable itself gives the controlling diagnosis in verse 9: Jesus speaks it against those who **“trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others.”** Everything in the Pharisee’s prayer expresses those two things.

It is not that fasting, tithing, sexual faithfulness, or avoiding injustice are bad. They are good, and the Pharisee may even be stating facts. Nor is the primary problem simply that he names other people’s sins. The problem is what he does with his virtues and with his neighbor.

### What condemns the Pharisee?

All the features named in the question are related, but the root is **self-righteous pride**:

- **Trust in himself:** He treats his moral record as his ground of acceptance before God. His prayer contains no confession, no plea for mercy, and no awareness that he too stands as a sinner before God.
- **Comparison:** His goodness is measured comparatively—“not as other men … or even as this publican.” He does not ask whether he is righteous before God’s holy judgment; he comforts himself by finding people who appear worse.
- **Contempt:** His comparison is not sorrowful discernment but scorn. The publican is not a fellow sinner to be pitied, prayed for, or loved; he is evidence supporting the Pharisee’s high opinion of himself.
- **Virtue as self-display:** Even if no human audience is present, the Pharisee uses prayer as an occasion to present his résumé. His virtues become material for self-exaltation rather than gratitude, obedience, and humble dependence on grace.
- **A false practical doctrine of justification:** The Pharisee may affirm many true religious doctrines, but in practice he acts as though he is justified by his own moral distinction. That is the theological error at the heart of the scene.

The phrase that he “prayed thus **with himself**” is fitting: the prayer sounds addressed to God, but it functions as self-congratulation. God is not being sought as merciful Judge and Savior; he is being invoked as a witness to the Pharisee’s superiority.

### Why is the tax collector spiritually truer?

The tax collector’s bodily posture—standing far off, lowered eyes, beating his breast—is not magical or meritorious. It expresses what his words plainly say: he knows who he is before God.

> “God be merciful to me a sinner.”

He does not bargain, compare, defend himself, or list compensating virtues. He comes without a claim. He acknowledges that he needs mercy.

The wording for “be merciful” is especially strong: it carries the sense of seeking God’s atoning mercy—asking God to deal with his sin rather than pretending it is not there. His posture is therefore spiritually “true” because it corresponds to reality: before God, he is a sinner dependent on grace.

Jesus does not say that the tax collector is justified because humility earns justification. Rather, he is justified because he seeks mercy from God instead of presenting himself as already worthy. His humility is the posture of faith and repentance; the Pharisee’s pride is the posture of self-justification.

So the contrast is not:

- religious morality versus irreligion,
- Pharisee versus tax collector as social types,
- or upright deeds versus no deeds.

It is **self-justification versus mercy**, **contemptuous comparison versus repentance**, and **confidence in one’s own righteousness versus dependence on God’s grace**.

That is why Jesus concludes: “every one that exalteth himself shall be abased; and he that humbleth himself shall be exalted.”
