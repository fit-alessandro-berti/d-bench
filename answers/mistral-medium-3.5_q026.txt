Matthew 12:34–35 presents a profound connection between speech and the moral state of the heart, portraying words as **all three**: *symptoms, choices, and revelations*—interwoven in a way that reflects Jesus' broader ethical teaching.

### 1. **Words as Symptoms**
   - Jesus describes speech as flowing from the **"abundance of the heart"** (v. 34), implying that words naturally reveal what is already stored within. Just as a tree’s fruit exposes its nature (cf. Matthew 7:16–18), speech is an involuntary *symptom* of the heart’s condition.
   - Evil speech (e.g., slander, deceit) is not a random act but evidence of a deeper moral corruption ("evil treasure"). Conversely, good speech arises from a heart cultivated with virtue.

### 2. **Words as Choices**
   - The imagery of a **"treasure"** (v. 35) suggests intentional stockpiling. The heart is not passive; it actively gathers and preserves moral resources (good or evil). Thus, speech is also a *choice*—a deliberate drawing from what one has prioritized.
   - This aligns with Jesus’ earlier warning that "every idle word" will be judged (Matthew 12:36), emphasizing accountability for how one uses language.

### 3. **Words as Revelations**
   - Jesus’ rebuke to the Pharisees ("generation of vipers") frames their speech as a *revelation* of their true allegiance. Their words expose hypocrisy, proving that their hearts are far from God despite outward piety.
   - The passage echoes Proverbs 4:23 ("Keep your heart with all vigilance, for from it flow the springs of life"), where speech is a window into the soul.

### **Synthesis: All Three**
Jesus’ teaching implies a dynamic interplay:
- **Symptom**: Speech naturally overflows from the heart’s hidden state.
- **Choice**: The "treasure" metaphor shows that the heart’s contents are shaped by deliberate moral investments.
- **Revelation**: Words uncover the heart’s true orientation, whether toward God or evil.

This tripartite view underscores the ethical gravity of speech in Scripture: it is both a diagnostic tool (revealing inner health) and a moral act (requiring intentional purity). As James 3:10–12 later reflects, a spring cannot yield both fresh and bitter water—speech is a faithful barometer of the heart’s allegiance.
