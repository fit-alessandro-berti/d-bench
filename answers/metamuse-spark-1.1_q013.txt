In Amos 5:21-24, God is not editing their worship service. He is closing it down. The language is deliberately absolute and disgusted:

> "I hate, I despise your feast days... I will not smell... I will not accept... I will not regard... I will not hear."

That is covenant lawsuit language - a *rib*. God is speaking as a betrayed husband and as a judge.

To understand why it has to be so sharp, you have to see what was happening at Bethel and Gilgal.

Under Jeroboam II, Israel was rich and religious. Business was booming, the army was winning, and the shrines were full. Tithes were up, festivals were crowded, choirs were singing with harps and viols. It looked like revival.

But Amos, an outsider - a shepherd from Judah - walks in and says: Look at how you got the money for those fat beasts you are offering.

In the same chapter he accuses them:

> "Ye... afflict the just, ye take a bribe, and ye turn aside the poor in the gate from their right." Amos 5:12
> "They sell the righteous for silver, and the poor for a pair of shoes." Amos 2:6

The courts in the city gate - where the poor were supposed to find *mishpat* [justice] - had become a place where the poor were robbed legally. Landlords were seizing fields, merchants were using false balances, debtors were being sold into slavery, and then everyone went to church on the Sabbath to sing louder.

That is the fraud Amos is exposing. It's not just hypocrisy; it's deeper than that:

**1. Compartmentalized religion.** 
They had divided life into two separate spheres: religion is what we do for God on feast days; business is what we do for ourselves the rest of the week. Amos says there is no such division. If there is no justice in the gate, there is no worship in the sanctuary, no matter how correct the ritual is.

**2. Worship as a cover and legitimizer.**
The elaborate worship wasn't just *in spite of* the injustice - it was *because* of it. The festivals made them feel righteous. They could tell themselves, "We can't be that bad, look at how devoted we are to God." Worship became spiritual whitewash for exploitation. In modern terms, it was reputation laundering.

**3. Worship as a bribe.**
The ancient worldview was transactional: if you offend a god, you give him more offerings. Israel was treating YHWH like a corrupt judge who can be paid off. Bring a fatter peace offering, sing a better song, and He will overlook the eviction of the widow next door. God says: I will not smell it. The Hebrew is visceral - the pleasing aroma of sacrifice has become a stench to Him.

That's why God can't just ask for "better balance: a little less injustice and keep the worship." Better balance would mean that worship and oppression *can* coexist if you get the proportions right.

Amos says they are mutually exclusive. Worship that coexists with oppression is not worship at all. It is "noise" - *hamon* in Hebrew. Just an irritating racket.

That is why the final verse shifts images so violently:

> "But let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream."

Your feasts are stagnant, contained, controlled, scheduled pools. What I want is not a contained ritual but a flood that breaks its banks and washes everything.

*Mishpat* - justice - is not vague fairness. It is concrete, legal, social action that makes things right for the vulnerable in public.
*Tsedakah* - righteousness - is not private charity. It is right relationship, living up to your covenant obligations to your neighbor.

He is saying: Stop trying to channel your religion into an hour on the festival day. Let justice be the ever-flowing stream that channels and defines everything else.
