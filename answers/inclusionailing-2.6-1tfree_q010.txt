To ask this is to touch the nerve of the Cross itself.  

The power being praised here is **kenotic authority**—power that is most fully itself when it empties itself, not when it fills itself with display.  

1. **What kind of power?**  
   It is power that does not *need* to grasp, because it already *is* (“being in the form of God”).  
   It is power that chooses descent as the medium of its expression, not because it is weak, but because its greatness is so complete it can afford to hide.  
   It is power that becomes “obedient unto death”—not the obedience of a subordinate forced to obey, but the obedience of love that freely binds itself to the other’s good.  
   In short, it is **sovereignty that becomes service**, greatness that becomes gravity, not spectacle.

2. **Why is humility joined to greatness more unsettling than open domination?**  

   - **Domination declares the rules of the game openly**: it says, “I am stronger, and I will take.” We know how to respond—fear, resistance, flattery, or submission. It confirms the world as a hierarchy of force.  

   - **Humility-in-greatness breaks the grammar of power**: when the one who *could* command instead stoops, the ground shifts beneath us. Our ordinary defenses—pride, suspicion, rivalry—suddenly look exposed, even ridiculous. We are forced to ask: *What if the highest power is actually love that gives itself away?* That question strips us of the excuse that “this is just how the world works.”  

   - **It reveals our secret preference for distance**: we often feel safer with tyrants than with saints, because tyrants keep their greatness at a distance; saints let greatness come near, near enough to show us ourselves. The Cross is not just a moral example; it is a *revelation* that undoes our calculus of leverage and control.  

   - **It creates moral accountability without coercion**: domination forces the body, but kenotic love lays claim to the will. The humility of Christ does not overpower us; it *unmakes* us by showing us a beauty we cannot seize, only receive. That is why it feels like a wound, not just an instruction.

In the end, the power praised here is **resurrection power wearing work clothes**—the power that dies and rises, that gives itself away and is given back by the Father. It unsettles us because it dares to suggest that the universe is held together not by the will to dominate, but by the willingness to let go—and in that letting-go, to win everything.
