### Analysis of Matthew 6:19–21

This passage from the Sermon on the Mount is a teaching on priorities, contrasting fleeting earthly wealth with enduring spiritual value. The core idea is to invest in what lasts eternally ("treasures in heaven") rather than what can be lost or corrupted on earth. The pivotal verse—"For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also"—serves as the rationale for this advice. Let's break down your questions step by step.

#### Does the Text Say Treasure *Reveals* the Heart, or That Treasure *Trains* the Heart?
The text leans more toward the idea that **treasure trains (or directs/shapes) the heart**, though it carries an element of revelation as a natural consequence. Here's why:

- **Training/Directing the Heart**: The phrasing "where your treasure is, *there will your heart be also*" implies a directional or causal relationship. Your "treasure" (what you prioritize, invest in, or value most) actively pulls your heart toward it. In biblical language, the "heart" represents the center of a person's will, desires, emotions, and loyalties—not just feelings, but the core of one's inner life. By choosing to "lay up" (store or accumulate) treasure in a particular place—earth or heaven—you're training your heart to follow suit. It's like setting the compass of your affections: earthly treasures train the heart toward temporary, self-centered pursuits (prone to corruption and theft), while heavenly treasures train it toward eternal, God-centered ones (secure and incorruptible). This is proactive and formative; Jesus is urging a deliberate reorientation of priorities to reshape where the heart naturally resides.

- **Revelation as a Secondary Implication**: While not the primary thrust, the verse can also reveal the heart's true state. If you observe *where* someone's treasure lies (e.g., obsession with wealth, status, or possessions), it discloses *where* their heart already is—divided, anxious, or misaligned. But the context emphasizes instruction and change ("lay not up... but lay up"), not mere diagnosis. It's less "your treasure shows your heart" and more "guard your treasure to guide your heart."

In short, the emphasis is on influence and formation: treasure as a trainer, not just a mirror. This aligns with broader biblical themes, like Proverbs 4:23 ("Above all else, guard your heart, for everything you do flows from it"), where protecting the heart's direction is key.

#### How Do Possession, Attention, and Love Become Tied Together Here?
The passage weaves these concepts into a unified framework of value and attachment, showing how they interconnect to either enrich or ensnare the heart. "Treasure" isn't just literal wealth (like gold or goods) but metaphorically represents *anything* we possess, pursue, or hold dear—time, talents, relationships, or ambitions. Here's how possession, attention, and love entangle:

- **Possession as the Foundation**: Treasure begins with *possession* or acquisition—what you "lay up" or store for yourself. The text warns of earthly possessions' vulnerability ("moth and rust doth corrupt, and... thieves break through and steal"), highlighting their instability. Possessing something creates a sense of ownership and security, but it also binds you to it. Heavenly treasures, by contrast, are possessions that can't be taken away, fostering a deeper, unassailable sense of value. Possession isn't inherently bad—it's the *type* and *location* that matter—but it sets the stage for deeper emotional investment.

- **Attention as the Bridge**: Once you possess (or pursue) a treasure, your *attention* inevitably follows. Attention is the energy, focus, and effort you devote to maintaining or growing it. Earthly treasures demand constant vigilance (guarding against "moths" of decay or "thieves" of loss), which trains your gaze downward and inward, breeding worry (as Jesus expands on in the following verses about anxiety). Heavenly treasures, being eternal, free your attention for higher purposes—like generosity, faith, and service—without the fear of loss. The verse implies that attention flows to the treasure's location, pulling the heart along: "Where your treasure is" becomes where your mind and efforts dwell.

- **Love as the Culmination**: Love ties it all together as the heart's response—the affection, devotion, and ultimate loyalty that possession and attention cultivate. In Scripture, to "love" something is to set your heart on it (e.g., Deuteronomy 6:5 commands loving God with all your heart). If your treasure is earthly, possession and attention breed a love that's possessive, fearful, and fleeting—your heart loves what can rust or be stolen, revealing (or training) a divided allegiance. But treasuring heaven trains the heart to love what endures: God's kingdom, justice, and relationships that reflect divine values. The result? A unified heart, where love aligns with eternal good rather than temporary gain.

This triad forms a cycle: Possession invites attention, which deepens love, which in turn reinforces what you possess and attend to. Jesus is cautioning against a downward spiral (earthly focus leading to a hardened, anxious heart) and inviting an upward one (heavenly focus leading to a generous, joyful heart). It's a call to audit your "treasures" to ensure they shape a heart oriented toward lasting love, not illusory security.
