To answer your question, we must look at the text through the lenses of both moral philosophy and psychology. The movement from verse 18 to verse 19 suggests that pride is not merely a "sin" in the sense of a broken rule, but a **structural failure of the self.**

Here is an analysis of why pride is linked to blindness and collapse, and what the contrast with "the lowly" reveals about the psychology of arrogance.

### 1. Why Pride leads to Blindness (The Cognitive Aspect)
The text implies that pride acts as a veil. When the proverb says pride "goeth before" destruction, it suggests a causal sequence: pride creates a state of being that makes destruction inevitable.

*   **The Loss of Feedback Loops:** In healthy psychology, humans rely on feedback (error correction) to navigate reality. Pride functions as a mechanism that filters out negative feedback. If I believe I am infallible or superior, I interpret criticism as "envy" and failure as "bad luck." 
*   **The Illusion of Autonomy:** Pride creates a psychological "narcissism of the ego" where the individual believes they are the sole author of their success. This blindness prevents them from seeing the dependencies, risks, and social realities that actually sustain them.
*   **Intellectual Rigidity:** Blindness is the inability to see the "cliff" ahead. Because the haughty spirit is focused upward (on status) rather than outward (on reality), they lose the ability to perceive the ground shifting beneath them.

### 2. Why Pride leads to Collapse (The Structural Aspect)
The "fall" mentioned in verse 18 is both moral and structural.

*   **The Gravity of Reality:** Arrogance is an attempt to defy the natural order of human limitation. Because pride is an inflation of the self, it creates an imbalance. In physics and in psychology, an over-extended structure becomes unstable. The "fall" is the inevitable moment when the weight of reality crashes against the inflated ego.
*   **Social Isolation:** Pride severs the social bonds that act as safety nets. A proud person alienates allies and incurs enemies. When the "destruction" comes, they find they have no foundation of community to catch them; they fall alone because they have pushed everyone else away.

### 3. The Contrast with "The Lowly": The Moral Psychology of Arrogance
Verse 19 provides the ethical "antidote" by contrasting the proud with the lowly. This reveals three key insights into the psychology of arrogance:

**A. The Value of Proximity vs. The Value of Profit**
The text says it is better to be with the lowly than to "divide the spoil with the proud." This suggests that arrogance is inherently **transactional.** The proud person is interested in the "spoil"—the status, the wealth, the victory. The lowly person is interested in **relationship and truth.** The arrogant person values what they can *get*; the humble person values where they *stand*.

**B. The Psychology of Risk Management**
The "spoil" represents a high-stakes game. To "divide the spoil with the proud" is to enter into a pact with instability. The proverb suggests that arrogance is a contagion; if you align your interests with the proud to gain wealth, you are inherently tethering your fate to their inevitable "fall." Humility, conversely, is a form of psychological and social stability.

**C. The Direction of the Gaze**
The moral psychology here is defined by the **direction of attention**:
*   **The Proud:** Their gaze is *vertical* (looking down on others) and *self-referential*. They are preoccupied with their own height.
*   **The Lowly:** Their gaze is *horizontal* (looking at others) and *reality-referential*. They are preoccupied with the truth of their situation.

### Summary
Pride is linked to blindness because it **severs the connection to truth**, and it is linked to collapse because it **defies the laws of human interdependence.** 

The contrast with "the lowly" suggests that true moral strength is found not in the height of one's status (the "spoil"), but in the integrity of one's position. Arrogance is an attempt to live *above* reality; humility is the wisdom of living *within* it.
