The passage in **Isaiah 55:6–7** highlights a profound and holistic understanding of repentance that goes beyond mere external behavior to include the inner life of the mind and heart. The call to the wicked to **"forsake his way"** and to the unrighteous man to **"forsake his thoughts"** reveals that true repentance must address both **outward conduct** and **inward disposition**.

### 1. **"Forsake His Way" (Outward Conduct)**
   - This refers to the **actions, habits, and behaviors** that are contrary to God’s will. The "way" of the wicked is their pattern of life—sinful deeds, unjust practices, or rebellion against God’s commandments.
   - Repentance requires a **turning away from these actions**, a visible break from sinful behavior. This is the **external** dimension of repentance, often what people associate with "changing one’s ways."

### 2. **"Forsake His Thoughts" (Inward Imagination)**
   - This goes deeper, addressing the **mind, attitudes, and motivations** behind the actions. The "thoughts" of the unrighteous man include his **beliefs, desires, and mental frameworks** that justify or fuel his sin.
   - Sin often begins in the **imagination and heart** (cf. **Matthew 15:19**, *"For out of the heart come evil thoughts..."*). If only outward behavior changes but the **mind remains corrupt**, the repentance is superficial.
   - True repentance requires a **renewal of the mind** (Romans 12:2), a rejection of sinful thought patterns (e.g., pride, greed, bitterness, lust) that lead to unrighteousness.

### **Why Both Are Necessary for Mercy**
- **God sees the heart (1 Samuel 16:7).** Outward reform without inward transformation is **hypocrisy** (Isaiah 29:13). Mercy is extended to those who **genuinely turn to God**, not just those who perform religious acts while harboring sin in their hearts.
- **Sin is rooted in the heart.** Jesus taught that **murder begins with anger, adultery with lust** (Matthew 5:21–28). If only the "way" is forsaken but the "thoughts" remain, the person is still in bondage to sin.
- **God’s mercy is abundant but requires sincerity.** The promise of **"abundant pardon"** (v. 7) is for those who **fully return**—not just in action but in **mind and spirit**. Partial repentance is no repentance at all.

### **Implications for the Believer**
1. **Repentance is not just stopping sin but changing the heart.** It’s possible to stop doing wrong things (e.g., stealing, lying) but still nurse resentment, greed, or self-justification. True repentance **hates the sin itself**, not just its consequences.
2. **The Holy Spirit must renew the mind.** Since sinful thoughts are deeply ingrained, **divine intervention** is needed (Ezekiel 36:26–27). This is why Scripture calls for **transformation by the Spirit** (Titus 3:5).
3. **Mercy is available but conditional on genuine return.** God’s **pardon is abundant**, but it is for those who **humble themselves** (2 Chronicles 7:14) and **turn wholeheartedly** (Joel 2:12–13).

### **Conclusion**
Isaiah 55:7 teaches that **true repentance is comprehensive**—it involves **both the hands (way) and the heart (thoughts)**. Mercy is extended to those who **abandon sin in action and in attitude**, trusting in God’s willingness to forgive. This dual emphasis ensures that repentance is **not a superficial ritual but a radical reorientation of life toward God**.
