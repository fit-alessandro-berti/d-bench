However, it reflects a common interpretation of the story of **The Woman Caught in Adultery** (John 7-9 context) or a parable of judgment. Regardless of textual nuance, the *moral destabilization* you are asking about is not just textual—it is ethical:

1.  **Why It Is Morally Destabilizing:**
    It creates an impossible moral standard that requires a person to be perfect to judge others. It implies that in every instance where someone casts a stone at the accused, they themselves possess a "sin" (guilt) or flaw. This destabilizes trust and makes human judgment fundamentally flawed by virtue of its own imperfection.
2.  **Does It Forbid Judgment?**
    No. It doesn't forbid moral judgment entirely; rather, it challenges the *authority* to judge. If no one is perfect to cast this first stone, then the stone-caster is also a criminal (or guilty) by the very logic of "perfect judging." It forces the accuser to self-examination rather than relying on an external standard.
3.  **Does It Expose Hypocrisy?**
    **Yes.** The instability arises because the requirement for "sinless" judges cannot be met. If you judge a sinner based on their own perceived perfection, you are not a moral authority but a liar. This exposes the hypocrisy of those who feel guilt over their inability to judge others perfectly.
4.  **Does It Interrupt Mob-Righteousness?**
    **Yes.** The "mob" or crowd in many stories (like John 7) is often swayed by collective pressure, not moral logic. This passage cuts through the "mob mentality" to demand a higher standard. If no one is sinless, then mob-righteousness loses its foundation because it requires a "better source."
5.  **What Is Deeper?**
    It points toward something deeper than just morality or law: **Human Flaws and Divine Perspective.** The sentence suggests that God's perspective on judgment does not require human "sinlessness" to cast the stone, but rather that judgment must come from a higher standard of love/guilt. This exposes the instability of human authority.

It destabilizes because it assumes human judgment is inherently flawed when applied to perfection, thereby challenging the authority of those who judge.
