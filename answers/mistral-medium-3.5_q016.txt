The elder brother’s deepest problem is likely a combination of **pride in his own obedience** and **discomfort with mercy itself**, which fuels his anger, envy, and sense of wounded fairness.

1. **Pride in Obedience** – He boasts of his faithful service ("these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment"), suggesting he sees his relationship with the father as transactional—earning rewards rather than resting in love. His obedience has become a source of self-righteousness rather than humble devotion.

2. **Discomfort with Mercy** – His outrage at the father’s celebration of the prodigal son reveals a resistance to grace. He cannot rejoice in his brother’s restoration because he resents that mercy is extended to one who, in his eyes, does not "deserve" it. This suggests a legalistic mindset where he believes justice should be strict, not generous.

3. **Anger, Envy, and Wounded Fairness** – These are symptoms of the deeper issues. His anger stems from feeling slighted, his envy from seeing the prodigal honored, and his sense of fairness violated because he expected his obedience to be uniquely rewarded. Yet, the father’s response—*"all that I have is thine"*—shows that the elder brother’s real problem is his inability to receive the father’s love on its own terms.

Ultimately, his struggle is **a refusal to embrace the father’s heart of mercy**, preferring instead a system of merit where he can control and claim what is "earned." His deepest issue is not just anger or envy, but a **hardness of heart toward grace**—both for his brother and, ironically, for himself.
