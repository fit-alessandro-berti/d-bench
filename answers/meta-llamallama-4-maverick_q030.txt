The portrait of love (or "charity" in the King James Version) in 1 Corinthians 13:4-7 is a rich and complex one, offering a nuanced understanding of what it means to embody love. The passage includes a striking number of negations—statements about what love is not—focusing on the absence of pride, envy, irritability, and suspicion. This approach isn't accidental; rather, it reflects a specific theological and ethical vision of what love entails and what it means to be strong in love.

1. **Negations as a Path to Positive Virtue**: By stating what love is not, Paul effectively highlights the contrary virtues that love embodies. For example, saying "charity envieth not" (v. 4) underscores the positive virtue of being content with others' successes and possessions, demonstrating a selflessness and generosity of spirit. Similarly, "is not puffed up" (v. 4) points to humility as a key characteristic of love. These negations serve to clarify the nature of love by contrasting it with its opposites, thereby illuminating its positive aspects.

2. **A Vision of Strength**: The passage proposes a vision of strength that is not about external displays of power or dominance but about inner qualities such as patience, kindness, and endurance. Love is strong not because it asserts itself or seeks its own interests at the expense of others, but because it can "suffer long" (v. 4), "beareth all things" (v. 7), and "endureth all things" (v. 7). This is a strength that is rooted in a deep commitment to others and to truth, rather than in self-aggrandizement.

3. **Countering the Corrupting Influence of Pride and Envy**: Pride and envy are corrosive forces that can undermine relationships and communities. By explicitly stating that love "envieth not" and "vaunteth not itself" (v. 4), Paul indicates that true love is not competitive or self-seeking. Instead, it operates on a different principle, one that values the well-being of others and rejoices in their successes ("rejoiceth in the truth," v. 6).

4. **Trust and Hope**: The passage also underscores a profound trust and hope in others. Love "believeth all things, hopeth all things" (v. 7), suggesting an openness and a predisposition to trust, rather than suspicion. This doesn't mean naivety but rather a willingness to give others the benefit of the doubt and to maintain a hopeful outlook, even in challenging circumstances.

5. **Theological and Ethical Implications**: Theologically, this vision of love is deeply rooted in the understanding of God's love for humanity, as exemplified in Jesus Christ. Ethically, it challenges believers to embody this love in their relationships, creating a community characterized by mutual support, humility, and a commitment to truth. It presents a countercultural model of strength, one that is not about personal achievement or status but about serving and loving others.

In summary, the portrait of love in 1 Corinthians 13:4-7 includes negations of pride, envy, irritability, and suspicion to reveal a profound and counterintuitive vision of strength. This strength is about enduring, trusting, and hoping in the face of challenges, and it is rooted in a deep commitment to the well-being of others and to living truthfully.
