• The passage points to an inner contradiction of identity and allegiance:

  - we bless God as worthy of honor, but we curse people made in God’s image.
  - If you truly see your words as coming from faithful reverence for God, you shouldn’t use the same tongue to dishonor
    His image in others.
  - In short, the same person is simultaneously professing devotion and enacting contempt.

  James says this is not a small inconsistency but a moral impossibility in a mature life, because it shows two
  competing loyalties running at once: loyalty to God in speech about Him, and loyalty to ego, resentment, or cruelty in
  speech about people.

  Why speech reveals moral disorder so quickly:

  1. Speech is the most immediate bridge between inner motive and outer action. It exposes what we most strongly feel—
     anger, pride, envy, contempt—before we have time to filter it.
  2. It requires little effort. Bad words are easy and instant; virtue in speech requires self-control.
  3. The tongue is visible and contagious. Unlike hidden actions, words can instantly harm, mislead, or corrupt
     relationships.
  4. It reflects the “inner man.” James’ point is that character is often first betrayed in language.

  So “speech reveals moral disorder quickly” because the tongue is a concentrated, low-latency signal of the heart.
