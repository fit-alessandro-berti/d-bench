# Why Enemy-Love Is Hard to Take Seriously

The command is almost impossible to hear cleanly. It has been sentimentalized into a greeting-card virtue, politicized as pacifist doctrine, and psychologized into a therapeutic exercise ("forgive for your own sake"). Each of these domesticates it. To read Matthew 5:44–45 honestly, you have to strip away what makes it easy.

## Why It Resists Us

**1. It collides with moral intuition.**
Hatred of what harms us feels righteous, and often *is* righteous in the sense that it tracks real injustice. To love an enemy seems to betray the victim, to flatten the moral difference between aggressor and aggrieved. This is the serious objection — not squeamishness, but a concern for justice.

**2. It threatens identity.**
We are partly constituted by our oppositions. Knowing who we are against tells us who we are. Loving the enemy dissolves a boundary the self relies on.

**3. It sounds strategically naïve.**
In a world where power is real, the command looks like unilateral disarmament. This is why it gets read either as otherworldly (for saints, not citizens) or as clever jiu-jitsu (shaming the oppressor into reform). Both readings let us off the hook.

## What It Is Not

- **Not sentimental.** The text doesn't say *like* your enemies or *feel warmly toward* them. The Greek *agapan* here denotes willed action — blessing, doing good, praying — not affection. You can love someone you would still fight in court, oppose in politics, or lock up to protect others.

- **Not strategic.** The grounding clause is decisive: *"that ye may be the children of your Father."* The reason given is not that it works, converts enemies, or breaks cycles of violence (though it sometimes does). The reason is **ontological conformity** — acting like the kind of Being who sends sun and rain indiscriminately.

- **Not passivity.** Blessing, doing good, and praying are all active verbs. Enemy-love is something you *do*, often against your feelings.

## What It Actually Is

A **deeper form of moral strength**, and specifically the strength to refuse the enemy's terms.

An enemy, by making himself your enemy, tries to conscript you into his logic: hate for hate, contempt for contempt. To return that currency is to let him set the exchange rate of your soul. Enemy-love is the refusal to be defined by what opposes you. It says: *my conduct is determined by who I am, not by what you do to me.*

This is why the passage grounds the command in the character of God rather than in outcomes. The sun rises on the evil because that is what suns do when the one behind them is generous by nature. The imitation is not of God's strategy but of God's **indifference to desert** when it comes to basic goods — rain, sun, the chance to be treated as human.

## The Honest Difficulty

The teaching is hard not because it is unclear but because it is clear and costly. It asks you to give an enemy something he has not earned and may not want: your refusal to treat him the way he treats you. It does not require you to trust him, protect him from consequences, or pretend the injury didn't happen. It requires that your response to evil not itself be shaped by evil.

That is why it reads as a form of strength rather than weakness. Weakness mirrors its aggressor; strength answers from a different source.
