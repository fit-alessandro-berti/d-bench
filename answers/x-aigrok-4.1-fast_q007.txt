### The Meaning of Luke 6:37
Luke 6:37 is part of Jesus' teachings in the Sermon on the Plain (Luke 6:20–49), emphasizing mercy, reciprocity, and humility in relationships. The verse isn't a blanket prohibition on *all* evaluation of right and wrong. Instead, it's a warning against a specific kind of judgment: hypocritical, self-righteous condemnation of others. Jesus pairs it with related commands like "Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven" and follows it with illustrations (e.g., Luke 6:41–42, the speck and the plank in the eye). The core idea is **reciprocity**—the measure you use on others will be used on you (Luke 6:38). If you condemn harshly without self-examination or mercy, expect the same in return.

This is **not** equivalent to saying "moral judgments never matter." That would contradict the Bible's repeated calls to ethical discernment, wisdom, and accountability. Let's break down the key differences.

### Moral Judgments *Do* Matter—They Are Essential to Godly Living
The Scriptures are replete with commands to make moral evaluations:
- **Recognize sin and evil**: "Abhor what is evil; cling to what is good" (Romans 12:9). Jesus Himself judged actions harshly, calling Pharisees "hypocrites" and "whitewashed tombs" (Matthew 23:27) while confronting sins like adultery or greed.
- **Discern truth from falsehood**: "Test everything; hold fast what is good. Abstain from every form of evil" (1 Thessalonians 5:21–22). Proverbs is a whole book on wise judgment: "The simple believes everything, but the prudent gives thought to his steps" (Proverbs 14:15).
- **Church discipline**: Matthew 18:15–17 instructs confronting sin privately, then publicly if needed, to restore the offender—not to condemn eternally, but to protect the community.
- **Fruit inspection**: "You will recognize them by their fruits" (Matthew 7:16–20), right after the "judge not" passage in the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 7:1–5). Jesus encourages evaluating character and behavior to avoid deception.

Saying "moral judgments never matter" leads to moral relativism or antinomianism (lawlessness), which the Bible rejects. Sin *is* real, harmful, and offensive to God (e.g., Romans 6:23). Without judgment, there's no repentance, justice, or holiness.

### Discernment vs. the Appetite to Condemn
The verse targets the *attitude* and *motive* behind judgment, distinguishing **discernment** (healthy, God-honoring) from **condemnation** (destructive, self-elevating). Here's the contrast:

| Aspect              | **Discernment** (Biblical Judgment) | **Appetite to Condemn** (What Jesus Warns Against) |
|---------------------|-------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|
| **Purpose**        | To seek truth, protect, guide, or restore. Aims at growth and holiness (e.g., "speaking the truth in love" – Ephesians 4:15). | To punish, exclude, or feel superior. Seeks dominance or vindication, not redemption. |
| **Motive**         | Humility and self-awareness ("remove the plank from your own eye first" – Luke 6:42). Rooted in love for God and neighbor. | Pride and hypocrisy. Ignores personal sin while magnifying others' faults (e.g., Pharisees in John 8:1–11). |
| **Scope**          | Evaluates *actions* and *patterns* objectively, often with grace. Distinguishes sinner from sin. | Passes a final, eternal verdict on the *person*. "Writes them off" without hope of forgiveness. |
| **Outcome**        | Leads to mercy, correction, and forgiveness (as in Galatians 6:1: "restore [the sinner] gently"). Builds community. | Breeds division, resentment, and reciprocal judgment (Luke 6:37–38). Destroys relationships. |
| **Biblical Example**| Nathan confronting David’s sin (2 Samuel 12): Discerns adultery/murder, but points to repentance and God’s mercy. | Pharisees condemning the adulterous woman (John 8): Lust for legalistic "gotcha," ignoring their own sin. Jesus flips it: "Let him without sin cast the first stone." |

- **Discernment in action**: Parents discern danger to guide children; believers discern false teaching to stay faithful (1 John 4:1). It's like a doctor diagnosing illness to heal, not to shame.
- **Appetite to condemn**: This is the "gotcha" mentality—gossip, cancel culture, or self-righteous outrage. It's emotional, selective (easy on self, harsh on others), and forgets we're all sinners saved by grace (Romans 3:23; Ephesians 2:8–9).

### The Balance Jesus Teaches
Luke 6:37 promotes a **both/and** approach:
- **Judge actions wisely** (discernment), but **withhold ultimate condemnation** (leave that to God, Romans 14:10–12).
- **Practice mercy first**: Forgiveness doesn't mean excusing sin; it means offering restoration as God does for us (Matthew 18:21–35).
- **Self-examination is key**: Before critiquing, ask: Am I doing this? Is my goal restoration or superiority?

In short, the verse elevates *how* we judge, not *whether* we judge. Moral discernment is vital for navigating life biblically, but the appetite to condemn poisons the soul. As James 4:11–12 warns, "There is only one lawgiver and judge... who are you to judge your neighbor?" True wisdom pairs judgment with compassion.
