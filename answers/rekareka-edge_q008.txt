The verse **1 John 4:20**—"If a man say, I love God, and hateth his brother, he is a liar: for he that loveth not his brother whom he hath seen, how can he love God whom he hath not seen?"—is a profound reflection on the separation between spiritual and physical dimensions of faith and love. It treats the affirmation of love for God despite hatred for one's neighbor as a test of genuine divine love, exposing a form of self-deception that influences our understanding of relationships and faith.

### Key Components of the Test:
1. **Contrast Between Visible and Invisible Love**:  
   The verse sets a stark contrast between love for the **visible** (physical) neighbor and love for the **invisible** (deity) God. The physical world is marked by human failings and contradictions, while the divine love is something we glimpse only through faith. This contrast exposes how humans might misinterpret or rationalize their love for the physical world, leading to injustices or blind spots toward others.

2. **Liar Diagnosis**:  
   The assertion that one who "loves God" yet "hateth his brother" is labeled a "liar" highlights the dishonesty of hiding genuine spiritual love behind outward demonstrations of faith. This diagnosis requires interpreting love not just for its outward commandments but for the internal motives and consequences of our actions.

3. **Self-Deception Exposed**:  
   Self-deception occurs when we believe we act out ofAttitude toward the invisible God when, in reality, our actions are driven by superficial tragedies or misunderstandings. The verse challenges us to look beyond outward claims of faith to discern whether your love for God truly springs from a sincere heart or from a concealed ego.

### Theological Insight:
The verse suggests that genuine divine love is not merely a belief but an **action** guided by faith. It forces believers to examine their relationships with others to ensure they reflect the love they claim to possess for God. This self-examination undermines false beliefs and fosters humility, acknowledging that true love for God and the neighbor cannot exist in isolation but requires a genuine, unapologetic commitment to both.

In essence, **1 John 4:20** is a reminder that love involves vulnerability, compassion, and integrity, all of which must be shown both in relationship and in our relationship with the invisible, eternal God. Self-deception lies in pretending to love or serve God while neglecting the real needs and challenges faced by others in the present. By confronting these dichotomies, believers are better equipped to live lives that honor both faith and humanity.
