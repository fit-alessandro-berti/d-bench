Based on the text of Luke 18:9–14, the condemnation of the Pharisee is not a single isolated error but a interconnected web of spiritual failures. However, if we must identify the *root* cause from your list, it is **pride** (specifically self-righteousness), which manifests through **comparison**, **contempt**, and the **use of virtue as self-display**.

Here is a detailed breakdown of what condemns the Pharisee and why the tax collector's posture is spiritually truer.

### 1. What Exactly Condemns the Pharisee?

Luke provides the interpretive key in **Verse 9**: Jesus speaks to those *"which trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others."* This verse isolates the two fatal flaws: **Self-Trust** and **Contempt**.

*   **Pride (The Root):** The Pharisee's fundamental error is theological anthropocentrism. He trusts in *himself* rather than in God. His prayer is not an act of worship but an act of self-congratulation. In Verse 11, the Greek suggests he prayed "with himself" or "about himself." God is present in the prayer only as a listener to the Pharisee's resume, not as the object of devotion.
*   **Comparison (The Metric):** The Pharisee does not measure his righteousness against God's holiness, but against other men's sinfulness ("I am not as other men are"). This is a relativistic righteousness. By comparing himself downward, he creates an illusion of holiness.
*   **Contempt (The Fruit):** Verse 9 explicitly states he "despised others." This is evident in Verse 11 when he singles out the tax collector ("or even as this publican"). He uses another human being's brokenness as a pedestal for his own ego. In biblical ethics, one cannot love God while despising the people God created.
*   **Virtue as Self-Display (The Method):** The Pharisee is not condemned for fasting or tithing; those were commanded by the Law. He is condemned for *weaponizing* them. He treats his obedience as a currency to purchase God's favor rather than a response to God's grace. He is performing a transaction, not a relationship.
*   **False Doctrine (The Underlying Assumption):** While not explicitly heretical in creed, his functional theology is false. He operates on the doctrine that **human merit can satisfy divine justice.** He believes he has a claim on God ("I thank thee that I am..."), whereas the tax collector knows he has no claim, only a need.

**Verdict:** The Pharisee is condemned because he seeks **self-justification**. He attempts to declare himself righteous rather than receiving righteousness from God. Because he claims he has no sin, he makes no room for grace.

### 2. Why is the Tax Collector's Posture Spiritually Truer?

The tax collector (publican) is treated as spiritually truer not because he is morally "better" in behavior (tax collectors were often viewed as traitors and thieves), but because his **spiritual posture aligns with reality.**

*   **Alignment with Truth:** The tax collector acknowledges the true state of affairs between a holy God and a fallen human. He admits, "I am a sinner." The Pharisee lives in a delusion of perfection; the tax collector lives in the truth of his condition. Truth is the prerequisite for redemption; you cannot be healed if you deny you are sick.
*   **Posture of Dependence:**
    *   *"Standing afar off":* Acknowledges unworthiness to approach the Holy.
    *   *"Would not lift up so much as his eyes":* Acknowledges shame and reverence.
    *   *"Smote upon his breast":* A physical gesture of grief and contrition (striking the heart, the seat of the will).
*   **Appeal to Mercy, Not Merit:** The Pharisee lists what he has *done*; the tax collector asks for who God *is* ("God be merciful"). The Greek word for "be merciful" (*hilaskomai*) is related to propitiation/atoning sacrifice. The tax collector is asking for a covering for his sin that he cannot provide himself.
*   **The Mechanism of Justification:** In Verse 14, the tax collector goes home "justified." In biblical theology, justification is a legal declaration of righteousness granted by God. The Pharisee sought to *earn* it; the tax collector *received* it. The tax collector is truer because he understands that righteousness is a gift, not a wage.

### Summary

The Pharisee is condemned because his **pride** closed the door to grace. By trusting in his own virtue and despising his neighbor, he placed himself outside the kingdom of God, which is built on humility and mercy.

The tax collector is justified because his **humility** opened the door to grace. His posture was truer because it reflected the actual spiritual reality: that before God, all have sinned, and the only hope for anyone is not their own record, but God's mercy.

As Jesus concludes in Verse 14: *"For every one that exalteth himself shall be abased; and he that humbleth himself shall be exalted."*
