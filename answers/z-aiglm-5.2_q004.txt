The trust asked for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is a wholehearted, comprehensive reliance on God that permeates every dimension of a person's life. It is not a segmented trust reserved for "spiritual" matters, but a foundational dependence on God’s wisdom, goodness, and sovereignty in all of life's decisions ("in all thy ways"). 

To properly understand this passage, it is vital to distinguish the "humble self-distrust" it advocates from mere anti-intellectualism. 

**Humble Self-Distrust**
Humble self-distrust is the recognition of human finitude and fallibility. It is the acknowledgment that human reasoning, while a good gift from God, is limited by finite perspective, blind spots, and the corrupting influence of pride. To "lean not unto thine own understanding" means that human intellect is not to be treated as the ultimate authority or the final arbiter of truth. 

In this view, the mind is still highly active. The book of Proverbs repeatedly commands the reader to seek wisdom, understanding, and knowledge (e.g., Proverbs 4:5, 7). Humble self-distrust involves using one's intellect to reason, plan, and observe, but submitting those conclusions to the authority of God's revelation. It is the posture of bringing one's thoughts captive to a higher wisdom. You are not abandoning your understanding; you are simply refusing to *lean* on it—to rely on it for ultimate support—because you know it can give way.

**Mere Anti-Intellectualism**
Mere anti-intellectualism, by contrast, is the blanket rejection of human reason, logic, study, and learning. It assumes that faith and intellect are at odds, suggesting that to be truly spiritual, one must "turn off" the brain or embrace ignorance. Anti-intellectualism views the pursuit of knowledge with suspicion and equates intellectual humility with a lack of critical thinking. 

If Proverbs 3:5–6 were promoting anti-intellectualism, it would contradict the broader context of the book of Proverbs, which is dedicated to cultivating shrewdness, discernment, and a disciplined mind. 

**The Distinction**
The distinction between the two lies in the role of the intellect and the nature of authority:

1.  **Role of the Mind:** Anti-intellectualism seeks to suppress or bypass the mind. Humble self-distrust engages the mind fully but subordinates it to God’s wisdom.
2.  **Nature of Authority:** Anti-intellectualism often replaces the authority of intellect with the authority of raw emotion or unchecked intuition. Humble self-distrust replaces the authority of the autonomous self (whether intellectual or emotional) with the authority of God.
3.  **Outcome:** Anti-intellectualism leads to naivety and an inability to navigate a complex world wisely. Humble self-distrust leads to the freedom of acknowledging one's limitations and receiving guidance from an infinite God ("he shall direct thy paths").

Therefore, the trust asked for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is not a call to intellectual laziness, but a call to intellectual surrender. It asks the believer to use their mind to the fullest, while maintaining the humility to admit that God's thoughts are higher than human thoughts, and that His wisdom is a far more reliable foundation than human intellect alone.
