The image is razor-sharp because of its grotesque, hyperbolic contrast: a tiny speck of sawdust (“mote”) versus an enormous wooden beam, both imagined lodged in the eye. The absurdity of a person blinded by a plank presuming to perform delicate surgery on another’s splinter creates an unforgettable, comically horrifying picture that exposes hypocrisy through visual overstatement.

The passage does not forbid moral correction—it reorders it. The final line says to *first* remove the beam from your own eye, “and then shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote out of thy brother’s eye.” Correction is still permitted, but only after honest self-examination and repentance. Hypocrisy is condemned not by outlawing fraternal help, but by demanding that it proceed from humility and clear sight, not from self-ignorant pride.
