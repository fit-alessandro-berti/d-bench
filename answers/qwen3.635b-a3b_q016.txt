While the elder brother exhibits anger, envy, a sense of wounded fairness, and pride in his obedience, his **deepest problem is a fundamental incompatibility with grace** (or, more precisely, **discomfort with mercy itself**). The other emotions are real, but they are symptoms of this root issue.

### 1. The Surface Emotions Are Present, But Derivative
- **Anger** is stated outright (v. 28), but it's a reaction, not the cause.
- **Envy** shows up in his comparison to his friends ("thou never gavest me a kid... that I might make merry with my friends").
- **Wounded fairness** is explicit: "But as soon as this thy son was come... thou hast killed for him the fatted calf."
- **Pride in obedience** is foundational to his self-narrative: "Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment."

None of these are isolated. They all flow from how he structures his relationship with his father.

### 2. The Root Issue: A Transactional vs. Relational Heart
The elder brother doesn't say, "I missed you," or "I was grieving while you were away," or "I wanted to celebrate my brother's return." He speaks entirely in the language of labor, reward, and accounting. He views his position as that of a **servant earning wages**, not a son enjoying inheritance. 

The father’s reply cuts to the core of this distortion:
> *"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine."* (v. 31)

The father isn't taking anything away from the elder; he's reminding him that his status was never conditional on performance. The elder has been working for a "kid" while already living in the master's house with full access to all the father's resources. His complaint isn't really about fairness; it's about **identity**. He measures love by visible rewards. When grace appears without proportional penance, it doesn't just surprise him; it threatens the entire framework by which he's related to his father.

### 3. Why "Discomfort with Mercy Itself" Is the Deepest Problem
In Luke 15, Jesus tells this parable in direct response to religious elites who "murmured" that Jesus welcomed and ate with sinners (v. 2). The elder brother mirrors that posture: mercy feels like a violation of justice when it's extended to someone who hasn't "paid his dues." 

His "fairness" is actually a defense of his own moral capital. If grace is free and celebratory, then his years of obedient service don't earn him special standing, nor do the younger brother's sins warrant exclusion. His inability to enter the feast isn't about principle; it's about **an inability to participate in a love that isn't earned**. He would rather stand outside in "righteous" loneliness than inside under unmerited joy.

### Conclusion
While anger, envy, wounded fairness, and pride in obedience are all faithfully depicted, they are surface manifestations. **The elder brother's deepest problem is a relationship lived by merit.** He cannot breathe in an economy of grace because it invalidates his entire scorekeeping identity. His tragedy isn't that the father is unjust; it's that he doesn't realize he's already fully loved, fully included, and fully an heir. The parable isn't just about the father's mercy; it's a mirror held up to those who would rather be "right" than reconcile, and who find grace more unsettling than sin.
