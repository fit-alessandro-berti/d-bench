**What makes the image so sharp?**  
The passage uses a vivid **contrast** between two physical objects:

| Element | Symbolic meaning |
|---------|-----------------|
| **Mote** (tiny speck) | A minor, easily removable fault or “small sin.” |
| **Beam** (large log) | A major, glaring fault that is far more obvious to the observer. |

By juxtaposing a speck with a beam, the writer creates a **sharp visual metaphor**: the observer’s own massive flaw dwarfs the tiny flaw they’re trying to correct in someone else. The mental picture is striking because it forces the reader to see the absurdity of trying to “pull out a mote” while a “beam” blocks their own vision.

**How the passage criticizes hypocrisy without banning correction**

1. **Conditional correction** – The text does not say “never correct others.” It says the correction must first address one’s own fault.  
   - *“First cast out the beam out of thine own eye; and then shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote out of thy brother’s eye.”*  
   This implies a **prerequisite**: self‑examination and personal repentance before offering guidance.

2. **Self‑awareness as a prerequisite** – The “beam” represents a serious, perhaps unnoticed, sin. By demanding that the critic remove it first, the passage forces the critic to **recognize** that their judgment is compromised. Only after that recognition can they speak with *integrity* and *accuracy*.

3. **Moral consistency** – The criticism is aimed at **hypocrisy**, i.e., judging others while ignoring one’s own shortcomings. The passage therefore condemns **double standards**, not the act of moral admonition itself.

4. **Restoring clear vision** – Once the beam is removed, the critic’s “vision” (understanding, discernment) is restored, allowing them to **see the mote** (the smaller fault) more clearly and to address it **fairly**. The passage thus links personal humility with effective, constructive correction.

5. **Implicit encouragement to help** – By framing correction as a two‑step process (self‑correction → clear vision → correct others), the text **encourages** genuine, compassionate moral guidance rather than dismissive condemnation.

**In short:** The image is sharp because it makes the absurdity of a tiny speck being corrected while a massive beam blinds the critic obvious. The passage condemns hypocrisy by insisting that anyone who wishes to correct another must first examine and remove their own larger fault, thereby ensuring that correction is rooted in humility, self‑knowledge, and genuine moral clarity rather than in self‑righteousness.
