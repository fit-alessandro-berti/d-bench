## The Core Distinction

The verse pinpoints the problem precisely with the phrase **"to be seen of them."** That little clause carries the entire moral weight. The danger isn't that the alms *are* seen—it's that being seen is the *purpose*. The Greek here (*theathēnai*) speaks to intention, the aim toward which the act is directed.

So the answer is: **the craving to be seen, not visibility itself.**

## Why Visibility Alone Can't Be the Problem

Consider that just one chapter earlier, in the same Sermon on the Mount, Jesus says:

> "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven." (Matthew 5:16)

Here visibility is *commanded*. People are supposed to see. The works are public. If mere visibility were the danger, these two passages would contradict each other.

The reconciliation lies in the **terminus**—where the seeing leads:

- **Matthew 5:16** — they see, and glory flows *to God*
- **Matthew 6:1** — they see, and glory flows *to me*

Same visible act. Opposite spiritual direction.

## What Makes the Craving Dangerous

The danger is a kind of **theft and substitution**:

1. **It redirects the worship.** The act of goodness was meant to point beyond yourself. When you crave the seeing, you intercept the gratitude that belonged to God.

2. **It collects the reward immediately.** The verse says you "have no reward of your Father." Jesus later adds that such people "have their reward" (6:2)—already, in full. The applause *is* the payment. There's nothing left owed. The transaction is closed and small.

3. **It hollows the act from within.** The deed looks identical from outside, but its root has rotted. The poor person still gets fed—but the giver has gained nothing eternal, having traded a great reward for a cheap one.

## The Practical Difficulty

This is harder than it sounds, because you cannot simply *will* yourself not to enjoy being noticed. The motive often hides even from the person who has it. This is why the surrounding verses recommend **concealment as a discipline**—the right hand not knowing what the left hand does (6:3). Hiddenness isn't required because visibility is evil; it's a *training tool* to starve the craving and expose to yourself what you truly want.

## Summary

| | Visibility | Craving to be seen |
|---|---|---|
| Morally neutral? | Yes | No |
| Condemned here? | No | Yes |
| The real target | — | The motive |

The verse isn't asking you to hide your goodness in shame. It's asking a sharper question: **When the watching eyes are removed, does your goodness remain?** If it evaporates without an audience, then it was never goodness aimed at God—it was performance aimed at people.
