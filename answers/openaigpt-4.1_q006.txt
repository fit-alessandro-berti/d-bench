This is a thoughtful question that touches on Christian theology, psychology, and even issues of culture and identity.

### **What kind of "self" is being denied?**

In Mark 8:34–35, Jesus speaks about denying oneself, taking up one’s cross, and following him. The "self" being denied here is not one's individuality, personality, or God-given identity, but rather the self-centered, self-preserving impulses—the "ego" or "flesh"—that want their own way, comfort, or agenda apart from God.

**Key points in context:**
- **Denial of the ego:** Jesus is inviting people to surrender their claims to autonomy, control, self-interest, and self-glorification.
- **Self-will vs. God’s will:** In essence, this is a call to subordinate one’s own desires and ambitions to God’s purposes ("not my will but yours be done").
- **The "old self":** In the broader New Testament context, this is sometimes called the "old self" or "old man" (see Ephesians 4:22–24), the part of us shaped by sin and separation from God.
- **Taking up one’s cross:** In Jesus’ day, the cross was a death sentence—so this metaphor suggests a willingness to go even to the point of suffering or death rather than clinging to self-protection or comfort.

### **Why is this liberating to some and offensive to others?**

#### **Why some find this liberating:**
- **Freedom from self-obsession:** Many people feel trapped by their own anxieties, selfish desires, or ego-driven pursuits. The call to lose oneself in something greater—God’s love and purpose—feels freeing.
- **True life found in surrender:** Jesus promises that in *losing* the self-centered life ("whoever shall lose his life for my sake and the gospel’s"), we find a deeper, truer life. There’s joy and meaning in self-giving love and obedience to God.
- **Release from the burden of self-salvation:** Christianity teaches that we don’t have to save ourselves, prove our worth, or create our own identity—God does this for us.

#### **Why some find this offensive:**
- **Counter to individualism:** In many modern cultures, especially Western ones, autonomy, self-expression, and self-actualization are the highest values. The idea of denying oneself can sound repressive and contrary to the pursuit of happiness.
- **Fear of losing freedom or identity:** Some interpret "self-denial" as requiring conformity, the erasure of personality, or blind obedience—which can feel dangerous or unjust.
- **Threat to the ego:** Our natural instinct is to preserve and assert ourselves, so surrendering control feels threatening.

### **Summary Table**

| Aspect                | Liberating                              | Offensive                                  |
|-----------------------|-----------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| Meaning of self       | Ego, sinful nature, false self           | True self, individuality, uniqueness       |
| Viewpoint             | Freedom to find true life in Christ      | Loss of autonomy/self-sovereignty           |
| Emotional reaction    | Joy, release, purpose                   | Fear, anger, suspicion                     |

### **In short:**
Jesus calls people to deny the self that clings to control, pride, and self-centeredness so that they can find real life in self-giving love and union with God. What feels liberating or offensive depends on *which* "self" we think is being denied, and whether we trust the One making the call.
