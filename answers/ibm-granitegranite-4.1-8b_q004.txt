In Proverbs 3:5–6, the kind of trust being urged is a **humble, wholehearted trust in God**, which involves both a reliance on divine wisdom and a conscious acknowledgment of God in every aspect of life. This trust is distinguished from mere anti-intellectualism in several key ways:

1. **Humble Self-Distrust**:  
   - **Giving Up One's Own Understanding**: The verse instructs the believer to "lean not unto thine own understanding." This is not a call to ignore the intellect or to reject rational thought outright. Instead, it is a call to **submit one’s plans and insights to God’s higher wisdom**. The "self-distrust" here is *humble*, acknowledging human limitations and the fallibility of personal understanding, especially in matters of ultimate significance.
   - **Heart-Centered Trust**: The instruction to "trust in the Lord with all thine heart" emphasizes that the trust springs from the innermost being, not merely the mind. It is a trust that involves the emotional, willful, and spiritual core, aligning one’s affections and desires with God’s purposes.

2. **Acknowledge Him in All Ways**:  
   - **Ongoing Recognition**: The phrase "in all thy ways acknowledge him" implies a continual, pervasive recognition of God’s presence and sovereignty. This is an active, conscious posture of dependence rather than a passive reliance on luck or fate. It means acknowledging God not just in overt religious practices but in everyday decisions, challenges, and opportunities.
   - **Direction from Above**: By acknowledging God in all aspects of life, the believer positions themselves to receive divine guidance, allowing God to "direct thy paths." This is not blind faith; it is an engaged trust that seeks God’s direction while remaining open to His leading in practical matters.

3. **Contrast With Anti-Intellectualism**:  
   - **Respect for Knowledge**: Anti-intellectualism often rejects or disparages intellectual inquiry, education, and the use of reason. In contrast, the trust advocated in Proverbs 3:5–6 **respects and integrates** human intelligence—recognizing it as a gift—but submits it to divine wisdom. The verse does not condemn thinking or problem-solving; it calls for aligning those processes with God’s truth.
   - **Integration of Faith and Reason**: The passage suggests a **harmonious integration**: one uses reason to understand problems, seeks God’s perspective to interpret those solutions, and acts in faith that God will guide the outcome. This is a balanced approach that honors both human capability and divine authority.
   - **Purposeful Distrust of Self-Reliance**: The distinction lies in the purpose of the distrust. In humble self-distrust, the goal is to **prevent arrogance and ego** from leading one astray, ensuring that one’s actions are aligned with a higher moral and spiritual framework. Anti-intellectualism, by contrast, often stems from fear or prejudice toward intellectual effort, dismissing it as irrelevant or harmful without a genuine theological or spiritual basis.

In summary, the trust called for in this proverb is **a deep, sincere, and active reliance on God that encompasses all areas of life**, while responsibly using human understanding under God’s guidance. It is a humility that acknowledges human limits without abandoning rational engagement, thus fostering a mature, integrated faith that is both intellectual and spiritual.
