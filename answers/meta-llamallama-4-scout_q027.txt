The Pharisee's condemnation in this parable can be attributed to a combination of factors, primarily **pride**, **comparison**, and **the use of virtue as self-display**. Let's break it down:

1. **Pride**: The Pharisee's prayer is essentially a self-congratulatory speech, where he exalts himself by highlighting his own virtues and moral superiority. He's not genuinely praying or seeking God's presence; instead, he's affirming his own self-righteousness.
2. **Comparison**: By saying "I am not as other men are," the Pharisee is engaging in a damaging comparison. He's measuring himself against others, which creates a false sense of security and moral superiority. This kind of comparison also fosters contempt for others, which is another condemnable aspect of his behavior.
3. **Contempt**: The Pharisee's tone is not only self-aggrandizing but also derogatory towards others, particularly the publican (tax collector). He looks down on the tax collector, implying that he's better than him.
4. **The use of virtue as self-display**: The Pharisee openly brags about his fasting and tithing, using these spiritual practices as a means to showcase his own righteousness. This attitude undermines the true purpose of these practices, which is to cultivate humility, obedience, and a deeper relationship with God.

On the other hand, the tax collector's posture is treated as spiritually truer for several reasons:

* **Humility**: The tax collector stands "afar off," indicating a sense of distance and unworthiness. He doesn't presume to approach God with confidence, recognizing his own sinfulness.
* **Sincerity**: The tax collector's prayer is a genuine cry for mercy, acknowledging his sinfulness and need for God's compassion. He's not trying to impress God or others; he's simply pouring out his heart.
* **Self-awareness**: The tax collector has a realistic understanding of himself, recognizing his own sinfulness and limitations. He doesn't compare himself to others or try to elevate himself; instead, he focuses on his own relationship with God.

In contrast to the Pharisee's self-focused, self-congratulatory prayer, the tax collector's posture embodies the biblical principle of humility and recognition of one's dependence on God's mercy (cf. Psalm 51:1-2, 1 John 1:9). Jesus' teaching highlights the importance of approaching God with a humble, contrite heart, rather than relying on external displays of righteousness or comparisons with others.
