The elder brother’s deepest problem is **alienation from the father’s heart**—even though he has been physically “at home” the whole time.

His anger, envy, sense of unfairness, and pride in obedience are all real, but they are symptoms of something deeper: he does not understand his relationship to the father as sonship and grace. He understands it as **service, merit, and reward**.

He says:

> “Lo, these many years do I serve thee…”

That sounds less like a son rejoicing in his father’s house and more like a hired servant keeping account. He sees his obedience as something that should have earned him special treatment:

> “Neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment: and yet thou never gavest me…”

So his problem is not simply that he is angry. His anger reveals that he believes the father’s generosity to the younger son somehow wrongs him. Mercy feels offensive to him because he has built his identity on being the deserving one.

Notice also that he refuses to call the younger son “my brother”:

> “This thy son…”

But the father gently corrects him:

> “This thy brother was dead, and is alive again…”

That is crucial. The elder brother’s heart is out of step with the father’s joy. The father sees resurrection, restoration, and family. The elder brother sees insult, injustice, and competition.

So, among the options, the deepest issue is not merely anger, envy, wounded fairness, or even pride in obedience—though all are present. The deepest problem is **a graceless heart that cannot rejoice in mercy because it does not truly know the father’s grace for itself**.

He is obedient, but not joyful. Near the father, but not sharing the father’s heart. In the house, but still estranged.

In context, Jesus is speaking to Pharisees and scribes who grumbled:

> “This man receiveth sinners, and eateth with them” — Luke 15:2

The elder brother represents that religious resentment: a person may be outwardly moral and yet inwardly resistant to grace.

So the answer is: **his deepest problem is discomfort with mercy, rooted in self-righteous, transactional sonship.** He cannot celebrate the lost being found because he has never grasped that everything in the father’s house is already gift.
