The Pharisee’s condemnation is **not a single flaw but an integrated syndrome** in which several vices reinforce one another. If we must identify the root, it is **self-righteous pride**—a fundamental misplacement of trust that then blossoms into comparison, contempt, and the use of religion as self-display. The tax collector’s posture is “truer” because it corresponds to the actual structure of reality before God: human unworthiness meeting divine mercy.

Here is a breakdown of how the text presents this.

---

### 1. What Condemns the Pharisee

**A. The Root: Trust in Self (Luke 18:9)**
The parable’s introduction is the interpretive key. Jesus speaks it against those who **“trusted in themselves that they were righteous”** (*pepoithotas eph’ heautois*). The Pharisee’s error is first and foremost **osteriological**: he treats his own moral performance as the ground of his acceptance before God. His fasting and tithing are facts, not fictions; the falsehood is the confidence he places in them. He does not need an advocate because he believes he *is* his own advocate.

**B. The Horizontal Fruit: Comparison and Contempt**
Self-trust immediately generates a social geometry of superiority. The Pharisee prays **“with himself”** (*pros heauton*—a chilling phrase suggesting he is really talking to an audience of one, and that audience is himself). He surveys the moral landscape and locates others—**“extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even as this publican”**—as the foil against which his own rectitude shines. 

The opening verse explicitly says these people **“despised others”** (*exouthenountas*). Contempt is not an accidental byproduct of his virtue; it is the necessary psychological accompaniment of self-justification. If my righteousness is measured by my distance from you, I am spiritually invested in your vilification.

**C. The Performative Mode: Virtue as Self-Display**
The Pharisee’s prayer is a **catalogue of achievements** masquerading as thanksgiving. True gratitude says, “Thank you, God, for your grace toward me, a sinner.” The Pharisee says, “Thank you that I am not like them.” The syntax reveals that the subject of the sentence is ultimately *himself*. Religion here has become a stage for the self; the temple is a mirror.

So the answer to your list is: **all of the above, but hierarchically ordered.** Pride (self-trust) is the root; comparison and contempt are the branches; self-display is the fruit. False doctrine is present only insofar as he misunderstands how a human being stands justified before God.

---

### 2. Why the Tax Collector’s Posture Is “Truer”

The tax collector does not simply offer a *better* prayer; he adopts a **posture that corresponds to the truth of the human situation before the Holy One**.

**A. His Body Preaches Theology**
- He stands **“afar off”**: conscious of unworthiness to draw near.
- He **will not lift his eyes to heaven**: a sign of shame and creaturely humility.
- He **beats his breast**: the gesture of one who identifies himself as the problem, not the solution.

These are not theatrical props. In the ancient world, they are the embodied grammar of repentance. His external posture matches his internal state.

**B. His Prayer Is God-Directed and Mercy-Centered**
Unlike the Pharisee, who speaks *pros heauton* (to himself), the tax collector speaks to God: **“God, be merciful to me, the sinner.”** Not “a sinner” in the abstract, but “*the* sinner”—as if he were the definitive case. He makes no appeal to his merits, no comparison to worse offenders, and no resume of compensatory good deeds.

The word translated **“be merciful”** (*hilasthēti*) is deeply resonant: it evokes the mercy seat (*hilastērion*) of the ark, the place of atonement. He is asking to be covered by a sacrifice that is not himself. He looks entirely outside his own moral capital.

**C. Justification as Divine Verdict**
Jesus concludes with a forensic shock: **“I tell you, this man went down to his house justified rather than the other.”** (*Dedikaiōmenos*). The tax collector receives a status he did not earn, while the Pharisee—who came to the temple to certify an already-assumed status—leaves without it.

---

### Summary

The Pharisee is condemned because he has turned religion into an **engine of self-aggrandizement**. He gets the facts of ethics right (tithing, fasting) but the grammar of salvation backwards: he offers God his virtue as a payment rather than receiving righteousness as a gift. The tax collector’s posture is truer because it acknowledges the one thing necessary: **before God, the only acceptable starting point is radical need**. The kingdom is not given to those who demonstrate they have climbed high enough; it is given to those who know they are too low to climb at all, and who cry out for mercy.
