This passage from the Sermon on the Mount suggests that where we choose to store our treasures - on earth or in heaven - both reveals and shapes the focus and desires of our heart. 

In saying "where your treasure is, there will your heart be also", Jesus is indicating that what we value and prioritize shows the true state of our heart. If we focus on accumulating earthly possessions and wealth, it demonstrates that our heart is set on worldly things rather than on God. Our treasure is not just what we own, but what we love, think about, and pursue. In this sense, our treasure reveals the inner workings and allegiances of our heart.

At the same time, Jesus seems to be warning that what we treasure will also train or direct our heart over time. By instructing his listeners to store up heavenly rather than earthly treasures, he is urging them to consciously orient their lives around eternal priorities. The implication is that the more we invest our resources, efforts and affections in God's purposes, the more our heart will be molded and drawn towards God. What we give attention to and value shapes the default direction of our desires.

So possessions, attention and love are intertwined. What we devote our resources and efforts to both exposes and reinforces what we really love and are devoted to at our core. Jesus is calling his followers to align their earthly lives - including their finances, goals and focus - with the lasting treasures of knowing and serving God. In doing so, their hearts will increasingly find joy and fulfillment in God rather than material things.

In summary, this passage teaches that the orientation of our lives, as seen in how we use our possessions and what we focus on, has the dual effect of revealing the current state of our heart's affections while also training our heart over time to love what we are pursuing. Jesus urges singleness of heart in devotion to God, aligning the practical outworking of life with the eternal inward treasures of knowing Him.
