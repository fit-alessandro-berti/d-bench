The passage is not criticizing worship because there is “too much” of it and “too little” justice, as though the problem could be solved by adjusting the proportions. It is criticizing worship that has become a substitute for justice—and perhaps a shield against repentance.

The surrounding context describes people who:

- sell the poor and needy for profit (Amos 2:6; 8:4–6),
- trample the poor and impose burdens on them (5:11),
- accept bribes and deprive the innocent of justice (5:12),
- yet gather for feasts, sacrifices, assemblies, and music.

Their worship presents them as loyal to God while their economic and legal practices deny God’s character. The ritual is therefore not merely incomplete; it is morally contradictory. They are asking God to receive offerings from hands that oppress the people whom God commands them to protect.

That is why “balance” is inadequate. Justice is not one religious duty among many that can be compensated for by more sacrifice or better music. It is evidence of whether their worship is genuine at all. The issue is not that they need to add a little righteousness to their liturgy, but that their liturgy has become a form of religious fraud.

The fraud has at least two dimensions:

1. **Trying to manage or bribe God:** They assume that feasts and offerings can secure divine favor while they continue unjustly.  
2. **Using worship to legitimize themselves:** Public religious performance creates the appearance of faithfulness and helps conceal exploitation.

Hence the deliberately extreme language—“I hate,” “I despise,” “I will not accept,” “take away.” The worship is aesthetically impressive but ethically false: the songs are “noise” because the lives of the worshipers contradict what they sing.

“Let justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like a mighty stream” does not mean that occasional acts of charity should be added to the existing system. It calls for justice to become the continuous, public character of the community. Amos is not replacing worship with ethics; he is exposing worship without justice as a denial of God. True worship cannot be used to evade the moral claims of the God being worshiped.
