

The elder brother’s deepest trouble is not merely his momentary flash of anger; it is the lingering sense that his faithful, obedient life has been treated unfairly while the wayward brother is lavishly rewarded. He voices this when he says, “All these years I have served you and never disobeyed your command, yet you never gave me even a young goat so I could celebrate with my friends.” His resentment stems from a wounded sense of fairness — the belief that his steady, righteous service deserves recognition and that the father’s mercy toward the prodigal is an injustice. This feeling of being slighted fuels his anger, envy, and refusal to join the feast, making the wounded‑fairness (or resentment) the core problem beneath the surface emotions.
