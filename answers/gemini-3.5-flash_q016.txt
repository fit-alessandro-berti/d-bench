While all of the issues listed—anger, envy, wounded fairness, pride in obedience, and discomfort with mercy—are vividly present in the elder brother, they are symptoms of a deeper, systemic root. 

The elder brother’s deepest problem is **a transactional, slave-like relationship with his father, which blinds him to the reality of grace and sonship.** 

He is physically in the father’s house, but relationally, he is just as lost as his younger brother was in the "far country." He is "lost in the house."

Here is how his various symptoms stem from this single, deepest problem:

### 1. The Slave Mentality (Pride in Obedience & Wounded Fairness)
When the elder brother defends himself, his language betrays how he views his relationship with his father: 
> *“Lo, these many years do I **serve** [Greek: douleuo, meaning "to serve as a slave"] thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment...”* (v. 29)

He does not see himself as a son who is an heir to the estate; he sees himself as a dutiful employee or a slave. Because he views his relationship with his father as a contract based on wages and performance, he believes love and favor must be *earned*. 
* **His pride in obedience** is his resume. 
* **His wounded fairness** is the logical result of a transactional mindset: if salvation/favor is based on merit, then rewarding the prodigal is a gross injustice.

### 2. Discomfort with Mercy
Because the elder brother’s entire identity is built on his own moral performance, **unmerited mercy is deeply threatening to him.** If the father can simply forgive the prodigal and throw him a feast for free, then the elder brother's years of grueling, perfect obedience are rendered unnecessary as a means of earning the father's love. To accept mercy for his brother is to admit that he, too, is dependent on grace rather than his own achievements. 

### 3. Alienation from the Father’s Heart (Envy & Anger)
The elder brother complains: *“thou never gavest me a kid, that I might make merry with my friends”* (v. 29). 
The father’s response is heartbreakingly beautiful: *“Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine”* (v. 31).

This exchange reveals that the elder brother did not actually value his father’s presence. He wanted a "kid" (a young goat) to go throw a party *away* from his father, with his own friends. He was working for the inheritance, not out of love for the giver. He is envious of the prodigal’s party because he has been begrudgingly enduring his duty rather than enjoying his father. 

### 4. The Rejection of Brotherhood
Because he does not understand his own sonship, he cannot understand brotherhood. He refers to his brother to the father as *"this thy son"* (v. 30)—refusing to claim him. The father gently corrects him in the final verse: *"this **thy brother** was dead, and is alive again."* 

### Conclusion
In the context of Luke 15, Jesus is addressing the Pharisees and scribes who were muttering because Jesus "receives sinners and eats with them" (Luke 15:1-2). 

The elder brother's deepest problem is **the tragedy of self-righteousness**. He represents those who believe they are close to God because of their external compliance, but who are utterly estranged from God’s heart. His problem is not that he was bad, but that he was "good" for all the wrong reasons. He wanted a master to pay him, not a father to love him—and therefore, he could not rejoice when a dead brother came home.
