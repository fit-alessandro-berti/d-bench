Enemy-love, as described in Matthew 5:44–45, poses a profound challenge for many individuals and societies. Understanding why it is difficult to take seriously requires unpacking the emotional, psychological, and social dimensions of this commandment.

1. **Sentimental vs. Authentic Love**: 
   - At first glance, enemy-love might appear sentimental; it challenges our instinctual reactions to negativity and harm. Many people associate love with kindness and warmth, qualities that feel at odds with the presence of enmity or betrayal. This dissonance can lead to trivializing the concept, making it seem unrealistic or overly idealistic in the face of genuine grievances or conflicts.

2. **Strategic Considerations**:
   - Some may view enemy-love through a strategic lens, considering it a tactic for self-preservation or social harmony. Forgiving or loving one's enemies can be perceived as a way to defuse conflict or prevent retaliation—not an authentic response to wrongdoing. This pragmatic approach, however, can undermine the deeper moral intention behind the teaching, reducing it to a bypass of deeper emotional work.

3. **Moral Strength**:
   - The practice of enemy-love can be seen as a higher form of moral strength. It requires deep emotional labor and resilience, as it demands overcoming feelings of anger, resentment, and fear. This deeper form of love acknowledges the humanity of the other, regardless of their actions, which can be an arduous path for many. It challenges individuals to transcend their instincts and societal norms that often endorse retribution or detachment. In this sense, enemy-love reflects a profound commitment to empathy and understanding, which necessitates a strength that is not easily developed or sustained.

Ultimately, enemy-love represents a complex intersection of emotional, strategic, and moral dimensions, making it a rich but challenging concept to embody in real life. It requires individuals to confront their woundedness and the often painful realities of human relationships, pushing against primal instincts for protection and retribution in favor of a more profound, transformative love.
