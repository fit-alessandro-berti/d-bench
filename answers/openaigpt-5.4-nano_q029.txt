The passage requires a **humble, restrained moral posture** toward the fallen: you correct, but you do so as one who recognizes their own vulnerability before God.

### What kind of posture is required?
“Ye which are spiritual” are not to respond with **superiority, anger, or moral swagger**. Instead, they are to **restore** the person—meaning the goal is healing and reconciliation, not punishment or humiliation—and to do it **“in the spirit of meekness.”**  
So the posture is:

- **Meekness** (gentleness, teachability, strength under control)
- **Restoration** (aimed at bringing the person back, not just exposing failure)
- **Spiritual awareness** rather than self-righteousness

### Why are restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion tied together?
They’re linked because **the “how” of correction depends on the “who” doing the correcting**.

- **Restoration** sets the purpose: if you truly want to restore, you won’t speak like a judge or a critic; you’ll speak like someone trying to help.
- **Meekness** is the necessary spirit: without it, correction turns into condemnation and drives the person away.
- **Self-suspicion** (“considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted”) provides the motive and safeguard: it protects the corrector from thinking, *“I would never be caught in that.”*  

In other words, Paul keeps these together because **self-distrust produces compassion**, and **compassion produces gentleness**, which in turn aims at **restoration** rather than retaliation.

So the moral posture is: *“I can speak because I’m spiritual, but I’ll speak meekly because I know I could fall too.”*
