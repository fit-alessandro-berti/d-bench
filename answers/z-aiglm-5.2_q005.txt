The command to love one’s enemies in Matthew 5:44–45 is one of the most radical and counterintuitive ethical demands in human history. To understand why it is so difficult to take seriously—and to categorize whether it is sentimental, strategic, or a deeper moral strength—we must examine human nature, cultural conditioning, and the specific mechanics of Jesus's teaching.

### Why Enemy-Love is Difficult to Take Seriously

**1. Biological and Psychological Wiring**
At a primal level, human beings are wired for self-preservation. When we are threatened, our biology dictates a "fight or flight" response. Retaliation against those who harm us is a deeply ingrained survival mechanism. To love a persecutor feels like a betrayal of our own survival instincts, making the command seem practically absurd to our nervous systems.

**2. The Demand for Retributive Justice**
Most human societies—ancient and modern—are built on the foundation of retributive justice: the idea that wrongs must be punished to restore balance. The prevailing human morality is reciprocal: "an eye for an eye" or "scratch my back, and I'll scratch yours." Jesus commands a complete break from reciprocity. Society views enemy-love as a failure of justice, a refusal to hold evildoers accountable, or even a form of enabling abuse.

**3. The Difficulty of the Action Itself**
The command is not just to feel warmly toward an enemy, but to *act*: to bless, do good, and pray. This requires immense emotional and psychological labor. It demands that a victim suppress their legitimate desire for vindication and instead expend energy seeking the well-being of their abuser. To many, this feels less like virtue and more like self-destruction.

### Sentimental, Strategic, or Deeper Moral Strength?

**Is it Sentimental?**
No. Sentimentality implies a naive, soft-heartedness that ignores the harsh realities of evil. It relies on warm feelings and a fuzzy optimism that "deep down, everyone is good." Jesus’s command is not sentimental. He does not ask us to *like* our enemies, nor does he ask us to ignore the reality of their evil. The text explicitly acknowledges that enemies hate, curse, persecute, and exploit. Enemy-love, as defined here, is not a feeling; it is a grueling, costly choice made in the face of real malice.

**Is it Strategic?**
It can appear strategic, and history shows it often works as a strategy. Think of Mahatma Gandhi or Martin Luther King Jr., who utilized nonviolent resistance. Strategic enemy-love seeks to expose the moral bankruptcy of the oppressor, break cycles of violence, and shame the enemy into changing their behavior. While Jesus's teaching certainly has these practical effects (loving an enemy often disarms them), reducing it to a mere strategy falls short of the text. If it were only strategic, it would be abandoned the moment it ceased to be effective. 

**Is it a Deeper Form of Moral Strength?**
Yes. Jesus frames enemy-love as the ultimate proof of spiritual maturity and moral strength. He says, "That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven." In ancient thought, to be a "child" of someone meant to bear their likeness and share their character. Jesus points to the impartiality of God, who sustains the lives of both the just and the unjust through the sun and the rain. 

Enemy-love as moral strength requires several profound things:
*   **Self-Mastery:** It takes far more strength to absorb a blow and refuse to strike back than it does to retaliate. Retaliation is instinctive; enemy-love requires the mastery of one's own ego and rage.
*   **Breaking the Cycle of Mimetic Violence:** Retaliation ensures that violence and hatred continue indefinitely. By refusing to mirror the enemy's hatred, the cycle is broken. The moral strength lies in refusing to become the very thing you are fighting against.
*   **Participation in Divine Grace:** Grace is unmerited favor. To love an enemy is to extend grace to someone who deserves wrath. It elevates the moral actor from the level of a victim reacting to circumstances to a sovereign agent who, like God, chooses to give life even to those who do not deserve it.

### Conclusion
Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it demands we surrender our most basic rights—the right to vengeance, the right to self-justification, and the right to be understood. It is neither the weak sentimentality of the naive nor the cold calculus of the strategist. Rather, it is a profound expression of moral strength. It is the arduous, courageous task of refusing to let the hatred of an enemy dictate one's own soul, choosing instead to reflect the impartial, life-giving nature of God.
