This is a thoughtful question that touches on the heart of Isaiah’s message and of much biblical ethics.

Let’s break down the passage:

> “Wash you, make you clean;  
> put away the evil of your doings from before mine eyes;  
> cease to do evil;  
> Learn to do well;  
> seek judgment, relieve the oppressed,  
> judge the fatherless, plead for the widow.”

The progression is:

1. **Cleansing / Removing evil** — This is about stopping wrongdoing, getting rid of what’s harmful or unjust.
2. **Cease to do evil** — This is negative ethics: don’t harm others.
3. **Learn to do well** — This shifts to positive ethics: actively pursue what is good.
4. **Specific acts of justice** — The passage then names concrete acts: seek justice, help the oppressed, defend the vulnerable.

**Why does it make this shift?**

The move from “cease to do evil” to “learn to do well” suggests that goodness is not just the absence of bad, but also the presence of good. It’s not enough to simply not harm others; true moral and spiritual health involves actively loving and serving others. The text implies:

- **Restraint alone is insufficient.** Just not committing evil doesn’t make you righteous or holy. You could be passive, neutral, or indifferent.
- **Goodness is an active pursuit.** “Learn to do well” means cultivate virtues, habits, and actions that are positive and constructive.
- **Justice and compassion are central.** The specifics that follow—judging the fatherless, pleading for the widow—show that “doing well” involves standing up for the vulnerable and working for justice.

**What does this say about the nature of goodness?**

It suggests that **goodness is not just self-restraint but a practiced form of love and justice**. It is relational and active. True righteousness involves loving your neighbor as yourself, seeking their well-being, and working to repair and restore what is broken. In this view, morality is not just about what you don’t do, but about what you do for others.

In summary:  
The passage teaches that morality is twofold: we must stop harming others, but we must also actively love and serve them. Goodness is therefore not just the absence of evil, but the presence of love and justice in action.
