According to Matthew 6:1, the moral danger of public goodness lies fundamentally in **the craving to be seen**, not in visibility itself. 

Here is a breakdown of why the motive—the craving for attention—is the true danger, and how we know visibility is not the inherent problem:

### 1. The Problem is the Motive ("To be seen")
The crucial phrase in this verse is not just "before men," but *"to be seen of them."* In the original Greek, the word used for "to be seen" is *theathēnai*, which is the root word for "theater." Jesus is condemning the act of turning righteousness into a theatrical performance. 

The danger is that the craving for human approval corrupts the action at its root. When the primary goal of an act of charity is to elevate one's own reputation, the action ceases to be about the needs of the recipient and becomes entirely about the ego of the giver. 

### 2. Visibility Itself is Not the Problem
If visibility were the moral problem, Jesus would be commanding His followers to hide all good deeds at all times. However, just a few verses earlier in the same Sermon on the Mount, Jesus explicitly commands the opposite: 
> *"Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."* (Matthew 5:16)

Therefore, goodness can be visible and public. The determining factor is **who gets the glory** and **what the actor is craving**. 
* In Matthew 5:16, public visibility is used as a prism to direct attention to God.
* In Matthew 6:1, public visibility is used as a mirror to reflect attention back onto the self.

### 3. The Danger of Misplaced Worship
The moral danger of craving to be seen is that it is a form of idolatry. Jesus points out that we have a choice of "audiences": we can perform for "men" or we can perform for our "Father which is in heaven." When a person craves the applause of the crowd, they are trading their divine reward for a cheap, earthly substitute. Jesus says they "have no reward" from God because they were already paid in full by the people who saw them. 

### 4. The Illusion of Righteousness
Finally, public goodness driven by the craving to be seen is morally dangerous because it creates an illusion of virtue. It allows a person to feel righteous while harboring the exact opposite disposition of God (who is humble and self-giving). It turns the poor, the marginalized, or the needy into mere "props" for the giver's vanity.

**Summary:**
Visibility is neutral; the craving is toxic. What makes public goodness morally dangerous in Matthew 6:1 is that the desire to be admired by others hollows out the virtue of the act, replacing genuine love with self-exaltation, and swapping the eternal reward of God for the temporary, fading applause of men.
