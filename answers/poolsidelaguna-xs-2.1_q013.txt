The sharp rejection of worship in Amos 5:21–24 is not merely about ritual imbalance but exposes a profound **moral and spiritual fraud**: **hypocrisy**. The Israelites were engaging in elaborate religious rituals—feast days, burnt offerings, and music—to maintain a veneer of piety while simultaneously perpetuating systemic injustice, especially against the poor and marginalized. Amos confronts them with the reality that their worship was empty because it lacked moral integrity.

### Why Such a Sharp Rejection?
1. **Hypocrisy of Empty Rituals**:  
   The people of Israel had developed a religion that prioritized external observance over internal righteousness. They offered sacrifices, celebrated festivals, and even played music in temples, but their lives were marked by exploitation, corruption, and oppression. Amos accuses them of seeking God’s favor while actively violating His standards of justice (Amos 5:24). This hypocrisy is what provokes God’s violent rejection of their worship.

2. **God’s Holiness and Demand for Integrity**:  
   For Amos, true worship requires alignment between belief and action. The Hebrew verbs *sā’ēh* (“hate”) and *ṭāmēh* (“despise”) emphasize God’s visceral revulsion—not because rituals themselves are inherently abhorrent, but because they were divorced from ethical living. God’s holiness demands that worship be accompanied by justice, mercy, and humility (Micah 6:6–8; Isaiah 1:11–17).

3. **Judgment as a Response to Fraud**:  
   The imagery of God refusing to “smell” their offerings (v. 21) echoes the sacrificial system in Leviticus, where God’s presence was symbolized by His “smell” of pleasing aroma. By rejecting this, Amos signals that their worship is no longer acceptable because it is tainted by injustice. The sharp language underscores that God’s relationship with Israel has become transactional and hollow.

4. **The Call to Repentance**:  
   Amos does not condemn worship itself but its misuse. Verses 21–23 serve as a warning: if their rituals are not rooted in justice, they will be stripped of their legitimacy. The stark contrast with “let judgment run down as waters” (v. 24) frames true worship as inseparable from societal righteousness. Justice is the only “stream” that reflects God’s life-giving nature; their festivals are “noise” without substance.

### The Moral and Spiritual Fraud
The fraud lies in **confusing form for substance**. The Israelites believed their religious performances would shield them from divine judgment, but Amos reveals that God sees through this facade. Their worship was a cover for greed (Amos 4:1), violence (Amos 1:11), and oppression of the poor (Amos 2:6–7). They were trying to buy God’s favor through rituals while living in direct violation of the Torah’s ethical demands (Deuteronomy 10:17–18).

### Why Not Just Ask for Balance?
Amos does not propose balancing ritual and ethics because **their practices were already fraudulent**. To ask for balance would imply that ritual and morality are equally valid but misaligned—a false equivalence. Instead, Amos insists that **morality is the foundation of authentic worship**. Without justice, their rituals are not just inadequate but offensive (Isaiah 1:12). The sharp rejection is a call to radical reorientation: true worship must begin with repentance and a commitment to justice.

In essence, Amos confronts the Israelites with the truth that **God’s relationship with His people cannot be sustained through external compliance when their hearts and actions are aligned with injustice**. Their worship was not a bridge to God but a mask for their moral decay. The prophet’s message is clear: only a society marked by justice and compassion can approach the holy God.
