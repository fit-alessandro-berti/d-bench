The sentence is destabilizing because it does not deny the woman’s guilt; it destabilizes the accusers’ innocence.

Several things happen at once:

1. **It interrupts mob-righteousness.**  
   The woman has become an object through whom the crowd can display its moral zeal. Jesus forces each accuser to become an individual moral agent: *Will you personally throw the stone?*

2. **It invokes the law against those using the law.**  
   Deuteronomy 17:7 required the witnesses to begin an execution. Jesus’ wording therefore exposes the responsibility of witnesses—their truthfulness, motives, and complicity. This case is already suspicious: the law condemned both participants in adultery, yet only the woman has been produced.

3. **It exposes hypocrisy—but not necessarily identical guilt.**  
   Jesus is not necessarily saying, “Whoever has never committed adultery may stone her.” The broader force is: no one may wield judgment as though standing outside the moral order by which others are judged.

4. **It does not simply forbid judgment.**  
   If only morally perfect people could make any judgment, courts, discipline, moral teaching, and even ordinary discernment would be impossible. The saying attacks **self-righteous, selective, punitive judgment**, not every distinction between right and wrong.

5. **It turns accusation into self-examination.**  
   The crowd asks, “What should be done to her?” Jesus makes each person ask, “Who am I to do it, and with what motive?” The law ceases to be a weapon held at a distance and becomes a mirror.

6. **It joins mercy to moral seriousness.**  
   Jesus neither stones her nor calls adultery harmless: “Neither do I condemn you; go, and sin no more.” Grace does not rename sin as innocence; it refuses to let sin have the final word.

The deepest destabilization may be Christological: **the one person in the scene who could meet the stated condition does not cast the stone.** Authority appears not as eagerness to punish but as the power to expose, spare, and call to transformation.

So the saying does not erase judgment. It removes moral superiority from the judge. It asks whether justice can remain justice once it becomes spectacle, scapegoating, or a means of avoiding one’s own need for mercy.

A textual note: the story of the woman taken in adultery (John 7:53–8:11) is absent from the earliest surviving manuscripts of John and appears in different locations in some later manuscripts. Most scholars therefore regard it as a later addition to John, though many also consider it an ancient Jesus tradition with profound influence in Christian teaching.
