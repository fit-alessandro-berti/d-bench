### Analysis of Matthew 6:1

Matthew 6:1 is part of Jesus' Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5–7), where he addresses the nature of true righteousness, contrasting external religious practices with internal motivations of the heart. Here, the verse specifically warns against performing acts of charity (alms, or giving to the poor) in a way that seeks human recognition. The full context extends into verses 2–4, where Jesus critiques "hypocrites" who publicize their generosity (e.g., "sounding a trumpet" before giving) to receive praise from others, versus those who give secretly and are rewarded by God.

#### What Makes Public Goodness Morally Dangerous?
The moral danger lies not in the act of doing good publicly—visibility alone isn't condemned—but in the underlying motivation that turns the deed into a performance for applause. Jesus emphasizes that righteous acts should stem from genuine devotion to God, not a desire for social validation or self-glorification. When "goodness" becomes a spectacle, it shifts the focus from serving others (and honoring God) to inflating one's ego, rendering the act hollow and self-serving.

#### Visibility Itself vs. the Craving to Be Seen
- **It's not visibility itself**: The Bible doesn't prohibit all public acts of kindness. For instance, Jesus himself performed miracles openly (e.g., healing the blind man in John 9), and communal worship or shared service can glorify God without ulterior motives. The issue arises only when publicity is contrived or exploited for personal gain.
  
- **It's the craving to be seen**: The phrase "to be seen of them" (Greek: *theathēnai*, implying theatrical display) points directly to the motive of seeking admiration from "men" (human observers). This craving corrupts the act because it replaces divine reward with fleeting human approval. As Jesus concludes in verse 1, such behavior forfeits "reward of your Father which is in heaven," suggesting that God values purity of heart over outward show. The danger is pride and hypocrisy, which erode authentic faith and turn altruism into vanity.

In essence, public goodness becomes dangerous when it feeds the sin of pride, prioritizing earthly praise over heavenly purpose. This teaching encourages self-examination: Are our good deeds for God's eyes alone, or do we secretly hope for a spotlight? For deeper study, cross-reference with similar ideas in Matthew 23:5–7 (Jesus' woes against the Pharisees for loving public acclaim) or Proverbs 21:2 ("Every way of a man is right in his own eyes: but the Lord pondereth the hearts").
