This is a profound and challenging question. The difficulty we have with enemy-love often reveals more about our own expectations of morality than about the command itself. Let's explore why it can be so hard to take seriously, and what it might actually be.

### 1. Why It's So Hard to Take Seriously
- **It Feels Self-Eradicating:** Our instinct for self-preservation and justice rebels against loving someone who intends us harm. It seems to require the erasure of our own right to protect ourselves or feel righteous indignation.
- **It Seems Sentimentally Unrealistic:** The modern concept of "love" as a feeling of affection makes this absurd. How can we feel warm fondness for someone who is cruel or unjust?
- **It Undermines Moral Boundaries:** We like clear categories of "good" and "evil." Loving an enemy blurs those lines in a way that feels like it might condone the evil or abandon the victim.
- **It Feels Ineffective:** In a pragmatic world, "blessing those who curse you" seems like a strategy that guarantees loss, not victory. It can look like weakness dressed up as virtue.

### 2. Is It Sentimental?
No. If it were mere sentiment, it would collapse on contact with real malice. This isn't about *feeling* good toward an enemy. The verbs are active and volitional: **Love** (Greek: *agape* – a committed, self-giving action), **bless** (speak well of, invoke good upon), **do good**, **pray**. It's about choosing a specific *course of action* regardless of the emotion. The sentimentality objection misunderstands the command—it's not about the heart's current state, but about the will's chosen direction.

### 3. Is It Strategic?
There can be strategic elements. Loving an enemy can:
- Disarm hostility and break cycles of retaliation.
- Reveal the irrationality of the aggressor's hatred.
- Build moral authority and a long-term witness.

But to reduce it to mere strategy is to miss its source and goal. The verse says to do this *"that ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven."* This is about **family resemblance**, not winning a conflict. God's sun and rain fall on "the evil and the good" not as a strategy to convert the evil, but as an expression of His generous, sustaining character. The primary motive isn't to change the enemy, but to align oneself with the nature of God.

### 4. Is It a Deeper Form of Moral Strength?
Yes. This is likely the most accurate lens.

- **It’s Strength That Transcends Reaction:** The natural strength of resentment and vengeance is reactive and tethered to the enemy's actions. Enemy-love is proactive. It chooses its own response, defining itself not by what it opposes but by what it affirms (good, blessing, prayer). This is the strength of sovereignty over one's own moral choices.
- **It’s Strength That Refuses to Be Corrupted:** To hate in return is to allow the enemy to determine your inner character. Enemy-love protects your own soul from being deformed by the very malice you're subjected to. It’s a declaration that the enemy's hatred does not have the power to destroy your capacity for good.
- **It’s Strength That Breaks the Cycle:** Most moral systems are built on symmetry (an eye for an eye) or escalation (the next person gets hurt worse). This command breaks symmetry entirely. It introduces a third, unexpected possibility that isn't retreat or attack, but a transformative engagement. This is the difficult, powerful work of **reconciliation**, which is far harder and stronger than either winning or withdrawing.

**In the end,** to take this seriously is to see it not as a sentimental rule for nice people, but as a radical, strength-based training for those who wish to participate in a different kind of moral reality—one that mirrors a generosity that even chaos and evil cannot exhaust. It doesn't come from human sentiment, but from a commitment to reflect a character that defines good *as* the source of life itself, regardless of worthiness. That is why it feels impossible to us—and why it is taken most seriously by those who have attempted it and found it to be the most difficult, transformative discipline there is.
