This is a profound question that gets to the very heart of Jesus’ teaching in the Sermon on the Plain. The difference between **Luke 6:37** and a claim that “moral judgments never matter” is the difference between a scalpel and a sledgehammer—both deal with reality, but one aims to heal while the other aims to destroy.

Let’s break down the distinction between **discernment** and the **appetite to condemn**.

---

### 1. **The Nature of the Verb: “Judge” (κρίνω)**
In this context, “judge” (Greek: *krinō*) often carried a sense of **final, authoritative condemnation**—like a magistrate passing a sentence. It implies assuming the role of ultimate arbiter over another person’s standing before God or their eternal worth. Jesus isn’t saying, “Never recognize right from wrong.” Elsewhere He instructs, “Do not give dogs what is holy” (Matthew 7:6)—which requires discernment.  
**Key point:** The prohibition is against **assuming the seat of final judgment**, not against moral perception.

---

### 2. **Discernment vs. Condemnation: The Inner Posture**
- **Discernment** asks: “What is true here? Is this action wise, healthy, righteous?” It operates with **clarity and care**, often accompanied by sorrow, a desire for restoration, and humility (“There but for the grace of God go I”).
- **The appetite to condemn** asks: “How can I categorize and dismiss this person?” It operates with **pride and pleasure**, often accompanied by self-righteousness, social posturing, and a desire to elevate oneself by lowering another.

Jesus exposes the heart motive. In the verses following (Luke 6:41–42), He uses hyperbole about a log in one’s own eye—not to say “never notice splinters,” but to warn that we often blind ourselves to our own faults while obsessively diagnosing others. That’s not discernment; it’s projection.

---

### 3. **The “Not” Isn’t Absolute—It’s Relational**
Jesus’ teaching is **sapiential** (wisdom literature), not **legislative**. He uses stark language to reset our default disposition.  
- “Judge not” sets a **presumption against harsh judgment**.  
- “Forgive” sets a **presumption toward mercy**.  

This creates a moral ecosystem where, when judgment is necessary (e.g., church discipline, parenting, ethical decisions), it is done **reluctantly, justly, and with a redemptive goal**—never as a first resort or with a condemning spirit.

---

### 4. **Why This Isn’t Moral Relativism**
If moral judgments never mattered, Jesus wouldn’t have:
- Called people to repentance (Mark 1:15).
- Strongly condemned religious hypocrisy (Matthew 23).
- Spoke of final judgment (Matthew 25).

The difference is in **authority and purpose**. Humans are not equipped to render final spiritual verdicts—that belongs to God. Our role is to discern truth from error **in order to restore, not to destroy** (Galatians 6:1).

---

### 5. **Practical Difference: A Medical Analogy**
- **Discernment** is like a doctor diagnosing an illness: “You have a tumor; it’s serious; let’s discuss treatment.” The goal is health.
- **The appetite to condemn** is like a bystander pointing and shouting, “You’re diseased! Get away from me!” The goal is separation and self-protection.

Luke 6:37 calls us to be doctors of the soul, not executioners of it.

---

### In Summary:
- **Moral judgments matter**—they are necessary for a good life and a just society.
- **Discernment** is the humble, careful exercise of moral perception.
- **Condemnation** is the proud, hasty exercise of moral superiority.

Jesus commands us to cultivate the first, avoid the second, and let God handle the final court of appeals. The fruit of this is a community characterized by **truthful mercy**—where people are both loved and called to holiness, without the poison of self-righteousness.
