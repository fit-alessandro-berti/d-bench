Ezekiel 36:26-27 is a biblical passage from the Hebrew scriptures, which uses metapthy to describe the process of spiritual renewal and transformation within a person. Here's an analysis of the text and the concepts it raises:

1. **New Heart/Heart of Flesh**: The phrase "new heart" is not meant literally; rather, it symbolizes a profound change in the individual's inner life. The "heart" in many religious texts is often a metaphor for one'seeming desires, intentions, and emotional condition. A "heart of stone" signifies hardness or a lack of compassion, suggesting that a person is unfeeling, legalistic, or unchangeable in their spiritual stance. Contrastingly, a "heart of flesh" implies tenderness, emotional warmth, and readiness to change, which characterizes a person who is open, compassionate, and open to spiritual guidance. External compliance refers to the adherence to religious observances and rituals, whereas receiving a "new heart" is about a deeper, inner transformation.

2. **Difference Between External Compliance and a New Heart**:
    - **External Compliance**: This involves following religious rites, prayers, and commandments. It can be comparable to external renovation, such as performing the rituals with physical participation. However, it doesn't necessarily touch upon the true motives or the personal spiritual state of an individual—it’s about surface-level adherence.
    - **New Heart**: This transformation takes place inside the person, at the core of their beliefs and emotions. To truly receive a new heart means a fundamental shift in how they perceive God, others, and life in general. It's a sincere commitment to live according to divine commandments. This involves a radical change in one's thoughts, attitudes, and heart's disposition.

3. **Why Hardness Described Like Material Condition**:  
    - Scriptural metaphors such as a "stone" or "hard heart" vividly depict the reality of spiritual inflexibility or impurity, suggesting that these issues are genuine, tangible impediments to growth. By using physical images, the text brings into stark relief the seriousness of spiritual ailments and the consequential impact on interpersonal and divine relationships.
    - The hardness as a material condition also implies that just as there is a physical remedy for a body, there must also be a spiritual remedy for the heart. It suggests that spiritual ailments, while intangible, bear consequences as real as physical sickness.
    - It creates an image for believers to grasp the need for a profound internal work, beyond mere outward observance, to truly connect with God and one's community. The idea is that the heart must be softened and made more receptive to divine love and truth for true communion and obedience to transpire.

4. **Effects of Taking Away the Stony Heart**:  
    - Removing the hardness implies the dissolution of pride, envy, hatred, and other negative traits. It suggests emotions and intentions will be purified, allowing the individual to genuinely connect with God and other people with humility, love, and brotherly kindness.
    - Essentially, having a "heart of flesh" doesn’t just mean metaphorically but signifies genuine sincerity, vulnerability, and emotional openness—allowing one's principles to govern not just intellectually but also on a visceral, emotional level.

5. **Actual Transformation**: This transformation wouldn't be instant, but a gradual, ongoing process involving self-reflection, repentance, and a conscious effort to adopt a more loving and responsive posture towards both God and others. It would be reflected in transformed behaviors and relationship patterns both within and outside the religious community.

In summary, the "new heart" suggests not just external compliance with God’s ways, but a deep, personal change in one’s life and heart, aligning one’s true self with God’s will. It entails becoming more perceptive to and responsive to divine love and guidance—a transformation requiring both humility and self-examination for a more authentic, affective, and responsive faith.
