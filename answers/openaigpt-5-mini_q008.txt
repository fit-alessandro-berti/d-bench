Short answer: Because love for a visible person is the clearest, least disputable evidence that the inner state called “love of God” is real. John is exposing a common self-deception: religious talk or feeling about God that isn’t translated into concrete, fellow‑human care. If you truly love God you will love God’s image present before you; if you do not, your claim to love God is hypocritical or delusional.

Why this works as a test (context and theology)
- Visibility and evidence: A neighbor is someone you can see and help. Actions (or the refusal to act) toward a real person are observable proof of what you value morally. Claiming love for an unseen God while treating people cruelly is a mismatch between claim and conduct.
- Imago Dei and Incarnation: Biblical theology presents humans as bearers of God’s image and, in the New Testament, God revealed in human life in Christ. Loving the human neighbor is therefore a fitting way to love God.
- Johannine context: 1 John repeatedly ties true Christian identity to love (1 John 3:14–18; 4:7–12). The letter combats falsehoods (including Gnostic tendencies to despise the material world and people) and insists that correct belief shows itself in love.
- Moral causality: John assumes genuine relationship with God produces moral transformation. Love of God and love of neighbor are not two unrelated commitments but one lived reality.

What kind of self‑deception is being exposed
- Hypocrisy: Saying you love God while habitually hating, neglecting or harming others—professing one thing, living another.
- Sentimental religion without ethics: Warm feelings or doctrinal talk about God that stop short of concrete compassion. John calls this a lie because it pretends a reality (union with God) that hasn’t occurred.
- Rationalizing avoidance: Excuses, compartmentalization, or spiritualized justifications that allow you to neglect needy people without seeing the inconsistency.
- Self‑deceit rather than merely lying to others: the Greek word and John’s context allow both senses. A person can both deceive others and convince themselves they love God while lacking the fruit of that love.

Practical upshot
- Love of neighbor is a built‑in, simple litmus test of spiritual authenticity. If your professed love of God does not produce mercy, forgiveness, generosity and care for others, the profession needs serious examination.

In short: John is saying that love of the unseen God is not an abstract inner state separable from behavior. The true presence of God is proved in how you treat the people you can see.
