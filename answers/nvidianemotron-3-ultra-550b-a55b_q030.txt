The passage you cite (1 Corinthians 13:4–7, KJV) defines love not by what it *feels* but by what it *refuses* to do. The heavy reliance on negation—*ou* (not) and *ouk* (not) repeated over a dozen times in the Greek—is a deliberate literary and theological strategy. It reveals a specific vision of strength: **strength as the capacity to absorb, rather than the capacity to assert.**

Here is why the portrait is built on negations, and what vision of strength emerges.

---

### 1. The Theological Logic of *Via Negativa* (The Negative Way)
In the ancient world (and in Scripture), the divine is often described by what it is *not* (immortal, invisible, immutable) because positive definitions inevitably limit the subject. Paul uses this same logic for *agape* because **love is not a behavior among behaviors; it is the absence of the ego’s gravity.**

The negations function as **exorcisms**. They name the specific demons that masquerade as "strength" in the Corinthian church—and in every human community:
*   **Pride/Arrogance (*physioumai, perpereuomai*):** The illusion of self-sufficiency.
*   **Envy (*zeloo*):** The resentment of another’s good.
*   **Irritability/Provocation (*paroxynetai*):** The hair-trigger defense of the ego.
*   **Suspicion/“Thinketh no evil” (*logizetai to kakon*):** The ledger-keeping of wrongs.

Paul uses negations because **the default setting of the fallen human heart *is* pride, envy, irritability, and suspicion.** We do not need to be taught *how* to be proud; we need to be told *not* to be. Love is defined negatively because it is the **cessation of the self’s centrifugal spin**—the stopping of the soul’s constant flight from the center.

### 2. The Corinthian Context: Strength as Performance vs. Strength as Endurance
The Corinthians were obsessed with *pneumatikos* (spiritual gifts)—tongues, prophecy, knowledge, power displays. For them, "strength" looked like **visibility, velocity, and volume**: speaking in tongues, working miracles, winning arguments, dominating the potluck.

Paul dismantles this. He lists the "positives" of the Corinthian ideal (tongues, prophecy, knowledge, faith to move mountains, martyrdom) in verses 1–3 and declares them **nothing** (*outhen*) without love.

Therefore, the negations in verses 4–7 are a direct polemic against **Corinthian "strength"**:
*   *Corinthian Strength:* "I speak truth forcefully." → *Love:* "Rejoices not in iniquity, but rejoices in the truth" (truth without cruelty).
*   *Corinthian Strength:* "I have knowledge that puffs up." → *Love:* "Vaunteth not itself, is not puffed up."
*   *Corinthian Strength:* "I endure persecution for show." → *Love:* "Endureth all things" (quiet, unseen endurance).

The negations strip away the **performance of power** to reveal the **anatomy of love**.

### 3. The Vision of Strength Proposed: "Power-Under"
If the negations clear the ground, the final quartet—*Beareth, Believeth, Hopeth, Endureth all things* (panta stegei, panta pisteuei, panta elpizei, panta hypomenei)*—builds the edifice. This is the positive vision of strength: **Omnipotent Vulnerability.**

#### A. Strength as *Absorption* (*Stegei* / Beareth all things)
The Greek *stegei* implies covering, roofing over, holding water like a vessel, or keeping a secret. It is the strength of a roof in a storm. The strong person is not the one who deflects the blow (pride), but the one who **absorbs the blow without collapsing or retaliating**. This is the strength of the Cross: God absorbing the worst of humanity without returning evil for evil.

#### B. Strength as *Generous Interpretation* (*Pisteuei* / Believeth all things)
This is not naïveté. It is the refusal to make "suspicion" the default epistemology. Cynicism is cheap; it protects the ego from disappointment. **To "believe all things" is the costly strength of risking trust.** It treats the other as a subject to be known, not an object to be managed.

#### C. Strength as *Defiant Futurity* (*Elpizei* / Hopeth all things)
Hope here is not optimism. It is the theological virtue that stares at the "not yet" and refuses to call it "never." The negations ("thinketh no evil") clear the past; hope secures the future. This is strength that **refuses to let the present darkness define the final reality.**

#### D. Strength as *Staying Power* (*Hypomenei* / Endureth all things)
*Hypomene* (remaining under) is a military term: holding a position under fire. It is the opposite of *flight* (cowardice) and *fight* (aggression). It is the strength of **non-retaliatory presence.** It says: "I will not leave. I will not destroy you. I will remain under this burden until it is redeemed."

---

### 4. Why This Looks Like Weakness to the World (And Why It Isn't)
The world defines strength by **boundary enforcement**: "I am strong because I keep you out, I win the argument, I protect my reputation, I calculate my risk."

Paul’s portrait defines strength by **boundary dissolution for the sake of the other**:
*   *Not easily provoked* = I surrender my right to be offended.
*   *Seeketh not her own* = I surrender my right to self-advancement.
*   *Thinketh no evil* = I surrender my right to be the judge.

**This is the strength of God.** In the logic of the Incarnation, God’s omnipotence is displayed not in the destruction of enemies, but in the endurance of the Cross. "Power is made perfect in weakness" (2 Cor 12:9).

### Summary
The negations exist because **the ego is a loud, insistent liar.** You cannot define the silence of love by listing the noises it makes; you define it by listing the noises it *stops* making.

The vision of strength proposed is **Kenotic (Self-Emptying) Strength**:
> **It is the strength to hold the weight of another’s brokenness without breaking yourself, and without breaking them.**

It is the strength of a container that holds the chaos of the world—sin, ingratitude, betrayal, failure—and refuses to leak poison back into it. It is the only strength that *lasts* (v. 8: *Love never faileth*), because it is the only strength that participates in the eternal life of God.
