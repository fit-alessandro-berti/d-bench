The danger highlighted in Matthew 6:1 is not visibility itself, but the *motive* of performing the act in order to be seen and approved by others.

The verse does not say “do not let your alms be seen by men.” It says do not do them *to be seen of them*. The Greek construction (“pros to theathēnai autois”) emphasizes purpose and intention: the giving is being done *for the sake of* the audience’s perception. This turns an act of righteousness into a transaction for social credit.

### Why the craving is the problem

- It replaces one audience for another. The act is no longer oriented toward God but toward human observers. Once that shift occurs, the “reward” changes accordingly—public approval is received, but the divine reward is forfeited.
- It introduces hypocrisy at the level of motive. The outward action may be identical (giving money to the poor), but the inner orientation has become self-referential. The person is no longer primarily concerned with mercy or obedience, but with being perceived as merciful and obedient.
- It trains the heart toward performance. Repeatedly seeking validation through visible goodness tends to distort character over time, making the person more sensitive to audience reaction than to the actual moral demand of the situation.

### Visibility is not the issue

The same chapter contains the counter-instruction in Matthew 5:16: “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven.” Public goodness is not only permitted but commanded when the purpose is God’s glory rather than self-display. The difference is not the presence or absence of witnesses, but whose approval is being sought.

Later in the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus criticizes those who pray and fast in ways designed to be noticed. The pattern is consistent: the moral problem is not the public nature of the act but the instrumental use of righteousness as a means of self-exaltation.

In short, the verse identifies a specific corruption—righteousness performed as theater—rather than a blanket condemnation of public virtue. The danger lies in the craving for the gaze, not in the fact that the gaze exists.
