The image is sharp because it turns moral blindness into something physically grotesque and comic.

- A **mote** is a tiny speck or splinter; a **beam** is a large piece of timber. The disproportion is deliberately absurd. A person with a beam in his eye would not merely be slightly impaired; he would be unable to see properly at all—perhaps unable even to approach another person safely.
- The sin is especially fittingly located in the **eye**, the organ by which one sees and judges. The person claims to have clear moral sight about his brother’s small fault, while being blinded by his own grave and unacknowledged fault.
- Jesus’ questions expose the presumption: “How wilt thou say…?” The proposed act—delicately removing a speck from someone else’s eye—requires care, closeness, and clear vision. Yet the would-be helper is himself manifestly unfit for the task.
- “Thou hypocrite” identifies more than ordinary imperfection. Hypocrisy is a mismatch between one’s moral posture toward others and one’s refusal to apply the same seriousness to oneself.

Yet the passage does not say, “Never remove the mote.” Its final clause matters: **“first cast out the beam out of thine own eye; and then shalt thou see clearly to cast out the mote out of thy brother’s eye.”** Correction remains possible—and, in the image, even helpful—but only after self-judgment and repentance.

So the teaching is not that no one may make moral judgments because everyone is imperfect. It condemns judgment that is:

- selective—severe toward another and indulgent toward oneself;
- blind—unable to recognize one’s own greater fault;
- self-exalting—using another’s sin to avoid confronting one’s own;
- unfit to help—offering correction without the clarity, humility, or mercy needed for it.

The proper order is therefore: examine oneself, repent, gain clearer sight, and then help one’s “brother” with his fault—not as a superior condemning an inferior, but as someone conscious of his own need for correction.
