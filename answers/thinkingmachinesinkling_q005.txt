It is hard to take seriously precisely because it sounds like a contradiction in terms—like being told to *embrace* the very thing that threatens your life, dignity, or justice. Psychologically, we are wired for reciprocity and tribal defense; morally, we fear that loving an enemy betrays the victim and dissolves accountability; practically, it looks like naivety that enables abuse. Jesus knows this, which is why he does not frame it as a mood or a tactic, but as an identity: **“That ye may be the children of your Father…”**

Here is how the three options break down against the text.

### 1. Not sentimental
Sentimentality is cheap: it asks for warm feelings without cost. Jesus demands the opposite—**active, costly practice**: bless, do good, pray. These are verbs of the will, not the heart’s whim. You can pray for a persecutor while still pursuing justice or protecting the vulnerable; the command targets your *disposition* and *action*, not your emotional temperature. If it were mere sentiment, it would collapse the moment the enemy strikes again. Instead, it is a disciplined refusal to let the enemy determine your moral character.

### 2. Not primarily strategic
There is undeniable strategic wisdom in enemy-love: hatred binds the hater to the hated, while love can break cycles of retaliation (a truth Gandhi and King drew from this passage). But notice Jesus’s reasoning: he does not say, “Love them so they will convert” or “so you will win.” He says, **love them because that is what your Father does.** God sends rain on the unjust *without strategic expectation of return*. If love is only a means to an end—disarmament, conversion, social harmony—it remains a form of manipulation. Jesus roots it in *imitation of God’s indiscriminate goodness*. The strategy may follow; the theology comes first.

### 3. A deeper form of moral strength
This is the heart of the matter. Enemy-love is not weakness disguised as virtue; it is **the strength to remain free**.

- **Freedom from definition by the enemy.** To hate back is to let the enemy write your script. To bless, do good, and pray is to reclaim agency.
- **Participation in divine nature.** The sun and rain image reveals a God who is not reactive—who does not withhold good based on merit. To love enemies is to share in that sovereignty: you are no longer a slave to the economy of retaliation.
- **Moral consistency at its limit.** The passage immediately contrasts this with the “tax collectors,” who love those who love them (Matt. 5:46). Enemy-love is therefore the proof that one’s ethics are not transactional. It is strength because it holds good and justice together without letting justice become vengeance.

### Why, then, is it so hard?
Because it asks us to surrender the one thing that feels like justice: **the right to hate with righteousness.** It threatens our sense of moral order—if I love my enemy, am I saying what they did was not evil? No. The text assumes real enemies, real curses, real persecution. It does not deny the evil; it denies evil the power to reproduce itself in *you*. That requires not less strength, but more: the strength of one who knows they are already a child of a Father who loves without scorekeeping.

In the end, enemy-love is not sentimental fantasy, nor cold strategy, but **the rugged discipline of divine likeness**—the refusal to let darkness have the last word, even in your own heart.
