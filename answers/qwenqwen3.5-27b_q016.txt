The elder brother’s deepest problem is **discomfort with mercy itself**.

While anger, envy, wounded fairness, and pride are all present in the text, they function as symptoms or mechanisms of this deeper spiritual condition. Here is why "discomfort with mercy" is the root issue:

**1. It reveals a transactional view of God**
The elder brother defines his relationship with his father not as one of sonship, but of servitude. He says, *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee"* (v. 29) and *"neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment"* (v. 29). He views the Father as a boss who owes him wages for good performance. In this worldview, mercy is not a gift; it is an accounting error. He cannot celebrate the younger brother’s restoration because it violates his understanding of "fairness." He prefers a God of strict justice over a God of unmerited grace.

**2. It exposes a lack of true sonship**
The father corrects him in verse 31: *"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine."* The father is telling him that he does not need to earn his inheritance; it is already his by virtue of relationship. The elder brother’s discomfort with mercy proves he does not believe he is a son. If he truly understood he was a beloved son, he would know that the Father’s generosity toward the younger brother does not diminish his own standing. His inability to accept the Father’s joy reveals that he, too, is "lost"—not geographically, but spiritually.

**3. It mirrors the Pharisees’ rejection of Jesus**
In the context of Luke 15, the parable is told to the Pharisees and scribes who were complaining, *"This man receives sinners and eats with them"* (Luke 15:2). The elder brother represents those who are religiously obedient but spiritually hostile to grace. Their problem was not merely pride (though that was present); their fundamental problem was that they could not comprehend a Savior who would welcome the "devourers of living" rather than just the "keepers of commandments."

**4. Pride is the cause, but rejection of grace is the result**
One could argue that **pride in obedience** is the root cause. However, pride is the *mechanism* that leads to the *state* of being uncomfortable with mercy. The elder brother’s pride blinds him to his own need for grace, which makes him view the Father’s mercy as an insult. Ultimately, the problem that keeps him outside the feast is not just his self-righteousness, but his refusal to embrace the Father’s character. He would rather be right in his own eyes than reconciled in the Father’s house.

**Conclusion**
The elder brother is angry because he is envious; he is envious because he feels he has been treated unfairly; he feels unfairly treated because he prides himself on his obedience. But the **deepest** problem is that **he cannot accept mercy**. He stands outside the door, physically present in the house, but spiritually dead, unable to share in the joy of a God who saves the lost.
