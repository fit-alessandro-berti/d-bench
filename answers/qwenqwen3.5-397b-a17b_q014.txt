This is one of the most profound psychological and spiritual insights in the Sermon on the Mount. To answer your question directly: **While the text implies that treasure *reveals* the heart, the logic of the command insists that treasure *trains* (or anchors) the heart.**

Here is an analysis of how the text constructs this relationship and how possession, attention, and love are inextricably tied together.

### 1. Reveals vs. Trains: The Direction of Causality

There is a common misconception that Jesus is saying, "Your heart loves something, so you spend money on it." That would mean the heart leads and the treasure follows (Treasure *reveals* Heart).

However, the grammar and context of Matthew 6:21 suggest the opposite direction of causality: **Treasure $\rightarrow$ Heart.**

*   **The Logic of the Command:** Jesus issues an imperative in verses 19–20: *"Lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven."* If the heart were the fixed variable that simply revealed itself through spending, this command would be futile. You cannot command an emotion; you can only command an action. Jesus commands an action (laying up treasure) with the promise that the affection (the heart) will follow.
*   **The Future Tense:** In the Greek text, the verb regarding the heart is future tense (*estai* — "will be"). It reads: "Where your treasure is, there your heart *will be* also." This implies a trajectory. The heart is drawn to the treasure. The investment pulls the affection.
*   **The Protective Reason:** Jesus warns against earthly treasure because it is vulnerable ("moth and rust"). If the heart follows the treasure, then placing your treasure in vulnerable things ensures your heart will be subject to anxiety, loss, and corruption. Placing treasure in heaven secures the heart.

Therefore, in this specific text, treasure is the **rudder**. It does not merely show where the ship is pointing; it determines where the ship will go.

### 2. The Triad: Possession, Attention, and Love

The text binds these three concepts into a feedback loop that defines human devotion.

**A. Possession (The Anchor)**
In this context, "treasure" (*thesauros*) is not merely currency; it is stored value, security, and identity. It is what you rely on for your future.
*   **The Tie:** You cannot possess something without it possessing you. By acquiring or storing something, you establish a claim on your own life. To own is to be tied to the object of ownership.

**B. Attention (The Magnet)**
Notice the verbs associated with the treasures: "corrupt," "break through," "steal." These require vigilance.
*   **The Tie:** Where your value is stored, your mind must go to protect it. If your treasure is earthly (status, wealth, comfort), your attention is consumed by maintenance and defense against "thieves" (time, market crashes, critics, decay). If your treasure is heavenly, your attention is drawn toward God and eternity. Attention is the bridge between what you *have* and what you *love*.

**C. Love (The Heart)**
In biblical anthropology, the "heart" (*kardia*) is not just the seat of emotion, but the center of the will and intellect. It is the "you" that decides and desires.
*   **The Tie:** Love is not a static feeling; it is a gravitational pull. Jesus argues that love is malleable. It flows toward where investment has been made. You do not love something and then invest in it; you invest in it, and you learn to love it.

### Summary: The Cycle of Devotion

The text describes a spiritual law of gravity:

1.  **Investment:** You place your resources (time, money, energy) into a container (Earth or Heaven).
2.  **Attention:** Your mind naturally wanders toward that container to check on its security and growth.
3.  **Affection:** Over time, that constant attention hardens into love and loyalty.

**Conclusion**
Matthew 6:19–21 teaches that **we become what we behold, and we behold what we bank.**

Jesus is not asking us to analyze our hearts to see where our treasure should go; He is asking us to move our treasure so that our hearts can be healed. By commanding us to "lay up treasure in heaven," He is providing a mechanism to train our hearts to love God more than the world. Possession captures attention, and attention cultivates love. Therefore, to change what you love, you must change what you keep.
