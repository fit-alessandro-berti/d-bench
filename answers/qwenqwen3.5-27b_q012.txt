This is a profound question that touches on theology, psychology, and ethics. To answer it, we must distinguish between a **diagnosis** of a condition and a **prognosis** for the future.

Here is an analysis of whether the passage is pessimistic or realistic, followed by practical guidance on how to discuss motives when self-knowledge is unreliable.

### 1. Pessimistic vs. Realistic: A Sober Diagnosis

While the language is stark, most biblical scholars and ethicists classify this passage as **sober realism** rather than nihilistic pessimism.

**Why it looks pessimistic:**
*   **The Language:** The Hebrew word translated as "desperately wicked" (*mi'ashav*) implies something incurable or beyond repair. It suggests the human heart is not merely "sometimes wrong," but fundamentally corrupted.
*   **The Epistemological Limit:** The rhetorical question *"Who can know it?"* denies humans the ability to fully comprehend their own internal state. If we cannot trust our internal witness, we are left in a state of existential confusion.

**Why it is actually realistic:**
*   **It aligns with observation:** Psychology confirms that humans are masters of rationalization. We naturally attribute our good actions to character and our bad actions to circumstance (the "fundamental attribution error"). Jeremiah is naming a universal human experience: we are blind to our own biases.
*   **It offers a solution (Verse 10):** If the passage ended at verse 9, it would be pessimistic. However, verse 10 introduces the **Divine Witness**. The text does not leave humanity in the dark; it posits that while *we* cannot know the heart, *God* can.
*   **It shifts authority:** The passage is not saying "Nothing matters." It is saying "Self-assessment is insufficient." This moves the locus of truth from the subjective (my feelings) to the objective (God’s judgment).

**Verdict:** It is a **realistic assessment of human limitation** that prevents spiritual pride. It is pessimistic about human *autonomy* (we cannot save ourselves) but hopeful regarding divine *omniscience* (God knows the truth and will judge fairly).

### 2. How to Talk About Motives When the Heart is Unreliable

If the heart is a "deceitful witness," then claiming "I know exactly why I did this" is often a claim of arrogance rather than truth. Here is how one should approach discussing motives in light of Jeremiah 17:9–10:

#### A. Prioritize "Fruit" over "Feeling"
Since the internal witness is compromised, the external evidence becomes the primary metric.
*   **The Shift:** Instead of saying, *"I did this because I wanted to help,"* say, *"I did this because it was the right thing to do, regardless of how I feel."*
*   **The Test:** Look at the outcome. Does the action produce peace, justice, and love, or does it create confusion and control? As verse 10 states, God gives according to the "fruit of his doings." If the fruit is bitter, the motive was likely flawed, regardless of what you told yourself.

#### B. Adopt Epistemic Humility
When discussing your own intentions, use language that acknowledges your potential blindness.
*   **Avoid:** "I know I had no ulterior motives."
*   **Adopt:** "I *believe* my intention was pure, but I ask for correction if I am blind to my own pride."
*   **Why:** This invites accountability. It signals to others that you are not claiming moral perfection, but are willing to be held to a higher standard.

#### C. Seek External Accountability
Since "who can know it?" (implying no human can fully know another's heart), you must rely on the community to see what you cannot.
*   **The Practice:** Ask trusted others, *"How does my behavior look to you?"* or *"Do you see me seeking recognition in this?"*
*   **The Theology:** We need the "mirror" of others to see the parts of the heart that are hidden from us.

#### D. Invite Divine Search (The "Psalm 139" Approach)
The practical application of Jeremiah 17 is found in Psalm 139:23–24: *"Search me, O God, and know my heart... See if there be any wicked way in me."*
*   **The Conversation:** When discussing motives with God (in prayer) or with a spiritual mentor, frame it as a request for revelation rather than a statement of fact.
*   **The Goal:** You are not trying to prove your innocence; you are asking for the truth to be revealed so you can repent or be corrected.

#### E. Distinguish Between "Pure" and "Mixed" Motives
Jeremiah suggests the heart is deceitful, not that humans are incapable of doing good. However, even our good deeds often have mixed motives (a desire to help + a desire to be seen as helpful).
*   **The Nuance:** Do not demand 100% purity of motive before acting. That leads to paralysis.
*   **The Strategy:** Acknowledge the mixed nature of the heart. "I am doing this because I love them, but I also want them to like me. I will proceed because the action is right, and I will ask God to purify the motive."

### Summary
Jeremiah 17:9–10 is **realistic** because it accurately diagnoses the human tendency toward self-deception. It is not pessimistic because it provides an external standard of truth (God) that transcends our internal confusion.

To talk about motives in this light is to **stop defending your heart** and start **examining your fruit**. It requires admitting that you may be the last person to know your own true intentions, and therefore, you must rely on God's search and the community's witness to find the truth.
