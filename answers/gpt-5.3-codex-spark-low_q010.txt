• The passage praises moral and redemptive power through self-emptying love—what Christian theology often calls kenotic
  (self-humbling) power.

  1. What kind of power?
     It is not power to dominate, control, or be defended, but power to:
      - renounce status (“not think it robbery to be equal with God”),
      - identify with the vulnerable (“form of a servant”),
      - submit to obedience (“obedient unto death”),
      - and still transform others through sacrificial love.

     In short: power that heals and reconciles rather than overpowers.
  2. Why this feels more unsettling than open domination
      - It breaks expectations: domination is predictable and visible; humility looks like weakness, even loss.
      - It exposes dependence on inner authority, not external force: “I can win only by taking, you persuade by
        giving.”
      - It threatens the ego-based model of identity: if worth is tied to control, visible status, or victory, self-
        emptying upends everything.
      - It is harder to resist morally: coercive power can be opposed; a person who serves, forgives, and suffers for
        good has a way of disarming hostility and exposing injustice without mirroring it.

  So the “unsettling” force is that this is power without coercion—strong enough to reshape loyalties, values, and power
  itself.
