The posture required is **humble, gentle, and restorative rather than superior or punitive**.

“Ye which are spiritual” does not mean a morally elite class. It refers to those who are walking by the Spirit in the sense of Galatians 5: they are marked by love, gentleness, and self-control. Their spiritual maturity is shown not by their ability to expose another person’s failure, but by the way they seek that person’s recovery.

- **“Restore”** translates the idea of mending or setting something right. The aim is not to shame the offender or establish the corrector’s superiority, but to help the person return to faithful life and fellowship.
- **“In the spirit of meekness”** means correction must be firm without being harsh, truthful without being humiliating, and compassionate without excusing the wrong. Meekness is controlled strength: the corrector does not weaponize the other person’s failure.
- **“Considering thyself”** calls for sober self-examination. The corrector must remember, “I too am vulnerable; I too can fall.” This guards against pride, hypocrisy, and the temptation to respond with contempt. It may also include awareness that one could be tempted by the same sin.

These qualities belong together because **the goal of correction determines its manner**. If the goal is restoration, the corrector must approach the offender as a fellow sinner in need of grace, not as a judge standing above a criminal. Self-awareness produces meekness; meekness makes restoration possible.

Paul therefore holds together two truths: the fault must be taken seriously, but the person must not be treated as beyond recovery. Christian correction is neither permissiveness nor condemnation. It is truth exercised with humility, for the sake of healing, while remembering that the one who helps restore another remains dependent on grace himself.
