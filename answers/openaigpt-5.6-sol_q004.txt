The proverb asks for **wholehearted reliance on God rather than ultimate reliance on oneself**.

“Lean not unto thine own understanding” does not mean, “Do not think.” The image is of putting one’s full weight on something as a support. Human understanding is real and valuable, but limited, fallible, and vulnerable to self-interest. It must not become the final authority by which God, truth, and duty are judged.

That is **humble self-distrust**:

- recognizing the limits of one’s knowledge;
- submitting plans and judgments to God’s revealed wisdom;
- remaining teachable and open to correction;
- seeking counsel and discernment;
- obeying God even when one cannot foresee or control the outcome.

It is **not anti-intellectualism**:

- refusing evidence or careful reasoning;
- treating ignorance as spirituality;
- rejecting learning, expertise, or wise counsel;
- calling every private impulse “God’s direction”;
- believing faith makes critical judgment unnecessary.

The wider book of Proverbs repeatedly commands the pursuit of wisdom, knowledge, instruction, prudence, and counsel. Thus the contrast is not **faith versus reason**, but **trusting God versus treating one’s own reason as self-sufficient**.

“In all thy ways acknowledge him” means to bring every sphere of life into conscious relationship with God—to know, consult, honor, and obey him. “He shall direct thy paths” can also be rendered “he will make your paths straight”: not necessarily making life easy or revealing every detail in advance, but providentially guiding a life ordered toward him.

In short: **use your understanding fully, but do not worship it; let reason serve within a posture of trust, obedience, and teachability before God.**
