This passage from Jeremiah 17:9-10, while sobering, provides a deep insight into the biblical perspective on human nature and divine justice. Let's delve into the two parts of your question.

1. **Pessimistic about human nature, or realistic about self-deception?**
   - The verse asserts that the human heart is "deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked." This can initially seem very pessimistic, suggesting that human nature is inherently corrupt and untrustworthy. From a pessimistic viewpoint, the text indicates a deeply flawed human condition, prone to evil and deception.
   - However, reading it as a statement of realism might be more productive in a theological or spiritual context. This might mean acknowledging the potential for self-deception inherent in human nature. Everyone, regardless of their moral standing, can be misled by their desires and instincts, making self-awareness and moral vigilance essential.
   - Whether you view it as pessimistic or realistic might depend on your perspective on the purpose of its teaching. If seen as a realistic depiction, it serves as a caution not to trust solely in one’s own heart but to seek greater wisdom and guidance.

2. **Discussing motives if the heart is an unreliable witness:**
   - Given the premise that the heart can deceive, discussing motives requires introspection guided by external, reliable standards and often divine assistance. Rather than solely relying on one's subjective feelings, engaging with objective moral truths (such as those offered by religious teachings, ethical philosophies, or communal norms) can provide a check against personal biases.
   - Reflection and prayer (or meditation in non-theistic contexts) are tools often suggested to transcend mere personal desire or instinct. In Christianity, for instance, prayer and scripture study are avenues to understand one's deeper motives away from the heart’s deceit.
   - Transparency and seeking counsel from others can also help in assessing motives more reliably. Friends, mentors, or confidants can offer perspectives that counteract personal biases and help align one's actions with one's higher values or communal norms.

In essence, Jeremiah 17:9–10 can be seen as both a realistic acknowledgment of human propensity towards self-deception and a moral caution against over-trusting one’s own heart without reflection or counsel. Discussing motives in light of such a scriptural insight involves seeking clarity through self-awareness, counsel, and adherence to higher moral principles beyond individual whim or impulse.
