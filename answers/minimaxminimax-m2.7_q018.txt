Excellent and perceptive question. This juxtaposition is one of the most theologically rich and practical instructions in the New Testament. The direct opposition isn't accidental; it's Paul's core pastoral strategy for addressing the Christian community's social and spiritual health.

Let's break down the text and your sub-questions:

### 1. The Structure of the Command
The passage is built on a powerful **contrast and transformation** model:
*   **First (v.31):** A negative command: "Let all... be put away from you." It’s a stripping away, a *cessation*.
*   **Then (v.32):** A positive command: "And be ye kind... tenderhearted, forgiving." It’s a putting on, a *replacement*.

The direct placement of "forgiving one another" against "bitterness" (and the cluster of related sins: wrath, anger, clamour, evil speaking, malice) is deliberate. Paul is saying: **The act of forgiveness is the direct, divinely-powered antidote to the poison of bitterness.** It’s not just "stop doing this" but "do this *instead*, and here’s the model."

### 2. The Nature of Bitterness: More Than a Feeling
Paul is indeed targeting the **posture and root** more than a single feeling. Here's why:
*   **It’s a "Root" Sin:** In the list, "bitterness" (*pikria*) is listed first, often understood as the **taproot** from which the others (wrath, anger, clamour) can spring. It's a deep-seated, persistent attitude of resentment.
*   **It’s a Chosen Posture:** The verb "let... be put away" (*arthētō*) is an aorist imperative, calling for a decisive, decisive act of will and will. It's not just managing an emotion but removing a **dominating disposition**.
*   **It’s Contagious (Your Insight is Spot On):** The phrase "with all malice" (*kakian* - ill-will, eagerness to harm) at the end of v.31 connects them. Bitterness, when nursed, *naturally wants to spread*. It moves from a personal feeling to a community toxin, leading to "clamour" (a loud, contentious outburst) and "evil speaking" (slander, defamation). Paul is warning that bitterness is **anti-community**. It infects relationships.

### 3. The Key: The Motive and Model of Forgiveness (v.32b)
This is the heart of the passage. The reason we can "put away" bitterness and "be kind... forgiving" is **not** human resolve alone, but **divine grace made visible in Christ**.
*   **The Model:** "Even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you." Our forgiveness is patterned after God's forgiveness. It’s a **reflection** of a prior, greater act.
*   **The Motive:** We forgive because we have been forgiven. This is not "be nice to each other" but a profound **theological imperative** grounded in our union with Christ. The memory of our own forgiven debt (often massive, though we don't see it) empowers us to release the much smaller debt someone owes us (cf. the Parable of the Unmerciful Servant, Matt. 18:21-35).
*   **The "More" Aspect:** Paul implies that this forgiveness is **supernaturally enabled**. We "put away" the old and "put on" the new (v.24) not by gritting our teeth, but by the power of the Spirit (v.30), who creates the new community where this kind of radical forgiveness is possible.

### Synthesis: Bitterness vs. Forgiveness in Ephesians
In this context, **bitterness is not merely a feeling; it is a settled state of the heart that refuses the logic of the gospel.** It says, in effect, "I will not let go of this offense; I will nurse it; it defines my relationship with you."

**Forgiveness, in contrast, is an act of obedient, Spirit-enabled trust** that releases the other person from the debt of their offense against us, trusting God's justice, and refusing to let that offense poison our connection to them or the community.

**So, why are they placed directly against each other?**
Because Paul is presenting **two mutually exclusive ways of relating to one another in the community of faith.** You cannot genuinely forgive *as God has forgiven* while holding onto bitterness. The one excludes the other. Bitterness is the old way of the "old self" (v.22); forgiving kindness is the new way of the "new self... created to be like God in true righteousness and holiness" (v.24).

The text is treating bitterness as a **soul-destroying, community-corrupting stronghold** that must be surgically removed, and forgiveness as the **divinely-modeled, grace-powered surgical procedure and its healing aftermath**. It's less about "feelings management" and more about **a radical reorientation of relational loyalty away from self and toward the God who first forgave us.**
