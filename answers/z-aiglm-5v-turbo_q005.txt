The passage from Matthew 5:44–45 represents one of the most radical and disruptive commands in the entire Sermon on the Mount. It moves beyond the "old law" of reciprocal justice (*lex talionis*)—"an eye for an eye"—into a framework of non-retaliatory grace.

To understand why this is so difficult to accept, and whether it is sentimentality, strategy, or strength, we must look at what Jesus is actually asking for, and the reasoning he provides.

### 1. Why is Enemy-Love So Difficult to Take Seriously?

The resistance to this command usually stems from three misunderstandings:

**A. Confusing "Love" (*Agape*) with "Likability" (*Phileo*)**
In modern English, "love" is usually a feeling—an emotional resonance or affection. We find it difficult to "take seriously" the idea of loving an enemy because we cannot force ourselves to *feel* warmth toward someone who is harming us. However, the Greek word used here is *agapē*. In the biblical context, this is not primarily an emotion but a **volitional commitment**. It is a determination to seek the ultimate good of the other person, regardless of their worthiness or your feelings toward them. It is an active verb: bless, do good, pray.

**B. The Instinct for Justice (Reciprocity)**
Human society operates on a ledger of credits and debts. If you hit me, my natural impulse—and the standard of most ethical systems—is to hit back, or at least to withhold good from you. Jesus identifies this as the standard of "tax collectors" (verse 46-47). Even sinners love those who love them. To love an enemy is to refuse to keep score. It feels dangerous because it seems to leave the victim unprotected and vulnerable to further exploitation.

**C. The Fear of Weakness**
We equate retaliation with strength and forgiveness with weakness. To "turn the other cheek" (verse 39) feels like capitulation—a surrender of one's dignity. Taking enemy-love seriously feels like asking to be a doormat.

---

### 2. Is it Sentimental?

**Verdict: No.**

Sentimentality is the focus on soft feelings to the exclusion of harsh reality. It is cheap because it costs nothing. Jesus’ command is the opposite of sentimental; it is **gritty and expensive**.

Sentimentality says, "If we just love everyone, no conflict will exist." Realism acknowledges that conflict exists and the enemy may continue to hate you. Enemy-love is difficult precisely because it demands action (**do good**, **pray**) while you are still experiencing the reality of being cursed, hated, or persecuted.

It requires a suppression of the ego and the adrenal "fight or flight" response. That is not a soft, fuzzy idealism; it is a rigid discipline of the will.

### 3. Is it Strategic?

**Verdict: Partially, but insufficient.**

There is undoubtedly a strategic dimension to enemy-love. Mahatma Gandhi and Martin Luther King Jr. utilized this teaching strategically to break cycles of oppression.
*   **Shaming the Oppressor:** When you refuse to retaliate, you strip the aggressor of their justification for aggression. You expose their brutality without mirroring it.
*   **Creating Cognitive Dissonance:** Responding to hate with blessing confounds the enemy's expectations and can potentially de-escalate conflict by inviting them to step out of the "monster" role you have assigned them.

However, if enemy-love is *only* strategic, it becomes manipulative. If I am only nice to my enemy so they will stop hitting me, I am not loving them—I am using kindness as a weapon. Jesus does not promise this strategy will work; he does not say "love your enemies **so that they will become your friends**." He offers no guarantee of success, only a command of action. Therefore, it transcends strategy.

### 4. A Deeper Form of Moral Strength

**Verdict: Yes—this is the primary category.**

Jesus grounds this command not in sociology or psychology, but in **Theology** ("That ye may the children of your Father"). He argues that enemy-love is the highest form of power because it mimics the creative power of God Himself.

Here is why this constitutes a unique form of moral strength:

**A. Freedom from Causality**
When someone insults you and you insult them back, you are their puppet—you are dancing to their music. They pull the string (insult), and you respond (retaliation). Enemy-love cuts the strings. By refusing to let the enemy's actions dictate your responses, you reclaim your autonomy. You are acting from your own center (your identity as a child of God), not reacting to external stimuli.

**B. The Power of Creation vs. Destruction**
Verse 45 notes that God sends rain on the just and unjust. This is the definition of *Providence*: the sustaining of life regardless of merit. To destroy life is easy (and natural); to sustain life—even the life of an enemy—is a divine attribute. To love an enemy is to participate in the creative, sustaining power of the universe rather than the entropic, destructive power of conflict.

**C. Breaking the Chain of History**
Most human history is a long trail of "he hit me first." Retribution binds us to the past—to the injury done to us. Grace binds us to the future. It takes immense moral strength to absorb the debt of a sin and forgive it rather than passing the cost down the line.

### Summary

Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it asks humans to override their survival instincts and their desire for fairness. It is not **sentimental**, because it demands grit in the face of pain. It can be **strategic**, but it is risky and offers no guaranteed payoff.

Ultimately, Jesus presents it as **moral transcendence**: the ability to reflect the character of God by valuing the existence of an image-bearer (even a broken one) more than one's own right to revenge. It is the assertion that the person loving is freer than the person hating.
