# The Nature of Trust in Proverbs 3:5–6

This is a genuinely important distinction, and one that the text itself helps us draw.

## What "lean not unto thine own understanding" means

The Hebrew verb here (*sha'an*, "to lean") suggests **propping yourself up**, resting your full weight on something. The image is architectural: a pillar supporting a structure. The command is not "do not use understanding" but **"do not make it the thing that holds everything up."**

The Wisdom literature *itself*—the entire book of Proverbs—is an exercise in understanding, observation, and reason. Proverbs 1:2 calls it "wisdom and instruction, to perceive the words of understanding." So the text is plainly not hostile to intellect.

## The distinction

**Humble self-distrust** recognizes that human understanding, even when functioning well, is:
- **Finite** — we cannot see the whole picture
- **Self-interested** — we subtly rationalize what we want
- **Fragile** — what looks certain today may collapse tomorrow

This is a *realistic* assessment of our epistemic condition, not a rejection of epistemic effort.

**Anti-intellectualism**, by contrast, treats thinking itself as the enemy—as if God asks us to switch off the mind He gave us. This misreads the passage and contradicts the broader scriptural call to "be transformed by the renewing of your mind" (Romans 12:2).

## The actual posture

The trust described is *relational*. The Hebrew word (*batach*) connotes the confidence one places in someone you **know personally** and have reason to trust—not blind faith, but the kind of trust a child places in a parent whose character has been proven.

"Acknowledge him in all thy ways" means bringing God into every deliberation—not replacing thought with passivity, but refusing to let your conclusions have the final word when they stand against His revealed character and wisdom.

In short: it is the difference between a scientist who humbly says "I may be wrong" and someone who says "thinking is pointless."
