Your question touches on a profound tension in human self-understanding. The Jeremiah passage is less a statement of pessimism and more a stark diagnosis—a *realism* about the human condition that acknowledges our capacity for self-deception.  

The Hebrew word translated “deceitful” (עָקֹב, *aqov*) can also mean “unsearchable” or “inscrutable”—the heart is not merely wicked, but mysterious even to ourselves. The verse doesn’t say the heart is *always* wicked, but that it is *desperately* (or “incurably”) sick in its capacity to hide truth from itself.  

So, if the heart is an unreliable witness, how do we talk about motives? A few guiding principles might help:  

1. **Humility and external accountability** – We can’t fully know our own hearts, so we need others to speak into our lives and help us see our blind spots (Proverbs 27:6; Hebrews 3:13).  
2. **Testing motives through action and time** – Jesus taught that “by their fruits you will know them” (Matthew 7:16). Our true motives often reveal themselves not in our intentions, but in the patterns of our behavior and the outcomes we produce.  
3. **Transparency before God** – The passage continues with God as the one who “searches the heart and tests the reins.” Prayerful honesty before God—even admitting confusion about our own motives—becomes a way to invite light into our inner darkness.  
4. **Speak about motives cautiously** – Rather than declaring “I did this with pure intentions,” we might say, “I believe my motive was X, but I’m aware I could be deceiving myself.” This frames self-awareness as an ongoing process rather than a settled fact.  

In essence, the passage isn’t meant to paralyze us with suspicion, but to free us from the illusion of perfect self-knowledge—leading us instead to rely on God’s discernment and to live in community where mutual discernment is possible.
