## Root and fruit

The pairing is deliberate, not redundant. In biblical anthropology, sin has a biography: it is conceived in the thought and born in the way. Micah puts it almost clinically — they "devise iniquity... upon their beds; when the morning is light they practise it" (Mic 2:1). The *machashavah* (thought, device, scheme) is the seed; the *derekh* (way, road, habitual course) is the harvest. So the double command answers the double anatomy:

- Forsake only the **way**, and you've pruned a plant while leaving the root. The conduct reforms for a season; the un-forsaken imagination grows the behavior back.
- Forsake only the **thoughts**, and you have remorse without repentance — interior regret that never touches the road you're actually walking. Isaiah condemns exactly this elsewhere: lips near, heart far (29:13).

God claims both stations of sin's journey. Repentance must go upstream to the spring, not just downstream to the flood.

## The exchange hidden in verses 8–9

The crucial key is what comes immediately after — and it uses the *same two words*: "My thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways" (v. 8). This is not a new topic; it's the explanation of verse 7. Repentance here is not merely moral cleanup but an **exchange of minds**: the wicked man trades in his self-devised project — his schemes, his calculations, his privately authored life-plan — for God's design, which is "higher than the heavens above the earth."

Notice where his thoughts appear earlier in the chapter: spending money "for that which is not bread," laboring "for that which satisfieth not" (v. 2). The thoughts to be forsaken include a whole false economics of the soul — the assumption that life must be earned, schemed for, secured. Against this stands the chapter's opening absurdity: "buy wine and milk without money and without price" (v. 1). Forsaking one's thoughts means forsaking the mercantile imagination itself.

## Mercy sized to the depth of the demand

Here the demand's severity becomes the ground of hope. If repentance must reach all the way down into the imagination, the honest penitent despairs: *my inner life is too tangled; God cannot pardon this.* But that despair is itself one of the "thoughts" to be abandoned — because God's thoughts are not ours. We forgive the way we calculate: grudgingly, partially, at a price. He "will abundantly pardon" — the Hebrew piles up the intensification, and *salach* (pardon) is a verb used in the Old Testament only of God. Forgiveness is not a human transaction scaled up; it is a divine prerogative that outruns human reckoning.

So the logic of the passage holds together:

1. Sin is comprehensive — thought and way, imagination and conduct.
2. Therefore repentance is comprehensive — forsaking both, and returning to a *Person* ("let him return unto the LORD... to our God"), not merely to a rule.
3. Therefore mercy must be more than comprehensive — "abundant," heavenly, disproportionate — or the depth of the demand would crush the very person it invites.

The urgency of verse 6 ("while he may be found, while he is near") seals it: the exchange is offered now, and it is offered by One who is *near* — close enough that a sinner need only turn around to find the mercy already standing there, larger than the thoughts that said it couldn't be.
