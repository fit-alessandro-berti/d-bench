1 John treats love of the visible brother/neighbor as a test of love for the invisible God because, in John’s thought, love for God cannot remain merely inward, verbal, or “spiritual.” It must become concrete.

The logic is:

> If you cannot love the person God has put in front of you — someone visible, tangible, needy, and real — then your claim to love the unseen God is suspect.

John is not saying that human beings are more lovable than God. Rather, he is saying that love for God is proved in the arena where love can actually be practiced: toward real people.

In the context of 1 John 4, God’s love is not an abstraction. God has shown his love concretely:

> “God sent his only begotten Son into the world…”  
> “Herein is love…”  
> “Beloved, if God so loved us, we ought also to love one another.”

So if someone says, “I love God,” but hates his brother, there is a contradiction. The invisible God is loved by obeying him, reflecting his character, and loving those whom he loves.

The verse exposes a particular kind of self-deception: religious self-deception.

It is the deception of thinking one can have a genuine relationship with God while refusing love to actual people. It is possible to love an idea of God, a feeling about God, a religious identity, or a private spirituality, while avoiding the costly demands of love in real life.

John says that is false.

A person may say, “I love God,” but if he harbors hatred, contempt, indifference, or malice toward his brother, his words are empty. His treatment of the visible person reveals the truth about his invisible claim.

In other words:

- Love of God is not merely a claim.
- Love of God is not merely emotion.
- Love of God is not compatible with hatred of God’s children.
- Love of God shows itself in concrete love for others.

The “brother whom he has seen” is the test because he is the immediate, practical, unavoidable place where love either becomes real or is exposed as illusion.

John is confronting the person who separates piety from love — the person who can speak warmly about God while remaining cold, hostile, or unforgiving toward people. Such a person is not simply inconsistent; John says he is “a liar,” because his life contradicts his confession.

So the verse exposes the self-deception of claiming vertical love for God while denying horizontal love to neighbor. Genuine love for the unseen God must become visible in love for the seen brother.
