**Realistic about self-deception, not mainly pessimistic.**

Jeremiah 17:9–10 is one of the Bible’s clearest statements on the *depth* of human self-deception rather than a blanket declaration that humans are as evil as possible in every moment. The verse does not say “the heart is the most evil thing in existence,” but that it is “deceitful above all things” (or “crooked, twisted”) and “desperately sick/wicked” (the Hebrew *ʿāqōb* and *ʾānūsh* carry the sense of incurably treacherous and morally wounded). The rhetorical question “Who can know it?” is answered immediately by God: *He* searches the heart and tests the mind (“reins” = the seat of emotion and will). So the passage is diagnostic, not despairing.

### Why “realistic about self-deception” fits better
- The immediate literary context (Jeremiah 17) is a contrast between trusting in human strength (“the man who trusts in man,” v. 5) and trusting in the Lord (v. 7). The diagnosis of the heart explains *why* self-trust is so dangerous: we lie to ourselves more skillfully than to anyone else.
- Parallel passages (Gen 6:5; 8:21; Ps 14:1–3; 51:5; 139:23–24; Eccl 9:3; Matt 15:19; Rom 3:9–18; 7:18–23) show a consistent biblical anthropology: the human heart is *bent* toward self-justification, rationalization, and hidden idolatry, yet it is not utterly annihilated or incapable of any good. Total depravity (in the classical theological sense) means corruption touches every faculty, not that every person is as bad as they could possibly be.
- The very next clause—“I the Lord search the heart”—prevents nihilism. The passage is pessimistic *apart from God*, but realistic *in light of God*. Self-knowledge is possible only because God knows us better than we know ourselves and is willing to reveal our motives.

### How to talk about motives when the heart is an unreliable witness

Because the heart is deceitful, biblical wisdom calls for **epistemic humility + external accountability + dependence on God**. Practical ways to speak about motives:

1. **Default to provisional language instead of absolute claims**  
   - Bad: “I’m doing this purely for God’s glory.”  
   - Better: “As far as I can tell, my desire is to glorify God, but I know my heart can mix in selfish ambition. Will you help me examine it?”

2. **Invite others to speak into your motives** (Prov 11:14; 12:15; 27:6; Gal 6:1–2)  
   The same passage that says the heart is unknowable to us says God *searches* it. One of the chief ways God searches us is through his Word, his Spirit, and his people. Trusted, mature believers often see blind spots we cannot. Asking “What do you see in me that I might not see?” is an act of obedience to Jeremiah 17:9–10.

3. **Use the fruit test that the passage itself gives**  
   God judges “according to his ways, and according to the fruit of his doings.” Motives are ultimately revealed by patterns of behavior over time, not by momentary feelings of sincerity. Jesus (Matt 7:15–20; 12:33–37) and James (3:13–18) make the same point. Talk about motives by talking about *patterns* rather than claiming to have pure ones.

4. **Regularly pray Psalm 139:23–24 and Psalm 19:12–14**  
   “Search me, O God, and know my heart; test me and know my anxious thoughts. See if there is any offensive way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting.”  
   The Bible’s answer to “who can know it?” is not “try harder to know yourself,” but “ask the One who already does.”

5. **Distinguish between**:
   - **First-order motives** (what I consciously intend)
   - **Second-order motives** (deeper loves, fears, or idols that may be mixed in or even primary)
   - **Ultimate heart orientation** (whether I am fundamentally living out of the flesh or the Spirit—Rom 8:5–9)

   We can usually see the first, sometimes glimpse the second, and must continually submit the third to God and to the church.

### Pastoral summary
Jeremiah 17:9–10 is sobering realism, not cynicism. It destroys naïve self-confidence and self-congratulatory “authenticity” language. At the same time, it does not leave us in paralysis. Because God searches the heart, we are free to be honest about its unreliability. The healthiest way to talk about motives is with **humility before God, openness to others, attention to fruit, and continual prayer for divine scrutiny**. That posture turns the bad news of the deceitful heart into the good news of being known and loved by the One who searches it.
