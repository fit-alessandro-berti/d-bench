
Great question! This is a classic passage from Luke 6:37, often called the “Be Like Christ” verse. Let’s unpack its meaning and contrast it with the claim that “moral judgments never matter.”

#### The Text: “Judge not, and ye shall not be judged...”

At first glance, this seems to discourage all moral evaluation or judgment. But the verse is nuanced. The Greek word translated as “judge” is *dikaiosune*, which originally means “to decide or to pass judgment.” However, in its ethical sense, it refers more to making a formal assessment—often with a sense of condemnation or condemnation *in your own heart*.

So, the verse is saying:

- If you are quick to judge others (to condemn, label, or pass moral judgment), then similarly you will be judged by God (possibly for your own sin or hypocrisy).
- The command is not to *never* recognize wrongdoing or injustice, but rather not to *impose your judgments on others* in the same way you’d impose them on yourself.

#### The Distinction: Discernment vs. Appetite to Condemn

**1. Discernment (Recognizing Reality) vs. Condemnation (Passing Judgment):**
- **Discernment** is the ability to perceive, understand, or recognize a situation, a motive, or a moral issue. It’s an act of attention, not condemnation.
    - Example: Noticing that someone is lying, or that a policy is unjust, is discernment. You see what’s happening.
- **Appetite to condemn** is the urge to judge, to label, to say “that person is evil,” “that action is wrong,” or “they deserve punishment.” It’s an emotional or moral response that goes beyond observation to ascription of blame or guilt.

The verse is not telling us to ignore wrongdoing. It’s telling us not to *pre-judge* or to *project our own judgment onto others in the same way we judge ourselves. We should be like Christ, who showed mercy and understanding even when we see sin.

**2. Why “Moral Judgments Never Matter” Is Different**

Saying “moral judgments never matter” is a much stronger claim:
- It would mean that it’s not only wrong to condemn others, but that *all* moral evaluation is meaningless or futile.
- It dismisses the entire enterprise of moral responsibility, ethics, and accountability.

But the passage does *not* say that. It says we should avoid the *hypocrisy* of judging others as we would ourselves—i.e., not to be double standard. It leaves room for recognizing wrongdoing and caring about it, but with a heart open to forgiveness and compassion.

**3. The Heart of the Commandment**

The real heart of the verse is about *how we relate to others*:
- We are called to approach others with the same mercy and readiness to forgive that we would like to receive.
- It’s about our *attitude*—not about the reality of wrongdoing or the importance of ethics.
- It’s not a denial of morality, but a call for humility and self-awareness in our moral judgments.

#### Practical Example

Imagine you see a friend who has made a mistake. If you respond by saying, “You’re such a fool for doing that!” you are judging in the same way you’d judge yourself, and the verse warns that such a posture could lead to spiritual judgment. But if you say, “I understand you were in a tough spot. I forgive you,” you are showing the kind of heart the verse demands.

#### In Summary

- **Discernment** is seeing and understanding.
- **Appetite to condemn** is judging and blaming.
- The verse isn’t saying we can’t recognize wrong or care about morality; it’s saying we should avoid judging others as we would ourselves, and respond with the same mercy we’d like to receive.
- Saying “moral judgments never matter” ignores the call to be compassionate, humble, and self-aware in our moral responses.

**Therefore, the verse calls for a transformed moral life—not moral amnesia, but moral humility and love.**
