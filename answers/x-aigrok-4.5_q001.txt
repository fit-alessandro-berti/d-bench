**Sin is portrayed as a crouching beast (predator) that desirably targets the person, with clear moral agency and full responsibility placed on the person to master/rule it by choosing to do well.**

In Genesis 4:6–7 God addresses Cain’s anger and dejection after the preferred offering. The text presents:

- **“If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?”** — Right action is possible and brings acceptance/ favor. Moral success is framed as within reach through well-doing.
- **“And if thou doest not well, sin lieth at the door.”** — Failure to do well leaves one exposed. The Hebrew verb (*rābats*) for “lieth”/“croucheth” is the language of an animal lying in wait (often a beast of prey ready to spring). Sin is externalized and personified as something lurking at the threshold, poised rather than simply an internal impulse, vague force, settled habit, or abstract temptation. (The masculine pronouns for “his desire… rule over him,” even though *ḥaṭṭā’t* is grammatically feminine, reinforce the animal/personal agency imagery.)
- **“And unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him.”** — Synonymous structure with the language of domination earlier in Genesis (cf. the woman’s *tĕšûqâ* “desire” and the man’s rule in 3:16; also human rule over animals). Sin’s predatory “desire” is matched by an explicit imperative or assertion of human mastery: Cain is told he *can and must* rule over it.

### Moral-agency picture
The text draws a picture of **free, responsible agency under challenge**. Evil/sin is neither inevitable fate, pure inner compulsion, nor a habit already formed; it is a real, intrusive, beast-like power that woman and man must confront and dominate. Choice remains open (“if thou doest well…” / “if thou doest not well…”), and the burden of ruling sin is placed squarely on the person. God does not coerce, remove the threat, or predetermine the outcome; God warns, clarifies the alternatives, and assigns the duty of mastery. Cain is treated as a genuine moral agent capable of self-governance who will be held accountable for failing to rule (as the narrative immediately shows).

This is not quietistic or deterministic; it is an intensified call to vigilance and dominion. The door image implies proximity and urgency without inevitability—the threat is real, yet mastery is the assigned human responsibility. 

In short: evil appears as a crouching, desiring beast at the door; the person is charged with ruling over it by doing well.
