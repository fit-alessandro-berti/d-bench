This is an excellent and deeply probing question. The elder brother's reaction is one of the most psychologically rich moments in the parable, and pinning down his "deepest problem" requires looking at how these emotions interconnect.

Let's examine your proposed options, as they form a kind of cascade:

1.  **Anger and Envy (The Surface Symptoms):** These are the immediate, visible reactions. He is "angry," and his speech drips with resentment ("this son of yours... who has squandered your property"). His envy is palpable—he wants the celebration *he* feels he has earned. But these are likely the symptoms of a deeper condition.

2.  **Wounded Fairness & Pride in Obedience (The Core Identity):** This feels closer to the heart of the issue. His argument is rooted in a **transactional, merit-based worldview**. "I have slaved for you... I never disobeyed your orders." He sees his relationship with his father not as a son, but as a dutiful servant keeping a strict account. His obedience is his currency, and he expects a proportional reward. The father's grace shatters this economy, making his lifelong "investment" feel worthless. His pride isn't just in being obedient, but in being *more deserving* because of it.

3.  **Discomfort with Mercy Itself (The Deepest Problem):** This is the ultimate, spiritual root. The elder brother cannot tolerate **unmerited grace**. His entire sense of justice is based on desert—good behavior gets rewarded, bad behavior gets punished. The father's mercy is wildly disproportionate and disrupts this entire system.

    **The father’s final words point directly to this:** "Son, you are always with me, and everything I have is yours." The elder brother already *had* the inheritance, the relationship, the status. But he was so focused on the ledger, on the "fatted calf" as a *reward*, that he failed to enjoy the **abundance that was already his**. His problem is that he cannot rejoice in the father's joy because the father's joy is rooted in a mercy that ignores the ledger.

**In summary:** The elder brother's deepest problem is **a transactional heart that cannot comprehend or receive gratuitous love.** It manifests as anger and envy, is justified by a wounded sense of fairness and pride, and culminates in a fundamental rejection of the father's character—one defined by mercy rather than merit.

He is not just angry about the party. He is, in a sense, rejecting the father's very nature. The younger son was "lost" in a far country; the elder son is "lost" in the courtyard, unable to enter into the joy of the house because he cannot accept the terms on which that joy is given: grace.
