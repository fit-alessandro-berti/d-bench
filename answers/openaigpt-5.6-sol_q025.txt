The problem is not **visibility itself**, but **performing goodness in order to gain an audience’s admiration**.

The key phrase is “**to be seen of them**.” The Greek expresses purpose: doing righteous acts *so that* others will notice. In that case:

- the needy person becomes a means of self-promotion;
- devotion to God becomes a performance;
- human praise replaces the Father’s approval as the desired reward.

This does not contradict Matthew 5:16: “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and **glorify your Father**.” The distinction is whom the visible act is meant to glorify:

- **Matthew 5:16:** people see the good and praise God.
- **Matthew 6:1:** people see the giver and praise the giver.

Jesus’ commands about giving secretly are therefore both a warning and a spiritual discipline. Secrecy helps expose and weaken the desire for applause. Some good works must necessarily be public, but public recognition must not be their controlling motive.

So public goodness is morally dangerous because visibility makes goodness easy to convert into **self-display**. The danger lies chiefly in the craving to be seen, though deliberately arranging publicity may reveal and strengthen that craving.
