Isaiah pairs **“way”** and **“thoughts”** to describe repentance as a turning of the whole person.

- **“Way”** (Hebrew *derek*) means one’s course of life—habitual conduct, choices, and direction. The wicked must not merely regret sin but abandon the path they have been walking.
- **“Thoughts”** (Hebrew *maḥšĕbôt*) means plans, purposes, calculations, and inward designs. It includes more than involuntary or passing thoughts: it is the inner reasoning and imagination by which sinful conduct is conceived and justified.

The two terms are parallel and overlapping rather than an absolute division between “external” and “internal.” Together they show that biblical repentance is neither mere behavioral reform nor merely a changed attitude. Outward conduct must be forsaken, and the inward purposes that produce and excuse it must also be surrendered.

The repeated language of turning is important:

> “forsake … and let him return unto the Lord”

Repentance is not only turning **from** a sinful way but returning **to** God personally. It therefore involves restored allegiance and trust, not simply self-improvement.

The following verses reinforce the contrast:

> “For my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways” (Isaiah 55:8).

Human ways and plans lead away from God; God’s ways and purposes—especially in this context—include a mercy more generous than sinners might imagine. The promise is therefore striking: the returning sinner meets not reluctant tolerance but mercy, because God “will abundantly pardon.” Repentance does not purchase that pardon; it is the turning by which the sinner abandons resistance and receives God’s offered mercy.

Thus the passage holds together three truths:

1. **Conduct matters:** sinful patterns must be left behind.  
2. **The inner life matters:** sinful intentions, plans, and self-justifications must also change.  
3. **Mercy remains possible:** however deep the outward and inward corruption, the Lord promises abundant pardon to the one who returns—hence the urgency to seek him “while he is near.”
