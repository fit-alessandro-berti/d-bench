### Luke 6:37

This passage does not simply say that moral issues can be ignored; rather, it establishes a relationship between how we handle judgment and how we are treated by God. Jesus’ command is about protecting yourself from divine condemnation through the act of mercy, while acknowledging that human understanding (our “judgments”) requires discernment to be accurate.

If someone suggests that **"moral judgments never matter,"** they are often speaking in a way that implies moral neutrality or objectivism: the idea that truth and wrongness exist independently of human perception. This would lead to an extreme form of relativism where no one could truly claim responsibility for being right or wrong in a moral sense, because "truth" is just something people decide it is without consequence.

**The fundamental difference** comes down to three key areas:
1.  **Action vs. Inaction:** Jesus’ teaching is prescriptive ("Judge *not*"), not negative ("You will not be judged"). This means the command is a protection for those choosing justice over mere human opinion.
2.  **Relationship vs. Neutrality:** If moral judgments "never mattered," then forgiveness would be impossible; there’s no guilt to forgive. But Luke implies that while we are forgiven because God knows our truth, we must not use our own limited perspective to condemn others.
3.  **Truth vs. Condemnation:** To judge is to evaluate something as right or wrong against a standard. The verse calls us to avoid this evaluation by trusting in the source of truth (Jesus), rather than trying to "solve" human problems with moral logic.

**The Difference Between Discernment and the Appetite to Condemn**
Once we understand why Luke 6:37 differs from a view that judgment isn't real, we can look at how we *discern* versus how we **condemn**.

#### Discernment: The Heart’s Alignment with Truth.
Discernment in Jesus’ teaching involves recognizing that God knows the human condition better than anyone else. We must discern where our heart is (for example, if it is focused on others or God). It is a process of understanding *how* truth appears to us through prayer and community. Discernment is not about saying "No one has the right to say X is wrong." It is about recognizing what God says when we turn toward Him with honesty.

The verse says, **"Judge not... ye shall not be judged."** This implies that true discernment leads us away from condemnation of others. When we try to understand why a person behaves wrongly or why something happens, we are trying to "judge." Jesus teaches that if we stop trying to judge ourselves and others, we receive forgiveness because God has the ultimate truth in His own wisdom.

#### The Appetite to Condemn: The Desire for Moral Superiority.
Condemnation does not merely mean deciding a sin or an action is wrong; it is the **appetite**—the desire to say "You are wrong" out of fear, ego, or superiority. This appetite often comes from our own limited perspective, not from divine truth.

*   **The Consequence:** If we condemn, as Jesus teaches ("you shall not be condemned"), we must realize that if we rely on our own understanding rather than God’s, we risk falling under judgment ourselves.
*   **The Danger:** If moral judgments "never matter," then condemnation is meaningless. But Luke shows us that condemnation *does* happen (it is a consequence of human failure). The danger isn't just that we are wrong; it's that if we condemn rather than love, we destroy the very relationship that allows God to be truly with us.

**Conclusion.**
Therefore, Luke 6:37 differs from "judgments never matter" by emphasizing the active, relational choice of mercy over the passive neutrality of moral opinion. It teaches that true wisdom is not about knowing every fact perfectly but about aligning judgment (our hearts) with what we know of God's love. This requires discernment to understand our own sinfulness, and it rejects the appetite to condemn simply because it keeps us from the "forgiveness" of grace."

The verse is a command to **love**, not just to **judge**. It invites us to choose mercy by trusting that if we judge with self-interest, we will be condemned; but if we discern truth through God's love, we are forgiven.
