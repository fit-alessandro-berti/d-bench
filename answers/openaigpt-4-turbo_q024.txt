The passage from Ezekiel 36:26-27 highlights a profound theological concept, particularly within the context of Old Testament prophetic literature. The difference between external compliance and receiving a “new heart” is central to the understanding of inward transformation versus mere outward conformity to laws or norms.

1. **External Compliance:** 
   - This involves obeying laws, commandments, or norms from an external motivation. Such obedience might stem from fear of punishment, desire for reward, or social pressures. The behavior is conditioned by factors external to the individual's inner convictions or values. The compliance may not reflect an individual's true nature; it can be shallow and easily mutable based on changing circumstances or external influences.

2. **Receiving a ‘New Heart’:**
   - This phrase represents a radical internal transformation. It signifies a deep change in a person's core motivations, desires, and inclinations. The "new heart" is not merely about following rules but involves a genuine transformation of character and desire, aligning the individual with God's will and desires sincerely and from within.
   - A "new heart" means being fundamentally directed towards the love of God and others, driven by an internalized spirit of God's laws and compassion, rather then external enforcement.

The text uses the metaphor of changing a "stony heart" with a "heart of flesh" to convey the profound nature of this change:

- **Stony Heart:** Refers to a heart that is hardened, unresponsive, and insensible. It symbolizes a resistance to divine influence and an inability to receive or respond to Godly impulses or commands with genuine feelings. It's like a barrier that prevents true emotional and spiritual engagement with God’s law.
  
- **Heart of Flesh:** In contrast, a fleshly heart suggests one that is alive, responsive, and sensitive. It's receptive and open to divine influence and embodies the qualities necessary for a true and deep relationship with God. This heart is tender and capable of true love and genuine repentance.

The transformation from a "stony heart" to a "heart of flesh" also suggests that true spiritual obedience requires internal transformation, not merely external adherence. The hardness is described almost like a material condition possibly because it conveys the depth and resistance of the human condition away from God’s statutes and the dramatic nature of divine intervention required to change it. 

This transformation and renewal by God's spirit (verse 27) is what empowers the individual to "walk in my statutes, and ye shall keep my judgments, and do them." It emphasizes that observance of God's laws is not superficial but is enabled and sustained by God's own spirit dwelling within, leading to a life that naturally expresses God's will and character.
