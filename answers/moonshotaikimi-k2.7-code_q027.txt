In this parable Jesus is diagnosing one spiritual sickness from several angles. None of the five things you mention can really be separated, because they all grow from the same root: **self-righteousness**—“trusting in themselves that they were righteous, and despising others” (Luke 18:9).

Here is how each element appears in the Pharisee, and why they converge in condemnation:

| What the Pharisee does | Why it condemns him |
|---|---|
| **Pride / self-trust** | He “prayed thus **with himself**” (v. 11). His prayer is really a monologue; God is a spectator, not the source of mercy. His opening, “God, I thank thee,” is actually self-congratulation. |
| **Comparison** | He measures himself against “other men” and finds them worse. His righteousness is entirely comparative: he is good because someone else is bad. |
| **Contempt** | He singles out the publican—“or even as this publican”—and despises him in the very place of worship. The parable is addressed to people who “despised others.” |
| **Use of virtue as self-display** | Fasting and tithing are lawful and good, but he turns them into exhibits in a courtroom of his own making. Virtue becomes currency for boasting. |
| **False doctrine** | He believes that his moral performance and religious observance place him in the right before God and entitle him to look down on others. He has no sense that he, too, needs mercy. |

So the Pharisee is not condemned merely for being religious, nor merely for being proud. He is condemned because **his piety has become the ground of his confidence before God**, and that confidence expresses itself in contempt for a fellow sinner.

### Why the tax collector’s posture is “truer”

The publican’s posture is not praised as a theatrical pose. It is the outward shape of an inward truth:

- He **stands afar off**, as if unworthy to draw near.
- He **will not lift up his eyes to heaven**, because he feels no claim on God.
- He **beats his breast**, the gesture of grief and shame.
- His words are bare and complete: “God be merciful to me a sinner.”

He does not compare himself with anyone. He does not excuse himself. He does not offer God a résumé of religious achievements. He simply acknowledges that he is a sinner and throws himself on God’s mercy. The Greek word for “be merciful” (*hilasthēti*) carries the idea of **atonement or propitiation**; he is asking God to deal with his sin, not to reward his virtue.

That is why Jesus says, “this man went down to his house justified rather than the other.” The tax collector’s posture matches the actual situation every human being stands in before God: **no one is righteous by his own record; everyone must be received by mercy.**

### The one-point summary

The Pharisee is condemned because he treats his religion as a reason God should accept him, and treats other people as evidence of his own superiority. The tax collector is justified because he knows he has no reason for God to accept him, and so he asks for mercy. In Luke’s terms: **“every one that exalteth himself shall be abased; and he that humbleth himself shall be exalted”** (18:14).
