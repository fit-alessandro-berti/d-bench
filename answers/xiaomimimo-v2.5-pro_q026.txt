This passage draws a profound and intimate connection between our speech and our inner moral state, using vivid metaphors to illustrate a deeply integrated view of the human person. Let's break down how it connects them and then address your specific question about words as symptoms, choices, and revelations.

### The Core Connection: "Out of the Abundance of the Heart"

Jesus uses two powerful images:
1.  **The Heart as a Source (an "Abundance" or "Overflow"):** The Greek word *perisseuma* means "that which is over and above, abundance, surplus." The heart isn't a quiet pond but an overflowing spring. Our words are the inevitable runoff from whatever fills that reservoir.
2.  **The Heart as a Treasury (or "Storehouse"):** The word *thesaurus* (from which we get "thesaurus") refers to a treasure-chest or storehouse. A person's moral character is depicted as a cache of accumulated "treasure"—good or evil.

The passage states that **speech is the inevitable expression and revelation of what is stored in this treasury.** It’s not merely a surface behavior but a diagnostic indicator of one’s core identity.

### Are Words Symptoms, Choices, or Revelations?

Based on Jesus' teaching here, words are **all three, in a sequence.**

**1. Words are a *Revelation* (and thus a *Symptom*) of the Heart's Condition.**
This is the most direct point. Jesus states it as a fact: "for out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh." A good treasure reveals a good heart; an evil treasure reveals an evil heart. In this sense, words are a **symptom**—a visible, audible sign of an internal spiritual condition. You cannot have a heart filled with genuine goodness and consistently speak malice, just as you cannot have a store of rotten fruit and yet consistently produce fresh, ripe ones. Words *reveal* the hidden moral stockpile.

**2. Words are also a *Choice*, but a Choice with Roots.**
This is the crucial nuance. While words reveal the heart's state, this passage is part of Jesus' pointed rebuke to the Pharisees (Matthew 12:24, 34). He is challenging their hypocrisy. They made conscious *choices* to speak slanderously (accusing Jesus of casting out demons by Beelzebul). Their words were deliberate acts of will.

However, Jesus connects those choices to a deeper, prior cause: the "evil treasure" they had stored in their hearts. So, while the speech act is a choice, **it is a choice that flows from and is shaped by the moral character one has already cultivated (or neglected).** A good person, *because of their stored-up goodness*, makes the choice to speak good. An evil person, *because of their stored-up evil*, makes the choice to speak evil. The word "choice" applies to the speech act itself, but the *predisposition* for that choice is the stored "treasure."

**3. Words as *Symptom* and *Revelation* Feed Back into Future Choices.**
Here is the feedback loop. If words are symptoms that reveal a diseased heart (evil treasure), then recognizing this symptom is the first step in making new choices about what to *store* in the heart. Jesus implies this earlier in Matthew 12: "Either make the tree good and its fruit good, or make the tree bad and its fruit bad" (v. 33). The call is not just to change your speech, but to undergo a heart transformation—to change the contents of your treasury.

### Conclusion

Matthew 12:34–35 teaches that:
*   **Speech is primarily a *revelation* and *symptom* of the heart's hidden contents.** It is the overflowing evidence of our inner moral "treasure."
*   **Speech is also a *choice*, but it is a choice deeply rooted in and conditioned by that pre-existing treasure.** The Pharisees chose to speak evil, but they chose it *because* they were evil.
*   Therefore, the passage suggests that **authentic change begins not just with policing our words (the symptoms), but with tending to the source—the heart itself.** To speak good things out of the "good treasure of the heart," one must first have stored up goodness there. Words are not just isolated actions; they are the inevitable fruit of an internal spiritual ecosystem.

In short, your words are the **symptoms** that **reveal** the state of your heart's treasury, and from that treasury, you make **choices** in speech that are consistent with its contents.
