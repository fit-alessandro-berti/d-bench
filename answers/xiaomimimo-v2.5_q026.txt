# The Heart as Treasury: Speech as Overflow

This is a rich question that gets at the layered logic of the metaphor. I think the answer is indeed **all three**, but in an integrated way rather than a simple list. Let me unpack how.

---

## The Metaphor's Structure

Jesus uses three interlocking images:

- **Abundance/overflow** — water rising until it spills
- **Treasure/treasury** — a stored reserve, accumulated over time
- **Bringing forth** — active production, bearing fruit

These correspond loosely to your three categories, and the genius is that they describe a **process with temporal depth**.

---

## Words as Symptoms (the Overflow)

The "abundance" language suggests something almost involuntary. When a cup overflows, the cup doesn't *decide* to spill. The Pharisees' slander against Jesus wasn't a carefully reasoned argument — it was what spilled out when pressure was applied. Speech as symptom says: *I can diagnose your inner state by what emerges unbidden.*

## Words as Choices (the Treasury)

But "treasure" implies **accumulation** — stockpiles don't appear overnight. Someone filled that treasury through repeated choices: what they dwell on, entertain, cultivate, rehearse. The "bringing forth" language also suggests agency. A good man doesn't accidentally produce good things; he draws from reserves he built. Speech as choice says: *you are responsible for what you've stored up.*

## Words as Revelations (the Treasure Exposed)

"Treasure" is also hidden by nature — it's kept in a treasury, not displayed. Speech **makes the interior exterior**. The heart was always there, but invisible. Now it's visible. Speech as revelation says: *your words are windows into what was concealed.*

---

## The Deeper Integration

What makes this passage so piercing is the **sequence**:

1. Repeated choices → accumulate into character (the treasury fills)
2. Character → produces speech naturally (the overflow)
3. Speech → reveals what was hidden (the treasure exposed)

The Pharisees aren't being condemned merely for saying something wrong in a moment of poor judgment. Jesus is saying: *this didn't come from nowhere. This is what you've been building. Your words are not a slip — they're a signature.*

---

## The Moral Weight

This framework carries uncomfortable implications:

- **No one's speech is neutral.** Every utterance draws from *some* treasury.
- **You can't outrun your accumulation.** Under enough pressure, the overflow reveals the contents.
- **Self-deception is possible but limited.** You may hide your treasury from others, but your mouth will eventually serve as witness.

So the passage suggests that words are **symptoms** (natural expressions), **choices** (products of accumulated agency), and **revelations** (exposures of hidden reality) — and that these three aspects are inseparable aspects of a single moral ecology.

The question it leaves us with: *What am I filling my treasury with?*
