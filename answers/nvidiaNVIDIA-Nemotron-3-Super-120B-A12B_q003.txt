

The Micah 6:8 exhortation is “demanding’’ because it packs three interlocking duties into a single sentence:  

1. **Do justly** – act in accordance with what is right and fair.  
2. **Love mercy** – show compassion, kindness and forgiveness toward others.  
3. **Walk humbly** – recognize our dependence on God and keep pride in check.  

These three virtues correct one another in a continual feedback loop:

- **Justice tempers mercy.**  Pure justice can become harsh, legalistic, or even vindictive if it is not softened by compassion.  Mercy reminds us that right‑doing must also be generous and forgiving, preventing justice from turning into mere rigor or cruelty.  

- **Mercy tempers justice.**  Unchecked mercy can drift into permissiveness, excuse‑making, or sentimentality that lets wrongdoing slide by.  Justice supplies the steady standard of fairness that keeps mercy from becoming lax or self‑indulgent.  

- **Humility grounds both.**  Pride is the common enemy of justice and mercy; when we walk humbly we acknowledge that our sense of what is right and what is merciful comes from God, not from our own ego.  Humility thus keeps justice from becoming self‑righteous arrogance and keeps mercy from turning into self‑pitying‑or‑self‑aggrandizement.  

In short, the summary is demanding because it asks us to hold three qualities that continuously check and balance each another.  Justice needs mercy to stay humane; mercy needs justice to stay firm; and both need humility to stay rooted in God.  Together they form a virtuous circle: acting justly leads to merciful behavior, which in turn is sustained by a humble walk before God, and that humble walk keeps our justice and mercy from distorting into pride or laxness.  This interlocking demand is what makes Micah 6:8 a compact yet challenging moral compass.
