## The Double Command and the Whole Person

The pairing of *derek* ("way") and *machshavah* ("thought") in Isaiah 55:7 is not redundancy but a deliberate anatomy of the human person. Hebrew anthropology does not slice the self into isolated compartments of "outward" and "inward," yet it does recognize that human life has a *trajectory* (the way one walks) and an *origin* (the deliberations from which action springs). True repentance must address both, because sin operates in both.

### The "Way" — More Than Isolated Acts

*Derekh* is not simply a single action but the **settled course of a life** — the path one has worn into habit, the direction one's feet have learned. To "forsake his way" means more than feeling sorry for a few deeds. It means turning around on the road itself, abandoning the trajectory that had become second nature. The OT wisdom tradition uses *derek* precisely this way: the way of the righteous versus the way of the wicked (Ps. 1:1, 6; Prov. 4:18–19). Outward conduct, then, is treated as the visible expression of a chosen direction — repentance must reach down to the level of the direction itself, not merely prune individual missteps.

### The "Thoughts" — The Moral Weight of Imagination

Even more striking is the inclusion of *machshevot* — **plans, devisings, the workings of the deliberative and imaginative mind**. The OT considers the heart (*lev*) and the thoughts to be morally serious territory. Long before modern psychology, the prophets saw that murder begins in anger, adultery in lust, idolatry in the crafting of competing loyalties (cf. Gen. 6:5; Jer. 4:14; Matt. 5:21–28). To be told to "forsake his thoughts" is to recognize that **imagination is not a neutral playground**. The plans I form, the scenarios I rehearse, the futures I picture — these are acts of the person, and they can be aligned against God even when no visible deed has yet emerged.

This is why the verse pairs the two: external behavior without internal reorientation produces the kind of religion the prophets repeatedly condemn — a worship that is " lips" while the heart is far (Isa. 29:13). Repentance of *thoughts* means bringing the planning faculty itself under the lordship of God, so that what is imagined and deliberated is itself brought into the light.

### The Structure: Repentance as Precondition, Not Purchase

Now consider what this does to the doctrine of mercy. Notice the sequence in v. 7:

> Forsake the way → forsake the thoughts → return to the Lord → **he will have mercy** → return to our God → **he will abundantly pardon**.

The two-fold forsaking comes *before* the divine response. Yet the divine response is described in extraordinary terms: *yarbeh liṣloaḥ* — "he will multiply to pardon," "abundantly pardon." The same God who insists on so thorough a repentance is the one who promises not reluctant forgiveness but a lavishly disproportionate one.

This is not a transaction in which human repentance earns divine mercy by its intrinsic worth. Rather, the repentance is what makes one **capable of receiving what is already on offer**. A person still planning evil cannot absorb pardon; it would curdle. The mercy is *abundant* — but the person must be turned around enough to receive it. God's "while he may be found" and "while he is near" (v. 6) underline the urgency: the offer is real, but it has a window, because the form a life takes can become hardened against receiving what is freely given.

### What It Implies

1. **Outward conduct is never merely outward** — it is the visible flow of an inner life, and repentance must reach the level of direction, not just deed.
2. **Inward imagination is never merely private** — it is the moral workshop where one's actual life is built, and it must be brought under repentance.
3. **Mercy is not cheap because repentance is deep.** The demand is total, but the pardon is described as overflowing. The very thoroughness of what must be forsaken highlights the abundance of what God pledges to give.
4. **The whole person is the unit of response.** The Hebrew self is integrated, and so the call to return addresses the integrated self — feet, hands, plans, and heart — because the God who invites is the one who made and seeks the whole.
