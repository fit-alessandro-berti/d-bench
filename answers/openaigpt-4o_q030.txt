The passage from 1 Corinthians 13:4–7 outlines a vision of love characterized by patience, kindness, and humility, contrasting sharply with traits such as pride, envy, irritability, and suspicion. The repeated negations of these negative qualities serve several purposes.

1. **Counteracting Human Tendencies**: Pride, envy, and irritability are common human tendencies that can damage relationships. By highlighting what love is not, the text encourages individuals to be aware of these tendencies and consciously work against them.

2. **Defining True Love**: The negations help to define true love by eliminating qualities that are self-centered and destructive. Love is shown not to be about self-aggrandizement or self-interest but about selflessness and care for others.

3. **Vision of Strength:** The passage proposes a vision of strength that is not about dominance or control but about endurance, resilience, and genuine care. True strength in love is characterized by the ability to withstand difficulties, maintain hope, and continue in belief and truth.

4. **Emphasizing Virtue over Vice**: In affirming what love does – rejoices in the truth, bears, believes, hopes, and endures all things – the passage underscores virtues that build up individuals and communities rather than tearing them down.

Overall, the negations emphasize a love that is transformative, promoting growth and unity. This vision of love suggests that true strength lies in the capacity to transcend base impulses and embody virtues that foster genuine connections and harmony.
