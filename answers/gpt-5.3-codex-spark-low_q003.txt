• Micah 6:8 is short but not simple. It compresses the whole covenant vocation into three checks on each other so it
  can’t be reduced to only one good intention.

  - “Do justly” demands alignment with right, truth, and fairness. It pushes against bias, exploitation, and
    indifference.
  - “Love mercy” refuses to let justice become cold punishment or self-righteous legalism. Mercy calls for restoration,
    compassion, patience, and willingness to bear with weakness.
  - “Walk humbly with your God” keeps both from becoming ego projects: you are not the final judge or savior.

  So it is demanding because each part corrects a different possible excess:

  1. Justice without mercy becomes harsh, punitive, and legalistic.
  2. Mercy without justice becomes sentimentality or permissiveness that leaves harm unaddressed.
  3. Humility without justice and mercy becomes private spirituality detached from public responsibility or relational
     accountability.

  Together they form one ethical shape:

  - justice speaks to what you do,
  - mercy governs how you do it,
  - humility keeps who does it in the right place.

  The result is not an impossible law, but an integrated way of living that resists idolizing power, purity, or
  performance. It is demanding because it requires a whole-person response—right action, compassionate intent, and a
  grounded dependence on God at the same time.
