## The Craving, Not the Visibility

The verse itself answers the question with surprising precision. The Greek behind "to be seen of them" is *pros to theathēnai autois* — a purpose clause. The condemnation falls not on alms that *happen* to be seen, but on alms performed *in order* to be seen. The danger lies in the intention, the inner orientation of the act.

This reading is confirmed by an apparent contradiction just one chapter earlier. In Matthew 5:16, Jesus commands the opposite: "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works." If visibility itself were the poison, Scripture would be at war with itself. The resolution is in the endings of each verse: in 5:16, others see your works and "glorify your Father"; in 6:1, you act so that others glorify *you*. Same audience, same visibility — entirely different gravitational center.

## Why the Craving Corrupts

Several things make the desire-to-be-seen morally dangerous:

**1. It changes what the act actually is.** An alm given for applause is not generosity wearing a costume — it is a *purchase*. The giver trades money for admiration. Jesus' language in the following verses is commercial: *apechousin ton misthon* — "they have received their reward in full," a phrase used on ancient receipts. Payment rendered, transaction closed. The act was never charity; it was theater. (Fittingly, the word "hypocrite" — *hypokritēs* — originally meant *stage actor*.)

**2. It quietly replaces God with the crowd.** Whoever's gaze you perform for is, functionally, your god. The craving to be seen relocates the soul's center of gravity from heaven to the audience — and makes the self the true beneficiary of every "selfless" act.

**3. It instrumentalizes the poor.** The recipient of the alms becomes a prop in someone else's reputation project. The needy person is no longer the point of the act but its occasion.

**4. It is self-deceiving.** This is perhaps the deepest danger. Outward goodness that feeds vanity *feels* like virtue from the inside. The hypocrite of Matthew 6 may genuinely believe himself righteous — that's what makes the warning necessary. Hence Jesus' radical remedy in 6:3: "let not thy left hand know what thy right hand doeth" — concealment not as a rule but as a discipline, a way of starving the appetite for applause until even *self*-congratulation is hard to come by.

## The Sharper Point

So the problem is not being seen — it is *needing* to be seen. Visibility is morally neutral; some good must be public to function (justice, teaching, leadership, witness). But the craving converts goodness into currency, and currency spent on reputation cannot also be offered to God. You can have the reward of the crowd or the reward of the Father, Jesus says — but the same act cannot be cashed twice.

The verse, in the end, is less a rule about where to give than a question about *whose eyes you live before*.
