# Judgment, Discernment, and the Appetite to Condemn

## The Common Misreading

The verse is frequently flattened into a general prohibition on moral evaluation, producing the familiar cultural maxim: *"You can't judge me."* This reading makes the passage self-refuting almost immediately — claiming someone **is wrong to judge** is itself a moral judgment. Jesus cannot plausibly be commanding the elimination of moral reasoning, since he issues moral commands throughout the same sermon.

Something more precise is being targeted.

---

## What the Text Actually Addresses

The Greek verb *krinō* carries a specific weight — not merely *noticing* or *evaluating*, but rendering **a verdict with finality**, positioning oneself as the adjudicating authority over another person's standing. The parallel structure is revealing:

| Command | What it opposes |
|--------|----------------|
| "Judge not" | Pronouncing final verdicts |
| "Condemn not" | Consigning someone to their worst moment |
| "Forgive" | The active alternative offered |

The passage is not abolishing the moral faculty. It is diagnosing a **particular posture** toward other people.

---

## The Crucial Distinction: Discernment vs. Condemnation

### Discernment is:
- **Situationally necessary** — Jesus in the same Gospel tells his disciples to recognize false prophets "by their fruits" (Matthew 7:16). You cannot do this without judgment.
- **Oriented toward reality** — its goal is accurate perception of what is true or harmful
- **Correctable** — it holds conclusions provisionally, remaining open to new information
- **Directed at actions and patterns** — it evaluates *what was done* rather than permanently categorizing the person

### The appetite to condemn is:
- **Self-serving** — it frequently involves using another's failure to establish one's own elevation
- **Totalizing** — it collapses the whole person into their worst act or attribute
- **Closed** — it resists evidence that might complicate the verdict
- **Emotionally satisfying in a suspicious way** — the pleasure it generates should itself be diagnostic

The difference can often be detected by asking: *Am I troubled by what this person did, or am I enjoying having something against them?*

---

## The Theological Anthropology Underneath

The verse rests on a claim about **epistemic position**. When human beings condemn finally, they are implicitly claiming:

1. Complete knowledge of the person's interior life, history, and formation
2. Perfect consistency in their own moral record
3. Authority to determine ultimate worth

All three claims are fraudulent for any human being. This is why the passage functions not primarily as a social rule but as a **correction of self-deception**. The person most eager to condemn is typically least aware of the beam in their own eye — the metaphor Jesus immediately deploys.

This is not moral relativism. It is **moral realism about the judge as much as the judged.**

---

## Why Moral Judgments Still Matter

Abandoning discernment in the name of non-judgment produces:

- **Inability to protect** — you cannot shield vulnerable people from predatory behavior without identifying predatory behavior as wrong
- **Collapse of accountability** — communities require shared standards to function
- **False kindness** — telling someone their destructive behavior is acceptable is not compassion; it is abandonment dressed as tolerance
- **Moral paralysis** — refusing to call anything wrong eventually means being unable to call *anything* right

The Sermon on the Mount does not counsel passivity toward injustice. Jesus overturns tables, names hypocrisy directly, and calls Herod a fox. The prophetic tradition he embodies is saturated with moral judgment about systems, behavior, and leaders.

---

## The Governing Principle

A working distinction might be stated this way:

> **Discernment** asks: *What is true here, and what does it require of me?*
> **Condemnation** asks: *How do I position myself above this person?*

The first is a form of attention and love, even when it names something as wrong. The second is a form of pride disguised as righteousness — which is precisely what the Pharisees in Luke's Gospel repeatedly demonstrate.

The command is not *"never evaluate"* but *"do not set yourself up as the final court."* That seat, the text implies, is already occupied.

---

## Conclusion

The verse is better understood as a **warning about judicial appetite than a prohibition on moral sight**. What Jesus targets is the human tendency to find identity, pleasure, and security in having verdicts against other people — the condemnatory posture that reduces persons to their failures and elevates the judge by comparison.

Discernment remains not only permitted but required. The difference lies in whether moral evaluation serves **truth and the good of others**, or whether it serves the ego's need for superiority. The person genuinely committed to the first will naturally resist the second — not because judgment never matters, but because they know precisely how much it does.
