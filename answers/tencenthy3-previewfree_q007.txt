The command in Luke 6:37 is one of the most frequently quoted—and frequently misunderstood—verses in the Bible. Taken in isolation, it can sound like a command for moral relativism: "Never say anything is wrong because if you do, you’re a hypocrite."

However, when read in context (both immediate and broader scriptural context), Jesus is not forbidding the exercise of moral reasoning or the protection of a community’s standards. Instead, He is distinguishing between **discernment** (assessing what is true) and **condemnation** (assessing a person's ultimate worth or final destiny).

Here is an exploration of the difference between moral indifference, discernment, and the appetite to condemn.

### 1. The Difference Between "Moral Judgments Never Matter" and Luke 6:37

If Jesus meant that moral judgments never matter, He would be contradicting His own ministry. Jesus constantly made moral judgments—He called out hypocrisy, denounced false teachings, and declared specific actions (like lust or hatred) to be sinful.

**Moral Relativism (The misinterpretation):**
*   **Premise:** "There is no objective standard of right and wrong, or it is rude/illegal to point it out."
*   **Result:** A refusal to call evil "evil." This leads to a passive acceptance of harm, injustice, and abuse under the guise of being "nice" or "tolerant."

**Luke 6:37 (The Biblical intent):**
*   **Premise:** "There is a standard of right and wrong (God’s holiness), but you are not the ultimate judge of the person violating it."
*   **Result:** A call to humility. We acknowledge the sin, but we remember that we stand equally in need of grace. We seek restoration, not destruction.

**The key distinction:** Luke 6:37 forbids a specific *posture* of the heart, not the *act* of distinguishing right from wrong.

### 2. Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn

To understand the difference, we must look at the motivation, the scope, and the goal of the judgment.

#### A. Scope: The Behavior vs. The Soul
*   **Discernment** looks at *actions* and *fruit*. Jesus said earlier in the same chapter (Luke 6:43-45), "A good tree does not bear bad fruit." That requires discernment—you must look at the fruit to know the tree. This is necessary for wisdom, safety, and spiritual growth.
*   **Condemnation** looks at the *person* and their *eternal standing*. It declares, "This person is beyond hope," or "God must surely reject this person." It usurps the role of God, who is the only one who knows the heart and the full context of a person's life.

#### B. Motivation: Love/Truth vs. The Appetite to Condemn
*   **Discernment** is motivated by love and a desire for truth. If you see a friend walking toward a cliff, discerning the danger allows you to warn them. You judge the path (dangerous) to save the person.
*   **The Appetite to Condemn** is motivated by pride, superiority, and often a desire for "righteous" vengeance. It is an emotional satisfaction derived from pointing out how others fall short. It seeks to elevate the judge ("At least I’m not like *that* person") rather than helping the neighbor.

#### C. Goal: Restoration vs. Destruction
*   **Discernment** aims for **restoration**. As Paul writes in Galatians 6:1, "If someone is caught in a sin, you who are spiritual should restore them gently." The word "restore" implies fixing something broken.
*   **Condemnation** aims for **isolation or destruction**. It seeks to cut the person off, shame them into submission, or marginalize them. It says, "You are your sin," rather than "You have sinned."

### 3. The "Measure" Principle (Luke 6:38)

Jesus follows verse 37 with: *"For with the measure you use, it will be measured to you."*

This highlights the difference:
*   **Discernment** uses a "measure" of grace. It says, "I see the fault, but I apply a measure of mercy because I hope someone will do the same for me."
*   **Condemnation** uses a "measure" of rigorism. It says, "I apply the strictest letter of the law to you, with zero context or mercy." Jesus warns that this measure will be applied back to you.

### Summary Table

| Feature | Discernment (Commended) | Condemnation (Forbidden) | Moral Indifference (Worldly) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Focus** | Actions, doctrine, "fruit." | The person's heart, motives, and destiny. | Ignoring actions entirely. |
| **Heart Posture** | Humble; aware of own sinfulness. | Proud; feels superior to the sinner. | Apathetic; unconcerned with holiness. |
| **Goal** | Truth, clarity, and restoration. | Shame, punishment, and exclusion. | Comfort and avoidance of conflict. |
| **Relationship to Law** | Upholds the standard while seeking mercy. | Uses the standard as a weapon. | Lowers the standard to avoid offense. |
| **Result** | Spiritual growth and safety. | Broken relationships and hypocrisy. | Decay of moral fabric. |

### Conclusion

Luke 6:37 is not a prohibition against having an opinion on morality; it is a prohibition against **playing God.**

Discernment says: *"That action is wrong, and I care about you too much to let you believe it is right."*

Condemnation says: *"You are a terrible person, and I am glad I am not like you."*

Jesus calls us to be people who see clearly (discernment) but love deeply (forgiveness), always remembering that the same measure of grace we extend to others is the measure we desperately need for ourselves.
