This passage presents a rigorous cause-and-effect relationship between the inner life and outward speech, using the metaphor of a treasury or storehouse to explain the mechanism of human expression.

Here is how the passage connects speech to the heart, followed by an analysis of whether words are symptoms, choices, or revelations.

### 1. The Connection: The Treasury of the Heart
The key term in Matthew 12:35 is **"treasure"** (Greek: *thesauros*), which implies a place where goods are stored, amassed, and kept for future use. This creates the image of the heart as a **moral stockpile**.

*   **Accumulation implies history:** A "treasure" is not built in a moment; it is accumulated over time. The passage suggests that every thought, fixation, and secret indulgence is a deposit into this internal vault. If a person dwells on malice, they are "stockpiling" evil. If they meditate on truth and love, they are amassing "good treasure."
*   **Speech as withdrawal:** The phrase "bringeth forth" implies a withdrawal from this internal bank. Speech is not viewed as a spontaneous, isolated event, but as the drawing out of resources that already exist inside.
*   **The connection is inevitable:** Jesus asks, "How can ye, being evil, speak good things?" This rhetorical question indicates that the connection is unbreakable. The mouth acts as a pressure valve for the heart. Whatever fills the heart to capacity ("abundance") must eventually overflow through the lips.

### 2. Are Words Symptoms, Choices, or Revelations?
The passage suggests that words are **all three**, functioning as distinct parts of a single moral process.

#### Words as Symptoms (The Nature Test)
In this passage, words function physiologically—they are symptoms of the heart's underlying condition.
*   Jesus addresses the Pharisees as a "generation of vipers." Just as a viper produces venom because it *is* a viper, the Pharisees produce evil speech because their nature is evil.
*   If the heart is the "root" and speech is the "fruit," then words are the symptoms of the tree's health. You cannot diagnose a tree as healthy if it bears poisonous fruit. Therefore, speech serves as an involuntary diagnostic tool, revealing the true state of the person even when they try to hide it.

#### Words as Revelations (The Unmasking)
If words are symptoms, they are also revelations—specifically, they reveal what is **hidden**.
*   The "heart" in biblical anthropology is the hidden center of the person—thoughts, motives, and desires that are invisible to others.
*   A person may successfully mask their "treasure" for a season through hypocritical behavior, but the "abundance" (the overflow) eventually betrays them. In the context of the chapter, the Pharisees were outwardly religious, but their blasphemous accusation against Jesus (attributing his miracles to Satan) revealed that their internal treasury was actually full of malice and unbelief. Speech acts as the lamp that exposes the contents of the dark room (Matthew 6:22-23).

#### Words as Choices (The Moral Agency)
While speech reveals what is *already* inside, the passage does not absolve the speaker of responsibility.
*   The text calls the man "evil," not merely "mistaken." The act of speaking is treated as a moral act ("bringeth forth"). The speaker is an active agent drawing from the treasury.
*   However, the "choice" aspect is nuanced. The passage suggests that the ultimate choice was made long before the word was spoken. The choice was made when the treasure was **stockpiled**.
*   *The moment of speech:* When the moment comes to speak, the mouth can only draw from what is available in the vault. If the vault contains only evil, the person is morally incapable of speaking true goodness. Thus, the "choice" of speech is the cumulative result of thousands of prior choices regarding what to allow into one's heart.

### Summary
The passage asserts that **speech is the audit of the soul.**

It connects speech to the heart by establishing that the mouth is merely the delivery system for whatever has been previously stored in the heart. Words are **revelations** because they expose hidden realities; they are **symptoms** because they indicate a deeper underlying nature; and they are **choices** insofar as they represent the final product of a lifetime of moral accumulation.
