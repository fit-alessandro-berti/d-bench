In Ephesians 4:31–32, Paul places bitterness and forgiveness in direct opposition because he is not merely giving tips on emotional management; he is describing the **fundamental operating system of the Christian life**.

To answer your question directly: The text treats bitterness not just as a feeling, but as a **habitual posture of the heart** that is highly **contagious**. Paul’s argument is that you cannot be a "new man" (v. 24) while retaining the old way of processing hurt.

Here is a breakdown of why these two concepts are placed in such sharp contrast and how the text defines bitterness.

### 1. Bitterness as a "Root" (The Posture)
While the word "bitterness" here (*pikria*) refers to the emotional state of being bitter, the wider context of Scripture (specifically Hebrews 12:15) treats bitterness as a **root**, not just a fruit.

*   **The Posture:** In the Greek, the command "Let... be put away" is in the active voice. It implies a decisive act of the will. You don't just "feel" bitterness; you **harbor** it. It is a chosen posture of the soul that refuses to let an offense die.
*   **The Contrast:** Paul contrasts this with being "tenderhearted" (*eusplagchnos*—literally "good bowels" or "good intestines"). In ancient anatomy, the bowels were the seat of deep emotion. Bitterness hardens the belly; tenderness softens it. Forgiveness is the act of unclenching the fist of the heart.

### 2. The Contagious Nature (The "Clamour and Evil Speaking")
You asked if bitterness is treated as something contagious. The text strongly suggests it is. Look at the chain Paul lists in verse 31:

> **Bitterness → Wrath → Anger → Clamour → Evil Speaking → Malice**

This is not a random list; it describes the **spread** of the infection:
1.  **Internal (Bitterness):** It starts hidden in the heart.
2.  **Volatile (Wrath/Anger):** It becomes an inner boiling that seeks release.
3.  **Vocal (Clamour/Evil Speaking):** It spills out of the mouth, disrupting the community.
4.  **Calculated (Malice):** It becomes a settled disposition to hurt others.

Paul knows that if you allow bitterness to stay in the heart, it will eventually infect the *community*. You cannot be bitter in a vacuum. It leaks out into "clamour" (loud, public disputes) and "evil speaking" (slander). Therefore, it must be surgically removed for the sake of the body of Christ (the Church in Ephesus).

### 3. The Theological "Why": The Motivation for Forgiveness
The most critical reason bitterness and forgiveness are placed together is found in the conjunction **"even as"** (Greek: *kathōs*) in verse 32.

> *"Forgiving one another, **even as** God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you."*

Paul is arguing that your forgiveness of others is not optional; it is the **evidence** that you have truly understood the Gospel.
*   **Bitterness** says: "What you did matters more than what God did."
*   **Forgiveness** says: "What God did in Christ matters more than what you did."

If God, through Christ, absorbed the ultimate insult and injury without holding it against us, how can we—the forgiven—hold injuries against others? To remain bitter is to deny the logic of your own salvation.

### Summary
Paul places them against each other because:
1.  **Bitterness is a habit of the heart:** It is a willful refusal to release a debt.
2.  **Bitterness is contagious:** It moves from the heart to the tongue, destroying the unity of the church (see Ephesians 4:3).
3.  **Forgiveness is the only cure:** It is the act of applying the Gospel to a specific relationship.

Paul is saying: *You are no longer who you used to be (v. 22–24). Therefore, stop acting like a debtor who holds grudges, and start acting like a debtor who has been released.*
