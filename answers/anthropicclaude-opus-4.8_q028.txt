The sentence is destabilizing precisely because it resists collapsing into any single one of those readings. Let me work through why.

**What it doesn't quite do:**

It doesn't forbid judgment outright. Notice Jesus doesn't say the woman is innocent, nor that the law is wrong. He even tells her afterward to "sin no more"—a judgment. So the pacifist "don't judge anyone ever" reading misses something; the moral category of sin remains intact.

It isn't only exposing hypocrisy, though it does that. If the point were merely "you're all sinners too," it would be a fairly ordinary moralism. Hypocrisy-exposure is the *mechanism*, not the deepest payload.

**What makes it genuinely unstable:**

The sentence relocates the question. The crowd arrives with a question about *her*—what does she deserve? Jesus answers a question they didn't ask: *what qualifies you to be the one who delivers it?* He doesn't dispute the verdict; he disputes the **standing to execute it**.

This is the move that destabilizes. Justice in the abstract may require a stone be thrown. But justice is never abstract—it must pass through an actual hand, and the moment you ask *whose* hand, the clean separation between judge and judged dissolves. There is no qualified executioner available. Each person in the crowd discovers that to throw the stone, they would have to claim a purity they don't possess, in front of witnesses who know they don't possess it.

**The deeper thing:**

It severs the act of condemnation from impersonal justice and forces it back into a relationship between two flawed parties. The stone-thrower cannot stand *outside* the moral situation; raising the stone implicates them in it. Judgment, Jesus suggests, is not a position one occupies but a thing one *does*, with one's own compromised hands.

This is why it's "morally destabilizing" rather than merely morally instructive. It doesn't give you a new rule. It removes the imagined vantage point—the neutral elevated ground—from which we prefer to issue verdicts. After this sentence, you can still judge, but you can no longer pretend you are doing it from nowhere.

**One more layer:** notice the temporal trap in "*first*." It turns a collective action into a sequence requiring an individual to go first—and no one will be the first, because going first means publicly asserting a claim about oneself. The mob's power depended on anonymity and simultaneity. Jesus dissolves the mob back into individuals, each now alone with conscience. The crowd leaves "one by one"—and the text adds, devastatingly, "beginning with the eldest," i.e., those with the most accumulated self-knowledge.

So: it does all four things you named, but the deepest move is the demolition of the spectator's seat. It denies us the comfort of judging as though we were not also on trial.
