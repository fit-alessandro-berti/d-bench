The Genesis passage you've quoted reveals a nuanced depiction of moral agency and accountability. Here, the Lord is not passive but engaging, offering both counsel and potential for reward and punishment. The text suggests that evildoing has a conditional nature associated with choice. Evil is not presented as an impulsive beast, blind drive, or habit, but rather as an act that can be averted through righteous living. The person is not mere vessel to his temptation; instead, he's positioned as a responsible agent capable of deciding in the face of his desire. The commandments of God here—"if thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted; if thou doest not well, sin lieth at your door”—insinuate the person as a moral beacon, capable of guiding his path. 


The responsibility of choice places the person as a moral agent in a position of power. If he can make good choices, he will be accepted by God and be able to rule. However, if his conduct is errant, he will confront his sin, demonstrating that sin and evil choices directly bear consequences. The responsibility implied by this passage, then, is the awareness and control of one's actions, and the understanding that the individual has a direct influence over their spiritual standing and destiny.


It reflects a belief in a divine moral order where one's choices actively determine their standing with God (acceptance or rejection, rule or face temptation' end), rather than sin being an automatic response or consequence of one's nature, or an external force. It is about conscious decision and personal responsibility in the cultivation or dismissal of goodness, which forms a distinct picture of responsibility.
