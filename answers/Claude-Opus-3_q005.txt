This is a profound and challenging passage that gets to the heart of Christian ethics. Loving one's enemies is incredibly difficult for several reasons:

1. It goes against our natural human instincts and emotions. When someone wrongs us, our gut reaction is usually anger, resentment, and a desire for justice or revenge. Choosing to love in the face of hatred and persecution requires overcoming those powerful impulses.

2. It seems illogical and counterproductive from a pragmatic, self-interested perspective. Why show goodwill to those trying to harm you? It can feel like inviting further abuse and failing to protect yourself. 

3. It's extremely costly in terms of emotional energy and personal sacrifice. It's hard enough to consistently show love to those close to us - extending that to enemies who wish us ill is taking on a monumental burden.

4. Our culture and society rarely incentivize or model this kind of radical, self-giving love. Retribution, competition and looking out for your own interests are more common mindsets.

So in light of all that, choosing enemy-love is not mere sentimentality - it requires immense moral strength and commitment, and a willingness to imitate the sacrificial love of God. Jesus points to how God indiscriminately gives sun and rain to all, regardless of their goodness. God's love is universal and unmerited.

Some argue there can be a strategic element to enemy-love, in that unexpected kindness can sometimes soften hearts and turn foes to friends. But I think Jesus' main point is that enemy-love is fundamentally an expression of moral character and spiritual strength - becoming "children of your Father in heaven" by aligning our character with God's perfect love. It's about who we are becoming, not pragmatic outcomes.

Ultimately, loving enemies is central to Christian ethics because it reflects the very heart of God's love shown to us. While we were still sinners, Christ died for us - loving us when we were hostile to God. If we are to become like our Heavenly Father, we must learn to love with that same costly, indiscriminate, transformative love. Not just when it's easy or reciprocated, but even toward those who hate and mistreat us.

This is an incredibly high calling that cuts against the grain of our instincts. But it's also the path to becoming more fully human, more fully alive, and more fully conformed to the likeness of the God who IS love. Enemy-love is the ultimate expression of moral strength empowered by God's own limitless mercy and grace.
