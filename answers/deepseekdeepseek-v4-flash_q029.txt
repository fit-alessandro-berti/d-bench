The moral posture required for correction in Galatians 6:1 is one of **humble, gentle restoration**—marked by meekness and self-awareness. It rejects any sense of superiority, judgment, or harshness, and instead treats the one at fault with the same compassion and patience one would hope to receive in a similar situation.

**Why restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion are kept together:**

1. **Restoration is the goal** – Correction is not about punishment or venting frustration; it is about helping a brother or sister return to spiritual health and community (cf. 2 Cor. 2:6–8). Without this aim, the interaction becomes destructive.

2. **Meekness (or gentleness) is the manner** – “The spirit of meekness” ensures that correction is communicated without pride, harshness, or personal attack. Meekness is controlled strength, guided by love and humility—it prevents the corrector from becoming self-righteous or domineering.

3. **Self-suspicion (considering yourself) is the guard** – The warning “lest thou also be tempted” reminds the corrector of their own moral fragility. No one is immune to sin; recognizing this breeds compassion, deters hypocrisy, and keeps the corrector from a feeling of invulnerability that could lead to their own fall.

These three elements are inseparable because correction without meekness becomes condemnation, and correction without self-suspicion becomes arrogant. Meekness without restoration is mere politeness; restoration without meekness is coercion. Keeping them together ensures that the act of correction reflects the gospel’s pattern of grace, truth, and humility.
