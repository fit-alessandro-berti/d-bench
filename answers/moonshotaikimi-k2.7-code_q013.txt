Amos’s language is deliberately shocking because the worship he sees is not merely **lacking** something; it has become **the opposite** of what true worship should be. The prophet does not say, “Add more justice to your festivals”; he says, “I hate, I despise your feast days… take away from me the noise of thy songs.” The issue is not balance but **integrity**.

## 1. Ritual has become a substitute for righteousness

In the covenant, Israel’s sacrifices, festivals, and music were meant to express gratitude, repentance, and loyalty to Yahweh. But in Amos’s day, the same people who filled the sanctuaries with solemn assemblies were:

- crushing the poor,
- perverting justice in the courts,
- taking bribes,
- trampling on the needy (see Amos 2:6–7; 5:11–12; 8:4–6).

Their worship had become a **religious veneer** over systemic cruelty. God does not ask them to worship *less*—he asks them to stop using worship as a way to feel pious while living unjustly.

## 2. The worship is “fraud” because it misrepresents God

The moral and spiritual fraud Amos exposes is **formalism without covenant faithfulness**. The people are performing the correct rituals on the correct days, but their lives deny the very God they claim to honor. True knowledge of God in the prophets is not measured by liturgical precision but by **justice and steadfast love** (Hos. 6:6; Mic. 6:6–8).

So the fraud is:

- **Hypocrisy**: hands lifted in praise, hands heavy with oppression.
- **Bribery**: treating God as someone who can be appeased by offerings while they continue their sins.
- **Idolatry of ritual**: making the forms of religion an end in themselves, as if God needed their fat beasts and music.

## 3. “I will not smell… I will not accept”

In the sacrificial system, the “soothing aroma” was a sign of God’s acceptance and pleasure (Lev. 1:9, 13). Amos turns that language on its head: Yahweh will not even *smell*—let alone accept—their offerings. The rituals have become **odious to him**, not because ritual is bad, but because these rituals are the smoke of an unrepentant, unjust people.

## 4. Justice is not an extra; it is the stream that gives worship life

“Let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream” does not merely add a social-justice requirement to worship. It declares that **justice is the natural overflow of covenant relationship with God**. Without it, worship is a dry channel—noisy, elaborate, and empty.

## Summary

The passage rejects worship so sharply because **the worship itself has become a lie**: it claims devotion to Yahweh while the worshipers live as though he does not see or care about justice. The fraud is not just imbalance; it is **religious hypocrisy**—using the forms of faith to conceal, and even sanction, a life that contradicts God’s character and covenant.
