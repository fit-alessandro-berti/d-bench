The trust in Proverbs 3:5–6 is **personal, total, and submissive**, not irrational or anti-intellectual.

### 1. What kind of trust is this?

From the Hebrew:

- **“Trust” (batach)** – to put your weight on, to rely on for security. It’s *relational dependence*, not just agreement with propositions.
- **“With all your heart” (lev)** – the “heart” in Hebrew is not just emotions; it includes mind, will, and desires. So this is:
  - Intellectual: trusting God’s wisdom.
  - Volitional: submitting your choices.
  - Affective: resting your fears, hopes, and anxieties on him.

So the verse is calling for **whole-life reliance** on God as the wise, good, and sovereign Lord.

### 2. What does “lean not on your own understanding” mean?

It does *not* mean:

- “Don’t think.”
- “Ignore evidence, logic, or learning.”
- “Treat your mind as an enemy.”

We know that because Proverbs as a whole:

- Commands: “Get wisdom, get understanding” (Prov 4:5,7).
- Praises knowledge, careful observation, and prudent planning.

So “your own understanding” here is not *reason as such* but **reason treated as self-sufficient and final**—understanding that:

- Claims independence from God (“I know enough without him”).
- Refuses correction from God’s Word or providence.
- Makes personal insight the ultimate standard of truth or goodness.

The picture is architectural: don’t *lean* (put your weight) on your own insight as your *ultimate* support.

### 3. Humble self-distrust vs. anti-intellectualism

**Humble self-distrust** means:

- Recognizing your mind is:
  - Limited (you can’t see all factors).
  - Fallen (subject to bias, pride, self-justification).
- Therefore:
  - You **submit your reasoning** to God’s revealed character and will.
  - You hold your own judgments as revisable in light of Scripture, wise counsel, and God’s providence.
  - You keep asking, “Am I possibly rationalizing what I want?” and invite God to correct you.

This produces:

- Thoughtful study, *then* prayerful surrender.
- Planning, *then* willingness to have plans redirected (Prov 16:9).
- Use of intellect as a **servant of trust**, not a replacement for it.

**Mere anti-intellectualism**, by contrast:

- Distrusts thinking itself, not just *proud* thinking.
- Treats ignorance as a virtue.
- Can refuse hard questions out of fear, not faith.
- Ignores the many biblical calls to wisdom, instruction, and understanding.

That is not what Proverbs advocates. In fact, it’s another form of “leaning on your own understanding,” because it quietly trusts *feeling*, *impulse*, or *group pressure* instead of God’s truth.

### 4. “In all your ways acknowledge him”

“In all your ways acknowledge (yada‘) him” literally means **“know him” in all your ways**:

- Bring God consciously into every area—moral choices, plans, relationships, work, suffering.
- Ask: “What has God said about this? What fits his character? Where might I be resisting him?”

That is the positive counterpart to “lean not on your own understanding”: your mind is active, but its **reference point** is God.

---

So the trust called for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is:

- **Intellectually engaged** (it seeks wisdom and understanding),
- **Morally and relationally submissive** (it yields to God when his wisdom confronts ours),
- **Suspicious not of reason itself, but of *autonomous* reason**—reason that wants to be its own god.
