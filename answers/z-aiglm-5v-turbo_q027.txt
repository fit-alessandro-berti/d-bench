Based on the text of Luke 18:9–14, the condemnation of the Pharisee is not a single isolated flaw but a compound spiritual failure. To answer your question directly: **Pride, comparison, contempt, and the use of virtue as self-display are all present, but the root cause is "Trusting in himself."**

Here is an analysis of why the Pharisee is condemned and why the tax collector’s posture is considered spiritually authentic.

### Part 1: What Exactly Condemns the Pharisee?

While you listed several options, the text weaves them together into a specific psychological and spiritual profile:

#### 1. The Root: Self-Trust (vs. God-Trust)
Verse 9 explicitly states the target of the parable: those who **“trusted in themselves that they were righteous.”**
This is the fundamental error. In biblical theology, the problem is not that the Pharisee *had* virtues (he did fast, and he did tithe); the problem is that he viewed those virtues as the **basis of his relationship with God**. He approached God as a creditor owed a debt rather than a sinner in need of mercy.

#### 2. The Mechanism: Comparison
The Pharisee’s prayer is built entirely on a sliding scale. He does not stand before God’s absolute holiness; he stands next to “other men.”
> *"I thank thee, that I am not as other men..."*

When you measure yourself by other people (especially the worst examples like extortioners or the socially despised tax collector), you will always look good. By using **comparison**, he creates a fictional standard of righteousness that he can easily meet.

#### 3. The Attitude: Contempt
Verse 9 notes that this group **“despised others.”** The Pharisee’s righteousness requires someone to look down upon. His "justification" is zero-sum; for him to be "up," the other man must be "down." This reveals that his religion lacks love; it is purely competitive.

#### 4. The Method: Virtue as Self-Display (Praying "With Himself")
Perhaps the most damning phrase in the passage is that the Pharisee **“prayed thus *with himself*.”**
This implies that his prayer was not actually directed toward God but was an act of self-affirmation. He recites his moral resume (fasting, tithing) not to commune with God, but to congratulate himself. His virtues were not used to serve God or neighbor; they were used as props to display his own superiority.

**Is it "False Doctrine"?**
Technically, no. The Pharisee’s facts were correct: extortioners *are* sinners, and tithing *is* a requirement of the Law. His error is not factual (doctrine) but relational (disposition). He had the right rules but the wrong heart. He treated the Law as a tool for self-exaltation rather than a revelation of God’s character.

---

### Part 2: Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture "Spiritually Truer"?

In literature and Scripture, posture often reflects inner reality. The tax collector’s physical stance is treated as spiritually superior because it aligns with **objective reality**.

#### 1. Alignment with Reality (Truth)
*   **The Pharisee lived in delusion:** He believed he was spiritually sufficient.
*   **The Tax Collector lived in truth:** He acknowledged that before a Holy God, he had no standing.
By standing **"afar off,"** the tax collector acknowledges the separation caused by sin. By refusing to lift his **"eyes unto heaven,"** he acknowledges he is unworthy to look upon the Divine face. His body is telling the truth about the human condition.

#### 2. Repentance over Merit
The tax collector’s action of **"smiting upon his breast"** was a cultural sign of extreme grief, anger, or regret. He essentially beats himself up, taking responsibility for his sin rather than blaming his environment or upbringing.
His prayer consists of seven words (in Greek): **"God be merciful to me a sinner."**
*   He offers no bargaining chips.
*   He lists no good deeds to offset the bad.
*   He asks only for **propitiation** (the Greek word for "be merciful" relates to the mercy seat/sacrifice). He throws himself entirely on the grace of God.

#### 3. The Result: Justification
Verse 14 concludes: **"this man went down to his house justified [declared righteous] rather than the other."**

The tax collector’s posture is "truer" because **justification flows from humility, not merit.** You cannot be "filled" if you are already "full." The tax collector was empty, aware of his emptiness, and therefore able to receive the grace of God. The Pharisee was full of himself, leaving no room for God to enter.

### Summary
The Pharisee is condemned because **he confuses morality with spirituality**, using his obedience as a weapon against others and a shield from having to rely on God.
The Tax Collector is justified because **his posture reflects the truth**: that we enter the Kingdom not by reporting our achievements, but by confessing our need.
