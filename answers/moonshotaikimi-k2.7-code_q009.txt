Romans 12:20–21 defines victory over evil not as **winning a fight** but as **refusing to let evil reproduce itself in you**. Paul says: “Be not overcome of evil, but overcome evil with good.” The word for “overcome” is the same Greek verb used elsewhere for conquering (*nikaō*). So the passage is saying:

- Evil “wins” when it makes you retaliate in kind, hardens your heart, or drags you into the same hatred.
- You “win” when you respond to evil with active, costly goodness—especially toward those who have harmed you.

### The “coals of fire”

The phrase “heap coals of fire on his head” comes from Proverbs 25:21–22. It is not a command to take revenge. In fact, Paul has just said, “Vengeance is mine; I will repay, saith the Lord” (Rom 12:19). The image most likely means one of two things:

1. **Shame leading to repentance.** In the ancient world, carrying burning coals on one’s head was sometimes a sign of remorse. By treating an enemy with kindness, you may awaken his conscience and shame him into turning from his hostility.
2. **God’s judgment.** If the enemy remains unrepentant, his own evil stands exposed before God’s justice, while you have done good.

Either way, the point is the same: **you are not the one returning harm**. You do good; God handles the outcome.

### Is it weak?

No. Feeding and giving drink to an enemy is not passivity. It demands self-control, forgiveness, and trust in God’s justice rather than your own. It takes far more strength to absorb evil and respond with mercy than to strike back.

### Is it manipulative?

It can be twisted that way. If someone helps an enemy *only* to make him feel ashamed or to look superior, the kindness is not real love. But Paul insists earlier in the chapter that love must be “without dissimulation” (Rom 12:9)—sincere. The goal is the enemy’s good and the breaking of the cycle of evil, not a secret payback.

### Is it genuinely strong?

Yes. It is a strong, Christ-like kind of victory:

- It refuses to let the enemy’s evil define your character.
- It imitates God, who “loved us while we were yet sinners” (Rom 5:8).
- It can transform the enemy, or at least expose evil by contrast.
- It overcomes the power of evil not by matching it, but by replacing it with good.

**In short:** Victory over evil is not destroying your enemy; it is refusing to become like him and instead overcoming evil by doing good to him.
