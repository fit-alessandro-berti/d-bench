Romans 12:20–21 defines victory over evil not as passive submission, but as a deliberate, active conquest in which the cycle of retaliation is broken through genuine good. The command is neither weak nor manipulative; it is a profoundly strong moral stance that requires more power than revenge ever could.

### 1. What does victory over evil mean here?
Paul anchors his command in the language of warfare: “**Be not overcome** of evil, but **overcome** evil with good.” The imagery is of a battle in which evil is a conquering force. If you respond to evil with more evil—hatred for hatred, vengeance for vengeance—evil has *overcome* you. You have been shaped into its image. True victory is to absorb the blow and return something redemptive, thereby disarming the evil and preserving your own character.

Victory is therefore defined as:
- **Breaking the cycle of evil:** By feeding the hungry enemy and giving drink to the thirsty, you refuse to let the enemy’s actions dictate your own.
- **Moral and spiritual triumph:** You maintain integrity, love, and trust in God’s justice while actively doing good.
- **Potential transformation of the enemy:** The enemy may be moved to shame, repentance, or reconciliation.

### 2. What about “heaping coals of fire on his head”?
This phrase (quoted from Proverbs 25:21–22) can sound like a hidden form of revenge—kindness that secretly burns. But historical and literary context gives a different picture:
- In an ancient Egyptian penitential rite, a person carrying a pan of burning coals on their head symbolized repentance. In this reading, the acts of kindness bring the enemy to a burning sense of shame leading to a change of heart.
- Others see it as divine judgment: by acting in love, you leave room for God to deal with the enemy justly (v. 19). The “coals” are then God’s purifying or punitive fire, not yours.
- Crucially, Paul’s preceding instruction is: “Never avenge yourselves, but leave it to the wrath of God.” The kindness is not a disguised weapon; it’s an expression of trust that God will set things right. Your job is to love; God’s job is justice.

Whatever the precise imagery, the *motive* Paul demands is unfeigned love (v. 9), not a clever tactic to harm the other while appearing righteous.

### 3. Is the command weak, manipulative, or genuinely strong?
**Not weak.** It takes far more strength to bless a person while they are mistreating you than to strike back. Retaliation is instinctive; proactive kindness toward an enemy requires immense self-control, courage, and emotional fortitude. It’s not cowardice—it’s the deliberate refusal to be conquered by animosity.

**Not manipulative.** Manipulation would mean using “good” as a covert instrument of harm, trying to control or destroy the enemy under a cloak of virtue. Paul forbids that: “Let love be genuine” (v. 9). The goal is to overcome the *evil*, not the *person*, and to do so through a sincere good that mirrors God’s mercy. The “coals” are a potential outcome (the enemy’s conscience awakening), not a secret vindictive purpose.

**Genuinely strong.** This is a radical, counterintuitive power—the power of the cross. Jesus conquered evil not by annihilating his enemies but by dying for them and returning forgiveness (Luke 23:34). Paul’s ethic flows from that same gospel logic: the strongest force in the universe is self-giving love that absorbs hatred and transforms it. Such love requires a security that is not threatened by injustice, because one trusts God as the ultimate judge and restorer.

### In summary
Paul defines victory over evil as an active, unshakeable goodness that refuses to mirror the enemy’s malice. The command is the opposite of weak; it demands the highest moral strength. It is not manipulative; it flows from genuine love and trusts God for justice. It is, in the fullest sense, a strength that breaks the back of evil by starving it of the fuel it needs to perpetuate itself.
