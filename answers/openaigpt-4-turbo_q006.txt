In Mark 8:34–35, the concept of "denying oneself" involves a profound sense of self-sacrifice and reprioritization of one's life and desires in favor of following Jesus. The "self" here refers to the ego or selfish desires, the natural human inclination to pursue one's own interests, comfort, and securities. This includes the tendency to center decision-making on personal gain or pleasure rather than on the teachings and example of Jesus.

For a Christian, denying oneself implies a voluntary renunciation of certain aspects of one’s life that are not in harmony with God’s will, or it could mean willingly enduring hardships and struggles as part of their spiritual journey and commitment to following Christ. This could include giving up personal dreams, material possessions, certain relationships, or even life paths that lead away from a Christ-centered life.

This teaching can be seen as liberating to some because it offers a path to transcend the usual attachments and desires that can govern life. It suggests a way to find deeper purpose and peace through commitment to something greater than oneself—aligning with divine will and contributing to a larger mission (the gospel). The liberation comes from escaping the often endless cycle of seeking personal satisfaction, which can be fleeting, and instead finding purpose and happiness in devotion to faith and service.

Conversely, this message might be offensive or at least challenging to others for several reasons. Firstly, it contradicts many modern cultural narratives that emphasize individualism, self-fulfillment, and personal freedom as the highest goods. The idea of denying one's self can thus be viewed as an imposition on personal liberty and autonomy. Moreover, it imposes a sense of duty and sacrifice that might appear unattractive or daunting to those who place a high value on personal success and material comfort as defined by societal standards.

Additionally, in a more psychological perspective, surrendering one's ego and self-driven desires can be a deeply unsettling process, as it challenges fundamental assumptions about identity and worth that are often shaped by personal achievements and societal accolades.

Therefore, the reaction to Jesus' call to deny oneself can be markedly different depending on individual beliefs, values, circumstances, and interpretations of what true fulfillment and success look like. For those seeking a spiritual rebirth or transformation, this is a liberating invitation to a new kind of life; for others, it could represent an unwelcome demand for self-renunciation that conflicts sharply with personal or cultural values.
