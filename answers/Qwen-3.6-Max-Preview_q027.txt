Based on the text of Luke 18:9–14 and its theological context, here is a detailed analysis of what condemns the Pharisee and why the tax collector’s posture is deemed spiritually truer.

### What Exactly Condemns the Pharisee?

The condemnation of the Pharisee is not due to a single isolated fault but a **compound spiritual failure** rooted in **pride**, which manifests as **contempt** and **self-justification**. While all the elements you listed are present, they function hierarchically:

1. **Pride (The Root Cause):**  
   The introductory verse (v. 9) explicitly states Jesus’ target audience: *“those who trusted in themselves that they were righteous.”* The Pharisee’s prayer begins with “God, I thank thee…” but quickly shifts focus from God’s grace to his own moral superiority. His pride lies in believing his righteousness is self-generated through works (fasting, tithing), rather than received by grace.

2. **Contempt (The Active Sin):**  
   The Pharisee does not merely boast; he actively despises others: *“I am not as other men are… or even as this publican.”* This violates the core ethical command to love one’s neighbor. His spirituality is defined by exclusion and judgment, not compassion. Contempt reveals a heart alienated from God’s mercy, because one cannot receive mercy while denying it to others.

3. **Comparison (The Mechanism of Self-Justification):**  
   He measures himself against others (“extortioners, unjust, adulterers”) rather than against God’s holiness. This horizontal comparison allows him to feel righteous without confronting his own sin. It is a form of self-deception that masks spiritual poverty.

4. **Use of Virtue as Self-Display:**  
   His fasting and tithing are real acts, but they are offered as credentials before God, not as expressions of gratitude or devotion. He uses religious observance to build a case for his own merit, turning virtue into a tool for self-exaltation rather than worship.

5. **False Doctrine (Implicit):**  
   While not explicitly stated as doctrinal error, his attitude reflects a false understanding of salvation: that righteousness can be earned by law-keeping. This contradicts the gospel truth that “all have sinned” (Romans 3:23) and justification comes through faith and mercy, not works.

> **In summary:** The Pharisee is condemned primarily for **prideful self-justification**, which expresses itself through **contempt for others** and the **misuse of religious practices** as tools for self-glorification rather than humble dependence on God.

---

### Why Is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?

The tax collector’s posture—both physical and verbal—is treated as spiritually authentic because it aligns with the fundamental biblical principle: **“God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble”** (James 4:6; cf. Proverbs 3:34).

1. **Honest Self-Assessment (Truthfulness):**  
   The tax collector acknowledges reality: he *is* a sinner. Unlike the Pharisee, who compares himself favorably to others, the tax collector stands alone before God, aware of his unworthiness. This honesty is the first step toward genuine repentance.

2. **Dependence on Mercy, Not Merit:**  
   His prayer is short and direct: *“God, be merciful to me, a sinner.”* The Greek word translated “be merciful” (*hilaskomai*) carries connotations of propitiation or atonement—he is asking God to cover his sin, recognizing he has no claim on God based on his own deeds. This reflects true faith: trusting in God’s character (mercy) rather than one’s own performance.

3. **Humility Expressed Physically:**  
   - *“Standing afar off”*: He recognizes the holiness of God and his own uncleanliness, maintaining a respectful distance.
   - *“Would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven”*: A sign of shame and reverence, acknowledging he has no right to demand an audience with God.
   - *“Smote upon his breast”*: A gesture of deep grief and contrition over his sin.
   
   These actions are not performative (as the Pharisee’s might be interpreted); they are involuntary expressions of a broken spirit. Psalm 51:17 affirms: *“The sacrifices of God are a broken spirit; a broken and contrite heart, O God, you will not despise.”*

4. **Receptivity to Grace:**  
   Because the tax collector empties himself of pretense and self-righteousness, he creates space for God’s grace. Justification (“went down to his house justified”) is granted not because he was morally better, but because he was spiritually honest and dependent on God’s mercy.

---

### Conclusion

The parable contrasts two approaches to God:
- The **Pharisee** approaches God with a resume of achievements, seeking validation. His pride blinds him to his need for mercy and leads him to condemn others.
- The **Tax Collector** approaches God with empty hands, seeking mercy. His humility opens him to receive justification.

Jesus concludes: *“For everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, but he who humbles himself will be exalted”* (Luke 18:14). The tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer because it reflects the only stance compatible with divine grace: **humble dependence on God’s mercy, coupled with honest acknowledgment of one’s sin.**
