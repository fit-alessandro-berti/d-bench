
This passage from Matthew 12:34–35 presents speech as deeply intertwined with the heart’s inner moral reserves, serving **all three roles**—symptom, choice, and revelation. Here’s how the text weaves these ideas together:

---

### 1. **Speech as a Symptom of the Heart’s Condition**  
Jesus frames words not as neutral acts but as **inevitable overflow** from the heart’s pre-existing "abundance" or "treasure." The metaphor of treasure emphasizes stored resources cultivated over time. Here, speech is a symptom in two ways:  
- **Inevitability:** "Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh" (v. 34). This suggests speech is an involuntary result, like water gushing from an overfilled spring. The heart’s state *determines* the words’ nature.  
- **Natural reflection:** An "evil" heart cannot genuinely produce good speech because its source (its moral reserves) is corrupt. Conversely, a "good" heart inevitably offers wholesome words. Speech thus functions as a symptom of the heart’s intrinsic health or decay.  

---

### 2. **Speech as a Choice (Past and Ongoing)**  
While speech itself may feel involuntary (as in the overflow metaphor), the heart’s "treasure" is not accidental. Jesus implies that:  
- **Prior choices** have stockpiled the heart’s moral resources: The "evil treasure" of the wicked and "good treasure" of the righteous (v. 35) are the result of habitual decisions—sinful thoughts or godly practices, respectively.  
- **Ongoing choices** still matter: A person could theoretically shift their treasure (and thereby their speech) through future acts of submission to God (e.g., repentance, prayer). Thus, while speech is an outcome of past choices, it also represents an ongoing *consequence* of ongoing decisions.  

---

### 3. **Speech as a Revelation of the Heart**  
The passage makes speech a mirror that **unmasks the heart**:  
- **Self-revelation:** Speech betrays one’s inner state. The "generation of vipers" would have tried to mask their hypocrisy with outward religiosity, but their words expose their true nature.  
- **Divine judgment:** In Jewish thought, speech was a key medium of judgment (e.g., "By your words you will be justified, and by your words you will be condemned," Matthew 12:37). Words, therefore, reveal God’s assessment of the heart.  
- **Public witness:** Speech also reveals to others the true source of one’s character. A person’s words illuminate whether their "treasure" is of this world (selfishness, greed) or of heaven (compassion, truth).  

---

### Interplay: A Threefold Dynamic  
The passage does not isolate these roles. Instead, it shows speech as a **threefold reflection** of the heart:  
- **Symptom:** The heart’s overflow (not purely voluntary).  
- **Choice:** Speech is cultivated through prior choices and shapes future outcomes.  
- **Revelation:** Words expose hidden truths, both to oneself and to others.  

In Jewish tradition, the heart (*leb*) was the seat of intention, will, and moral life. Jesus here aligns with that view, framing speech as inseparable from the heart’s moral economy. Speech cannot ultimately be divorced from who we are—or yearn to become.  

**Final Note:** The passage assumes no neutral ground: speech is either *from* goodness or evil, *by* goodness or evil. It challenges listeners to examine both their words *and* the treasures they’ve stored away.
