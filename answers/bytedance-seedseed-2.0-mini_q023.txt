### Breaking Down Isaiah 55:6–7 and the Two Dimensions of Repentance
This verse uses poetic parallelism to describe a single, holistic repentance required of the "wicked" and "unrighteous": both terms refer to the same person, who has allowed corruption to take root in both their outward lives and inner hearts. To answer the question, we first clarify the two focal areas, then unpack why both are necessary, and finally explore their implications for conduct, thought, and divine mercy.

---

#### 1. Distinguishing "Way" and "Thoughts"
- **"Way" (דֶּרֶךְ, derech):** This Hebrew term refers to external conduct, lifestyle, and observable actions—the visible path a person walks in the world. It includes overt sins, broken moral habits, and choices that harm self or others.
- **"Thoughts" (מַחְשָׁבוֹת, machshavot):** This describes inward, unobservable realities: corrupt intentions, secret motivations, twisted imaginations, and attitudes of hatred, greed, or rebellion that may not always lead to overt acts but are still alien to God’s nature.

---

#### 2. Why Both Are Required for True Repentance
True repentance (teshuvah in Hebrew, meaning "return to God") is not a surface-level moral tweak—it is a whole-person turning away from sin and toward God. Neither dimension of sin can be ignored without undermining genuine transformation:
- **Interconnection of Inner and Outer:** As Proverbs 4:23 notes, "from the heart flow the springs of life." Inward corrupt thoughts drive outward sinful actions. Renouncing only external behavior while leaving unrighteous motivations intact risks falling back into sin (e.g., quitting theft but still nurturing a greedy spirit). Conversely, inward conviction without outward change is not true repentance (James 2:17, which teaches that faith without works is dead).
- **God’s Holism:** The Bible consistently teaches that God judges both outward deeds and inward hearts (1 Samuel 16:7: "The Lord looks not on the outward appearance, but on the heart"). For example, Jesus expanded the definition of adultery to include lustful thoughts in the heart (Matthew 5:28), framing unobservable sin as equally worthy of repentance.
- **Full Commitment to God:** The call to "return unto the Lord" demands surrender of every part of the self. A person cannot claim to follow God while clinging to both selfish actions and the corrupt attitudes that justify them.

---

#### 3. Implications for Outward Conduct, Inward Imagination, and Mercy
##### For Outward Conduct and Inward Imagination
- **Neither Is Sufficient on Its Own:** Outward moral compliance without inner transformation is empty ritual (Isaiah 29:13 criticizes "honoring me with your lips while your hearts are far from me"). Similarly, private guilt over wrong thoughts without changing behavior does not repair the harm of sin or align one with God’s will.
- **Both Require Transformation:** Genuine repentance demands that outward conduct align with God’s commands *and* that inward imagination and motives be renewed to reflect God’s righteousness (Romans 12:2, which calls believers to be transformed by the renewing of their minds). The wicked cannot merely "clean up their acts"—they must also renounce the selfish, broken thinking that defines their relationship to God and others.

##### For the Possibility of Mercy
This passage’s promise of mercy has three critical implications:
1. **Mercy Is for the Fully Unrighteous:** The call addresses "the wicked" and "unrighteous" without qualification: no one is too far gone to receive God’s pardon, so long as they turn from both their ways and thoughts.
2. **Mercy Is Comprehensive:** God does not merely overlook outward sins or internal corruptions—he "abundantly pardons" both. Forgiveness covers not just the harm done by overt actions, but the secret, unspoken rebellion of the heart.
3. **Mercy Empowers Transformation:** The promise of mercy is not a license to continue sinning, but an incentive to turn fully to God. When one returns to him, God’s grace transforms both inward and outward reality, restoring fellowship with him.

---

In short, Isaiah 55:6–7 frames repentance as a holistic shift that touches both how we live and how we think, and it affirms that God’s mercy is great enough to cover every dimension of brokenness for anyone who seeks him.
