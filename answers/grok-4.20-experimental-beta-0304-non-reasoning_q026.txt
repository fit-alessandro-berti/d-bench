**The passage directly equates speech with the "treasure" (θησαυρός) stored in the heart.**

### 1. The Core Metaphor: Hidden Moral Stockpiles
- The heart is portrayed as a **storehouse or treasury** that accumulates moral/spiritual inventory over time.
- "Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks" (ἐκ τοῦ περισσεύματος τῆς καρδίας τὸ στόμα λαλεῖ) means that speech is not primarily a surface-level decision made in the moment; it is an **overflow**. What the heart has been hoarding in abundance eventually spills out through the lips.
- Jesus uses the same word "treasure" (θησαυρός) that appears in Matthew 6:19–21 ("Do not lay up for yourselves treasures on earth... but lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven... for where your treasure is, there your heart will be also"). The heart is not neutral; it is a moral warehouse that stockpiles either good or evil.

This is why Jesus can diagnose the Pharisees' hearts from their words alone: their venomous speech ("generation of vipers") reveals the nature of what they have stored up.

### 2. Speech as Symptom, Choice, and Revelation — All Three

The passage presents speech in all three dimensions simultaneously:

**A. Symptom**  
Words are the **outward evidence** of an inward condition. Just as a tree is known by its fruit (the very next paragraph, vv. 33), speech is the fruit that reveals whether the hidden root system (the heart) is healthy or diseased. You cannot consistently speak good things if evil is what fills the storehouse. The mouth is like a gauge or a pressure valve; it shows what is under pressure inside.

**B. Choice**  
While the content of the heart shapes the speech, the passage still treats people as morally responsible agents. Jesus rebukes them: "How can ye, being evil, speak good things?" The rhetorical question implies they *could* choose to change what they store up. Elsewhere in the same chapter (v. 37), Jesus says people will be "justified" or "condemned" by their words, which only makes sense if speech is a volitional act for which we are accountable. We choose what to "treasure" through repeated thoughts, desires, meditations, and habits. The stockpiling itself is the result of many prior choices.

**C. Revelation**  
Speech is the moment when the hidden becomes public. The "abundance of the heart" is invisible until it is verbalized. Words therefore function as **self-disclosure**. They reveal our true moral and spiritual condition to others and, ultimately, to God ("every idle word that men shall speak, they shall give account thereof," v. 36). In this sense, speech is not just diagnostic for others; it is self-diagnostic. What we find ourselves saying when unguarded tells us what we have actually been treasuring.

### 3. Practical and Theological Implications
- **Diagnosis**: Listen to your words (especially under pressure, in anger, in humor, in private) and you will discover what your heart has been storing.
- **Transformation**: If evil things keep coming out, the solution is not merely "try harder to speak nicely." The storehouse itself must be cleansed and refilled with good treasure (cf. the strong man parable that follows in vv. 43–45). This is why the gospel centers on the heart, not merely behavior modification.
- **Consistency**: A genuinely good man *will* bring forth good things because the treasury is good. Hypocrisy (speaking good while treasuring evil) is spiritually unstable and will eventually collapse.

In short, Matthew 12:34–35 teaches that **speech is the overflow of the heart’s moral inventory**. It is simultaneously:
- a **symptom** of what has been stored,
- a **choice** for which we are responsible, and
- a **revelation** of our true inner condition.

The three aspects are inseparable. Jesus is not giving mere communication advice; He is giving a diagnosis of the human condition and pointing to the necessity of heart-level redemption.
