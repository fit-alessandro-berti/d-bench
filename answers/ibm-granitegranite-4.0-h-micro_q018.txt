The passage from Ephesians 4:31-32 is a clear and direct call to believers to rid themselves of certain negative emotions and behaviors, specifically bitterness, wrath, anger, clamor, evil speaking, and malice. The text then contrasts these with virtues such as kindness, tenderness, and forgiveness, urging believers to emulate the forgiveness they have received from God through Christ.

The juxtaposition of bitterness with forgiveness is significant for several reasons:

1. **Contrast of Emotions and Behaviors**: Bitterness is presented as an emotion or state of mind that is contrary to the virtue of forgiveness. The text does not suggest that bitterness is a habit or a chosen posture, but rather an emotion that can be actively resisted and replaced with a positive virtue. Forgiveness, on the other hand, is not just a feeling but an action that involves letting go of resentment and choosing to treat others as Christ has treated us.

2. **Spiritual Discipline**: Bitterness is often seen as a spiritual discipline that can hinder spiritual growth. It can lead to a hardened heart and a refusal to see others as God sees them. By urging believers to put away bitterness, the text is encouraging a spiritual discipline that promotes emotional and spiritual health. Forgiveness, in contrast, is a virtue that fosters spiritual growth and maturity.

3. **Contagious Nature of Bitterness**: The text does not explicitly state that bitterness is contagious, but it does imply that negative emotions and behaviors can spread from one person to another. The call to "let all bitterness, and wrath, and anger, and clamour, and evil speaking, be put away" suggests that these negative emotions can have a contagious effect on the community of believers. By contrast, kindness, tenderness, and forgiveness are virtues that can spread positivity and healing within the community.

4. **Christ's Forgiveness**: The text emphasizes that forgiveness is modeled after God's forgiveness of believers through Christ. This suggests that bitterness is not just a personal emotion but also a reflection of a lack of understanding and appreciation for the forgiveness that believers have received. By urging believers to forgive as they have been forgiven, the text is reinforcing the idea that forgiveness is a central aspect of Christian faith and practice.

5. **Practical Application**: The call to put away bitterness and to be kind, tenderhearted, and forgiving is a practical application of the Christian life. It is not merely a theoretical ideal but a command that has real implications for how believers interact with one another. Bitterness can lead to conflict and division, while forgiveness promotes reconciliation and unity.

In summary, the text treats bitterness as an emotion or state of mind that is directly opposed to the virtue of forgiveness. It is not just a feeling but a choice that can be actively resisted and replaced with a positive virtue. The juxtaposition of these two concepts underscores the importance of emotional and spiritual discipline in the life of a believer, and it emphasizes the transformative power of forgiveness in the Christian community.
