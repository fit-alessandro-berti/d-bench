
Let’s break down the question and the passage:

**Question Restated:**  
What picture of moral agency does Genesis 4:6–7 paint? Is evil portrayed as impulse, a beast, a habit, a temptation, or something else? And what responsibility does the text assign to the person involved (Cain)?

Let’s analyze the passage and answer both parts.

---

## 1. **What picture of moral agency is being drawn?**

**Text (Genesis 4:6–7, NIV):**

> “And the Lord said unto Cain,  
> ‘Why art thou wroth? and why is thy countenance fallen?’  
> ‘If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?  
> And if thou doest not well, sin lieth at the door.  
> And unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him.’”

**Key elements in the passage:**

- **Cain is addressed directly by God.** God is not blaming external circumstances or fate; he’s engaging with Cain’s *actions* and *state of mind*.
- Cain is “wroth” and “his countenance is fallen,” indicating an internal emotional state, but the cause is not just emotion—it’s his response to something (likely the murder of Abel).
- The Lord asks, “If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?” This frames moral agency in terms of *choice*: what Cain does (does well or not) determines his outcome.
- “Sin lieth at the door.” This suggests that Cain’s wrongdoing (the murder) is present in his heart or actions, but it is not outside of his control as an external force.
- “To thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him”—here, the “him” is likely the land (as in later tradition) or the result of his actions. It speaks of consequence and authority stemming from his choices.

**Interpretation:**
The text presents **moral agency as a matter of free choice and responsibility**. Cain is held accountable for his actions. His anger and fall are the result of his own decisions (“if thou doest not well…”). There is no external compulsion (like a beast or a curse), nor is evil portrayed as an uncontrollable impulse or mere temptation. Instead, the responsibility rests with Cain as the agent who chooses to act against God’s will.

**Temptation?**
While “temptation” is a common motif in the broader biblical narrative (e.g., Adam and Eve), this passage does not frame the sin as caused by a specific external temptation. Instead, it’s the result of Cain’s own actions and moral failure.

**Summary:**  
Genesis 4:6–7 presents **moral agency as a free, responsible choice**. Evil (in this case, murder) is the result of Cain’s deliberate action, not an impulse, beastly instinct, or unavoidable temptation. The responsibility for the act is placed squarely on Cain; he is the agent and the one to be judged and corrected.

---

## 2. **What responsibility does the text place on the person (Cain)?**

The text places **full responsibility for the sin on Cain himself**. God’s words are not offering excuses or blaming external forces. Instead, the Lord’s message is an invitation to repentance and responsibility:  
- “If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?” (implying he can still be forgiven if he turns from his wrong)
- But if he does not do well, the consequences (“sin lieth at the door”) rest with him.
- The phrase “thou shalt rule over him” (the land or the outcome) implies that his choices will determine his destiny.

Cain is not depicted as a puppet of his environment, a victim of a beast, or someone simply overcome by a force. Instead, he is an agent whose moral decisions determine his fate.

---

### **In summary:**

- **Evil is presented as the result of free, moral choice—not as impulse, beastly instinct, or irresistible temptation.**
- **Responsibility is placed entirely on Cain.** The text holds him accountable for his actions and invites him to repent and choose differently.

This reflects an ancient Near Eastern worldview where individual responsibility before God is a central theme, but it also aligns with the biblical emphasis on personal accountability for one’s sin.
