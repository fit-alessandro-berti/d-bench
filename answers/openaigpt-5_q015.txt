What makes the image so sharp
- Vivid, comic hyperbole: a “speck” (tiny splinter) versus a “beam” (a load‑bearing log) creates an absurd, memorable picture.
- Eye imagery: the eye is delicate; “speck removal” evokes careful surgery. A person with a beam is obviously unfit to operate—exposing the absurdity of moral meddling while blind.
- Reversal and irony: obsessing over a minor fault in another while ignoring a major one in oneself.
- Verbal punch: the repeated “behold” and the blunt verdict “Hypocrite!” heighten the sting.
- Everyday concreteness (carpentry terms) makes the moral point unforgettable.

How it condemns hypocrisy without banning correction
- It doesn’t say, “Never address your brother’s speck.” It commands sequence and condition: “First remove the beam… then you will see clearly to remove the speck.”
- The target is self‑blind, performative judgment—pretending to be a moral surgeon while unexamined oneself—not discernment itself.
- By requiring self‑examination and repentance first, it transforms correction into humble, restorative help rather than condemnation.
- The criterion is clarity and proportion: once your own larger fault is dealt with, you’re able to help gently and rightly.

In short: Jesus forbids hypocritical, censorious judgment but authorizes careful, compassionate correction that flows from prior self‑correction.
