Based on the Greek text and the context of the Sermon on the Mount, the moral danger in Matthew 6:1 is not **visibility**, but rather **motivation** and **validation.**

Here is an analysis of why public goodness is framed as dangerous:

### 1. The Problem is the "In Order To" (*Pros To*)
In the original Greek, the verse uses the phrase *pros to theathenai autois*. The construction denotes **purpose or intent.** 

The verse does not say, "Take heed that men do not see your alms." It says, do not do them **"to be seen of them."** The danger is not the accidental witness of an act, but the deliberate curation of an act for an audience. When the goal is human observation, the act ceases to be a service to God and becomes a performance for a "theater" (the root of the word *theathenai*).

### 2. The Displacement of the Object
In Christian ethics, an "alms-deed" is intended to be an act of love directed toward two parties: the recipient and God. 
*   **The Danger:** When one performs for public witness, the "object" of the act changes. The neighbor in need becomes a mere prop, and God becomes a secondary bystander. 
*   **The Result:** The person is no longer practicing "righteousness" (*dikaiosynē*); they are practicing **self-marketing.** 

### 3. The "Reward" Paradox
Jesus warns that those who seek public acclaim "have their reward" (*apechousin ton misthon auton*). 
*   In the commercial language of the time, this phrase meant "received in full" or "paid in full." 
*   The moral danger is that human praise is a **closed system.** If you perform for human applause, the applause is your total payment. You have essentially "traded" a spiritual, eternal connection with the Father for a fleeting, horizontal ego-boost. 

### 4. Visibility vs. Intent (The Context of Matthew 5:16)
To understand if visibility itself is the problem, one must look back just one chapter to **Matthew 5:16**: *"Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."*

*   **Matthew 5:16:** Visibility is good if the result is that people look **past you** to God.
*   **Matthew 6:1:** Visibility is dangerous if the intent is that people look **at you** and stop there.

### Summary
The problem is specifically **the craving to be seen.** 

Public goodness is morally dangerous because the ego is incredibly adept at hijacking virtuous impulses. For Jesus, the "secret" act (doing alms so the left hand doesn't know what the right is doing) is a spiritual discipline designed to starve the ego. If your virtue requires an audience to be satisfying, Matthew 6:1 suggests that it isn't actually virtue—it is vanity in disguise.
