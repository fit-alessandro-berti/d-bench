The sharpness of Amos 5:21–24 is not an exaggeration; it is a necessary rhetorical device to shatter a dangerous illusion. The prophet does not ask for "better balance" because the issue at hand is not a matter of *quantity* or *priority*, but of **fundamental incompatibility**.

Here is why the rejection is so absolute, and what specific moral and spiritual fraud is being exposed:

### 1. The Fraud of Compartmentalization
The primary spiritual fraud exposed here is the belief that one can compartmentalize life into "religious duty" and "ethical conduct." The Israelites believed that as long as they performed the correct rituals (feasts, offerings, songs), God was obligated to bless them, regardless of how they treated their neighbors.

Amos exposes this as **magical thinking**: the idea that ritual acts have automatic power to appease God, independent of the heart’s condition or societal justice. By saying, *"I hate, I despise... I will not smell... I will not accept,"* God declares that these rituals are not just insufficient—they are **offensive**. They are not neutral acts done poorly; they are active insults when performed by unjust people.

### 2. Ritual as a Cover for Injustice
The context of Amos is crucial. Earlier in the chapter (and throughout the book), Amos details the social sins of Israel:
- Selling the righteous for silver and the poor for a pair of sandals (Amos 2:6).
- Trampling on the heads of the poor (Amos 2:7).
- Denying justice to the oppressed in the courts (Amos 5:10–12).

The "fraud" is using worship as a **smokescreen** or a **bribe**. The wealthy and powerful were likely offering lavish sacrifices ("fat beasts") precisely to assuage their consciences or to publicly display their piety while continuing to exploit the vulnerable. God rejects the worship because it is being used to **legitimize oppression**. To accept the offering would be to endorse the system that produced it.

### 3. The Nature of True Worship: Covenant Fidelity
In the Hebrew prophetic tradition, worship is not a separate sphere from ethics. It is the expression of a covenant relationship with Yahweh, who is defined by justice (*mishpat*) and righteousness (*tzedakah*). 

- **God’s character is just.** Therefore, worship that ignores justice is a misrepresentation of God’s nature.
- **Worship without justice is idolatry.** It turns God into a deity who can be manipulated by rituals, rather than the Holy One who demands ethical fidelity.

By rejecting the worship so sharply, Amos is saying: **"You cannot worship the God of Justice while practicing injustice."** The two are mutually exclusive. There is no "balance" because you cannot serve two masters: you cannot serve both Mammon (through exploitation) and God (through ritual) and claim loyalty to either.

### 4. Why Not "Better Balance"?
Asking for "better balance" would imply that:
- The rituals themselves are inherently good, just needing adjustment.
- Social justice is an optional add-on to faith.
- God is primarily interested in ceremonial correctness, with ethics as a secondary concern.

Amos rejects this entirely. He argues that **without justice, ritual is noise**. The "noise of thy songs" and "melody of thy viols" are described as irritating distractions because they drown out the cries of the oppressed. God refuses to listen to the music of the oppressors while ignoring the suffering of the victims.

### Conclusion: The Moral Fraud Exposed
The passage exposes the fraud of **hypocritical religiosity**—the use of sacred forms to mask profane actions. It reveals that:
1. **Ritual without righteousness is abomination.**
2. **God prioritizes ethical treatment of humans over ceremonial observance.**
3. **True worship is inseparable from social justice.**

The famous climax—*"But let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream"*—is not a suggestion for improvement. It is the **only acceptable form of worship**. Justice and righteousness are not alternatives to sacrifice; they are the very substance of true devotion. Without them, all other religious acts are empty, offensive, and rejected.
