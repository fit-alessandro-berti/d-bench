In Luke 18:9–14, the condemnation of the Pharisee and the justification of the tax collector hinge on the orientation of the heart toward God. While several of the factors you mentioned play a role, they are symptoms of a deeper, foundational failure.

Here is an analysis of what condemns the Pharisee and why the tax collector is vindicated.

### 1. What Condemns the Pharisee?

The text does not condemn the Pharisee for **false doctrine** (his theology of fasting and tithing was biblically sound) or even for his **good works** (which were actually in excess of the Law). Instead, he is condemned by a toxic combination of the following:

*   **Self-Reliance (The Root):** The preface states he "trusted in [himself] that [he was] righteous." He believed righteousness was an achieved status rather than a gifted one. He did not come to the temple to *receive* from God, but to *report* to God.
*   **Vertical Pride through Horizontal Comparison:** His prayer was "with himself." He used other people—specifically the "extortioners, unjust, and adulterers"—as a spiritual yardstick. By defining his goodness by being "better than the worst," he shielded himself from the holiness of God, which would have exposed his own need.
*   **Contempt as a Identity Marker:** The text notes he "despised others." His sense of holiness required the degradation of those around him. In his mind, his proximity to God was calculated by his distance from the "publican."
*   **Virtue as a Barrier to Grace:** The Pharisee’s tragedy is that his virtues became his greatest vices. Because he was "full" of his own accomplishments (fasting, tithing), there was no room for God’s mercy. He used his morality to put God in his debt.

### 2. Why the Tax Collector’s Posture is "Spiritually Truer"

The tax collector (publican) is not justified because he is a sinner, but because he **agrees with God** about his condition. His posture is treated as truer for several reasons:

*   **Objective Realism:** The tax collector acknowledges the reality of the situation. In the presence of a holy God, the only honest human response is a plea for mercy. The Pharisee is living in a delusion of perfection; the tax collector is living in the truth of his insufficiency.
*   **The Greek "Propitiation":** In the original Greek, the tax collector’s plea (*hilastheti moi*) is more specific than a general request for kindness. It essentially means, "Be propitiated to me" or "Let the atonement be for me." He is appealing to the sacrificial system—the mercy seat—acknowledging that only a blood sacrifice can bridge the gap between his sin and God’s holiness.
*   **Dependency vs. Autonomy:** The tax collector stands "afar off" and cannot "lift up his eyes." This physical posture reflects a spiritual reality: he knows he has no "rights" to claim and no "works" to bargain with. He casts himself entirely upon the character of God rather than his own performance.
*   **Brokenness as the Entry Point:** The "spiritually truer" posture is one of *penitence*. Scripture frequently asserts that "a broken and a contrite heart, O God, thou wilt not despise" (Psalm 51:17). The tax collector is "justified" because he leaves the temple having received what he asked for (mercy), whereas the Pharisee received what he asked for (nothing, since he asked for nothing).

### Conclusion
The Pharisee is condemned because he used his **righteousness to hide from God**, while the tax collector is justified because he used his **sin to run to God**. The "truth" of the tax collector’s position is found in the total abandonment of self-justification in favor of divine justification.
