### Short answer  
In this parable the **Pharisee is condemned for self‑righteousness** – a heart‑attitude that combines a doctrinal error (the belief that righteousness can be earned by one’s own works), pride, the habit of comparing himself with others, open contempt for the “sinner,” and the use of his piety as a public‑relations stunt.  

The tax‑collector is “justified” because his posture is the opposite: genuine humility, brokenness, confession of sin, and trust that God’s mercy (not personal merit) is the ground of his acceptance.  

Below is a step‑by‑step unpacking of why the Pharisaic self‑righteousness is condemned and why the publican’s humility is spiritually true.

---

## 1. The cultural & literary setting

| Element | What the text says (KJV) | Why it matters |
|---------|--------------------------|----------------|
| **Audience** (v 9) | “*to certain which trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others*” | Jesus warns the “self‑righteous” that they are the target of the parable. |
| **Two‑fold contrast** (vv 10‑14) | A **Pharisee** (a respected, law‑observant teacher) vs. a **publican** (a collaborator with Rome, socially and religiously outcast). | The social hierarchy is inverted: the “religious elite” is rejected, the “social outcast” is accepted. |
| **Prayer style** (v 11‑12) | *“God, I thank thee, that I am not as other men … I fast twice in the week, I give tithes of all that I possess.”* | The prayer is a **self‑congratulatory litany** – not a dialogue with God. |
| **Posture of the publican** (v 13) | *“standing afar off, would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven, but smote upon his breast, saying, God be merciful to me a sinner.”* | Physical humility (distance, averted eyes, self‑beating) mirrors internal repentance. |

The literary device is a **synkrisis** (Greek “comparison”). The reader is invited to weigh the two men and see which aligns with God’s kingdom.

---

## 2. What actually condemns thePharisee?

### 2.1. **False doctrine – works‑righteousness**

- **Greek term** – *δικαιοσύνη* (dikaiosýnē) is the noun “righteousness.” The Pharisaic prayer presupposes that **righteousness can be achieved by *doing* (fasting, tithes)**, a theology that Paul later explicitly refutes (Rom 3:20; Gal 2:16).  
- **Implication** – The Pharisaic prayer is, in effect, a *theological statement*: “My conduct earns me a place with God.” This is a doctrinal error about the *basis* of justification.

### 2.2. **Pride – the root of self‑righteousness**

- **Greek word** – *ὑπερηφανία* (hyperēphanía) is the usual term for “pride.” The prayer’s first clause, “I thank *thee* that I am **not** like other men,” is a classic expression of *self‑exaltation*.  
- **Result** – Pride makes the worshiper place himself **above** God’s mercy and above his neighbor, a condition the New Testament repeatedly condemns (James 4:6; 1 Peter 5:5).

### 2.3. **Comparison and contempt**

- **Greek word** – *καταφρονέω* (kataphronéō) means “to despise.” The Pharisaic prayer says, “or **even as this publican**.” The tone is **contemptuous**, a judgment of the other’s moral status.  
- **Effect** – Comparing oneself favorably *and* looking down on another is a violation of the “love your neighbor” command (Lev 19:18; Matt 22:39).

### 2.4. **Virtue as self‑display**

- **Greek phrase** – *ἐγὼ νηστεύω δίς τοῦ σαββάτου* (“I fast twice in the week”) and *δίδωμι τὰς δεκάτας πάντων* (“I give tithes of all that I possess”). The Pharisaic list is presented **publicly**, almost as a résumé.  
- **Problem** – When good deeds are performed **to be seen**, Jesus warns they receive their reward *in full* from human applause, not from God (Matt 6:2, 5‑6). The Pharisaic prayer is “*doing* righteousness to be *admired*,” not to *glorify* God.

### 2.5. **Synthesis: self‑righteousness (pride + works + contempt + display)**

In the New Testament, **self‑righteousness** is the overarching sin that gathers all the above elements. The *Pharisee* trusts **himself** (the pronoun *ἐγώ* is emphatic), **trusts his deeds**, and **despises** others. The Lord’s verdict in the parable is that such an attitude makes one *unrighteous* in God’s sight.

---

## 3. Why the tax‑collector’s posture is “spiritually truer”

| Feature of the publican | Biblical/theological significance |
|--------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Standing afar off** (v 13) | Acknowledges his **distance** from God because of sin; mirrors the prophetic image of a *sinner* who cannot approach the holy God (Isaiah 59:2). |
| **Would not lift up his eyes to heaven** | A sign of *awe* and *reverence*; the publican recognises God’s transcendence, unlike the Pharisaic “I thank thee” which treats God as a *witness* to his merit. |
| **Smote upon his breast** | Physical expression of *contrition*; in the Old Testament, *beating the breast* is linked with mourning and repentance (Ezek 21:12; Joel 2:12‑13). |
| **“God be merciful to me a sinner”** | **Confession** of sin *and* appeal to God’s **grace** (Greek: *ἱλάσκομαι* “be propitiated”). The focus is *God’s mercy*, not his own performance. |
| **Justified** (v 14) – *δικαιόω* | The verb “to justify” in the LXX and in Paul means “to declare righteous on the basis of faith and grace” (Rom 3:24‑26). The publican receives this declaration because his heart *trusts* God’s mercy, not his deeds. |

**Key theological principle**: *Justification* (the act of being declared “righteous”) is a **gift** received through **faith** that expresses itself in humility (Rom 3:28; 4:5). The publican’s posture embodies the “poor in spirit” and “meek” Beatitudes (Matt 5:3‑5) and the psalmist’s “a broken and a contrite heart, O God, thou wilt not despise” (Psalm 51:17).

---

## 4. The theological stakes – why this matters today

| Issue | What the parable teaches | Modern‐day application |
|-------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Justification by faith vs. works** | The pharisee’s “I do X, therefore I am righteous” is rejected; the publican’s “God be merciful” is accepted. | Christians must guard against “salvation by checklist” – religious activity alone cannot earn God’s favour. |
| **Pride vs. humility** | Pride creates a **spiritual barrier** (James 4:6); humility opens the way for God’s grace (1 Peter 5:5‑6). | In daily life, humility in relationships, leadership, and self‑evaluation reflects the publican’s heart‑posture. |
| **Contempt for others** | Despising the “sinner” is a *violation* of love (Matt 22:39) and blinds one to one’s own sinfulness. | Believers are called to “speak the truth in love” (Eph 4:15) and to recognize that all have fallen short (Rom 3:23). |
| **Self‑display vs. true worship** | When worship becomes a performance, the reward is human applause, not divine acceptance (Matt 6:2). | Authentic worship is “in spirit and in truth” (John 4:24) – motivated by love for God, not the desire to be seen. |
| **Heart vs. external ritual** | The core of righteousness is a *renewed heart* (Ezek 36:26‑27). | Church ministries and personal disciplines should flow from transformed hearts, not merely from “outward conformity.” |

| Condemnation of thePharisee | Why it matters |

| **Virtue as self‑display** | Turns obedience into a public performance, losing spiritual reward. |
| **Overall: self‑righteousness** | The root that gathers all the above – the decisive reason for condemnation. |

|--------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Humility (standing afar off, averted eyes)** | Recognizes personal distance from God because of sin. |
| **Repentance (beating breast)** | Physical expression of a broken heart. |
| **Confession (“God be merciful to me a sinner”)** | Centers on God’s mercy, not personal merit. |
| **Faith in grace (justified)** | Receives the gift of righteousness through faith. |
| **Overall: true worship** | Authentic worship flows from a humble, trusting heart. |

---

### Bottom line

The Pharisaic prayer is condemned not merely because the man *does* good works, but because **his heart treats those works as the basis for God’s acceptance**, thereby rejecting the need for divine mercy. The tax‑collector is justified because his *whole being*—posture, words, and trust—expresses the humility that God honors: “God, be merciful to me, a sinner.” The parable thus clinches the New‑Testament doctrine that **righteousness is a gift of grace received through faith, and that a humble, broken heart is the only soil in which that gift can take root**.
