## What the Text Actually Says

The grammar matters here. Jesus says: "For where your treasure is, *there will your heart be also.*"

The heart **follows** the treasure. That's a directional claim—the heart goes to where the treasure already is. On the plain reading, this leans toward **training** rather than mere **revealing**.

If the text only meant *revealing*, we'd expect the logic reversed: "Where your heart is, there your treasure will be"—your inner affections producing your outward investments. But Jesus puts the treasure first and the heart second. The placement of what you value pulls your heart after it.

## But It's Not Either/Or

In practice the two collapse into a feedback loop:

- **Revealing**: You can read someone's loves by tracing their expenditures—of money, yes, but also time and thought. "Show me your calendar and your bank statement, and I'll show you your god."
- **Training**: But the act of investing *also forms* the love. Where you sink your resources, your heart learns to lean. Affection isn't only expressed by treasure; it's *cultivated* by it.

So the text's emphasis is on training, but the diagnostic use (revealing) comes along for free, because a trained heart is also a readable one.

## How Possession, Attention, and Love Get Tied Together

Notice the progression embedded in the passage:

1. **Possession** — "treasures... upon earth." Where you *store* and *secure* what you have.
2. **Attention** — what corrupts treasure (moth, rust, theft) is precisely what you must *watch*, *guard*, *worry over*. Earthly treasure demands vigilance; it colonizes your attention by being perishable and vulnerable.
3. **Love** — "there will your heart be also." Sustained attention to a possession becomes affection for it. You cannot guard something obsessively without your heart migrating toward it.

The chain runs: **what you hold → what you watch → what you love.** Possession sets the object, attention is the bridge, and love is the destination the heart arrives at.

## The Sharp Edge of It

This is why Jesus frames it as a *command about storage* rather than a command about feeling. He doesn't say "love heavenly things more." He says *relocate your treasure*—because he knows the heart is not autonomous. It is *trainable*, and treasure is one of its trainers. You don't fix the heart directly; you redirect the investment, and the heart is dragged after.

The unsettling implication: you are, to a real degree, *making* your loves right now by where you place your resources—often without choosing to.
