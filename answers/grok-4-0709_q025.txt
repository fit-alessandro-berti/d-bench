### Interpretation of Matthew 6:1

Matthew 6:1 is part of Jesus' Sermon on the Mount in the New Testament (King James Version here, as quoted). It addresses the practice of "alms" (acts of charity or righteousness), cautioning against performing them in a way that prioritizes human approval over divine purpose. Let's break down your question step by step, drawing directly from the verse and its context.

#### What Makes Public Goodness Morally Dangerous?
In this verse, Jesus warns that doing good deeds publicly can become morally problematic if it undermines the spiritual integrity of the act. The "danger" lies in the potential for hypocrisy or self-serving behavior:
- **Loss of Heavenly Reward**: The verse explicitly states that if your good deeds are done "to be seen of [men]," you forfeit any reward from "your Father which is in heaven." This implies that the act, while outwardly positive, becomes spiritually void or counterfeit. It's not truly "good" in a moral or eternal sense because it's tainted by impure motives.
- **Moral Corrosion**: Broader context in Matthew 6 (e.g., verses 2-6 on hypocrisy in giving, praying, and fasting) suggests that public displays can foster pride, vanity, or a performative righteousness. This turns altruism into a tool for social status, admiration, or manipulation, eroding genuine compassion. It's "morally dangerous" because it risks turning virtue into vice—encouraging people to prioritize appearances over authentic faith or ethics.

This isn't a blanket condemnation of all public goodness; elsewhere in the Bible (e.g., Matthew 5:16: "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven"), visible good deeds are encouraged when they point others toward God rather than self-glorification.

#### Is the Problem Visibility Itself, or the Craving to Be Seen?
The core issue is **not visibility itself, but the craving to be seen** (i.e., the intent or motivation behind the act). Here's why:
- **Visibility Isn't Inherently Bad**: The verse doesn't prohibit public acts of goodness outright. In fact, many biblical figures (like Jesus himself) performed miracles and taught publicly. The Greek word used here for "to be seen" (theaomai) implies a theatrical or ostentatious display—think of it as "putting on a show" for applause. If the good deed happens to be visible but isn't driven by a desire for attention, it's not condemned.
- **The Craving as the Root Problem**: The danger is the internal "craving" or desire for human recognition, which Jesus frames as a form of idolatry—worshipping praise from people instead of seeking approval from God. This motive corrupts the act, making it self-centered rather than God-centered or other-centered. As the verse puts it, "otherwise ye have no reward"—the earthly acclaim becomes your only payoff, leaving nothing eternal.

In modern terms, this could apply to "virtue signaling" on social media, where someone donates publicly not out of genuine care but to boost their image. The verse encourages self-examination: Why am I doing this? For likes, status, or something deeper?
