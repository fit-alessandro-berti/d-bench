**Step‑by‑step analysis**

1. **Identify what the Pharisee actually does in his prayer**  
   - He *stands* (physically places himself before God).  
   - He *prays to himself* (“with himself”), not addressing the divine presence but an inner monologue that others can overhear.  
   - His words are a list of **comparisons**: “I am not like … extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even this publican.”  
   - He enumerates his own **religious practices** (“I fast twice a week… I give tithes…”).  
   - The tone is one of **self‑exaltation** and **judgment of another**.

2. **Pinpoint the theological problem**  
   In the Gospel’s moral teaching, prayer is meant to be an act of *humble dependence* on God (cf. Matthew 6:5‑8). The Pharisee violates this by:

   - **Pride** – He trusts in his own righteousness (“I am righteous”) and uses that trust as a basis for superiority.  
   - **Comparison & Contempt** – By measuring himself against others he *exalts* himself while *deprecating* the publican, which is explicitly condemned (James 4:6; Romans 12:3).  
   - **Virtue‑display** – His tithing and fasting become a *public résumé* rather than inward worship. This turns religious duty into “self‑advertisement,” a form of false piety that Jesus denounces (Matthew 23:5‑7).

   All three are intertwined, but the **root cause is pride**; comparison and contempt flow from it, and the use of virtue as display *manifests* that pride.

3. **Why does Luke label this “condemnation”?**  
   - **False doctrine** (the belief that one can earn righteousness by works) underlies the Pharisee’s posture, but Jesus’ criticism is not directed at a doctrinal system; it is aimed at the *attitude* of the heart.  
   - The condemnation rests on two biblical principles:  
     1. **“God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble.”** (James 4:6; Proverbs 3:34) – Here the Pharisee’s pride is directly opposed by God.  
     2. **The Law of the Kingdom**: Righteousness comes *by faith* and *by mercy*, not by self‑righteous boasting (Romans 10:9). The Pharisee substitutes works for grace, thereby nullifying the law’s true intent (cf. Romans 3:20).

4. **Examine the publican’s posture**  
   - He stands **“afar off”**, refusing even to lift his eyes toward heaven—a gesture of *humility* and awareness of unworthiness.  
   - He beats his breast, a physical expression of contrition (cf. Ezekiel 9:4; Psalm 35:13).  
   - His prayer is a single plea: **“God be merciful to me a sinner.”** This acknowledges dependence on divine grace alone.

5. **What makes the publican’s attitude “spiritually truer”?**  

   | Aspect | Pharisee | Publican |
   |--------|----------|----------|
   | **Heart‑orientation** | Self‑focused, boastful pride | God‑focused humility, acknowledgment of sin |
   | **Source of justification** | Personal merit (fasting, tithes) | Divine mercy alone |
   | **Response to the Law** | Uses it as a weapon against others | Recognizes law’s condemnation and pleads for grace |
   | **Biblical precedent** | Contrasts with “the proud” who are condemned (James 4:6; 1 Peter 5:5) | Mirrors the tax‑collector in Isaiah 66:2, David’s contrition (Psalm 51), and Jesus’ own teaching on humility (Luke 14:11). |

   The publican exemplifies **true repentance**—the “spiritual truth” that true righteousness comes from God’s mercy received by a humble heart. This aligns with Jesus’ frequent emphasis that *“whoever exalts himself will be humbled, and whoever humbles himself will be exalted.”* (Matthew 23:12; Luke 14:11).

6. **Synthesis – What exactly condemns the Pharisee?**  

   - The primary fault is **pride**, which manifests as:
     - **Comparison** (“I am not like …”) → creates a false hierarchy.
     - **Contempt** toward the publican → violates Jesus’ command to love one’s neighbor (Mark 12:31).
     - **Display of virtue** (public fasting, tithing) → turns religious duty into self‑glorification, i.e., *false piety*.

   - While “false doctrine” (the erroneous belief that works earn righteousness) is a background error, the **condemnation in this parable hinges on the attitude of pride** and its outward expressions. The Pharisee’s prayer is not an act of worship but a performance for self‑exaltation.

7. **Why is the tax collector’s posture more spiritually authentic?**  

   - His humility acknowledges his *sinfulness* before God, prompting a sincere request for mercy—precisely what the Gospel calls “faith” (cf. Romans 10:9).  
   - By refusing self‑justification and instead relying on divine grace, he models the **heart posture Jesus repeatedly teaches**: trust in God’s compassion rather than one’s own merit.  
   - Consequently, Scripture declares him *“justified”*—not because of works but because his faith (trust in mercy) is counted as righteousness (cf. Genesis 15:6; Romans 4:5).

---

### **Conclusion**

- The Pharisee is condemned **primarily for pride**, which surfaces as:
  - Comparative self‑exaltation,
  - Contempt toward others,
  - Using religious observances as a platform for self‑display (false piety).  

- The publican’s posture embodies true repentance: humble acknowledgment of sin and reliance on God’s mercy, which Scripture identifies as the genuine source of justification. Hence his prayer is “spiritually truer” because it aligns with the Gospel principle that **righteousness comes by grace through faith**, not by human achievement or self‑glorification.
