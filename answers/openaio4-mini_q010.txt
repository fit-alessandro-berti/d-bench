1. What kind of power is being praised?  
   Paul is holding up a paradoxical, redemptive power: not the power of coercion or spectacle, but the power of self-emptying love (the Greek term is kenōsis, “emptying”).  Christ’s greatness isn’t in lording it over others, but in voluntarily setting aside his divine privileges, taking the form of a servant, and submitting even to a shameful death.  This is a power to transform hearts and relationships by giving rather than grasping—an authority rooted in sacrifice and service.

2. Why is humility joined to greatness so unsettling?  
   a. It subverts our expectations.  We’re used to strength being showy—bullets, speeches, ranks.  When someone with real power refuses to wield it in the obvious way, we don’t know how to read or resist them.  
   b. It disarms us.  A person who insists on serving rather than commanding robs opponents of their usual leverage: you can’t fight back with the same weapons (intimidation, one-upmanship, pride).  
   c. It confronts our own insecurities.  Someone who is truly secure in their dignity and worth doesn’t need to prove themselves.  That silent confidence makes us restless, because it mirrors every time we’ve tried—and failed—to shore up our own value by asserting superiority.  
   d. It calls for a higher ethic.  When greatness is expressed in self-sacrifice, we’re challenged to rethink ambition, success, and authority.  It feels unsettling because it shakes the foundations of “how the world works” and forces us to choose whether we will follow its logic or cling to the familiar routes of domination.
