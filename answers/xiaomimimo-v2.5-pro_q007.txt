This is an excellent and profound question that cuts to the heart of ethical living. The verse is often misinterpreted as a call for total moral indifference or agnosticism, but its meaning is far richer and more demanding.

The difference lies in the **object, intent, and authority** behind the act.

### The Appetite to Condemn
This is a judgment that is:
*   **Egocentric:** It places the judger in the seat of ultimate authority. It's about my superiority, my vindication, or my security.
*   **Punitive and Final:** It seeks a verdict of "guilty" and demands a penalty, often social exclusion or damnation. It is a closing of the book.
*   **Hypocritical:** It assumes the judger's own flaws are irrelevant or lesser. As Jesus says elsewhere in the same sermon (Matt 7:3-5), it's focusing on the speck in another's eye while ignoring the plank in your own.
*   **Motivated by Appetite:** It can feed a sense of moral superiority, righteousness, or even vengeance. It seeks a kind of emotional or social satisfaction.

**Example:** "That person is a terrible sinner and deserves to be shunned." This is a verdict that assumes the speaker has perfect knowledge of the heart and the right to assign final consequences.

### Discernment
This is a judgment that is:
*   **Truth-seeking:** Its goal is accurate perception and understanding of reality. It asks, "What is true about this situation, action, or character?"
*   **Prudent and Practical:** It leads to wise decisions about how to act, who to trust, what to support or avoid, and how to help. It is a tool for navigation, not for sentencing.
*   **Humble:** It acknowledges its own limited perspective and fallibility. It makes a distinction between an action being wrong and the entire person being irredeemable.
*   **Accountable and Other-Oriented:** It is the foundation of responsible action. We must discern what is harmful in order to protect the vulnerable, to correct what is broken, and to nurture what is good. A parent discerns a child's lie not to condemn the child's soul, but to teach honesty and restore trust.

**Example:** "That action is dishonest and harmful. I cannot participate in it, and I must address it to prevent further harm." This is an assessment that guides action while leaving ultimate judgment and the possibility of redemption to a higher authority (or to the future).

### The Core Difference for Jesus's Teaching
Luke 6:37 is not an instruction to become blind. It is an instruction about the **posture of the heart and the jurisdiction of the soul.**

1.  **It's About Ultimate Judgment:** The "judging" forbidden here is the attempt to pronounce final verdict on a person's eternal worth or destiny—a right Jesus reserves for God. When we condemn, we are not just evaluating a behavior; we are attempting to exile someone from the community of grace.

2.  **It Links Judgment to Receiving Judgment:** The verse is deeply pragmatic and relational. It states a spiritual law: the measure we use for others will be measured back to us. A life of condemnation is a life lived in a courtroom of your own making, where you will inevitably be tried by your own standards. Discernment, however, is a tool used in the workshop of repair and building.

3.  **Forgiveness is the Key Differentiator:** The second half of the verse, "**forgive, and ye shall be forgiven**," completes the picture. Condemnation writes someone off. Discernment identifies the wrong *so that* forgiveness and reconciliation can have a target. You cannot truly forgive a sin you refuse to see. Discernment is the diagnosis; forgiveness is the treatment.

In short: **We are commanded to be discerning—clear-eyed about good and evil, truth and falsehood. We are forbidden from being condemnatory—usurping God's role as the final judge of the human heart.**

The difference is not in the use of the eyes to see, but in the use of the gavel to sentence. One is the work of a scout or a physician; the other is the work of a hanging judge. Jesus calls us to be the former, not the latter.
