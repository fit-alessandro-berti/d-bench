The way Paul stacks these terms suggests he's not treating bitterness as merely one feeling among others, but as something more like a root condition—and the text gives a few clues about its nature.

**The progression in verse 31 is telling.** Notice the order: bitterness, then wrath, then anger, then clamour, then evil speaking. It moves from something internal and quiet to something increasingly external and loud. Bitterness sits at the head of that list. It reads less like a list of equivalent sins and more like a sequence—bitterness as the seedbed from which the louder things grow. This argues against reading it as merely a passing feeling. A feeling doesn't generate a chain of consequences the way a settled disposition does.

**On whether it's feeling, habit, or chosen posture**—the text seems to resist a clean separation. The command "be put away from you" (the Greek *arthētō* carries the sense of being lifted off and removed) treats bitterness as something that can be deliberately set aside. You don't command someone to stop having an involuntary emotion that way. So there's a volitional dimension: it's something you *hold*, and can therefore release. But the fact that it must be actively removed also implies it tends to *stay* unless dealt with—which points to its habitual, lodging quality. So perhaps: a feeling that, when nursed, hardens into a posture.

**The contrast with forgiveness is the interpretive key.** Bitterness and forgiveness are opposed because they are two opposite responses to the *same* situation—being wronged. Both presuppose an injury. Bitterness is what you do with a wound when you keep it; forgiveness is what you do with a wound when you release it. They aren't opposite emotions so much as opposite *transactions*. That's why Paul can command the switch from one to the other—not "feel differently" but "handle the debt differently."

**On contagiousness**—the text doesn't explicitly call it contagious, but two things hint that way. First, the relational frame: "kind one to another... forgiving one another." Bitterness held privately doesn't stay private; it surfaces as the wrath, clamour, and evil speaking that wound others, who then have their own wounds to keep or release. Second, elsewhere Hebrews 12:15 warns of a "root of bitterness springing up" that "many be defiled"—using exactly the image of spread. So the broader scriptural witness does treat it as something that propagates.

**The deepest grounding is in the last clause:** "even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you." The standard for releasing the debt is not the size of the offense against you but the prior reality that your own enormous debt was released. Bitterness, in this light, is partly a *memory failure*—forgetting what you've been forgiven. Forgiveness is remembering it. That's why the two are set against each other so directly: they flow from two different accounts of your own standing.

So the most faithful reading is probably "all of the above in sequence"—a feeling that becomes a posture that becomes a habit that becomes contagious—which is precisely why Paul wants it lifted off *now*, before it completes that progression.
