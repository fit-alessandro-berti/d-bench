The Pharisee is condemned primarily for **self-righteousness**—specifically, trusting in his own moral performance as the basis for his standing with God. This is the explicit framing Jesus gives at the outset (v. 9): the parable targets those “who trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others.”

### What is not the main issue
- **False doctrine** is not the problem. The Pharisee’s listed practices (twice-weekly fasting, comprehensive tithing) were not heretical; they exceeded the minimum requirements of the law and were, in themselves, legitimate expressions of devotion. Jesus does not correct his theology here.
- The issue is not that he possessed virtues, but that he treated them as currency with God and as a platform for superiority.

### What actually condemns him
The text shows several interlocking expressions of the same root problem:

- **Pride that turns virtue into self-display**: He “prayed with himself” (v. 11). The prayer is not directed toward God in dependence but functions as a self-referential inventory of his superiority. It is closer to a performance review than a petition.
- **Comparison**: He defines his righteousness negatively—“I am not as other men are…”—rather than positively before God. His goodness requires the existence of worse people to be visible.
- **Contempt**: The comparison is not abstract. He specifically names “this publican” as the lowest point of reference, using another man’s presence to elevate himself.

These are not separate categories but symptoms of the same disease: he has positioned himself as the standard by which righteousness is measured. In doing so, he has no actual need for mercy, only for recognition.

### Why the tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer
The tax collector’s justification (v. 14) is not because his life was morally superior—it was not—but because his approach to God was structurally different:

- He makes no comparative claims. He does not mention the Pharisee at all.
- He offers no ledger of virtues. His only statement is a direct appeal for mercy (*hilaskomai*—a term with sacrificial overtones, “be propitiated toward me”).
- His physical posture (standing at a distance, unwilling to lift his eyes, beating his breast) externalizes the internal recognition that he has nothing to bring except his sin.

Jesus’ verdict is therefore not moral relativism (“the tax collector was actually the good one”). It is a statement about the proper relationship to God: the one who comes empty-handed, acknowledging his need for mercy, receives the verdict of “justified.” The one who comes with a full account of his own righteousness does not.

The parable is not ultimately about which man was worse in behavior, but about which posture God accepts. The tax collector’s humility is not a superior moral achievement; it is the honest recognition of reality before a holy God. The Pharisee’s posture is false because it denies that reality.
