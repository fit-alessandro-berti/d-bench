This passage establishes a direct, causal relationship between the internal state of a person (the "treasure of the heart") and their external expression (speech). To understand how it connects speech with hidden moral stockpiles, we can analyze it through the three lenses you provided: symptoms, choices, and revelations.

### 1. Words as Symptoms (The Diagnostic View)
Jesus presents speech as a diagnostic tool. In medical terms, a symptom is an outward sign of an internal condition. Here, the "abundance of the heart" acts as the source, and the words are the overflow.

The phrase *"out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh"* suggests that speech is not an isolated event but a result of a saturated internal state. Just as a cup that is filled with water will spill water when shaken, a heart filled with "evil treasure" will inevitably produce evil words. In this sense, words are **symptoms** of the heart's moral health. If the speech is toxic ("vipers"), it indicates a toxic internal environment.

### 2. Words as Revelations (The Unmasking View)
The passage suggests that while the heart is "hidden" (a stockpile), speech is the mechanism that makes the hidden visible. This is a **revelatory** process.

The "treasure" (*thesauros* in Greek) refers to a storehouse or a vault. Normally, a vault is closed and its contents are secret. However, Jesus argues that it is impossible to maintain a complete separation between the vault and the voice. Eventually, the contents of the heart "leak" out through the mouth. Therefore, speech functions as a revelation—it strips away the persona or the pretense of the speaker to reveal the actual moral inventory of their soul.

### 3. Words as Choices (The Volitional View)
While the passage emphasizes the *source* of the words, it also implies a moral responsibility for what is being "brought forth." 

The distinction between the "good man" and the "evil man" suggests that the heart is not a passive vessel, but a curated collection. A "treasure" is something gathered, stored, and maintained. By describing the heart as a storehouse, the text implies that individuals have, over time, chosen what to cultivate—whether it be bitterness, pride, and malice (evil treasure) or mercy, truth, and love (good treasure). Thus, while a specific word may feel like an impulsive reaction, it is the result of a lifelong series of **choices** regarding what one allows to take root in their heart.

### Conclusion: The Integrated Connection
The passage suggests that words are **all three**. 

*   They are **choices** in the sense that we cultivate the "treasure" we store.
*   They are **symptoms** in the sense that they naturally flow from that stored abundance.
*   They are **revelations** in the sense that they prove to the world what is actually inside the hidden vault of the heart.

Ultimately, Jesus is arguing that moral transformation cannot be achieved through "speech therapy" (simply trying to say better things) but requires "heart surgery" (changing the treasure within). You cannot produce "good things" from an "evil" storehouse; to change the output, one must change the stockpile.
