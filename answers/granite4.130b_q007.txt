Luke 6:37 presents a profound teaching often encapsulated in the phrase “Judge not, lest you be judged.” At first glance, one might interpret this as advocating for an absolute disregard of moral judgment or suggesting that moral judgments never matter. However, a deeper exploration reveals a nuanced distinction between **discernment** and the **appetite to condemn**, offering insights into ethical living, personal growth, and community cohesion.

### Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn

#### 1. **Definition of Terms**

- **Discernment**: This involves the ability or judgment to perceive what is true or right; it’s a thoughtful consideration that weighs actions, motivations, and consequences without immediate rush to judgment. Discernment seeks understanding, acknowledges complexity, and often aims at fostering growth, correction, or reconciliation rather than merely pointing out wrongdoing.

- **The Appetite to Condemn**: This refers to an inclination or desire to hastily pass moral judgment on others, typically accompanied by a sense of superiority, schadenfreude (pleasure derived from another person's trouble), or the need to assert one’s own moral authority. It is less about understanding and more about condemnation, often leading to division, resentment, and harm.

#### 2. **Intent and Outcome**

- **Discernment** comes from a place of empathy, humility, and a desire for truth that benefits all parties involved. Its outcome tends towards healing, understanding, and constructive change. For instance, discerning the reasons behind someone’s actions might lead to offering support or guidance rather than punishment.

- **The Appetite to Condemn**, however, is driven by ego, judgmentalism, and sometimes hidden personal grievances. The outcome is typically polarizing; it alienates the condemned, entrenches divisions, and can exacerbate conflict. It reflects a zero-sum mindset where one’s moral standing is elevated at another's expense.

#### 3. **Application in Luke 6:37**

- **Judging Not** in this context isn’t a call to ignore morality or ethical behavior but rather an admonition against adopting a condemning stance that lacks mercy, understanding, and the recognition of our own fallibility. Jesus’ teaching emphasizes that when we withhold judgment, we open ourselves to receiving grace and forgiveness—a reciprocal dynamic foundational to many spiritual and philosophical traditions.

- **Condemn Not** further refines this by specifically targeting the desire to see others punished or diminished morally. It’s an encouragement to refrain from actions or attitudes that seek to lower another's standing for our benefit or satisfaction.

- **Forgive, and You Shall Be Forgiven** ties these concepts together by suggesting that extending forgiveness (a form of discernment that sees beyond transgressions to the potential for change and redemption) creates a pathway for receiving forgiveness in return. It underscores a cycle of grace over retribution.

### How This Differs from Saying Moral Judgments Never Matter

- **Acknowledgment of Moral Standards**: Luke 6:37 does not nullify the existence or importance of moral standards. Instead, it critiques how and why we apply these standards. Moral judgments matter insofar as they are applied with discernment—seeking understanding, growth, and reconciliation rather than condemnation.

- **Focus on Heart Attitude**: The teaching shifts the focus from outward actions alone to the heart attitude behind our judgments. It’s not so much about whether someone steals or lies (the act) but why they do it and how we respond with either grace (discernment) or harshness (condemnation).

- **Encouragement of Self-Reflection**: By warning against judgment, Jesus prompts introspection—ask ourselves if our readiness to judge stems from a genuine concern for moral integrity or an ego-driven need to feel superior. This self-aware reflection is central to spiritual and personal development.

### Conclusion

Luke 6:37 champions a paradigm where discernment—rooted in empathy, understanding, and a commitment to mutual growth—replaces the appetite to condemn, which thrives on division and moral superiority. It teaches that while moral judgments are indeed significant, their application must be tempered with grace, humility, and a recognition of our shared humanity and fallibility. This approach not only fosters personal integrity but also builds communities grounded in forgiveness and mutual respect rather than judgment and exclusion.
