The elder brother’s deepest problem is **alienation from his father hidden beneath outward obedience**. His anger, envy, wounded fairness, and discomfort with mercy are all expressions of that deeper condition.

He understands the relationship transactionally:

- “These many years do I **serve** thee” portrays his life as labor for a master.
- “Neither transgressed I…yet thou never gavest me…” treats obedience as a claim to compensation.
- “This thy son” refuses to call the prodigal his brother.
- He wants to celebrate “with my friends,” suggesting that mere fellowship with his father does not feel like his reward.

His pride in obedience therefore produces a merit-based sense of justice: the obedient deserve celebration; the disobedient deserve exclusion. Mercy offends him because it appears to erase the distinction upon which his identity and claim to superiority depend.

The father’s answer exposes the mistake:

> “Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine.”

The elder son has been thinking like a neglected servant, though he is a beloved son and heir. Moreover, while claiming never to disobey, he is presently refusing his father’s invitation and refusing to share his father’s joy.

The father also gently corrects “this thy son” with:

> “This thy brother…”

Thus, the central issue is not merely anger or envy. It is **self-righteous estrangement: he cannot rejoice in the father’s gracious love because he believes sonship should operate by earned merit rather than mercy**. Like the younger brother, he is “lost,” but his lostness takes the respectable form of obedience without love.

Significantly, the father goes out to him too and entreats him. The same mercy that welcomes the younger son is being offered to the elder; the parable ends without telling us whether he will enter the feast.
