In this verse, Jesus is warning against the moral dangers of doing good deeds publicly with the primary motivation of being seen and praised by others. The key issue is not visibility itself, but rather the underlying motive and desire for recognition.

A few key points:

1. Craving recognition - The fundamental problem Jesus highlights is doing acts of righteousness and charity with the goal of receiving accolades from other people. It's the inward craving for fame, respect and a reputation for holiness that corrupts the moral value of the deed. The good act becomes a means to feed the ego rather than an expression of genuine love and obedience to God.

2. Hypocrisy - There is an element of hypocrisy when people make a show of their giving and good works to impress others. It presents a false facade of righteousness that is really masking pride and self-promotion. The outward appearance does not match the inward heart motivation.

3. Misplaced reward - Jesus says that those who do their alms to be seen by others "have no reward from your Father in heaven." Their reward is the fleeting praise of men, not the eternal affirmation of God. They forfeit a heavenly reward by seeking an earthly one.

4. Secrecy and humility - The antidote Jesus prescribes is doing good deeds in secret, not letting the right hand know what the left is doing (v.3-4). The point is nurturing a heart of humility that is content with God alone seeing and rewarding our acts of love and obedience.

Ultimately, the problem is not doing good publicly per se - visibility itself is not categorically wrong. It is possible to do public acts of charity with a pure heart that desires to love others and glorify God, not seek personal credit. The key is examining our motivations honestly. But because the temptation to crave human approval is so subtle and strong, Jesus advises doing good in hidden ways to avoid that moral pitfall and cultivate the humility God desires. The highest righteousness is for God's eyes only.
