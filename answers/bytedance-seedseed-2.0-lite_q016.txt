All the listed struggles appear in the passage, but they are layered symptoms leading to one core, deepest problem: discomfort with the radical, unearned mercy the father extends to the younger son. Let’s break down the hierarchy of these issues to illustrate this:
1. **Surface, observable reactions**: Anger is the elder brother’s first visible emotion, a outward expression of his inner turmoil, not a root cause. Envy and his sense of wounded fairness are his stated grievances: he resents that his wasteful younger brother receives a celebration he feels he was denied after years of loyal service.
2. **Intermediate, framing struggle**: Pride in his unbroken obedience leads him to frame his relationship with his father as a transactional contract, where compliance earns rewards, rather than a permanent, unconditional filial bond. He keeps score of his own good behavior to measure his worthiness of his father’s generosity, and resents that the younger son’s recklessness seems to be rewarded without any accounting.
3. **Deepest core problem**: All of these symptoms stem from his rejection of the unmerited mercy at the heart of the father’s joy. He cannot embrace that belonging, celebration, and love in his father’s house are not earned through good behavior—they are freely given to all who belong to the family. The father’s response cuts through his scorekeeping entirely: he reminds the elder son that everything he owns has always been the elder son’s, with no need to earn it. The elder brother refuses to enter the feast because he cannot rejoice that his lost brother is alive again; the mercy shown to the younger son upends the entire transactional framework he has built his life around. He would rather earn his place than receive love freely, and resents that same free welcome being extended to his brother.
