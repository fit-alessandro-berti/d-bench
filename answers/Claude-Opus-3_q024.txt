The passage from Ezekiel 36:26-27 draws a stark contrast between external compliance with God's laws and a genuine, heart-level transformation that only God can provide. The difference lies in motivation and enablement.

External compliance means obeying rules and judgments out of duty, fear, or self-effort. It is behavior modification without a change in the underlying attitudes and desires of the heart. This kind of obedience is rigid and can even breed resentment, much like the hardness of stone. 

In contrast, God promises to give His people a "new heart" and a "new spirit"—an internal change at the deepest level of their being. This isn't just an improvement of the old but a complete replacement, a regeneration. The "heart of flesh" represents a softened, responsive heart that beats in harmony with God's desires.

The stony heart symbolizes the hardened human condition—cold, dead, unreceptive and even antagonistic to God. It is set in its ways, much like stone is hard and inflexible. This is the natural state of the human heart without divine intervention (Jeremiah 17:9). 

But God promises to take away this stubborn, unresponsive heart and replace it with a living, malleable heart open to Him. The new spirit He puts within is the Holy Spirit who writes God's laws on the heart, changes desires, and gives the power to obey (Jeremiah 31:33, Ezekiel 11:19-20).

So the new heart and spirit bring an internal transformation—new loves, new motivations, new power to obey from the inside out. Obedience flows from a changed nature, not just from external pressures. It is a willing cooperation with God's Spirit rather than begrudging conformity to rules.

In summary, external compliance modifies behavior, but the new heart changes the person at the core, enabling true obedience. Hardness of heart, without divine intervention, makes us unresponsive to God. But in His grace, God does heart transplant surgery, replacing our heart of stone with a heart of flesh responsive to Him.
