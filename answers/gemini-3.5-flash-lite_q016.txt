The elder brother’s situation in Luke 15:28–32 is one of the most psychologically and spiritually profound portraits in the New Testament. While **anger, envy, wounded fairness, pride in obedience, and discomfort with mercy** are all clearly present on the surface, they are ultimately symptoms of a deeper, foundational problem. 

If we must identify his *deepest* problem, it is best described as **discomfort with mercy rooted in a transactional, rather than relational, understanding of grace.** 

However, to fully diagnose his condition, we have to look at how these elements interconnect to form his core spiritual malady:

### 1. The Surface Symptoms
*   **Anger and Envy:** He is furious that the sinner is celebrated while the faithful servant is ignored. He looks at the "fatted calf" given to the prodigal with deep resentment.
*   **Wounded Fairness & Pride in Obedience:** He views his relationship with his father through the lens of a strict meritocracy: *"These many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I... and yet thou never gavest me a kid."* He believes his obedience has earned him preferential treatment.

### 2. The Deeper Malady: A Transactional Relationship
The elder brother’s deepest problem is that **he does not understand sonship; he understands employment.** 

Notice his language: *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee..."* The Greek word used here for "serve" is *douleuo*—the work of a slave or a hired servant, not a son. He has been living in his father's house, sharing the father's wealth, and eating at the father's table, yet he has felt like a laborer earning wages rather than an heir receiving an inheritance. 

Because he views the relationship as transactional (Good behavior = Reward; Bad behavior = Punishment), grace offends him. To him, the father’s unconditional mercy looks like injustice. If the younger brother gets the fatted calf without paying his dues, then the elder brother's years of self-denial and moral compliance feel pointless and wasted. 

### 3. The Core Issue: Discomfort with Mercy and Grace
This leads directly to his ultimate barrier: **he is fundamentally unlike his father.** 

The father operates on the logic of *grace* ("this thy brother was dead, and is alive again"), while the elder brother operates on the logic of *retributive justice* ("this thy son... which hath devoured thy living"). 

By refusing to go into the feast, the elder brother excludes himself. His deepest problem is that **he wants justice for others, but strict mercy for himself.** He is trapped outside the party not just because he is angry at his brother, but because he is fundamentally estranged from the heart of his father. He possesses the father's estate ("all that I have is thine"), but he does not share the father's heart of compassion for the lost. 

### Conclusion
While pride, anger, and a sense of unfairness are his most visible struggles, the elder brother's deepest problem is a **spiritual blindness to the nature of grace**. He believes he has earned his place through obedience, which blinds him to the reality that everything he supposedly "earned" was already his by grace simply because he was a son, not a servant.
