In Ephesians 4:31-32, Paul is not giving you a random list of bad behaviors vs. good behaviors. He is showing you two completely different ways of being human.

And bitterness vs. forgiveness is the root contrast that holds it all together.

> Let **all** bitterness, and wrath, and anger, and clamour, and evil speaking, be put away from you, with **all** malice: and be ye kind one to another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you.

### 1. Why are they placed directly against each other?

Because biblically, bitterness *is* calcified unforgiveness.

Look at the order in verse 31. It's not random, it's a progression from the inside out:

* **pikria - bitterness:** The inner, settled resentment. Literally a bitter root, poison.
* **thymos and orge - wrath and anger:** *Thymos* is the hot flare-up, the explosion. *Orge* is the slow burn, the smoldering grudge you keep warm.
* **krauge - clamour:** That inner poison now becomes public - shouting, quarreling, losing control in front of others.
* **blasphemia - evil speaking / slander:** Now you are trying to tear the other person's name down.
* **kakia - malice:** The umbrella term - a settled desire for harm to come to someone.

Bitterness is the root, malice is the fruit. Everything in between is how it grows.

Forgiveness is placed against it because it attacks the problem at the root, not just the fruit. You can suppress wrath, you can stop shouting, you can stop slandering - but if you keep the root, it all grows back.

And the context makes this even sharper. Ephesians 4:22-24:

> That ye put off concerning the former conversation the old man... And be renewed in the spirit of your mind; And that ye put on the new man...

Verse 31: "Let... be put away" - *artheto* - is the language of taking off a garment. Verse 32: "be ye kind" - *ginesthe* - become, keep becoming. This is old humanity vs. new humanity. Bitterness belongs to the old man who has been wronged and lives to get even. Forgiveness belongs to the new man who has been forgiven and lives to give out what he received.

That's why verse 32 ends where it does: "even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you." It is the only way that root can actually come out. You don't forgive because bitterness is impolite. You forgive because you cannot afford to keep it if you have understood how much you have been forgiven.

### 2. Is bitterness a feeling, a habit, a posture, or contagious?

The text treats it as all four, in that order - but it is primarily a **chosen posture that becomes contagious.**

**1. It starts as a feeling.**
*Pikria* was used in Greek for bitter water, a bitter herb, something sharp and pointed to the taste. It's a real, involuntary reaction to being wounded or wronged. To feel that sting is not sin.

**2. It becomes a habit and then a posture.**
This is why Paul says "let ALL bitterness be put away." You can't put away a fleeting temptation the same way you put away a garment. You put it away because you picked it up, you put it on, you decided to live in it. Bitterness is resentment that you have agreed to nurse and keep.

Hebrews 12:15 uses the same word to warn:

> "lest any root of bitterness springing up trouble you, and thereby many be defiled."

A root is invisible, it lives underground, and it settles in. In Acts 8:23 Peter tells Simon, "thou art in the gall of bitterness." He's *in* it. It's his location, not just his emotion.

**3. And yes, it is contagious.**
Notice the words "all" and "from you" plural - from *all of you*. This is written to a church community. Bitterness is never private. Deuteronomy 29:18 warned of "a root that beareth gall and wormwood" - one person's secret idolatry that spreads poison to the whole tribe.

An unforgiving person doesn't just hurt himself. His *krauge* and *blasphemia* will recruit others, create sides, justify itself. Forgiveness is also communal - "forgiving *one another*."

In short: Paul treats a feeling you didn't ask for as something that, if not released through forgiveness, becomes a posture you *do* choose, a garment you refuse to take off, and a root that will defile more than just you.

That's why he counters it not with "try to feel less bitter," but with charizomenoi - from *charis*, grace. "Be graciously giving grace to one another." Forgiveness in this verse is not just letting go of a debt; it is actively extending favor. You cannot be full of *pikria* and *charis* at the same time.
