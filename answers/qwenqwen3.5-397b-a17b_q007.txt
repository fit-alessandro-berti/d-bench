This is one of the most frequently quoted—and frequently misunderstood—verses in the New Testament. To understand Luke 6:37, we must distinguish between **moral discernment** (recognizing right and wrong) and **condemnatory judgment** (assuming the role of final arbiter over a person's soul).

Here is an exploration of how this verse differs from moral relativism, and the critical distinction between discernment and the appetite to condemn.

### 1. Why This Is Not Moral Relativism
If "Judge not" meant "moral judgments never matter," the rest of Jesus' teaching would collapse. Throughout the Sermon on the Plain (and the Sermon on the Mount in Matthew), Jesus makes numerous moral distinctions. He calls for repentance, warns against greed, lust, and hypocrisy, and instructs followers to identify "bad trees" by their "bad fruit."

You cannot forgive a sin you do not recognize. You cannot restore a brother you do not believe has wandered. Therefore, Luke 6:37 cannot mean that we should be morally blind.

**The verse is not prohibiting the *recognition* of sin; it is prohibiting the *usurpation* of God's role.**
*   **Moral Relativism says:** "There is no objective wrong, so I cannot call your action sin."
*   **Luke 6:37 says:** "I recognize this is wrong, but I do not have the right to sentence you to irredeemability, because I am subject to the same standard."

### 2. Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn
The core of the verse lies in the difference between acting as a **physician** (discernment) and acting as an **executioner** (condemnation).

#### Discernment (The Surgeon's Eye)
Discernment is the ability to evaluate actions and character based on truth. It is necessary for protection, wisdom, and restoration.
*   **Motivation:** Love, protection, and healing.
*   **Posture:** Humble. It acknowledges, "I see a problem here, and I am also capable of problems."
*   **Goal:** Restoration. "How do we fix this?" or "How do I protect the flock from this behavior?"
*   **Biblical Parallel:** In Luke 6:41, Jesus asks, "Why do you see the speck... but do not notice the log?" This implies you *can* see the speck. Discernment is seeing the speck accurately.

#### The Appetite to Condemn (The Executioner's Gavel)
The "judging" Jesus forbids is specific. The Greek word *krino* in this context, paired with *katadikazo* (condemn), suggests a legal sentencing. It is an internal posture of superiority.
*   **Motivation:** Self-justification, superiority, control, or vengeance. It is an "appetite" because it feeds the ego. By lowering someone else, we raise ourselves.
*   **Posture:** Hypocritical. It ignores one's own faults while magnifying the faults of others.
*   **Goal:** Rejection. "You are defined by this error, and you are cast out."
*   **The Reciprocity:** Jesus warns, "ye shall not be judged." This suggests that if we adopt a posture of merciless severity toward others, we invite that same standard of severity upon ourselves from God.

### 3. The Key Distinction: Hypocrisy and Finality
The context of Luke 6 clarifies that the prohibition is against **hypocritical** and **final** judgment.

**Hypocrisy:**
Jesus illustrates this immediately after verse 37 with the image of the log and the speck. The problem isn't noticing the speck in your brother's eye; the problem is trying to remove it while you have a log in your own. Moral judgment matters, but it must be administered by those who are aware of their own need for mercy.

**Finality:**
To "condemn" is to write someone off. It is to decide that a person is nothing more than their worst action.
*   **Discernment says:** "This action is destructive and must stop."
*   **Condemnation says:** "You are a destructive person and are beyond hope."

### Summary
Luke 6:37 is not a command to abandon moral truth; it is a command to abandon moral **arrogance**.

To say moral judgments never matter is to live in a world without boundaries, where love is indistinguishable from enabling. To follow Luke 6:37 is to live in a world of clear boundaries, where truth is spoken, but always through the filter of mercy. It demands that we hold our standards high, but our gavel low. We are called to diagnose the disease (discernment) without acting as though we are the ones who decide the patient's life or death (condemnation).
