In Matthew 6:1, Jesus cautions against doing "alms before men, to be seen of them." The issue here isn't the act of being seen per se but the motivation behind the act. The problem lies in doing good deeds with the primary intention of being observed and admired by others, rather than genuinely serving or pleasing God.

The danger Jesus highlights is not visibility itself but the craving to be seen and recognized by others. When the motivation for doing good is to gain the approval, admiration, or praise of others, it becomes self-serving. Such actions are driven by a desire for human accolades rather than a genuine desire to serve God or others.

Jesus indicates that when actions are performed with the intention of being seen and praised by others, the doer "has no reward" from their "Father which is in heaven." The implication is that the reward sought from men has already been received, and thus, there is no additional reward from God. The verse suggests that true righteousness and goodness should be motivated by a sincere desire to honor God, not to gain recognition from others.

Therefore, the morally dangerous aspect of public goodness, as addressed in this verse, is the underlying motivation to be seen and praised by others, rather than the visibility of the act itself.
