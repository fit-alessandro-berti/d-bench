The passage from **Luke 6:37**—*"Judge not, and ye shall not be judged: condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned: forgive, and ye shall be forgiven"*—is often misunderstood as a blanket prohibition against all moral evaluation. However, this interpretation conflicts with other biblical teachings (e.g., **1 Corinthians 5:12-13**, **Matthew 7:15-20**, **John 7:24**) that call for discernment and even correction within the community of faith. The key distinction lies between **discernment** (a necessary, humble, and redemptive evaluation) and **condemnation** (a self-righteous, final, and destructive judgment).

### **1. The Difference Between Discernment and Condemnation**
#### **A. Discernment: Wise and Redemptive Evaluation**
- **Definition:** Discernment is the ability to distinguish right from wrong, truth from falsehood, and healthy from harmful—**with the goal of restoration, protection, or growth**.
- **Biblical Basis:**
  - Jesus Himself calls for discernment: *"Beware of false prophets… you will recognize them by their fruits"* (**Matthew 7:15-16**).
  - Paul instructs the Corinthians to judge *within* the church (**1 Corinthians 5:12**) and to *"test everything; hold fast what is good"* (**1 Thessalonians 5:21**).
  - **John 7:24** says, *"Do not judge by appearances, but judge with right judgment."*
- **Motivation:** Love, protection, and truth (e.g., **Galatians 6:1**—restoring a sinner gently).
- **Posture:** Humility (recognizing one’s own sinfulness, **Matthew 7:3-5**) and a desire for reconciliation.

#### **B. Condemnation: Self-Righteous and Destructive Judgment**
- **Definition:** Condemnation is a final, harsh verdict that writes someone off as irredeemable, often stemming from pride, hypocrisy, or a desire to elevate oneself.
- **Biblical Warnings:**
  - **Luke 6:37** and **Matthew 7:1** warn against hypocritical judgment—where one condemns others while ignoring their own sins.
  - The Pharisees exemplified this (e.g., **Luke 18:9-14**, the Parable of the Pharisee and the Tax Collector).
  - **James 4:11-12** says, *"There is only one lawgiver and judge… Who are you to judge your neighbor?"*
- **Motivation:** Self-justification, superiority, or vengeance.
- **Posture:** Arrogance, finality (no room for grace or repentance), and often a lack of self-awareness.

### **2. Why Moral Judgments *Do* Matter (But Must Be Handled Rightly)**
- **Moral truth exists:** The Bible affirms objective right and wrong (e.g., **Romans 1:18-32**, **1 John 3:4**).
- **Discernment is necessary for:**
  - **Personal holiness** (avoiding sin, **1 Corinthians 10:15**).
  - **Protecting the vulnerable** (e.g., calling out abuse, **1 Timothy 5:20**).
  - **Church discipline** (restoring the straying, **Matthew 18:15-17**).
  - **Exposing false teaching** (**2 Peter 2:1**, **1 John 4:1**).
- **But judgment must be:**
  - **Self-examined first** (**Matthew 7:5**—*"First take the log out of your own eye"*).
  - **Merciful** (**James 2:13**—*"Mercy triumphs over judgment"*).
  - **Redemptive** (**Galatians 6:1**—*"Restore him gently"*).
  - **Not hypocritical** (**Romans 2:1**—*"You have no excuse, O man, every one of you who judges"*).

### **3. The Danger of Misapplying "Judge Not"**
- **Moral relativism:** If taken to an extreme, "judge not" could imply that no behavior is ever wrong—which contradicts Scripture (e.g., **1 Corinthians 6:9-10**).
- **Enabling sin:** Refusing to discern can lead to harm (e.g., tolerating abuse, false teaching, or exploitation in the name of "not judging").
- **Spiritual laziness:** It can become an excuse to avoid hard conversations or accountability.

### **4. Practical Applications**
| **Discernment** | **Condemnation** |
|------------------|------------------|
| *"This behavior is harmful; let’s address it with love."* | *"You’re a terrible person and beyond hope."* |
| *"The Bible says this is sin; how can I help you overcome it?"* | *"I’m better than you because I don’t do that."* |
| *"We need to protect the flock from false teaching."* | *"You’re a heretic and going to hell."* |
| *"I struggle too, but let’s seek God’s truth together."* | *"I would never do that—you’re disgusting."* |

### **5. The Heart Behind Luke 6:37**
Jesus is not abolishing moral discernment but **condemning a critical, unloving spirit**. The call is to:
- **Forgive as we’ve been forgiven** (**Colossians 3:13**).
- **Show mercy, as God shows us mercy** (**Matthew 5:7**).
- **Leave final judgment to God** (**Romans 14:10-12**).

### **Conclusion**
Moral judgments *do* matter—but they must be made with **humility, love, and a redemptive purpose**, not with pride or a desire to destroy. The difference between discernment and condemnation is the difference between a **physician diagnosing to heal** and a **prosecutor sentencing to punish**. One reflects the heart of Christ; the other reflects the hypocrisy of the Pharisees.

As **Augustine** wisely said:
*"Let us hate the sin, but love the sinner."* That is the balance of biblical judgment.
