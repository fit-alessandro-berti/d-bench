The trust called for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is **radical, comprehensive reliance on God’s wisdom over one’s own**, characterized by humility, surrender, and active acknowledgment of divine guidance. Here’s a breakdown to distinguish it from anti-intellectualism:

---

### **1. The Kind of Trust Requested**
- **Radical Surrender**: The verse urges total trust ("trust in the Lord with *all* thine heart"), rejecting self-reliance ("lean not unto thine own understanding"). This is not passive fatalism but an intentional, conscious choice to prioritize God’s wisdom above personal judgment.
- **Acknowledgment of God’s Sovereignty**: By "acknowledging Him in all thy ways," the believer recognizes that every decision—big or small—is under God’s authority. This fosters humility, as human understanding is limited and fallible.
- **Result: Divine Direction**: The outcome is not just peace (as stated in verse 6) but alignment with God’s purposes. Trust here is relational, not merely theoretical—it involves seeking His will actively.

---

### **2. Humble Self-Doubt vs. Anti-Intellectualism**
- **Humble Self-Doubt**:
  - **Rooted in Reverence**: Acknowledges human limitations while honoring God’s omniscience. It’s a posture of learning, where one says, "I don’t know everything, but You do."
  - **Active Engagement**: Involves using wisdom, discernment, and knowledge—but always filtered through spiritual truth (e.g., Proverbs 2:1–5).
  - **Example**: A doctor trusting God’s healing power while diligently applying medical science.

- **Anti-Intellectualism**:
  - **Rejection of Reason**: Dismisses intellectual effort as irrelevant or harmful, often due to fear of being wrong or arrogance.
  - **Emotional Reaction**: May stem from pride ("I don’t need books!") or trauma (e.g., past failures where knowledge led to harm).
  - **Example**: Refusing to study evidence for climate change out of distrust of "human reason," even when facts align with biblical stewardship.

---

### **Key Distinction**
- **Humility ≠ Ignorance**: Trusting God doesn’t negate wisdom; it integrates it. Proverbs 3:5–6 assumes the reader is wise enough to *seek* God’s direction (not ignorant of it). Anti-intellectualism, however, often conflates humility with ignorance.
- **Faith vs. Fatalism**: Biblical trust engages the world actively (e.g., Esther’s courage in influencing a king), whereas anti-intellectualism may paralyze action under the guise of "surrender."

---

### **Conclusion**
Proverbs 3:5–6 calls for **faithful intellectual engagement under God’s sovereignty**. It demands humility—not doubt in one’s capacity to think, but confidence in God’s ability to guide thought and action. True trust balances wisdom with surrender, avoiding both blind reliance on intellect and dismissive skepticism toward truth. As James 1:5 promises, seeking God’s wisdom (with humility) unlocks understanding (James 1:6).
