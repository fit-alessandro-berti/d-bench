This is an excellent question that gets to the heart of Jesus’s anthropology in the Gospels. The passage is dense with metaphor and paints a powerful picture of the relationship between our inner world and our outward expression.

Let's break it down, addressing your core question: **Are words symptoms, choices, revelations, or all three?**

The answer is **all three**, but in a very specific, layered way that Jesus clarifies using the imagery of a "treasure" (stockpile) and the process of "bringing forth."

### 1. Words as Symptoms (The "Abundance of the Heart")

The key phrase is: *"out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh."*

This is the foundational layer. The Greek word for "abundance" (*perisseuma*) means "overflow" or "surplus." Imagine a glass of water being filled to the brim. When it can hold no more, the slightest disturbance causes it to spill over. The water that spills is not *caused* by the disturbance; it is a *symptom* of the glass being full.

- **Whispered words** when you think no one hears.
- **Blurted words** in a moment of stress or anger.
- **Words of comfort** that flow naturally from a compassionate person.

These are symptoms. They reveal what has been quietly accumulating in the heart. The "abundant" part implies that the heart is not neutral; it is a space of accumulation, a stockpile. A person's speech is a reliable indicator of what that stockpile contains.

**However, if words were *only* symptoms, we would be mere bystanders to our own speech, like a thermometer that merely registers a temperature. Jesus doesn't leave it there.**

### 2. Words as Choices (The "Treasure" and "Bringing Forth")

This is where the "stockpile" metaphor becomes active. Jesus uses the word **"treasure"** (*thēsauros*). A treasure is not a static pile; it is something we *curate*. We choose what to put in it.

- **"A good man out of the good treasure of the heart..."** The man had to *acquire* that good treasure. Through habits, meditation on God's law (Psalm 119:11), acts of charity, and choosing virtue, he has been making deposits.
- **"...bringeth forth good things."** The verb "bringeth forth" (*ekballō*) is active. It can mean "to throw out," "to produce," or "to put forth." This isn't a passive leak; it's an active, though often unconscious, production.

This introduces the element of **choice**. We choose what we think about, what we dwell on, what we watch, what we desire. These are the deposits we make into our spiritual and moral stockpile. Our words are then a choice to open the storehouse and release what is there.

- The **evil man** has *chosen* to accumulate malice, bitterness, envy, and lust. His speech is not just a symptom of being evil; it is the current, chosen output of his actively managed (or mismanaged) treasure chest.
- The **viper's brood** were *choosing* to blaspheme the Holy Spirit (the immediate context of this passage). Jesus is saying their evil words are not a random accident of birth, but the deliberate fruit of their own inner choices to call the work of God the work of Satan.

### 3. Words as Revelation (The Great "Giveaway")

This is the ultimate purpose of the passage. Speech is the *unveiling* of what is truly there.

- **"How can ye, being evil, speak good things?"** The rhetorical question implies it is impossible. A corrupt tree cannot bear good fruit. This reveals a fundamental identity.
- The "generation of vipers" has hidden venom. But their words are the fangs that inject it. Their pious questions and malicious accusations are not just poor choices; they are a revelation of their parentage (Satan, the father of lies, as John 8:44 puts it).

Words are the great decoder. People can act noble for a time. They can perform rituals. But when the pressure is on, or when they think they are safe, the "abundance" spills out. The "treasure" is opened.

- A **foolish** person gives away their lack of wisdom with every boast (Proverbs 29:11).
- A **wicked** person reveals their corruption with gossip and slander (Psalm 52:2-4).
- A **humble** person reveals their love for God with thanksgiving and intercession (Colossians 4:6).
- A **wise** person reveals their stockpile of understanding with words that heal and build up (Proverbs 12:18).

### Synthesis: The Complete Picture

So, to answer your final question: **Yes, all three, but not as separate categories. They are a single theological reality with three aspects:**

1.  **Words are Symptoms** of the **state** of the heart (the fullness of the cup).
2.  **Words are Choices** that reveal the **management** of the heart (the contents of the treasure chest).
3.  **Words are Revelations** that **identify** the person (the fruit reveals the tree).

The passage's radical claim is that this connection is **inevitable and inescapable.** You cannot hide your moral stockpile. It will come out. The "stockpile" is not a passive warehouse; it is the operating system of your soul. Your mouth is the primary user interface. Your words are the output.

**The Practical Imperative:**

This is not merely an observation. It is a warning and an invitation.

- **The Warning:** If you find your words are consistently evil (bitter, angry, lustful, deceitful), do not try to merely "tame your tongue" (a momentary fix). Attack the source. Go to the treasure chest of your heart. What are you depositing? What garbage are you hoarding? You need a fundamental change of stock, which only God can provide (Ezra 11:19, Matthew 5:8).
- **The Invitation:** If you want to speak good things, cultivate a good treasure. Fill your heart with the Word of God (Colossians 3:16). Meditate on what is true, noble, and pure (Philippians 4:8). Practice gratitude and forgiveness. The speech will then follow naturally, symptomatically, and revealingly.

Your speech is the most honest thing about you. Listen to it. It's showing you what's in your treasure chest.
