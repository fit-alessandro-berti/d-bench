Jesus’ words, “Judge not, … condemn not … forgive,” do not abolish moral evaluation; they forbid a particular spirit in which that evaluation is carried out.  The difference can be seen by looking at the vocabulary Jesus uses, His immediate context, and the rest of Scripture.

1.  The vocabulary  
   • “Judge” (Greek: krínō) can mean “to distinguish/to separate,” but in a legal setting it carries the sense of sitting in court and rendering a verdict.  
   • “Condemn” (katadikázō) is the next step: pronouncing the guilty sentence and fixing the penalty.  
   • In the same sermon Jesus will say that a tree is “known by its fruit” (6:43-45) and that a disciple must remove the plank from his own eye “and then you will see clearly” (6:42).  That is the language of discernment (diakrinō), not of verdict-rendering.

2.  The context: imitate the Father’s mercy  
   Verse 36, the line immediately before our text, is programmatic: “Be merciful, just as your Father is merciful.”  The ban on judging is one concrete way of mirroring the Father’s posture toward a still-sinful world.  Jesus is not suspending moral categories; He is prohibiting the urge to occupy God’s bench and relish the proclamation of guilt.

3.  Scripture still commands moral discernment  
   • “Stop judging by appearances but judge with righteous judgment” (John 7:24).  
   • “Test the spirits” (1 John 4:1).  
   • The church at Corinth is told to exclude the man living in blatant immorality (1 Cor 5).  
   • Elders are to “exhort and convict those who contradict” (Titus 1:9).

   The same Bible that says “Judge not” also tells believers to detect false prophets, correct sin and guard the flock.  Therefore Luke 6:37 cannot mean “moral judgments never matter.”

4.  Discernment versus condemnation  

   a.  Aim  
      • Discernment seeks truth and restoration (Gal 6:1; Jas 5:20).  
      • Condemnation seeks to display someone’s failure and close the door on him.

   b.  Attitude  
      • Discernment is humble: “There but for the grace of God go I.”  
      • Condemnation is self-exalting: “I thank you that I am not like that man” (Luke 18:11).

   c.  Authority claimed  
      • Discernment recognises that final judgment belongs to God alone (Rom 14:4).  
      • Condemnation presumes to issue the final verdict now.

   d.  Method  
      • Discernment begins with self-examination (Luke 6:41-42).  
      • Condemnation skips self-examination and magnifies another’s fault.

5.  Illustrations  

   • Jesus and the adulterous woman (John 8).  He does not deny her sin (“sin no more”) but refuses to join the mob’s condemnation.  
   • Nathan and David (2 Sam 12).  Nathan discerns the king’s sin and confronts him, yet leaves the sentencing to God.  
   • Paul and Peter at Antioch (Gal 2).  Paul publicly rebukes Peter’s hypocrisy, not to write him off but to preserve the gospel.

6.  Early Christian reflection  

   • Didache 4.3: “Do not judge bitterly, for with the measure you use it will be measured to you.”  
   • Chrysostom: “Christ did not forbid judging but the passing of a harsh sentence that overlooks one’s own sins.”

7.  Practical guidance for believers today  

   1. Begin with prayerful self-scrutiny; remove the plank first.  
   2. Distinguish between facts and motives.  Actions can be named as sin; motives are largely hidden.  
   3. When moral wrong is clear, speak in order to restore, not humiliate (Matt 18:15-17).  
   4. Keep the gospel in view: Christ bore the condemnation, so the church’s discipline is remedial, not retributive.  
   5. Leave ultimate verdict and recompense to God (Rom 12:19).

8.  Summary  

Luke 6:37 is not a veto on moral discernment.  It is a veto on the relish of playing prosecutor, judge and executioner toward fellow sinners.  Christians are still called to perceive right and wrong and, in love, to address what is wrong.  What they must abandon is the appetite to condemn—the impulse to treat the sinner as finished.  Discernment identifies evil in order to heal; condemnation announces a final sentence and walks away.  Jesus bans the latter so that His followers may imitate the mercy of the Father while still upholding what is good.
