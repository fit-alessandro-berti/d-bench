
Based on a close reading of the text and the father’s response, **the elder brother’s deepest problem is a fundamental misunderstanding of his relationship with his father.** This misunderstanding manifests as **discomfort with mercy itself**, but that discomfort is rooted in a **prideful, transactional view of obedience** and a **wounded sense of fairness**.

Here’s a breakdown:

1.  **The Core Issue: A Servant’s Heart, Not a Son’s Heart.**
    The elder brother’s complaint, “Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment,” reveals his mindset. He views his life with his father as one of **servitude and earned wages**, not as a relationship of **sonship and shared inheritance**. He says, “All these years I’ve been *working for you*,” implying he expects a “fair” payment (a kid for his friends). The father’s response, “Son, thou art ever with me, and **all that I have is thine**,” is a direct correction. The father is saying, “You already *possess* everything I have—you don’t need to earn it. You are in the family.”

2.  **How This Manifests:**
    *   **Anger & Envy:** His anger stems from seeing his younger brother receive lavish, unearned grace (the robe, ring, and fatted calf). This triggers his envy and sense of injustice.
    *   **Wounded Fairness:** His entire argument is based on a meritocratic view of the world: “I obeyed, so I deserve X. He disobeyed, so he deserves Y.” The father’s mercy shatters this “fair” system.
    *   **Pride in Obedience:** His obedience is not an expression of love for the father but a ledger of credits. He takes pride in his own performance as a means to an end (his reward).
    *   **Discomfort with Mercy Itself:** This is the ultimate symptom. He is not just upset about the party; he is upset that his brother is *forgiven and restored* without apparent cost or penance. The very idea that the father would give grace to someone so undeserving **threatens his entire worldview**. If the father gives grace freely, then his own years of “service” lose their transactional value. This discomfort reveals that he never truly trusted the father’s character—he saw him as a demanding employer, not a loving father.

**Conclusion:**

The elder brother’s deepest problem is **spiritual blindness to the father’s love and the nature of their relationship.** He is lost in his own house, trapped in a mindset of earning, while his brother is found. His issue is not merely anger or envy, but a **hardened heart that cannot comprehend or receive unconditional love and mercy.** Therefore, **discomfort with mercy itself** is the most accurate answer, as it points to this root inability to accept grace—both for himself (he thinks he must earn it) and for his brother (he thinks his brother must earn it). The father’s final words are an invitation to move from the slavery of meritocracy into the freedom of grace.
