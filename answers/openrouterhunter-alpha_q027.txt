What a profound question — and one that gets to the very heart of Jesus’s teaching here.

The Pharisee is condemned not by false doctrine — he’s actually quite orthodox in his practices — but by a **spiritual posture that inverts the very purpose of prayer and righteousness**. Let’s break it down:

**1. The core fault is pride expressed as contemptuous comparison.**  
He doesn’t just thank God for his own righteousness; he explicitly contrasts himself with “other men,” and especially with “this publican.” His prayer is not a dialogue with God but a **self-congratulatory monologue** performed in God’s direction. The virtue he lists (fasting, tithing) becomes a weapon to elevate himself and diminish others. This is why Jesus names those who “trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others” (Luke 18:9). Pride and contempt are intertwined here.

**2. Virtue as self-display rather than self-offering.**  
Notice the Pharisee “stood and prayed *thus with himself*.” There’s a chilling self-absorption in the Greek — he’s essentially talking to himself, using God as a backdrop. His good deeds are performed and presented as achievements that earn status. They’ve become **moral currency** for his spiritual account, not expressions of gratitude or love. The law was meant to lead to love of God and neighbor; here it’s been turned into a ladder of superiority.

**3. The tax collector’s posture is truer because it aligns with reality — and with grace.**  
He “would not lift up so much as his eyes to heaven, but beat his breast, saying, ‘God, be merciful to me, a sinner!’”  
His posture is one of **radical honesty and humble dependence**. He doesn’t compare himself to others. He doesn’t list his good deeds. He comes empty-handed, acknowledges his need, and casts himself entirely on God’s mercy. In doing so, he embodies the very attitude the law was designed to produce: a broken and contrite heart (Psalm 51:17).  
Jesus’s conclusion — “this man went down to his house justified rather than the other” — is a stunning reversal. It declares that **God’s grace responds to humility, not to moral performance**. The tax collector’s prayer is “truer” because it recognizes who God is (merciful) and who he is (in need of mercy). That alignment with truth opens him to justification.

**In short:**  
The Pharisee is condemned not for what he *does*, but for the **heart behind it** — a heart that uses religion to build a self, rather than to meet God. The tax collector is justified not because he’s morally better, but because his humility creates the space for grace to enter.  
Jesus consistently taught that the kingdom belongs to the poor in spirit — those who know their need. Here, that truth is dramatized in a parable that still startles us with its clarity.
