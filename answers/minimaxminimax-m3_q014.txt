# The Future Tense: Treasure as Pilgrim, Not Mirror

The Greek is decisive here. Jesus says, *"For where your treasure is, **there your heart will be** also"* (ὅπου ἐστὶν ὁ θησαυρός σου, **ἐκεῖ ἔσται** καὶ ἡ καρδία σου). The verb is **future** (ἔσται), not present. 

If Jesus had meant *treasure reveals the heart*, he would have written *ἐστίν*—"there your heart **is** also." Instead, the heart hasn't yet arrived. It is on its way to wherever the treasure has been deposited. This is the **"treasure trains the heart"** reading, and grammatically it is the stronger one.

The metaphor works like this: a person walks into a strange city and lays down their luggage at an inn. Their body is not yet there—but it will be. They have already committed themselves to the destination. Treasure is luggage; the heart is the traveler who follows it. We do not love what we have invested in, then give it our possessions. We *invest our possessions first*, and love accrues afterward as a kind of gravitational pull.

This is why the next verses (v. 22–24) immediately develop the theme:
- **v. 22–23**: The eye is the *lamp* of the body—a healthy (ἁπλοῦς, "single, undivided") eye fills the body with light. Attention shapes the whole person.
- **v. 24**: "No one can serve two masters." Possession becomes *service*. The thing you bank with becomes the thing you labor for.

So the movement is **possession → attention → service → heart**. The heart is the last thing to arrive, not the first.

# The Integration of Possession, Attention, Love

You ask how these three become tied together. The text implies they are not three separate faculties but three aspects of one act:

**Possession is frozen attention.** To "lay up treasure" is to give sustained, deliberative focus to something until it accumulates. A scattered glance is not treasure. Treasure requires *stayed looking*—the same word-picture as "fixing your eyes" (ἀτενίζω). The eye that dwells on an object is the hand that gathers it.

**Attention is incipient love.** A "single eye" (v. 22) is not merely a focused eye but a *generous* one—it gives light to the whole body. An "evil eye" (πονηρός) in Jewish idiom meant a *stingy* eye, one that hoards its gaze. Jesus is saying that how you look at the world *is* how you love it. To look long at something is to begin to belong to it.

**Love is totalizing possession.** The heart (καρδία) in Hebrew anthropology is not primarily the seat of emotion but the seat of *covenantal loyalty*—the place where the whole person is oriented. When your heart arrives where your treasure is, you are no longer free; you are *given over*. This is why v. 24 escalates to the language of slavery: "you cannot serve (δουλεύειν) two masters."

# The Feedback Loop

A caution, though: the "trains" reading does not exclude the "reveals" reading. They form a virtuous—or vicious—circle:

1. We *attend* to what we already partially *love*.
2. The more we attend, the more we *possess* (in attention, in time, in commitment).
3. The more we possess, the more our *love* deepens and consolidates.
4. Love then reorients attention, and the cycle tightens.

This is why Jesus does not say "examine your heart and adjust your treasure accordingly"—a piece of advice that would be psychologically impossible to follow, since we cannot escape the loop by introspection alone. Instead he commands at the level of *practice*: *stop laying up treasures on earth; start laying up treasures in heaven.* The reorientation of the hand will, in time, draw the heart after it.

# Why This Matters

The danger of the passage is not that possession is intrinsically evil (the Father owns everything, and Abram, Job, and the Psalmists hold wealth without condemnation). The danger is the **concealed finality** of where you put your ultimate reserves. Earthly treasure has a hidden liability: it can be *lost*, and its loss reveals that the heart, having followed it there, is also lost. Heavenly treasure—understood as the kingdom, character, relationships, and God himself—cannot be lost, and so the heart that travels toward it travels toward something that will hold it forever.

The text is therefore not a tip about retirement planning. It is a *theory of spiritual formation*: **you will inevitably become like what you have given yourself to.** The only question is whether you have chosen your destination, or let your attention drift you there.
