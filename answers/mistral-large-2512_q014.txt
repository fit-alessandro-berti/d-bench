This is a profound question that touches on the relationship between *treasure*, *heart*, and *discipleship* in Jesus’ teaching. The passage (Matthew 6:19–21) suggests a **dynamic interplay** between revelation and formation—treasure both *reveals* the heart *and* *trains* it. Let’s break this down by examining the text’s logic, the nature of "treasure," and how possession, attention, and love are interconnected.

---

### 1. **Treasure *Reveals* the Heart**
The final line—*"For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also"*—is often read as a **diagnostic statement**: your heart’s true allegiance is exposed by what you value most. In this sense:
- **Possession as a mirror**: What you *own*, *pursue*, or *protect* reflects what you *love*. If your treasure is earthly (wealth, status, security), your heart is tethered to the temporal. If it’s heavenly (God’s kingdom, justice, mercy), your heart is oriented toward eternity.
- **Attention as evidence**: Jesus’ warning about "moth and rust" and "thieves" highlights the *fragility* of earthly treasures. The fact that we *worry* about losing them (or *hoard* them) reveals where our trust and affections lie. As Augustine wrote, *"Love, and do what you will"*—our loves shape our actions, and our actions reveal our loves.
- **Love as the root**: The heart (Greek: *kardia*; Hebrew: *lev*) in biblical thought is the seat of *desire*, *will*, and *loyalty*. Treasure is not just a neutral object; it’s a *rival god* (cf. Matthew 6:24, "You cannot serve God and mammon"). Thus, treasure *exposes* whether we love God or idols.

This is the **revelatory** aspect: treasure acts as a litmus test for the heart’s true devotion.

---

### 2. **Treasure *Trains* the Heart**
But the passage also implies a **formative** dimension: what we treasure *shapes* our hearts over time. This is where the relationship between possession, attention, and love becomes *causal*:
- **Possession as formation**: Jesus doesn’t just say, "Don’t store up earthly treasures"; he commands, *"Lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven."* This is an *active, habitual* practice. The verb *"lay up"* (Greek: *thēsaurizō*) implies *accumulation*—a repeated choice to invest in what is eternal. Over time, these choices *reorient* the heart.
- **Attention as cultivation**: Where we direct our *focus* (time, energy, resources) shapes our *desires*. If we constantly attend to earthly treasures (e.g., wealth, comfort, approval), our hearts grow *attached* to them. Conversely, if we "seek first the kingdom" (Matthew 6:33), our hearts learn to *delight* in God. As James K.A. Smith argues, we are *"liturgical animals"*—our habits and practices *form* our loves.
- **Love as a muscle**: Love is not static; it’s *trained* by what we value. If we treasure God’s kingdom, our love for God *grows* through obedience, generosity, and worship. If we treasure earthly things, our love for them *hardens* into idolatry. The heart follows the treasure like a plant follows the sun.

This is the **pedagogical** aspect: treasure *educates* the heart, for better or worse.

---

### 3. **The Cycle of Possession, Attention, and Love**
The text ties these three together in a **self-reinforcing loop**:
1. **Possession → Attention**: What we *possess* (or pursue) captures our *attention*. If we own a car, we notice cars; if we invest in a hobby, we think about it. Jesus warns that earthly treasures *demand* our attention (e.g., anxiety over loss, Matthew 6:25–34).
2. **Attention → Love**: What we *attend to* shapes what we *love*. The more we focus on earthly treasures, the more we *desire* them; the more we focus on heavenly treasures, the more we *love* God’s kingdom.
3. **Love → Possession**: What we *love*, we *pursue* as treasure. If we love God, we "lay up" treasures in heaven; if we love mammon, we hoard earthly wealth.

This cycle is why Jesus’ command is so radical: it’s not just about *avoiding* earthly treasures but *actively* investing in heavenly ones to *reprogram* the heart.

---

### 4. **Practical Implications**
- **Diagnosis**: Ask, *"What do I spend my time, money, and energy on?"* That’s where your heart is.
- **Discipline**: Intentionally "lay up" heavenly treasures (e.g., generosity, prayer, justice) to *train* your heart toward God.
- **Warning**: Earthly treasures are *deceptive*—they promise security but deliver anxiety (Matthew 6:25–34). Heavenly treasures are *eternal* and *secure*.
- **Hope**: The heart is *malleable*. Even if your treasure has been earthly, repentance and reorientation can reshape your loves.

---

### 5. **Theological Context**
This passage is part of Jesus’ **Sermon on the Mount**, where he redefines righteousness for his disciples. The call to "lay up treasures in heaven" is not about *earning* salvation but about *living into* the kingdom’s values. It’s a call to:
- **Detachment**: Not asceticism (rejecting all possessions), but *freedom* from their control (cf. 1 Timothy 6:17–19).
- **Attachment**: Redirecting our loves toward God’s kingdom (cf. Colossians 3:1–2, "Set your minds on things above").
- **Trust**: Recognizing that God, not wealth, is our true security (Matthew 6:33).

---

### **Conclusion: Both/And**
The text does **both**:
- **Treasure reveals the heart** (it exposes what we already love).
- **Treasure trains the heart** (it shapes what we will love).

This is why Jesus doesn’t just say, *"Don’t love earthly things"* (which would be a command about the heart alone), but *"Don’t store up earthly treasures"* (a command about *action*). The heart follows the treasure, but the treasure is also *chosen* by the heart. The solution is not just to *feel* differently but to *act* differently—to invest in what is eternal so that our loves are *reformed*.

As C.S. Lewis put it:
> *"Do not waste time bothering whether you ‘love’ your neighbor; act as if you did. As soon as we do this, we find one of the great secrets. When you are behaving as if you loved someone, you will presently come to love him."*

The same applies to treasure: *Lay up treasures in heaven*, and your heart will follow.
