In **Matthew 6:1**, Jesus warns against performing acts of goodness (like alms-giving) *primarily* to be seen by others. The moral danger lies not in visibility *per se*, but in the **motivation behind the action**—specifically, the **craving for human recognition** rather than a sincere desire to please God.

### Key Distinctions:
1. **Visibility ≠ Sin**
   - Jesus doesn’t condemn *all* public acts of goodness (e.g., public worship, evangelism, or even charitable deeds that naturally draw attention). The issue isn’t whether others see the action, but *why* it’s done.
   - Even Jesus’ miracles were often public, but His motivation was love and obedience to the Father, not self-promotion.

2. **The Problem: **A Desire for Human Approval**
   - The danger is **pride**—seeking validation from people rather than from God. When goodness becomes a performance for applause, it risks becoming **self-serving** rather than **selfless**.
   - Jesus contrasts this with **secret generosity** (v. 4), which ensures the reward comes from God alone, not human admiration.

3. **Theological Implications**
   - **Rewards vs. Recognition**: Jesus promises a "reward from your Father in heaven" (v. 1, 4, 6). If the goal is human praise, the true reward (eternal approval) is forfeited.
   - **Hypocrisy**: Public displays of piety without genuine devotion can lead to **pharisaism**—outward righteousness masking inward corruption (cf. Matt. 23:27–28).

### Practical Application:
- **Intent Matters**: Ask, *"Am I doing this to honor God, or to be honored by people?"*
- **Balance**: Some acts (e.g., evangelism) require visibility, but the heart must remain focused on Christ, not self.
- **Humility**: True goodness flows from a heart that seeks God’s approval first (cf. 1 Cor. 10:31).

In short, the verse isn’t anti-publicity but **anti-selfishness**. The danger is when visibility becomes a **substitute for sincerity**.
