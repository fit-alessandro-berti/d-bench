Enemy-love is hard to take seriously because nearly every framework we bring to it mistranslates it first. We hear "love" as feeling, so the command sounds like a demand to manufacture warm affection toward someone who harmed us—which is either impossible or fake. We hear it through a moral economy built on reciprocity, where good returned for evil looks like a bookkeeping error that rewards aggression. And we've watched the command be weaponized against victims—abused spouses told to turn the other cheek, congregants pressured to "forgive" predators—so some of our suspicion is honestly earned. A command this often counterfeited becomes hard to hear in its original register.

But notice the grammar of the passage. The imperatives are verbs of action: bless, do good, pray. Nothing about feeling. And the enemies are not softened into "difficult people"—they are those who *curse you, hate you, despitefully use you, persecute you*. The command presupposes real enmity and real harm. That already disqualifies the sentimental reading, which survives only by denying the evil it claims to transcend. Sentimental enemy-love requires pretending the enemy isn't one; the text's enemy-love begins by naming him precisely. Sentimentality is cheap, unfalsifiable, and changes nothing—which is why it deserves the contempt it receives.

The strategic reading is more serious, because enemy-love genuinely *works* in ways that can be described tactically. It denies the enemy control of your interior life; he who angers you conquers you. It breaks retaliation spirals, which game theory confirms are nearly impossible to exit without some unilateral act of forgiveness. King and Gandhi wielded nonviolent enemy-love with tactical genius, exposing brutality and winning the watching world. So yes, strategic effects abound. But the strategic reading fails as an account of what the command *is*, for two reasons. First, the text's rationale isn't efficacy—it's resemblance: "that ye may be the children of your Father." The sun doesn't rise on the just and unjust because indiscriminate sunshine outperforms targeted sunshine. That's simply what the Father is like. Second, strategy has a success condition, and enemy-love sometimes gets you crucified. If the practice is justified by outcomes, the cross refutes it. Worse, loving your enemy *in order to* defeat him makes him a means to your end—which is a subtle betrayal of the command from within. The enemy loved strategically hasn't been loved at all; he's been played.

That leaves the third option, which I think the text itself supports: enemy-love as a form of strength, and specifically the strength of refusing imitation. Retaliation is mimicry—when you answer violence with violence and hatred with hatred, your enemy becomes your teacher; his logic now runs your life. Girard saw this clearly: vengeance is the sincerest form of flattery, and the cycle of reprisal is a hall of mirrors in which everyone becomes indistinguishable from everyone else. Enemy-love is the only move the enemy's categories don't contain. It is not passivity but a refusal to let the terms be set. Jesus offers a different model to imitate: the Father, whose giving is *teleios*—whole, undivided, single-handed. The rain doesn't audit. To love an enemy is to enact that family resemblance, to be undivided the way God is undivided.

This reading also has the resources to answer the strongest objections. Nietzsche charged that Christian love is ressentiment—weakness congratulating itself, the slave's revenge dressed as virtue. The honest response is: sometimes, yes. Enemy-love has counterfeits, and Nietzsche is a useful assay for detecting them. The test is whether the strong can do it—whether someone with the power to retaliate, and every justification for doing so, relinquishes it without self-deception. That's why the tradition's credibility rests on exemplars rather than arguments: King, Mandela, the Amish of Nickel Mines who forgave the man who murdered their children and attended his funeral. Nobody at Nickel Mines thought they were watching weakness. The event was unintelligible within ordinary moral categories, which is precisely why it was legible as the real thing.

The harder objection is the third-party problem: loving the oppressor can look like abandoning the oppressed. Here it's worth distinguishing what the command governs. Enemy-love in the *agape* sense—willing the good of the other as a human being, refusing to reduce him to his worst act—is compatible with fierce opposition to his deeds. You can stop a man, resist him, even fight him, without hating him or denying his humanity; you cannot love his cruelty. Whether the command permits *violent* resistance is genuinely contested ground between pacifist and just-war traditions. But the command at minimum governs the will's posture toward the enemy, and that posture is not permissiveness—it costs the perpetrator nothing and excuses nothing.

So my verdict: enemy-love in its sentimental and strategic forms deserves much of the skepticism it gets, but those forms are dilutions. The difficulty of taking it seriously is itself diagnostic. We sentimentalize what we cannot perform; domesticating the command into niceness is a defense mechanism, because the actual demand—to see the evil clearly, feel the full pull of vengeance, and will your enemy's good anyway—is harder than hatred, harder than revenge, and harder than indifference. The passage grounds it in sonship rather than argument because it's less a thesis than a family practice: something learned by resemblance, credible only when embodied, and almost unintelligible until you watch someone strong enough to do it.
