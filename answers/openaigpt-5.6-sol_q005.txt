Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it contradicts several powerful instincts: self-protection, retaliation, tribal loyalty, and the desire to see moral balance restored. Hatred can feel like proof that we recognize evil for what it is. Loving an enemy can therefore seem weak, unjust, or emotionally dishonest.

But in Matthew 5, “love” is not primarily a command to feel affection. It means willing and doing the good of another: refusing vengeance, praying for the persecutor, and treating even an opponent as a person before God. One can love an enemy while resisting that enemy, naming wrongdoing, protecting victims, establishing boundaries, and seeking justice. Enemy-love does not require trust, reconciliation without repentance, or passive submission to abuse.

Nor is it merely sentimental. Sentimentality minimizes evil; Jesus’ command assumes real enemies—people who curse, hate, exploit, and persecute. The command is radical precisely because the injury is real.

It may have strategic effects. Nonretaliation can interrupt cycles of violence, expose injustice, and sometimes convert an opponent. But if love is merely a tactic for defeating or changing the enemy, it remains a disguised form of control. Christian enemy-love is offered even when it “fails” strategically.

Its deepest basis is theological:

> God “maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good.”

God’s ordinary generosity is not limited to the deserving. To become “children of your Father” means to resemble that indiscriminate generosity—not to earn divine sonship by moral heroism, but to display the family likeness. The disciple refuses to let the enemy determine the kind of person the disciple will become.

Thus enemy-love is a form of moral strength because it holds together truths that retaliation tends to separate:

- **The wrong is truly wrong.**
- **Justice still matters.**
- **The wrongdoer remains human.**
- **My suffering does not authorize me to become cruel.**
- **The enemy’s good and transformation are still worth desiring.**

Praying for enemies is especially demanding because prayer places them within the sphere of God’s concern. It also opens one’s own anger to judgment and healing. This does not erase anger; it prevents anger from becoming sovereign.

So enemy-love is neither softness nor mere strategy. It is disciplined freedom: the power to oppose evil without reproducing its hatred, to seek justice without vengeance, and to desire redemption rather than destruction. In Christian terms, its ultimate pattern is the cross—where evil is neither denied nor imitated, but answered with costly mercy.
