While all the options listed are present in the elder brother’s reaction, the **deepest problem** is his **discomfort with mercy itself**, which is fueled by a **pride in obedience** and a **transactional view of relationship**.

Here is the breakdown of why this is the root issue, supported by the text:

**1. Anger and Envy are Symptoms**
The text begins by stating, "And he was angry" (v. 28). However, anger is the *reaction*, not the cause. Similarly, his envy ("thou never gavest me a kid") is a result of comparing his status to his brother's. These emotions are surface-level indicators of a deeper spiritual disconnect.

**2. Wounded Fairness is the Logic**
The brother operates on a strict economy of merit: *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee... neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment"* (v. 29). He views his relationship with the father as a contract. In his mind, obedience should purchase rewards (a kid for merrymaking). When the father shows grace to the unworthy brother, the brother feels his "fairness" has been violated. However, this sense of fairness is based on a false premise: that he is a hired hand rather than a son.

**3. Pride in Obedience is the Fuel**
The brother’s self-identity is built on his moral performance. He lists his virtues as a resume ("never transgressed"). This pride makes him feel superior to the prodigal ("this thy son," v. 30, rather than "my brother"). This pride is what makes the father's grace offensive to him; if the younger son is forgiven freely, the elder brother’s "hard work" loses its special value.

**4. Discomfort with Mercy is the Root**
Ultimately, the elder brother’s tragedy is that he is **lost inside the house**. He has access to the Father ("thou art ever with me," v. 31) and the inheritance ("all that I have is thine," v. 31), yet he refuses to participate in the Father's joy.
*   The Father invites him to the party of grace.
*   The brother refuses to go in.

His refusal to "make merry" (v. 32) reveals that he does not truly love the Father; he loves the *system* of merit he has built. He cannot celebrate the brother's restoration because to do so would require him to admit that the Father's love is not something he earned, but something he receives. Therefore, his deepest problem is an inability to accept **grace**—both for his brother and, ironically, for himself.

**Conclusion**
The elder brother represents a spiritual state often more dangerous than the younger brother's open rebellion: **religious self-righteousness**. His deepest problem is not simply that he is angry or feels treated unfairly, but that **his heart is hardened against the nature of mercy.** He believes he deserves love, whereas the Father offers love as a gift. As long as he demands fairness, he will never experience the joy of being a son.
