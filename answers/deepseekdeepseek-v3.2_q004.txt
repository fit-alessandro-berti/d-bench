The trust called for in Proverbs 3:5–6 is **relational and wholehearted**, rooted not in the rejection of intellect but in the recognition of divine supremacy. It asks for a shift in one’s fundamental orientation—from self-reliance to God-reliance.

**1. Humble Self-Distrust vs. Anti-Intellectualism**  
- **Humble Self-Distrust** means recognizing the limits of human understanding—our finite perspective, biases, and incomplete knowledge—while still valuing reason as a gift. It is an attitude that says, “I will use my mind diligently, but I will not treat it as the final authority.”
- **Anti-Intellectualism** rejects the use of intellect, reason, or study altogether. That is *not* what this passage advocates. Biblical wisdom literature (including Proverbs) consistently prizes knowledge, discernment, and learning—but always under the fear of the Lord (Proverbs 1:7).

**2. The Nature of the Trust**  
The trust described is:
- **Comprehensive** (“with all thine heart”) – not partial or conditional.
- **Active** (“in all thy ways acknowledge him”) – involving daily, practical dependence.
- **Relational** – based on knowing God’s character (faithful, wise, good).
- **Directional** – it results in guidance (“he shall direct thy paths”), not passivity.

**3. How This Works Practically**  
One can still study, plan, and think critically (“own understanding”), but without *leaning* on that alone as the ultimate support. Acknowledging God means inviting Him into every decision, seeking His wisdom through prayer and Scripture, and being willing to follow even when His direction contradicts initial human logic.

Thus, the passage calls for **faith that transcends, but does not negate, reason**—a trust that places God’s wisdom above our own, while still engaging the mind in service to that higher trust.
