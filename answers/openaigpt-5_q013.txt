Short answer: God isn’t asking for a better mix of songs and ethics; he’s rejecting worship that has become a lie. In Amos, the cult has turned into a cover for injustice, so the whole thing is odious to him until justice is restored.

Why the sharp rejection instead of “balance”:
- The issue isn’t imbalance but betrayal. In covenant terms, injustice is covenant-breaking; ritual alongside it is like bringing flowers to a spouse you’re actively betraying. The strong “I hate, I despise” is lawsuit language, not fine-tuning.
- Worship had become complicit with oppression. The shrines (Bethel, Gilgal) were thriving while courts were corrupt, the poor were crushed, and bribes ruled the gate (Amos 2:6–8; 5:7, 10–12). God rejects the entire package because the liturgy props up a violent social order.
- It confronts a superstition: that offerings and music can secure God’s favor while daily life denies his character. Yahweh won’t be “paid off” with sacrifices (cf. Ps 50; 1 Sam 15:22; Isa 1).

What fraud is being exposed:
- Performative piety: using religious observance to launder reputation—“praise-laundering”—while exploiting the vulnerable.
- Transactional religion: treating sacrifices as offsets for injustice, as if worship credits erase ethical debits.
- Mixed allegiance: invoking Yahweh’s name while trafficking in idols (Amos 5:26) and the values of other gods—power, wealth, impunity.
- Stolen gifts: presenting offerings likely funded by oppression (ivory houses, hewn-stone estates; Amos 3:15; 5:11), turning worship itself into the fruit of injustice.
- False security about the “day of the LORD” (Amos 5:18–20): expecting God to vindicate them because they are devout, when their deeds ensure judgment.

“Let justice roll down like waters and righteousness like an ever-flowing stream” names what God actually wants at the core (mishpat and tsedaqah). Justice isn’t an add-on to worship; it’s the riverbed in which true worship flows. Until that river runs, the festivals, offerings, and songs are noise.
