In Matthew 12:34–35, Jesus masterfully connects speech not as an isolated behavior, but as a direct overflow—and thus a diagnostic outlet—of what He calls the **"treasure"** of the heart. The passage suggests words function as all three: symptoms, choices, and revelations.

Here is how each category applies to this passage:

### 1. Words as **Symptoms**
The passage uses organic imagery to describe the relationship between the heart and the mouth. The phrase **"the mouth speaketh"** (Greek: *lalei*) indicates an inevitability—just as a fire produces smoke or a tree produces fruit, the mouth produces the contents of the heart.

*   **The Storage Model:** Jesus describes the heart as a "treasure" (*thesaurou*). In the ancient world, a treasury was a place where valuables were stored up. The speech is the symptom of the "stockpiles" already hidden within a person’s character.
*   **The Diagnosis:** By calling his listeners a "generation of vipers," Jesus identifies their speech (their accusations and blasphemy) as a symptom of a systemic internal corruption. You do not ask where the smoke is coming from once you find the fire; the fire (the heart) explains the smoke (the words).

### 2. Words as **Revelations**
The word "abundance" (*perisseumatos*) is the key here. It literally means an "overflow" or "super-abundance." This implies that the heart is a container that has reached its limit or is rich enough to spill over.

*   **What is Hidden Becomes Open:** The heart is the "hidden" part of a person, while the mouth is the "public" part. The passage suggests that words reveal the true, often hidden, interior landscape of soul.
*   **The Unavoidable Discovery:** If the heart is a warehouse, the mouth is the loading dock. You can perhaps guard your internal thoughts for a while, but eventually, what you have stored up will come out. In this view, speech is not the *cause* of the moral state, but the *proof* of it. It reveals whether the "treasure" is "good treasure" or "evil treasure."

### 3. Words as **Choices**
While the passage uses the language of inevitability ("bringeth forth"), the ethical force of the words assumes human accountability.

*   **The Agency of the "Bringer":** Jesus says a good man "**bringeth forth**" (Greek: *ekballei*) good things. The word *ekballei* implies an active effort or "thrusting out." While the source is internal, the act of speaking is a decision. 
*   **Identity and Output:** The passage links speech to the "man" himself—good or evil. The words are choices because they are the only way the hidden treasury can be accessed. An evil man *chooses* to open up his evil treasury; a good man *chooses* to access his good one.

### Conclusion
This passage suggests that **words are a tripartite mirror of the soul.**

1.  **As Symptoms:** They are the unavoidable evidence of an internal condition.
2.  **As Revelations:** They are the mechanism by which the "hidden" heart is made "visible" to the world.
3.  **As Choices:** They are the active, purposeful act of a person drawing from one of two available stocks to communicate with the world.

Ultimately, Jesus is saying that the mouth is the **heart’s translator**. It takes the "stockpile" of our character—our forgiveness, bitterness, love, or malice—and translates it into a language others can hear. You cannot separate the quality of the fruit from the root; the speech *is* the overflow of the man.
