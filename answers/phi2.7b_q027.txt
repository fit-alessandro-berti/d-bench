 
According to the text, the Pharisee's condemnation stems from his false presumption of being morally superior to the publican, despite evidence of the publican's more sincere spiritual endeavor. This is a form of comparison – the publican's humility, despite his less than ideal spiritual conduct, is seen as more authentic than the publican's pride. This is true humility is not about self-disgust or disgust for others, but a deep respect for the divinity within. The tax collector's posture, on the other hand, shows true humility as he genuinely looks for God, showing an attitude of submission and not superiority. 

The Pharisee uses his "righteousness and justice" as a virtue, which is seen as a form of self-display rather than an expression of faith. The tax collector, by comparison, practices humility by offering a prayer even when he isn't particularly blessed. His humility is not out of self-deprecation, but out of a deep respect for divine presence that he understands to be greater than his human failings. Despite not performing the same outward ritual, he genuinely seeks to communicate with God. The tax collector's posture is a spiritual truer, representing humbleness and respect rather than false, self-admiring conceit.
