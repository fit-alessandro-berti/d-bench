Galatians 6:1 requires a posture of **humble, restorative responsibility**.

Paul does not tell believers to ignore sin, nor does he authorize them to expose or punish a fallen person from a position of superiority. Those who are “spiritual”—that is, those walking by the Spirit (Gal. 5:16, 22–25)—are to act.

### 1. Correction aims at restoration, not condemnation

“Restore” (*katartizō*) can mean to mend, repair, set right, or put something back into proper condition. It was used, for example, of mending nets or setting a broken limb.

The person “overtaken in a fault” is not treated as disposable. The goal is his or her recovery: return to truth, obedience, fellowship, and spiritual health. Correction is therefore not an occasion to win an argument, display discernment, or establish one’s moral rank. It is an act of care directed toward healing.

### 2. Meekness governs the manner

The correction must be performed “in the spirit of meekness” or gentleness. Meekness is not indifference toward sin; it is strength submitted to God and expressed without cruelty, impatience, humiliation, or self-importance.

A harsh corrector may speak true words while acting contrary to the Spirit’s fruit. Since the aim is restoration, the manner matters: a person already caught in sin needs truth, but truth administered in a way fitted to repentance rather than despair or defensiveness.

### 3. Self-suspicion guards the corrector

“Considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted” keeps correction from becoming Pharisaic. The person correcting another must remember: *I am not beyond temptation. The same weakness, or another one, may overtake me.*

The temptation may include:

- falling into the same or a similar sin;
- becoming proud because one is correcting another;
- responding with anger, contempt, gossip, or harshness;
- imagining oneself secure apart from grace.

Thus correction must be accompanied by watchfulness. The spiritual person is not the person who feels above others, but the one who knows his or her dependence on the Spirit.

### Why these belong together

They form one moral posture:

- **Restoration** gives correction its purpose.
- **Meekness** gives correction its proper tone.
- **Self-suspicion** gives correction its humility.

Without restoration, correction becomes punishment.  
Without meekness, it becomes brutality.  
Without self-watchfulness, it becomes hypocrisy or pride.

Paul’s vision is of a community in which believers take sin seriously precisely because they take people—and their own vulnerability—seriously. The corrector approaches a fallen brother or sister not from above, but as another sinner sustained by grace, seeking to help restore what sin has damaged.
