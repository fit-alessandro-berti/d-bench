Matthew 12:34–35 connects speech to the hidden moral condition of the heart in a way that suggests **all three**: words are *symptoms* of what lies beneath, *choices* that reflect or reveal one’s inner state, and *revelations* of the true nature of the heart. Here’s how each dimension plays out:

### 1. **Words as Symptoms of Hidden Moral State**
The passage frames speech as a **visible manifestation** of what is already present in the heart. The "abundance of the heart" (the moral stockpile) determines what comes out of the mouth. If someone speaks "good things" (e.g., kindness, truth, or even neutral words), it implies their heart is *already* good—but the text doesn’t just say "good people speak well"; it says *the opposite*: evil people *bring forth* evil things. The words are a **sign** of what’s already there, like a feverish rash revealing a sickness. Jesus’ question—*"How can ye, being evil, speak good things?"*—assumes that if someone’s heart is truly evil, their words will reflect that, not the other way around.

### 2. **Words as Choices That Reflect Inner Truth**
The passage also treats speech as a **voluntary act** that mirrors the heart’s true nature. The "good man" and "evil man" are not just passive observers of their hearts; they *choose* what to bring forth. The "treasure" in the heart is not just a static condition but an active source of what they *decide* to express. This aligns with Jesus’ broader teaching in the Sermon on the Mount (e.g., Matt. 12:36–37), where words are tied to accountability: *"For by your words you will be justified, and by your words you will be condemned."* Here, the choice to speak "good" or "evil" is a reflection of the heart’s true state, not just a surface-level behavior.

### 3. **Words as Revelations of the Heart**
Finally, the passage treats speech as a **revelation** of the heart’s true condition. The "abundance" of the heart isn’t just a private inventory; it’s what *emerges* in speech. The text suggests that words are not arbitrary but *necessarily* reveal what’s already there. This is why Jesus’ question is so damning: if someone speaks "good things," it’s not because they’ve *chosen* to be good (though they may have), but because their heart *already* contains good. The words are the heart’s **unfiltered output**, exposing its true nature.

### **Key Takeaway**
The passage doesn’t just say words are *symptoms* (they are), nor just *choices* (they are), nor just *revelations* (they are). Instead, it presents speech as a **triple threat**:
- **Symptom**: Words show what’s already in the heart.
- **Choice**: The heart *chooses* what to express.
- **Revelation**: Words expose the heart’s true nature, whether good or evil.

This aligns with Jesus’ broader theme in the Gospels: the heart is the *source* of all moral action, and speech is one of the most transparent ways to see it. The "generation of vipers" isn’t just a group of sinners; it’s a group whose words *prove* they’re already evil, not just in their actions but in their *very language*.
