In these verses, Jesus utilizes the metaphor of a **"treasury" (*thesauros*)** to explain the mechanics of human behavior. By framing the heart as a storehouse, he shifts the focus from the act of speaking to the state of the speaker.

Here is an analysis of how this passage connects speech to hidden moral stockpiles, and why the answer to your question is that words operate as **all three: symptoms, choices, and revelations.**

---

### 1. The Heart as a "Moral Stockpile"
Jesus argues that the heart is not a passive vessel but a vault that accumulates value over time.
*   **The "Good Treasure":** Represents a lifetime of disciplined moral habits, reflections, and internal orientations toward truth and goodness. 
*   **The "Evil Treasure":** Represents the accumulation of bitterness, selfish ambition, and deceit.

He posits that we do not merely "happen" to say things; rather, we "bring forth" (*ekballō*—literally "to cast out" or "to eject") what has been stored up. Speech, therefore, is the *expenditure* of one’s internal currency.

---

### 2. Are words symptoms, choices, or revelations?

The passage suggests that speech functions as all three, operating in a hierarchy of depth:

#### A. Speech as a Symptom (The Diagnostic View)
In verse 34 ("out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh"), Jesus presents speech as an **involuntary diagnostic**. If a person produces poison, it is because poison is what is stored in the reservoir. Like a thermometer measuring a fever, the words are symptoms of a pre-existing condition. They prove that the moral stockpile is compromised before the words are even spoken.

#### B. Speech as a Revelation (The Disclosure View)
The word "abundance" (*perisseuma*) implies that the heart reaches a point of being so full that it inevitably overflows. In this sense, speech is an **unmasking**. 
*   **The Reveal:** A person may project piety or kindness in controlled circumstances, but Jesus suggests that the "overflow" of crisis or casual conversation acts as a truth-teller. 
*   **The Exposure:** By bringing forth these things, the speaker reveals their true nature to the world. A "generation of vipers" is identified not by their secret thoughts, but by the public "treasure" they cannot help but reveal through their speech.

#### C. Speech as a Choice (The Moral Responsibility View)
While the heart dictates the supply, the passage carries a strong undertone of moral accountability. By calling his audience an "evil generation," Jesus implies that they are responsible for the contents of their own treasury. 
*   **The Selection:** The phrasing "bringeth forth" implies agency. Because the stockpile is built over time through repeated choices, the *act of speaking* is the culmination of those choices. 
*   **The Accountability:** Jesus is not suggesting that people are "mindless puppets" of their hearts; he is suggesting that people are **curators of their own hearts.** If your speech is consistently evil, your "choice" was made long ago when you decided what to deposit into your internal treasury.

---

### Summary
The passage suggests an **inseparable link between inward character and outward expression.** 

*   **Words are symptoms** because they prove what is already inside. 
*   **Words are revelations** because they act as a "public audit" of one’s moral net worth. 
*   **Words are choices** because they are the final output of the internal habits and values one has spent a lifetime accumulating. 

Jesus effectively warns that you cannot truly "fake" your character for long; eventually, the vault will open, and the contents of your moral treasury will be forced into the light of day.
