Based on the text of Matthew 6:1 and the broader context of the Sermon on the Mount, the moral danger in this verse lies not in visibility itself, but in the **craving to be seen**. 

Here is a breakdown of why the craving is the problem, rather than the visibility:

**1. The Text Specifies the Motive**
The verse does not say, "Do not do your alms before men." It says, "Do not your alms before men, **to be seen of them**." The Greek phrase translated "to be seen" (*pros to theathēnai*) indicates purpose or intent. The warning is directed at the internal motivation of the heart. If the act is done specifically to gain an audience, it crosses the line into moral danger.

**2. Visibility is Not Inherently Sinful**
If visibility itself were the moral problem, it would contradict Jesus’ teaching just a few verses earlier in Matthew 5:16: *"Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."* 

In this earlier verse, Jesus actually commands that good works be seen. The difference is the *purpose* of the visibility. In Matthew 5:16, the visibility is meant to point people toward God. In Matthew 6:1, the visibility is meant to point people toward the self. Therefore, the act of doing good in public is not condemned; the ego-driven motive behind it is.

**3. The Danger of the "Closed Reward"**
Jesus explains the danger of craving human attention with a statement about reward: *"otherwise ye have no reward of your Father which is in heaven."* 

The moral danger is that seeking human praise creates a closed transaction. If you perform goodness to be seen by others, you receive your reward in the form of their admiration, applause, or social status. Because you have already accepted your "payment" from human approval, you forfeit the divine reward. The craving for human praise effectively cheapens the act, turning a spiritual virtue into a transaction for ego.

**4. The Corruption of Virtue**
Philosophically and theologically, the craving to be seen corrupts the nature of the good deed itself. When the primary goal is to be perceived as good, the focus shifts from the *recipient* of the alms (the poor person) to the *giver* (the self). The act becomes a performance rather than an act of genuine mercy or love. 

**Summary**
In Matthew 6:1, public goodness is morally dangerous only when it is driven by the craving to be seen. Visibility becomes a vice when it is used as a tool for self-glorification, because it replaces the desire to please God with the desire for human applause, thereby forfeiting any higher spiritual reward.
