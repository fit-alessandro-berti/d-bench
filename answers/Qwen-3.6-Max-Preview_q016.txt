This is a profound theological and psychological question. While all the options you listed (anger, envy, wounded fairness, pride, discomfort with mercy) are present in the text, most biblical scholars and theologians argue that these are **symptoms** of a deeper, root issue.

The elder brother’s deepest problem is **a transactional view of relationship with God (the Father), rooted in self-righteousness and a lack of understanding of grace.**

Here’s a breakdown of why this is the core issue, and how the other factors relate to it:

### 1. The Transactional Mindset (“I served… you never gave me”)
The elder brother says: *“Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment: and yet thou never gavest me a kid…”*

- He views his relationship with his father as a **contract**: obedience = reward.
- He believes he has *earned* favor through compliance.
- His anger stems from the belief that the father has broken the contract by rewarding someone who didn’t “earn” it.
- This reveals that he does not see himself as a **son** enjoying an inheritance, but as a **servant** working for wages.

### 2. Pride in Obedience as Identity
His identity is built on being the “good one.” When the younger brother returns, it threatens his sense of superiority and moral standing. If grace is given freely to the undeserving, then his own “goodness” loses its value as a currency for earning love or status.

### 3. Discomfort with Mercy (Because It Undermines Merit)
He is not just angry at the party; he is offended by the **principle** of unconditional forgiveness. To him, mercy feels like injustice because it bypasses the system of merit he has lived by. He cannot rejoice in his brother’s restoration because it exposes the emptiness of his own self-righteousness.

### 4. The Father’s Response Reveals the Truth
The father’s reply cuts to the heart of the matter:
> *“Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine.”*

- The father reminds him that he already possesses everything—not as a wage, but as an heir.
- The elder brother was **already rich**, but he lived like a poor servant, counting his duties and feeling deprived.
- His problem wasn’t that he lacked things; it was that he lacked **joy** and **relationship**. He was physically near the father but emotionally distant, trapped in a mindset of earning rather than receiving.

### Conclusion
While **anger** and **envy** are the visible emotions, and **wounded fairness** is the perceived grievance, the deepest problem is **spiritual blindness to grace**.

He fails to understand that:
- Love is not earned by obedience.
- Joy is found in relationship, not in moral superiority.
- The father’s heart is expansive enough to celebrate both the faithful son and the returned sinner without diminishing either.

In essence, the elder brother is **lost in the house**—just as the younger brother was lost in the far country. Both need the father’s pursuing love, though they resist it in different ways.
