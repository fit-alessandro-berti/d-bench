Genesis 4:6–7 presents a sophisticated and psychologically acute picture of moral agency. It moves beyond a simple binary of "good vs. evil" to describe the **internal dynamics of temptation, choice, and responsibility**.

Here is an analysis of how evil is portrayed and what responsibility is placed on Cain (and by extension, humanity):

### 1. The Picture of Moral Agency
The text portrays moral agency as **active, rational, and consequential**. God does not command Cain’s emotions directly but addresses his *response* to them. The passage assumes that:
- Humans have self-awareness (“Why art thou wroth?”).
- Humans have the capacity for self-reflection (“why is thy countenance fallen?”).
- Humans possess the freedom to choose between two paths (“If thou doest well… if thou doest not well…”).

Moral agency here is not passive; it is a moment of decision where the individual must exert willpower against internal inclinations.

### 2. How Evil Is Presented
Evil is depicted through several overlapping metaphors, each adding depth to the concept:

#### A. As a Crouching Beast or Predator
> *“Sin lieth at the door.”*

The Hebrew word for “lieth” (*rovetz*) often describes an animal crouching in wait, ready to pounce. This suggests that sin is:
- **External yet imminent**: It is waiting at the threshold of the heart/mind.
- **Predatory**: It seeks to devour or dominate.
- **Patient**: It waits for the right moment of vulnerability (anger, resentment).

This imagery aligns with the idea of evil as a **beast** or **predator**, emphasizing its dangerous, active nature.

#### B. As Temptation with Desire
> *“And unto thee shall be his desire…”*

The phrase “his desire” is ambiguous but profound. It can mean:
- Sin desires to control Cain.
- Or, echoing Genesis 3:16 (“thy desire shall be to thy husband”), it may suggest a twisted relational dynamic where sin seeks mastery over the person.

This presents evil as **temptation** that has an agenda—it wants to rule. It is not neutral; it is actively seeking dominion.

#### C. Not Merely Impulse or Habit
While anger (Cain’s emotion) might be seen as an impulse, the text distinguishes between the *emotion* and the *sin*. Anger itself is not condemned; rather, the failure to master it leads to sin. Thus, evil is not just an uncontrollable impulse but a **choice to yield** to that impulse. It is not yet a habit (since this is the first murder), but it is the *potential* for habitual domination if not checked.

### 3. Responsibility Placed on the Person
The text places **full responsibility** on Cain, emphasizing human capability and duty:

#### A. The Call to Self-Examination
God begins with questions: *“Why art thou wroth? and why is thy countenance fallen?”*
- This implies that Cain is responsible for understanding his own emotional state.
- He is expected to reflect on the root of his anger (jealousy over Abel’s accepted offering) rather than act out blindly.

#### B. The Power to Choose Well
> *“If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?”*

- God affirms that doing good is possible and leads to acceptance.
- This rejects fatalism. Cain is not doomed to fail; he has the agency to “do well.”

#### C. The Duty to Rule Over Sin
> *“…and thou shalt rule over him.”*

This is the core of the moral responsibility:
- **Dominion**: Just as humans were given dominion over creation (Genesis 1:28), they are called to exercise dominion over their own sinful inclinations.
- **Active Resistance**: Ruling over sin requires effort, vigilance, and will. It is not automatic; it is a task assigned to the moral agent.
- **Accountability**: If Cain fails to rule over sin, he is held accountable because he had the power and the warning to do so.

### Conclusion
Genesis 4:6–7 draws a picture of moral agency where:
- **Evil** is portrayed as a **crouching predator** (beast) and a **desiring tempter** that waits at the door of the heart.
- **Responsibility** lies with the individual to **recognize their emotional state**, **choose to do well**, and **actively rule over** the sinful impulse before it masters them.

The passage teaches that while temptation is inevitable and powerful, **mastery over it is both possible and required**. Failure to exercise this rule results in sin’s dominion—and ultimately, tragic consequences (as seen in Cain’s murder of Abel).
