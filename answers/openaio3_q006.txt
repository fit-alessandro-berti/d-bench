1. What “self” has to be denied?  
   • The Greek verb aparneomai (“deny”) is the word that is used for Peter’s denial of Jesus in the same Gospel (Mk 14:30-31, 72).  It means “to disown, to refuse any further connection with.”  
   • Jesus therefore calls the disciple to “disown” the inner claimant to the throne of one’s life—the autonomous, self-directing, self-protecting ego that tells us “my will, my reputation, my security first.”  
   • This is the same reality Paul later calls “the old person” (Eph 4:22; Col 3:9) or “the flesh” (Rom 8:7-8).  It is not one’s God-given individuality, creativity, or personality; it is the fallen, self-centred principle that insists on being its own lord.  
   • In v. 35 Jesus uses psychē (“life, self, soul”): the impulse to preserve and promote that old self at all costs.  The disciple must be prepared to let that false life be crucified so that the true life God intends can be received.

2. Why does Jesus add “take up his cross”?  
   • In Roman-occupied Galilee a cross meant one thing only: the public, shameful death reserved for rebels.  To “take up” a cross was to walk to one’s execution.  
   • Jesus therefore tells prospective followers up-front that loyalty to him may cost them everything—even literal life.  Christian discipleship is not an add-on to an already self-directed life; it is a transfer of ownership.

3. Why some hear liberation in these words  
   a. Freedom from the tyranny of the ego: People who have grown weary of compulsive self-promotion, comparison, guilt, or addiction find relief in handing the steering wheel to Someone wiser and kinder.  
   b. Discovery of the “true self”: Paradoxically, when the false, grasping self is crucified, the real creature God imagined comes alive (Gal 2:20).  Identity is received instead of constructed.  
   c. Release from fear of death: If the cross is already embraced, even physical death loses its power.  That is why martyrs could be both fearless and joyful.

4. Why others find the saying offensive  
   a. Collision with modern individualism: Western culture prizes expressive autonomy—“I must be free to define myself.”  Jesus demands the surrender of that very right.  
   b. Apparent assault on dignity: To many ears “deny yourself” sounds like self-hatred or repression, contradicting widespread therapeutic ideals of “self-esteem” and “self-actualisation.”  
   c. Threat to cherished loyalties: Career plans, political causes, or even family ties may have to yield to Jesus’ lordship.  That unconditional claim feels intolerable unless one has first come to trust him.

In short, Jesus is not asking people to erase their personalities but to renounce the idol of autonomous self-rule.  To those who see the bankruptcy of that idol, the invitation is pure freedom; to those still devoted to it, the same words are a stumbling block.  “Whoever loses his life for my sake and the gospel’s will save it.”
