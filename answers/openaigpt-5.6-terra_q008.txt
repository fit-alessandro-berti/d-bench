John treats love for the visible “brother” as a test of love for the invisible God because love for God is not merely an inward feeling or verbal claim. It necessarily takes visible, embodied form in one’s treatment of people God loves and commands us to love.

The logic is:

1. **God is unseen, so a claim to love him can be easily insulated from testing.**  
   Someone can say, “I love God,” while never having to demonstrate that claim in a costly, concrete way. Religious language, correct beliefs, emotional experiences, or private devotion can all be used to sustain such a claim.

2. **The brother is seen and encountered.**  
   A fellow human being—especially in 1 John, a fellow Christian—is present with needs, offenses, weaknesses, and claims upon us. Love there requires patience, mercy, generosity, forgiveness, and service. It cannot remain purely abstract.

3. **God’s command and character bind the two together.**  
   Verse 21 immediately says: “this commandment have we from him, That he who loveth God love his brother also.” To reject the brother in hatred is to reject the God who commands love and who has loved that brother. One cannot sincerely cherish God’s will while refusing it at the point where it becomes inconvenient.

4. **Love for God is meant to be reflected in love for those who belong to him.**  
   Earlier John says, “If God so loved us, we ought also to love one another” (4:11), and that God’s love is “perfected in us” as we love one another (4:12). Love of neighbor is therefore not a substitute for love of God; it is its visible fruit.

The self-deception exposed is the attempt to separate **devotion to God** from **conduct toward people**. A person may imagine that hostility, contempt, unforgiveness, indifference, or refusal to help a brother is compatible with genuine love for God. John says it is not. Such a person is called “a liar” not necessarily because every religious feeling is consciously fabricated, but because his profession contradicts the actual moral reality of his life.

John is not saying believers never feel irritation, conflict, or anger. His concern is with a settled posture of hatred—refusing love, fellowship, mercy, and obedience. Nor is he saying that love for others earns love for God. Rather, love for others is evidence that God’s love has truly been received and is at work in a person.

In short: the unseen love of God is authenticated in the seen love of the brother. If someone will not love the person placed before him, his claim to love the God he cannot see has no credible reality behind it.
