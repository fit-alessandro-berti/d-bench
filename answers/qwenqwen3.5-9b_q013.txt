Amos 5:21–24 is one of the most blistering indictments of the religious establishment in the Old Testament. To answer your question, I will break this down into two parts: first, why the rejection is sharp rather than corrective about "balance," and second, the specific moral and spiritual fraud being exposed.

### 1. Why is the rejection so sharp? (Ritual vs. Relationship)

The text does not just challenge the *behavior* of the people; it dismantles their *religious identity*. You ask why God rejects worship sharply instead of asking for better balance. The reason is that **the worship itself was the false foundation.**

*   **Worship as an "Offense":** The phrase "I hate, I despise" (Hebrew: *nada de’eina*) suggests more than disappointment; it implies moral revulsion. To God, these rituals weren't neutral requests for minor adjustment; they were *active offenses*. Why? Because they were being used as badges of righteousness while the rest of their lives contradicted that badge.
*   **The Illusion of Religious Safety:** The Northern Kingdom of Israel was crippled economically and morally. They were oppressing the poor, ignoring justice, and hoarding wealth. However, they were constant users of the temple system. They believed that the *rituals* guaranteed God's protection.
    *   **The Sharp Rejection:** God isn't asking for "sincere sacrifice while oppressing." He is tearing up the contract. He is saying: **"Your religion is not a safety net; it is a screen for your greed."**
*   **It’s a Category Error:** If you show up to a bank and don't deposit money, the bank might ask you to balance your ledger. But if you bring a pile of counterfeit bills to the counter and try to buy a house, they will confiscate the house and arrest you. The people of Israel were trying to purchase righteousness (merit, protection, peace) with counterfeit currency (sin). God is not asking them to balance; He is asking them to stop counterfeit.

### 2. The Moral and Spiritual Fraud

The passage exposes a specific type of fraud involving three main layers:

#### A. The Self-Deception of "Righteous" People
The fraud here is that the people were not considering themselves fraudsters. They were genuinely believing they were the best citizens of Israel.
*   **The Trap:** They equated religious observance with moral fiber. They were waking up early for dawn sacrifices (Ex 20:8) and singing songs (Hos 5:10).
*   **The Expose:** By offering "burnt offerings," they were trying to empty the *burdens* of their sin onto the priests and the altar. They were seeking a "get-out-of-jail-free" card for their sins of oppression.
*   **Amos's Point:** God does not accept the "membership fee" if you don't behave like the person it is for. He exposed the fraud that they had been baptizing themselves into holiness while living in uncleanness.

#### B. Ritualism as an Illusory Power
The "fraud" is the transactional fallacy: **"Give me the sacrifice, and I keep my hands clean of the suffering you ignore."**
*   Amos exposes the idea that religion is about "paying dues" to the Divine rather than "loving the Divine."
*   The fraud lies in the *separation of the holy and the profane*. The temple was holy, but the marketplace and the halls of government were profane. They thought the walls of the temple could contain their sins. Amos pulls the curtain apart and says, "God loves the things you hide, and the things you call 'thee.'"

#### C. The Mask of Obedience
This is the deepest level of the fraud. The crowd believed that *doing what was right* was a sacrifice.
*   **Wrong Mindset:** "I have followed the Law, I have fed the priests, I have sung the songs, therefore I deserve justice."
*   **The Fraud:** This assumes that *their standards* are what God approves.
*   **The Reality:** God's standard is that *Justice* is the defining mark of His character. If they are singing songs and scattering justice on the floor with their "mud of the heart," the sacrifice is an insult. It's like dressing in a suit for a funeral yet stealing from your neighbor's house for a better lifestyle—you cannot claim the funeral rites for a life stolen.

### 3. The Remedy: The "Water" Metaphor

The passage concludes with a unique metaphor: *"But let judgment run down as waters..."*

*   **Nature of the Fraud:** The fraud was that they thought God was "at the top" managing the heavens. Amos reminds them that God is the source of water—justice that is *natural* and *gravity-driven*. It doesn't need to be purchased or scheduled in the temple.
*   **The Solution:** "Righteousness" shouldn't be a "solemn assembly" gathered once a year. It should be a "mighty stream" that flows shouldn't be stagnant (like their religion is). Righteousness must be practical, constant, and relentless, soaking the soil (society) like water, rather than being a "sacred event."

### Summary

The passage rejects worship sharply because **the worship had become the mask for sin.** It exposes the fraud that people could maintain a public spiritual sphere (hymns, sacrifices) and a private sphere of organization (oppression, ignorance, poverty), while trying to keep the two from mixing.

It is a prophetic indictment of **hypocrisy.** God is not angry because they *had* rules; He is angry because they **had rules but no substance.** The forgiveness offered is not forgiveness of the *rituals*; it is forgiveness of the *hypocrisy*—but only once they abandon the rituals to embrace the truth: **"I hate you for your sacrifice of justice."**

*(The phrase "judgment run down as waters" implies that justice should not just happen once, but like rain, it must fall naturally and relentlessly over the land.)*
