The image is sharp because it is both **visually absurd and morally exact**.

- A *mote* is a tiny splinter or speck; a *beam* is a large piece of timber. Jesus exaggerates the contrast until the scene becomes comical: someone with a plank protruding from his own eye is attempting delicate eye surgery on someone else.
- The eye is an especially apt image for perception. The person with the beam is not merely guilty; he is **unable to see clearly**. His moral vision is distorted by his own unexamined fault.
- The proposed help is therefore dangerous. A person who cannot see because of his own beam may injure the brother he claims to be helping.
- The language of “thy brother” also makes the criticism relational. This is not detached moral analysis but a supposedly helpful intervention in the life of someone close to him.

The hypocrisy lies not simply in having a fault while noticing another’s fault. Everyone has faults. It lies in the **disproportion and pretense**: treating another’s small defect as urgent while ignoring, excusing, or concealing one’s own much greater problem. The critic assumes the position of a moral surgeon while refusing to undergo examination himself.

Yet Jesus does not forbid moral correction altogether. The sequence is important:

1. **“First cast out the beam”** — examine oneself honestly and repent.
2. **“Then shalt thou see clearly”** — self-correction restores moral vision and humility.
3. **“To cast out the mote”** — the purpose may still be to help the other person, not to condemn or humiliate him.

Thus the issue is not whether one ever points out wrong, but **from what posture and in what order**. Correction is legitimate when it arises from self-awareness, repentance, compassion, and a genuine desire for the other person’s good. It does not require perfect sinlessness; it requires that one not be hypocritically blind to one’s own serious failures. Jesus rejects condemning superiority, not loving and discerning moral care.
