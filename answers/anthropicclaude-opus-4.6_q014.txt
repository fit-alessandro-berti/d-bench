# Treasure and the Heart in Matthew 6:19–21

## The Critical Question of Direction

The natural reading — the one most people assume — is that Jesus is saying something **diagnostic**: look at where your treasure is, and you will discover where your heart already was. Treasure *reveals* the heart.

But read the passage again carefully. The verbs are **imperatives**. Jesus does not say "notice where your treasure happens to be." He says *lay up* treasure in one place rather than another. This is a command about **action first**, with the heart following. The logic runs:

> Place your treasure → your heart will go there

This means the relationship is not merely diagnostic but **formative**. Treasure does not just reveal the heart. Treasure *trains* the heart.

## Why This Distinction Matters Enormously

If treasure only reveals the heart, then the solution is an internal one: fix your desires first, and your spending and storing will naturally follow. But Jesus reverses this. He tells people to **move their treasure** and promises that the heart will follow. This is a striking claim about how human beings actually work.

It suggests that we do not first love something and then invest in it. Or rather, that this is not the *only* direction of causation. We also invest in something and then come to love it. The act of storing, guarding, and attending to a thing **generates** attachment to it.

## The Threefold Binding: Possession, Attention, and Love

The passage quietly maps how these three things become entangled.

**Possession** comes first in the text. You place something in a location — you store it, you accumulate it. This is a concrete, physical act. You have grain in a barn, gold in a box, clothing in a chest.

**Attention** follows necessarily. Whatever you possess, you must watch over. The passage itself names the threats — moth, rust, thieves — and these are not abstract. They require vigilance. The person with earthly treasure must *think about* earthly treasure. The mind orbits what it is responsible for. You check the locks. You worry about decay. Your awareness is pulled, again and again, toward the thing you are trying to preserve.

**Love** is the final consequence, and this is what Jesus means by "there will your heart be also." The heart here is not mere emotion. It is the deep orientation of the self — what you care about most fundamentally, what organizes your life, what you would grieve to lose. And Jesus is saying that this orientation is *produced* by the first two steps. You possess, therefore you attend, therefore you love. The chain binds.

## The Uncomfortable Implication

This means that the relationship between a person and their possessions is not neutral. You cannot "just have things" without those things shaping what you love. The mere act of accumulation begins a process that ends in the heart being captured. Jesus does not say this *might* happen. He states it as a law: **where your treasure is, there your heart will be.**

This also works in the positive direction. Lay up treasure in heaven — give, serve, sacrifice, orient your resources toward God's purposes — and your heart will be drawn upward. Not because you were already perfectly holy, but because you *placed your treasure there*, and your attention followed, and your love followed your attention.

## What This Says About Human Nature

Jesus is making a claim that most people would prefer not to be true: that we are not sovereign over our own loves. We do not stand above our choices, coolly directing our affections. Instead, our choices about concrete, material things — where we store, what we guard, what we accumulate — reach back and reshape us. We become what we invest in.

The passage is therefore both a warning and a strange mercy. The warning is that earthly accumulation will, with mechanical certainty, produce earthly hearts. The mercy is that you do not need to wait until your heart is pure to begin. You can **act first**. Move the treasure. The heart will follow.
