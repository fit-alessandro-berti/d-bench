The passage from Philippians 2 is one of the most profound theological paradoxes in the Christian canon, often called the *Christ Hymn*. It describes the *kenosis*—the self-emptying—of Christ. 

To understand what kind of power is praised here, and why humility joined to greatness unsettles us, we must look closely at the inversion of worldly power dynamics.

### 1. What Kind of Power Is Being Praised?

The power praised here is **kenotic power**—the power of voluntary self-emptying, radical servitude, and sacrificial descent. It operates entirely contrary to the human conception of power, which is about accumulation, dominance, and self-preservation. 

This specific power has three distinct characteristics:

*   **The Power of Refrained Omnipotence:** Verse 6 states that Christ did not consider equality with God something to be "grasped" (or clung to). The power praised is the extraordinary restraint of power. It takes immense strength *not* to use strength. Christ’s refusal to cling to his divine status praises the sovereignty over one's own ego and prerogatives. 
*   **The Power of Descent:** The trajectory of this power is downward—from the "form of God" to the "form of a servant," and finally to "even the death of the cross." In the ancient world, power was ascending (rising in rank, status, and honor). Power was about elevating yourself above others. Here, power is praised for its capacity to lower itself. 
*   **The Power of Vulnerability and Obedience:** Crucifixion was the ultimate spectacle of domination—public torture designed to shame and destroy the victim. Yet, Christ’s obedience to this fate transforms the meaning of the cross. The power praised is the willingness to absorb the worst of human evil without retaliating, to remain faithful to a mission even when it requires total vulnerability.

Ultimately, this is the power of **love**. It is a power that conquers not by crushing the opponent, but by dismantling the hostility through self-giving. 

***

### 2. Why Does Humility Joined to Greatness Feel More Unsettling Than Open Domination?

It is entirely natural to understand and cope with open domination. Tyrants create a predictable world: the strong dominate, the weak submit, and if you want to survive, you learn the rules of the hierarchy. It is a hard, brittle world, but it is clear. 

However, when true greatness is united with profound humility, it feels deeply unsettling—even terrifying—for several profound reasons:

**It Robbs Us of the Ability to Rebel**
You can rebel against a tyrant. You can plot against a master who rules by the sword. The ego loves a struggle because it justifies its own resistance. But what do you do when the master washes your feet? When the one with absolute power uses that power to serve you, you have no enemy to fight against. The natural response of the ego is resistance, and humility joined to greatness leaves the ego with no target. The only way to rebel against love is to become hateful or callous, which exposes the rebel's own brokenness rather than the master's tyranny. 

**It Strips Away Our Moral Excuses**
Under open domination, the oppressed can justify their bitterness, their cynicism, and their own selfishness. The system is rigged, so why try to be good? But when someone of immense greatness comes to you in absolute humility, it eliminates your excuse for your own pride. The humble master exposes the absurdity of our self-justification. We are forced to confront our own hardness of heart, which is a deeply uncomfortable mirror to hold up.

**It Demands Total Vulnerability and Reciprocity**
Domination is non-negotiable; it demands your submission, but it does not demand your heart. You can bow your head to a tyrant while secretly plotting in your mind. Humility joined to greatness, however, demands more than obedience—it demands your trust. When someone of immense power becomes vulnerable before you, they are essentially saying, "I am handing you the power to hurt me." To accept this invitation, you must drop your own defenses, which requires a terrifying level of trust and reciprocal vulnerability.

**It Breaks the Logic of the World**
Human survival logic dictates that if you have power, you use it to protect yourself and secure your own interests. To voluntarily give up power for the sake of others feels like a violation of the natural order. As the Indian poet and philosopher Rabindranath Tagore wrote: *"We have grown fearful of the idea of the sacred... because we cannot bear the sight of the infinite wearing the robes of the finite."* When the infinite dresses itself in the finite, when absolute power reduces itself to absolute weakness, the human mind recoils from the sheer impossibility of it. It feels unsafe because it is unpredictable by worldly standards.

**Conclusion**
Open domination enslaves the body; humility joined to greatness enslaves the *ego*. Tyranny can be endured because we have seen it before and know how to keep our inner selves intact. But the radical, self-giving humility of true greatness attacks our defenses at their core. It does not just ask us to submit; it asks us to fundamentally change how we understand power, greatness, and the very meaning of life—from climbing to descend.
