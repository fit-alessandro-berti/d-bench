Pride is linked to destruction not only because it leads to doing wrong, but because it warps perception and self-government—so that you stop seeing reality clearly and can’t govern yourself wisely. In the moral psychology behind Proverbs:

- **Pride produces blindness.** A “haughty spirit” tends to treat its own judgment as final and others as inferior or irrelevant. That makes a person less able to read warning signs, less likely to learn from correction, and more prone to misjudge risk. The fall comes partly because arrogance disables accurate perception.

- **Pride also produces collapse.** The proverb’s sequence (“before destruction… before a fall”) suggests pride isn’t just an internal feeling that later triggers guilt; it becomes a destabilizing force. When you overestimate yourself and overconfidently “divide the spoil,” you set yourself up for consequences you can’t anticipate or withstand.

- **The contrast with “the lowly” reveals the social-moral psychology of arrogance.** “The lowly” are not merely victims of oppression; they represent a stance of receptivity—humility that can coexist with others, listen, and submit to truth. The arrogant “divide the spoil” with the proud, implying that arrogance doesn’t just isolate a person from God or conscience; it also reshapes relationships into competition and injustice.

So the text suggests that arrogance is morally self-destructive because it both **misreads reality (blindness)** and **breaks the conditions for stable moral living (collapse)**. Humility, by contrast, aligns one with truth and with others—making fall less likely and community and justice more possible.
