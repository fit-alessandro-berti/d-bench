Proverbs 3:5–6 calls for **wholehearted reliance on God as the ultimate source of wisdom and direction**, especially when one’s own judgment is partial, self-serving, or unable to see the outcome.

It is not a command to stop thinking.

### 1. “Trust” means reliance, not mere agreement

The word translated “trust” carries the sense of placing one’s confidence or security in someone. The call is not simply, “Believe that God exists,” but: **make God the one on whom you finally depend when deciding how to live.**

“To trust … with all your heart” means not reserving an area of life in which one says, “God may speak about religion, but my ambitions, money, relationships, ethics, and plans are mine alone to govern.”

### 2. “Lean not unto thine own understanding” is humble self-distrust

“Lean” evokes putting one’s weight on a support. Proverbs warns against treating one’s own insight as a sufficient foundation.

That is humble self-distrust because human understanding is:

- **limited** — we do not see all consequences or know the future;
- **morally vulnerable** — we easily rationalize what we already want;
- **socially shaped** — conventional wisdom may oppose God’s ways;
- **creaturely** — our perspective is not God’s perspective.

The point is not, “Your understanding is worthless.” It is, “Your understanding is not ultimate.” We may use reason, experience, counsel, and careful planning—but we must hold them under God’s wisdom rather than making them supreme.

### 3. It is not anti-intellectualism

Proverbs itself is a book devoted to gaining wisdom, instruction, discernment, prudence, knowledge, and sound judgment. It repeatedly urges listeners to seek understanding:

> “Get wisdom: and with all thy getting get understanding” (Proverbs 4:7).

So Proverbs 3 cannot mean, “Do not think,” “Ignore evidence,” “Reject learning,” or “Treat faith as irrational.” Anti-intellectualism says that thought, study, or expertise is opposed to faith. Proverbs says that genuine wisdom begins with reverence for the Lord and is exercised through thoughtful, morally formed judgment.

The contrast is therefore not:

- **faith versus reason**,  

but:

- **self-sufficient reason versus God-governed wisdom**.

### 4. “In all thy ways acknowledge him”

“To acknowledge” God means more than mentioning him or asking for a blessing on plans already settled. It means to know, recognize, and take account of him in every path of life.

One acknowledges God by asking:

- What does God command here?
- What kind of person is this choice forming me into?
- Does this fit God’s character—his justice, truth, mercy, and holiness?
- Have I sought wise counsel and honestly faced facts?
- Am I following God’s way, or merely baptizing my preference?

This is an active and thoughtful posture, not passivity.

### 5. “He shall direct thy paths”

The promise is that God will “make straight” one’s paths: he will guide, clear obstacles, correct direction, and lead the faithful toward the way of life. It does not necessarily promise a painless, obvious, or immediately successful route. In Proverbs, the “straight path” is especially the path of righteous and wise living.

So the trust requested is neither blind credulity nor intellectual surrender. It is **a disciplined refusal to make one’s own judgment the final court of appeal**. It uses the mind gratefully, diligently, and responsibly—but submits the mind, along with every other part of life, to the wisdom and rule of God.
