# The Logic of Tested Love

## Why the Visible Matters

The verse sets up a logical hierarchy: **the harder test reveals the truth about the easier one**.

It's easier to claim love for an abstract, distant God than for the actual person irritating you this morning. So proximity becomes an **acid test**—love of the neighbor you *see* cannot be faked as easily as devotion to one you don't.

This inverts what we might expect: the visible seems lesser, yet it's treated as the more reliable indicator.

## The Self-Deception Exposed

The verse identifies a specific lie people tell themselves:

**"I love God, but..." (and then the list of people they avoid, dismiss, resent, or refuse to serve)**

This exposes several interconnected deceptions:

- **Confusing sentiment with action** — calling an invisible feeling "love" while treating visible people harshly
- **Selective obedience** — obeying the "hard" command (love God) while ignoring the "easier" one (love your neighbor)
- **Pharisaic piety** — maintaining inner devotion while neglecting concrete responsibility
- **The displacement theory** — imagining you can skip the difficult human work and jump to the spiritual experience

## The Deeper Problem

The verse suggests you *cannot* actually know what your love of God is made of until you see how you treat people. Love of God becomes real—or is exposed as hollow—through its social expression.

The self-deception isn't primarily intellectual but **moral**: it's the lie we tell ourselves to avoid the demands love actually makes.
