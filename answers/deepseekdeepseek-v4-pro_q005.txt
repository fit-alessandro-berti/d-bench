The command to love one’s enemies is so radical that it can slide off our minds like water off oil. We might nod at its beauty but then file it away as a poetic exaggeration. To take it seriously is to feel the full weight of its impossibility. Here’s why that weight is real, and how the command transcends both sentimentality and mere strategy, pointing to something far deeper.

---

### Why it’s so difficult to take seriously

**1. It contradicts every natural instinct of justice and self-preservation.**  
We are wired to protect ourselves, our loved ones, and our communities. When someone curses, hates, or persecutes us, our internal alarm systems scream for defense, withdrawal, or retaliation. Loving an enemy can feel like a betrayal of our own dignity, or even a collaboration with evil. We suspect it means letting the oppressor win.

**2. We confuse love with emotion.**  
We imagine that “loving” an enemy means we must feel warm affection for them. Since we *can’t* conjure up that feeling on command, the whole enterprise seems insincere or impossible. We mistake a command of active will for a demand of spontaneous sentiment, and so we dismiss it.

**3. It appears to undermine justice.**  
If we love those who do evil, are we not condoning the evil? The fear is that enemy-love means abandoning the language of right and wrong, turning a blind eye to abuse, or becoming a doormat. In a world that rightly values accountability, this command can look like moral cowardice.

**4. It’s been tamed by familiarity.**  
The phrase “love your enemies” has been domesticated by centuries of repetition. We’ve heard it so often that it’s become a piece of cultural background music, stripped of its original shock. Jesus spoke these words into a context of Roman occupation and deep social enmity. To his first hearers, it would have been an almost offensive, world-upending demand. We lose that rawness and so lose its seriousness.

Given these obstacles, we often try to make the command more manageable. We typically do so in one of two ways, which correspond to your categories: the sentimental and the strategic.

---

### The sentimental reduction

This is the Hallmark-card version of enemy-love: a soft-focus, passive niceness that avoids conflict at all costs. It says, “Just be kind, always smile, and don’t make waves.”  

The problem is that sentimental love ignores the reality of evil. It treats an enemy not as a real threat but as a misunderstood friend whom a little more sweetness will win over. It often demands no change, no truth-telling, no justice. Sentimental love is inoffensive precisely because it doesn’t take the enmity seriously. It’s a fake peace that papers over wounds without healing them. That’s not what Jesus describes; he speaks of people who curse, hate, despitefully use, and persecute—real, active harm. Responding to that with mere sentiment is not love; it’s an escape from love’s cost.

---

### The strategic reduction

Some interpret enemy-love as a clever social strategy: a form of nonviolent manipulation or moral jiu-jitsu. The idea is that by doing good to your persecutor, you “heap burning coals on their head” (Romans 12:20), shame them publicly, or break their will through moral pressure. This approach can be persuasive because it *works*—many nonviolent movements have leveraged the power of enemy-love to expose injustice and change structures.  

But if the ultimate goal is merely to win, to shame, or to manipulate the enemy into a different outward behavior, then “love” is just a tactic. It becomes a disguised form of power. The New Testament doesn’t present enemy-love as a way to get results, even good results. Its horizon is not pragmatic success but the character of God himself: “That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven.” The strategic reading can make the command “serious” in terms of effectiveness, but it still falls short of its deepest ground.

---

### The deeper form of moral strength

Jesus grounds enemy-love entirely in the nature of God: “He maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust.” God’s impartial, life-sustaining generosity is the pattern. The Father doesn’t withhold sunlight until the evil have repented. He gives them life, time, and blessing, not as a reward but as a revelation of who He is. To love your enemy, then, is not to pretend the enemy isn’t an enemy, nor to use a clever tactic to win, but to *imitate the divine character*—to be a visible child of such a Father.

This is a form of moral strength so deep that it can only be called a participation in the life of God. It has several distinctive marks:

- **It is an act of the will, not a feeling.** The love commanded is *agape*—a volitional, active commitment to seek the other’s true good, even at cost to oneself. You can pray for someone you don’t like. You can do good to someone while your heart is still aching or angry. The action, not the emotion, is the raw material of the miracle.

- **It absorbs evil without replicating it.** Real enemy-love refuses to become the mirror image of the oppressor. By blessing instead of cursing, praying instead of plotting, the lover breaks the cycle of hatred. This is not passivity; it is a more intense kind of action—one that refuses to let the enemy dictate the terms of the relationship. It is the strength to remain an agent of good when every instinct screams for revenge.

- **It seeks the enemy’s redemption, not their humiliation.** Prayer for the persecutor immediately reorients the relationship. You cannot genuinely pray for someone’s well-being and simultaneously wish for their destruction. Enemy-love thus holds together two things that the natural mind separates: a firm opposition to evil, and a relentless desire for the evildoer’s ultimate good. This is why Jesus can command love even while speaking plainly about judgment. Love doesn’t ignore sin; it conquers it from within.

- **It trusts God’s justice, so it can relinquish personal vengeance.** Only when you believe that there is a just Judge who sees all things can you afford not to take vengeance into your own hands. Enemy-love is not a declaration that wrongs don’t matter; it is an entrusting of final justice to God, freeing you to act redemptively in the present.

- **It is impossible without a transformed heart.** Jesus doesn’t present this as a “tip for better living.” He presents it as the family trait of the children of God. It requires a new birth, a new source of life. The moral strength here is not the stoic heroism of self-reliance but the receptive strength of a branch drawing life from a Vine. The command is so serious precisely because it reveals our absolute dependence on a love that is not our own.

---

### Serious as grace

So, is enemy-love sentimental? No, because it faces the full reality of hatred and evil without flinching. Is it merely strategic? No, because its ultimate aim is not worldly success but conformity to the Father’s image. It is a deeper form of moral strength—but not the strength of gritted teeth and heroic willpower. It is the strength of a life re-rooted in God, able to love with a love that the world can neither give nor take away.

That is why it’s so hard to take seriously. To take it seriously is to admit that we cannot do it on our own, and that the whole structure of our natural self—our pride, our fear, our sense of justice—must be dismantled and rebuilt on the foundation of grace. It’s not a comfortable teaching; it’s a doorway into a different kind of life. And once you see that, the command stops being a platitude and becomes the most serious, exhilarating, and terrifying invitation in the world.
