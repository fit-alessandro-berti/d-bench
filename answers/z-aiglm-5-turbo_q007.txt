To read Luke 6:37 as a mandate for moral relativism—that is, the idea that moral judgments never matter and "anything goes"—is to misunderstand both the immediate context of Jesus’s teachings and the nature of human psychology. 

Jesus was not abolishing the concepts of right and wrong; in fact, just a few verses later, He talks about recognizing good and bad "fruit" in people's lives (Luke 6:43-45). Rather, He was drawing a razor-sharp line between **discernment** and the **appetite to condemn**.

Here is an exploration of the profound difference between the two.

### 1. The Nature of Discernment: Seeing to Heal
Discernment is the act of perceiving reality clearly. It is moral vision. A doctor must discern the difference between healthy tissue and a tumor to treat a patient. Similarly, a person must discern between right and wrong, truth and falsehood, safety and danger, to navigate life.

Discernment:
*   **Is concerned with truth, not superiority:** When you discern a wrong action, you are simply acknowledging what is. You do not feel elevated by your realization.
*   **Leads to restoration:** In the biblical context, discernment is meant to lead to help. If you discern that a friend is destroying themselves through addiction or deceit, your discernment is the first step toward intervening to save them (as outlined in Galatians 6:1).
*   **Requires self-awareness:** True discernment humbles you, because in recognizing a flaw in someone else, you are simultaneously reminded of your own vulnerabilities. 

Jesus actually commands discernment elsewhere, saying, "Do not judge by appearances, but judge with right judgment" (John 7:24). Therefore, Luke 6:37 cannot mean "never make a moral assessment." 

### 2. The Appetite to Condemn: Using to Destroy
If discernment is about seeing clearly, the "appetite to condemn" is about weaponizing what you see. The word *appetite* is crucial here. Condemnation is not just a mental conclusion; it is a visceral craving of the ego. 

The appetite to condemn:
*   **Is concerned with power, not truth:** When we condemn, we are often trying to position ourselves above the other person. It is a way of saying, "I am not like *that* sinner." It feeds our pride.
*   **Leads to destruction, not restoration:** Condemnation doesn't want to fix the person; it wants to banish, punish, or cancel them. It seeks the death of the sinner, not the death of the sin.
*   **Is blind to itself:** Jesus immediately follows this verse with the famous metaphor of the speck and the log (Luke 6:41-42). The person with an appetite to condemn is so fixated on the speck in their brother's eye that they are entirely blind to the wooden plank in their own. 

To condemn someone is to attempt to play God. It is to look at a fellow flawed human being and declare them irredeemable, worthless, or permanently defined by their worst moment. 

### 3. How Luke 6:37 Preserves Morality While Killing Condemnation
The genius of Luke 6:37 is that it does not say, "Ignore wrong behavior." It says, "Do not condemn." 

Jesus provides the alternative to condemnation in the very same breath: **"Forgive."** 
You cannot forgive someone unless you have first discerned that they have done something wrong. If morality didn't matter, there would be nothing to forgive! Forgiveness is a moral action that requires a moral judgment, but it totally rejects the appetite to condemn. 

Think of the difference like this:
*   **Moral Relativism:** "What you did isn't wrong, so I don't care." (No judgment, no forgiveness).
*   **The Appetite to Condemn:** "What you did is wrong, therefore you are garbage and I cast you out." (Judgment without mercy).
*   **The Way of Christ:** "What you did is wrong, it caused real damage, but I will not condemn you. I forgive you, and I stand with you to help you walk out of it." (Discernment accompanied by grace).

### Summary
Saying "moral judgments never matter" leads to a chaotic, loveless society where no one is protected from harm and no one is called to grow. 

Jesus is not banning moral gravity; He is banning the gavel. He is telling us that our role in the world is not to be judges who hand down sentences, but neighbors who bring grace. We must have the courage to discern right from wrong, but we must ruthlessly starve the appetite that wants to use those wrongs to destroy others, remembering that we ourselves live entirely by the mercy of God.
