This passage from Philippians 2:5–8 is a profound meditation on the nature of true power. The kind of power being praised here is **self-emptying, servant leadership—the power to choose humility, obedience, and self-sacrifice rather than domination and self-aggrandizement**.

Let’s break it down:

#### 1. **What kind of power is being praised?**

The passage describes Jesus, who existed “in the form of God,” meaning he was fully divine and possessed ultimate power. Yet, instead of insisting on his equality with God or using his power for self-glorification, he **chooses to empty himself, taking on the form of a servant and becoming like a human**. He humbles himself to the point of accepting death on a cross.

So the power praised here is not the power to command or rule over others, but the **power to relinquish power for the sake of others**. It’s the power of humility, compassion, and self-giving love. It’s the strength to choose weakness, the courage to serve, and the willingness to suffer for others.

#### 2. **Why can humility joined to greatness feel more unsettling than open domination?**

Open domination is straightforward. When someone wields power openly, everyone knows where they stand. Domination is loud, obvious, and easy to understand—even if it’s frightening or oppressive.

But **humility joined to greatness is unsettling because it inverts our expectations**. We expect greatness to come with pride, entitlement, and control. When someone with ultimate power instead chooses humility, self-abnegation, and service, it destabilizes the usual social order. It makes us uncomfortable because:

- **It challenges our understanding of power.** We’re used to power meaning “getting ahead,” but here power means “lifting others up.”
- **It requires us to respond differently.** With open domination, we can react with fear or submission. But with humility in greatness, we’re called to respond with trust, vulnerability, and perhaps even transformation in our own lives.
- **It exposes our own pride and insecurity.** When someone with power humbles themselves, it lays bare our own tendency to seek status and recognition. It’s a mirror that can be uncomfortable to look into.

In short, the unsettling quality comes from the **paradox**: true greatness is not about exalting oneself, but about emptying oneself for others. This kind of power is deeper and more radical, and it calls us to a higher standard of living and being.

#### **In summary:**
The passage praises the power of self-emptying humility—a power that is exercised not by dominating others but by serving and sacrificing for them. This is more unsettling than domination because it turns our understanding of power on its head, calling us to humility and self-giving love rather than self-assertion and control.
