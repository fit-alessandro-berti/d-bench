In **Galatians 6:1**, the apostle Paul gives specific guidance on how Christians should handle the moral failings of fellow believers. The “moral posture” required here is **humble, gentle restoration** — not condemnation, superiority, or harsh judgment.  

Let’s break down the key elements and their interconnection:

---

### **1. Restoration (not punishment or disregard)**
The goal is **restoration** (Greek: *katartizō*), a term used for mending nets (Matt 4:21), setting broken bones, or reconciling people.  
This implies:
- The person’s spiritual/moral health is the priority, not their humiliation.
- Correction is meant to heal and return them to fellowship and righteousness, not to exclude or shame them.

---

### **2. Meekness (“in the spirit of meekness”)**
Meekness (Greek: *prautēs*) is strength under control — gentleness, humility, patience.  
Why?
- The one who fell could be any of us; spiritual pride ruins correction.
- Meekness makes the correction receivable rather than defensive.
- It reflects Christ’s character (Matt 11:29; 2 Cor 10:1).

---

### **3. Self-suspicion (“considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted”)**
Paul warns the restorer to be aware of their own vulnerability.  
This means:
- Recognizing that the same weaknesses or different ones could overtake you.
- Guarding against spiritual arrogance (“I would never fall like that”).
- The act of correcting can become a temptation to pride, judgmentalism, or even falling into the same sin.

---

### **Why kept together?**
These three elements form an integrated approach because **true spiritual correction is impossible if any is missing**:

- **Without restoration** → Correction becomes destructive, focused on fault-finding.
- **Without meekness** → Correction becomes arrogant, provoking resistance.
- **Without self-suspicion** → The corrector elevates themselves above the fallen, ignoring their own sinfulness and opening themselves to fall or to abuse spiritual authority.

Paul is teaching that **how** we correct reveals whether we are “spiritual” (Gal 6:1a) or fleshly. Those led by the Spirit (Gal 5:22–25) will handle others’ faults with love, humility, and fear of God, not with a sense of moral superiority.

---

### **Key principle:**
The community’s health depends on dealing with sin in a way that heals the sinner, protects the restorer from pride, and upholds the law of Christ — which is to “bear one another’s burdens” (Gal 6:2). This reflects the gospel itself: we are all sinners restored by grace, so we restore others graciously.
