**Answer:**

The trust being asked for in Proverbs 3:5‑6 is **a wholehearted, relational trust in God**—a reliance that engages the whole person (heart, mind, and will) and acknowledges God’s sovereign authority over every aspect of life.  

| Aspect | What the verse calls for | How it differs from “humble self‑distrust” | How it differs from “mere anti‑intellectualism” |
|--------|--------------------------|--------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------|
| **Scope of trust** | **Complete reliance** on God in *all* areas of life (relationships, work, decisions, morals). | Humble self‑distrust is a *negative* attitude toward one’s own abilities; it stops short of actively trusting God. Trust in Proverbs is *positive*—it means placing confidence in God while still using the capacities He has given us. | Anti‑intellectualism rejects reason or study altogether. Proverbs 5‑6 does **not** dismiss intellect; it says “lean not unto thine own understanding” *only* when that understanding is **self‑reliant** and **independent of God**. It encourages using reason, but always *under* God’s guidance. |
| **Attitude toward self** | Recognizes human limitation (“all thine heart”) and therefore **humility**. | Humble self‑distrust can become a **self‑defeating** stance that denies any contribution of the mind or effort. The biblical trust says, “I am limited, so I turn to God, but I still act responsibly.” | Anti‑intellectualism often **exalts feeling or tradition** over reasoned inquiry, dismissing the mind’s role. Proverbs invites *acknowledgment* of God *in* our thinking, not a rejection of thinking. |
| **Use of intellect** | “In all thy ways acknowledge him” → **integrate** God’s wisdom with human reasoning. | Humble self‑distrust may lead to **passivity** (e.g., “I can’t think, so I’ll just wait”). The verse calls for *active* engagement: we are to *acknowledge* God in every decision, which includes thoughtful consideration. | Anti‑intellectualism says “don’t think at all” or “don’t study,” which contradicts the biblical call to *use* understanding while submitting it to God. |
| **Outcome** | God “shall direct thy paths,” i.e., **guidance, peace, and right direction**. | Humble self‑distrust may leave one **stuck** or **paralyzed**; it does not guarantee divine direction. | Anti‑intellectualism may produce **erroneous or uninformed choices** because it neglects the God‑given capacity for discernment. |

### Key Distinctions

1. **Humble Self‑Distrust**  
   - *Definition*: Recognizing one’s limitations and refraining from relying on one’s own strength.  
   - *Positive aspect*: Encourages humility and openness to God.  
   - *Negative aspect*: Can become a **self‑imposed ceiling** that prevents us from using the gifts (including intellect) God has given us.

2. **Mere Anti‑Intellectualism**  
   - *Definition*: Rejecting reason, study, or critical thinking as inherently flawed or unnecessary.  
   - *Why it’s insufficient*: Faith is not a substitute for thoughtful engagement; Scripture repeatedly calls believers to “think” (e.g., Romans 12:2, 1 Peter 1:13). Trusting God does not mean abandoning the mind He gave us.

### Summary

The trust Proverbs 3:5‑6 asks for is **a wholehearted, humble reliance on God** that **recognizes our limits** but **still engages our intellect** under God’s authority. It is *not* a call to abandon reason (humble self‑distrust) nor a call to reject thoughtful inquiry (anti‑intellectualism). Rather, it invites us to **acknowledge God in every decision**, using the mind He gave us while keeping Him at the center of our trust.
