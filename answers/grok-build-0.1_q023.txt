Repentance in Isaiah 55:6–7 is deliberately described as involving both the **way** and the **thoughts** because biblical repentance is not merely behavioral reform but a total reorientation of the person—outward conduct and inward life together.

### The Meaning of the Two Terms

- **“His way”** (*derek* in Hebrew) refers to a person’s path or manner of life—their habitual conduct, choices, relationships, and outward pattern of living. It is the visible trajectory of someone’s existence.
- **“His thoughts”** (*machashavot*) refers to the inner workings of the mind: plans, imaginations, intentions, and the reasoning processes that produce and justify behavior. This is not just “random thoughts” but the deliberate mental and emotional life that shapes a person.

By naming both, the text refuses to let repentance be reduced to either external compliance or internal sentiment alone.

### What This Implies

**1. Outward conduct must actually change.**  
A person cannot continue walking in the same sinful patterns and claim to have repented. The “wicked” must *forsake* his way. This is not optional or secondary. God is not satisfied with someone who feels sorry while continuing to live in the same direction. The call to “return unto the Lord” includes a decisive break with previous behavior.

**2. Inward imagination must be addressed at the root.**  
Many people can change their outward behavior for a time through willpower, social pressure, or self-interest, while their thoughts and desires remain unchanged. Isaiah insists that this is insufficient. The problem is not only that the wicked *do* certain things, but that they *think* in a way that produces and sustains those things. Repentance that stops at behavior modification is superficial; God requires that the source—the imagination and reasoning—be brought under the lordship of God as well.

**3. True repentance is therefore deeper than moral improvement.**  
This pairing protects against two common distortions:
- **Moralism**: the idea that God mainly wants better behavior.
- **Sentimentalism**: the idea that God mainly wants a change of feelings or “sincerity” while behavior remains largely untouched.

By requiring both, the text makes repentance a matter of the whole person. The outward change without inward renewal is hypocrisy; the inward feeling without outward change is self-deception.

### The Possibility of Mercy

The promise of mercy is attached to this comprehensive repentance, not to a lesser version of it. God offers “abundant pardon” precisely to those who have been wicked in their ways *and* unrighteous in their thoughts. This is both sobering and hopeful:

- **Sobering**: God is not fooled by partial repentance. He sees the thoughts as clearly as the deeds.
- **Hopeful**: The very fact that He calls the wicked and unrighteous to return shows that He is willing to pardon people whose lives and minds have been deeply corrupted. The depth of the required repentance corresponds to the depth of the offered mercy. He does not ask for cosmetic changes because He is prepared to give a real, thorough pardon.

In short, the verse teaches that God’s mercy is real and abundant, but it is extended to those who are willing to be searched and changed at both the level of practice and the level of imagination. Partial repentance receives only partial results; comprehensive repentance opens the door to comprehensive pardon.
