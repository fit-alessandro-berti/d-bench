Luke 6:37 is not saying, “Nothing is right or wrong,” or “No one may ever identify evil.” It is warning against a particular *posture*: the desire to sit in God’s seat over another person, to condemn them as though their whole worth and final destiny were ours to decide.

There is a difference between **moral discernment** and **the appetite to condemn**.

## 1. Discernment says, “This is good; that is evil.”

The Bible itself requires moral judgment in this sense. We are told to recognize sin, pursue righteousness, protect the vulnerable, correct wrongdoing, and distinguish truth from falsehood.

For example, if someone lies, abuses, exploits, or betrays, it is not “judgmental” to name that as wrong. Moral clarity matters. Love does not require pretending harm is harmless.

Discernment asks questions like:

- Is this action just or unjust?
- Is this behavior truthful or deceitful?
- Is this person in danger, or putting others in danger?
- What response would be wise, truthful, and loving?
- How can restoration, justice, or protection happen?

Discernment can lead to correction, boundaries, discipline, or even legal consequences. Forgiveness does not mean abandoning justice or allowing continued harm.

## 2. Condemnation says, “I am above you.”

The kind of “judging” Jesus warns against is not the careful recognition of right and wrong. It is the spirit that delights in passing sentence.

Condemnation often says, implicitly:

- “I know the whole story.”
- “I am morally superior.”
- “You are reducible to your worst act.”
- “There is no hope for you.”
- “I want you shamed, exposed, or punished.”
- “Your failure makes me feel righteous.”

That is different from discernment. Discernment may grieve over evil; condemnation feeds on it.

Jesus is attacking the appetite to condemn: the habit of looking at others primarily through accusation, contempt, and superiority.

## 3. Discernment can be humble; condemnation is proud.

A discerning person may say, “That was wrong, and it must be addressed.”

A condemning person says, “You are worthless, and I am better than you.”

The first can coexist with humility: “I also need mercy. I too am capable of sin. I do not see everything God sees.”

The second places the self in the position of final judge.

This is why Jesus’ teaching is closely related to the image of the speck and the plank: before correcting another, one must examine oneself. The problem is not that the speck does not matter. The problem is trying to remove someone else’s speck while ignoring the plank in one’s own eye.

## 4. Forgiveness is not moral indifference.

“Forgive, and ye shall be forgiven” does not mean, “The offense did not matter.”

In fact, forgiveness assumes that something real happened. If there were no wrong, there would be nothing to forgive.

Forgiveness means releasing the claim to personal vengeance. It means refusing to let hatred become one’s ruling spirit. But it does not necessarily remove consequences, erase boundaries, or cancel the need for repentance.

One can forgive and still say:

- “What you did was wrong.”
- “I cannot trust you in the same way right now.”
- “You need to make restitution.”
- “Others must be protected.”
- “This relationship cannot continue as before.”

Forgiveness is not denial. It is mercy without falsehood.

## 5. Jesus condemns condemnation, not moral seriousness.

Jesus himself makes moral judgments. He confronts hypocrisy, greed, cruelty, hard-heartedness, and religious pride. So Luke 6:37 cannot mean that moral judgments never matter.

Rather, Jesus teaches that moral seriousness must be joined to mercy.

The disciple must not become a person whose righteousness consists in accusing others. True righteousness is not eager to damn. It seeks truth, but also restoration. It names evil, but without enjoying another’s shame. It corrects, but remembers its own need for grace.

## 6. A simple distinction

**Discernment** says:

> “This is wrong, and because it matters, we must respond truthfully, wisely, and mercifully.”

**Condemnation** says:

> “You are beneath me, and I am glad to see you judged.”

The first belongs to love.  
The second belongs to pride.

Luke 6:37 is not a command to abandon moral judgment. It is a command to abandon the cruel pleasure of condemnation. Jesus is not making morality irrelevant; he is making mercy essential.
