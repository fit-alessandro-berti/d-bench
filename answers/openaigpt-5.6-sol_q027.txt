The parable itself gives the controlling diagnosis:

> He addressed it to those who **trusted in themselves that they were righteous and treated others with contempt** (v. 9).

So the Pharisee is not condemned simply for fasting, tithing, or recognizing that extortion and adultery are wrong. His failure is the way he uses those virtues.

### What is wrong with the Pharisee?

Several related things converge:

- **Self-trust:** His standing before God rests on his own moral record. Though he begins, “God, I thank you,” the prayer quickly becomes an inventory of his achievements.
- **Comparison:** He measures righteousness horizontally—“I am not like other people”—rather than standing truthfully before God.
- **Contempt:** His supposed righteousness produces disdain for the tax collector. In Luke’s framing, this is decisive evidence that his piety is spiritually disordered.
- **Self-display:** Fasting and tithing become credentials presented before God and an imagined audience. Virtue has been converted into a basis for status.
- **A practical false doctrine:** He may not state a formal theological error, but he acts as though righteousness is a possession that puts God in his debt and places him above others.

The problem, then, is not virtue itself but **virtue used for self-justification**. His prayer contains no confession, no request, and no awareness of dependence. God is addressed, but the Pharisee himself is effectively the subject of the prayer.

### Why is the tax collector’s posture truer?

The tax collector does not offer a counter-list of merits. He simply says:

> “God, be merciful to me, a sinner.”

The Greek can carry the sense “be propitious to me” or “make atonement for me.” He recognizes two realities the Pharisee obscures:

1. **The truth about himself:** he is a sinner with no grounds for boasting.
2. **The truth about God:** his hope lies in divine mercy rather than self-achieved standing.

His lowered eyes, distance, and beating of the breast are not meritorious techniques. They embody contrition. He is justified not because humiliation earns favor, but because he abandons the attempt to justify himself and appeals to God’s mercy.

The contrast is therefore between **self-exaltation and humble dependence**, not between morality and immorality. The Pharisee says, in effect, “Judge me by my superiority”; the tax collector says, “I have no plea except your mercy.” Jesus declares the latter spiritually true because justification is God’s verdict received by the humble, not a status established through comparison and displayed virtue.

The omitted conclusion of the passage states the principle plainly:

> “Everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, but the one who humbles himself will be exalted.”
