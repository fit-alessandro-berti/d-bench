### The Sharpness of the Metaphor

The image in Matthew 7:3–5 is strikingly vivid due to its exaggerated, absurd contrast between a tiny "mote" (often translated as "speck" or "splinter") and a large "beam" (or "plank" in some versions, implying something as big as a tree trunk). It's a hyperbolic parable where someone with a huge log jutting from their eye absurdly offers to remove a negligible particle from another's eye. This irony highlights hypocrisy through humor and visual exaggeration, making the criticism memorable and impactful—it's like a cartoonish impossibility that forces self-reflection. The passage doesn't invent this imagery; similar motifs appear in ancient rhetorical styles (e.g., in Greek and Roman satires emphasizing foolishness), but Jesus employs it to expose moral blindness, aligning with his Sermon on the Mount's themes of inner purity over superficial judgment (Matthew 7:1–2 warns against judging to avoid being judged).

### Criticism of Hypocrisy While Allowing Moral Correction

The passage condemns hypocrisy by portraying it as self-blind arrogance: attempting to fix others while ignoring one's own massive flaws. It doesn't forbid moral correction altogether—in fact, it encourages it—but insists on a prerequisite of self-examination. Verse 5 explicitly says, "Then you will see clearly to remove the speck from your brother’s eye." This implies that correction can follow personal reform, but only when done with humility and certainty, not from a stance of superiority. Contextually, the Sermon on the Mount warns against legalism (Matthew 7:1–2) while promoting genuine discipleship, so the critique targets judgmental self-righteousness without abolishing accountability. Biblical scholars like those in works such as the Expositor's Bible Commentary note this balances inner transformation with communal exhortation, echoing Proverbs 11:2 ("When pride comes, then comes disgrace, but wisdom is with the humble"). In practice, it discourages rash, hypocritical behavior (e.g., Pharisees critiqued by Jesus in Matthew 23) while permitting righteous correction once self-corrected, as seen in Jesus' own teachings elsewhere (e.g., Galatians 6:1 on restoring others gently).
