This passage—**Luke 6:37**—is one of Jesus’ most direct teachings on **non-judgmentalism**. Let’s break down what it means and how it differs from the idea that *moral judgments never matter*.

---

## 1. **What Does the Passage Mean?**

> “Judge not, and ye shall not be judged: condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned: forgive, and ye shall be forgiven.”

- **“Judge not”** does not mean we should never make any kind of evaluation or discernment at all. Rather, it warns against **harsh, unforgiving, or self-righteous judgment**—judgment that condemns others harshly or seeks to elevate oneself by putting others down.
- **“Condemn”** refers to passing severe, final, or punitive verdicts on others.
- **“Forgive”** is the antidote: if you extend forgiveness, you will also be forgiven.

So, Jesus is teaching a principle of **mercy over condemnation**, encouraging humility and compassion rather than self-righteousness or harshness.

---

## 2. **How Is This Different From Saying Moral Judgments Never Matter?**

**Moral judgments always matter** in the sense that our actions have consequences (for ourselves and others), and God cares about right and wrong. The Bible is full of passages about justice, righteousness, and accountability.

But **Jesus’ point here is not that morality doesn’t matter**; rather, he’s warning against:
- **Judging others harshly**
- **Using judgment to exclude or condemn**
- **Being self-righteous (“I’m better than you”)**

He calls for discernment—**wise, loving, and merciful judgment**—not condemnation.

---

## 3. **Discernment vs. The Appetite to Condemn**

- **Discernment:**  
  - The ability to see situations clearly, understand motives, and act with wisdom and compassion.
  - It involves distinguishing between sin and grace, truth and error, but always with mercy as its foundation.
  - Example: “Let me help you grow, not shame you.”

- **The Appetite to Condemn:**  
  - A desire to judge others harshly, often out of pride, fear, or self-righteousness.
  - It focuses on fault-finding, exclusion, and punishment.
  - Example: “You’re bad, so you deserve to be punished.”

**In summary:**  
Jesus teaches us to judge with discernment and mercy, not to condemn with harshness. Moral judgments do matter, but they must be rooted in love, not judgmental superiority.

---

**In short:**  
*We are called to judge wisely and forgive freely—not to condemn unkindly or to ignore right and wrong.*
