Paul places **bitterness** and **forgiveness** opposite each other because, in this passage, he is contrasting **two whole ways of relating to people**:

- one that **stores up injury and turns inward**
- one that **releases injury and turns outward in mercy**

So the contrast is not merely “bad feeling vs. good feeling.” It is broader than that.

### 1) “Bitterness” here is more than a feeling
In Ephesians 4:31, “bitterness” sits in a list with **wrath, anger, clamour, evil speaking, and malice**. That makes it look less like a fleeting emotion and more like a **settled attitude that expresses itself in speech and behavior**.

In other words, Paul is not mainly talking about the momentary sensation of being hurt. He is talking about the **permanent souring of the heart** that keeps hurt alive, replays it, and lets it spill out in hostile words and actions.

### 2) It is a posture of the heart
The word “bitterness” in biblical usage often has the sense of being **poisoned, resentful, or harsh**. It is a kind of inner posture that says:

- “I will not let this go.”
- “I will keep this offense active.”
- “I will define this person by what they did to me.”

That makes bitterness the moral and relational opposite of forgiveness, because forgiveness says:

- “I will not hold this against you.”
- “I release my claim to revenge.”
- “I will seek your good, not your destruction.”

### 3) Yes, it is contagious
Paul may also be treating bitterness as something that **spreads**. Notice the cluster:

- bitterness
- wrath
- anger
- clamour
- evil speaking
- malice

These do not stay private for long. Bitterness tends to **infect relationships**: it hardens tone, fuels arguments, encourages gossip, and creates a culture of mutual suspicion. That’s probably why Paul says it must be “put away” rather than managed.

By contrast, forgiveness also spreads. A forgiving community becomes one where people can repent, be restored, and not be permanently trapped by past offenses.

### 4) Why forgiveness is the direct opposite
Paul’s logic is theological: believers forgive **“even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you.”**

So forgiveness is not just the absence of resentment. It is an imitation of God’s action toward us in Christ. If God has dealt with us by grace, then holding onto bitterness toward others is a contradiction of the gospel.

### In short
Paul is treating bitterness as:

- a **heart posture**
- a **settled relational habit**
- an **active, contagious force** that produces hostile speech and behavior

And he sets forgiveness against it because forgiveness is the gospel-shaped response that stops the cycle of injury and retaliation.
