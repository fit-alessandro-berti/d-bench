Based on the text of Luke 15:28–32, the elder brother’s deepest problem is **not** merely his anger or envy, though those are the visible symptoms. His deepest problem is a **false understanding of his relationship with the father**, which manifests as **pride in his obedience** and, ultimately, **discomfort with the father's mercy.**

Here is a breakdown of why this is the case, moving from the surface to the root:

### 1. The Surface: Anger, Envy, and Wounded Fairness
The elder brother is clearly angry ("he was angry") and envious ("thou never gavest me a kid"). He feels that the distribution of rewards is unfair. He has worked hard; the younger brother has squandered wealth. In his mind, the father’s math is wrong.
*   **The "Fairness" Issue:** He views the family not as a home of love, but as a transaction. He believes his obedience has earned him a "kid," and the father’s failure to provide one is a breach of contract.

### 2. The Root: Pride in Obedience (Legalism)
The key to his deep problem lies in verse 29: *"Lo, these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment..."*
*   **The Illusion of Sinlessness:** The younger brother sinned openly (harlots, gluttony). The elder brother sins by claiming to be sinless ("neither transgressed I at any time"). This is a mark of immense spiritual pride. He views himself as a servant (the Greek word here is *douleuō*, implying slavish service) rather than a son.
*   **Service vs. Sonship:** He says, "I serve thee," whereas the father corrects him in verse 31 by saying, "Thou art **ever with me**." The brother separates his actions from his identity. He thinks he is earning his place, whereas the father reminds him that his place is secure because of their relationship, not his performance.

### 3. The Deepest Problem: Discomfort with Mercy (The Heart of the Gospel)
This is the climax of Jesus' parable. The elder brother’s problem is that **he does not want the father to be gracious; he wants the father to be just.**
*   If the father had been "fair" (according to the brother), the younger son would have been punished or at least given a cold reception. The father’s reckless, exuberant mercy creates a crisis for the elder brother.
*   He cannot rejoice that his brother is "alive again" because his worldview is built on **merit**. If the "bad" son gets grace, then the "good" son’s hard work feels devalued.
*   C.S. Lewis famously noted that in the end, there are two types of people: those who say to God, "Thy will be done," and those to whom God says, "Thy will be done." The elder brother wants a world where people get what they deserve. He is uncomfortable with a father who gives good things to those who do not deserve them.

### Conclusion
If we must choose one as the *deepest* problem, it is his **misunderstanding of the Father's character.**

He believes the Father is a **Taskmaster** to be served, rather than a **Father** to be enjoyed. Because he sees the Father as a Taskmaster, he resents the grace shown to the younger brother. He is the "prodigal" in reverse—he stayed home, but his heart was far from the father, calculating wages and watching the clock.

The father’s response in verse 31 is the cure: **"Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine."** The father invites him to stop looking at what the other brother got and start looking at what he already possesses: the father himself.
