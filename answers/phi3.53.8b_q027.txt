In Luke 18:9-14, Jesus is criticizing the Pharisee's attitude and approach to piety, which is condemned for several reasons:

1. **False Doctrine**: The Pharisee's understanding of righteousness is flawed as he seems to believe righteousness comes from outward displays of religious observance and self-imposed ascetic practices. He thinks that his fasting, giving tithes, and distinguishing himself from others by not engaging in their sinful behaviors make him righteous. He misinterprets the Law and self-righteousness, mistaking external ritual cleanlinency for true piety.

2. **Pride**: The Pharisee's approach is filled with self-righteousness and arrogance. He compares himself favorably to others whom he considers impure—"adulterers" and "extortioners"—implying that he is morally superior because of his religious observance and self-discipline. This self-esteem blinds him to the sinfulness that is an inherent part of the human condition and leads to a sense of detached self-worth and superiority.

3. **Comparison**: The Pharisee is constantly comparing himself negatively to others, suggesting that his moral standing is significantly higher based on surface-level observations rather than genuine transformation of heart or spiritual state. He sees them, the tax collector (publican), as his inferiors but does not recognize the possibility that his righteous appearance may be deceiving.

4. **Contempt**: There is an element of contempt or disdain in the Pharisee's prayer; he looks down on others from a position of perceived moral righteousness, as seen in his failure to recognize his own sins and spiritual failure. While this is not overtly expressed, his attitude towards others is dismissive and does not align with the humility that true piety would involve.

5. **Use of Virtue as Self-Display**: The Pharisee appears to be using his piety as an act of self-display, using religious observance as a means to boost his self-image rather than seeking genuine connection with God. His praise of his religious acts lacks humility and understanding of his own true place before God.

In contrast, the tax collector's posture is treated as more spiritually authentic because:

1. **Humility**: The tax collector, despite performing a role that was regarded unjustly by others, demonstrates humility by beating his breast, an act of supplication and acknowledgment of his sins. He does not compare himself to others but acknowledges his unworthiness, which reflects a contrite and repentant heart, essential to genuine faith and salvation.

2. **Recognition of Suffering as a Human**: Despite his occupational position, the tax collector recognizes his own sinfulness and shortcomings, admitting, "God be merciful to me, a sinner." This indicates his understanding that he too needs God'thy mercy, not because he lacks good deeds, but because he is a sinner at heart, highlighting his vulnerability and brokenness, which invites God's grace.

3. **Desire for God's Mercy**: The tax collector's focus is on his need for God's mercy rather than self-righteous boasting about his religious practices, pointing to an authentic seeking God.

The tax collector's approach is in line with the core Christian teaching that righteousness comes from God and faith in Jesus Christ, not from one's own performance. While external actions are valuable, true piety emerges from recognition of one's sinful nature, humble confession, and reliance on God's grace, as revealed in Jesus’ sacrifice.

In essence, what condemns the Pharisee in this parable is not just his individual choices and attitudes, but a combination of pride, arrogance, lack of understanding of his own spiritual state, and the misuse of religious practices for self-aggrandizement. Conversely, the tax collector's humility, self-awareness, and desire for God's mercy reveal a heart more in touch with genuine spirituality. The Christian message emphasizes interior transformation and relying on God's grace, rather than boasting over outward religious observances or self-justifications, underpinning why his posture is spiritually truer.
