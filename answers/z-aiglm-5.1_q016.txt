While the elder brother exhibits all the traits listed—anger, envy, wounded fairness, and pride in obedience—his **deepest problem is his discomfort with mercy itself**, which is born out of his **pride in obedience**. 

To understand why, it helps to look at the options as a hierarchy: anger and envy are the *surface symptoms*, wounded fairness is the *psychological rationale*, pride in obedience is the *spiritual sickness*, and discomfort with mercy is the *ultimate root*.

Here is how these layers break down, moving from the surface to the deepest problem:

### 1. The Surface Symptoms: Anger and Envy
The text begins, "And he was angry." He is also clearly envious, comparing the kid goat he never received to the fatted calf his brother received. However, anger and envy are merely emotions; they are the smoke, not the fire. They beg the question: *Why* is he angry? *Why* is he envious? Addressing only his anger would be like treating a cough while ignoring the underlying disease.

### 2. The Psychological Rationale: Wounded Fairness
The elder brother operates on a transactional worldview: *If I work, I get paid; if I obey, I am rewarded.* His sense of fairness is wounded because his ledger is out of balance. He kept his end of the bargain ("neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment"), but he feels the father broke his. However, the parable suggests that "fairness" is actually a trap. If the father gave the elder brother what was strictly *fair* based on the elder brother's cold, transactional view of the relationship, the elder brother would find himself condemned, too—for the sin of lacking love for his brother and resentment toward his father.

### 3. The Spiritual Sickness: Pride in Obedience
This gets closer to the heart of the issue. The elder brother defines his identity entirely by his moral performance. Notice his language: "Lo, these many years do I *serve* thee..." He does not say, "These many years I have *loved* you," or "I have delighted in being your son." He views himself not as a son, but as an employee—a servant bound by commandments. 

His pride in his own obedience blinds him to two vital realities:
*   **He is just as lost as his brother.** The younger brother was lost in a far country through reckless rebellion; the elder brother is lost in his own backyard through reckless self-righteousness. Both are alienated from the father.
*   **He has missed the gift.** The father says, "Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine." The elder brother was so busy working for the inheritance that he failed to realize he already possessed it. His obedience became a prison rather than a pathway to relationship.

### 4. The Deepest Root: Discomfort with Mercy Itself
The elder brother's ultimate problem is that he hates the way his father loves. He is offended by grace. 

If the father had punished the younger brother, the elder brother would have been satisfied. Instead, the father runs to the prodigal, embraces him, and throws a party. This reveals the elder brother's deepest issue: **he does not want a father who is merciful; he wants a God who operates a karma-machine.** 

Mercy levels the playing field. It insists that the "righteous" and the "sinner" both come to the table as desperate beggars in need of grace. The elder brother refuses to enter the party because he cannot stand sharing a father with someone who doesn't deserve it. Notice his language in verse 30: *"this thy son"*—he refuses to even call him his brother. He would rather stand alone in the dark, clutching his moral resume, than walk into a light that shines equally on a moral failure.

### The Tragic Irony
The tragedy of the elder brother is that his discomfort with mercy leaves him outside. The younger brother was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found. But at the end of the parable, the elder brother is still outside the party. He is the one who is currently "lost." 

In the context of Jesus’ original audience, the elder brother represented the Pharisees and religious leaders who were grumbling that Jesus ate with tax collectors and sinners (Luke 15:1-2). Their deepest problem was not that they were angry at sinners; it was that they despised the mercy of God that welcomed them in.
