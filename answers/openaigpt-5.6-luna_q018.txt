Bitterness and forgiveness stand opposite one another because they represent two different ways of handling injury within the Christian community.

### 1. Bitterness is more than a passing feeling

The Greek word for “bitterness” (*pikria*) originally suggests something bitter to the taste. In this context it means a resentful, harsh, sour disposition toward another person. It is not merely the momentary experience of hurt or anger. The surrounding terms show how it develops and expresses itself:

- **bitterness** — the inward resentful disposition  
- **wrath and anger** — heated hostility  
- **clamour** — angry, disruptive outbursts  
- **evil speaking** — damaging speech or slander  
- **malice** — a settled desire for another’s harm

Thus, bitterness is both an attitude and a pattern. It may begin as an understandable wound, but it becomes morally significant when it is nursed, interpreted against the other person, and expressed in hostile conduct.

The command “let it be put away” does not imply that Christians never feel pain, anger, or grief. It means that such responses must not be allowed to harden into a governing posture.

### 2. Forgiveness is not simply the absence of bitterness

Verse 32 does not merely say, “Stop being bitter.” It gives a replacement:

> “Be ye kind one to another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another.”

The Christian response is not emotional blankness but active kindness, compassion, and grace. The word translated “forgiving” is related to *charis*, “grace.” It has the sense of granting forgiveness freely, rather than exacting the full debt of the offense.

Bitterness keeps the injury present as a continuing claim against the other person. Forgiveness entrusts the claim to God and refuses to make retaliation, humiliation, or continued hostility the basis of the relationship. It does not call evil good, eliminate appropriate boundaries, or prevent justice. Nor does it require immediate restoration of trust. But it does renounce the desire to keep harming the offender.

### 3. The contrast is grounded in God’s forgiveness

The decisive phrase is:

> “even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you.”

God’s forgiveness is both the model and the source of Christian forgiveness. Believers are not asked to produce grace merely by willpower or to forgive because the offense was insignificant. They forgive in light of having received undeserved forgiveness through Christ.

There is also a deliberate contrast between the two dispositions:

- bitterness keeps a record of the wrong and treats the offender according to it;
- forgiveness releases the offender from personal vengeance because God has acted graciously toward us.

The standard is not simply, “Forgive when you feel ready,” but “forgive in the manner and spirit in which God has forgiven you.”

### 4. It is also socially contagious

The passage likely presents bitterness as something that spreads. An inward sourness becomes angry speech; angry speech produces hostility; hostility damages the whole body. This fits the wider biblical image of a “root of bitterness” that troubles and defiles many (Hebrews 12:15).

Forgiveness is likewise communal. It interrupts the chain reaction. Kindness and tenderheartedness make the community less governed by grievance and retaliation.

So the text treats bitterness as **a feeling that can become a habit, a chosen posture, and a contagious relational force**. The initial feeling may not be chosen, but what one does with it is addressed morally. Ephesians calls believers not to deny their wounds, but to prevent those wounds from becoming a settled identity or a source of harm—and to replace them with the grace they themselves have received from God.
