This is a profound question about the heart of Christian ethics and how we approach "goodness" (mercy, charity, giving). To answer it carefully, we need to distinguish between the **nature of the deed** and the **motivation behind it.**

### 1. Is Visibility Itself the Danger?
The short answer is: **Visibility itself is not inherently bad.** Hiding a good deed is not wrong; in fact, hiding an act from worldly judgment can sometimes protect the person's integrity or allow them to be less burdened by public opinion.

However, in Jesus' context ("do your alms before men... to be seen of them"), the danger lies in **public performance.** If a good deed is done *in order to be seen*, it creates a moral trap because:
*   It treats love as a currency to buy human recognition.
*   It elevates human judgment over God's will.

### 2. Is the Craving to be Seen the Danger?
**Yes.** This is where the moral danger truly sits. The root issue is the **prideful motivation of seeking applause**, not the act of giving itself. If someone gives alms because they need the "satisfaction" of knowing other people see it, this shifts their focus from God to themselves.

Jesus' teaching here is about **"doing good for God's glory, not to be seen by man"** (Matthew 6:20).
*   The warning in verse 1 ("do... to be seen") implies a transactional mindset: "I give to gain the favor of men." When that motivation is removed and replaced with selfless service toward God, the act becomes spiritual and valuable.
*   If someone does a good deed *in secret*, it doesn't make them morally flawed (unless it was done out of greed/lie). It only makes the motive **private**.

### 3. Why this creates Moral Danger
The moral danger in this verse isn't hiding the kindness in itself, but when that kindness becomes a tool for self-pleasing or validation:

*   **It creates spiritual anxiety:** Doing it publicly requires constant performance. When you do it only to be seen, your success depends entirely on human applause (pride), while God knows nothing of it and offers no real reward (Matthew 6:2).
*   **It distorts the definition of "goodness":** True goodness is often quiet or hidden (like a good work not seeking fame). When we make goodness public for credit, we risk turning an act of mercy into theater.

### Conclusion
The verse warns us that if our "visibility" becomes the *goal* of the action instead of God's glory, we have turned charity into vanity. However, a hidden deed in private can still be spiritually valuable if done for God's sake rather than human recognition. True morality in this context is **sincerity of heart**, not secrecy, but the fear of public judgment destroys that sincerity and replaces true worship with social validation.

So to answer directly: The problem is **not visibility itself, but the craving** to be seen by others as a transaction or performance for their approval. That desire corrupts goodness into ego.
