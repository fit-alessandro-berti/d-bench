In **Ephesians 4:31–32**, Paul places bitterness directly next to forgiveness because he’s describing a *relational chain*: what you *nurture* toward someone will shape what you *do* toward them.

### 1) Bitterness is more than a momentary feeling
The list in 4:31 (“bitterness, wrath, anger, clamour, evil speaking…with all malice”) is not a set of emotions that pop up and then vanish. It’s a description of a **settled disposition** that then spills outward into speech and action.

“Bitterness” here functions like a **root**—something that grows in the heart and then produces further behaviors (wrath/anger, loud quarrelling, harmful talk, and malice). So it’s not merely “a feeling I had.” It’s closer to a **habitual posture** of the heart: resentment that remains and begins to govern your reactions.

### 2) The “put away” language points to something chosen/guarded
Paul says, **“be put away from you”**. That wording suggests an **intentional removal**—not just suppressing a transient emotion, but rejecting the stance that keeps producing sin.

So bitterness is treated as:
- something that can be **cultivated** (“held on to,” protected),
- and therefore something that can be **discarded** (“put away”).

### 3) It’s also implicitly “contagious”
Notice the progression: bitterness → outward expressions that harm the community:
- bitterness
- wrath and anger
- clamour (shouting/uproar)
- evil speaking
- malice (ill will)

That pattern implies bitterness isn’t sealed in the private sphere. It **spreads** into relationships through how people speak and act. In that sense, yes—bitterness is portrayed as something that becomes **contagious in a community**, because it tends to generate more relational damage.

### 4) Forgiveness is presented as the alternative operating system
Then Paul immediately supplies the replacement: **“be kind…tenderhearted…forgiving…even as God…has forgiven you.”**

Forgiveness here isn’t only an emotion; it’s an embodied orientation:
- kindness rather than hostility,
- tenderness rather than hardness,
- forgiveness rather than maintaining resentment.

And the “even as God…has forgiven you” grounds forgiveness in a **decisive divine act** that Christians are to mirror in their ongoing dealings with others.

---

## Bottom line
Ephesians 4:31–32 treats bitterness as a **deep-seated, chosen relational posture** (a resentful “root”) that naturally issues into **anger and harmful speech/actions**—and therefore needs to be **actively put away**. Forgiveness is the corresponding posture that breaks that chain and replaces it with kindness and tenderhearted mercy.
