### Moral posture required for correction
The passage calls for **a spirit of meekness**—that is, humility, gentleness, and a willingness to bear one another’s burdens rather than to judge or condemn. It is not a harsh, self‑righteous, or “I‑know‑better” attitude; it is a posture that **recognizes our own frailty** and therefore approaches the erring person with compassion and patience.

### Why restoration, meekness, and self‑suspicion are linked

| Element | How it contributes to the moral posture | Why they are kept together |
|---------|------------------------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Restoration** | The goal is to bring the person back into a right relationship with God and the community, not merely to punish. | Restoration can only succeed when it is done gently; a forceful or contemptuous approach would push the person further away. |
| **Meekness** | Meekness (Greek *πραΰτης*) means “gentle, humble, low‑key.” It guards against pride, which would make the corrector think they are above the fault. | Meekness keeps the heart focused on the other’s welfare rather than on one’s own superiority. |
| **Self‑suspicion (or self‑examination)** | “Considering thyself” urges the corrector to examine his own motives, character, and potential for temptation. | If we ignore our own weakness, we risk becoming the very “tempted” person the verse warns against; self‑examination prevents hypocrisy and ensures the correction is truly for the other’s good. |

#### The theological logic
1. **Humility prevents abuse** – A proud corrector may impose harsh judgments, turning restoration into condemnation.
2. **Self‑awareness protects the corrector** – By reminding ourselves that we, too, are prone to sin, we stay vigilant against being “tempted” ourselves (i.e., falling into the same fault or reacting with anger).
3. **Mutual edification** – The process is relational: the one who restores is also being built up in meekness and humility, while the one restored is given a chance to repent and grow.

### Practical implication
When confronting a fellow believer’s sin, the corrector should:

1. **Approach gently**, seeking the person’s restoration rather than merely meting out punishment.
2. **Maintain a humble heart**, recognizing that any of us could be “overtaken” and that we are not above the need for grace.
3. **Engage in self‑examination**, asking, “Am I spiritually mature enough? Could my own pride or impatience be influencing my response?”

In short, the moral posture required is **humble, compassionate, and self‑critical**—a posture that safeguards both the one being corrected and the one doing the correcting.
