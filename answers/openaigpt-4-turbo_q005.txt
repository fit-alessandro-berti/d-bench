Enemy-love, as discussed in Matthew 5:44-45, challenges conventional human instincts and societal norms about how to treat those who oppose or hurt us. The difficulty in taking this concept seriously stems from various factors, both psychological and societal, and can be explored through three key perspectives: sentimental, strategic, and moral strength.

1. **Sentimental Perspective:**
   - **Emotional Challenges:** Loving enemies contradicts basic emotional reactions such as anger and the desire for retribution. Emotionally, it feels counterintuitive and often at odds with feelings of pain or betrayal inflicted by others.
   - **Perceived Naivety:** Some might view enemy-love as overly idealistic or naive, especially in a world where aggression and competition are often seen as necessary for survival or success. The notion of loving those who harm us can appear sentimental, weak, or impractical, especially to those who prioritize self-protection or justice in more traditional senses. 

2. **Strategic Perspective:**
   - **Nonviolence and Conflict Resolution:** Practically, loving enemies can be a powerful strategy for breaking cycles of violence and initiating dialogue. History shows examples where enemy-love in the form of nonviolent resistance (e.g., Martin Luther King Jr., Mahatma Gandhi) led to profound social and political changes.
   - **Long-term Benefits:** Although difficult in the short term, showing love and kindness to enemies can potentially transform adversarial relationships and reduce conflicts over time. It can disrupt expected patterns of hostility and vengeance, potentially leading to more peaceful interactions.

3. **Moral Strength Perspective:**
   - **Ethical High Ground:** From a moral standpoint, loving one’s enemies reflects a profound strength of character. It demonstrates an ability to uphold principles of compassion, forgiveness, and humility, even under provocation.
   - **Universal Benevolence:** This approach aligns with a moral vision where everyone, regardless of their actions, is worthy of respect and kindness. It rests on the belief in the inherent dignity of all human beings and the transformative power of unconditional love.

**Conclusion:** Ultimately, the directive to love one’s enemies as prescribed in the Bible can be seen not merely as sentimental but as a radical act of moral courage that challenges individuals to transcend their immediate instincts for retribution. It is a strategy aimed at achieving deeper, more sustainable peace and demonstrates a profound moral strength that fosters genuine community and human connection. This form of love is hard to practice because it requires a substantial shift in perspective and values, demanding ongoing commitment and self-reflection.
