You've pinpointed the central ethical tension in this passage. Ephesians 4:31–32 places bitterness and forgiveness in direct juxtaposition not by accident, but as a deliberate theological and ethical contrast. They are treated as fundamentally opposed states of the heart and modes of relating.

Here’s how the text conceptualizes bitterness and why it is set so starkly against forgiveness:

1.  **Bitterness as a **Chosen Posture** and Relational Dynamic** (though it begins as a feeling):
    The Greek word used (*πικρία*, *pikria*) refers to a sharp, acrid taste (like gall or wormwood). Metaphorically, it describes a corrosive, resentful attitude. It is not merely an involuntary *feeling* of hurt, but a **willful clinging** to injury, an unwillingness to release the debt the other person owes you. It is a stance you take. The passage lists it alongside *wrath, anger, clamour, evil speaking, and malice* – all of which are active, outward expressions of a hardened heart. It is a *habit* of the heart that manifests in gossip, slander, and hostility. Its "contagiousness" comes from how it poisons relationships, creating cycles of retaliation and mistrust, but the text's primary focus is on it as a **moral choice** to remain embittered.

2.  **Forgiveness as a **Mirror of Divine Action**:**
    Forgiveness (*ἀφιέναι*, *aphiénai* – to let go, send away, release) is not presented as a mere emotional feeling, but as a **deliberate act of grace**. The command "be ye kind... forgiving one another" directly links human action to divine example. The logic is: *Because God forgave you in Christ (an act of costly grace), you must forgive others*. Bitterness says, "You owe me, and I will hold it against you." Forgiveness says, "You owed a debt you could not pay, and I release it because I have been released."

3.  **The Direct Opposition:**
    The text sets them against each other because they are **mutually exclusive**.
    *   **Bitterness is a debt ledger kept open; forgiveness is the tearing up of that ledger.**
    *   **Bitterness is corrosive and dehumanizing; forgiveness is renewing and restores community.**
    *   **Bitterness is an assertion of self-justification; forgiveness is an act of justification by grace.**
    You cannot cling to bitterness and be "tenderhearted" (the Greek *σπλαγχνίζομαι*, *splagchnizomai*, means to be moved in the deepest parts of one's being, like the bowels – the seat of compassion) toward someone.

4.  **The Role of the Heart:**
    While starting as a feeling, the passage clearly addresses the **will and practice**. It is what resides in and is expressed from the "heart" (the core of one's being). The progression from "let all bitterness... be put away" to "be ye kind... forgiving" shows a transformation of the inner disposition that must then manifest in outward actions.

**Conclusion:**
The text treats bitterness primarily as a **chosen moral posture and a destructive habit** that begins with a wounded feeling but is sustained by the will. It is "contagious" in its relational destructiveness, but more centrally, it is the **antithesis of the grace-based, costly forgiveness** modeled by God. The force of the passage lies in this stark contrast: one path leads to death (spiritual and relational), the other to life and reconciliation. The command to forgive is not a suggestion but a necessary response to the reality of having been forgiven.
