The elder brother’s deepest problem in Luke 15:28–32 is a complex interplay of several issues, but the most fundamental one is **discomfort with mercy itself**—specifically, his inability to rejoice in the father’s radical, unconditional love for the prodigal son. While anger, envy, wounded fairness, and pride in obedience are all present, they stem from a deeper resistance to the father’s mercy, which challenges his own self-righteousness and sense of entitlement.

### Breaking Down the Layers:
1. **Pride in Obedience (Self-Righteousness):**
   The elder brother boasts of his loyalty ("these many years do I serve thee, neither transgressed I at any time thy commandment"), revealing a transactional view of his relationship with the father. He sees himself as deserving of reward for his obedience, which makes the father’s mercy toward the prodigal feel like an injustice. His pride blinds him to the fact that the father’s love is not earned but freely given.

2. **Wounded Fairness (Entitlement):**
   He resents the celebration for the prodigal ("thou never gavest me a kid... but for this thy son... thou hast killed the fatted calf"). His sense of fairness is rooted in comparison and merit, not grace. He fails to see that the father’s joy is not about fairness but about restoration.

3. **Envy and Anger:**
   His anger ("he was angry, and would not go in") and envy (resenting the prodigal’s reception) are symptoms of his deeper issue: he cannot accept that the father’s love is not zero-sum. His obedience has not made him secure in the father’s love; instead, he feels threatened by the prodigal’s inclusion.

4. **Discomfort with Mercy Itself:**
   The elder brother’s core problem is that he **does not understand or trust the father’s mercy**. He expects love to be conditional (earned through obedience) and is offended when it is lavished on the undeserving. His discomfort reveals a heart that is closed to grace—he would rather cling to his own righteousness than celebrate the father’s joy in redemption.

### The Father’s Response:
The father’s words ("Son, thou art ever with me, and all that I have is thine") expose the elder brother’s blindness. He has always had access to the father’s love and provision, but his legalistic mindset has kept him from enjoying it. The father’s mercy toward the prodigal is not a rejection of the elder brother but an invitation for him to join in the joy of restoration.

### Conclusion:
While anger, envy, and pride are all present, the elder brother’s deepest problem is his **rejection of mercy as the foundation of the father’s love**. His self-righteousness and entitlement are barriers to experiencing the father’s grace, and his discomfort with mercy reveals a heart that is still lost—even though he never left home. The parable challenges us to examine whether we, like the elder brother, resist grace when it is extended to others.
