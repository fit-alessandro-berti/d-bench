The passage from Amos 5:21-24 is a vehement denunciation against the Israelites' religious and social practices, and it speaks to deeply entrenched moral and spiritual issues within the society of Judah at that time. The preaching of the prophet Amos was meant not simply to reject certain aspects of worship practices but to call the people to a higher level of holiness, justice, and true piety. Here's an analysis of the rejection and the morality/spiritual fraud being exposed:

1. **Elitism and Idolatry**: The passage indicates a rejection of the people's religious practices that were corrupted by the luxuries and idolatries of their times, specifically their feasts and music, which may have been intertwined with pagan rituals. The feast days with their excesses and the noise of the viol's music were associated with an elite class that had lost sight of the sacred, focusing instead on the profane. By refusing these offerings and noise, Amos rejects not just the practice itself, but the underlying attitudes and materialism that fed such behavior.

2. **Fraternization with Foreigners and Syncretism**: The language used ("fucks with the vine"), coupled with the condemnation of festivities and peace offerings, suggests that Israel had been influenced by surrounding pagan cultures, adopting practices that were not true to their monotheistic faith. Such syncretism was seen as a moral failing because it showed a lack of faithful distinction between Israelite norms and neighboring cultural practices.

3. **Superficial Piety and Hypocrisy**: The worship being rejected in the text includes practices that were performed without true conviction or understanding of God's holiness. It implies that religious merriment, feasts, and artistic expressions like music were used as mere ornaments rather than vehicles for genuine worship and reverence, which suggests that the people were displaying a form of hypocrisy. True worship for Amos should be marked by sincere fear and holiness – not a demonstration that aimed to impress or entertain.

4. **Social Injustice**: The mention of "fat beasts," the animals sacrificed as peace offerings, symbolizes a class of offerings that likely only the wealthier Jews could provide, possibly pointing to a social stratification where the poor are marginalized and excluded from full participation in worship. Thus, the religious practices appear disconnected from the concerns of the common Jewish people who are the subject of Torah's laws (Pikuach Nefesh, the preservation of life).

5. **Spiritual Insincerity**: Amos' rejection of the noise of the songs and festivity implies that worship without depth, without spirit, is spiritually insincere or deceiving. Worship should lead to righteousness, not indulgence. The emphasis on righteousness as a "mighty stream" suggests that true religious piety is about more than just external offerings and rituals; it should overflow into one's deeds and treatment of others, leading to a life that reflects the ethical values central to God’thy teachings.

6. **Demand for Justice**: The passage also demands societal righteousness – this is a call not only to personal morality but also to social justice within Israel. It exposes the fraud of a society that presents a facade of holiness and piety while committing various injustices that lead to the spread of poverty and suffering.

7. **Divine Judgment Necessity**: Amos underscores the need for a purification of worship, not a mere balance. This balance cannot be achieved with half-heartedness or half measures in piety. His rejection is a sharper critique of both the practices and the culture that allowed them to persist. The divine judgment he references, like a mighty stream, will not spare any but will cleanse the land of its moral failings, bringing a reckoning that aligns with God's commandments for justice and mercy.

Amos' message is uncompromising in its call for a radical return to ethical purity and social equity, challenging both the external religious worship and the internal moral conduct of the people. He expects the establishment of an environment where the righteousness flows as naturally and robustly as water, bringing life and nourishment rather than just surviving ritual observance. This stark rejection is not a denial of religious expression but an imperative reorientation towards authentic, sincere, and God-centered worship where social and personal ethics are aligned with divine expectations.

In summation, what Amos exposes here is not a simple call for balance, but a profound moral and spiritual crisis within Judah, where excess, injustice, and disingenuousness threaten the core of Judah's covenant with God. His message requires fundamental change, a purification of both heart and society, not only in what one is expected to offer but also in how they live their daily lives, ensuring actions align with God's teachings on justice and compassion. The ultimate goal isn't merely adjusting religious observance but aligning oneself with God's character of righteousness and love for the neighbor.

The rejection isn't against the ritualistic worship per se but against worship that is empty of deep meaning, one that doesn't align with God's heart of mercy and justice, which permeates not just into the synagogue walls but into the everyday lives of the people. The prophet demands a transformation of the people's very heart and actions such that their religious expressions and social conduct are intertwined with truth, love, and equity.

Here, Amos is demanding profound repentance — a radical departure from self-indulgence, greed, and social inequality — and he is calling for a return to a genuine faith characterized by the righteousness that should be as pervasive and unavoidable as a powerful stream cleaning a previously stagnant and polluted environment — bringing about spiritual renewal and a healthy, well-ordered society.

This unwavering condemnation, articulated in stark language, serves as a moral wake-up call for the community, signifying a necessary overhaul of their social structure, their heart-level engagement with faith, and their approach to justice — not just reformations that keep the surface of religious observance intact. Amos' message, as intense and forthright as it is, is ultimately a hopeful plea for a society that fully mirrors the holiness and justice that is essential to the life of God and his people. The stark tone is less about the worship instruments themselves but about a pervasive spiritual enfeebling that permeates the people's social and religious lives.
