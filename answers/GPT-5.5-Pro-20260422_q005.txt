Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it seems to violate our deepest moral instincts. We usually think justice means giving people what they deserve: kindness for kindness, hostility for hostility, punishment for harm. Jesus interrupts that logic. He does not merely say, “Do not become cruel.” He says, “Love your enemies,” and then defines that love in active terms: bless, do good, pray.

That is hard because enemies are not abstract. An enemy is someone who threatens your safety, reputation, people, dignity, or future. To love such a person can sound like moral weakness, emotional dishonesty, or surrender. It can feel as though Jesus is asking victims to minimize evil.

But enemy-love is **not sentimental** if sentimentality means warm feelings, niceness, or pretending harm did not happen. Jesus does not say enemies are secretly harmless. He names cursing, hatred, abuse, and persecution. The command assumes real hostility. Christian enemy-love is not affection first; it is willing and acting for the other person’s ultimate good without becoming captive to their evil.

Nor is it simply **strategic**, though it can have strategic effects. Refusing retaliation may expose injustice, disarm hostility, or break cycles of revenge. But in Matthew 5, the main reason is not tactical success. The reason is theological: “that ye may be the children of your Father.” In other words, love your enemies because this is what God is like. God gives sun and rain not only to the righteous, but also to the unrighteous. Divine generosity precedes human worthiness.

So enemy-love is best understood as a **deeper form of moral strength**.

It requires strength because it refuses the enemy the power to determine your character. The enemy curses; you do not become a curse. The enemy hates; you do not organize your soul around hatred. The enemy persecutes; you do not let persecution make you a mirror image of the persecutor. Enemy-love is not passivity. It is disciplined freedom from revenge.

It also requires strength because it holds together two truths that are easier to separate: evil is real, and the evildoer remains a creature before God. Sentimentality denies the first. Vengeance denies the second. Jesus denies neither.

This does **not** mean staying in danger, enabling abuse, abandoning justice, or refusing to protect the vulnerable. Love of enemy may include boundaries, truth-telling, legal accountability, resistance, and distance. But it forbids hatred as the final posture of the heart. It forbids the desire that the enemy simply be destroyed rather than redeemed, restrained, or made right.

The most scandalous part of the passage is that Jesus grounds ethics in imitation of God. The Father “maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good.” God’s goodness is not rationed according to moral desert. To be God’s children, then, is to resemble that generosity in human form.

So enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it is not merely difficult behavior. It is a different vision of reality. It says that justice without hatred is possible, that mercy is not weakness, and that the highest moral strength is the ability to confront evil without being spiritually conquered by it.
