# Discernment vs. Condemnation: The Crucial Difference

## What the verse *isn't* saying

It's not claiming that moral distinctions vanish—that theft equals charity, or cruelty equals kindness. A parent discerning that their child needs discipline isn't violating this teaching. Neither is a judge determining guilt, or a person recognizing that a relationship is harmful.

## The actual distinction

**Discernment** = seeing clearly what something *is*
- "This action caused harm"
- "This pattern is destructive"
- Practical, often humble, oriented toward understanding or necessary response

**Condemnation** = pronouncing final judgment on a *person's soul*
- "You are irredeemable"
- "Your moral worth is settled"
- Definitive, often self-righteous, closes the door on mercy

## Why this matters practically

The appetite to condemn typically involves:
- **Certainty about interior motives** you can't actually see
- **Permanence**—treating current failures as destiny
- **Distance**—judging from assumed moral superiority
- **Satisfaction**—the emotional payoff of finding someone guilty

Discernment can coexist with:
- Uncertainty about motivations
- Hope for change
- Recognition of your own capacity for the same failures
- Reluctance—doing it because the situation requires it

## The reciprocal structure

"Ye shall not be judged" isn't reward/punishment—it's describing how judgment *works*. When you cultivate the habit of condemning, you train yourself to see people as fixed and guilty. You'll naturally expect to be seen the same way. Conversely, mercy practiced toward others creates the conditions where you might receive it.
