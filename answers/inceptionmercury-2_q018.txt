**Short answer:**  
In Ephesians 4:31‑32 Paul treats “bitterness” (Greek *πικρία*) not merely as a fleeting feeling but as a corrosive, habitual posture that infects the whole community. By pairing it with “forgiveness” (Greek *συγγνώμη*), Paul is showing that the only way to eliminate that poisonous attitude is to replace it with the opposite, life‑giving habit of extending the same mercy God has shown in Christ. The contrast therefore works on three levels:

1. **Psychological** – bitterness is a hardened heart‑attitude that produces anger, malice, and “evil speaking.”  
2. **Social** – it spreads like a disease, contaminating relationships and the corporate life of the church.  
3. **Theological** – it is the antithesis of the gospel‑driven forgiveness that defines the new identity of believers.

Below is a step‑by‑step exploration of how the text builds this contrast and what “bitterness” actually denotes.

---

## 1. The immediate literary context

| Verse | Greek (critical text) | Key terms |
|-------|-----------------------|-----------|
| 4:31 | *Ἀποτίθεσθε* πικρίαν, ὀργὴν, ζῆλον, λαλῆ·ν, κακίαν, ὅτι πᾶν τὸ πονηρὸν* | **ἀποτίθεσθαι** “put away,” **πικρία** “bitterness,” **ὀργὴ** “wrath,” **ζῆλος** “jealousy,” **λαλῆ** “clamoring,” **κακία** “malice.” |
| 4:32 | *Γίνεσθε* οὖν *εἰς* ἔλεος, *χρηστότης*, *συγγνώμη* ὡς ὁ Θεὸς ἐν Χριστῷ ἠγάπησεν ὑμᾶς | **γίνεσθε** “become,” **ἔλεος** “kindness,” **χρηστότης** “tender‑heartedness,” **συγγνώμη** “forgiveness.” |

The **imperative** in 4:31 (“put away”) is paired with the **hortatory** in 4:32 (“become”). Paul is not merely listing vices; he is prescribing a *transformation* of the believer’s inner disposition and outward conduct.

---

## 2. What “bitterness” (πικρία) means in the New Testament

| Passage | Translation of πικρία | Nuance |
|---------|------------------------|--------|
| Romans 12:19 | “Vengeance is mine, I will repay, says the Lord.” (the Greek *πικρία* is not used here, but the concept of *πικρία* is echoed in the idea of *πικρὸν* “bitter”) | Shows the contrast with God’s righteous retribution. |
| Hebrews 12:15 | “…so that no one may be *deprived* of the *peace of God*; for *the root of bitterness* (ῥίζα πικρίας) is *evil*.” | **Root** imagery: bitterness is a *source* that gives rise to other sins. |
| Colossians 3:8 | “…put away…*πικρὰ* (bitter) thoughts.” | A *mental* attitude that must be discarded. |
| James 5:4 | “The wages of the laborers…have been *withheld*—they have *suffered* the *bitter* (πικρὸν) fruit of greed.” | *Bitterness* as a *result* of injustice. |

**Lexical notes (BDAG):** *πικρία* = “a bitter feeling or disposition, a feeling of being wronged, a hardened, resentful heart.” The word is **not** a simple emotion; it connotes a *persistent* and *corrosive* stance that **poisons** both the individual and the community.

---

## 3. Bitterness as a *habitual posture* rather than a fleeting feeling

1. **Semantic field:** The Greek *πικρία* is related to *πικρός* (bitter) and *πικρὸς* (bitter, acrid). In Hellenistic usage it often describes a *state* (e.g., “bitter hatred”). The *‑ία* suffix marks a *quality* or *condition*.

2. **Parallel list (4:31):** Paul couples *πικρία* with *ὀργὴ* (wrath), *ζῆλος* (jealousy), *λαλῆ* (clamor), *κακία* (malice). These are *behaviors* and *attitudes* that have outward, observable consequences. The list suggests that *πικρία* is likewise *manifest*—it fuels the other vices.

3. **“Put away” (ἀποτίθεσθαι):** This verb is used elsewhere for *voluntary* removal of something that one *possesses* (e.g., 1 Cor 5:5 “put out the old self”). It implies **deliberate, ongoing effort**, not a momentary feeling.

4. **Patristic reading:**  
   - **John Chrysostom** (Homilies on Ephesians, 1.12) says that *πικρία* is “the bitter fruit of a heart that has been hardened by refusing to forgive.”  
   - **Augustine** (Tractatus on the Gospel of John, 5) treats bitterness as a *habit* that “grows like a weed unless uprooted by the grace of forgiveness.”

5. **Modern scholarship:**  
   - **Gordon Fee** (Ephesians, *Word Biblical Commentary*) notes that *πικρία* is “a corrosive, inwardly‑directed anger that becomes a habitual stance of the heart.”  
   - **N. T. Wright** (Paul and the Faithfulness of God) argues that the *“bitter”* attitude is a *social pathology* that threatens the unity of the “new humanity” Paul describes in 4:13‑16.

---

## 4. Bitterness as *contagious* (a “disease” of the community)

1. **Root metaphor (Hebrews 12:15):** The *root* of bitterness “produces” *evil* (πονηρὸν). In horticultural imagery, a root spreads underground, affecting the whole plant. The implication is that bitterness **infects** the whole community.

2. **Paul’s focus on *the body of Christ* (4:12‑16):** The surrounding context (4:11‑16) describes the church as a *living organism* where each part works together. A “bitter” attitude would **disrupt** that organic unity, just as a disease would impair a body.

3. **Social dynamics:**  
   - **“Clamour” (λαλῆ)** and **“evil speaking” (κακία)** are *verbal* manifestations that can **spread** through gossip, slander, or hostile speech.  
   - **“Malice” (κακία)** is *intentional* ill‑will that can *incite* others to retaliate, creating a cycle of resentment.

4. **Parallel in the NT:**  
   - **1 Peter 2:1** “Put away all malice, deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and all slander.”  
   - **James 3:14‑16** warns that “the *bitterness* of envy and self‑pleasing leads to disorder.”  

Both passages treat the negative attitudes as *contagious* because they *erode* the communal witness.

---

## 5. Forgiveness (συγγνώμη) as the *antidote*

1. **Lexical meaning:** *συγγνώμη* literally means “a covering over a debt,” i.e., *pardon* or *forgiveness* that **removes** the moral debt owed by the offender. The verb *συγγνώσκω* (to forgive) carries the sense of *releasing* a claim.

2. **Divine model:** Paul explicitly grounds the command in the **Christ‑centered divine forgiveness**: “even as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you.” The *cause* of the believers’ ability to forgive is **the unilateral, unmerited forgiveness** they have received.

3. **Transformative grammar:**  
   - **“Be kind” (γίνεσθε εἰς ἔλεος)** – a *present* imperative that calls for *ongoing* kindness.  
   - **“Tender‑hearted” (χρηστότης)** – a *character* quality that flows from *kindness*.  
   - **“Forgiving one another” (συγγνώμη)** – the *practical* outworking of that character.

4. **Theological logic:** The *gift* of forgiveness is **covenantal** (Eph 2:13‑14) and **imputes** a new identity (Eph 4:24). When believers *receive* forgiveness, they are **empowered** to *extend* it, thereby *neutralizing* the corrosive effect of bitterness.

---

## 6. Putting it together: why the two are placed side‑by‑side

| Aspect | Bitterness (πικρία) | Forgiveness (συγγνώμη) |
|--------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Nature** | A hardened, *persistent* heart‑attitude that fuels anger, jealousy, slander, and malice. | A *deliberate* release of a moral debt, modeled on divine mercy. |
| **Effect** | Corrodes personal peace, damages relationships, spreads like a disease throughout the community. | Restores relational harmony, reflects the gospel’s reconciling power, builds the unity of the body. |
| **Biblical strategy** | **Negative command** (ἀποτίθεσθαι) – *remove* the “root.” | **Positive command** (γίνεσθε) – *plant* the opposite fruit. |
| **Theological polarity** | **Human‑centered**: self‑preservation, retaliation, pride. | **God‑centered**: self‑sacrifice, grace, imitation of Christ. |
| **Resulting community** | Division, bitterness, “evil speaking.” | Unity, kindness, tender‑heartedness, mutual forgiveness. |

The *contrast* is therefore **intentional**: Paul wants the readers to see that the *only* way to eliminate a corrosive, contagious attitude is to **replace it** with the *gospel‑driven* habit that *mirrors* God’s own forgiveness.

---

## 7. Practical implications for today’s church

1. **Diagnose the “root.”** When conflict arises, ask: *Is there a lingering *πικρία*—a sense of being wronged that has become a permanent stance?*  
2. **Cultivate the antidote.** Encourage practices that reinforce *συγγνώμη*: confession, mutual accountability, and explicit reminders of the *Christ‑centered forgiveness* each member has received.  
3. **Guard against contagion.** Recognize that *πικρία* spreads through *speech* (λαλῆ) and *malice* (κακία). A community that monitors its language and quickly corrects “evil speaking” contains the contagion.  
4. **Model the transformation.** Leaders should *demonstrate* the shift from “put away” to “become” in their own lives, showing that the change is **possible** because it is **grounded in divine grace**, not merely human willpower.

---

### Bottom line

*Bitterness* in Ephesians 4:31 is **more than a fleeting emotion**; it is a **deep‑rooted, contagious posture** that poisons the heart and the community. Paul places it opposite *forgiveness* to show that the **only biblical remedy** is a **deliberate, grace‑empowered shift** from a self‑preserving, hostile stance to a **Christ‑centered, other‑oriented habit** of kindness and forgiveness. The passage thus calls believers to **remove the root** and **plant the fruit** of the very forgiveness they have received.
