The passage portrays the **heart as a hidden treasury** and speech as what is drawn from it.

- **“Heart”** in biblical usage is not merely emotion; it includes desires, judgments, loyalties, intentions, and will.
- **“Treasure”** means a stored-up supply. A person accumulates habits, loves, resentments, convictions, and purposes within.
- **“Out of the abundance”** pictures speech as an overflow: what fills the inner person eventually comes out.
- **“Bringeth forth”** suggests actively drawing from that storehouse and putting its contents into circulation.

Thus words are **all three**:

1. **Symptoms** — They are outward effects of an inward moral condition, like fruit revealing the kind of tree (vv. 33–34).
2. **Choices** — Speaking is still a morally accountable act. Jesus immediately says people will answer for their words (vv. 36–37); the heart’s influence does not remove responsibility.
3. **Revelations** — Words disclose what is otherwise hidden. Especially over time, habitual speech reveals what a person values, trusts, loves, or resents.

The saying should not be taken to mean that every isolated word gives a complete picture of someone’s heart, or that an evil person can never utter something superficially correct. Rather, the **characteristic flow and moral direction of speech** reveal the dominant contents of the heart. In context, the Pharisees’ accusation against Jesus is not treated as an accidental slip; it exposes a deeper hostility toward what is good.

The practical implication is that lasting reform of speech requires more than verbal restraint. The “treasury” itself must be changed and filled with what is good—though restraint and deliberate speech remain part of moral obedience.
