The verse is frequently weaponized into something it isn't. "Judge not" doesn't suspend moral reality—it addresses a specific *kind* of judging. The difference is worth tracing carefully.

## What the verse actually prohibits

Notice the structure. Luke 6:37 isn't a single prohibition but a quartet of paired actions:

- Judge not → you shall not be judged
- Condemn not → you shall not be condemned
- Forgive → you shall be forgiven
- Give → it shall be given to you

The judging being prohibited is **condemnatory and unilateral**—the kind that pronounces sentence, forecloses redemption, and assumes the position of final arbiter. It's judging without the humility to imagine oneself also under judgment. Note the reciprocity in verse 38: *the measure you give will be the measure you get.* This presupposes that moral judgments have real weight; it simply warns that a harsh one rebounds.

If "judge not" meant "moral judgments never matter," the rest of the passage becomes incoherent. You cannot *forgive* except by first recognizing that something genuinely wrong was done. Forgiveness is not a vague niceness—it is a moral act that requires discernment about what is being pardoned.

## Discernment: a Christian virtue

The New Testament is emphatic that moral perception is required of the mature:

- Hebrews 5:14: solid food is for the mature, "whose faculties are trained by constant practice to distinguish good from evil."
- 1 Thessalonians 5:21: "test everything; hold fast what is good."
- 1 John 4:1: "test the spirits to see whether they are from God."

And in the very next section of Luke 6, Jesus says to take the log out of your own eye *so that you can see clearly to take the speck out of your brother's* (6:42). He assumes the speck needs to be addressed—that moral correction is real, loving, and necessary. He forbids the *log*, not the *speck*.

Discernment is the capacity to perceive moral reality accurately. It is *krisis* in its healthy sense—sifting, distinguishing, weighing. It can be exercised gently (1 Peter 4:8, "love covers a multitude of sins," is not the same as pretending no sins exist) and it can produce sharp words when sharp words are needed (Jesus himself denouncing the Pharisees is recorded in the same Gospels that record "judge not").

## The appetite to condemn: something else entirely

What the verse forbids is closer to what we might call the *censorious will*—the drive not merely to see moral fault but to crush the person who carries it. A few features distinguish it from discernment:

- **It is projective.** People who condemn fiercely often cannot face the same fault in themselves. Theologians from Augustine to the Desert Fathers noticed that harsh judgment of others is frequently a displaced self-loathing or a defense against one's own unexamined life.
- **It is final.** Condemnation forecloses. It does not leave room for repentance, growth, or surprise. It pronounces the verdict the Divine Judge reserves for himself (cf. James 4:12: "There is one lawgiver and judge, he who is able to save and to destroy").
- **It is asymmetrical.** It applies a stricter standard to others than to oneself. This is the log.
- **It enjoys the act.** The appetite to condemn has a particular flavor of pleasure—moral elevation through another's diminishment. Discernment does not.

## A useful analogy

A physician diagnosing a disease is exercising discernment. The same physician, declaring a patient irredeemable and refusing treatment while savoring the diagnosis, has crossed into the appetite to condemn. Diagnosis and death-sentence are different acts, even when the underlying perception is identical.

Or, in the language of the courtroom: a fair judge weighs evidence, allows for defense, and submits to appeal. A prosecutor who wants only conviction, regardless of evidence, has abandoned judgment for the appetite to condemn.

## Why the distinction matters

If you collapse the two, you land in one of two bad places:

1. **Moral nihilism**—the position that no judgments are valid, that everything is equally fine. This makes ethics impossible, including the very ethic of forgiveness and love the verse enjoins. It also leads to a quiet cruelty, because refusing to name injustice is a way of siding with it.

2. **Moral sadism**—the position that everyone is guilty and someone must pronounce it. This makes community impossible, because every relationship becomes a tribunal.

The narrow path the verse is carving is harder than either: *perceive clearly, speak truthfully, but leave the final verdict to the One whose judgment is untainted by self-interest.* You can call a thing what it is without declaring a person irredeemable. You can warn without crushing. You can correct without performing the role of God.

In short, the verse does not say moral judgments don't matter. It says they matter so much that we should not dare to wield them carelessly—and that the only safe way to wield them at all is alongside a posture of self-suspicion and readiness to forgive.
