
The teaching in Luke 6:37, "Judge not, and ye shall not be judged," is often misunderstood as advocating for moral relativism or the dismissal of ethical distinctions. But it points to a deeper distinction: the difference between **discernment** (wise, compassionate discernment of good and evil) and **the appetite to condemn** (a judgmental attitude rooted in self-righteousness or vengeance). Here’s how these two concepts diverge:

---

### 1. **Discernment vs. Condemnation**  
- **Discernment** is a moral faculty that evaluates actions and motivations without dehumanizing others. It involves recognizing wrongdoing or harm while maintaining humility about one’s own fallibility. For example, a parent might discern that their child’s behavior is harmful and address it with correction, not cruelty.  
- **The appetite to condemn**, however, is a prideful eagerness to judge others—and often to punish them—for their failings. This impulse can stem from unresolved shame, insecurity, or a need to assert moral superiority. Condemnation narrows empathy and justifies cruelty, whereas discernment seeks understanding and growth.

---

### 2. **Why Moral Judgments *Do* Matter**  
The verse does *not* say moral judgments are irrelevant. Without discernment, society would collapse into chaos, as we need ethical frameworks to distinguish justice from injustice. The problem arises when judgment becomes **absolute** (e.g., declaring someone irredeemable) or **self-serving** (e.g., avoiding self-examination while obsessing over others’ flaws).  

Jesus’ words emphasize the **ethical "return"** of one’s actions: refusing to judge or condemn is tied to the reciprocal promise of avoiding such treatment oneself. This underscores the interconnectedness of human relationships—a call to recognize that we, too, are flawed and dependent on mercy.

---

### 3. **The Role of Forgiveness**  
The command to "forgive, and ye shall be forgiven" (Luke 6:37) is not about excusing wrongdoing but about **breaking cycles of retribution**. Forgiveness acknowledges harm without erasing accountability but reframes the response from condemnation to restoration. This reflects a deeper form of discernment: prioritizing healing over vengeance, even when justice may demand consequences.

---

### 4. **The Danger of the "Appetite to Condemn"**  
The desire to condemn often masks unresolved projections of our own guilt. Psychologically, this can lead to scapegoating, dehumanization, and abuse—the very things that Luke 6:37 warns against. By contrast, discernment asks:  
- *"Do I judge this person with humility and awareness of my own faults?"*  
- *"Does my criticism aim to heal or to destroy?"*  
- *"Am I condemning an action or a whole person?"*

---

### 5. **A Balance of Wisdom and Mercy**  
Christian theology (and many ethical traditions) stresses that discernment must be paired with mercy. This is the *via media* between Pharisaic legalism (harsh judging) and moral relativism (ignoring wrongs). It’s the difference between:  
- **Saying**: *"Your actions are wrong, but you are still worthy of love and redemption,"* and  
- **Saying**: *"Your actions are wrong, and therefore you deserve no compassion."*

---

### Conclusion  
Luke 6:37 does not nullify the necessity of moral judgment but critiques the *habit* of judgment rooted in a destructive appetite to condemn. True discernment allows us to call out injustice while reflecting humility, empathy, and a commitment to forgiveness. This balance protects both the integrity of moral truth and the dignity of every person, acknowledging that all humans are fallible—and worthy of grace.
