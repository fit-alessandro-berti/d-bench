In Luke 18:9–14 the Pharisee is condemned **not for having “good works” in themselves**, but for *how* he uses them and what his heart is doing while he performs them.

### What exactly condemns the Pharisee?
The passage points to several overlapping issues, but the **main condemned feature is self-justifying pride expressed through comparison and contempt**, with **virtue turned into self-display**.

1. **Trusted in himself that he was righteous** (the parable’s explicit setup in v.9).  
   That frames the Pharisee’s entire prayer as reliance on self rather than repentance.

2. **Comparison** (“not as other men are … or even as this publican”).  
   The Pharisee defines his standing before God by contrasting himself with others.

3. **Contempt / despising others** (the parable’s stated target: v.9, “despised others”).  
   Even if the Pharisee doesn’t use an insult word-for-word, his “or even as this publican” posture is contemptuous.

4. **Self-display through “virtues.”**  
   He lists fasting, tithes, and moral abstentions—yet the prayer functions like a résumé before God: *Look at my superiority*.  
   This isn’t “false doctrine” so much as **a misuse of true religious actions** for boasting and proving righteousness.

So among your options:  
- **False doctrine:** not really the point.  
- **Pride:** yes.  
- **Comparison:** yes.  
- **Contempt:** yes (tied to “despised others”).  
- **Use of virtue as self-display:** yes (virtues become evidence for his self-righteousness).

**Conclusion:** The strongest “condemnation” is **self-righteous pride expressed by comparison and despising, with virtue used as self-display to claim justification.**

---

### Why is the tax collector’s posture spiritually truer?
The tax collector’s physical stance (“standing afar off,” not lifting his eyes, striking his breast) matches the spiritual reality the parable highlights:

- **He does not presume standing with God.**  
  He won’t “look up” as though he has rights or moral leverage.
- **He practices confession and need.**  
  “God be merciful to me, a sinner” is the opposite of self-justification. He approaches God as someone who must receive mercy.
- **Humility produces receptivity.**  
  The parable’s logic is that justification comes to the one who recognizes his condition and asks for mercy, not to the one who tries to earn it by self-comparison.

In short, the tax collector’s posture is “truer” because it symbolizes (and expresses) **repentant humility and dependence on God’s mercy**, whereas the Pharisee’s posture symbolizes **confident self-reliance and moral boasting**.
