In the context of Ezekiel 36:26–27, the transition from a "stony heart" to a "heart of flesh" represents the shift from the Old Covenant (relying on human effort to follow external laws) to the New Covenant (inward transformation by God).

Here is a breakdown of the differences between external compliance and a "new heart," and why the condition of the heart is described in material terms.

### 1. External Compliance vs. The New Heart

The distinction lies in the **source of the behavior** and the **nature of the motivation.**

*   **External Compliance (Duty and Pressure):** This is characterized by following rules based on social pressure, fear of punishment, or a sense of cold obligation. Under external compliance, the person’s desires remain unchanged; they may obey the law while secretly hating it or wishing they could break it without consequence. It is "law from the outside in."
*   **The New Heart (Desire and Instinct):** Receiving a new heart means that God alters the "internal OS" of the person. The law is no longer a list of rules carved on stone tablets (like the Ten Commandments), but a desire woven into the human personality. Obedience becomes the natural byproduct of a new nature. It is "life from the inside out."

In verse 27, God says he will "cause you to walk in my statutes." This implies that the motivation for righteousness is no longer a burden to be carried, but a fueled engine within the person.

### 2. Hardness as a "Material Condition"

The use of "stone" versus "flesh" is a powerful physiological metaphor used to describe the **receptivity** of the human soul.

**The Stony Heart: Dead and Impermeable**
Stone is the imagery of spiritual death. Ezekiel describes it as a material condition for several reasons:
*   **Unresponsiveness:** A stone does not feel pain, warmth, or pressure. It is "calloused." A stony heart is a heart that has become indifferent to the voice of God or the needs of others. No matter how much "water" (word/truth) you pour on a stone, it never absorbs it; it only runs off the surface.
*   **Inflexible:** A stone cannot be reshaped; it can only be broken or discarded. This represents a human will that has become so hardened in its own ways that it is incapable of repentance or growth.
*   **Static:** Stone is cold and inorganic. It lacks the "breath" or "spirit" required for life.

**The Heart of Flesh: Living and Responsive**
In this context, "flesh" does not refer to "carnality" or sin (as it often does in the New Testament), but rather to **organic life.**
*   **Sensitivity:** A heart of flesh has nerves; it can feel. It is sensitive to God’s conviction and responsive to His touch.
*   **Pliability:** Unlike stone, flesh is soft and can be molded. A heart of flesh is a "teachable" heart that is willing to be shaped by divine influence.
*   **Absorption:** Living tissue can absorb nutrients. A heart of flesh "takes in" the spirit of God, allowing it to permeate the person's entire being.

### Why Must it be Replaced?
The text suggests that the human condition is not merely "damaged"—it is **functionally dead.** You cannot "fix" a stone to make it act like a heart; you cannot perform surgery on a rock to give it a pulse. 

By describing the condition as a material reality, Ezekiel emphasizes that **self-reformation is impossible.** If the heart is made of stone, the human owner cannot change its substance. Therefore, God must act as a surgeon, performing a "transplant"—removing the old nature entirely and replacing it with something capable of life.
