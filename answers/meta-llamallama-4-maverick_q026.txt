This passage, Matthew 12:34-35, connects speech with the inner moral character or the "treasure of the heart" by suggesting that the words a person speaks are directly related to their inner moral state. Let's analyze the passage to understand how it views the relationship between speech and the heart's moral condition.

### Speech as a Reflection of the Heart

The passage begins with Jesus addressing his audience as a "generation of vipers," a metaphor for being wicked or evil, and questions how they, being evil, can speak good things (Matthew 12:34). The underlying premise is that their speech should reflect their true nature. Jesus then explains that "out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh" (Matthew 12:34). This statement implies that the content of one's speech is derived from the condition or the "abundance" of their heart. In biblical terminology, the "heart" often refers to the center of a person's being, encompassing their thoughts, feelings, and moral character.

### The Heart as a Treasure

The passage further elaborates on this idea by using the metaphor of a "treasure of the heart." It states that a "good man out of the good treasure of the heart bringeth forth good things," and conversely, "an evil man out of the evil treasure bringeth forth evil things" (Matthew 12:35). Here, the heart is likened to a storehouse or treasury that contains either good or evil. The nature of what is stored in this inner treasure determines what is produced or expressed outwardly.

### Interpretation: Symptoms, Choices, or Revelations?

1. **Symptoms**: The passage suggests that words are symptoms of the heart's condition. Just as a symptom indicates an underlying health issue, the words we speak indicate the state of our inner being. The speech is a manifestation or an outward sign of what is internally present.

2. **Choices**: While the passage emphasizes the heart's condition as the source of our words, it can be inferred that the choices we make about what to store in our "heart's treasure" influence our speech. Thus, in a broader interpretation, it implies that our daily choices either to nurture good or evil within us will be reflected in our speech.

3. **Revelations**: The passage clearly indicates that speech is a revelation of the heart's true state. What we speak reveals what is within us. It suggests that our words are not accidental but are revelatory of our inner character.

### Conclusion

In conclusion, this passage suggests that words are all three: symptoms of the heart's condition, results of the choices we have made regarding what to harbor within, and revelations of our true inner state. The connection made between speech and the heart underscores the importance of cultivating a good and righteous inner character, as it inevitably influences our outward expressions and actions.
