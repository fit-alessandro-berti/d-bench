Pride is linked to **blindness** because it corrupts the faculties by which a person recognizes danger, limitation, and fault. The proud person does not merely do wrong; he becomes less able to see that he is wrong. A “haughty spirit” tends to:

- exaggerate its own competence and control,
- minimize dependence on God and others,
- interpret correction as insult,
- surround itself with flattery rather than truth,
- and treat warnings as weakness or opposition.

Thus blindness becomes the mechanism of collapse. Because pride resists correction, mistakes compound until “destruction” or “a fall” exposes the weakness that pride had concealed. The proverb is not simply saying, “Pride deserves punishment,” but also, “Pride makes ruin more likely from within.” In biblical wisdom, that inward dynamic also belongs to God’s moral ordering of the world: self-exaltation invites abasement.

The contrast in verse 19 deepens the point. To be “of an humble spirit with the lowly” means accepting a modest place, truthful self-knowledge, teachability, and solidarity with those who possess little status. The alternative—“to divide the spoil with the proud”—offers prestige, wealth, and the appearance of victory, but it joins one to people whose success rests on domination and is therefore unstable.

This reveals several features of arrogance’s moral psychology:

1. **It values rank over reality.** The proud would rather appear victorious than actually be wise or good.
2. **It treats relationships instrumentally.** Others become rivals, admirers, or partners in “spoil,” rather than persons from whom one may learn.
3. **It cannot tolerate dependence.** Humility admits need; pride experiences need as humiliation.
4. **It confuses temporary success with moral vindication.** Possessing the spoil seems to prove superiority, even when it prepares the way for ruin.
5. **It isolates itself from corrective truth.** The lowly can receive wisdom because they do not have a grand self-image to defend.

The proverb therefore presents humility not as low self-esteem, but as **accurate, receptive self-knowledge**. Better to share the vulnerable position of the lowly and remain open to truth than to share the proud person’s triumph while also sharing the blindness that will bring it down.
