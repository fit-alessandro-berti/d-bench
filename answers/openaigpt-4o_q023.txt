In Isaiah 55:6–7, the call to repentance involves both the "way" and "thoughts" of an individual, highlighting the comprehensive nature of true repentance. Let's break down what this implies about outward conduct, inward imagination, and the possibility of mercy:

1. **Outward Conduct ("way"):** The "way" refers to the external actions and behaviors of an individual. Repentance requires a change in one's conduct—abandoning sinful or unethical actions and choosing a path that aligns with righteousness and divine standards. This suggests that genuine repentance is not simply a matter of changed thoughts or intentions, but must be visible in the way a person lives and behaves.

2. **Inward Imagination ("thoughts"):** The focus on "thoughts" underscores the importance of inner transformation, indicating that repentance is more than mere behavioral change. It involves a shift in one's mindset, desires, and intentions. Inner thoughts and motivations often drive outward actions, so a change in thought patterns is essential for genuine transformation. This mirrors the biblical view that true repentance and holiness begin within the heart and mind.

3. **Possibility of Mercy:** The passage emphasizes God's readiness to show mercy and pardon to those who genuinely repent. By including both "way" and "thoughts," the text assures readers that repentance is holistic and accessible. No matter the depth of one’s sin, divine mercy is abundant for those who sincerely forsake their wrong ways and unrighteous thoughts to return to God.

Overall, the inclusion of both “way” and “thoughts” in the call to repentance highlights the necessity for a comprehensive transformation that encompasses both external actions and internal attitudes. It reinforces that genuine repentance is integral to receiving God's mercy and pardon.
