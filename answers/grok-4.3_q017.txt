Light is hated because it strips away the protective cover that darkness provides. In the passage, the "light" is not merely information or moral teaching—it is the penetrating revelation of reality as it actually is, centered in Christ. When that light arrives, it does two things at once: it illuminates what was hidden, and it judges what it illuminates. Most people do not primarily fear *seeing* the truth; they fear *being seen by* it.

### Exposure and the Loss of Control

Darkness allows deeds to exist without being named. A person can maintain a private narrative in which their actions are justified, necessary, or at least not as bad as they appear. Light collapses that narrative by making the deed public—not necessarily to other people, but to reality itself. Once something is truly seen, it can no longer be treated as a private matter between a person and their own rationalizations. The deed now stands in relation to what is true, and that relation often exposes a gap between who someone claims to be and what they have actually done.

This is why the text says people "neither cometh to the light." It is not that they are indifferent to truth; they actively avoid the conditions under which truth can do its work. The avoidance is strategic. Exposure threatens the ability to continue the behavior without internal contradiction.

### Shame and the Fear of Being Known

Shame is the emotional consequence of unwanted exposure. It is not merely guilt (which can be about a specific act) but the deeper sense that one's self has been seen in its actual condition and found wanting. The desire "not to be seen clearly" is therefore a desire to preserve a certain version of the self—the version that can still negotiate with its own conscience. 

When people sense that their deeds will not survive examination, they often prefer the darkness where those deeds can remain ambiguous. Ambiguity is protective. It allows someone to say, "It's complicated," or "Everyone does it," or "I had my reasons." Light removes that ambiguity. It forces the deed to be evaluated according to its actual nature rather than according to the story told about it.

This dynamic explains why some people become hostile toward clarity itself. The hostility is not really about the content of the truth but about what the truth does to their self-conception. They would rather live with a distorted self-image than risk the pain of having the distortion corrected.

### The Counter-Movement

The passage contrasts this with those who "doeth truth." Notice the phrasing: it is not merely those who *believe* the truth or *know* the truth, but those whose manner of living is aligned with it. For them, coming into the light is not threatening because their deeds are already "wrought in God"—that is, they have their origin and meaning in something outside themselves. When such deeds are exposed, the exposure confirms rather than condemns. The light does not create new shame; it reveals the source from which the actions came.

This suggests that the hatred of light is not ultimately about morality in the abstract, but about the location of one's identity. If a person's sense of self is built on hidden or compromised ground, light is an existential threat. If their identity is rooted elsewhere, light becomes clarifying rather than destructive.

In short, light is hated when people have more invested in remaining unseen than in being aligned with what is real. The darkness is not preferred for its own sake, but as the necessary condition for preserving a self that cannot bear examination.
