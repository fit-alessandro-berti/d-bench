The passage in Genesis 4:6–7 presents a nuanced and profound picture of moral agency, combining several of the metaphors you mention—though the text is particularly rich in its imagery of **sin as an external presence (a beast or threat) that nonetheless remains subject to human authority**.

### The Picture of Moral Agency
1. **Sin as an External Beast or Temptation**:  
   The phrase *"sin lieth at the door"* uses vivid imagery—sin is described as a crouching animal, a predatory force waiting to attack. This suggests evil is not merely an internal impulse but a real, active presence outside the person, lying in wait. Yet it is **at the door**, not inside: it awaits entry but does not yet possess the soul.

2. **Sin as an Impulse to Be Ruled**:  
   The latter part—"*unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him*"—shifts to a different metaphor. Here, "he" likely refers to sin (or Abel, the brother, in a deeper reading). If "he" is sin, then sin has a "desire" (a will to dominate), yet **Cain is commanded to rule over it**. This paints moral agency as a struggle for mastery: sin is personified as an opposing force, but humans are given authority to subdue it.

3. **Evil as Habit or Condition?**:  
   The text does not explicitly call sin a "habit," but the warning implies that sin can become a pattern if not resisted—it "lieth at the door" as if to pounce when one is vulnerable.

### Responsibility in the Text
The key to responsibility lies in the questions the Lord poses to Cain:
- *"Why art thou wroth? and why is thy countenance fallen?"* — Cain is held accountable for his emotional state (anger, resentment).
- *"If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?"* — This affirms that **rightdoing is possible** and brings acceptance.
- *"And if thou doest not well, sin lieth at the door"* — Here, the text seems to offer a warning: sin is near, but its presence does not excuse failure to resist.
- *"And unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him"* — This is the climax of responsibility: **Cain is promised authority over sin's desire**. The responsibility is not removal of the threat, but **mastery over it**.

### Synthesis: What Picture Emerges?
The text draws moral agency as follows:
- **Evil is real and active**, not merely a passive human weakness. It is an external force with its own "desire."
- **Yet humans are not helpless**. They are addressed as responsible agents who can "do well" and "rule over" sin.
- **The heart matters**: Cain's anger and "fallen countenance" show that sin's approach finds a ready soil in the unreconciled heart. The warning is both admonition and offer of empowerment.
- **Responsibility is personal and relational**: Cain is responsible for his response to both God and brother (the "desire" may also hint at Abel's innocent longing for fellowship, which Cain is called to rule over righteously).

### Conclusion
In this narrative, **evil is presented as both impulse and external temptation, but the text emphasizes human authority to resist and rule over it**. The picture of moral agency is one of **active stewardship**: humans must recognize sin's presence, reject its dominion, and exercise the God-given capacity to do well. The tragedy of Cain, of course, is that he fails to rule over the sin that crouches at his door—showing that moral agency is real but not automatic; it requires faithful response to God's invitation.

In short, Genesis 4:6–7 presents a deeply **relational and dynamic model of moral agency**: sin is real, God is present, and the human person is responsible to "do well" and rule over the impulse to evil.
