There is an important distinction between the unrighteous judgment that Jesus warns against in this verse, and the need for moral discernment and accountability. 

Jesus is not saying that we should never use good judgment or hold people accountable for wrongdoing. Rather, he is warning against a condemning spirit - an eagerness to find fault, a quickness to assume the worst about others, a lack of mercy and forgiveness. This passage calls us to examine our own hearts and motives when we make judgments.

Proper moral discernment still matters greatly. We need wisdom to recognize right from wrong, to name evil and injustice when we see it. There are times when difficult truths must be spoken. Parents must hold children accountable, societies must have laws and consequences, individuals must sometimes confront or challenge others. Jesus himself showed righteous anger at times and called out hypocrisy and hard-heartedness.  

The key difference is the posture of the heart. Is it to condemn or to redeem? Fault-finding or healing? Moral discernment wielded with humility says "I too am a sinner in need of forgiveness, but this is not right." It leaves the door open for repentance and reconciliation. But judgment stemming from self-righteousness relishes the chance to look down on others. It rushes to throw the first stone.

Additionally, Jesus links our judgments of others to God's judgment of us. If we cling to bitterness and condemnation, we block the flow of grace in our own lives. But if we release others through forgiveness, we open ourselves to receiving more of God's undeserved mercy as well. There is a spiritual principle at work.

So in summary, Jesus' command not to judge is not a call to ignore all moral standards, but rather a challenge to our eagerness to condemn, our lack of forgiveness, the plank in our own eye. It is a reminder that God is the ultimate Judge, that we all fall short, and that we reap what we sow in how we treat others. Wise discernment wielded with humility and hope is still desperately needed. But self-righteous judgment poisons the one who judges as well.
