Let’s break down the passage and its moral posture step by step:

**The Passage (Galatians 6:1, ESV):**
> “Brethren, if anyone is caught in any transgression, you who are spiritual should restore him in a spirit of gentleness. But watch yourself, or you too will be tempted.”

**Key Elements:**
1. **Restoration ("restore such an one")**: The call is not to judge, condemn, or abandon the offender, but to help bring them back into right relationship with God and the community.
2. **Meekness ("in the spirit of meekness")**: The manner of correction must be gentle, humble, and not self-serving or harsh. Meekness here means a gentle, teachable, non-coercive approach—marked by patience, compassion, and a lack of pride.
3. **Self-suspicion ("considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted")**: The corrector must be aware of their own weaknesses and vulnerabilities. There’s a warning: in the process of correcting others, one may be tempted to stumble or fall into sin oneself.

---

## The Required Moral Posture

The moral posture required here is **humility mixed with vigilance**. It’s not one of superiority or self-righteousness, but of shared brokenness and mutual care.

### 1. Humility and Gentleness (Meekness)
The instruction is to “restore… in the spirit of meekness.” This is not weakness, but strength under control (cf. Matthew 5:5). It means:
- Correcting out of love, not anger or pride.
- Approaching the sinner with compassion, recognizing your own need for grace.
- Avoiding condemnation, which drives people away, and instead offering help and hope.

### 2. Responsibility to Restore
There’s an expectation that those who are spiritually mature (“which are spiritual”) have a duty to help others recover from sin. This is not about legalism or performance, but about the church’s role in healing and reconciliation.

### 3. Self-Awareness and Caution (Self-suspicion)
The phrase “considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted” is a warning:
- Even those who correct others are not immune to temptation.
- Moral superiority is dangerous; the act of correcting can make one complacent or judgmental.
- One must examine their own heart, motives, and vulnerabilities, lest they fall into the same sin or become prideful.

---

## Why Are These Linked?

**Restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion are kept together because true correction is not about power, but about love and shared brokenness.**

- **Meekness ensures the correction is done in love and not in arrogance or self-righteousness.** If you correct harshly, you may wound, not heal.
- **Restoration is the goal, not punishment or exclusion.** The community is to be a healing body, not a court of law.
- **Self-suspicion is a safeguard.** The corrector must not forget their own need for forgiveness and must guard against becoming judgmental or prideful. This keeps the process humble and healthy.

In summary, the required moral posture is **humble, loving, and self-aware correction**, recognizing that all are sinners in need of grace. The combination of restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion ensures that correction is carried out in a way that builds up rather than tears down, and that protects the corrector from spiritual pride and failure.
