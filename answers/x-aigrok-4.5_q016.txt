The elder brother is angry (v. 28), feels envy and a wounded sense of fairness (vv. 29–30), and is openly proud of his record of obedience and service. Yet none of these is fundamental.  

His real complaint is not that the father threw a party, but that the father threw a *free* party for someone who had done nothing to earn it. The elder brother’s entire self-understanding is built on performance: “these many years I have been slaving for you and never disobeyed.” Grace that is given without reference to desert therefore threatens the whole moral economy he has constructed. He cannot rejoice in the father’s mercy because mercy itself—unconditional, unearned welcome—renders his years of scrupulous obedience practically worthless as currency.  

That is why he stays outside: he refuses to enter a household whose defining joy is the recovery of the undeserving. The deepest problem is therefore discomfort with mercy itself.

Final answer: discomfort with mercy itself
