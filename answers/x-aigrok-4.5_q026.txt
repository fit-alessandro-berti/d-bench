**Words are revelations and symptoms of the heart’s stored treasures; they also involve moral choice, but the passage treats them primarily as inevitable outflow rather than unconstrained free inventions.**

### Connection between speech and the heart’s hidden stockpiles
Jesus’ imagery is direct and dual:

- **Abundance / overflow** (“out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh”): Speech is not a detached skill or social performance; it is the natural surplus that spills from whatever fills the inner person. The heart is pictured as a reservoir or storehouse whose contents eventually surface through the mouth.
- **Treasure** (“good treasure \ldots evil treasure”): The heart holds accumulated moral capital—what has been stored, valued, and cultivated over time. A good person brings forth good things; an evil person brings forth evil things. The verbs “bringeth forth” emphasize production and disclosure of what is already present inside. Speech therefore functions as an external transaction that withdraws from this internal moral inventory.

In short, the “hidden moral stockpiles” are the decisive reality; words are the public evidence and expression of them. Jesus’ rhetorical “how can ye, being evil, speak good things?” underscores that the quality of speech cannot finally be separated from, or better than, the character of the reservoir from which it is drawn.

### Symptoms, choices, revelations—or all three?
The passage treats words as **all three**, with clear ranking of emphasis:

- **Revelations** (primary): Speech discloses the heart’s store. The treasure metaphor is about manifestation—“bringeth forth” makes the inner content visible and audible. Listeners learn the moral state of the speaker by what comes out of the mouth.
- **Symptoms** (equally primary): Because speech overflows from the abundance of the heart, evil speech is diagnostic of an evil heart (and good speech of a good one). Like a medical symptom, the words are not the root disease; they signal the underlying condition.
- **Choices** (real but constrained): People still “speak” and “bring forth”—agency and moral responsibility remain. Yet the choices are not free-floating options that can invent goodness independent of character. The heart’s stored treasure sets the range of possibilities; an evil heart cannot consistently produce good speech. Choice operates *within* and as an expression of the heart rather than in isolation from it.

Thus the text does **not** present speech as pure autonomous decision-making that might magically produce virtue regardless of motive and disposition. It presents speech as the self-revealing syndrome of the heart’s actual moral capital. This is why Jesus can call the Pharisees a “generation of vipers” and immediately move from their words to the impossibility of their being good while remaining evil: the words are symptoms and revelations of the treasure; the choices are real, yet they proceed from and confirm that treasure.

### Practical theological force
The passage therefore presses for heart-level diagnosis and transformation rather than mere verbal reform. Cleaning up language while leaving the stockpile unchanged does not fulfill the diagnosis Jesus gives. Conversely, genuine goodness of heart—by implication, the treasure cultivated in relation to God—will produce corresponding speech as its natural overflow and public fruit.

In sum: speech is the outward administration of the heart’s hidden moral inventory. Words primarily reveal and symptomatically display that inventory; the speaker’s choices are genuine yet are themselves shaped and limited by it.
