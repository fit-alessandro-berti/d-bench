Amos rejects the worship so sharply because the issue is not that Israel has “too much ritual” and “not enough justice,” as if the two could be balanced on a scale. The issue is that their worship has become false worship: an attempt to honor God ceremonially while denying him in the treatment of their neighbors.

In Amos’s setting, prosperous Israelites were exploiting the poor, corrupting courts, taking bribes, seizing land, and treating the vulnerable as disposable (see Amos 2:6–7; 5:10–12; 8:4–6). Yet they continued to hold festivals, bring sacrifices, sing songs, and gather in solemn assemblies. They apparently imagined that these practices secured God’s favor.

God’s response is not, “Keep the festivals, but add some justice.” It is, “I hate your festivals.” Why? Because sacrifices and songs offered by people committed to injustice are not neutral religious acts. They become lies. They publicly confess devotion to God while their social life denies God’s character and covenant.

The fraud being exposed includes several things:

- **Using worship as cover for exploitation.** Religious observance can make an unjust community look faithful—to others and to itself.
- **Treating God as someone to be managed or bribed.** Offerings are treated as though they can compensate for oppression: “We may cheat the poor, but we have brought our sacrifices.”
- **Separating devotion from obedience.** Israel acts as though God cares about liturgical beauty but not about courts, markets, debts, wages, and the treatment of the weak.
- **Performing repentance without turning.** Their songs and sacrifices make noise, but their lives have not changed direction.

That is why even beautiful elements of worship—“the melody of thy viols,” costly offerings, festive gatherings—become offensive. Music itself is not condemned; sacrifice itself is not condemned. The Bible elsewhere commands both. What is condemned is worship emptied of truth by a life of settled injustice.

“Let judgment [justice] run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream” is therefore not a request for occasional charitable gestures. The imagery is of a permanent, overflowing river—justice woven continuously into the life of the people. *Mishpat* (“justice”) concerns right judgment and the protection of those wronged; *tsedaqah* (“righteousness”) means rightness, integrity, and covenant faithfulness in relationships.

So Amos is not pitting ethics against worship. He is insisting that worship of the God of Israel must produce a just people. When worship becomes a substitute for obedience, it is no longer worship at all; it is religious theater.
