Great question. In Romans 12:20–21, **victory over evil is not “defeating” your enemy by force**, but **refusing to let evil reproduce itself in you**.

### What “overcome evil with good” means
Paul’s logic in the surrounding verses is:

- Don’t repay evil for evil (12:17).
- Don’t take personal vengeance (12:19).
- Leave final justice to God.
- Actively do good to the one who harmed you (12:20).

So the “win” is this:  
**evil hits you, but does not control your response**. You remain free, obedient, and loving.

### “Heap coals of fire on his head” — manipulative?
This phrase (from Proverbs 25:21–22) is usually taken in two main ways:

1. **Conviction/shame leading to repentance**  
   Your kindness exposes the wrong and may melt hostility.
2. **Leaving judgment to God**  
   If they persist, your good response shows your innocence and God handles judgment.

In either case, Paul’s command is **not** “be nice so you can secretly hurt them.”  
That would contradict the whole chapter’s spirit of sincere love (12:9).

### Weak or strong?
It is **genuinely strong**—often harder than retaliation.

- Retaliation is instinctive.
- Benevolent restraint requires self-mastery, faith, and courage.
- It breaks cycles of revenge and can transform enemies.

So this is not passivity, nor manipulation.  
It is **moral strength under provocation**: refusing to mirror evil, and actively choosing good.
