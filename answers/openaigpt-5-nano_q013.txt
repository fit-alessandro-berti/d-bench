Short answer
Amos 5:21–24 exposes a kind of religious life that is outwardly ritual and inwardly corrupt. God does not want a balance between ceremonies and ethics; he wants a covenant relationship whose worship is inseparable from justice. The moral fraud here is religious hypocrisy: people perform rituals to worship God while actively oppressing the vulnerable and pursuing gain through injustice. The prophet’s sharp rejection is intentional to wake them up to this betrayal.

Key points

- What is being confronted
  - The people are engaging in festival worship, offerings, and musical praise, yet their social behavior contradicts the nature of the covenant. The book of Amos repeatedly shows that Israel’s wealth and power were built on exploiting the poor (see Amos 2:6–7; 4:1–5; 5:11–12).
  - The prophet’s appeal is not “don’t worship,” but “your worship is unacceptable because it is disconnected from justice and righteousness.”

- What the words mean
  - “I hate, I despise your feast days …” signals that God’s sense of betrayal is severe. In Hebrew Bible imagery, offerings and sacrifices can become a stench or fragrance depending on the life that accompanies them.
  - “Take thou away from me the noise of thy songs … I will not hear the melody of thy viols” shows that liturgical music and ritual do not please God when they mask or justify wrongdoing.
  - “Let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream” elevates a different priority: concrete justice in the community. In Hebrew, mishpat (judgment/justice) and tzedakah (righteousness) are the core attributes God desires to govern social life.

- Why sharp, not merely call for balance
  - The problem is systemic hypocrisy, not a simple imbalance. The people are using religion as a social veneer to legitimize oppression and self-interest.
  - The prophets insist that true worship is face-to-face with how people treat the vulnerable. If the heart and the social order are corrupt, ritual cannot be “balanced” into acceptability; it must be transformed.
  - This is not an anti-ritual stance; it is a critique of hollow ritual divorced from covenant fidelity. The call to justice is the corrective that makes worship genuine.

- The kind of fraud exposed
  - Moral/spiritual fraud: aligning with God publicly while contradicting God in the marketplace, courts, and social life. It’s worship-as-a-cover for greed, exploitation, bribery, or neglect of the poor.
  - The claim that “you shall know them by their fruits” before the prophets: people treat wealth, power, and ritual as ends in themselves rather than as means to mercy, justice, and fidelity to God.

- How this fits the broader prophetic message
  - Amos consistently links genuine piety to just actions toward the vulnerable. The famous call to let justice flow like waters and righteousness like a mighty stream represents a vision where social transformation is the true fulfillment of covenant faith.
  - Other prophetic voices (Hosea 6:6; Micah 6:6–8; Isaiah 1) likewise emphasize that God desires faithful, just living over mere ritual.

Practical takeaway for today
- Don’t separate worship from ethics. When you gather for worship, let it be accompanied by acts of justice, mercy, and care for the marginalized.
- Test your own practices: are you benefitting from systems that oppress others? Are your religious activities masking harmful behavior?
- Pursue a holistic faith where “what you do” (justice, honesty, generosity) and “what you say” (worship, praise) reinforce one another.
