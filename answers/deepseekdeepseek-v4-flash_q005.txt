The difficulty of taking enemy-love seriously lies in its radical defiance of our most basic instincts: self-preservation, fairness, and emotional justice. When someone hurts us, the natural response is to strike back, withdraw, or at least demand recompense. Jesus’ command to love, bless, do good, and pray for such people feels almost inhuman—an impossible ideal that clashes with our sense of righteous anger and the desire for retribution. This tension is why the teaching often gets dismissed as unrealistic, naive, or even dangerous.

But is it merely *sentimental*—a kind of soft-hearted wishfulness that ignores real evil? Not if we look at the context. Jesus himself was not sentimental; he confronted hypocrites, cleansed the temple, and faced his own crucifixion with clear-eyed resolve. Enemy-love is not about pretending harm doesn’t matter or feeling warm fuzzies toward abusers. It is a deliberate, costly act of the will—requiring us to see the enemy as a human being made in God’s image, while still acknowledging the wrong they do.

Could it be *strategic*? In some practical contexts, yes—loving enemies can disarm hostility, break cycles of revenge, and even transform opponents (think of Gandhi or King). But Jesus doesn’t frame it as a tactic for winning. He grounds it in the character of God: “He makes his sun rise on the evil and the good.” This is not a tool for social change, but a participation in the divine nature. The strategy angle downplays the inner transformation needed; it reduces love to a means rather than an end.

I would argue that enemy-love is best understood as a **deeper form of moral strength**. It requires the courage to resist the easy, reactive path of hatred, the self-mastery to absorb injury without returning it, and the wisdom to see that justice and mercy are not opposites but partners. This is not weakness—it is the strength to hold onto your own humanity while refusing to dehumanize another. It’s the strength of God’s own patience, who gives sunshine and rain to both the just and unjust. To love an enemy is to undergo a kind of spiritual death to the ego, which is why it feels so difficult. But that very difficulty is evidence of its depth: it is the hardest thing a human being can do, and therefore the most profound expression of moral maturity.

In short, enemy-love is difficult because it calls us beyond our comfort zone, beyond our sense of justice, and into a way of life that mirrors God’s own extravagant mercy. It is not sentimental (it’s costly), not merely strategic (it’s a command), but a deep, demanding strength that, when practiced, can heal both the lover and the hated.
