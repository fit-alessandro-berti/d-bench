This is one of the most penetrating questions about the parable. The elder brother’s outward complaints are about anger, envy, and fairness—but his deepest problem is **the pride of self-righteousness**, which stems from a fundamental misunderstanding of his father’s character and his own need for grace.

Here’s a layered look:

1. **He views his relationship with the father as transactional**. “I serve you… I never transgressed…” He sees obedience as a contract that earns him rights and recognition. His identity is built on being the “good son,” which makes him blind to his own need.

2. **He cannot accept grace as unmerited**. The father’s generosity to the younger son offends his sense of justice. Mercy given freely to someone who “doesn’t deserve it” feels like an insult to those who “do.”

3. **He has never truly seen himself as a son**. Notice his words: “this **thy** son” (not “my brother”). He distances himself. He is essentially a servant in his father’s house, not a child resting in his father’s love.

4. **He refuses to enter the feast—meaning he refuses the father’s joy**. His stance isolates him from the very relationship he claims to value.

The anger, envy, and wounded fairness are all *symptoms*. The **root** is a heart that trusts in its own performance rather than in the father’s unconditional love. He is, in effect, the older brother to all of us—someone who risks missing the party because he’s too busy tallying his own merits.

In the end, the father’s tender reply (“Son, you are always with me, and all that is mine is yours”) reveals the cure: the elder brother is already fully loved and included—he just doesn’t believe it. His deepest problem is that **he does not know the heart of the father, and therefore cannot recognize his own need for the same grace he resents**.
