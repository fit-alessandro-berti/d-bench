The parable of the Pharisee and the Tax Collector (Publican) in Luke 18:9–14 is one of the most profound explorations of human psychology and soteriology (the doctrine of salvation) in the New Testament. 

To answer your question directly: **The Pharisee is not condemned for just one of the things you listed, but for a cascading spiritual pathology in which all of them are inextricably linked.** The root is a false doctrine of justification, which breeds pride; pride necessitates comparison and contempt; and this internal reality manifests as the use of virtue for self-display. 

Conversely, the tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer because it accurately reflects the objective reality of the human condition before a holy God: total dependence on divine mercy.

Here is a detailed breakdown of both figures.

---

### Part 1: The Anatomy of the Pharisee’s Condemnation

The text explicitly frames the parable by stating Jesus spoke it to those who **"trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and despised others"** (v. 9). This sets the stage for a multi-layered condemnation:

**1. False Doctrine (The Theological Core)**
The deepest error of the Pharisee is his doctrine of justification. He operates under a silent assumption that righteousness is a commodity to be earned and presented to God as payment. He believes God’s approval is secured through moral performance and religious rigor. By presenting his resume to God, he fundamentally misunderstands the nature of grace. He does not see himself as a debtor needing forgiveness, but as a creditor expecting a reward. 

**2. Pride and Self-Trust (The Root)**
Luke notes the Pharisee prayed "thus *with himself*" (Greek: *pros heauton*). Commentators note this can mean he was praying so quietly only he could hear, or that his prayer was ultimately self-referential. He is not conversing with God; he is congratulating himself in the presence of God. His trust is in his own moral architecture, not in God’s character.

**3. Comparison and Contempt (The Relational Symptom)**
Because his righteousness is self-generated, it can only be measured relatively. The Pharisee does not measure himself against God’s perfect holiness (which would crush him); he measures himself against "extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even as this publican." His virtue requires the degradation of others to feel secure. The explicit "contempt" (v. 9) is evident when he uses the tax collector not as a fellow human being, but as a prop in his own spiritual performance: "or even as *this* publican." 

**4. The Use of Virtue as Self-Display (The Behavioral Manifestation)**
The Pharisee’s actions—fasting twice a week and tithing everything—were actually beyond the strict requirements of the Mosaic Law (tithing was required on crops, but he tithed "all that I possess," and fasting twice a week was a voluntary extra). However, these acts become toxic because of their orientation. He weaponizes his obedience, turning acts of devotion into merit badges displayed before God to prove his superiority.

In short, the Pharisee is condemned because he has successfully created a god in his own image—a god who is satisfied with moral checklists and who requires no atonement, only acknowledgement of the Pharisee’s superior effort.

---

### Part 2: Why the Tax Collector’s Posture is Spiritually Truer

The tax collector (publican) was a socially reviled figure, viewed as a collaborator with the Roman occupiers and a professional thief. Yet, Jesus declares him the one who "went down to his house justified." His truthfulness is found in both his physical and theological posture.

**1. The Physical Posture of Humility**
*   **"Standing afar off":** This echoes the Old Testament reverence for the holiness of God (e.g., the Israelites standing at a distance from Mount Sinai). He recognizes he is unholy and has no inherent right to casually approach the Divine.
*   **"Would not lift up so much as his eyes unto heaven":** In the ancient world, looking up was a sign of confidence and open appeal. Looking down was a sign of profound shame, contrition, and an acknowledgment that he cannot bear the gaze of a holy God.
*   **"Smote upon his breast":** This was a culturally recognized, visceral gesture of intense grief, mourning, and agony over one's own sin. It is the physical manifestation of a broken heart.

**2. The Theological Posture of Dependence**
His prayer is only seven words in the original Greek, but it is theologically massive: *"God, be merciful to me, the sinner."*
*   **The cry for mercy:** The word used for "be merciful" (*hilaskomai*) is the same root used for "propitiation" or "atonement" (as in Romans 3:25 and Hebrews 2:17). He is not asking God to overlook his sin or to weigh it against his good deeds. He is crying out for God to provide the atoning sacrifice that he cannot provide for himself. He is asking for grace, not a wage.
*   **"The" sinner:** In Greek, he does not say "be merciful to me *a* sinner." He uses the definite article: "be merciful to me *the* sinner" (*ho hamartolos*). He does not compare himself to the Pharisee or to other tax collectors. He isolates himself before God and says, "If there is a sinner standing in this room, it is me, and I deserve nothing but your mercy."

### Conclusion

The parable presents a stark dichotomy of two approaches to God. 

The Pharisee went home with his "righteousness" intact, and therefore, there was no room for God to give him anything. He was full of himself, and thus empty of grace. 

The tax collector went home empty. He brought no resume, no moral leverage, and no comparative superiority. He brought only his profound, crushing need. And because he came empty-handed, asking solely for mercy, he was the one who received the free, unmerited gift of justification. Jesus’ point is that **justification is not the reward of the righteous, but the gift given to those who know they are sinners.**
