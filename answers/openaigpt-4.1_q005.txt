Enemy-love, as Jesus advocates in Matthew 5:44–45, is one of the most radical and challenging commands found in any scripture or ethical tradition. Let’s break down why it’s so difficult to take seriously, and what kind of strength it actually requires.

---

## Why is enemy-love so difficult?

### 1. **It Goes Against Our Instincts**
   - **Natural response:** When hurt or threatened, our natural instinct is to respond in kind—retaliation, self-defense, or avoidance.
   - **Emotional resistance:** Loving someone who has harmed us feels counterintuitive and even unjust.

### 2. **Cultural Conditioning**
   - **Norms:** Societies often promote justice as retribution (“an eye for an eye”), not reconciliation.
   - **Pride and honor:** In many cultures, forgiving or loving enemies is seen as weakness or a failure to stand up for oneself.

### 3. **Perceived Naivety**
   - **Sentimentality:** Some dismiss love for enemies as unrealistic idealism or sentimental foolishness.
   - **Fear of enabling:** People worry that loving enemies condones or enables injustice or invites further harm.

---

## Is it Sentimental, Strategic, or a Deeper Moral Strength?

### A. **Sentimental?**
- **Not merely feelings:** Jesus isn’t calling for “warm fuzzies” toward opponents. Biblical love (“agape”) is active goodwill—not mere sentiment.

### B. **Strategic?**
- **Practical wisdom:** Enemy-love can de-escalate conflict, “heap burning coals” (Romans 12:20), and break cycles of violence.
- **Not manipulative:** But Christian teaching emphasizes love not for what it gets us, but because it reflects God’s character.

### C. **Moral Strength**
- **Radical alignment with God:** The deepest sense of Jesus’ teaching is a call to a moral strength that imitates God—who “makes his sun rise on the evil and the good.”
- **Transcending reciprocity:** Most people love those who love them. To love an enemy is to act freely, not as a reaction, but as a moral agent who refuses to be defined by another’s actions.

---

## What Kind of Strength Is Required?

- **Self-mastery:** Enemy-love requires us to govern our resentment, anger, and desire for revenge.
- **Vision:** To see others, even enemies, as God’s children—with dignity and hope for redemption.
- **Courage:** It’s hard and often costly; it risks misunderstanding, scorn, or further injury.
- **Faith:** Trusting that love, not hatred, is ultimately transformative—even if not immediately effective.

---

### **Conclusion**

Enemy-love is not sentimental naïveté, nor is it merely clever strategy. It is a demanding, courageous act of moral strength. Jesus roots it in God’s indiscriminate grace—a love that does not depend on the worthiness of its recipient. Taking enemy-love seriously invites us into the very heart of God’s own way of being in the world.

**“Be perfect, therefore, as your heavenly Father is perfect” (Matthew 5:48)** — that is, be perfected in love, even for enemies.
