The moral posture required here is one of **humble, self-suspicious gentleness**. Paul is not merely giving instructions on *how* to correct; he is describing the inner disposition that must accompany any attempt to address another believer’s sin. Three elements are deliberately bound together because they form a single, integrated stance.

### 1. Restoration as the Goal
The word “restore” (*katartizō*) carries the sense of mending something that is broken—setting a dislocated joint, repairing a torn net, or restoring something to its proper function. This immediately rules out several wrong postures:
- A judicial posture (“I must confront and expose”)
- A superior posture (“I need to straighten this person out”)
- A detached posture (“I’ll tell them the truth and let them deal with it”)

The aim is not primarily to make the person feel the weight of their sin, but to return them to spiritual wholeness. This requires patience and a willingness to stay involved in the process of recovery.

### 2. Meekness as the Manner
Paul specifies that this restoration must happen “in the spirit of meekness.” Meekness here is not weakness or passivity; it is strength under control, specifically the refusal to use one’s spiritual advantage or moral clarity as a weapon. The corrector must approach the fallen person without the subtle (or not-so-subtle) sense of superiority that often accompanies moral correction.

This is why Paul addresses “ye which are spiritual.” Even those who are walking in the Spirit are not exempt from the temptation to handle another’s failure with a harsh or condescending spirit. Meekness is the safeguard against turning correction into domination.

### 3. Self-Suspicion as the Guardrail
The clause “considering thyself, lest thou also be tempted” is not an afterthought. It is the psychological and spiritual mechanism that makes meekness possible. Paul assumes that the person doing the correcting is vulnerable to the same category of sin (or to the sin of pride in their own righteousness). This creates a posture of **solidarity in frailty** rather than distance from the sinner.

When someone corrects another while carrying a quiet awareness that they themselves could fall—and perhaps have fallen in similar ways—the tone changes. Condescension becomes difficult. Harshness becomes unnatural. The corrector is more likely to speak with gravity and compassion simultaneously.

### Why These Three Are Kept Together

Paul binds restoration, meekness, and self-suspicion because each one protects the others:

- **Without self-suspicion**, restoration easily degenerates into moralistic bullying. The corrector begins to enjoy the position of strength and loses the tenderness required to actually help.
- **Without meekness**, even well-intentioned restoration becomes crushing. Truth is delivered in a way that drives the person further into shame or defensiveness rather than back to God.
- **Without a commitment to restoration**, meekness can become mere tolerance or cowardice—the refusal to speak at all under the guise of being “humble.”

Together, they create a distinctly Christian approach to dealing with sin in others: the corrector stands not above the fallen person, but beside them, aware that both stand on the same ground of need. This posture reflects the gospel itself, in which God restores sinners without condescending to them from a position of moral detachment.

In short, the required moral posture is not “I see your fault and will help you,” but “I see your fault, and I approach you as one who could easily be in your place.”
