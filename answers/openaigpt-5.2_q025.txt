In Matthew 6:1 the moral danger is not *being observed* but *aiming at being observed*—doing “righteousness” **in order to be seen** and admired.

### 1) The target is the motive (“to be seen of them”)
The key phrase is purpose: *“to be seen of them.”* Jesus isn’t condemning actions that happen to be public; he’s warning against **performing virtue as a display**—turning almsgiving into self-advertisement. In that case, the “reward” sought is human approval, and that is all one gets (cf. Matt 6:2: “they have their reward”).

### 2) Visibility itself is not the issue (cf. Matthew 5:16)
Just one chapter earlier Jesus says:

> “Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works…” (Matt 5:16)

So he cannot mean, “Never let anyone see anything good you do.” The difference is the *end* (goal):

- **Matt 5:16:** good works are visible so that **God is glorified**.
- **Matt 6:1:** good works are done so that **the doer is glorified**.

### 3) Why that makes public goodness morally dangerous
Public goodness is “dangerous” because it easily shifts the heart’s orientation:

- It tempts a person to **exchange God’s approval for human praise**.
- It can subtly turn virtue into a tool for **status, identity, or control**.
- It can produce “hypocrisy” in the literal sense of “acting”—performing goodness as a role rather than as love.

### Answer
The problem is **not visibility as such**; the problem is the **craving to be seen**—the intention to perform righteousness for applause. When that becomes the motive, the act may still look good externally, but it no longer functions as a God-directed act of love, and its “reward” is reduced to the fleeting recognition of others.
