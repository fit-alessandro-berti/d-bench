The pairing of “way” and “thoughts” in Isaiah 55:7 provides a rich, holistic definition of biblical repentance. By addressing both, the prophet reveals that true turning to God cannot be superficial; it must encompass the entirety of the human experience—both the visible and the invisible. 

Furthermore, this verse sets up the famous contrast in the immediate following verses (Isaiah 55:8–9), where God declares, *“For my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways.”* Therefore, to align with God, the unrighteous must abandon their own ways and thoughts. 

Here is what this implies about outward conduct, inward imagination, and the possibility of mercy:

### 1. The Implication for Outward Conduct (“Forsake his way”)
The word “way” (*derek* in Hebrew) refers to a person’s path, habits, lifestyle, and outward behaviors. 
* **Action is Necessary:** Repentance is not merely feeling sorry; it requires a deliberate, directional shift in how one lives. Forsaking one's "way" implies abandoning sinful habits, unethical practices, and destructive behaviors. 
* **Behavior Matters to God:** It implies that faith is meant to be visible. God cares about how people conduct their daily lives, treat others, and interact with the physical world. A claim to know God without a change in one's "way" is incomplete.

### 2. The Implication for Inward Imagination (“And his thoughts”)
The word “thoughts” (*makhshavah*) refers to intentions, calculations, imaginations, and the internal worldview of a person. 
* **The Root of Sin:** Outward conduct (the "way") is merely the fruit; inward imagination (the "thoughts") is the root. Sin begins in the mind—through rationalization, pride, lust, greed, and a desire for autonomy from God. 
* **Beyond Behavior Modification:** By demanding the forsaking of *thoughts*, Isaiah implies that mere behavior modification or religious hypocrisy is insufficient. A person might stop committing visible crimes out of fear of punishment, yet still harbor hatred, greed, or rebellion in their heart. True repentance requires surrendering one's intellect, motives, and desires to God's authority. 
* **Admitting Our Foolishness:** To forsake our thoughts means acknowledging that our perspective on life, justice, and truth is inherently flawed and needs to be replaced by God's truth. 

### 3. The Synergy of Both
By commanding the forsaking of *both* ways and thoughts, Isaiah defines true repentance as total transformation. 
* Changing the "way" without the "thoughts" creates a **hypocrite or a legalist**. 
* Claiming to change the "thoughts" without altering the "way" creates **dead faith** (as James writes, faith without works).
Repentance requires bringing both the internal compass and the external journey into submission to the Lord.

### 4. The Implication for the Possibility of Mercy
The verse concludes with a staggering promise: *“...he will have mercy upon him; and to our God, for he will abundantly pardon.”*
* **Mercy is Guaranteed, Not Just Possible:** The language is definitive (*"he will"*). It implies that God is not reluctant to forgive. When a person meets the condition of bringing their whole self (ways and thoughts) back to God, mercy is the absolutely certain response. 
* **The Scope of Forgiveness:** The phrase "abundantly pardon" literally translates as God will "multiply to pardon." It implies that the depth of God's grace vastly exceeds the depth of human sin. No outward conduct ("way") is too wicked, and no inward imagination ("thoughts") is too corrupted to be beyond the reach of God's forgiveness, provided there is genuine returning.
* **The Urgency of the Invitation:** Verse 6 says to seek the Lord "while he may be found" and "while he is near." This implies that the window of grace is currently wide open, but it is tied to the present moment. God has drawn near, making this abundant mercy immediately accessible to anyone willing to turn.
