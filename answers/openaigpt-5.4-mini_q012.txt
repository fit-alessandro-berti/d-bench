Jeremiah 17:9–10 is best read as **both**—but with a particular emphasis.

## 1) It is realistic about self-deception
The passage is not mainly saying, “Humans are evil in every possible way,” as if no good, love, courage, or sincerity ever exists. Rather, it says the **inner self is not a reliable courtroom witness about itself**.

- The “heart” in biblical language is not just feelings; it includes the mind, will, desires, and intentions.
- Jeremiah’s point is that people are **capable of being badly wrong about their own motives**.
- We often rationalize, confuse good intentions with actual goodness, and tell ourselves stories that flatter us.

So yes: it is a deeply **realistic diagnosis of self-deception**.

## 2) It is also pessimistic in a moral sense
At the same time, the verse is more than a neutral psychological observation. It is a moral warning:

- The heart is “deceitful above all things.”
- It is “desperately wicked” (or “incurably sick,” depending on translation nuance).

That is strong language. Jeremiah is saying human inwardness is not just cloudy; it is **corrupted**. Left to itself, the heart does not merely lack clarity—it tends to bend truth for self-protection and self-justification.

So the passage is realistic, but it is also **severe** about human moral unreliability.

## 3) How should someone talk about motives?
A good takeaway is: **talk about motives humbly, tentatively, and with examination**.

### A. Don’t assume you fully know your own motives
You can say:
- “I think my motive was partly generosity, but I may also have wanted approval.”
- “I’m not sure whether I acted out of love or fear.”
- “I need to examine whether I was defending truth or defending myself.”

That kind of speech fits Jeremiah well.

### B. Judge actions and patterns, not just self-description
Because the heart can deceive, we should not treat “I meant well” as the final word. Look at:
- consistent behavior
- outcomes
- repeated patterns
- whether the action bears good fruit

Jeremiah 17:10 itself points in that direction: God gives “according to… the fruit of his doings.”

### C. Use outside help
If the heart is unreliable on its own, then motives should be tested by:
- Scripture
- wise counsel
- prayer
- honest friends
- long-term fruit

A person often needs external mirrors to see what is inside.

### D. Leave room for mixed motives
Many actions are not purely selfish or purely noble. Someone may genuinely help and also enjoy being seen as helpful. Someone may tell the truth and also want to win an argument. Scripture’s view of the heart makes room for that complexity.

## 4) The practical posture: humility without despair
The passage should not lead to cynicism like “no motive can ever be trusted.” Rather, it calls for:
- humility about self-knowledge
- caution about self-justification
- dependence on God’s search of the heart
- openness to correction

In short:

> **We should not treat the heart as a final authority on itself.**
> Motives need to be examined, tested, and submitted to God.
