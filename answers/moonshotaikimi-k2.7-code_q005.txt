 Enemy-love is hard to take seriously because, at first glance, it looks like it is asking us to *erase* the difference between right and wrong. It seems to say that the cruel person, the oppressor, the one who “despitefully uses” us, should be treated with the same warmth as a friend. That feels not only unfair but dangerous. Our natural moral reflex is proportion: good is rewarded, harm is resisted, enemies are restrained or defeated. To love an enemy appears to short-circuit that reflex.

But the difficulty is not only emotional. It is also *intellectual*: enemy-love can seem either **sentimental** (“just be nice to everyone”) or **strategic** (“kill them with kindness,” or use nonviolence as a more effective weapon). If it is merely sentimental, it is unrealistic; if it is merely strategic, it is not really love at all—just a smarter form of self-interest.

Matthew 5:44–45 points to something deeper than either.

### 1. Why it is not mere sentiment
Sentimental love avoids conflict and pretends everyone is already nice. Jesus does not say, “Pretend your enemies are not hurting you.” He says: **“Bless them that curse you, do good to them that hate you, and pray for them which despitefully use you.”** These are concrete actions addressed precisely to people who *are* cursing, hating, and persecuting. Enemy-love is not denial; it is a deliberate response to real hostility. It does not cancel justice; it refuses to let hatred define the lover.

### 2. Why it is not mere strategy
Strategy asks, “What will gain me the upper hand?” But Jesus grounds the command in the character of God: **“That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven: for he maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust.”** God’s provision falls on everyone, not because He is indifferent to evil, but because His love is not limited to those who deserve it. If the disciple loves only the lovable, he is not reflecting God; he is merely exercising ordinary human preference.

This means enemy-love is not primarily a technique for winning arguments or conflicts. Its goal is not to manipulate the enemy into being nice. Its goal is to make the lover like God.

### 3. Why it is a deeper form of moral strength
To love an enemy is to refuse the story that *“because you harmed me, you have the power to make me into someone like you.”* Hate aims to draw its target into the same logic: us versus them, retaliation, dehumanization. Enemy-love breaks that cycle. It requires:

- **Self-mastery**: restraining the impulse to return evil for evil.
- **Moral imagination**: seeing the enemy as a human being, loved by God, capable of repentance.
- **Courage**: acting for the other’s good even when the other is hostile.
- **Hope**: believing that love, not vengeance, has the last word.

At the same time, loving an enemy does not necessarily mean trusting him, surrendering to injustice, or refusing to resist evil. One can love a tyrant and still oppose tyranny; one can pray for a persecutor and still seek protection for the persecuted. The love Jesus commands is an *internal disposition and active goodwill*, not a demand to become a passive victim.

### Summary
Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it offends our sense of justice and self-protection. But in the Sermon on the Mount, it is neither weak sentiment nor clever strategy. It is the **moral strength of the children of God**: the capacity to love as God loves—not because the enemy deserves it, but because the lover has been shaped by a mercy that falls on the just and the unjust alike.
