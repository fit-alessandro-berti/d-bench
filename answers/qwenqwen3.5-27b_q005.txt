The command in Matthew 5:44–45 is widely regarded as one of the most radical ethical injunctions in human history. It sits in the center of the Sermon on the Mount, a place where Jesus dismantles the prevailing wisdom of his day (and arguably all days).

To answer your question: **Enemy-love is difficult because it contradicts our evolutionary wiring, our psychological need for justice, and our cultural definition of power.** It is neither sentimental nor merely strategic; rather, it is a **profound form of moral strength rooted in theological identity.**

Here is an analysis of why this command is so hard to take seriously and what it actually demands.

### 1. Why is it so difficult?
The difficulty of enemy-love is not just a matter of willpower; it is a collision between the text and human nature.

*   **The Biological Imperative:** Humans are tribal creatures wired for "in-group/out-group" dynamics. Our nervous systems are designed to perceive threats and respond with fight, flight, or freeze. To love an enemy requires overriding a survival mechanism that says, "This person threatens my safety or status."
*   **The Psychology of Justice:** We have a deep-seated craving for retributive justice. When we are wronged, our internal ledger demands balance. To bless a persecutor feels like a violation of cosmic order. It feels like saying, "Your harm to me doesn't matter," which is psychologically painful.
*   **The Definition of Power:** In most cultures, power is defined by the ability to inflict consequences. To "do good to them that hate you" looks like weakness. It requires a redefinition of strength where the victor is not the one who dominates, but the one who refuses to be consumed by hatred.

### 2. Is it Sentimental?
**No.** If enemy-love were sentimental, it would be impossible.

*   **Feeling vs. Will:** In the modern world, "love" is often conflated with affection, warmth, or emotional attachment. If Jesus meant, "Feel warm fuzzies toward your tormentor," the command would be absurd. No one can manufacture affection on demand.
*   **Agape:** The Greek word used here is *agape*. In the biblical context, *agape* is not an emotion; it is a **volitional commitment to the good of the other.** It is the decision to act in ways that promote the well-being of the enemy, even when your feelings scream otherwise.
*   **The Reality of the Enemy:** Taking it seriously means acknowledging that the enemy may remain an enemy. You can pray for a persecutor without them ever changing their mind. If the command relied on the enemy's reciprocation or your emotional comfort, it would be sentimental. Because it relies on *your* obedience regardless of the outcome, it is not.

### 3. Is it Strategic?
**Only incidentally.** There is a pragmatic side to enemy-love, but it cannot be the primary motivation.

*   **The Disarming Effect:** Historically, figures like Gandhi or Martin Luther King Jr. demonstrated that non-retaliation can disarm an oppressor by exposing the brutality of their actions. If you strike back, the oppressor can claim self-defense. If you bless them, you strip them of their moral cover.
*   **The Trap of Strategy:** However, if you love your enemy *only* to win them over or to manipulate the situation, you are still treating them as a means to an end. That is not love; that is calculation.
*   **The Text's Motivation:** Notice the text does not say, "Love your enemies so they will stop persecuting you." It says, **"That ye may be the children of your Father."** The goal is not the enemy's conversion; it is your own identity. The strategy is secondary to the sonship.

### 4. Is it a Deeper Form of Moral Strength?
**Yes.** This is the core of the command.

*   **Imitatio Dei (Imitating God):** The text grounds the command in the character of God: *"He maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good."* God sustains the universe impartially. To love an enemy is to participate in the divine nature. It is an act of aligning your will with the reality that God's grace is not earned by merit.
*   **Freedom from the Past:** Hatred is a chain. To hate is to remain bound to the person who hurt you. They hold the keys to your emotional state. To bless them is to cut the chain. It is the ultimate act of liberation.
*   **Strength Over Self:** It requires more energy to forgive than to retaliate. Retaliation is a reflex; forgiveness is a discipline. It is the strength to say, "I will not let your evil define my character."

### Conclusion
Enemy-love is difficult to take seriously because it asks us to trust a God who sustains the unjust, even when our human instinct demands we destroy them.

It is **not sentimental**, because it does not rely on how we feel.
It is **not primarily strategic**, because its goal is our identity as God's children, not a tactical victory.
It **is moral strength**, because it breaks the cycle of violence and asserts that we are defined not by how we are treated, but by who we choose to be.

As the theologian Reinhold Niebuhr noted, this command is "impossible" for fallen humans to achieve perfectly, yet it remains the standard that exposes our limitations and drives us toward grace. To take it seriously is to acknowledge that we cannot do it alone, which paradoxically makes it the most practical command of all.
