**Answer:**

The passage from Genesis 4:6‑7 presents Cain’s inner conflict and offers a nuanced picture of moral agency that emphasizes **personal responsibility and conscious choice** rather than attributing his wrongdoing to an external, uncontrollable force or to mere impulse. Here’s a step-by-step analysis of the key elements:

1. **Identification of the Problem**  
   - The Lord asks Cain two questions: “Why art thou wroth? and why is thy countenance fallen?”  
   - These questions point to Cain’s **emotional state** and **behavioral manifestation** (a fallen countenance), suggesting that the issue is internal—not just the result of external circumstances.

2. **Dividing Possibilities: “If thou doest well, shalt thou not be accepted?”**  
   - The Lord presents a **binary** view of moral action: *doing well* (righteous conduct) leads to acceptance, while *not doing well* (acting otherwise) opens the door to sin.  
   - This establishes the **capacity for choice**—Cain can either choose righteousness or another path.

3. **The Nature of Sin as “Lieth at the Door”**  
   - The phrase “sin lieth at the door” (Hebrew: *chametz* or “leaven”) indicates that **sin is present and accessible**—it is not something that simply erupts spontaneously but **awaits** Cain’s decision.  
   - It is **temptation** in a proximate sense, suggesting that the conditions for sin are already in place, ready to be embraced.

4. **Desire and Dominion: “And unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him.”**  
   - **“His desire”** (Hebrew *ra‘ shav*) refers to the **tempting, covetous desire** that accompanies the presence of sin.  
   - The promise that Cain “shall rule over him” implies that **Cain has the power to master this desire**—he is not a passive victim of an inner beast or habit.  
   - The text thus frames **evil as a temptation** that Cain must confront, not as a seething beast or an inevitable habit. He is given the agency to either yield to that desire or overcome it.

5. **Moral Agency Illustrated**  
   - **Impulse vs. Choice:** The passage does not depict evil as a sudden, uncontrollable impulse; rather, it portrays the temptation as something that *awaits* Cain’s response.  
   - **Beast vs. Human:** It avoids portraying sin as an internal animalistic force (a “beast”) that overwhelms reason. Instead, it emphasizes the rational, moral capacity to **rule over** the temptation.  
   - **Habit vs. Decision:** It does not present sin as a habit that gradually takes root without decision. The focus is on a **single decisive choice** (whether to act well or not).  
   - **Temptation as a Present Option:** The text explicitly calls sin “at the door,” making it a **temptation** that must be met with conscious will.  

6. **Responsibility Imposed on the Person**  
   - Cain is **responsible** for his decision: if he acts rightly, acceptance follows; if he succumbs, he must bear the consequences of sin.  
   - The text underscores that **the person remains the sovereign agent** who must either **subdue** the temptation (“rule over him”) or **yield** to it.  
   - This aligns with the broader biblical motif that humanity is called to exercise moral agency, accountable before God for the choices made.

**Conclusion:**  
Genesis 4:6‑7 draws a picture of moral agency where **evil is presented as an accessible temptation (“sin lies at the door”) that one must confront rather than as an uncontrollable impulse, beast, or ingrained habit**. The text places the **full responsibility** on Cain, encouraging him to exercise his capacity to **master his desires** and thereby choose righteousness. This moral framework anticipates the biblical theme that humans are called to act volitionally, with the awareness that every choice carries divine judgment.
