# The Danger of Performed Goodness

## Reading the Verse Carefully

The verse repays close grammatical attention. Jesus does not say:

> "Do not your alms before men."

He says:

> "Do not your alms before men, **to be seen of them**."

The qualifying clause is everything. The Greek phrase *πρὸς τὸ θεαθῆναι αὐτοῖς* expresses **purpose** — it marks the intention behind the act, not the mere fact of its occurring in public space.

## So What Exactly Is Dangerous?

The problem is not visibility. It is **the orientation of the will toward an audience**.

Consider the distinction:

- **An act that happens to be seen** — a person gives generously; others notice. The act's purpose was the good itself.
- **An act performed in order to be seen** — the giving is real, but it has been reorganised around the spectator. The good deed becomes a stage, and the giver becomes a performer.

Jesus identifies something precise: the moment when **the reward you seek shifts from God to the crowd**, the entire moral structure of the act collapses. You still did something externally charitable. But you have already collected your payment — in admiration, reputation, social capital. There is nothing left to reward.

## Why This Is Morally Dangerous Rather Than Merely Impure

Several layers of danger are worth distinguishing:

**1. It corrupts the giver.** The person who gives to be seen gradually loses the capacity to give for any other reason. The appetite for recognition grows; the quiet good becomes unsatisfying. You train yourself to need the audience.

**2. It instrumentalises the recipient.** The person in need becomes a prop in someone else's moral performance. Their suffering is useful primarily as an occasion for the giver's display.

**3. It falsifies the relationship with God.** If almsgiving is meant to express dependence on God and love for neighbour, performing it for spectators substitutes a horizontal transaction for a vertical one. You are no longer acting *before God* but *before men* — and these are fundamentally different stages on which to live.

**4. It is self-deceiving.** Perhaps most dangerously, the publicly generous person can convince themselves they are righteous precisely because the evidence is so visible. The applause feels like confirmation. This makes repentance nearly impossible — why repent of what everyone praises?

## The Subtlety Worth Noting

Jesus does not demand invisibility as an absolute rule — He Himself taught publicly, healed publicly, and elsewhere says *"Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works and glorify your Father which is in heaven"* (Matthew 5:16). 

This creates an apparent tension that resolves at the level of **intention and direction**:

- In 5:16, the visibility serves so that observers **glorify God**
- In 6:1, the visibility serves so that the giver **receives glory**

The identical external act points in opposite directions depending on what the heart is reaching for.

## The Core Answer

The problem is not that others saw you. The problem is that **you arranged to be seen**, and in doing so, you revealed that the real object of the exercise was never the neighbour's need or God's glory — it was your own image reflected back in the eyes of an audience. Public goodness becomes morally dangerous at the exact point where it becomes *performed* goodness, because performance demands a reward that has nothing to do with love.
