**1. Speech as symptom**  
The logic is forensic: a tree is known by its fruit (v. 33–34). When the mouth produces “good” or “evil” speech, it registers the prior condition of the heart. The symptom is reliable because the source is consistent; you do not find figs on thistles. Thus words are **effects** whose cause lies deeper; they are the audible surface of an interior deposit.

**2. Speech as choice—qualified**  
The passage does not portray utterance as a neutral raw material we shape at will. Rather, the treasury has already been filled (v. 35: “bringeth forth”). What comes out is *what is there* to draw upon. This relativizes choice: we choose from our store, but we did not (by an act of that moment) determine the store itself. Over time, of course, repeated choices *do* shape the treasury—evil choices enlarge the evil reserve, good choices the good—so volition is operative, yet indirectly: words express present moral capital that past choices (or refusals) have accumulated.

**3. Speech as revelation**  
This is the polemical force of “O generation of vipers.” The opponents’ speech exposes what they try to conceal: an evil root beneath apparently correct religious foliage. Words rip open the hidden inventory; the mouth discloses the heart’s account book. Speech is therefore a window into moral identity that is already constituted before utterance.

**4. All three, ordered by priority**  
- **Revelation** comes first: speech unmasks.  
- **Symptom** follows: what is unmasked is evidence of an inner state.  
- **Choice** (or its history) is the deepest layer: the treasury was built by prior orientation toward good or evil.

In sum, the passage ties speech to hidden moral stockpiles by insisting that words cannot outrun the heart’s capital. They reveal that capital, manifest its quality as symptomatic fruit, and reflect the cumulative choices that filled it. The mouth speaks not what it decides on the spot, but what the treasury has become.
