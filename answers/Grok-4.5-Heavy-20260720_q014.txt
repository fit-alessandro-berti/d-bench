**The text primarily says that treasure *trains* (forms, orients, directs) the heart, not merely that it reveals the heart.**

The pivotal statement is: “For where your treasure is, there will your heart be also.” The grammar and logic run in one clear direction. In the Greek, the treasure’s location is stated in the present (“where your treasure *is*”), while the heart’s location is future (“there *will* your heart be also”). Jesus does not say the reverse—“where your heart is, there your treasure will be.” Treasure leads; the heart follows.

This is reinforced by the causal “for” (γάρ). The reason Jesus commands “do not lay up… but lay up” is precisely because the act of storing treasure will claim and shape the heart. Commentators consistently note the formative force: possessions change you so that you care more about them; your investments shape your desires; the heart follows the treasure the way a compass needle follows north.

It can *secondarily* function diagnostically—observing where someone has already stored treasure discloses the orientation the heart has taken—but that is a consequence of the primary dynamic, not the main point of the teaching. The force of the passage is hortatory and formative: intentionally place your treasure so that it trains your heart toward heaven rather than earth.

### How possession, attention, and love become tied together

They form a tight causal chain (and, over time, a reinforcing cycle):

- **Possession** is the deliberate act of “laying up” or storing treasure—investing resources, time, energy, and effort into a particular sphere (earthly goods or heavenly realities such as generosity, mercy, and kingdom priorities).
- That act necessarily draws and sustains **attention**. Earthly treasures require ongoing vigilance against moth, rust, and thieves; heavenly treasures orient focus toward lasting realities. The immediately following verses about the eye as the lamp of the body (vv. 22–23) continue this theme of directed focus.
- Attention and investment in turn attach the **heart** (καρδία—the biblical center of desire, affection, will, and loyalty). What you repeatedly possess and attend to, you come to love. What you love, you ultimately serve (v. 24: “No one can serve two masters”).

Thus possession trains attention, and attention cultivates love. By choosing where to store treasure, a person is choosing what will capture their focus and claim their affections. Earthly storage trains the heart toward the temporary and the anxious; heavenly storage trains it toward the secure and the eternal.

In short: the text does not merely observe that treasure reveals the heart. It teaches that treasure *trains* the heart, and therefore urges deliberate placement of treasure so that possession, attention, and love are drawn upward together.
