Luke 6:37 is a profound teaching that distinguishes between **discernment** (necessary moral evaluation) and **condemnation** (a self-righteous, judgmental spirit). The verse does not advocate for moral relativism or the abandonment of moral judgment; rather, it warns against a specific kind of judgment—one that is hypocritical, unloving, and destructive. Here’s how we can unpack the difference:

---

### **1. Discernment vs. Condemnation**
#### **Discernment (Good Judgment)**
- **Definition:** The ability to perceive and evaluate truth, right, and wrong in a way that aligns with wisdom, love, and God’s standards.
- **Biblical Basis:**
  - Jesus Himself exercised discernment (e.g., calling the Pharisees "whitewashed tombs" in Matthew 23:27, or warning against false prophets in Matthew 7:15-20).
  - Paul urges believers to "test everything" and "hold fast to what is good" (1 Thessalonians 5:21).
  - The church is called to judge *within* the body (1 Corinthians 5:12-13) to maintain holiness and protect the vulnerable.
- **Characteristics:**
  - **Humble:** Acknowledges one’s own flaws and limitations (Matthew 7:3-5).
  - **Redemptive:** Aims to restore, not destroy (Galatians 6:1).
  - **God-centered:** Seeks to align with God’s truth, not personal bias or cultural trends.
  - **Loving:** Motivated by care for others, not self-righteousness (1 Corinthians 13:6).

#### **Condemnation (Bad Judgment)**
- **Definition:** A spirit of harsh, final, or self-exalting judgment that writes people off as irredeemable.
- **Biblical Basis:**
  - The Pharisees exemplified this (John 8:1-11), using the law to shame rather than save.
  - James warns against slander and judgmental speech (James 4:11-12).
  - Jesus’ warning in Luke 6:37 is directed at those who "condemn" (Greek: *katadikazō*), meaning to pass a final, damning sentence.
- **Characteristics:**
  - **Hypocritical:** Ignores one’s own sin while magnifying others’ (Romans 2:1).
  - **Destructive:** Tears down rather than builds up (Proverbs 18:21).
  - **Self-righteous:** Elevates the self above others (Luke 18:9-14).
  - **Unforgiving:** Withholds grace, mirroring the "unforgiving servant" (Matthew 18:21-35).

---

### **2. Why the Distinction Matters**
Luke 6:37 does not say, *"Never make moral judgments."* Instead, it says:
- **Don’t judge *as if you are the final authority*** (only God is).
- **Don’t condemn *as if you are without sin*** (Romans 3:23).
- **Don’t withhold forgiveness** (because you’ve been forgiven, Matthew 6:14-15).

The verse is a call to **humility in judgment**, not the abolition of judgment. For example:
- A parent *discerns* that a child’s behavior is wrong but disciplines with love, not condemnation.
- A pastor *discerns* sin in the church but seeks repentance and restoration, not expulsion without hope.
- A friend *discerns* a harmful pattern in another but speaks truth in love (Ephesians 4:15), not with contempt.

---

### **3. The Danger of Moral Relativism**
Some misuse Luke 6:37 to argue that *all* moral judgment is wrong, leading to:
- **Moral cowardice:** Refusing to call evil "evil" (e.g., ignoring abuse, injustice, or heresy).
- **False tolerance:** Equating discernment with bigotry (e.g., "You’re judging me!" as a shield for sin).
- **Cultural capitulation:** Abandoning biblical standards to avoid conflict.

But Jesus’ teaching is not a ban on moral clarity; it’s a ban on **moral superiority**. The apostles continued to make moral judgments (e.g., Paul on sexual immorality in 1 Corinthians 5), but they did so with tears, not triumph (2 Corinthians 2:4).

---

### **4. Practical Application**
- **Examine your heart:** Are you judging to *help* or to *hurt*? To *restore* or to *rejoice in others’ failures*?
- **Check your standards:** Are you holding others to a higher standard than you hold yourself? (Matthew 7:3-5)
- **Prioritize grace:** Forgiveness doesn’t mean ignoring sin, but it does mean refusing to write people off.
- **Speak truth in love:** Discernment without love is harsh; love without discernment is naive (Ephesians 4:15).

---

### **5. Key Takeaway**
Luke 6:37 is not a rejection of moral judgment but a **rejection of the spirit behind bad judgment**. It’s a call to:
- **Judge like God judges** (with mercy, Psalm 103:8-10).
- **Forgive as you’ve been forgiven** (Colossians 3:13).
- **Discern without condemning** (John 3:17).

In short: **You can (and must) recognize sin without delighting in it, and you can call out wrongdoing without damning the wrongdoer.** The difference lies in the heart—whether it’s shaped by love or by pride.
