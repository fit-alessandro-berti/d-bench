This parable is precise about what it indicts, and it's worth ruling things out before naming the thing itself.

**It isn't false doctrine.** The Pharisee's theology is fine. Fasting, tithing, avoiding extortion and adultery — these are good. Luke doesn't stage a debate about correct belief. The Pharisee could pass any doctrinal exam and still walk away condemned.

**Pride is close but not quite it, or rather it's the root but not the visible mechanism.** Pride is the soil; the parable shows what grows out of it.

**What actually condemns him is the structure of his prayer: comparison used as the basis of standing before God.** Look at the grammar — "I thank thee that I am *not as* other men... or even as this publican." His righteousness isn't measured against God's holiness; it's measured against another man's failure. He needs the publican to be worse so that he can be verified as better. His prayer isn't really addressed to God at all — Luke says he "prayed thus *with himself*." God is almost incidental, a witness called in to ratify a self-assessment already complete.

**Contempt is the fruit of that comparison** — "despised others" in verse 9 is the frame Luke puts around the whole scene before we even meet the two men. The comparison isn't neutral data-gathering; it curdles into contempt, which is why the parable begins by naming that posture explicitly.

**Virtue as self-display** is real but secondary — the fasting and tithing aren't the problem in themselves; they become evidence in a case he's building for his own justification, submitted to God as proof rather than offered as gift or grateful practice.

So the layered answer: pride is the root, comparison is the mechanism, contempt is the fruit, and self-displayed virtue is the material used to construct the case. All of these coalesce in a man who has turned prayer into self-report.

**The tax collector is truer because his posture matches his actual position.** He doesn't compare — there's no "at least I'm better than the Pharisee." He doesn't build a case. He doesn't even complete a full sentence: "God, be merciful to me, a sinner" is stripped down to essential address, essential need, essential self-naming. He stands "afar off," won't lift his eyes, strikes his chest — his body enacts the truth his words state. There is no gap between what he presents and what he is.

That's the deep logic Luke is exposing: justification doesn't go to the one with better content in his prayer, but to the one whose prayer has no distance between the sayer and the said. The Pharisee's prayer is accurate reporting of real virtues, delivered by someone not actually present to his own need. The tax collector's prayer is minimal, but he is entirely inside it. Luke's verdict — "this man went down justified rather than the other" — isn't a comment on their moral records. It's a comment on which man actually showed up to talk to God, and which man showed up to talk to himself with God as audience.
