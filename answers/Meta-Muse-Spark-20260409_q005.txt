### Why enemy-love feels impossible to take seriously

**Matthew 5:44–45** hits a nerve because it violates two instincts we trust most: fairness and self-protection. 

**1. It breaks reciprocity** 
Most of our moral software runs on tit-for-tat. You help me, I help you. You harm me, I guard myself or strike back. That pattern kept small tribes alive. Enemy-love tells you to short-circuit that loop and give good for evil. It feels like dropping your shield in a fight.

**2. It looks like surrender** 
We confuse forgiveness with approval, and kindness with weakness. If I "bless them that curse me," am I saying the curse was okay? The verse doesn't say that. But our culture equates boundary-lessness with love, so the command gets misread as "be a doormat." Nobody wants to take that seriously because it seems like moral self-erasure.

**3. It costs something real** 
Loving friends pays you back in loyalty and warmth. Loving enemies pays you back in... nothing, at first. Maybe scorn. So the brain files it under "bad investment." It only makes sense if there's a value higher than payback.

### So is it sentimental, strategic, or deeper strength?

| View | What it claims | Where it fails |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Sentimental** | Enemy-love means having warm feelings for abusers. Just "be nice" to everyone | Breaks down immediately. You can't manufacture affection for someone who harms you, and Jesus didn't say "feel affection." He said "do good" and "pray" - those are actions |
| **Strategic** | It's enlightened self-interest. Kill them with kindness, de-escalate, win the moral high ground | Sometimes true. Non-retaliation can defuse conflict and shame an opponent. But if it *only* works when it's useful, you'd drop it the second it stops working. The text doesn't give you that out |
| **Moral strength** | It's a refusal to let the enemy define you. You don't mirror their malice. You act from a standard that isn't set by their behavior | This fits the passage best. "That ye may be the children of your Father" - the point is to imitate a kind of goodness that doesn't depend on who deserves it. The sun and rain line is the key: God gives good things without running a deserving-test first |

**The deeper claim here**: Enemy-love is strength because it requires you to be an agent, not a reaction. 

Retaliation is easy. Anyone can do it. You're just copying what was done to you. Sentiment is also easy when you feel it, impossible when you don't. 

But to choose good for someone who gave you evil means your will isn't chained to theirs. You're not controlled by what they did. That's not weakness. It's refusing to become what you hate.

It’s also the only thing that actually stops the cycle. Revenge creates two people committed to harm. Sentiment creates one person pretending harm didn't happen. Enemy-love creates one person who acknowledges the harm, doesn't repay it, and leaves space for the other person to become something different.

**Why we still resist it**: It demands we give up the story where we're purely the wronged party. If I have to "do good" to my enemy, I can't keep nursing my grievance as an identity. And it asks us to trust that justice and love aren't opposites. Jesus doesn't say "there is no justice." He says "you be the child of your Father" - meaning justice is His job, and your job is to not become unjust while you wait.

So it's not sentimental. It's not merely strategic. It’s a moral stance that your actions are grounded in who you choose to be, not who your enemy chose to be. That's why it's hard. And why it's taken seriously by people who actually try it.
