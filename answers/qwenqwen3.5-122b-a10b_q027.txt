Based on the text of Luke 18:9–14 and its immediate context, the condemnation of the Pharisee is not due to a single isolated error, but rather a specific **spiritual orientation** that corrupts his religious practice.

Here is an analysis of what exactly condemns the Pharisee and why the tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer.

### 1. What Condemns the Pharisee?
If we must select from your list, the primary condemnation is **Pride** (specifically self-righteousness), which manifests through the **use of virtue as self-display**, necessitating **comparison** and resulting in **contempt**. **False doctrine** is the least likely culprit.

Here is the breakdown of why this is the case:

*   **Pride (The Root):** The introduction in verse 9 identifies the target audience: *"certain which trusted in themselves that they were righteous."* The Pharisee’s sin is not that he broke the law, but that he trusted his own performance. His prayer is not directed toward God in dependence; it is a monologue directed toward God in declaration of his own merit. He is not asking for help; he is submitting a résumé.
*   **Comparison (The Method):** The Pharisee defines his righteousness relatively, not absolutely. He says, *"I am not as other men are... or even as this publican."* His standing before God is contingent on his superiority over others. This makes his righteousness fragile and relational rather than grounded in God's holiness.
*   **Contempt (The Fruit):** Verse 9 explicitly states these men *"despised others."* By categorizing the publican as a sinner to be avoided, the Pharisee fails the second great commandment (love your neighbor). He cannot love the person he views as beneath him.
*   **Use of Virtue as Self-Display (The Mechanism):** The Pharisee’s fasting and tithing were technically correct according to the Mosaic Law. However, he lists them to God as if they were debts owed to him by God. He uses his virtue not to honor God, but to elevate himself. The acts themselves were good; the idolatrous intent behind them was not.
*   **False Doctrine (Ruled Out):** The Pharisee actually holds the correct doctrine regarding the Law. He knows what is required (tithing, fasting). His error is not theological ignorance but **soteriological arrogance** (thinking he can save himself through the Law).

**Verdict:** The Pharisee is condemned because his **Pride** transforms his **Virtue** into a tool for **Self-Display**, which requires **Comparison** and breeds **Contempt**. He is saved by his own works, which is impossible.

### 2. Why is the Tax Collector’s Posture Spiritually Truer?
The tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer because it aligns with **spiritual reality** and the **nature of God**.

*   **Acknowledgment of Reality:**
    *   **The Pharisee** denies the reality of his own sinfulness. He claims a status (righteous) that he has not earned.
    *   **The Tax Collector** accepts the reality of his condition. He admits, *"I am a sinner."* In the biblical worldview, truth is found in humility before God's holiness. The tax collector is "truer" because he is honest.
*   **Posture of Dependence:**
    *   **Physically:** Standing "afar off," refusing to lift his eyes, and beating his breast are culturally recognized signs of deep shame, grief, and unworthiness. He places himself in the position of a beggar.
    *   **Spiritually:** His prayer (*"God be merciful to me a sinner"*) uses the Greek word *hilastērion* (propitiation/mercy). He asks for nothing he can earn. He asks only for grace.
*   **The Nature of Justification:**
    *   Jesus concludes that the tax collector went home *"justified."* In Pauline theology (which aligns with Jesus' teaching here), justification is a legal declaration of righteousness granted by God, not a status achieved by human effort.
    *   The Pharisee tried to manufacture righteousness; the tax collector received it. The tax collector is truer because he recognizes that **righteousness is a gift, not a wage.**

### Summary
The Pharisee is condemned not for his virtues, but for his **idolatry of self**. He turned his religious discipline into a ladder to climb above others.

The tax collector is vindicated because he embodies the fundamental requirement of the Kingdom of God: **Humility.** As Jesus summarizes in verse 14: *"Everyone that exalteth himself shall be abased; and he that humbleth himself shall be exalted."* The tax collector's posture was spiritually truer because it was the only one that left room for God to act.
