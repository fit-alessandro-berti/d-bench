This passage draws a direct, causal link between the inner moral state of a person and their outward speech. It uses two powerful metaphors:

1.  **The "Treasure" or "Storehouse" (θησαυροῦ):** The heart (καρδία) is depicted as a storage room. What accumulates there—the "abundance" (περισσεύματος)—is either "good" or "evil." This is not a neutral storage; it's a **moral stockpile** built over time through choices, dispositions, and affections.

2.  **The "Bringeth Forth" (ἐκβάλλει):** The mouth doesn't just *reflect* this treasure; it *produces from* or *throws out* what is inside. Speech is the inevitable output, the visible fruit of the hidden root.

**Regarding your question—symptoms, choices, or revelations—the text suggests a synthesis:**

*   **As Revelation:** Primarily, speech is a **revelation** of the heart's true contents. Jesus says, "out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks." Words are diagnostic, unveiling what is otherwise hidden. They are the tell-tale overflow that reveals the spiritual and moral nature of the "storehouse." In this sense, they are **symptoms**—outward signs of an inward condition.

*   **As Consequential Choice:** However, the passage is not deterministic in a way that removes moral agency. The metaphor of "treasure" implies something that is **stored, accumulated, and valued**. A person is responsible for the kind of moral "treasure" they stockpile through their will, attention, and repeated choices. The speech that follows is the **revelation of a prior series of choices** about what to cherish and cultivate in the heart. Therefore, while a specific word may feel like a reflex, it is the culmination of a deeper moral orientation one has chosen.

*   **Not a Neutral Symptom:** It's crucial that Jesus frames this in stark moral categories ("good" vs. "evil"). Speech is not a mere neutral symptom; it carries moral weight because it actively expresses and releases the moral quality of the heart. To "speak good things" from an evil heart is, in this framework, a moral impossibility—it would be like a viper producing grapes (cf. Matthew 7:16).

**In summary:** Matthew 12:34-35 presents speech as the **inevitable revelation** and **active output** of the heart's moral stockpile. Words are **symptoms** that diagnose the heart's condition, but that condition itself is the result of prior moral **choices** about what one treasures. Thus, they are **all three**: the choice to cultivate a certain heart leads to a certain nature, which is then *revealed* and *expressed* through speech. The passage is a profound call to moral and spiritual inventory, focusing on the source (the heart's treasure) rather than merely managing the symptoms (the words).
