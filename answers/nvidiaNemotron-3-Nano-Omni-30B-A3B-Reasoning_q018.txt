### Short answer  
In Ephesians 4:31‑32 the writer **contrasts a negative, self‑centered posture with a positive, God‑centered posture**.  
- **Bitterness** is presented as a *chosen stance* (a habit of the heart) that blocks the “tender‑hearted” and “forgiving” attitude that the passage commands.  
- **Forgiveness** is not merely a feeling; it is a *deliberate, relational practice* that mirrors the way God has forgiven us.

---

## Why the contrast is so stark

| Aspect | Bitterness (v. 31) | Forgiveness (v. 32) | How the contrast works |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Source** | Internal (the heart) – “all bitterness, wrath, anger, clamour, evil speaking, malice.” | Relational, outward‑looking – “be kind… tender‑hearted, forgiving.” | The passage moves from *what we must abandon* (internal, destructive attitudes) to *what we must adopt* (external, gracious actions). |
| **Direction** | Turns us inward, breeds resentment, isolates us. | Turns us outward, builds community, restores relationship. | The “put away” command (v. 31) is a **negative imperative**; the “be” command (v. 32) is a **positive imperative**. |
| **Theological anchor** | Implies a failure to trust God’s sovereignty and grace. | Mirrors “as God for Christ’s sake hath forgiven you” – a *participatory* imitation of divine forgiveness. | The contrast highlights that *our forgiveness of others* is **grounded in** and **enabled by** the forgiveness we have received from God. |
| **Result** | Leads to division, spiritual death, and a hardened heart. | Produces unity, peace, and spiritual life. | The writer uses the *sharp juxtaposition* to make the choice unmistakable: either we cling to bitterness or we actively practice forgiveness. |

---

## Is bitterness a feeling, a habit, a posture, or contagious?

1. **Feeling** – While bitterness certainly *feels* painful, the passage treats it as more than a fleeting emotion. It is listed among “wrath, anger, clamour, evil speaking, malice,” all of which are **behaviors** that can be cultivated or abandoned.

2. **Habit** – The plural “all” (πᾶσα) signals a *pattern* that has become part of the believer’s character. It is a **habitual disposition** that has been allowed to take root and shape daily life.

3. **Chosen posture** – The verb “put away” (ἀποτίθημι) is an *active, intentional* command. The believer is called to **decide** to discard bitterness, not merely to wait for it to disappear. This makes bitterness a *posture* of the heart that the believer must consciously reject.

4. **Contagious** – In the broader biblical context, bitterness *spreads* (cf. Hebrews 12:15 “lest any root of bitterness spring up”). The writer’s list of vices suggests that once bitterness is present, it can generate other sins (wrath, anger, slander). Thus, it is **contagious** in the sense that it can infect the whole community if left unchecked.

**Conclusion:** The text treats bitterness as a *chosen, habitual posture* that is both an internal feeling and a relational habit. Its removal is a deliberate, intentional act, and the contrast with forgiveness underscores that forgiveness is likewise a *deliberate, relational practice* that must be cultivated.

---

## Theological implication

- **Imitation of God**: “Even as God … hath forgiven you” (v. 32) ties the believer’s forgiveness to the *motive* of reflecting God’s character. The stark contrast therefore serves a *Christ‑centered* purpose: we are called to **mirror** the forgiving nature of the triune God, not simply to feel good about ourselves.

- **Community health**: By putting away bitterness, the church protects itself from the “root of bitterness” that can corrupt the whole body (cf. Heb 12:15). Forgiveness, on the other hand, **heals** relational wounds and sustains the communal life that the Spirit intends.

---

### Bottom line

Ephesians 4:31‑32 sets **bitterness** and **forgiveness** in direct opposition to make clear that the Christian life is a *choice* between two mutually exclusive postures. Bitterness is a *habitual, self‑focused stance* that must be actively “put away,” while forgiveness is a *deliberate, God‑mirroring practice* that we are called to embody. The contrast is therefore both **theological** (imitating God’s grace) and **practical** (maintaining healthy, unified community).
