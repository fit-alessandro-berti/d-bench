**Step‑by‑step unpacking**

1. **The context of Isaiah 55:6‑7**  
   - The passage sits in a larger prophetic invitation (Isaiah 55) urging people to “seek the Lord” before it is too late and promising abundant mercy when they do so.  
   - Verses 6‑7 spell out **what true repentance looks like**: a *complete turning away* from sin, not merely an external change.

2. **The two nouns – “way” (דֶּרֶךְ *derakh*) and “thoughts” (מַחְשָׁבוֹת *makh·shavot*)**  
   - **“Way”** = the *course of action*, the habitual, observable behavior that defines a person’s life (“the path one walks”).  
   - **“Thoughts”** (or “purposes”) = the inner mental‑emotional realm where desires, intentions, and fantasies are formed before they become deeds.  

3. **Why both must be addressed in repentance**  

   | Aspect | Biblical/Spiritual Reasoning |
   |--------|------------------------------|
   | **Outward conduct (“way”)** | The *visible* fruit of a person’s heart (Matt 7:16‑20). In the Old Testament, God repeatedly ties covenant obedience to “walking in My ways” (e.g., Deut 10:12; Ps 119:1). If one continues on the same sinful path, repentance is superficial. |
   | **Inward imagination (“thoughts”)** | The *root* of action. Jesus amplified this principle: hatred, lust, and murder begin in the heart (Matt 5:21‑30; 15:19). Jeremiah 17:9 says “the heart is deceitful above all things.” Repentance that stops at behavior leaves the contaminating source untouched, making relapse inevitable. |
   | **Integral whole** | Human persons are *embodied souls* (imago Dei). Moral culpability attaches to both deed and intent. Scripture therefore demands a dual “forsaking”: abandon the sinful trajectory **and** renounce its originating thoughts. |

4. **Implications for outward conduct**  
   - **Concrete change**: The sinner must *stop* walking on the old path—no more theft, lying, idolatry, oppression, etc. This is measurable: “the wicked forsakes his way.”  
   - **Renewal of habits**: New patterns (e.g., generosity, honesty) replace former practices, showing that repentance has taken hold at the level of daily life.

5. **Implications for inward imagination**  
   - **Heart‑level transformation**: The sinner must *turn away* from mental entertainments that justify or glorify sin—lustful fantasies, proud schemes, covetous longings. This involves **cognitive re‑orientation**, prayerful confession, and the work of the Holy Spirit (2 Cor 10:5).  
   - **Preventive guard**: By rejecting impure thoughts before they crystallize into action, the repentant person builds a spiritual “firewall” that safeguards outward conduct.

6. **The promise of mercy** – why both are necessary for *abundant pardon*  

   - **Mercy is conditioned on genuine turning**, not merely external compliance (see Ps 51:17; Ezek 18:30‑32). If one “forsakes his way” but continues to cherish sinful thoughts, the heart remains unclean; God’s covenantal mercy awaits *wholehearted* repentance.  
   - **God’s readiness**: The opening clause (“seek ye the Lord while he may be found…”) stresses a limited window—while He is “near.” The invitation underscores urgency: take advantage of this divine proximity by surrendering both deeds and desires now, lest the door close.  
   - **Abundant pardon** (the phrase *he will abundantly pardon*) conveys that God’s forgiveness is not merely conditional on outward reformation but also responds to a sincere inward turning. When both “way” and “thoughts” are relinquished, mercy flows freely because the repentant soul mirrors God’s own character of covenant faithfulness.

7. **Practical takeaway for believers today**  

   - **Diagnose**: Identify sinful patterns (behaviors) *and* recurring mental temptations.  
   - **Confess**: Bring both to God in prayer, using Scripture (e.g., Psalm 139:23‑24).  
   - **Renew**: Replace the old way with obedience (Eph 4:22‑24) and fill the mind with “whatever is true… noble” (Phil 4:8).  
   - **Trust**: Rest in the promise that when this dual repentance occurs, God’s mercy *abounds*—His forgiveness is not a stingy drop but a river of grace.

---

**Answer Summary**

Repentance entails forsaking both “way” and “thoughts” because true biblical conversion requires **a holistic turnaround**:  

- The **outward conduct** (the “way”) must be abandoned, demonstrating that the sinner no longer walks in sin.  
- The **inward imagination** (the “thoughts”) must also be relinquished, showing that the heart—the source of all moral action—has been turned away from evil desires and inclinations.  

This dual requirement reveals that God judges both deeds *and* motives; mercy is promised only when there is genuine, comprehensive repentance—an outward turning coupled with an inward renewal. Thus, “seek ye the Lord… while he may be found” calls people to act now, offering them **abundant pardon** precisely because they have surrendered the entirety of their life—both feet and mind—to God’s gracious work.
