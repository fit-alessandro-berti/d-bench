Short answer
- The Pharisee is condemned not mainly for false doctrine, but for pride, self-righteousness, and the use of religious works as a self-justifying display. He prays in a way that shows he trusts in his own merit, looks down on others, and does not come to God with a contrite heart.
- The tax collector’s posture is treated as spiritually truer because it expresses humility, confession of sin, and reliance on God’s mercy rather than on personal merit. In Jesus’ terms, justification comes from God’s mercy received by faith, not from outward works performed to elevate oneself.

What condemns the Pharisee
- He prays “with himself,” not to God. The prayer expresses self-centered thinking rather than genuine communication with God.
- He boasts about his own righteousness: fasting, tithing, and his superiority over other people. He uses his works as the basis of his standing before God.
- He compares himself with others and looks down on the publican, implying contempt for him.
- He thanks God that he is not like “other men,” which reveals self-justification through comparison rather than repentance.
- He does not plead for mercy or admit sin; his posture shows self-sufficiency rather than repentance.

Why the tax collector’s posture is spiritually truer
- He stands far off and beats his breast, a humble, penitent posture that signals contrition.
- He confesses his sin explicitly: “God, be merciful to me, a sinner.” He acknowledges his need for mercy, not merit.
- He does not claim righteousness or compare himself to others; he relies entirely on God’s mercy.
- Jesus concludes that this man went home justified, not the Pharisee, illustrating the core biblical claim that righteousness before God comes from mercy received by faith, not from self-justifying works.

Implications
- The parable critiques external religiosity when it is divorced from a humble, trusting heart before God.
- It affirms that proper religion is oriented toward God, recognizes one’s sin, and rests on grace, rather than on personal achievement or self-display.
