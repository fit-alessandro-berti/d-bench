Light is hated in the passage because it functions like truth made visible: it removes concealment. When “light is come into the world,” people who are practicing evil recognize that what they do will be seen “reproved” (shown to be wrong).

Here’s the chain the text implies:

1. **Truth exposes reality**
   - “He that doeth truth cometh to the light” because coming into exposure makes sense: their deeds are consistent with God, so clarity doesn’t threaten them.

2. **Evil is threatened by exposure**
   - The evil-doer “hateth the light” because the light makes wrongdoing undeniable. Once exposed, the hidden deeds can’t remain comfortably unchallenged.

3. **Shame and defensiveness follow exposure**
   - When light reveals what is really happening, a person may experience shame, fear of judgment, or the feeling that their self-justifications will collapse.
   - That produces an impulse to avoid correction: *if I don’t go near the light, my deeds won’t be clearly seen.*

4. **Avoidance becomes a moral choice**
   - The verse says such a person “neither cometh to the light, lest his deeds should be reproved.”  
   - This frames avoidance not as mere ignorance, but as a deliberate refusal to let the truth confront them.

So, light is hated because it **forces accountability**. Truth doesn’t just inform; it **reveals**, and revelation threatens those whose deeds depend on darkness—on secrecy, rationalization, and the ability to remain unchallenged. People therefore prefer darkness because it lets them keep living without being clearly seen.
