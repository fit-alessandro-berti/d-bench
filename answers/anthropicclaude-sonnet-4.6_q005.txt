# On the Difficulty of Taking Enemy-Love Seriously

## Why We Resist It

The resistance operates on multiple levels simultaneously, which is worth being honest about rather than explaining away.

**We suspect it's naive.** The command sounds like it was written by someone who hadn't encountered genuinely dangerous enemies - people who interpret kindness as weakness and exploit it systematically. History provides ample evidence for this suspicion.

**It feels like self-betrayal.** Love, in ordinary usage, involves positive regard, vulnerability, investment. Extending that toward someone who wishes you harm seems to violate basic self-respect, perhaps even complicity in your own abuse.

**It's socially punished.** Communities cohere partly through shared enemies. Refusing to hate who your group hates marks you as suspect, disloyal, possibly dangerous yourself.

---

## What It Actually Isn't

**It isn't sentimentality**, because Jesus demonstrably wasn't sentimental. He overturned tables, pronounced woes, called people vipers. The same sermon contains sharp moral distinctions.

**It isn't strategic** in the obvious sense - "be nice and they'll stop." That reading domesticates the command into enlightened self-interest, which renders it neither remarkable nor particularly demanding.

**It isn't indifference** to justice. The passage sits alongside serious moral requirements, not beside moral relativism.

---

## What It Might Actually Be

### A Cosmological Claim Disguised as Ethics

The passage's justification is crucial and almost always overlooked: *"for he maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good."*

This isn't sentimentality. It's a claim about the **structure of reality** - that existence itself is distributed without moral gatekeeping. Rain falls on fields regardless of the farmer's virtue. This is presented not as regrettable but as revelatory of the Father's character.

Enemy-love then becomes **participation in a metaphysical pattern** rather than merely psychological achievement. You aren't being asked to feel warmly toward someone who harmed you. You're being asked to act in accordance with how reality actually operates at a deeper level than human grievance.

### A Discipline Against Corruption

Hatred corrodes the one who hates in ways that enemies rarely experience directly. The person consumed by justified grievance often becomes less capable of clear perception, moral judgment, and genuine relationship - with anyone.

This isn't "hatred hurts you more than them" as therapeutic advice. It's the recognition that defining yourself against enemies *gives them structural power over your character formation*.

### A Distinction Worth Making

**Love here almost certainly doesn't mean:**
- Pretending harm wasn't harm
- Removing yourself from necessary conflict
- Treating the enemy as trustworthy

**It more likely means:**
- Refusing to reduce a person entirely to their hostility toward you
- Retaining the capacity for their humanity while defending against their actions
- Not constructing your identity around opposition to them

This is genuinely harder than hatred, not easier. It requires holding two things simultaneously - clear-eyed assessment of threat and refusal of dehumanization.

---

## The Deeper Difficulty

The real reason enemy-love is hard to take seriously is that **it requires moral confidence most people don't actually have.**

Hatred is partly compensatory. We hate enemies loudly because we need external validation that we're right, that they're wrong, that the universe is structured around our grievance. Enemy-love requires enough internal stability that you don't need the enemy's degradation to confirm your own worth.

This is why the passage frames it as becoming "children of your Father" - it's presented as requiring participation in a kind of being that most people don't yet possess, not merely following a rule they're currently ignoring.

---

## The Honest Tension

It would be intellectually dishonest to dissolve the difficulty entirely. There are hard cases:

- Does it apply to those committing ongoing violence against others, not just yourself?
- Does it constrain just war, resistance to tyranny, protective force?
- Can it be demanded of victims by those who aren't?

These tensions are real. The passage doesn't fully resolve them, and any reading that pretends it does has probably replaced the command with something more comfortable.

What seems clear is that **enemy-love is neither sentimental nor merely strategic. It represents a form of moral strength so demanding that it requires a transformation of character, not just a change of behavior** - which may be precisely why it has been discussed for two thousand years without being practiced much.
