In Ephesians 4:31–32, bitterness and forgiveness are placed side by side because Paul is contrasting two whole ways of relating to people.

He is not merely saying, “Stop having a bad feeling and start having a nice feeling.” He is describing the difference between the old self and the new self in Christ.

### 1. Bitterness is the inward root; hostility is its outward fruit

Paul lists several things together:

> “bitterness, wrath, anger, clamour, evil speaking… malice”

The order is significant. **Bitterness** is named first. It is the sour, resentful inward condition that can feed the others.

- **Bitterness**: settled resentment, a “sourness” of spirit
- **Wrath and anger**: heated emotional reactions
- **Clamour**: loud quarrelling, shouting, public agitation
- **Evil speaking**: slander, abusive speech
- **Malice**: the desire to harm or see harm come to another

So bitterness is not treated as an isolated emotion. It is more like an inner spring that can overflow into speech, conflict, and harm.

Paul’s point is: if bitterness remains, it rarely remains private.

### 2. Forgiveness is the opposite “logic” of bitterness

Bitterness keeps the wound alive by holding the offender in debt.

Forgiveness, in Paul’s sense, is the gracious release of that debt before God. The word translated “forgiving” is related to grace: forgiving one another as God has graciously forgiven you in Christ.

So the contrast is direct:

- Bitterness says, “You owe me, and I will not release you.”
- Forgiveness says, “God has forgiven me in Christ, so I will not make vengeance my identity.”

That is why forgiveness is not merely the absence of anger. It is a positive, grace-shaped posture toward another person.

### 3. Paul treats bitterness as a feeling that can become a habit and posture

Bitterness may begin as a real emotional response to hurt, betrayal, or injustice. Paul is not denying that people are genuinely wounded.

But in this passage, bitterness is something to be “put away.” That means it is not only something one suffers; it is something one can begin to nurse, protect, rehearse, and justify.

So yes, the text treats bitterness as:

- **A feeling**: resentment, hurt, anger
- **A habit**: repeatedly rehearsing wrongs
- **A chosen posture**: refusing mercy, clinging to grievance
- **A relational force**: something that spills into speech and community life

It begins inwardly, but it does not stay inward.

### 4. Bitterness is contagious because it moves through speech

Notice the connection with “clamour” and “evil speaking.” Bitterness spreads by words: complaining, slander, accusation, sarcasm, factions, and retelling injuries in a way that recruits others into resentment.

That is why Paul places this in a section about the life of the Christian community. Bitterness does not merely damage the bitter person; it can poison the atmosphere of a household, church, friendship, or community.

### 5. Forgiveness does not mean pretending evil was good

Paul is not saying:

- that the wrong did not matter,
- that justice is unnecessary,
- that reconciliation is always immediately possible,
- or that boundaries should disappear.

Forgiveness is not denial. It is refusing to let another person’s sin become the organizing center of your heart.

Christian forgiveness is grounded in this:

> “even as God in Christ forgave you.”

In other words, believers forgive not because sin is trivial, but because God has dealt with sin through Christ. The forgiven become forgiving.

### In summary

Bitterness and forgiveness are placed directly against each other because they are rival responses to being wronged.

Bitterness clings to injury, feeds anger, corrupts speech, and spreads malice. Forgiveness receives God’s mercy and extends mercy to others, replacing resentment with kindness and tenderheartedness.

So Ephesians 4 treats bitterness as more than a passing feeling. It is a potentially settled, contagious posture of the heart—one that must be put away and replaced by the grace-shaped practice of forgiveness.
