The passage from Philippians 2:5–8 highlights a profound and transformative understanding of **power**, which stands in stark contrast to conventional notions of dominance or control. Here's an analysis of the two parts of your question:

---

### **1. What Kind of Power Is Being Praised Here?**

The passage praises **self-emptying, sacrificial power rooted in humility and service**. This power is exemplified in Christ Jesus, who:
- **Possessed divine equality with God** (v. 6), implying limitless authority and glory.
- **Chose not to cling to this status for selfish gain** (v. 6), rejecting the "robbery" of divine supremacy for personal aggrandizement.
- **Emptyied himself (kenosis)** (v. 7), taking the form of a servant and embracing human fragility.
- **Ended in obedience even to death on a cross** (v. 8), the ultimate act of self-sacrifice.

This is **power that prioritizes others over self**, characterized by:
- **Kenosis (self-emptying):** Voluntarily relinquishing privilege and authority.
- **Servant leadership:** Active service rather than domination.
- **Humility:** A willingness to lower oneself, even to the point of shame or death.

This kind of power is paradoxical. It does not seek to dominate, coerce, or assert superiority but instead seeks the well-being of others. It aligns with Jesus’ teaching that "the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve" (Mark 10:45). The passage calls believers to emulate this mindset, making it a model of true strength.

---

### **2. Why Is Humility Joined to Greatness More Unsettling Than Open Domination?**

Humility combined with greatness challenges deeply ingrained assumptions about power, making it emotionally and psychologically disorienting for several reasons:

#### **a. It Subverts Expectations of Power**
- **Open domination** aligns with cultural narratives that equate power with control, coercion, or superiority. When someone wields power overtly, we recognize the dynamic as "normal"—even if we dislike it.
- **Humility with greatness**, however, defies these expectations. A powerful figure who serves, submits, or sacrifices (like Christ) disrupts our understanding of how power "should" function. We might struggle to reconcile their strength with their gentleness, questioning whether they are truly powerful or merely pretending.

#### **b. It Threatens Control and Predictability**
- Domination provides a clear hierarchy and predictability. Those in power assert their will, and those beneath them know their place. This is stable, if oppressive.
- Humility, especially in the powerful, introduces uncertainty. If someone with authority chooses to yield it, we may feel unmoored. For example, a leader who listens instead of commanding, or a wealthy person who gives instead of hoarding, challenges our assumptions about how power is "supposed" to be used. This unpredictability can provoke anxiety or suspicion ("What do they really want?").

#### **c. It Reveals the Illusion of Power as Self-Interest**
- Open domination relies on fear or respect to maintain control. Humility exposes the emptiness of power when it is not used to uplift others. It forces us to confront uncomfortable questions: *Is power inherently corrupting? Can true strength exist without assertion?* Such questions unsettle our worldview.

#### **d. It Demands a Shift in Values**
- Humility coupled with greatness requires a radical rethinking of what constitutes "power." It demands that we value **selflessness over self-advancement**, **service over supremacy**, and **love over dominance** (cf. 1 Corinthians 13:4–7). This shift can feel destabilizing because it contradicts the systems we often take for granted—whether in politics, business, or personal relationships.

#### **e. It Exposes Hypocrisy or Insecurity**
- When those with power act humbly, we might question their motives: Are they being genuine, or is this a strategic ploy? Do they fear losing influence? This suspicion is absent with open domination, where motives are clearer (e.g., "I am strong; I will use it").

---

### **Conclusion**
The power praised in Philippians 2:5–8 is a **countercultural, self-giving power** that mirrors Christ’s example. It unsettles because it upends societal norms, forcing us to rethink power as a tool for domination into a force for redemption. Open domination, while harsh, is at least "familiar"—a predictable dynamic. Humility and greatness, however, demand that we confront uncomfortable truths about ourselves and our world. They challenge us to embrace a strength that is not about control but about love, a revolution that can feel more threatening than tyranny because it calls us to die to self-interest and live in communion with others.
