# The Cravings Behind the Curtain

The verse draws a remarkably precise distinction, and the Greek syntax reveals it. The problem is not stated in the phrase "before men" (ἐμπροσθεν τῶν ἀνθρώπων) but in the purpose clause that follows: **"to be seen of them"** (πρὸς τὸ θεαθῆναι αὐτοῖς). The preposition πρὸς with the infinitive marks *aim* or *intent*. So the danger lies in the trajectory of the act, not in whether other eyes happen to fall on it.

## Three things to notice

**1. The transaction has been pre-spent.**
The verse operates on a striking economic logic. To seek the gaze of an audience is to cash in your reward at the moment of the act. There is no surplus left for heaven. The act may still be outwardly good—bread still goes to the hungry, prayer still rises—but the spiritual capital has been already withdrawn. Goodness becomes a currency in the marketplace of reputation.

**2. The agent is subtly reoriented.**
When the heart aims itself at human applause, the act is no longer directed toward the neighbor in need or toward God. It bends back toward the self. The giver becomes a spectator of his own virtue. This is what makes the hypocrisy so corrosive: the self is now both actor and audience, and genuine other-regard is lost in the loop.

**3. The act itself is transformed.**
Even when the alms are real, the same bread that was meant to be a gift becomes a performance piece. The recipient becomes a prop. The poor man is converted, against his will, into a stage on which the giver plays the role of benefactor. The act is no longer love; it is the staging of love.

## So is visibility itself the problem?

Clearly not, on the text's own terms. Jesus healed publicly, taught in the temple courts, fed multitudes in the open air. The Sermon on the Mount itself is a very public act of generosity. What the verse forbids is not the presence of witnesses but the *catering to them*. Public good is dangerous when the audience has become the end; it is not dangerous when it is merely the accidental context.

The diagnostic question, then, is not "Was anyone watching?" but "For whom was this done?" Visibility is a condition; craving is the disease. One can be seen and still be free; one can be unseen and still be performing for an imagined audience of one.

## The deeper pathology

Behind the craving to be seen lies a more troubling condition: a self that cannot bear to be invisible. Such a self has lost the capacity to act without witness, which means it has lost the capacity to act freely. It is, in a small but real way, enslaved to the opinions of others—and the verse names this as a form of idolatry. The self, not God, sits on the throne of the heart. The remedy is not secrecy as a strategy (the next verses forbid that too—"do not sound a trumpet"), but the cultivation of a self whose goodness does not require its own corroboration.

That, at least, is what the verse is asking you to consider.
