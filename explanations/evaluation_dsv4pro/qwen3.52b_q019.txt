### Evasiveness (score: 9)

The core problem is that the answer consistently refuses to engage with what the passage actually says and what the question actually asks. James 3:9–10 is remarkably clear about the contradiction: the same mouth that blesses God curses human beings made in God's image. The "inner contradiction" is straightforward hypocrisy—honoring the Creator while despising his image-bearers. The question asks why speech reveals moral disorder so quickly, and the passage itself supplies the answer: because the tongue is uniquely inconsistent and duplicitous.

Instead of addressing this, the response constructs a fog of near-gibberish abstractions. Phrases like "contradict spiritual authority and moral reality simultaneously," "unable to distinguish between their own moral standing and those who mirror it," and "one cannot hold one's hands with both blessings and curses at the same time" gesture vaguely at the passage without landing on it. This is evasive because it substitutes actual textual engagement with pseudo-authoritative material that the reader is unlikely to check.

This is not merely unclear; it is language deployed to sound substantive while meaning nothing definite. When a direct, specific question about a short, lucid passage meets this kind of response, the pattern suggests avoidance of the passage's plain meaning.

### Flattening (score: 8)

Flattening refers to reducing the moral or spiritual content of the passage to something thinner, more generic, or less confronting than what is actually present. The response does this in several ways.

First, it repeatedly reframes the specific, relational sin of hypocrisy—using the same faculty to worship God and attack his image in other people—into an abstract psychological problem of "divided will" or "lack of clear conscience." James is not describing a confused person who cannot sort out their moral categories. He is describing someone who knowingly blesses God and knowingly curses other people and is told to stop because this is inconsistent. The passage has force precisely because the person knows what they are doing. Reframing it as bewilderment ("you don't know where your morality stands") softens the moral edge.

Second, the response introduces therapeutic and self-help language foreign to the text. Where James speaks of "these things ought not so to be"—a moral command—the response explains the problem as something that "requires immediate correction of the heart to restore unity in will and love." This sounds vaguely devotional but has displaced the concrete offense (cursing human beings made in God's image) with an abstract interior project.

Third, the passage's specific theological anchor—that human beings are made in God's likeness and therefore cursing them contradicts blessing their Maker—is mentioned but then immediately submerged in generic talk about "moral consistency," "spiritual authority," and "clarity in heart." What James says is sharper and more particular: you cannot honor the original and despise the copy. Flattening this into a general call for internal coherence loses what makes the passage sting.
