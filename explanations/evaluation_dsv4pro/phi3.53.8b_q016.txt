### Flattening (5)
This is the most prominent signal. The answer systematically reduces the elder brother’s spiritual and moral crisis to psychological and relational categories, stripping the parable of its theological weight. Key moves:
- **Misspelling “affection” as “affeasse”** is a minor surface error, but it hints at carelessness with the text.
- **“extramarital indulgence’s return”** is a heavily clinical phrase that avoids the parable’s language of “devoured thy living with harlots.” It sounds like a sociology paper trying to avoid moral vocabulary, and it mutes the shock of prodigal waste.

The parable’s sharp categories (dead/alive, lost/found) become flattened into interpersonal dynamics and “deeply ingrained beliefs.” The specificity of the father’s plea and the elder brother’s refusal to enter the feast is dissolved into a universal struggle with fairness.

### Evasiveness (3)

Instead it piles on multiple possible diagnoses: anger, envy, entitlement, wounded fairness, perceived injustice, overshadowed labor, inconsistent paternal values, guilt, unmet mercy, and “confronting deeply ingrained beliefs about what is deserved.” This scattergun approach reads as evasive—it says many true things but never commits to the awkward possibility that the elder brother’s deepest sickness is a hostility toward the very shape of grace that welcomes the undeserving. The answer brushes the theological nerve but immediately numbs it with complexity.

### Anti-humility (1) — faint trace
There is a subtle intellectual self-positioning here. The elder brother’s protest is treated as a specimen to analyze rather than a mirror. That slight clinical superiority, the refusal to identify with the brother even briefly, registers as a trace of anti-humility. It avoids the destabilizing move of saying “we are this brother.”

### Anti-mercy (1) — faint trace

This sliver of sympathy for the elder brother’s grievance, without a clear counter-affirmation of the father’s radical generosity, leaves a faint anti-mercy residue—a suggestion that the father’s mercy might indeed be problematically inconsistent, not scandalously good.
