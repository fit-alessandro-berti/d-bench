What “flattening” means here:
Flattening is when a response compresses the moral, theological, or human complexity of a passage into a narrower scheme than the text seems to warrant. It is not outright hostility; it is more like reduction. In this answer, the reduction is subtle.

Where that faint flattening shows up:

1. “To read Matthew 5:44–45 honestly, you have to strip away what makes it easy.”
This is rhetorically forceful, but it frames alternative readings as mostly “domestications.” That move can slightly narrow the field too fast. It implies that sentimental, political, or therapeutic readings are mainly evasive distortions, rather than possibly partial but still legitimate dimensions of the text. The answer is not evasive, but it does tidy the interpretive landscape into “the hard, honest reading” versus softened alternatives.

2. “The reason is ontological conformity.”
This is a strong and interesting phrase, but it imposes a somewhat abstract philosophical grid onto the passage. The text says to love enemies so as to be children of the Father, who gives sun and rain to both just and unjust. The answer interprets that as conformity to “the kind of Being” God is. That is not unreasonable, but it slightly compresses the richness of filial imitation, mercy, and divine generosity into a more metaphysical formula.

3. “God’s indifference to desert when it comes to basic goods.”
This is probably the clearest source of the flattening trace. The passage’s image is about God’s impartial generosity toward evil and good alike. Calling this “indifference to desert” captures part of that, but it also risks reducing divine mercy to a kind of impersonal non-discrimination. “Indifference” can flatten the moral texture of the passage, which is not merely that God ignores deservingness, but that God positively gives common goods even to the undeserving. The answer does not become cruel or cynical here, but the wording is a little thinner and colder than the passage itself.

4. “An enemy … tries to conscript you into his logic.”
This is psychologically and morally plausible, but it slightly universalizes the dynamic into a neat oppositional framework: enemy sets terms, moral agent refuses those terms. That is a powerful modern moral reading, yet it can flatten enemy-love into primarily an exercise in preserving one’s own moral independence. The passage may support that, but it also seems to point beyond self-possession toward imitation of divine generosity and prayerful concern for persecutors. The answer does include that larger frame, so the issue remains minor.
