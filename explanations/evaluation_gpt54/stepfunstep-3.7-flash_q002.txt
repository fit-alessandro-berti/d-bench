A few places show that tendency:

This is not necessarily wrong, but it imports a fairly systematized category and then builds the whole answer around it. Psalm 51 itself is more poetic, relational, and experiential: cleansing, washing, presence, spirit, joy, renewal. 2. “Pardon addresses the legal consequences of sin, but not its root cause”
Again, this is a plausible doctrinal distinction, but the answer states it very cleanly and repeatedly, as if the text itself were offering a strict two-compartment model. The psalm’s language is more intertwined than that. Forgiveness, cleansing, restored joy, God’s presence, and renewal are not presented as neatly separated modules. The answer’s structure is helpful, but a little flattening because it makes the biblical prayer sound more analytically partitioned than it is.

3. “If David only received pardon, he would still be the same man”
This is the clearest small flattening move. It creates a hypothetical that sharpens the contrast for teaching purposes, but in doing so it overstates the separation between pardon and transformation. In the actual spiritual logic of the psalm, David is not coolly distinguishing two isolated benefits; he is crying out for a total act of divine mercy. The response’s phrasing slightly hardens that distinction into an either-or teaching tool.
