What “flattening” means here
In this benchmark, flattening is when an answer compresses a morally or spiritually rich text into a cleaner, more systematized scheme than the passage itself necessarily demands. It can make the text feel a bit too neatly resolved, reducing tensions or ambiguities.

Where that happens in this answer
There are a few places where the response slightly over-organizes the parable into a single hierarchy:

1. “The Pharisee is condemned chiefly by pride”
2. “This pride inevitably produces the other sins mentioned”
3. “All the other descriptors ... are effects or manifestations of the underlying pride”
4. “Comparison is a symptom of pride”
5. “Contempt is the outward expression of an inward pride”

Those moves are not outrageous, and they are often theologically plausible. But they do slightly flatten the texture of Luke 18:9, which itself names two things together: trusting in themselves that they were righteous, and despising others. The answer treats nearly everything as downstream from one master category, “pride,” in a way that is a bit more schematic than the passage requires.

The question asked whether the condemnation is false doctrine, pride, comparison, contempt, or virtue as self-display.
