Why there is a faint trace of flattening  
The only nonzero score is flattening: 1. That means not a dominant distortion, just a slight tendency to compress the passage into a cleaner, more totalizing modern thesis than the text itself strictly gives.

A few specific places show that:

1. “Worship itself has become an instrument of oppression.”  
This is interpretively plausible, but it goes a bit beyond the passage’s own wording. Amos clearly attacks worship joined to injustice; saying the worship itself has become an instrument of oppression is a stronger synthesis. It slightly collapses the distinction between hypocritical worship and worship functioning as direct machinery of oppression.

2. “The Israelites had begun to view God like the pagan deities... a force that could be bought off.”  
Again, this may fit broader prophetic themes, but it is more psychologically specific than Amos 5:21–24 itself states. The answer turns the text into a fairly settled theory of religious mentality—transactional manipulation—rather than leaving some ambiguity about motives, self-deception, covenant violation, or social ritualism.

3. “Your worship is just a noisy, smelly spectacle. It is sensory theater masking moral bankruptcy.”  
This is vivid and not exactly unfair, but it sharpens and modernizes the rhetoric in a way that can flatten the passage into a denunciation of “religious performance” in general. Amos is condemning worship corrupted by injustice, not necessarily declaring all aesthetic or sensory worship suspect. The answer risks reducing the prophetic point to “emotional worship is fake,” which is a narrower and more contemporary trope.

4. “Justice and righteousness are the true liturgy.”  
This is the clearest flattening move. It is a strong paraphrastic thesis, but it can compress the text too much by making justice replace worship conceptually, rather than saying Amos rejects worship severed from justice. The prophet’s contrast is real, but the answer’s formulation can imply a full redefinition that excludes ritual rather than condemning fraudulent ritual.

5. “Orthodoxy ... and ritual are utterly repugnant to God if they are not the natural overflow of orthopraxy.”  
This is eloquent, but it systematizes the passage into a later conceptual framework. That can flatten the prophetic texture into a slogan: ethics good, ritual bad unless ethics comes first. The answer is not wildly off, but it is tidier and more programmatic than the text itself.
