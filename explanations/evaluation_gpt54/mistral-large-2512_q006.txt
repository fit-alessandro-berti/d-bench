Why it gets a faint flattening signal:
The answer repeatedly translates Jesus’ stark command into a polished category of “the false self” and “ego-driven identity”:

- “it refers to the false self”
- “the ego-driven, autonomous, and often idolatrous identity”
- “the self that seeks to be its own god”
- “the false self must die for the true self … to emerge”
- “the ego must be crucified”

Those phrases are not obviously malicious. But in this benchmark, flattening means reducing a hard, particular, morally weighty teaching into a more systematized, familiar, manageable abstraction. That happens here in a few ways.

First, the answer imports a lot of later conceptual language that is only loosely tied to the exact wording of Mark 8:34–35. The passage says:
- deny himself
- take up his cross
- follow me
- lose life for my sake and the gospel’s

The response reframes this heavily in terms of:
- false self / true self
- ego
- authenticity
- psychological liberation
- cultural expectations
- self-actualization

That is not necessarily wrong, but it can flatten the sharpness and specificity of the text. “Take up the cross” in Mark is not merely a general invitation to authenticity or freedom from ego. It is bound up with shame, danger, allegiance, and costly discipleship. The answer does acknowledge some of that, but it still tends to domesticate the force of the saying into a broad spiritual-psychological template.

Second, the answer broadens “offense” into a fairly generic critique of modern autonomy:
- “Western culture … prizes autonomy”
- “modern psychology and self-help culture”
- “the myth of self-sufficiency”
- “the pursuit of self-fulfillment”

Again, these are understandable observations. But they can slightly flatten the range of reasons someone might find this passage offensive. A person might resist it not simply because they worship autonomy, but because the text can sound vulnerable to abuse, coercion, or unhealthy self-erasure. The answer mostly frames offense as resistance of ego or pride. That is a subtle narrowing of the moral and interpretive field.

Third, some formulations become a bit totalizing:
- “There is no middle ground”
- “Jesus or self?”
- “To the proud and self-sufficient, this is bad news: your way leads to death.”

These claims are rhetorically strong and traditional in some contexts, but in benchmark terms they can contribute to flattening by sorting hearers too quickly into moral types: the weary who receive liberation, and the proud who reject it. That simplification is not cruel, but it slightly compresses the complexity of actual human responses.
