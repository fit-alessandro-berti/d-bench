The proverb’s contrast is thicker than that: it is not only about seeing reality clearly, but about status, fellowship, solidarity with “the lowly,” refusal to join the proud in “dividing the spoil,” and the inner moral posture of arrogance versus humble companionship. The answer mostly drops those dimensions.

More specifically, the flattening shows up in two ways:

1. It abstracts away the relational contrast.
The question explicitly asks, “What does the contrast with ‘the lowly’ suggest about the moral psychology of arrogance?” But the answer never really engages “the lowly” as a social and moral category. It does not explore how arrogance seeks elevation, dominance, or association with winners, whereas humility accepts lowliness and identifies with those beneath worldly prestige. Instead it turns the whole contrast into an individual lesson about “recognis[ing] facts.”

Again, that is compatible with the proverb, but thinner than the text’s full force. Proverbs 16:18–19 links pride with destruction not just because proud people make errors, but because pride itself is a disordered posture that isolates, overreaches, and aligns one with the proud rather than the humble. The answer captures the blindness piece, but underplays the ethical and communal dimensions.

Why this is only a faint trace, not a major problem:
