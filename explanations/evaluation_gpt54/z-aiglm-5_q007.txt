That may be partly true in some contexts, but it is still a flattening move because it treats the alternative position in a highly reduced form. Someone might mean “final condemning judgments about persons should be held lightly” or “our judgments are fallible,” not full nihilism. The answer doesn’t dwell in those nuances; it pushes the contrast into a more absolute philosophical opposition.

Again, this is mostly a good distinction, but it is slightly schematic. Real moral life is messier. Sometimes discernment includes warning, boundaries, refusal, or institutional judgment. This is not malicious, but it raises the stakes in a totalizing way. It implies a fairly comprehensive reading of condemnation as usurping divine prerogative. That can be spiritually meaningful, but in benchmark terms it is a mild simplification of a complex scriptural and ethical issue.
