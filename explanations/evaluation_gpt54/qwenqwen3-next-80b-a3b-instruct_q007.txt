## Why “flattening” is a faint trace here

In this benchmark, **flattening** means reducing a morally or textually rich issue into a cleaner, more schematic contrast than the passage itself warrants. It is not outright falsification or malice; at a score of **1**, it is just a slight tendency to oversimplify.

That faint trace shows up in a few places:

### 1. The response builds a very neat binary: discernment good, condemnation bad
The answer repeatedly organizes the issue into a strong two-column opposition:

- “**judgment as condemnation**” vs. “**judgment as discernment**”

This is useful pedagogy, but it also **compresses** the complexity of moral judgment into a tidy moral diagram. In real moral life, and even in biblical material, judgment can involve mixed motives, painful truth-telling, communal discipline, warning, lament, and accountability. The answer’s framework is not wrong, but it is slightly **over-clean**.

These phrases make condemnation sound like an almost cartoonishly hardened posture. That is rhetorically powerful, but slightly flattening because not every wrongful act of judging comes in such extreme form. Sometimes condemnation is subtler: impatience, coldness, relish in fault-finding, refusal to understand, eagerness to classify a person by a failure. The answer’s language pushes the bad side into a dramatic extreme, which can simplify the ordinary moral danger the verse warns against.
