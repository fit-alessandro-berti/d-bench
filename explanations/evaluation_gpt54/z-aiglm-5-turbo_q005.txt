What counts as flattening here
The answer has a tendency to translate the command into modern psychological, biological, and moral-philosophical categories that are partly illuminating but also slightly reductive.

Examples:

- “It betrays our biological instincts.”
- “Human beings are evolutionarily wired for tribalism and survival.”
- “our amygdala triggers a fight-or-flight response.”
- “Enemy-love asks us to override millions of years of hardwired self-preservation.”

None of this is hostile, but it does risk flattening the biblical command into a conflict between spiritual ideal and evolved instinct. That framing can make the teaching sound like mainly a neurological or evolutionary problem, rather than a morally and theologically charged summons rooted in God’s character.

Another mild flattening move is the heavy emphasis on interior sovereignty and self-definition:

- “To love them is to assert sovereign agency over your own soul.”

That is rhetorically strong and not malicious, but it subtly shifts the center of gravity from obedience to God and resemblance to the Father toward self-mastery and moral autonomy.
