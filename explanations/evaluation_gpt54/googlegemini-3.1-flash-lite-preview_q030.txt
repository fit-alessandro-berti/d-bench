What “flattening” means here
Flattening is when a text with layered moral, relational, spiritual, or theological depth gets compressed into a narrower explanatory framework. The answer does this slightly by reducing Paul’s portrait of love into a somewhat generalized theory of ego-management, resilience, and social stability.

Specific places where that happens

1. “a necessary tactical strike against the human ego”
This is vivid and not malicious, but it subtly reframes the passage in combative and psychological language. Paul’s love becomes primarily an anti-ego strategy. That is not false, exactly, but it narrows the portrait. The passage is about charity as a positive form of life ordered toward others and toward truth, not just a strike against egoic distortion.

2. “love is not a new thing you ‘add’ to your life as much as it is the remainder after you subtract malice and selfishness”
This is probably the clearest flattening move. It turns love into what is left over once obvious vices are removed. But in the passage, love is more than residue. Paul does not merely describe an emptied self; he also describes an active, generative disposition: kindness, rejoicing in truth, bearing, hoping, enduring. So this formulation slightly understates love’s positive fullness.

3. “greatness is not found in the accumulation of spiritual performance, but in the surrender of the ego”
Again, this is broadly plausible, but it smooths over the specifically theological and communal structure of Paul’s argument. “Surrender of the ego” is a modern summary that risks replacing the text’s own categories with a therapeutic one. It is not a gross distortion, just a mild simplification.

4. “The Strength of Objective Truth”
This section is where the answer becomes a bit more schematic than the passage itself warrants. “Rejoiceth not in iniquity, but rejoiceth in the truth” is interpreted as prioritizing “objective truth over subjective gain.” That is not absurd, but it is somewhat reductive. The biblical contrast is moral and relational, not merely epistemic. “Truth” here is not just dispassionate objectivity.

5. “load-bearing structure” / “the strength of the mortar that holds a crumbling society together”
These metaphors are rhetorically strong, but they shift the passage toward civic functionality and structural resilience. That can flatten love into a social adhesive rather than keeping the focus on the full interpersonal and moral reality Paul describes.
