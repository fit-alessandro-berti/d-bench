Flattening: 4
This is the strongest concern in the evaluation. “Flattening” here means the answer reduces a rich command into a narrower psychological or power-based framework.

Several moves do that:

- “It demands you act in direct opposition to logic, self-interest, and likely political power.”
  This is overstated and flattening. The passage grounds enemy-love in God’s character and common grace, not in a blanket rejection of “logic.” Calling it contrary to logic makes it sound irrational or anti-human in a simplistic way.

- “To truly love an enemy is to say my safety doesn't outweigh our connection.”
  That is a very loaded paraphrase. The text says love, bless, do good, and pray; it does not directly say one’s safety should be subordinated in this absolute way. The answer compresses a complex moral command into a dramatic self-risk formula.

- “The deeper moral strength” becomes “a redefinition of identity.”
  Identity is present in the text (“children of your Father”), but the answer turns that into a heavy abstraction about selfhood rather than keeping close to the stated rationale: God is kind in providence to both evil and good.

- “It requires cognitive discipline to shift your focus from ‘who wins’ to ‘who God makes.’”
  Again, the answer turns the passage into a kind of mental or spiritual technique. That is flatter and more abstract than the text’s moral vision of imitation of divine generosity.

- “If one is truly capable of loving their enemy, they have already mastered this form of moral strength.”
  This especially flattens the command into a mastery framework, as though the point were achieving a high moral state.

So the flattening score is not saying the answer is nonsense. It is saying the answer persistently reduces a relational, God-anchored moral command into psychology, strategy, identity-construction, and moral performance.
