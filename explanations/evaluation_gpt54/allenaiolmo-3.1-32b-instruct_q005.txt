Specific places this happens:
- “Most people are wired for self-preservation...”  
  This is not wrong, but it shifts quickly into a universal human-nature explanation. That can flatten the specifically theological and eschatological force of the text into a fairly standard psychology-of-conflict account.
- “Societies often reward toughness...”  
  Again, plausible, but broad. It turns the saying into a generic critique of social conditioning rather than dwelling on Matthew’s more pointed claim: imitate the Father who gives sun and rain indiscriminately.

  This is the clearest flattening move. It reframes the command primarily in the register of “moral strength,” which is compatible with the text but not identical to it. Calling it “the ultimate test of strength” can subtly domesticate the passage into virtue-talk that modern readers already admire.

  This is rhetorically appealing, but it stylizes the teaching a bit. It risks converting enemy-love into an inspiring heroic posture, which can flatten the difficult, prayerful, obedient quality of the command.
