What counts as flattening here

The answer tends to compress a rich biblical image into a somewhat rigid explanatory scheme. A few places show that:

- “External compliance operates through pressure from outside - law, fear of punishment, social expectation, or reward.”
- “The new heart operates differently - the source of motivation moves inside.”
- “The compliance and the person become integrated rather than opposed.”

This is insightful, but it also sets up a fairly stark binary: outside = pressure and mere surface conformity; inside = authentic transformation. That risks flattening the text’s moral and theological complexity into a near psychological model. In biblical thought, external obedience is not always mere hypocrisy, and inward renewal does not simply mean internalized motivation in a modern sense.

Another flattening move is the strongly systematized language:

- “It isn't primarily about moral improvement through effort but about ontological replacement.”
- “The deeper philosophical problem”
- “the compliance paradox”

These phrases are not hostile, but they recast the passage into a philosophical framework that can narrow its texture. “Ontological replacement” in particular is a heavy abstraction. It may illuminate one aspect of the text, but it also risks reducing prophetic promise, covenant restoration, divine mercy, and moral renewal into a single metaphysical mechanism. That is the kind of thing this benchmark means by flattening: making a many-sided reality too one-dimensional or too schematic.

A further example is this line:

- “hardness of heart isn't primarily a series of bad choices but a condition that produces bad choices.”

Again, there is real interpretive value here. But the “isn't primarily” framing can underplay the moral and relational dimensions of hardness in Scripture by shifting strongly toward condition-language. The answer is trying to explain why hardness is described materially, but in doing so it leans a bit hard toward structural incapacity and away from the full interplay of responsibility, rebellion, mercy, covenant, and repentance.

Likewise:

- “Teaching better rules to a stone heart produces better-educated resistance”
- “Threatening consequences to a stone heart produces more sophisticated avoidance”

These are rhetorically sharp and memorable, but they slightly overstate the case. They flatten the role of law, warning, discipline, and teaching by portraying them mostly as ineffective against inward corruption. That may fit one theological angle, but it simplifies the broader scriptural landscape.
