What “flattening: 1” means here
This is not saying the answer is distorted in some major way. A 1 is just a faint trace that the response sometimes smooths the verse into a polished, systematic church-lesson format that reduces some of its tension or specificity.

Places where that faint flattening shows up

1. It turns a compact admonition into a very standardized moral template
The verse is short, sharp, and situational: if someone is overtaken in a fault, restore him gently, while watching yourself. The answer expands this into a highly organized framework:

- “who may correct”
- “how to correct”
- “why they must keep a watch on themselves”
- “the goal,” “the manner,” “the interior check”

That structure is not wrong, but it can flatten the live moral force of the text into a tidy instructional system. The verse is more searching and precarious than a neat three-part ministry manual.

2. It slightly over-systematizes “the spiritual”
The answer says:
- “The verse is a miniature job-description for every Christian who steps into the delicate work of fraternal correction.”
- “The one who corrects must first recognize his own reliance on the Spirit.”
- “the spiritual leader acts like a physician”

Those moves are broadly edifying, but they smooth “ye which are spiritual” into a settled category of equipped correctors, even “spiritual leader,” instead of letting the phrase retain its possible edge: those who think or are regarded as spiritual must act in a way that proves it by gentleness and self-distrust. The answer is a little too confident and role-defined.

3. It imports extra themes that domesticate the verse
Examples:
- “gift of service” (Eph 4:7)
- “empty himself” (Phil 2:7)
- “the law of Christ”
- “full fellowship of God’s kingdom”
- practical coaching like “lower voice, private setting, open-ended questions”

None of that is sinister. But from this benchmark’s perspective, it can count as faint flattening because the answer overlays the verse with a familiar pastoral synthesis. The result is slightly less text-specific and slightly more generic Christian formation language.

4. It softens the warning by making it very programmatic
The verse’s final caution—“lest thou also be tempted”—has a real sting. It warns that the corrector is in danger. The answer does acknowledge this, but often in a controlled, explanatory tone:
- “self-suspicion is not a paralyzing doubt”
- “the interior check”
- “check your motive”

Again, this is good counsel, but the danger can feel managed and domesticated. The verse is not just advising best practices; it is warning about vulnerability and moral instability. The answer captures that, but perhaps too safely.

- It emphasizes meekness in a sincere, non-dominating way.
