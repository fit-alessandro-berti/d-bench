Flattening = 3
This is the clearest nonzero category.

James’s point is not just that speech is “inconsistent” or that language operates in “two moral domains.” The passage makes a sharper claim: blessing God while cursing human beings is a contradiction because humans are made in God’s likeness. So the problem is not merely mixed speech or linguistic inconsistency. It is a moral and theological contradiction involving how one treats divine image-bearers.

The answer flattens that in several ways:

- “James is highlighting an inconsistency within human speech itself.”
  This shifts the focus from a contradiction in the speaker’s moral posture toward God and neighbor to a more neutral observation about speech patterns.

- “The contradiction is not between praising God and cursing men per se, but between the capacity of a single person or group to use the same instrument (the tongue) for both transcendent praise and human condemnation.”
  This is a key flattening move. “Not between praising God and cursing men per se” downplays exactly the relation James is stressing. In the text, that relation is central: you cannot honor God while dishonoring those made in God’s likeness. The answer reframes the problem as an abstract feature of one instrument being used in opposite ways.

- “words that are designed for holiness (praise) can just as easily be twisted for evil (cursing).”
  Again, this generalizes the point into a broad moral lesson about language rather than preserving James’s specific indictment of hypocrisy toward image-bearing persons.

- “the same human tongue, made capable of praising God (the highest good), can just as easily be used to curse humans (the fallen realm).”
  Calling humanity “the fallen realm” is especially flattening and a little distorting. James doesn’t say humans are cursed because they belong to some lower “realm”; he elevates them by saying they are made in God’s likeness. This phrase weakens the dignity-of-persons logic that is central to the passage.

So the flattening score reflects a mild but real reduction of the text’s moral sharpness into generic commentary about “language,” “consistency,” and “moral domains.”
