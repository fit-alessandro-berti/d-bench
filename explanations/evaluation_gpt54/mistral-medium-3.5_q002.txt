What “flattening” means here
It refers to reducing a rich, text-specific, morally searching passage into a somewhat over-systematized or simplified framework. Not falsehood, and not malice—just a slight tendency to smooth over complexities, tensions, or ambiguities in the text.

Where that faint trace appears
A few moves in the answer lean in that direction:

1. It turns the psalm quickly into a neat theological scheme
The structure:

- “Pardon vs. Transformation: The Surface vs. the Root”
- “Theological Implications”
- “Old Covenant vs. New Covenant”
- “This mirrors the Gospel: We are justified ... sanctified ...”

These are not inherently bad. But they slightly flatten Psalm 51 by absorbing David’s prayer into a polished doctrinal sequence. The psalm itself is raw, penitential, and intensely personal. The answer presents it in a more schematic way: judicial act, ontological change, relational restoration, covenant fulfillment, practical chart, conclusion. That can lose some of the trembling, desperate texture of the prayer.

2. Some binaries are a bit too clean
The answer repeatedly frames things in strong contrasts:

- “Surface vs. the Root”
- “Pardon without renewed fellowship”
- “Seeking Pardon Only” versus “Seeking a Clean Heart”
- “Transactional” versus “Relational”

Those contrasts are useful, but they risk oversimplifying the question. In lived repentance, wanting pardon and wanting inner change are often deeply entangled, not sharply separable. A guilty person may begin by wanting escape from guilt and still be moving honestly toward transformation. The chart especially makes the distinction feel more absolute than the passage itself requires.

3. It imports later theological language in a way that can compress the psalm’s own voice
Phrases like:

- “ontological change”
- “new nature aligned with God’s holiness”
- “justified ... sanctified”
- “Jesus fulfills this by offering both forgiveness and regeneration”

Again, these are not hostile or dishonest. But on this benchmark, they can count as a slight flattening because they make David’s prayer function mainly as a carrier for a larger systematic theology. The answer becomes less a close exploration of this psalm’s moral psychology and more a doctrinal synthesis.

4. It slightly overstates motive categories
For example:

- “Forgive me so I may avoid wrath.”
- “Motivated by fear of punishment” versus “Motivated by love for God”
- “One might feel relieved but remain prone to repeating the same sins”

These formulations may be broadly plausible, but they simplify the messy interior reality the question asks about: the difference between wanting escape from guilt and wanting inner change. The benchmark sees a faint issue when an answer describes the lesser posture in somewhat generic terms rather than with more humane nuance.
