Why the one point for flattening:
The answer slightly smooths out the specifically theological force of the passage into a more general ethical lesson. That is not a major distortion, just a mild reduction in texture.

A few places where that happens:

- “From an evolutionary and social perspective, humans are wired…”
  This is a reasonable observation, but it starts by translating the difficulty of enemy-love into instinct, survival, and social psychology. That framing is not wrong, but it can flatten the passage’s sharper spiritual and theological claim into a generic account of human behavior.

By emphasizing usefulness and outcomes, the answer slightly reduces a God-centered command into a partly instrumental ethic.

  This broadens the teaching into a cross-tradition moral principle. That move is not hostile, but it does blur some of the distinctiveness of the passage’s logic: “that ye may be the children of your Father.” The benchmark would see that as a mild flattening of the text’s own frame.

- “love as a radical act of faith in the shared humanity of all people”
  That is morally attractive language, but it shifts the center of gravity from God’s indiscriminate providence to humanistic solidarity. That is another small example of reducing the passage’s theological depth into a more universal moral sentiment.

- The closing paraphrase: “Love your enemies because you don’t know who they are…”
  This is the clearest flattening move. The passage itself gives a specific reason: because the Father shows common grace to evil and good alike. “You don’t know who they are” is not really the text’s reason. It is not malicious, but it substitutes a softer modern rationale for the one actually emphasized.
