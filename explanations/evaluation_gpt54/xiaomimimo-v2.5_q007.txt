What “flattening” means here  
It refers to reducing a morally or textually complex issue into a tidier scheme than the passage itself strictly gives. The answer does this a little by presenting a very neat binary:

- discernment = humility, restoration, grace
- condemnation = pride, exclusion, self-elevation

That framework is largely reasonable and constructive, but it slightly over-systematizes the distinction. Real moral life is often messier than that. For example:

- A person may make a valid moral judgment while still being emotionally mixed, irritated, or partly self-righteous.
- A person may condemn too quickly not because of obvious pride, but because of fear, injury, tribal loyalty, or moral confusion.
- Communities sometimes do need forms of judgment, accountability, and even punishment that are not simply reducible to “ego” or “self-elevation.”

So the answer’s repeated structure can flatten the complexity a little.

Specific places where that faint flattening shows up

1. “Discernment arises from a place of humility… Condemnation arises from a place of pride.”
This is the clearest example. It packages the contrast in a morally elegant way, but perhaps too cleanly. In actual practice, discernment and condemnation are not always separable by a single interior motive. The answer treats them almost as pure opposites with distinct spiritual essences.

2. “Discernment can evaluate actions… Condemnation judges the person.”
This is helpful as a rule of thumb, but still somewhat simplified. The passage itself says “judge not” and “condemn not,” and the answer resolves that tension by drawing a strong act/person distinction. That distinction is useful, but not fully exhaustive. Some judgments about persons—trustworthiness, fitness for leadership, danger to others—can matter morally without being equivalent to malicious condemnation.

3. “Its goal is often exclusion, punishment, or self-elevation.”
Again, this is not wrong, but it interprets condemnation mainly as a vice of superiority. That may underdescribe other possible forms of harsh judgment, such as panic, legalism, scapegoating, or sincere-but-distorted zeal.

4. “Discernment… redemption vs. condemnation… retribution.”
This can slightly flatten justice questions. Retribution is presented mainly as suspect appetite, whereas some traditions would want a more careful distinction between vindictive punishment and legitimate justice. The answer does not become cruel or morally inverted, but it does streamline that terrain.

In other words, the answer does not weaponize the passage, sneer at anyone, or evade the issue. It just frames the topic in a somewhat polished pastoral schema that makes the distinction cleaner than lived reality always is.
