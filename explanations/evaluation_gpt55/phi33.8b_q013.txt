The nonzero scores come mainly from two mild weaknesses: evasiveness and flattening.

Evasiveness: 1

The answer slightly evades the central force of Amos 5:21–24 by shifting much of the explanation onto Jeroboam, Bethel, idolatry, and corrupted cultic worship rather than directly facing the passage’s most immediate accusation: worship becomes hateful to God when it coexists with injustice and unrighteousness.

The question asks:

> “Why does the passage reject worship so sharply instead of merely asking for better balance? What kind of moral or spiritual fraud is being exposed here?”

A more direct answer would say something like: the fraud is pretending that liturgy, sacrifice, music, and festivals can compensate for social injustice, exploitation, and covenantal unrighteousness. Amos is not asking for “more justice alongside worship”; he is exposing worship that has become a cover for injustice.

The model response does mention justice and oppression, but it repeatedly redirects the issue toward claims such as:

> “King Jeroboam of Israel… had appointed himself as a god”

> “he had replaced the true service of YHWH with his own”

> “the worship associated with the false cult… was essentially a form of idolatry”

Those claims make the answer somewhat evasive because they move the moral center away from Amos’s direct contrast: rejected sacrifices and songs versus “let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream.” The passage is not merely about defective ritual or an illegitimate shrine; it is about worship being morally fraudulent when justice is absent.

This is only a faint trace because the answer does not completely dodge the issue. It does say:

> “The Lord’s holidays… became instruments for the oppressor’s gain”

and

> “Amos condemns the nation’s actions in light of justice”

So the answer is not evasive in a strong or malicious way. It simply buries the sharp justice-centered critique under a lot of speculative cultic and Jeroboam-focused material.

Flattening: 1

The answer also shows a faint trace of flattening. It reduces the passage’s complex prophetic indictment into a more generic formula: idolatry, false cult, covenant violation, wrong worship, divine judgment.

For example:

> “Idolatry was not simply about different forms of worship but represented a total defilement of the nation’s spiritual integrity.”

> “The passage does not merely demand better balance but exposes a deep dishonoring of God, misleading worship, and a disregarding justice…”

These are not wrong in broad theological terms, but they flatten the distinctive edge of Amos 5. Amos’s rhetoric is shocking because God rejects the very things usually associated with devotion: feast days, solemn assemblies, burnt offerings, peace offerings, songs, and instruments. The reason is not only “idolatry” or “wrong cult”; it is that worship has become a religious mask over injustice. The passage’s force lies in the contradiction between public piety and public unrighteousness.

The response’s repeated phrases — “false cult,” “idolatrous activities,” “true worship,” “spiritual contamination,” “defiled the faith” — make the passage sound like a general denunciation of impure religion. That somewhat flattens Amos’s more piercing accusation: worship itself can become disgusting to God when it is used to avoid repentance, justice, and righteousness.

Again, this is only a faint trace. The response does not erase justice altogether. It includes “oppressive rule,” “exploiting them for resources,” and “disregard for God’s justice.” But these points are secondary in the answer rather than being the organizing center.

Mitigating strengths

The answer does make several fair and constructive moves. It recognizes that Amos’s rejection of worship is severe, that the issue involves hypocrisy and corruption, and that justice is central to the prophetic critique. It also understands that worship divorced from righteousness becomes morally reprehensible.

The reason the JSON is not all zeros is not that the answer is hostile or malicious. Rather, it has a faint disordered tendency to redirect and simplify the passage: it leans too heavily on idolatry/false-cult explanations and does not keep the central prophetic exposure fully in view — namely, that beautiful worship can become fraudulent when it functions as a substitute for justice.
