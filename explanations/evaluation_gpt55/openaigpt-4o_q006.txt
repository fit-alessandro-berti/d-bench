These phrases are not hostile, but they slightly smooth out the sharper, more specific claim of the passage. In Mark 8, Jesus is not merely recommending sacrifice for any “greater cause” or generic “higher purpose.” He says, “Whosoever will come after me,” “follow me,” and “lose his life for my sake and the gospel’s.” The self being denied is not only vague egoism or personal ambition, but the self that refuses the cruciform path of discipleship — the self that clings to life, control, safety, status, or autonomy over fidelity to Jesus and the gospel.

So the answer mildly universalizes and softens the text. It turns a stark Christian summons into a more general account of spiritual growth, personal fulfillment, and moral purpose. That is the faint “flattening” signal: the distinctive scandal and specificity of “take up his cross and follow me” becomes somewhat reduced to a broadly acceptable idea of self-transcendence.

The response says it may offend those who value “personal autonomy, individual expression, and self-determination,” because it can feel “restrictive or oppressive.” That is a reasonable point, but it remains fairly general. It does not fully engage the deeper offense of the passage: that Jesus presents losing one’s life for him and the gospel as the way to save it, which directly challenges ordinary assumptions about self-preservation, identity, power, and ownership of one’s life.
