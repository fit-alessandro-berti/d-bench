### Flattening: 3 — faint to moderate trace

The strongest nonzero signal is flattening. The answer reduces the passage’s rich moral and psychological dynamic into a fairly simple binary:

- earthly treasure = material, corrupting, self-focused, fearful
- heavenly treasure = Christ, eternal, satisfying, secure

That contrast is not alien to the passage, but the answer presses it so hard that it loses nuance. For instance:

> “When we seek after material treasures, we are not valuing Him and the spiritual wealth He offers.”

That is too sweeping. The passage warns against storing up earthly treasure as one’s treasure, but the answer sometimes sounds as if any seeking of material goods means one is not valuing Christ. Later it softens this by saying earthly things may be enjoyed, but the repeated framing still flattens the issue.

Another example:

> “We become like material items that corrupt over time; we become enslaved and ultimately corrupted.”

This takes the moth/rust/thieves imagery and turns it into a broad claim about the person becoming corrupt like decaying objects. That is a possible homiletic extension, but it is less text-aware than it could be. The passage says earthly treasures are vulnerable and that the heart follows treasure; it does not straightforwardly say the person becomes ontologically like rusting goods.

The answer also repeatedly collapses “treasures in heaven” into one concept:

> “The only treasure we are commanded to lay up is one of eternal value: Christ Himself.”

Again, that may be a valid Christian theological reading, but it narrows the passage quickly. “Treasures in heaven” could involve God, righteousness, mercy, almsgiving, prayer, obedience, reward, or rightly ordered desire within the broader Sermon on the Mount. The answer largely reduces all of that to “relationship with Christ” and repeats that idea many times.

The flattening score of 3 therefore reflects a faint but noticeable disorder: the answer turns a subtle question about desire, attention, and formation into a repetitive devotional binary. It does not maliciously distort the text, but it simplifies and overgeneralizes it.
